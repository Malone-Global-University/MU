Why I Think Rick Chow is Guilty of Murder

An examination of the pursuit, shooting, and legal proceedings surrounding the death of Cyrus Carmack-Belton and why I believe the verdict represents a failure of justice.

Why I Believe Rick Chow Was Guilty and Why Cyrus Carmack-Belton Was Denied Justice

Cyrus Carmack-Belton was 14 years old. He was a child. He was chased. He was cornered. And he was shot dead.

That is the reality at the center of this case, and no courtroom language, no self-defense argument, and no legal maneuvering can erase it.

I do not accept the idea that this was some clean, reasonable, split-second misunderstanding where adults were simply forced into a tragic outcome. I believe Cyrus was the victim from the beginning. I believe he was wrongly accused inside the store, pursued anyway, and then killed when he tried to defend himself from grown men who had already decided to treat him like prey.

The basic facts matter. A child was accused of theft. The accusation was not proven. He left the store. The adults followed him. The situation did not resolve peacefully. It escalated into a chase. That chase ended in death.

That is not justice. That is not restraint. That is not a system behaving as though a Black child’s life matters as much as anyone else’s.

I do not accept the idea that Cyrus pointing a gun, if that is what happened, somehow magically converted the men chasing him into the innocent party. That logic is backwards. If a person is being chased by armed adults, the law should recognize that the person being chased is the one in danger. A frightened child trying to defend himself is not the aggressor in any moral sense. He is the person trying not to be killed.

What I reject most is the way this kind of case gets sanitized after the fact. Once the child is dead, suddenly we are told to focus only on a “split-second” moment. Suddenly the men doing the chasing become the people we are supposed to understand. Suddenly the boy who ran for his life is the one whose actions are treated as suspicious. That is the real outrage.

I am not persuaded by the idea that a chase into a corner somehow creates a fair self-defense claim for the pursuers. I am not persuaded that a child’s desperate act of resistance should be used to excuse the people who forced him into that position. I am not persuaded that this is anything other than a case where adults escalated a situation and then tried to hide behind law after a child died.

To me, this is the plain truth: Cyrus Carmack-Belton was denied justice.

He was denied justice when he was chased instead of protected. He was denied justice when his fear was not treated as reasonable. He was denied justice when the adults involved were allowed to frame his resistance as proof against him. And he was denied justice again when the system accepted a theory that, in effect, asks the public to believe that a child running for his life was the real danger.

I do not accept that. I do not believe it. And I do not think honest people should accept it either.

Cyrus should have been treated as a child in danger, not as a problem to be hunted down. He should have been allowed to go home alive. The fact that he did not is the failure. The fact that anyone can still dress that failure up as a lawful outcome is the deeper failure.

The Basic Facts

On May 28, 2023, 14-year-old Cyrus Carmack-Belton entered the Xpress Mart Shell station in Columbia, South Carolina. What began as a dispute over suspected shoplifting ended with the teenager dead from a gunshot wound. Store owner Rick Chow was charged with murder and later stood trial for the killing.

The case became controversial because investigators determined that Cyrus had not stolen the water bottles that triggered the confrontation. Nevertheless, Chow and his adult son pursued the teenager from the store, and the pursuit ended approximately 130 yards away when Chow fired the fatal shot.

On June 1, 2026, a South Carolina jury found Chow not guilty of murder. Public reaction to Chow’s not-guilty verdict is deeply divided along different viewpoints:

  1. Many observers accepted the verdict but if support is measured by community goodwill, open defense of his character, or public allies, it is incredibly low.

  2. Many others viewed it as a profound failure of justice. The visible public presence surrounding the trial remains overwhelmingly critical of Chow.

I fall into the latter category.

What Happened Inside the Store?

The confrontation began with an accusation that Cyrus had stolen four bottles of water. According to evidence presented during the investigation and trial, the accusation was mistaken.

Surveillance footage reportedly showed Cyrus returning the bottles to the cooler. Prosecutors argued that he did not leave the store with any stolen merchandise and that no evidence of theft was ever found.

Witness testimony indicated that Cyrus attempted to demonstrate that he had taken nothing. According to trial reporting, he pulled at his clothing and indicated that his pockets were empty before leaving the store.

This detail matters because the entire confrontation rests on the original accusation. If the accusation was false, then the moral and legal basis for everything that followed becomes substantially weaker.

The Pursuit

After Cyrus exited the store, the incident should have been over. Instead, Rick Chow and his adult son chose to follow him.

The pursuit continued for approximately 130 yards. During that chase, Cyrus ran from two adults who believed he had stolen from them despite evidence suggesting otherwise.

In my view, this pursuit is the most important fact in the entire case. Without the pursuit there is no confrontation. Without the confrontation there is no shooting. Without the shooting there is no death.

The defense focused heavily on the final moments before the shot was fired. I believe that approach ignores the decisions that created those final moments. The danger cited to justify the shooting did not emerge from nowhere. It emerged from a confrontation the Chows voluntarily continued.

The Shooting

The shooting occurred at the end of the pursuit. What happened during the final seconds remains the most disputed part of the case.

The defense argued that Cyrus possessed a handgun and pointed it at Andy Chow, creating an immediate threat that justified deadly force. Rick Chow maintained that he fired to protect his son.

Prosecutors presented a different account. While acknowledging that Cyrus possessed a firearm, they argued that he never threatened anyone with it and that the weapon fell during the chase. Multiple witnesses reportedly testified that they did not see Cyrus point a gun at anyone.

What is not meaningfully disputed is that Cyrus was shot in the back and that the encounter occurred only after he had been pursued from the store.

Why I Reject the Self-Defense Claim

The central issue in this case is not whether Rick Chow claimed to be afraid. The central issue is whether that fear was reasonable and whether the law should reward people who create dangerous situations and then cite those situations as justification for deadly force.

Self-Defense Belongs to the Person Being Chased

When armed adults pursue a fleeing teenager, the stronger claim to fear belongs to the teenager. Cyrus was the one attempting to escape. Cyrus was the one being followed. Cyrus was the one facing armed pursuers.

If self-defense exists to protect innocent people from imminent danger, then it is difficult to understand why the fleeing child is denied that protection while the pursuers receive its benefit.

Why Pointing a Gun Does Not End the Analysis

Even if one accepts the defense's version of events, context still matters. The law does not evaluate conduct in a vacuum.

A person who encounters danger while pursuing another individual is not in the same position as a person who is suddenly attacked without warning. The pursuit remains part of the analysis.

The Problem With Split-Second Arguments

Supporters of the acquittal frequently focus on the final seconds before the shot was fired. I believe that approach is fundamentally flawed.

The final seconds cannot be separated from the minutes that preceded them. The chase created the confrontation. The confrontation created the fear. The fear became the justification for deadly force.

The Pursuit Created the Threat

In my view, the threat cited by Rick Chow was a threat that Rick Chow helped create. Had the pursuit never occurred, there would have been no need for anyone to claim self-defense.

The Problem With the Verdict

The verdict asks the public to accept that the law's protection belongs to the armed pursuer rather than the fleeing child. That conclusion is difficult for many people to accept.

Jurors were required to apply the law as instructed by the court, but a jury can reach a legally binding verdict and still reach the wrong conclusion.

To me, the evidence pointed toward an unjustified killing rather than a legitimate act of self-defense.

The verdict also leaves troubling questions about how fear, threat, and reasonableness are interpreted when the victim is young, Black, and unable to testify on his own behalf.

What Justice Would Have Required

Justice would have required recognizing that the pursuit was the defining fact of the case.

The law should not permit people to manufacture danger through pursuit and then claim protection from the danger they helped create.

A conviction would have affirmed a simple principle: individuals who escalate a false accusation into a deadly confrontation cannot avoid responsibility merely by asserting that they became afraid during the confrontation they initiated.

Such a verdict would not have restored Cyrus's life. It would, however, have affirmed that the law applies equally to those who pursue and those who are pursued.

Historical Parallels

Ahmaud Arbery’s killers are all serving life sentences in separate Georgia Department of Corrections facilities. The three men received their life sentences for the 2020 murder of Ahmaud Arbery, with both McMichaels denied any chance of parole. You can track their current custody statuses and locations using the Georgia Department of Corrections Offender Search. The three mobsters Chow, his son, and his wife are all still free after initiating the the whole event; even though their own cameras that they were watching prove Cyrus did not steal, yet they false accussed him anyways.

The Arbery case shows the consequence of using a false allegation to justify lynch mob mentality and justice, and how chasing a man can lead to forced conflict, loss of human life, and subsequent murder charges.

Kyle Rittenhouse was pursued by armed men and shot them in what was later ruled self defense. A chased person who is armed has the right to defend themselves if faced with imminent threat. Kyle was given that right. Cyrus was fleeing two grown men who were armed yet he was not given the same standard of justice for armed self defense that Kyle was. If Cyrus pointed a gun then it would have been out of self defense as a last resort when no other safe option against imminent harm remained due to his aggressor actions.

The Rittenhouse can shows how a fleeing victim can be in fear of his life and have a legal claim to self defense when his pursuers catch up to him.

The parallels are strong for citing similar initial and final conditions (critical moment of battle) of all three cases led to similar outcomes of justice except for the Cyrus Belton Shooting. This proves that justice was not dispensed fairly. Sadly, the outcome of Cyrus Beltom is similar to cases such as Emmitt Till, Latasha Harlins, and the countless African American men, women, and children of mob violence.

Conclusion

In my view, Cyrus Carmack-Belton was not killed because he posed an unavoidable threat. He was killed because adults chose to continue a confrontation that should have ended when he left the store.

The danger used to justify the shooting arose from a pursuit that never had to occur. For that reason, I do not believe this was a case of legitimate self-defense. I believe it was a case in which a false accusation, an unnecessary pursuit, and a fatal decision combined to produce an outcome that the justice system ultimately failed to correct.

People ask "What was a 14 year old doing with a gun..."; my response is running for his life from a modern day lynch mob:

  • The theft accusation was false.
  • Cyrus attempted to leave.
  • The Chows chose pursuit.
  • The pursuit created the confrontation.
  • A pursuer cannot easily transform himself into a victim by citing a danger created during the pursuit.
  • Therefore the self-defense claim fails.

The logic I am challenging is essentially:

  • We chased him.
  • We caught up to him.
  • We were afraid of him.
  • Therefore we were justified in shooting him.

This is why many observers compare the case to other historical incidents involving false accusations followed by pursuit. The comparison is not necessarily that the events are identical. The comparison is that a relatively minor or nonexistent offense becomes the justification for a chase, the chase becomes the justification for fear, and the fear becomes the justification for deadly force.

I do not think the prosecution failed to make their case. I think the jury failed to apply any reasonable standard to the facts at hand.

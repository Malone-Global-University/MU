Failed Justice for African Americans

A recurring investigative column examining cases where justice failed African Americans.

From slavery-era violence to modern courtroom controversies, this series documents the gap between American ideals and American outcomes.

About This Column

Throughout American history, African Americans have often encountered a legal system that failed to provide equal protection, equal accountability, or equal justice. In some cases, crimes against Black citizens were ignored. In others, wrongful accusations, biased investigations, unequal sentencing, or controversial verdicts raised serious questions about fairness and the rule of law.

This recurring column examines those cases. Each article focuses on a specific incident, trial, investigation, or legal controversy and asks a simple question: Did justice succeed, or did it fail?

The purpose of this series is not partisan advocacy. The purpose is historical documentation, civic education, and critical examination of how legal institutions have operated in practice.

Why This Series Exists

Justice systems derive legitimacy from public confidence. That confidence depends on equal treatment under law. When communities believe justice is unevenly applied, trust deteriorates.

Understanding past failures is necessary for evaluating present institutions. Historical memory allows citizens to recognize recurring patterns, identify reforms that worked, and understand the human consequences of legal decisions.

Editorial Approach

Every article in this series is expected to rely on court records, investigative reporting, primary documents, historical sources, and publicly available evidence whenever possible.

The column does not assume guilt or innocence beyond what evidence supports. Instead, it evaluates whether legal institutions acted fairly, competently, consistently, and in accordance with the principles they claim to uphold.

Column Standard

This series is committed to evidence-based analysis, historical accuracy, and intellectual honesty. Cases may be controversial, but controversy is not the objective. The objective is understanding whether justice was achieved, denied, delayed, or compromised.

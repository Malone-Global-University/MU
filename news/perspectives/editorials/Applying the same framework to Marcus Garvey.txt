Applying the same framework to Marcus Garvey:

**1. What problem did he identify?**

Garvey believed the fundamental problem facing people of African descent was not merely segregation, voting restrictions, or lack of education.

He believed the deeper problem was dependence.

In his view, Black people around the world lacked:

* Political power.
* Economic independence.
* Collective confidence.
* National consciousness.
* Control over major institutions.

He argued that a people who depended on others for jobs, capital, transportation, leadership, and recognition would remain vulnerable regardless of legal reforms.

In simple terms:

**Problem:** Black dependence, powerlessness, and loss of collective confidence.

**2. What solution did he propose?**

Garvey's solution was large-scale racial self-organization.

His program included:

* Black pride.
* Racial solidarity.
* Economic self-reliance.
* Black-owned businesses.
* International organization.
* Independent institutions.
* African redemption and development.
* Mass mobilization.

The primary organizational vehicle was the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA).

He also launched business ventures, most famously the Black Star Line, which was intended to symbolize and facilitate Black economic independence.

Unlike Du Bois, whose audience was often educated elites and policymakers, Garvey appealed directly to ordinary working people on a massive scale.

**3. How did he define success?**

Under this framework, success would be measured by:

* Movement size.
* Organizational reach.
* Racial pride.
* Economic initiative.
* Institutional independence.
* Global Black consciousness.
* Mobilization of previously disengaged people.

Garvey's scorecard was psychological, organizational, and economic.

His question was often: "Can Black people believe in themselves and organize themselves independently?"

**4. What results occurred?**

Garvey achieved remarkable successes in mobilization.

The UNIA became one of the largest mass movements in Black history.

Its chapters spread across:

* The United States.
* The Caribbean.
* Latin America.
* Africa.
* Europe.

Millions encountered Garvey's message either directly or indirectly.

He helped popularize ideas such as:

* Black pride.
* African identity.
* Global racial solidarity.
* Economic self-help.
* Independent Black institutions.

Many later movements drew inspiration from his rhetoric and vision.

However, his economic and organizational programs faced serious difficulties.

The Black Star Line struggled financially and operationally.

Garvey was convicted of mail fraud related to promotion of the shipping venture, and his movement lost momentum after his imprisonment and deportation.

Many of the institutions he hoped would become permanent engines of Black economic power proved less durable than he envisioned.

**5. Evaluation**

Under your framework, the key question is:

"Did Garvey successfully create Black pride, mass confidence, and independent organizational energy?"

The answer is overwhelmingly yes.

Few leaders of the twentieth century inspired such a large popular following among ordinary Black people across multiple continents.

If the standard is psychological transformation and mass mobilization, Garvey ranks among the most successful Black leaders in history.

If the standard is creation of durable economic institutions capable of transforming the material condition of Black people on a large scale, the record is more mixed.

His vision was enormous, but many of the specific enterprises did not survive.

Using your formulation:

**Problem:** Black dependence, powerlessness, and lack of collective confidence.

**Solution:** Mass organization, racial pride, economic self-help, Black enterprise, global solidarity, and independent institutions.

**Result:** Massive international movement, strengthened racial consciousness, expanded Black pride, and lasting influence on later nationalist and Pan-African movements.

**Assessment:** Major success in mobilization, identity formation, and psychological liberation; partial success in building durable economic institutions capable of sustaining the full program he envisioned.

One interesting thing that emerges from your framework is that Garvey and Washington are sometimes treated as similar because both emphasized self-help and institution-building. But they were actually solving different problems.

Washington's question was: *How do we develop practical economic capacity under Jim Crow?*

Garvey's question was: *How do we transform a dispersed and psychologically subordinated people into a self-conscious global nation?*

Those are related problems, but not the same problem, which is why their methods and measures of success differ.

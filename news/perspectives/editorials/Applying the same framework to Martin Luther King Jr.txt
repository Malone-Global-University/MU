Applying the same framework to Martin Luther King Jr.:

**1. What problem did he identify?**

King believed the central problem facing Black Americans was the system of legalized segregation and exclusion that had developed after Reconstruction.

He saw:

* Segregation.
* Disenfranchisement.
* Racial discrimination.
* Unequal access to public institutions.
* Systematic denial of democratic rights.

as the primary obstacles to freedom.

As his thinking evolved, he increasingly argued that poverty and economic inequality were also fundamental problems, affecting both Black and poor white Americans.

In simple terms:

**Early and middle career problem:** Jim Crow and denial of democratic citizenship.

**Later career problem:** Jim Crow plus entrenched economic inequality.

**2. What solution did he propose?**

King's strategy centered on nonviolent direct action.

He advocated:

* Mass protest.
* Boycotts.
* Civil disobedience.
* Coalition building.
* Moral pressure.
* Media exposure.
* Federal intervention.
* Movement organization.

The goal was not merely to persuade individuals but to create political crises that government leaders could no longer ignore.

Organizations such as the Southern Christian Leadership Conference became vehicles for this strategy.

Later, King increasingly focused on:

* Economic justice.
* Labor rights.
* Anti-poverty programs.
* Broader democratic reform.

**3. How did he define success?**

Under this framework, success would be measured by:

* Desegregation.
* Voting rights protection.
* Federal civil-rights legislation.
* Greater democratic participation.
* National recognition of Black citizenship.
* Reduction of racial barriers.

Later in life he also hoped to see:

* Reduced poverty.
* Greater economic opportunity.
* Structural economic reform.

**4. What results occurred?**

King helped lead one of the most successful mass movements in American history.

During his public career:

* The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was enacted.
* The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was enacted.
* Formal segregation was significantly weakened.
* Black voter participation increased in many areas.
* The civil-rights movement gained national and international legitimacy.

His campaigns in places such as Montgomery, Birmingham, and Selma became turning points in American political history.

At the same time, the economic transformation he increasingly sought in his final years was not achieved.

He was assassinated while supporting sanitation workers in Memphis, before the Poor People's Campaign could fully develop.

**5. Evaluation**

Under your framework, the key question is:

"Did King successfully destroy Jim Crow and secure legal equality?"

The answer is largely yes.

If the standard is dismantling the legal framework of segregation and securing federal protection for civil and voting rights, King was extraordinarily successful.

The principal targets of his movement were directly attacked through major legislative victories.

However, if the standard is achieving the broader economic justice agenda that occupied much of his final period, the record is incomplete.

That project remained unfinished at his death.

Using your formulation:

**Problem:** Jim Crow segregation, political exclusion, and denial of democratic citizenship.

**Solution:** Nonviolent direct action, mass mobilization, coalition building, moral pressure, and federal intervention.

**Result:** Major civil-rights legislation, expanded voting rights, weakened segregation, and greater democratic inclusion.

**Assessment:** Major success in dismantling the legal structure of Jim Crow and securing civil-rights victories; partial success on the larger economic transformation agenda that became increasingly important to him in his final years.

One interesting result of applying your framework across all six leaders is that King and Douglass emerge as the figures most clearly associated with achieving their primary political objectives during their lifetimes. Douglass saw slavery abolished. King saw the legal foundations of Jim Crow substantially dismantled. The others made major contributions, but their principal goals were either only partially realized or were inherently harder to measure because they involved consciousness, institution-building, or long-term social development rather than a specific legal system that could be formally abolished.

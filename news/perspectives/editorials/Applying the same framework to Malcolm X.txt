Applying the same framework to Malcolm X:

**1. What problem did he identify?**

Malcolm X believed the central problem facing Black Americans was not simply segregation laws or lack of economic opportunity.

He believed the deeper problem was powerlessness.

In his view, Black Americans had been conditioned to accept mistreatment, rely on others for protection, and seek approval from institutions that often had no intention of treating them as equals.

He saw:

* White supremacy.
* Political weakness.
* Psychological subordination.
* Dependence on outside leadership.
* Inability to defend Black communities.

as interconnected parts of the same problem.

In simple terms:

**Problem:** Black powerlessness and psychological subordination.

**2. What solution did he propose?**

Malcolm's solution evolved throughout his life, but several themes remained consistent.

He advocated:

* Self-respect.
* Self-defense.
* Political awareness.
* Independent Black organization.
* Economic self-reliance.
* Community control.
* International pressure on the United States.
* Human-rights framing of the Black struggle.

He rejected the idea that oppressed people should passively endure violence.

One of his most influential arguments was that Black people should defend themselves "by any means necessary" when attacked.

After breaking with the Nation of Islam, his perspective broadened. He increasingly emphasized international alliances, Pan-African connections, and universal human rights while retaining his commitment to Black self-determination.

**3. How did he define success?**

Under this framework, success would be measured by:

* Increased self-confidence.
* Political consciousness.
* Independent thinking.
* Willingness to defend community interests.
* Greater international awareness.
* Stronger demands for dignity and respect.

His scorecard was largely psychological and political.

He wanted Black Americans to stop seeing themselves as passive subjects and start seeing themselves as actors with agency.

**4. What results occurred?**

Malcolm achieved extraordinary influence despite a relatively short public career.

He transformed debates about race, power, and self-defense.

His speeches reached audiences that felt alienated from mainstream civil-rights organizations.

He helped popularize ideas such as:

* Black pride.
* Black self-determination.
* Political independence.
* International solidarity.
* Skepticism toward gradualism.

His influence can be seen in later movements such as the Black Panther Party, broader Black Power activism, and many forms of modern Black political thought.

However, Malcolm was assassinated at age 39.

Because of that, he never had the opportunity to build a large, stable, long-lasting organization under his own mature post–Nation of Islam vision.

He left behind ideas, influence, and inspiration, but not a fully developed governing system or enduring institutional network comparable to Tuskegee, the NAACP, or the Southern Christian Leadership Conference.

**5. Evaluation**

Under your framework, the key question is:

"Did Malcolm X successfully challenge Black powerlessness and transform consciousness?"

The answer is largely yes.

If the standard is awakening political awareness, encouraging self-respect, and forcing Americans to confront uncomfortable realities about race and power, Malcolm was enormously successful.

If the standard is building durable institutions that could carry out a long-term political program, his record is necessarily incomplete because his life was cut short.

Using your formulation:

**Problem:** Black powerlessness, psychological subordination, and inability to control one's own destiny.

**Solution:** Self-defense, political education, independent organization, internationalization of the struggle, and uncompromising demands for dignity.

**Result:** Expanded Black political consciousness, greater emphasis on self-determination, strengthened traditions of Black pride and self-defense, and lasting influence on later movements.

**Assessment:** Major success as an ideological and psychological liberator; incomplete as a nation-builder or institutional architect because he was killed before he could fully develop and implement his mature political vision.

What's striking when you compare Malcolm X to the others is that his primary achievement was not a law, a school, a court victory, or a business network. His greatest achievement was changing how millions of people thought about themselves, their rights, and their relationship to power. In that sense, he was operating largely in the realm of consciousness and political psychology.

South Africa Faces a Split Screen: Ramaphosa Pressure and Improved Credit Outlook

South Africa’s month brought political pressure over the revived Farmgate impeachment process and a more positive signal from Moody’s on debt and fiscal management.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Africa Politics and Economy

Summary

South Africa delivered a complicated story this month. Reuters reported that President Cyril Ramaphosa faced renewed pressure after the Constitutional Court revived the impeachment process connected to the Farmgate scandal, while the ANC publicly backed him. Separately, Moody’s revised South Africa’s credit outlook from stable to positive while keeping the rating at Ba2. Those developments point in different directions: political controversy on one side, improved fiscal confidence on the other.

Two stories at once

South Africa’s May news cycle produced a split screen. On one side, President Cyril Ramaphosa faced renewed political pressure over the Farmgate scandal after the Constitutional Court revived the impeachment process. On the other side, Moody’s changed South Africa’s outlook from stable to positive, citing stronger fiscal performance and progress toward debt stabilization. Together, the stories show a country where political risk and economic repair are moving at the same time.

The Farmgate issue

The Farmgate controversy concerns foreign currency stolen from Ramaphosa’s farm and questions about how the matter was handled. Reuters reported that South Africa’s top court revived the impeachment process and that parliament would have to establish a committee. Ramaphosa said he respected the court ruling and would defend himself. The ANC’s executive later backed him, making it less likely in the near term that the process would remove him from office. But the political damage is not only about removal. It is also about trust.

Why impeachment processes matter even when removal is unlikely

Impeachment proceedings can shape public life even when they do not end a presidency. They consume legislative attention, affect investor confidence, sharpen opposition attacks, and force ruling parties to publicly defend their leaders. They also test whether institutions can handle allegations involving powerful officeholders without appearing captured or partisan. For South Africa, the key question is whether the process can be conducted transparently enough to preserve institutional credibility.

The Moody’s signal

Moody’s decision to move South Africa’s outlook to positive gives the government a different kind of headline. Reuters reported that Moody’s cited stronger fiscal performance, a rising primary surplus, and expectations of debt stabilization. The rating itself remained below investment grade at Ba2, so the decision should not be overstated. Still, a positive outlook is meaningful because it signals that creditors see some improvement in the country’s debt trajectory.

Inflation and growth risks remain

The improved outlook does not erase South Africa’s economic challenges. Reuters also reported that inflation quickened in April, increasing expectations that the South African Reserve Bank could consider tighter monetary policy. Higher inflation can squeeze households and raise borrowing costs, while weak growth and inequality remain long-term constraints. Moody’s positive outlook is therefore not a declaration of success. It is a sign that fiscal management may be moving in a better direction despite difficult fundamentals.

The bigger lesson

South Africa’s story is important because it resists a simple narrative. The country is not only a scandal story, and it is not only a reform story. It is both. Strong institutions must be able to investigate political controversy while economic institutions work to stabilize public finances. The next phase will depend on whether parliament handles the impeachment process credibly, whether the government maintains fiscal discipline, and whether ordinary South Africans see any improvement in prices, jobs, and public services.

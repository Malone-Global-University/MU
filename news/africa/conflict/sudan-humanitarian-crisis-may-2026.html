Sudan's Humanitarian Crisis Deepens as Drone War and Hunger Spread

Civilian deaths, mass displacement, and worsening food insecurity made Sudan one of Africa's most urgent humanitarian emergencies in May 2026.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 25, 2026

Updated: June 21, 2026

Section: Conflict

Summary

Sudan's war intensified in May 2026 as drone strikes and ground fighting continued to kill civilians, disrupt markets and hospitals, and push millions deeper into hunger. Humanitarian agencies warned that the conflict remained one of the world's worst displacement and food crises, with families trapped between the front lines and shrinking aid access.

Drone Warfare Intensifies

Drone attacks became one of the defining features of Sudan's conflict in May. Strikes on cities, roads, and civilian infrastructure added to the death toll and reinforced the sense that no area was fully safe. Reports from the month described a sharp escalation in attacks that hit people far from the battlefield, including displaced families, commuters, and neighborhoods already struggling with shortages.

The widening use of drones reflected how the war between Sudan's armed forces and the Rapid Support Forces had evolved into a fast-moving, technologically driven conflict with devastating consequences for civilians. Each new strike raised fears of retaliation, further displacement, and a deepening collapse of public order.

Hunger Reaches Millions

Food insecurity remained a central part of the crisis. By mid-May, aid reports indicated that nearly 20 million people were facing acute hunger, with many families unable to buy basic staples because prices had risen and supply routes had broken down. In some areas, markets were damaged or unreachable, leaving communities dependent on irregular aid deliveries.

The war has turned farming, transport, and trade into high-risk activities. With the planting and harvest cycles disrupted, Sudan faced the danger of a long-term hunger emergency that could extend well beyond the fighting itself. Humanitarian workers warned that malnutrition, disease, and displacement were feeding one another in a vicious cycle.

Hospitals, Aid Routes, and Civilian Life Under Pressure

Medical facilities and aid corridors came under repeated strain during the month. Hospitals struggled with shortages of fuel, medicine, and staff, while attacks and insecurity made it harder for aid groups to move supplies into contested areas. Families seeking treatment often had to travel long distances or go without care altogether.

The disruption of everyday life was visible across the country. Teachers, merchants, farmers, and civil servants were all affected as schools closed, wages went unpaid, and public services broke down. For many Sudanese civilians, the crisis was no longer only about front lines and military offensives; it was about survival from one day to the next.

International Response

Humanitarian organizations continued to call for expanded access, protection for civilians, and more funding for emergency relief. International diplomats warned that Sudan's war was increasingly becoming a regional stability problem as displacement crossed borders and food insecurity spread across the wider Horn of Africa.

Aid agencies also urged donors not to lose sight of Sudan as other global crises competed for attention. The country's emergency remained immense, but the funding and access needed to respond were still falling short of the scale of the disaster.

Risks for Regional Stability

Sudan's collapse has consequences far beyond its borders. Neighboring states have had to absorb refugees, manage border pressures, and prepare for possible spillover from the conflict. At the same time, worsening hunger and insecurity in Sudan risk adding pressure to already fragile humanitarian systems across East and Central Africa.

May 2026 showed that the war had entered yet another dangerous phase. With no clear path to peace and civilians continuing to bear the heaviest burden, Sudan remained one of the continent's most urgent and unresolved crises.

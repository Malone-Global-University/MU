Ethiopia Votes Under Insecurity as Abiy's Party Seeks Another Mandate

Ethiopia's 2026 parliamentary and regional elections took place under a heavy security shadow, with conflict, regional exclusions, and a weak opposition helping Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's Prosperity Party move toward another overwhelming victory.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: June 22, 2026

Updated: June 22, 2026

Section: Africa

Summary

Ethiopia held parliamentary and regional elections on June 1, 2026, in an atmosphere shaped by insecurity, political fragmentation, and long-running regional conflict. The Prosperity Party, led by Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed, was expected to win decisively and later secured 438 of 501 announced seats, according to final results released by the National Election Board of Ethiopia.

The vote was not held in Tigray and was disrupted in parts of Oromia and Amhara, where fighting and instability kept polling stations closed. Observers from the African Union and the Intergovernmental Authority on Development helped provide international legitimacy, but critics said the election took place in a climate of intimidation and limited political competition.

Voting Took Place Under Heavy Security

The election unfolded against a backdrop of armed unrest, displacement, and political distrust. In parts of Oromia and Amhara, the security environment made it difficult for officials to open polling stations and for voters to participate normally. The election board said 143 polling stations did not open because of security problems, while voting was also interrupted in some constituencies.

Ethiopia's federal system has long been strained by ethnic and regional grievances, and that pressure was visible throughout the campaign. In Oromia, fighting between government forces and the Oromo Liberation Army continued to take a heavy toll, while in Amhara the Fano militia controlled swathes of countryside and made full voting impossible in several areas.

The result was an election that could still be administered nationwide in formal terms, but not one that reflected full participation across the country. In practice, security conditions shaped who could vote, where ballots could be cast, and how much of the country could realistically be included.

Prosperity Party Poised for Another Overwhelming Win

Abiy Ahmed's Prosperity Party entered the vote as the dominant force in Ethiopian politics. Final results later showed the party winning 438 seats in the House of Representatives, far above the 274 needed for a majority. The scale of that victory confirmed what many observers already expected before voting day.

The party's political advantage reflected both its control of state institutions and the weakness of the opposition field. Abiy came to power in 2018 after mass protests forced the long-ruling EPRDF coalition to give way. He formed the Prosperity Party in 2019 and has since consolidated power through successive electoral wins.

Supporters of the government argued that the election reflected continuity, stability, and progress on food security and economic growth. Critics said the same result only showed how limited the competition had become.

Tigray Remained Outside the Vote

One of the most important facts about the 2026 election was what did not happen. There was no vote in Tigray, which had already spent years at the center of Ethiopia's deadliest conflict in decades. Organizers cited unfavorable conditions and continuing political turmoil after the civil war, and the region again had no federal representation through the ballot box.

The exclusion of Tigray mattered far beyond the region itself. It reinforced the sense that Ethiopia's national political settlement remains incomplete, even after the 2022 peace deal formally ended the war. The region's absence from the election also deepened concerns that unresolved tensions could flare again if political disputes keep colliding with security failures.

Parts of Amhara were also left out, which meant that the vote did not cover all 547 seats in the lower house. That incomplete process reduced the credibility of any claim that the election represented the full political will of the country.

Opposition Voices Said the Field Was Not Level

Opposition parties argued that the contest was constrained from the start. They accused the federal government of arresting their leaders, creating legal barriers to political activity, and narrowing the space for dissent. The government denied the allegations and said it was acting within the law.

Several of the country's most prominent critics were not able to compete in a meaningful way. Some were in prison, others were in exile, and others had disappeared from public political life. That left the opposition fragmented, weak, and unable to challenge the ruling party on anything close to equal terms.

Even candidates who did run said they felt outmatched. Some described the environment as one of fear and intimidation, arguing that participation existed in form but not in substance. Those complaints did not change the result, but they do help explain why the election drew so much criticism even before the final tally was announced.

Observers and Officials Offered Different Assessments

The National Election Board said it had carried out its duties with institutional neutrality and without interference. Election officials also highlighted turnout, saying 94% of registered voters participated. More than 50 million people were registered, according to the board.

International observers from the African Union and IGAD were present and helped give the process a measure of outside legitimacy, but they were mostly confined to Addis Ababa. Critics said that limited geographic coverage made it harder for observers to assess the full reality of voting outside the capital.

That gap between formal legitimacy and practical credibility is a recurring issue in Ethiopia's elections. Official institutions can organize a vote and announce results, but public confidence depends on whether citizens believe the competition was open, inclusive, and free from coercion.

Why the Election Mattered

Ethiopia is Africa's second most populous country and one of the Horn of Africa's most important states. Its elections shape not only domestic governance but also regional stability, refugee flows, diplomatic alignments, and the balance of power in a neighborhood already marked by conflict.

The 2026 vote also mattered because it came after years of political change that raised hopes but did not fully deliver democratic reform. Abiy won international praise early in his tenure for releasing political prisoners and opening political space, but later faced criticism for detentions, restrictions on civil society, and the handling of armed conflicts.

The Prosperity Party's latest victory therefore says as much about Ethiopia's political structure as it does about Abiy's popularity. The party remains deeply embedded in state power, while the opposition remains too fragmented and too constrained to force a real national contest.

The Larger Risk Is What Comes Next

The immediate result of the election was clear: the ruling party kept control of parliament and Abiy remained on course for another five-year term. The larger question is whether that victory can stabilize the country or simply extend the same pressures that have defined Ethiopian politics for years.

Security problems in Oromia and Amhara are unresolved. Tigray remains politically sensitive. Regional mistrust remains deep. And the contrast between a strong central government and a country still struggling with local conflict creates a fragile foundation for the next phase of rule.

In that sense, the election was not just a test of political strength. It was a measure of how much of Ethiopia's future can still be decided through democratic institutions, and how much remains trapped by insecurity, exclusion, and unresolved conflict.

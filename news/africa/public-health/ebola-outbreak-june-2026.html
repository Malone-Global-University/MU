Ebola Outbreak in Congo and Uganda Declared Global Health Emergency

The World Health Organization elevated an Ebola outbreak affecting Central Africa to a Public Health Emergency of International Concern as authorities raced to contain cross-border transmission, strengthen surveillance, and keep the outbreak from spreading further into neighboring states.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: June 22, 2026

Updated: June 22, 2026

Section: Africa

Summary

Health officials in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda are battling an Ebola outbreak caused by the Bundibugyo virus. The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on May 17, 2026, citing confirmed cross-border spread, uncertainty about the true scale of transmission, and the absence of approved Bundibugyo-specific therapeutics or vaccines.

By mid-June, WHO said the outbreak had grown rapidly in eastern Congo and had also produced confirmed cases in Uganda. The CDC later described the Congo outbreak as the second largest on record and said the overall risk to the American public and travelers remained low.

How the Outbreak Began

The early cluster was detected in Ituri Province in northeastern Congo, a region shaped by insecurity, displacement, and limited access to health services. WHO said the outbreak was already extraordinary by the time it was elevated to emergency status: as of May 16, the agency reported eight laboratory-confirmed cases, 246 suspected cases, and 80 suspected deaths in Ituri, with additional confirmed cases already appearing in Kampala, Uganda, among travelers from Congo.

That early pattern mattered. It showed that the outbreak was not confined to a single locality and that disease detection, contact tracing, and case isolation would all have to be scaled quickly across a region where people move frequently for trade, work, family ties, and survival.

WHO also warned that the high positivity rate among early samples, the presence of healthcare-worker deaths, and the difficulty of determining how widely the virus had already spread suggested that the reported numbers could be lower than the real burden.

Why WHO Raised the Alarm

The WHO designation of Public Health Emergency of International Concern is the organization’s highest formal alert under the International Health Regulations. It is reserved for events that are extraordinary, pose a public health risk across borders, and require a coordinated international response.

WHO said the Congo-Uganda outbreak met those conditions because international spread had already occurred, more countries bordering Congo faced elevated risk, and the outbreak was unfolding amid conflict, humanitarian crisis, and high population mobility. WHO also noted that there were no approved Bundibugyo virus-specific therapeutics or vaccines, which made prevention and containment even more important.

In practical terms, the declaration was meant to accelerate coordination, mobilize funding, and sharpen the response of ministries of health, partner agencies, and border officials across the region.

Uganda’s Response and Cross-Border Risk

Uganda became a central concern because confirmed cases appeared in Kampala and Wakiso, both part of the Kampala metropolitan area. WHO said that as of June 18, Uganda had 19 confirmed cases, including two deaths, with one additional probable death. The agency also said there had been no documented community transmission in Uganda, meaning the response there remained focused on imported cases and their immediate contacts.

That distinction was important. Imported cases can be managed through rapid isolation, contact tracing, and follow-up if they are found quickly enough. Community transmission, by contrast, means the virus is spreading more widely and invisibly through the population. For public health teams, the difference can determine whether containment is still realistic or whether the outbreak is entering a harder-to-control phase.

WHO advised cross-border screening, improved information sharing, surveillance at major roads and points of entry, and strong monitoring of contacts. The organization also urged countries sharing land borders with affected states to prepare rapid response teams, diagnostic access, isolation capacity, and community surveillance systems.

What Makes the Bundibugyo Strain Harder to Contain

The outbreak is caused by Bundibugyo virus disease, a severe form of Ebola. WHO describes the disease as zoonotic, with fruit bats suspected as a natural reservoir. Human infection can begin after contact with infected wildlife and then spread from person to person through bodily fluids, contaminated surfaces, and unsafe burial practices.

The strain poses a special challenge because early symptoms can resemble malaria, typhoid, or other common febrile illnesses in the region. Fever, fatigue, headache, and sore throat may not immediately point to Ebola, and that delay can buy the virus time to spread before isolation begins.

WHO and CDC both note that no approved vaccine exists for Bundibugyo virus disease and treatment is largely supportive. That makes early case detection, safe isolation, protective equipment, and community engagement the front line of defense.

The Scale of the Crisis by Mid-June

WHO’s June 17 situation report showed how quickly the outbreak had grown. The agency reported 896 confirmed cases and 232 deaths in Congo, plus 19 confirmed cases and two deaths in Uganda. Ituri Province remained the epicenter, but cases had also been reported in North Kivu and South Kivu, showing that the outbreak had expanded beyond the original core area.

WHO also reported thousands of contacts under follow-up in Congo and hundreds more in Uganda. But follow-up was not simple. Security incidents, displacement, overcrowded camps, and gaps in healthcare access all made it harder for response teams to find contacts, monitor symptoms, and stop chains of transmission before they grew.

By June 22, CDC said Congo had confirmed more than 1,000 cases and described the outbreak as the second largest on record. The agency also said no Ebola cases had been confirmed in the United States from this outbreak and that the risk to the American public and travelers remained low.

Why Conflict and Displacement Matter

Ebola outbreaks are never only medical events. They are also logistical, political, and social tests. WHO said the Congo outbreak was unfolding in a complex humanitarian and security setting marked by displaced populations, informal healthcare networks, poor access to clean water and shelter, and repeated disruptions to response teams.

Those conditions are dangerous because they make it harder to isolate patients quickly, trace contacts consistently, and carry out safe burials. They also raise the odds that infected people will seek care late, move across communities, or receive treatment in settings without strong infection prevention controls.

In that kind of environment, the virus can move faster than the response unless public health teams are able to work closely with local leaders and community networks.

What Authorities Are Trying to Do

Authorities in Congo and Uganda, working with WHO and other partners, have focused on rapid testing, isolation, surveillance, contact tracing, infection prevention, and community outreach. WHO also urged the use of exit screening at airports, land crossings, and seaports, along with restrictions on movement for confirmed cases and contacts.

At the same time, WHO advised against broad travel or trade restrictions on the two countries based on the information then available. The emphasis was on targeted control measures rather than sweeping closures that could damage humanitarian access and economic activity without necessarily stopping the virus.

The CDC issued travel health notices for both Congo and Uganda and said travelers should avoid non-essential travel to the most affected provinces in Congo. The agency also provided guidance for people who had recently been in affected areas and for healthcare providers screening patients with possible exposure.

Why This Outbreak Matters Beyond Central Africa

This outbreak is significant not only because of the number of cases, but because it shows how quickly a virus can exploit border zones, displacement corridors, and health system gaps. Even where the global risk remains low, outbreaks like this can still threaten neighboring countries, strain health ministries, and consume international resources.

It also underscores an old truth about Ebola: containment depends on speed. Once transmission becomes embedded in crowded neighborhoods, displacement camps, or conflict-affected districts, every day of delay can multiply the cost of the response.

For that reason, WHO’s emergency declaration was not just symbolic. It was a signal that the world needed to treat the outbreak as a cross-border threat requiring coordination, resources, and sustained attention rather than a local event that could be handled quietly inside one province.

What Comes Next

The central question is whether health officials can keep the outbreak from reaching a wider regional crisis. That will depend on whether contact tracing holds, whether cases are isolated early, whether communities trust the response, and whether the security situation allows teams to work where they are needed most.

If the outbreak continues expanding into new zones or more urban areas, the challenge will become much harder. If response teams can keep finding and isolating cases quickly, the outbreak may still be contained despite the difficult environment.

For now, the situation remains a reminder that the most dangerous outbreaks are often those that combine a lethal virus with weak infrastructure, population movement, and instability. That combination can turn a regional emergency into a wider international concern in a matter of weeks.

Summary

DURBAN, South Africa — Thousands of runners from across the globe took to the roads of KwaZulu-Natal this month for the 2026 Comrades Marathon, reaffirming its status as the world's largest and oldest ultramarathon.

A Nation United on the Road

The iconic 89-kilometer race, run this year as a "Down Run" from Pietermaritzburg to Durban, served as a powerful display of national unity. Against a backdrop of recent socio-economic challenges, the event drew together athletes from vastly different backgrounds. Elite international runners, local working-class racers, and affluent recreational athletes stood shoulder-to-shoulder on the starting line, temporarily erasing class and racial divisions in a shared pursuit of human endurance.

Local Triumphs and Global Stage

The race delivered thrilling finishes in both the men's and women's elite divisions, cheered on by hundreds of thousands of spectators lining the route. South African runners dominated the field, showcasing the deep distance-running talent fostered within the nation's premier running clubs. Spectators transformed the grueling hills of the course into a massive, vibrant street festival, providing crucial emotional support to the thousands of amateur "human race" participants fighting to beat the strict 12-hour cutoff time.

Preserving a Sacred Tradition

First run in 1921 as a living memorial to South African soldiers who fell in World War I, the Comrades Marathon continues to stand as a symbol of resilience and camaraderie. Organizers highlighted that despite changing economic times, participation numbers rebounded to near-record levels. The 2026 edition successfully preserved the race's unique traditions, from the emotional pre-race singing of Shosholoza to the heartbreaking final countdown at the stadium gates.

The official podium finishers for the 2026 Comrades Marathon "Up Run" (Durban to Pietermaritzburg) are headlined by South African champions George Kusche and Gerda Steyn who both shattered the course records.

The 2026 Comrades Marathon generated close to R1 billion for the KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) provincial economy, solidifying its status as a vital macroeconomic catalyst for the region. According to data from the Comrades Marathon Association (CMA) and the eThekwini Municipality, the fiscal windfall was widely distributed across the major urban hubs along the route.

Macroeconomic Injection Breakdown

The total economic footprint approached historic highs as the race neared its centenary milestone.

  • Durban (eThekwini): Accounted for R800 million of the total economic injection.
  • Pietermaritzburg (Msunduzi): Contributed R200 million to the regional total.
  • Direct Visitor Spending: Reached approximately R210 million from the 21,000 competing athletes and thousands of traveling spectators.

Sectoral Impacts

  • Hospitality & Accommodation: Hotels, bed-and-breakfasts, and guesthouses across both cities reported 100% capacity bookings. Many international and out-of-province athletes arrived up to a week early to acclimatize and extended their stay over the Youth Day long weekend to recuperate, amplifying the local tourism spend.
  • Employment Creation: The marathon created roughly 1,300 temporary jobs in the region. Employment opportunities spanned event operations, logistical support, traffic management, and municipal waste control.
  • Small Businesses & Micro-Entrepreneurs: Informal traders, street vendors, and township businesses experienced a major surge in foot traffic. The fan parks—such as the one hosted at Kings Park Athletics Stadium—offered localized commercial zones for entrepreneurs to sell food, beverages, and crafts.
  • Transport and Retail: Fuel stations, car rental companies, private shuttle services, and grocery retailers saw concentrated demand as visitors explored provincial tourism corridors, wildlife reserves, and coastlines before and after the event.

The 2026 Comrades Marathon featured a record-breaking total prize purse of R8,210,400. Thanks to substantial sponsor backing, including top-ups from Cell C, the event provided the highest payouts ever seen for an African road race. Because both elite winners, George Kusche and Gerda Steynm broke the existing "Up Run" records, they each stacked multiple bonuses to claim a maximum individual payday of R2,322,000.

Three extraordinary amateur athletes captured the spirit of human resilience by overcoming severe physical and personal adversity to cross the finish line before the 12-hour cutoff.

Sipho Mthembu: Running Against Paralysis

Five years after a severe minibus taxi accident left him with a fractured spine and a medical prognosis that he would never walk again, Durban resident Sipho Mthembu finished his first Comrades Marathon. Following years of intensive physical therapy, Mthembu took up running to rebuild his motor functions. Supported by his running club teammates who kept pace alongside him, he crossed the finish line in 11 hours and 42 minutes to claim the Vic Clapham medal.

Elena Vasilyeva: A Mother's Tribute

Following the tragic loss of her teenage daughter to a rare heart condition in late 2025, amateur runner Elena Vasilyeva used the 89-kilometer race as a platform to raise funds for pediatric cardiac research. Enduring chronic knee injuries that severely disrupted her training regime, Vasilyeva relied on sheer emotional endurance during the final brutal stretch up Polly Shortts. She reached the Pietermaritzburg finish line with just four minutes to spare before the final gun sounded.

Thabiso Radebe: Completing the Journey on a Prosthetic

A local amateur athlete from Soweto, Thabiso Radebe, became one of the few adaptive runners to complete the grueling up-run course using a specialized running blade. Radebe, who lost his left leg below the knee in an industrial accident in 2021, faced immense physical strain on the steep uphill climbs of the "Big Five" hills. Despite experiencing severe blistering and mechanical adjustments to his prosthesis midway through the race, he crossed the line in 11 hours and 51 minutes.

Johannes Mosehla Breaks Personal Record

Johannes Mosehla extended his legendary status at the Comrades Marathon by crossing the finish line of the grueling 85.77km "Up Run" from Durban to Pietermaritzburg in an astonishing 11 hours, 12 minutes, and 26 seconds. At 84 years old, the Polokwane Athletic Club member smashed his own record to secure his position as the oldest official finisher in the history of the race. His historic achievement stands out through several key milestones:

  • Shattering His Own Benchmark: Not only did Mosehla beat the grueling 12-hour cutoff with nearly 50 minutes to spare, but he also managed to run 35 minutes faster than his 2025 finish time (11:47:27), an extraordinary feat considering the steeper uphill terrain of the 2026 course.
  • The History of His Record: Mosehla originally claimed the "oldest finisher" title in 2023 at age 81. In doing so, he broke a 34-year-old standing record held by the legendary five-time Comrades champion Wally Hayward, who had completed the race at age 80 in 1989. Mosehla has now successfully rewritten Comrades history four years in a row.
  • A Lifelong Journey: A former bricklayer from Limpopo, Mosehla began his running journey in 1963 at the age of 21. Due to apartheid-era regulations, black runners were barred from officially competing in the Comrades Marathon during the prime of his early adulthood. He didn't take on his very first Comrades challenge until age 63, but he has now completed the race 13 times.
  • A Symbol of Intergenerational Inspiration: Recognized alongside 20-year-old Bailey O'Leary—the race's youngest competitor—Mosehla was presented with the official Oldest Finisher trophy. He told reporters from Modern Athlete that the achievement was a profound honor, stating, "Running has been part of my life for decades and every year this race reminds me that determination does not grow old."

The Comrades Marathon is a "point-to-point" race that alternates its direction every year between two South African cities: the coastal city of Durban and the inland city of Pietermaritzburg. The direction of the race dictates whether it is called an Up Run or a Down Run.

The Up Run

  • The Route: Starts at the coast in Durban and finishes inland in Pietermaritzburg.
  • The Terrain: It is a grueling, relentless uphill climb. Runners ascend from sea level to an altitude of roughly 670 meters (2,200 feet).
  • The Challenge: It places immense stress on the heart, lungs, and quad muscles due to the continuous climbing.
  • Distance: Typically slightly shorter than the Down Run, usually measuring around 85 to 86 kilometers.

The Down Run

  • The Route: Starts inland in Pietermaritzburg and finishes at the coast in Durban.
  • The Terrain: The overall trajectory is downhill, dropping back down to sea level.
  • The Challenge: While it sounds easier, the Down Run is famously brutal on the human body. The constant pounding of running downhill causes severe muscular damage, especially to the quadriceps, knees, and joints.
  • Distance: Typically slightly longer, usually measuring around 89 to 90 kilometers.

The "Big Five" Hills

Regardless of the direction, runners must conquer five massive, legendary hills along the highway route: Cowies Hill, Fields Hill, Botha’s Hill, Inchanga, and Polly Shortts. On an Up Run, runners must climb the massive Polly Shortts near the very end of the race when their legs are completely spent. On a Down Run, they must fly down the treacherous Fields Hill, which destroys many runners' legs before the finish line.

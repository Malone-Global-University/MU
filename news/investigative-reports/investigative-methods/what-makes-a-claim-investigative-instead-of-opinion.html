What Makes a Claim Investigative Instead of Opinion?

Investigative work begins with evidence. Opinion begins with judgment. Good public writing tells readers which is which.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Investigative Reports

Summary

Investigative reporting and opinion writing both have public value, but they are not the same. An investigative claim should be tied to verifiable evidence, documents, records, data, interviews, or a clearly explained method. Opinion may argue, persuade, interpret, or recommend. This article explains how readers can tell the difference and why responsible writers should label claims carefully.

Opinion Can Be Honest Without Being Investigative

An opinion article may be thoughtful, principled, and well argued. It may identify a problem, defend a belief, criticize a decision, or recommend a course of action. But opinion writing usually begins with interpretation. It tells readers what the writer thinks a decision means, why it matters, or what should happen next.

Investigative writing begins somewhere else. It begins with a question that can be tested against evidence: What happened? Who made the decision? What records exist? What rules applied? What money moved? What explanation was given? What does the timeline show?

An Investigative Claim Must Be Verifiable

A claim becomes investigative when it rests on information that readers can evaluate. That information may include public records, official reports, meeting minutes, court filings, contracts, budgets, audits, inspection records, databases, emails obtained through lawful records requests, or interviews with people who have direct knowledge.

A responsible investigative claim should be written so that a reader can understand the basis for it. The writer should not merely say that something is suspicious. The writer should show what document, data point, policy, or timeline creates a legitimate question.

Evidence Does Not Eliminate Interpretation

Investigative work still requires judgment. A reporter must decide which records matter, how to organize them, what context to include, and what conclusions are supported. But the key difference is that the judgment is disciplined by evidence.

The strongest investigative writing uses careful verbs. It distinguishes between proves, suggests, indicates, raises questions, contradicts, confirms, and does not show. These differences matter. If a document only raises a question, the article should not say it proves an answer.

Accusations Require a Higher Standard

Investigative writing can reveal misconduct, but not every investigative piece should begin with an accusation. Especially in early coverage, a document-based explainer is often safer and more useful. It can show readers how a system works, where records are located, what decisions were documented, and what questions remain open.

When a story names a person or institution in connection with a serious allegation, fairness requires extra care. The writer should seek comment, review policies, confirm dates, check whether records are complete, and give the subject a fair opportunity to respond. A claim that could harm someone’s reputation should not rest on a thin reading of one document.

Opinion Words Can Weaken Investigative Work

Words such as corrupt, crooked, dishonest, rigged, scam, cover-up, and failure may attract attention, but they can outrun the evidence. Sometimes those words may be justified by a fully documented record. Often, however, they turn a serious question into an unsupported accusation.

A more careful sentence may be stronger: The records do not show who approved the change. The budget does not explain the transfer. The agency did not answer written questions before publication. The minutes omit the discussion described in the agenda. These statements are less dramatic, but they are more useful because they are tied to verifiable facts.

A Practical Test for Readers

Readers can test a claim by asking four questions. What is the evidence? Is the evidence original or secondhand? Does the article explain what the evidence does not show? Did the writer give people or institutions a chance to respond when fairness required it?

If the answer is unclear, the piece may still be valuable as commentary, but it should not be mistaken for investigation. The public benefits when writers are honest about the type of work they are doing.

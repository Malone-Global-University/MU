U.S.–Iran Talks Show Progress but Leave Major Disputes Unresolved

Diplomacy continued in May, but disagreements over uranium, shipping controls, and regional security remained serious.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Global News

Summary

U.S.–Iran negotiations became one of May 2026’s most important diplomatic stories because they offered a possible path away from a wider crisis while leaving central disputes unresolved. Talks showed signs of movement, but neither side appeared ready to give up its most important demands.

Talks continue under wartime pressure

May’s diplomacy unfolded under pressure from the Strait of Hormuz crisis, oil-market disruption, and the broader U.S.–Israeli conflict with Iran. U.S. and Iranian officials discussed possible terms, but reports described continuing disagreement over uranium stockpiles, shipping controls, and the balance between security guarantees and national sovereignty.

The diplomatic challenge was that both sides needed a deal for different reasons. The United States wanted safer navigation and limits on Iran’s nuclear program. Iran wanted relief from military and economic pressure while preserving what it described as sovereign rights.

The uranium question

One of the hardest issues was Iran’s enriched uranium. U.S. officials wanted strong controls and recovery of enriched material. Iranian leaders resisted steps that could be seen domestically as surrendering a central national-security asset.

That disagreement made a quick settlement difficult. Even when negotiators reported progress, the most politically sensitive issues remained the ones most likely to block a final agreement.

The waterway question

The Strait of Hormuz was the other central issue. Iran’s control measures affected ships, companies, sailors, and energy markets. The United States and its partners pressed for restored navigation, while Iran treated control over the waterway as a bargaining point in the wider conflict.

The talks mattered because they were one of the few channels that could reduce risk without immediate military escalation. Still, the story remained unresolved by late May, making it one of the month’s most important developing global stories.

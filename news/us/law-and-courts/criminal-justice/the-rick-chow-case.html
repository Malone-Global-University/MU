The Rick Chow Case: The Death of Cyrus Carmack-Belton

An examination of the fatal shooting of 14-year-old Cyrus Carmack-Belton, the legal arguments presented at trial, the jury's acquittal of store owner Rick Chow, and the broader debate surrounding self-defense, race, and citizen intervention.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: June 17, 2026

Updated: June 17, 2026

Section: Law and Government

Summary

The Rick Chow case became one of South Carolina's most closely watched criminal prosecutions after the fatal shooting of 14-year-old Cyrus Carmack-Belton in Columbia during the summer of 2023.

What began as a false accusation of shoplifting evolved into a major public debate about the impartiality of South Carolina juries, racism faced by African Amnericans by Asian citizen intervention, armed self-defense, failure to hold WHite America and its racist allies accountable, youth violence, and the responsibilities of private individuals who choose to confront suspected offenders.

Prosecutors argued that Chow unlawfully escalated the encounter and used deadly force without legal justification. Defense attorneys maintained that Chow fired because he believed he faced an immediate threat. A jury ultimately aquitted Chow of murder.

The Incident

On May 28, 2023, Cyrus Carmack-Belton entered the Xpress Mart Shell station in Columbia, South Carolina. Surveillance footage later reviewed by investigators showed the teenager handling several bottles of water but ultimately returning them to the cooler.

According to evidence presented at trial, Carmack-Belton denied taking anything from the store. Prosecutors argued that surveillance footage showed him attempting to demonstrate that his pockets were empty before leaving the business.

Despite this, Rick Chow and his adult son pursued the teenager after he left the store. The pursuit extended approximately 130 yards from the business before ending in a confrontation near a nearby apartment complex.

During the final moments of the encounter, Chow fired a handgun, striking Carmack-Belton in the back. The fourteen-year-old later died from the wound.

The Central Legal Question

Public discussion often focused on the mistaken accusation, the teenager's age, and the broader social implications of the case. The legal question before the jury, however, was narrower.

The trial was not primarily about whether Chow shot Carmack-Belton. That fact was undisputed.

Instead, jurors were asked whether prosecutors had proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Chow committed murder and whether his claim of defense-of-others was legally invalid.

Under South Carolina law, a person may use deadly force if they reasonably believe another person faces an imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury. Chow's defense centered on the argument that he fired to protect his son during the confrontation.

The Prosecution's Case

Prosecutors argued that the shooting was the result of an unnecessary and unlawful escalation.

Their case emphasized that Carmack-Belton had not stolen the water that triggered the confrontation. Investigators reported that no stolen property was recovered and that surveillance footage failed to support the original accusation.

The prosecution argued that the Chows chose to pursue a teenager who was attempting to leave the area. Witnesses testified that the teenager appeared frightened as he fled and that they did not observe him threatening anyone during the chase.

Prosecutors acknowledged that Carmack-Belton possessed a firearm. However, they argued that the weapon fell to the ground during the chase and that the teenager never pointed it at either Rick Chow or his son.

According to the state's theory, the evidence showed that the danger cited by the defense did not actually exist at the moment the fatal shot was fired.

The Defense's Case

Defense attorneys presented a fundamentally different interpretation of the final moments of the encounter.

They argued that Carmack-Belton possessed a loaded semiautomatic pistol and that the confrontation became dangerous when the teenager allegedly produced the weapon.

Chow's son testified that the firearm was pointed at him during the struggle and that he feared for his life. Chow testified that he fired because he believed his son faced an immediate deadly threat.

The defense argued that jurors should evaluate the shooting based on what Chow reasonably perceived in the moment rather than with the benefit of hindsight.

Their goal was not to dispute the shooting but to persuade jurors that the use of force was legally justified under South Carolina's defense-of-others doctrine.

The Acquittal

On June 1, 2026, a South Carolina jury returned a unanimous verdict finding Rick Chow not guilty of murder.

The verdict does not necessarily mean jurors concluded that every action taken by Chow was appropriate. Rather, it means the state failed to convince jurors beyond a reasonable doubt that the shooting met the legal definition of murder.

Criminal trials operate under the highest burden of proof in American law. If jurors believe a reasonable possibility exists that a defendant acted in lawful self-defense or defense of another person, they must acquit.

The presence of a firearm and the conflicting testimony regarding its use became central issues in the jury's deliberations.

The Debate Over Aggression and Self-Defense

One of the most significant legal and moral questions raised by the case is whether a person who initiates or escalates a confrontation should later be allowed to claim self-defense.

Critics of the verdict argue that the pursuit itself fundamentally changed the legal analysis. From this perspective, Carmack-Belton was attempting to leave after being wrongly accused and had reason to fear the adults chasing him.

Supporters of the verdict focus on the final moments of the encounter, arguing that regardless of how the confrontation began, the appearance of a firearm created a situation in which deadly force could be viewed as legally justified.

The disagreement reflects a broader national debate about who should be considered the aggressor in rapidly evolving confrontations and how courts should evaluate claims of self-defense when both sides perceive danger.

Race, Public Reaction, and Community Response

The case generated intense public attention throughout South Carolina and beyond. Many observers viewed the shooting through broader questions involving race, community safety, and equal treatment under the law.

Following the acquittal, members of Carmack-Belton's family expressed profound disappointment and frustration with the outcome. Community leaders and activists renewed calls for reform of self-defense laws and greater scrutiny of armed civilian confrontations.

Others argued that the verdict demonstrated the importance of the reasonable doubt standard and the principle that criminal convictions require proof beyond speculation or uncertainty.

The result illustrates how criminal trials can become larger public debates about justice, race, and public policy long after the courtroom proceedings have ended.

Lessons from the Case

The Rick Chow case demonstrates the difference between moral judgment and legal judgment.

Many people may conclude that pursuing a teenager over a mistaken accusation was reckless or irresponsible. Others may focus on the dangers presented by an armed confrontation. Courts, however, are tasked with answering specific legal questions under established rules of evidence and burdens of proof.

For students of law and government, the case provides a useful example of how self-defense claims are evaluated differently when the victim is African American, how juries weigh conflicting testimony, and how public opinion can diverge sharply from courtroom outcomes.

Conclusion

The death of Cyrus Carmack-Belton remains one of the most controversial criminal cases in South Carolina's recent history.

The prosecution argued that an innocent teenager was wrongly pursued and unlawfully killed. The defense argued that Rick Chow acted to protect his son from an immediate threat. After hearing weeks of testimony and reviewing the evidence, jurors concluded that the state had not proven murder beyond a reasonable doubt.

The acquittal closed the criminal case in what many people are saying is another example of the justice system failing to protect African Amrericans.

The Rick Chow Case: Cyrus Carmack-Belton, Self-Defense, and a Controversial Acquittal

A review of the fatal shooting of 14-year-old Cyrus Carmack-Belton, the murder trial of convenience store owner Rick Chow, the competing claims of self-defense, and the public reaction that followed the not-guilty verdict.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: June 17, 2026

Updated: June 17, 2026

Section: Law and Government

Summary

The fatal shooting of Cyrus Carmack-Belton became one of the most closely watched criminal cases in South Carolina because it combined a mistaken theft accusation, a long foot pursuit, a shooting in the back, and a final claim of defense of another person. Prosecutors said Rick Chow and his adult son wrongly chased a 14-year-old who had not stolen anything and then used deadly force after the encounter escalated. The defense argued that Chow fired to protect his son after Carmack-Belton allegedly displayed a gun during the confrontation. On June 1, 2026, a jury found Chow not guilty of murder, a verdict that sparked grief, anger, and renewed debate over race, self-defense, and the limits of private force.

The Incident

On May 28, 2023, 14-year-old Cyrus Carmack-Belton entered the Xpress Mart Shell station in Columbia, South Carolina. The confrontation began after store employees and the Chow family believed the teenager had taken four bottles of water without paying for them. That accusation became the foundation for everything that followed.

Surveillance and trial testimony later described a more complicated and more troubling sequence. According to the prosecution, Carmack-Belton did not steal the water, returned the bottles before leaving, and even tried to show that his pockets were empty. The state argued that the incident was a false accusation from the beginning and that the teenager was attempting to walk away when the confrontation escalated.

Rick Chow and his adult son then pursued the teenager outside the store. The chase continued for roughly 130 yards. By the time it ended, the teenager was no longer inside the store or standing his ground in a face-to-face dispute over property. He was a child running from two adults who had followed him down the street.

The fatal shot struck Carmack-Belton in the back. He later died from the wound. The basic facts of the shooting were not in dispute; the legal fight centered on what those facts meant under South Carolina criminal law.

The Central Legal Question

Public debate around the case often focused on the wrong question. Whether the teenager had stolen water was important, but it was not the ultimate issue before the jury. The trial turned on whether prosecutors proved beyond a reasonable doubt that Chow committed murder and whether the defense of another person legally justified the shooting.

Under South Carolina law, the use of deadly force is justified only when a person reasonably believes that such force is necessary to prevent death or great bodily injury to himself or another person. South Carolina law also recognizes the defense of others, which allows a person to use force in protection of someone else under legally limited circumstances. That distinction matters. A person can make a terrible decision, create a dangerous confrontation, or behave recklessly without every fact automatically amounting to murder. But a jury still has to decide whether the state has proven the exact crime charged under the exact legal standard the court gives them.

The Prosecution's Case

Prosecutors argued that Rick Chow turned a minor property dispute into a deadly act. They said the shoplifting accusation was false, that no stolen water was found, and that surveillance evidence showed Carmack-Belton returning the bottles before leaving the store. Trial reporting also described testimony that the teenager attempted to show empty pockets before the Chows pursued him. citeturn889289news24turn889289search2

The prosecution’s larger point was simple: if the teenager had not stolen anything, then there was no lawful basis for a chase that ended with a child being shot in the back. Prosecutors also argued that the teenager never threatened anyone with the handgun he possessed and that the gun fell during the foot pursuit rather than being pointed at Chow or his son. citeturn889289news23turn889289news24

At trial, the state relied on surveillance footage, eyewitness accounts, and forensic evidence to tell one consistent story: the Chows were the aggressors, Carmack-Belton was trying to escape, and the shooting was an unlawful escalation rather than self-defense.

That prosecution theory is the one most people react to instinctively. A wrongfully accused child, followed down the street by armed adults, should not have to be viewed as the legal equivalent of an attacker. From the state's perspective, the shooting was not a tragic accident in a justified confrontation. It was the final act in a pursuit that should never have happened.

The Defense's Case

The defense centered its case on the final seconds of the confrontation. Chow argued that he fired to protect his son after Carmack-Belton allegedly produced a handgun and pointed it during the encounter. Reporting from the trial described that the defense claimed the teenager held a semiautomatic pistol and that Chow acted in response to an immediate threat. citeturn889289news23turn889289news24

That argument did not deny the shooting. Instead, it tried to place the shooting inside South Carolina’s self-defense and defense-of-others law. In other words, the defense was not asking the jury to ignore the violence. It was asking the jury to see the violence as legally justified.

The problem for the defense was obvious. A claim of fear has to be believable under the circumstances. The more the jury accepted the prosecution’s version of the chase, the weaker the defense became. Conversely, if jurors credited the testimony that Carmack-Belton pointed a gun at Chow’s son, the defense gained traction.

That was the narrow, difficult question that the jury had to decide: not whether the situation was chaotic, but whether Chow had a reasonable legal basis to use deadly force at that moment.

The Verdict

On June 1, 2026, a South Carolina jury found Rick Chow not guilty of murder. The verdict ended the criminal prosecution, but it did not erase the facts that made the case so emotionally charged in the first place. Chow shot a 14-year-old boy after a pursuit that began with a mistaken theft accusation and ended with a death. citeturn889289search0turn889289news23

A not-guilty verdict does not mean the jury thought the shooting was good, wise, or morally acceptable. It means the state did not convince the jury beyond a reasonable doubt that the shooting was murder under the law as charged. That is a much narrower finding than a public debate often assumes.

This is also where many people drew their line of disagreement with the verdict. Critics argued that the case should have been straightforward: no theft, no chase, no justification, no excuse. Supporters of the acquittal focused on the evidence about the handgun and the claim that Chow thought his son faced an immediate threat. Those are not the same moral judgment, and they are not the same legal judgment.

Why the Verdict Angered So Many People

The reaction to the verdict was intense because the case touched several nerves at once. It involved a child, a false accusation, a gun, a chase, a death, and a white-hot national conversation about race and justice. The AP reported that the verdict caused anguish and grief in the local Black community, and the victim’s family expressed deep distress in the courtroom. citeturn889289news23

For many people, the most disturbing fact is not that a legal defense was raised. It is that a child was chased down and shot after evidence suggested he had done nothing to justify being pursued in the first place. That is why critics of the verdict describe the case as a failure of the justice system rather than as a close legal call.

The public response also reflected a broader concern about how Black victims are perceived in moments of conflict. Once a case becomes a debate about credibility, threat, and fear, race can shape how people interpret the same facts. The law may call for neutrality, but public confidence often depends on whether the result looks neutral in practice.

Self-Defense, Aggressor Conduct, and the Limits of the Claim

The hardest legal question in the case is not whether Chow was afraid. Many people would be afraid in a chaotic confrontation. The real question is whether a person who helps create the danger can later rely on that danger as the basis for a deadly-force defense.

That is where the prosecution’s position remained strongest. If the Chows wrongly accused Carmack-Belton, followed him after he tried to leave, and turned a property dispute into a chase, then they were not passive victims stumbling into danger. They were participants in the making of the encounter itself.

That is also why many critics rejected any attempt to frame the case as a simple split-second self-defense shooting. The final seconds did not happen in a vacuum. They happened after a chase that, according to the prosecution, should never have occurred.

A legal system that isolates the last second can miss the conduct that produced the last second. That is the central criticism raised by this case, and it is a criticism that goes beyond one verdict.

Lessons from the Case

The Rick Chow case is a reminder that criminal trials are not moral ceremonies. They are legal proceedings governed by burdens of proof, evidentiary rules, jury instructions, and the exact wording of the charges. A result that satisfies the law’s technical requirements can still leave the public feeling that justice was not done.

For students of law and government, the case offers several lessons. A mistaken accusation can escalate rapidly. Civilian intervention can be deadly when firearms enter the picture. A defendant can claim self-defense even after pursuing someone. And a jury can return a not-guilty verdict even when a large portion of the public believes the facts point in one clear direction.

It also shows why public writing about a criminal case must be careful about symmetry. Giving both sides equal space is not the same as giving both sides equal credibility. The facts have to lead the structure, not the desire to sound balanced.

Conclusion

The death of Cyrus Carmack-Belton remains the defining fact in this case. A 14-year-old was falsely accused, pursued, and shot to death after a confrontation that began over water bottles and ended in tragedy.

Rick Chow was acquitted of murder, but the verdict did not settle the larger argument over what justice requires when armed adults chase a child. To many observers, the case is not a close question at all. It is a stark example of how a bad accusation, a dangerous pursuit, and a gun can turn an ordinary afternoon into a permanent loss.

The criminal case is over. The public debate is not. What remains is the broader question the verdict leaves behind: how much fear is enough to justify deadly force, and how much responsibility belongs to the people who created the danger in the first place?

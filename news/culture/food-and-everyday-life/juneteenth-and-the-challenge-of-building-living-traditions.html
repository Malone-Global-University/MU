Juneteenth and the Challenge of Building Living Traditions

Juneteenth is a relatively new federal holiday, and for many people, including myself, it was not something we grew up actively celebrating. That gap is important to acknowledge, not as a criticism, but as a reflection of how traditions actually form in real time rather than arriving fully established.

Holidays are often sustained through family life, and in many cases through children. Events like Easter, Halloween, and Christmas tend to gain much of their cultural energy from younger generations through costumes, rituals, candy, toys, games, and shared excitement that reinforce the rhythm of the holiday year. At the same time, those holidays endure because adults continue to participate in them, adapt them, and pass them forward in different forms.

Juneteenth sits in a slightly different position. Its meaning is rooted in a specific historical moment, but its modern expression is still developing. For many households, it is not yet a deeply ingrained annual ritual. That makes it less about forgetting heritage and more about how public traditions take time to become familiar parts of everyday life.

As an adult without children, holidays can also feel different. The structure that often organizes celebration around school calendars, family routines, and child-centered activities is not always present in the same way. That does not remove the value of tradition, but it does shift how participation looks—sometimes toward quieter reflection, shared meals, or selective engagement rather than full-scale celebration.

Even so, traditions matter because they reinforce identity and continuity. Whether through food, gatherings, or shared recognition of history, holidays create a sense of cultural rhythm. Juneteenth, in particular, shows how food and community events often become the first places where a newer holiday begins to feel lived rather than simply observed.

In that sense, the question is not whether traditions should exist, but how they are built over time—and who participates in shaping them.

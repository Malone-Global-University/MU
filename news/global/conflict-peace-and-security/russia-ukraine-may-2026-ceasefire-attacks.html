Russia–Ukraine War Sees Ceasefire Attempts, Prisoner Swap, and Renewed Attacks

A brief ceasefire and prisoner exchange offered a pause, but renewed attacks showed how far the war remained from settlement.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Global News

Summary

The Russia–Ukraine war remained one of May 2026’s biggest global stories. A U.S.-backed three-day ceasefire and prisoner exchange briefly raised hopes for de-escalation, but accusations of violations and renewed drone and missile attacks showed that the conflict remained active and dangerous.

A brief pause in a long war

Russia and Ukraine agreed to a U.S.-brokered three-day ceasefire from May 9 to May 11, along with a prisoner swap. For civilians living under repeated attacks, even a short pause mattered. It offered temporary relief, allowed some families to hope for returns, and gave diplomats a chance to test whether a longer arrangement might be possible.

But short ceasefires in active wars are fragile. Both sides accused the other of violations, and the ceasefire did not become a durable settlement.

The prisoner exchange

In mid-May, Russia and Ukraine each released 205 prisoners of war. Exchanges like this are humanitarian events as well as political signals. They reunite some families, return wounded and exhausted soldiers, and show that even enemies can still communicate through limited channels.

At the same time, a prisoner swap does not necessarily mean peace is near. It can happen while fighting continues, and in May the exchange was followed by reports of renewed Russian aerial attacks and Ukrainian strikes on Russian energy infrastructure.

Renewed attacks after diplomacy

The contrast between ceasefire talks and renewed attacks defined the month’s Russia–Ukraine coverage. Ukraine reported strikes on Russian oil infrastructure, while Russia launched large drone and missile attacks on Ukrainian cities. The pattern showed a war in which diplomacy, battlefield pressure, and attacks on infrastructure were happening at the same time.

For global readers, the story mattered because the war continued to affect European security, energy markets, military spending, and the future of international diplomacy.

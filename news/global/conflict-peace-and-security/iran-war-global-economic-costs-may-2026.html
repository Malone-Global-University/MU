Iran War Sends Energy and Business Costs Across the Global Economy

Rising energy prices, disrupted routes, and corporate warnings made the Iran war a global economic story.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Global News

Summary

The Iran war’s economic effects became a major May 2026 story as companies warned of higher costs, disrupted supply chains, and pressure from energy markets. The Strait of Hormuz disruption linked a military conflict to ordinary business expenses and consumer prices across multiple regions.

A war bill beyond the battlefield

In May 2026, the Iran war became a corporate and consumer story as well as a military one. Reuters reported that companies around the world had already absorbed at least $25 billion in costs connected to the conflict, with the total still rising as firms adjusted to higher energy prices and disrupted trade routes.

Oil and gas prices matter because they move through the entire economy. Airlines pay more for fuel, manufacturers pay more to move parts, farmers pay more for fertilizer and transport, and consumers often face higher prices after companies pass along part of the increase.

The Strait of Hormuz effect

The disruption centered on the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow but globally important shipping route. When access through the strait became uncertain, businesses had to plan around delay, risk, insurance cost, and rerouting. Even companies far from the Middle East felt the pressure because modern supply chains are tied together through energy, shipping, and finance.

The crisis also exposed the limits of just-in-time global logistics. When a critical route becomes dangerous or politically restricted, businesses can be forced to hold more inventory, pay more for transport, or pause operations.

Why fertilizer became part of the story

One sign of the crisis spreading beyond oil markets was the attention to fertilizer. European officials moved to ease duties on some fertilizer products to reduce pressure on farmers. That policy response showed how an energy crisis can quickly become a food-cost and agricultural-production issue.

For readers, the economic lesson is straightforward: geopolitical chokepoints do not stay isolated. A maritime crisis can become an inflation story, a food story, a transportation story, and a political story at the same time.

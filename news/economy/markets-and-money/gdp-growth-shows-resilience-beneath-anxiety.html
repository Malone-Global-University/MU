GDP Growth Shows Resilience Beneath a Month of Economic Anxiety

First-quarter GDP grew at a 2.0 percent annual rate, showing that the economy entered spring with momentum despite rising public concern.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Economic Literacy

Summary

The Bureau of Economic Analysis reported that real GDP increased at a 2.0 percent annual rate in the first quarter of 2026, after growing 0.5 percent in the fourth quarter of 2025. The report showed that the economy had resilience, even as inflation and consumer sentiment worsened in May.

Growth improved from the previous quarter

The BEA’s advance estimate showed real gross domestic product increasing at an annual rate of 2.0 percent in the first quarter of 2026. That was stronger than the 0.5 percent growth reported for the fourth quarter of 2025.

GDP is not a full picture of household well-being, but it is one of the broadest measures of economic activity. A 2.0 percent annualized increase suggested that the economy was still expanding as the year began.

What helped the economy grow

According to the BEA, the increase reflected contributions from investment, exports, consumer spending, and government spending. Imports also increased, which subtracts from GDP accounting because imported goods and services are produced outside the United States.

This mix showed that growth was not coming from a single narrow source. Business investment and exports mattered alongside household and government activity.

Why the good news felt complicated

The GDP report landed in a month when other indicators were less comfortable. Inflation moved higher, mortgage rates rose, and consumer sentiment fell sharply. That combination created a divided economic picture: output was growing, but households felt pressured.

This is why May’s economy could not be explained with one headline. Growth data suggested resilience. Sentiment data suggested frustration. Inflation data suggested risk. The labor market suggested stability but not overheating.

The next question

The central question after the GDP report was whether first-quarter momentum could continue. If inflation erodes purchasing power, if rates stay high, or if energy prices weigh on households, growth can slow even after a solid quarter.

For now, the GDP story showed that the United States economy was not simply weakening across the board. It was expanding, but under conditions that made many families and policymakers uneasy.

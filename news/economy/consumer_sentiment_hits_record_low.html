Consumer Sentiment Hits Record Low as Living Costs Weigh on Households

The University of Michigan’s May survey showed a steep drop in confidence as households worried about inflation, gasoline, and the economic outlook.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Economic Literacy

Summary

Consumer sentiment fell to 44.8 in May 2026, according to the University of Michigan’s final monthly survey. The decline showed that many Americans felt worse about the economy even while employment and spending data remained more stable than public mood suggested.

A weak mood in a still-working economy

The University of Michigan’s final May 2026 survey placed the Index of Consumer Sentiment at 44.8, down from 49.8 in April and 52.2 a year earlier. The decline showed that households were not simply reacting to one bad headline; they were expressing deeper concern about their financial position and the direction of the economy.

Consumer sentiment matters because the American economy depends heavily on consumer spending. If people expect worse conditions, they may postpone large purchases, reduce discretionary spending, or become more cautious with debt.

Inflation expectations became part of the concern

The sharp decline in sentiment was closely tied to inflation worries. When consumers believe prices will keep rising, they may change behavior in ways that reinforce economic stress. They may demand higher wages, buy sooner to avoid future price increases, or cut spending because they believe future expenses will be harder to manage.

Federal Reserve officials pay attention to inflation expectations because expectations can become part of the inflation process itself. A central bank can fight one month of higher gasoline prices, but it has a harder problem if the public begins to believe inflation will stay high.

The household economy looked different from Wall Street

One reason the May story was striking is that consumer sentiment and market performance did not tell the same story. Stocks could rise on corporate earnings and technology investment while ordinary households still felt strained by gasoline, groceries, insurance, rent, interest rates, and credit card payments.

This disconnect is common in modern economic reporting. Financial markets often react to profits, interest-rate expectations, and investor positioning. Households react to paychecks, bills, and the costs of daily life.

Why the sentiment drop matters

A low sentiment reading does not automatically mean recession. People can feel bad and still spend, especially when they have jobs. But a record-low mood is still a warning sign. It suggests the economy has less emotional and financial cushion if energy prices rise further, hiring slows, or borrowing costs remain high.

For May 2026, consumer sentiment became one of the clearest measures of public economic anxiety. It showed that even without a collapse in employment, many Americans felt the economy was moving in the wrong direction.

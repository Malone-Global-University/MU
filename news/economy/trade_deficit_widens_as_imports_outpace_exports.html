Trade Deficit Widens as Imports Outpace Exports

The March trade report showed a wider goods and services deficit, highlighting how imports, exports, and global supply chains remained central to the economy.


By: Joseph Malone

Published: May 22, 2026

Updated: May 22, 2026

Section: Economic Literacy

Summary

The U.S. goods and services trade deficit increased to $60.3 billion in March 2026, up from a revised $57.8 billion in February. Exports rose, but imports rose more, keeping trade in the center of the national economy discussion.

The March deficit increased

The U.S. Census Bureau and the Bureau of Economic Analysis reported that the goods and services deficit increased to $60.3 billion in March. That was $2.5 billion higher than the revised February deficit of $57.8 billion.

The report showed exports at $320.9 billion and imports at $381.2 billion. Both increased, but imports rose more than exports, which widened the overall deficit.

Why the trade balance matters

A trade deficit is not automatically good or bad by itself. It can reflect strong domestic demand, global supply chains, a strong dollar, foreign investment, or dependence on imported goods. The meaning depends on the broader context.

In May 2026, trade mattered because the economy was already dealing with inflation and uncertainty. If imports become more expensive because of energy costs, tariffs, currency movements, or supply disruptions, those costs can reach American consumers.

Goods and services told different stories

The March report showed the goods deficit increasing while the services surplus also increased. That split is important because the United States often runs a goods deficit and a services surplus. Goods include items such as vehicles, electronics, machinery, and consumer products. Services include areas such as travel, finance, intellectual property, and business services.

The balance between these categories helps explain where the United States is importing physical products and where it is exporting high-value services.

A global economy story inside a domestic economy story

Trade became one of the month’s biggest economic stories because domestic inflation cannot be separated from global supply chains. Imports, exports, shipping, energy, and tariffs all influence what American businesses pay and what households eventually see on shelves.

The March trade report did not by itself define the economy. But it added another piece to a May picture shaped by inflation, energy, rates, and consumer anxiety.

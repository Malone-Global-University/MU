WORKFLOW NOTE

Title
Cross-Language “To Be” Verb Comparison

Date
2026-05-17

Area
MGU Language / Grammar / Comparative Linguistics Notes

Problem
The English verb “to be” had been identified as a core action/linking verb meaning “to exist,” but its relationship to similar verbs in other languages had not yet been organized. The main issue was that English uses one broad verb, while languages such as Spanish and Japanese divide the same grammatical territory into multiple verbs or structures.

Intended Goal
Create a clear comparative explanation showing how English “to be” corresponds to Spanish **ser/estar**, Japanese **です・だ / ある / いる / ている**, and similar forms in other major languages. The correct state should make it easy to explain that “be” covers existence, identity, condition, location, and state, while other languages may split those functions.

Root Cause
The English verb “be” is unusually broad and irregular. It combines meanings that many languages separate into different verbs, copulas, existence markers, location verbs, adjective constructions, or omitted present-tense forms. A simple one-to-one translation does not work across languages.

Steps Taken

1. Defined the English verb **“be”** as a central verb of existence, identity, condition, state, location, and grammatical support.

2. Reviewed the main English forms of **be**: **be, am, is, are, was, were, being, been, will be**.

3. Clarified that **be** has present, past, and future tense forms in basic grammar, while also appearing across broader English tense-aspect patterns such as **has been**, **had been**, **is being**, and **was being**.

4. Compared English **to be** with Spanish **ser** and **estar**, first using the classroom distinction of permanent versus temporary, then refining it into the stronger rule: **ser** describes what something is, while **estar** describes how, where, or in what condition something is.

5. Gave Spanish examples such as **Ella es maestra**, **Estoy cansado**, and **El libro está en la mesa** to show identity, condition, and location.

6. Compared English **to be** with Japanese / Nihongo structures: **です / だ** for identity or classification, **ある** for existence/location of non-living things, **いる** for existence/location of living beings, and **ている** for ongoing or resulting states.

7. Expanded the comparison to other languages, including Latin **esse**, French **être**, German **sein**, Italian **essere/stare**, Portuguese **ser/estar**, Russian **быть**, Arabic and Hebrew present-tense omission patterns, Greek **είμαι**, Hindi/Urdu **honaa**, Mandarin **是 / 有 / 在**, and Korean **이다 / 있다**.

Key Change
The session reframed English **“to be”** as a single broad grammatical powerhouse whose functions are divided differently across languages.

Outcome
The comparison now shows that English **be** is not merely equivalent to one foreign-language verb. It maps to different systems depending on whether the sentence expresses identity, existence, location, condition, state, tense, or grammatical support. This gives MGU language notes a stronger foundation for explaining why Spanish uses **ser/estar**, Japanese uses several constructions, and some languages omit present-tense “be” entirely.

Validation
The explanation was validated through side-by-side examples across English, Spanish, Japanese, and other languages. The comparison successfully handled basic identity sentences, existence sentences, location sentences, temporary condition sentences, and languages where “to be” is omitted in the present tense.

Rules Going Forward
When comparing English **“to be”** with other languages, do not assume a one-word equivalent. First identify the function being expressed: identity, existence, location, condition, state, passive construction, or progressive tense. Then match that function to the target language’s actual structure.

Notes
This session can support a future MGU grammar or comparative language article titled something like **“The Verb To Be Across Languages”** or **“How Languages Express Being, Existence, and Identity.”** A useful future expansion would be a table comparing English, Spanish, Japanese, Mandarin, Korean, Arabic, Hebrew, Russian, Latin, French, and German by grammatical function.

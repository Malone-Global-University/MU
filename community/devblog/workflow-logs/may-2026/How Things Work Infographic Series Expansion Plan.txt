WORKFLOW NOTE

Title
How Things Work Infographic Series Expansion Plan

Date
2026-05-17

Area
MGU image library / infographic / how-things-work-series

Problem
The infographic series had grown quickly, but the folder structure and inventory were becoming difficult to track. Several categories were strong, while others were underbuilt or empty. Some folders overlapped in purpose, and several filenames contained typos, duplicates, or incomplete names. This made it harder to know what already existed, what needed cleanup, and what should be generated next.

Intended Goal
The infographic library should have a clean, scalable category structure with consistent folder names, consistent `.webp` filenames, clear subject groupings, and planned future batches that expand the series without creating duplicates or miscellaneous clutter.

Root Cause
The series expanded faster than the tracking system. New image batches were added before the taxonomy was fully stabilized, which caused overlapping folders, misspelled directory names, duplicate files, and uneven coverage across categories.

Steps Taken

1. Reviewed the current PowerShell inventory for `C:\MGU\image\infographic\how-things-work-series`.

2. Identified the strongest existing folders, including `transportation-and-vehicle-systems`, `home-and-building-systems`, `medical-and-biological-technology`, `energy-and-power-systems`, `agriculture`, `physics-foundations`, `physics-laws`, and `astronomy-&-cosmology`.

3. Identified underbuilt folders that need future expansion, including `civil-infrastructure`, `machines-and-mechanical-systems`, `home-and-appliance-systems`, and `materials-science`.

4. Flagged folder-name cleanup items, including changing `material-sciience` to `materials-science` and `astronomy-&-cosmology` to `astronomy-and-cosmology`.

5. Flagged likely accidental or legacy folders, including `databaseClear-History` and the older `food-agriculture-and-everyday-systems` structure.

6. Identified category overlap among `electronics`, `computing-and-electronics`, and `communications-and-computing-systems`, and recommended consolidating into cleaner categories such as `computing-and-electronics` and `communications-and-networks`.

7. Identified filename problems, including `how-how-arch-bridges-work.webp`, `how-stayed-cabke-bridges-work.webp`, `how-a-geothermal.webp`, `how-a-natural-gas-power-plant.webp`, duplicate hydroelectric dam files, and duplicate fiber optic cable files.

8. Proposed new 10-file expansion batches for multiple existing categories, including home appliances, food processing, agriculture, transportation, aerospace, energy, water infrastructure, civil infrastructure, machines, defense/safety, medical technology, computing/electronics, communications, and materials science.

9. Proposed several new top-level folders for long-term growth: `manufacturing-and-industrial-systems`, `environmental-and-recycling-systems`, `public-health-and-emergency-systems`, `navigation-and-measurement-systems`, and `sound-music-and-media-technology`.

10. Produced a recommended long-term folder taxonomy for the full `how-things-work-series` library.

Key Change
The infographic series was reframed from a fast-growing loose collection into a structured, expandable visual reference library organized by stable subject categories and planned batch production.

Outcome
The next production steps are clearer. The user now has a cleanup list, a corrected folder taxonomy, specific filename corrections, and multiple ready-to-generate 10-image batches. The structure can scale without creating more duplicate folders or inconsistent naming.

Validation
The recommendations were based directly on the user’s PowerShell inventory. Existing files were compared against folder names, category purposes, duplicates, typos, and missing coverage areas. The proposed expansions avoided repeating files already listed in the inventory and focused on gaps in the current structure.

Rules Going Forward
Use clean folder names with lowercase words separated by hyphens. Avoid ampersands, misspellings, temporary folders, and duplicate category meanings. Keep `.webp` filenames descriptive and consistent, usually following the pattern `how-[subject]-works.webp`. Generate images in batches by category instead of scattered one-off additions. Before generating a new batch, compare it against the current folder inventory to avoid duplicates.

Notes
The physics and astronomy folders are valuable, but they function more like concept infographics than mechanical cutaway “how things work” images. They can remain inside the series for now, but may eventually deserve a separate science-concepts infographic parent if the collection grows much larger.

The Myth of “Everyone Is Racist”

Author: Joseph Malone
Date: 4-12-2026

Home Community News Opinion The Myth of “Everyone Is Racist”

Main Article

There is a phrase that gets repeated often enough that people start accepting it without thinking: “all groups are racist.” It sounds balanced. It sounds fair. It sounds like the kind of statement that levels the playing field.

It is none of those things.

It is a distortion.

In the United States, racism has never been an abstract idea floating evenly across society. It has operated as a system. It was built, reinforced, and scaled through law, policy, and institutional design. That reality is not theoretical. It is documented history, from Slavery in the United States to Jim Crow laws and beyond. These were not isolated attitudes. They were coordinated frameworks that shaped housing, education, employment, and access to capital.

That system did not emerge from all groups equally. It was constructed and maintained by specific power structures, overwhelmingly controlled by white institutions, and directed primarily at Black Americans.

So when people say “everyone is racist,” they are not clarifying reality. They are flattening it.

Now, let’s be precise. Anyone can show bias. Anyone can make a race-based judgment in a moment of frustration, fear, or experience. That is not the argument here. The argument is about what is probable, practical, and sustainable as a strategy.

Racism, when it functions at scale, requires incentives. It requires reinforcement. It requires some expectation of benefit. Historically, those conditions have not existed for African Americans. There has been no structural advantage to be gained, no institutional backing to support it, and no system to plug into that rewards that behavior.

Instead, the reality has been the opposite.

Black Americans have spent generations navigating a system that often worked against them, not building one to exclude others. The posture has been defensive, not offensive. Time and energy have gone toward survival, mobility, and protection, not toward engineering exclusionary frameworks.

That matters.

Because when accusations of “Black racism” surface, they are often detached from this context. In many cases, they arise when someone is:

  • responding to perceived discrimination
  • setting boundaries
  • speaking about structural inequality
  • expressing frustration rooted in lived experience

Those responses are then reframed as if they are equivalent to the systems they are reacting to.

They are not.

There is also a strategic element that cannot be ignored. If you can convince people that “everyone is equally racist,” you remove hierarchy from the discussion. You blur responsibility. You create the illusion that there is no meaningful difference between a historically dominant system and a reactive response.

That is convenient for those who benefit from that confusion.

It also creates a trap. If Black individuals are provoked, mischaracterized, or pushed into reactive generalizations, those moments can be isolated and amplified as proof of equivalence. The narrative becomes: “See? They are just as bad.” From there, the door opens to normalizing the very systems that created the imbalance in the first place.

That is not an accident. That is a rhetorical strategy.

The reality is more straightforward.

Racism in America has been a structured, sustained system, not a random distribution of attitudes. While any individual can exhibit bias, African Americans have had neither the incentive nor the institutional capacity to develop racism as a coherent, scalable practice. The idea that they have, or that it exists at comparable levels, is not supported by history or by the way systems actually function.

So no, the claim that “all groups are racist” is not a neutral observation. It is a misleading one.

And in practice, it does more to obscure the truth than to reveal it.

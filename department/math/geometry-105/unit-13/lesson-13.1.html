Lesson Description

This lesson introduces vectors in the coordinate plane. Students learn how vectors are represented, how to compute component form, and how to calculate vector magnitude using the distance formula. These ideas form the foundation for later vector operations and applications.

Learning Objectives

  • Represent vectors using coordinate components.
  • Determine vectors between two points.
  • Calculate the magnitude of vectors.
  • Interpret vectors geometrically in the coordinate plane.

Topics Covered

  • Vector notation and directed segments
  • Component form of vectors
  • Vectors between two coordinate points
  • Vector magnitude formula
  • Geometric interpretation of vectors

Key Terms

  • Vector: A quantity with both magnitude and direction.
  • Component Form: Representation of a vector as \( \langle x, y \rangle \).
  • Magnitude: Length of a vector.
  • Initial Point: Starting point of a vector.
  • Terminal Point: Ending point of a vector.

Lesson Examples

Example 1: Vector from Two Points

Find the vector from A(2, 3) to B(7, 9).

  1. Subtract coordinates: \( \langle 7-2,\; 9-3 \rangle \)
  2. Vector = \( \langle 5, 6 \rangle \)

Example 2: Magnitude of a Vector

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Always subtract coordinates in correct order: terminal minus initial.
  • Do not forget to square negative values correctly.
  • Magnitude is always non-negative.
  • Vectors describe movement, not location.

Summary

Vectors represent direction and magnitude in the coordinate plane. Using component form, we can easily compute vector length and prepare for vector operations such as addition, subtraction, and dot products in later lessons.

Challenge Problems

  2. Find a vector with magnitude 10 in the first quadrant.
  3. Show that vectors \( \langle 3, 4 \rangle \) and \( \langle -3, -4 \rangle \) have equal magnitudes.

Lesson Description

This lesson focuses on applying proportions and similar triangles to indirect measurement problems. Students will use scale factors and proportional relationships to calculate unknown heights, distances, and lengths that cannot be measured directly.

Learning Objectives

  • Set up and solve proportions correctly.
  • Identify corresponding sides in similar triangles.
  • Apply similarity to indirect measurement scenarios.
  • Use scale factor to determine missing dimensions.

Topics Covered

  • Solving proportions using cross multiplication
  • Scale factor interpretation
  • Shadow and mirror measurement problems
  • Parallel lines and triangle similarity
  • Real-world geometric modeling

Key Terms

  • Proportion: An equation stating that two ratios are equal.
  • Scale Factor: The ratio of corresponding side lengths in similar figures.
  • Indirect Measurement: Finding a measurement using similarity rather than direct measurement.
  • Cross Multiplication: Method used to solve proportions.

Lesson Examples

Example 1: Shadow Problem

A 6 ft person casts a 4 ft shadow. A tree casts a 20 ft shadow. Find the height of the tree.

\[ \frac{6}{4} = \frac{h}{20} \]

\[ 6 \cdot 20 = 4h \Rightarrow 120 = 4h \Rightarrow h = 30 \]

The tree is 30 ft tall.

Example 2: Scale Drawing

A map uses a scale of 1 inch = 5 miles. If two cities are 3 inches apart on the map, the real distance is:

\[ 3 \times 5 = 15 \text{ miles} \]

Example 3: Similar Triangles in Parallel Lines

If a segment parallel to one side of a triangle divides the other two sides proportionally, set up a proportion to find the missing length.

Practice Problems

  1. A 5 ft pole casts a 3 ft shadow. A nearby building casts a 45 ft shadow. Find the buildingâ€™s height.
  2. If a blueprint uses scale 1:50 and a wall measures 8 cm on the blueprint, what is its actual length?
  3. Two similar triangles have side ratio 2:5. If the smaller triangle has a side of 6, find the corresponding side of the larger triangle.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Ensure corresponding sides are matched correctly before setting up proportions.
  • Maintain consistent units throughout calculations.
  • Do not invert ratios accidentally when cross multiplying.

Summary

Proportions and similarity allow us to measure indirectly when direct measurement is impractical. By identifying corresponding parts and applying scale factors, we can solve real-world geometric problems accurately.

Challenge Problems

  1. Design your own indirect measurement problem involving a flagpole and solve it.
  2. If two similar triangles have areas in ratio 9:16, what is the ratio of their corresponding side lengths?
  3. Explain why doubling the scale factor multiplies area by four.

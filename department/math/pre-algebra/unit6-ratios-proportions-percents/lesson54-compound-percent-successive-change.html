Lesson 54: Compound Percent & Successive Change

Understand how to calculate compound percent changes and successive increases or decreases, and apply them in real-life contexts like prices, interest, and population growth.

Lesson Description

This lesson focuses on calculating compound percent changes and understanding successive increases or decreases applied to quantities over multiple steps. Real-world applications include finance, discounts, and population growth.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate successive percent increases and decreases.
  • Understand compound percent change formulas.
  • Apply these methods to practical real-world scenarios.

Topics Covered

  • Successive percent increase and decrease
  • Compound percent change formula: Final = Original √ó (1 ¬± p1) √ó (1 ¬± p2) ...
  • Applications in price changes, discounts, and interest

Key Terms

  • Compound Percent Change: The overall change after applying multiple successive percent changes.
  • Successive Increase: Increasing a value multiple times in sequence, each on the previous result.
  • Successive Decrease: Decreasing a value multiple times in sequence, each on the previous result.

Lesson Examples

Example 1: Successive Increase

  1. An item costs $100. It increases by 10% in year 1 and 5% in year 2. Find the final price.
  2. Year 1: 100 √ó 1.10 = 110
  3. Year 2: 110 √ó 1.05 = 115.50
  4. Final Price: $115.50

Example 2: Successive Decrease

  1. A population of 2000 decreases by 5% in year 1 and 10% in year 2. Find the final population.
  2. Year 1: 2000 √ó 0.95 = 1900
  3. Year 2: 1900 √ó 0.90 = 1710
  4. Final Population: 1710

Practice Problems

  1. An item's price is $150. It is increased by 8% and then decreased by 5%. Find the final price.
  2. A salary of $3000 is increased by 5% and then increased by 3%. Calculate the new salary.
  3. A population of 5000 decreases by 4% each year for two consecutive years. Find the final population.
  4. An investment of $1000 grows by 6% in the first year and 4% in the second year. Calculate the final value.

Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Always apply each percent change to the most recent value, not the original.
  • Convert percentages to decimals before multiplying.
  • Do not simply add or subtract the percentages; compound change multiplies them.

Summary

Compound percent change allows you to calculate the effect of multiple successive increases or decreases on a quantity. It is used in finance, population studies, and pricing calculations.

Challenge Problems

  1. A product price is increased by 12% and then 15%. Calculate the final price if the original price was $250.
  2. A population decreases by 7% one year and increases by 5% the next. If the initial population is 10,000, find the final population.
  3. An investment grows by 10% per year for 3 consecutive years. What is the total percent increase?

Lesson 27: Solving Exponential Equations

Exponential equations are equations in which the variable appears in the exponent, e.g., \[ 2^x = 16 \quad \text{or} \quad 3^{2x+1} = 81 \] To solve: - Rewrite both sides with a common base, if possible - Apply logarithms (often using the change of base formula) - Solve the resulting linear equation

Overview

Why it matters

- Models growth and decay phenomena: population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest - Critical in science, engineering, finance, and computer science - Provides a bridge to logarithms, reinforcing algebraic manipulation skills

Worked examples

**Simple base match**
Solve \(2^x = 16\)
\[
16 = 2^4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2^x = 2^4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = 4
\]
**Variable in exponent with coefficient**
Solve \(3^{2x+1} = 81\)
\[
81 = 3^4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 3^{2x+1} = 3^4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2x+1 = 4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2x = 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = 1.5
\]
**Using logarithms**
Solve \(5^x = 200\)
\[
x = \log_5(200) = \frac{\ln(200)}{\ln(5)} \approx \frac{5.298}{1.609} \approx 3.29
\]
**Exponential with negative exponent**
Solve \(2^{-x} = 8\)
\[
8 = 2^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2^{-x} = 2^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -x = 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = -3
\]

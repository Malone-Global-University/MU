Lesson 4 — Linear Equations: Solving Single-Variable Equations

Linear equations have the highest power of the variable equal to 1. This lesson focuses on solving single-variable linear equations using addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and two-step processes, including handling negatives and fractions.

Overview

Linear equations are algebraic statements where the highest power of the variable is 1. Solving them means finding the value of the variable that makes the equation true. This lesson focuses on isolating the variable using inverse operations and properties of equality.

Why it matters

Linear equations appear in nearly every branch of mathematics and countless real-world situations: budgeting, motion problems, rates, and scientific formulas. Mastery of solving them builds the foundation for algebra, functions, systems of equations, and calculus.

Worked examples

Basic equation: x + 5 = 12 ⇒ x = 12 − 5 = 7

Equation with subtraction: y − 3 = 10 ⇒ y = 10 + 3 = 13

Multiplication: 4m = 20 ⇒ m = 20 / 4 = 5

Division: x / 6 = 2 ⇒ x = 2 × 6 = 12

Two-step equation: 2x + 3 = 11 ⇒ 2x = 11 − 3 = 8 ⇒ x = 8 / 2 = 4

Equation with negatives: −3x + 7 = 1 ⇒ −3x = 1 − 7 = −6 ⇒ x = −6 / −3 = 2

Practice problems

  1. Solve: x + 8 = 15
  2. Solve: y − 12 = 4
  3. Solve: 5a = 45
  4. Solve: m / 7 = 9
  5. Solve: 3x + 4 = 19
  6. Solve: −2n + 5 = −9
  7. Solve: 4p − 6 = 10
  8. Solve: z / 3 + 2 = 7

Answer key (brief): 1 → 7 2 → 16 3 → 9 4 → 63 5 → 5 6 → 7 7 → 4 8 → 15

Teaching tips

  • Stress the balance model: what you do to one side, you must do to the other.
  • Students may skip steps (e.g., not writing subtraction explicitly) and make errors—encourage showing work.
  • Remind that division by zero is undefined.
  • Highlight the difference between solving and simplifying: solving produces a specific solution.
  • For fractions, multiply both sides by the denominator to clear fractions.

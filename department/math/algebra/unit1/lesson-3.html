Lesson 3 — Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

This lesson teaches how to simplify algebraic expressions by combining like terms, using the distributive property, handling negatives, and working with fractions.

Overview

Simplifying algebraic expressions means rewriting them in their simplest form by combining like terms and applying the properties of real numbers. This makes equations easier to solve and prepares students for more advanced algebraic manipulation.

Why it matters

Without simplification, algebraic problems quickly become unwieldy. Recognizing like terms, applying the distributive property, and reducing expressions builds fluency for solving equations, graphing, and modeling real-world situations.

Worked examples

Combining like terms: 3x + 4x − 2 = (3 + 4)x − 2 = 7x − 2

Using the distributive property: 2(x + 5) = 2x + 10

Distributive property with negatives: -3(2x − 7) = -6x + 21

Multiple steps: 4(x + 3) + 2x = 4x + 12 + 2x = (4x + 2x) + 12 = 6x + 12

Simplifying with fractions: (1/2)x + (3/2)x = (4/2)x = 2x

Practice problems

  1. Simplify: 5x + 7x − 3
  2. Expand and simplify: 3(y + 4)
  3. Simplify: 2a + 3a + 4 − 7
  4. Expand: -2(3m − 5)
  5. Simplify: x + 2x + 3x + 4
  6. Simplify: (2/3)y + (4/3)y

Answer key (brief): 1 → 12 2 → 3y + 12 3 → 5a − 3 4 → -6m + 10 5 → 6x + 4 6 → 2y

Teaching tips

  • Emphasize like terms: terms must have the same variable raised to the same power.
  • Students often try to combine unlike terms (e.g., 3x + 5y); stress that they cannot be simplified further.
  • When distributing negatives, use extra parentheses to avoid sign errors.
  • Show how simplification is not about “solving”—it’s about making the expression cleaner.

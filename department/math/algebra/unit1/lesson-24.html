Lesson 24: Introduction to Logarithms

A logarithm is the inverse operation of exponentiation. If \[ b^y = x \] then \[ \log_b(x) = y \] Key points: - Base \(b\): the number being raised to a power - Argument \(x\): the result of exponentiation - Value \(y\): the exponent needed to get \(x\) from \(b\) Common bases: - \(b = 10 \Rightarrow\) common logarithm (\(\log\)) - \(b = e \Rightarrow\) natural logarithm (\(\ln\)) Logarithms convert multiplication into addition and exponentiation into multiplication, simplifying calculations.

Overview

Why it matters

- Crucial for solving exponential equations - Models population growth, radioactive decay, pH, and earthquakes - Introduces algebraic relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions

Worked examples

**Basic Conversion:** \[ 2^3 = 8 \Rightarrow \log_2(8) = 3 \]

**Common Logarithm:** \[ 10^4 = 10000 \Rightarrow \log_{10}(10000) = 4 \]

**Natural Logarithm:** \[ e^2 \approx 7.389 \Rightarrow \ln(7.389) \approx 2 \]

**Equation Solving:** Solve \(10^x = 500\) \[ x = \log_{10}(500) \approx 2.699 \]

**Inverse Check:** If \(\log_5(125) = y\), then \[ 5^y = 125 \Rightarrow y = 3 \]

Practice problems

  1. Find \(\log_3(27)\)
  2. Solve \(\log_2(x) = 5\)
  3. Calculate \(\log_{10}(1000)\)
  4. Solve for \(x: 5^x = 625\) using logs
  5. Solve \(\ln(e^4) = ?\)

Teaching tips

  • Emphasize that logarithms represent exponents
  • Base must be positive and not equal to 1
  • Apply inverse checking: \(\,b^{\log_b(x)} = x\,\)
  • Graph logarithms as reflections of exponentials across \(y = x\)

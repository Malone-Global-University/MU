Lesson 9 — Graphs of Functions

Graphing functions is the process of visually representing the relationship between inputs (x-values) and outputs (y-values) on a coordinate plane.

Overview

Key concepts: - Cartesian plane: x-axis (input), y-axis (output) - Intercepts: points where the graph crosses axes - Shape and symmetry: linear, quadratic, absolute value, cubic, etc. - Domain and range visualization

Why it matters

Graphs allow students to: - Quickly understand how a function behaves - Identify trends and patterns - Solve equations visually - Predict outcomes in real-world contexts Graphing also prepares students for calculus concepts like slopes and areas under curves.

Worked examples

Linear function: f(x) = 2x + 1 Table of values: | x | f(x) | |---|------| | -1 | -1 | | 0 | 1 | | 1 | 3 | Plot points and draw a straight line Intercepts: y-intercept = 1, x-intercept = -0.5

Quadratic function: f(x) = x² − 4 Table of values: | x | f(x) | |---|------| | -2 | 0 | | 0 | -4 | | 2 | 0 | Parabola opens upwards, vertex at (0, -4) Symmetry about y-axis

Absolute value function: f(x) = |x−1| Graph forms a “V” shape Vertex at (1, 0) Reflect across the line of symmetry x = 1

Practice problems

Answer key (brief): 1 → y-intercept = −2, x-intercept = 2/3 2 → Vertex = (0, 5), axis of symmetry x = 0 3 → Vertex = (−1/2, 0) 4 → Domain and range vary per graph 5 → Linear, quadratic, cubic, or absolute value as appropriate

Teaching tips

  • Always label axes and scale consistently
  • Plot at least 3–5 points before drawing curves
  • Discuss symmetry and patterns to make graphing easier
  • Warn against connecting points incorrectly for non-linear functions
  • Emphasize interpreting graphs, not just drawing them

Resources & printable handout

- Graph paper - Graphing calculators - Desmos or GeoGebra

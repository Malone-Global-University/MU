Lesson 32: Continuity & Function Behavior

Students will learn to: - Define continuity formally and informally - Identify points of discontinuity - Classify discontinuities (removable, jump, infinite) - Relate continuity to limits and function behavior - Examine function behavior near critical points Continuity describes whether a function has breaks, jumps, or holes.

Overview

Why it matters

- Continuous functions are predictable and smooth, essential for calculus - Discontinuities affect integration, differentiation, and real-world modeling - Continuity is crucial in physics, engineering, and economics - Helps understand graph behavior, asymptotes, and real-world constraints

Worked examples

**Simple Polynomial**
\[
f(x) = x^2 + 3x - 4
\]
Polynomials are continuous everywhere—no breaks or holes.
**Rational Function**
\[
f(x) = \frac{x^2-1}{x-1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x+1
\]
- Hole at \(x=1\) (removable discontinuity)
- Continuous everywhere else

**Piecewise Function** \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & x<1 \\ 2x & x \ge 1 \end{cases} \] - Left-hand limit: \(\lim_{x \to 1^-} x^2 = 1\) - Right-hand limit: \(\lim_{x \to 1^+} 2x = 2\) - Limits differ ⇒ jump discontinuity at \(x=1\)

**Infinite Discontinuity**
\[
f(x) = \frac{1}{x-2} \quad \text{as } x \to 2, f(x) \to \infty
\]
- Vertical asymptote, infinite discontinuity

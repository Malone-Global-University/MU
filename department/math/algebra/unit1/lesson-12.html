Lesson 12 — Composition of Functions

Function composition involves applying one function to the result of another. For functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, the composition $f \circ g$ is defined as: $$ (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) $$ Key points: * Order matters: $f \circ g \neq g \circ f$ in general * Composition allows layering functions to model complex processes * Useful for multi-step mathematical modeling

Overview

Why it matters

* Models real-world chains of processes (e.g., currency conversion then applying tax) * Critical in calculus, especially for the chain rule * Strengthens understanding of input-output behavior of functions

Worked examples

**Simple composition** Let $f(x) = 2x + 3$ and $g(x) = x^2$:

$$
(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = 2x^2 + 3
$$
$$
(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = (2x + 3)^2 = 4x^2 + 12x + 9
$$
*This demonstrates that the order of composition matters.*

**Trigonometric composition** Let $f(x) = \sin(x)$ and $g(x) = 3x$:

$$
(f \circ g)(x) = \sin(3x), \quad (g \circ f)(x) = 3\sin(x)
$$
*Graphs of $\sin(3x)$ and $3\sin(x)$ are visibly different.*

**Applied example: currency conversion + tax** Let

$$
C(x) = x \cdot 1.1 \quad (\text{convert USD to EUR})
$$
$$
T(y) = y \cdot 1.2 \quad (\text{apply 20\% tax})
$$
$$
(T \circ C)(100) = T(C(100)) = T(110) = 132 \text{ EUR after tax}
$$

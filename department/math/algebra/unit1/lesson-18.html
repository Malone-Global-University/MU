Lesson 18: Factoring Techniques

Factoring is the reverse of multiplication. This lesson covers methods to break polynomials into simpler components, including GCF, trinomials, difference of squares, and sum/difference of cubes.

Overview

Factoring is the reverse of multiplication—it breaks a polynomial into a product of simpler polynomials or numbers. Key techniques include: - Greatest Common Factor (GCF): Factor out the largest expression shared by all terms - Trinomials: Factor into two binomials when possible - Difference of Squares: \(a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)\) - Sum/Difference of Cubes: - \(a^3 + b^3 = (a+b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)\) - \(a^3 - b^3 = (a-b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)\)

  • Factor out GCF first
  • Recognize patterns for special products
  • Apply formulas for sum/difference of cubes
  • Confirm factoring by multiplication

Why it matters

- Simplifies equations for solving and algebraic manipulation - Essential for understanding roots, zeros, and graph behavior - Foundation for advanced topics like rational expressions, inequalities, and partial fractions

Worked examples

**GCF Factoring:** \(6x^3 + 9x^2 = 3x^2(2x + 3)\)

**Trinomial Factoring:** \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x+2)(x+3)\)

**Difference of Squares:** \(x^2 - 16 = (x-4)(x+4)\)

**Sum of Cubes:** \(x^3 + 27 = (x+3)(x^2 - 3x + 9)\)

**Difference of Cubes:** \(8x^3 - 27 = (2x-3)(4x^2 + 6x + 9)\)

Practice problems

  1. Factor: \(12x^3 - 18x^2\)
  2. Factor: \(x^2 + 7x + 12\)
  3. Factor: \(y^2 - 49\)
  4. Factor: \(x^3 + 8\)
  5. Factor: \(27x^3 - 1\)
  6. Factor completely: \(6x^3 + 9x^2 - 15x\)

Answer key (brief): - \(6x^3 - 18x^2 = 6x^2(2x-3)\) - \((x+3)(x+4)\) - \((y-7)(y+7)\) - \((x+2)(x^2 - 2x +4)\) - \((3x-1)(9x^2 + 3x +1)\) - \(3x(2x^2 +3x -5)\) (example simplified)

Teaching tips

  • Always look for a GCF first
  • Remind students to check for special patterns like difference of squares or cubes
  • Encourage verification by multiplication
  • Warn against confusing sum/difference of cubes formulas

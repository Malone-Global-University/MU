Lesson 13 — Inverse Functions

An inverse function undoes the action of the original function. For a function $f(x)$ mapping $x$ to $y$, the inverse $f^{-1}(x)$ maps $y$ back to $x$: $$ f(f^{-1}(x)) = f^{-1}(f(x)) = x $$ Key points: * Only one-to-one functions (bijective) have inverses * Graphically, the inverse is the reflection over the line $y=x$ * Essential for solving equations, modeling, and calculus

Overview

Why it matters

* Solve for inputs when outputs are known * Fundamental in logarithms (inverse of exponentials) and trigonometry (inverse trig functions) * Crucial in real-world problems: reversing transformations, decoding, back-calculating

Worked examples

**Simple linear function:**

$$
f(x) = 3x + 2
$$

Solve $y = 3x + 2$ for $x$:

$$
x = \frac{y-2}{3}
$$

Inverse:

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \frac{x-2}{3}
$$

**Quadratic (restricted domain):**

$$
f(x) = x^2, \quad x \ge 0
$$

Solve $y = x^2$ for $x$:

$$
x = \sqrt{y}
$$

Inverse:

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}
$$
Domain restriction is necessary for one-to-one property.

**Exponential and logarithmic:**

$$
f(x) = e^x
$$

Inverse:

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \ln(x)
$$

Check:

$$
f(f^{-1}(5)) = e^{\ln 5} = 5
$$

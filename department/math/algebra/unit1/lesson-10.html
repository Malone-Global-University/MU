Lesson 10 — Transformations of Functions

Function transformations modify the position, size, or orientation of a graph without changing its fundamental shape. Types include: - Translations – shifting horizontally or vertically - Reflections – flipping across an axis - Stretches/Compressions – changing width or height - Combinations – applying multiple transformations simultaneously Mathematical notation: - f(x)+k → vertical shift by k - f(x−h) → horizontal shift by h - −f(x) → reflection across x-axis - f(−x) → reflection across y-axis - a⋅f(x) → vertical stretch/compression - f(bx) → horizontal stretch/compression

Overview

Why it matters

Transformations allow students to: - Quickly sketch complex functions without plotting many points - Understand how formula parameters affect the graph - Prepare for higher-level topics like inverse functions, composite functions, and trigonometric wave analysis

Worked examples

Vertical shift: f(x) = x² + 3 Original: f(x) = x² Shift up 3 units → vertex moves from (0,0) to (0,3)

Horizontal shift: f(x) = (x−2)² Original: f(x) = x² Shift right 2 units → vertex moves from (0,0) to (2,0)

Reflection across x-axis: f(x) = −x² Parabola opens downwards instead of upwards

Vertical stretch: f(x) = 2x² Parabola becomes narrower because y-values are doubled

Combination: f(x) = −2(x+1)² + 3 Reflect across x-axis → stretch by factor of 2 → shift left 1 → shift up 3 Vertex: (-1, 3)

Practice problems

Answer key (brief): 1 → Shift left 3, down 2 2 → Reflect across x-axis, compress vertically by factor 0.5, shift up 4 3 → Shift right 2, up 5, vertex at (2,5) 4 → Shift right 4, up 2 5 → Reflect across x-axis, stretch by 3, shift left 1, down 2

Teaching tips

  • Introduce one transformation at a time to avoid confusion
  • Use graph overlays: plot original graph lightly, then draw transformation
  • Apply order correctly: reflections, stretches, then translations
  • Use real-life analogies: moving objects on a grid, stretching springs, flipping mirrors

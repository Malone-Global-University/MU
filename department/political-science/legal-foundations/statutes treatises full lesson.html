Federal Statutes & Treaties

Legislative process, codification, treaty-making, and interaction with the Constitution.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe how a bill becomes law and where federal statutes are published and codified.
  • Explain treaty-making and ratification procedures.
  • Analyze how statutes and treaties interact with the Constitution and state law under the Supremacy Clause.

Estimated class time: 75 minutes (lecture + mock legislative exercise)

Lecture: From Bill to Statute; Treaty to Law

Congress passes laws through a multistage process: drafting, committee consideration, floor debate, passage, presidential action (sign / veto / pocket veto). Enacted laws are published as the Statutes at Large and later codified by subject in the United States Code (U.S.C.). For practical legal research, the U.S.C. is usually the starting point.

Treaties

The President negotiates treaties and the Senate must give advice and consent by a two-thirds vote. Once ratified, treaties are binding, but conflicts with later federal statutes or constitutional text may raise difficult interpretive questions. Courts often resolve such conflicts by considering the chronology and the intention of Congress.

Preemption and Limits

When federal law and state law collide, preemption doctrines instruct courts. Preemption can be express or implied (field preemption or conflict preemption). But federal laws themselves are constrained by constitutional limits — a federal statute that exceeds delegated powers risks invalidation by the courts.

Mock Bill Exercise (45 minutes)

  1. Students are divided into small committees and given a policy prompt (e.g., regulate emerging technology X).
  2. Each group drafts a short bill (one page) with a purpose clause, operative provisions, and a brief note on constitutional basis.
  3. Groups present and floor votes are simulated; one bill is selected and critiqued for constitutional vulnerabilities and implementation challenges.

Homework

Find one federal statute relevant to your career interest. Prepare a one-page summary: statutory purpose, key operative sections, implementing agencies, and any known preemption or constitutional questions. Cite the U.S.C. section.

Grading rubric: accuracy (30), clarity (30), research & citations (25), analysis (15).

Quiz (short)

  1. Describe the difference between the Statutes at Large and the United States Code. (4 pts)
  2. What vote in the Senate is required to ratify a treaty? (1 pt)
  3. Briefly explain express preemption. (3 pts)
Answer key
  1. The Statutes at Large publish laws chronologically as enacted; the United States Code organizes laws by subject matter (codification). (4 pts)
  2. Two-thirds of Senators present. (1 pt)
  3. Express preemption occurs when a federal statute explicitly states that federal law preempts state law in a specified field. (3 pts)

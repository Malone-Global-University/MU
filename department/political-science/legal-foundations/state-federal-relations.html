Federalism & State Constitutions

Division of powers between the federal government and the states.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the constitutional basis for federalism, including the Tenth Amendment.
  • Explain preemption doctrine and situations of conflict between federal and state law.
  • Identify how state constitutions interact with and sometimes extend rights beyond the federal baseline.

Core Content

Federalism splits authority: the federal government has those powers granted by the Constitution; remaining powers are reserved to the states or the people. State constitutions create state institutions and often protect civil liberties — sometimes offering broader protections than the federal Constitution.

Preemption

Federal law may expressly or implicitly preempt state regulation. Courts examine legislative intent and the field of regulation to determine preemption.

Cooperative vs. Dual Federalism

Historically, doctrines have shifted between a "dual" model (distinct spheres) and "cooperative" models (overlap and federal involvement). These shifts affect policy areas like commerce, welfare, and public health.

Activities

  • Comparative brief: choose a state constitutional provision and compare how it protects a right differently from the federal Constitution.
  • Preemption analysis: present a hypothetical state law that conflicts with a federal statute and ask students to analyze whether preemption applies.

Assessment

Students write a 500-word analysis of a state law vs federal statute conflict and recommend likely judicial outcomes with reasoning.

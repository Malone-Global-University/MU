The Bill of Rights & Individual Liberties

Overview of Amendments I–X and their continuing influence.

Learning Objectives

  • Summarize each of the first ten amendments and their legal effect.
  • Understand incorporation doctrine and how most Bill of Rights protections apply to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment.
  • Identify and debate contemporary constitutional questions arising under these rights.

Estimated class time: 90 minutes (lecture + case briefs)

Lecture: From Text to Protections

The Bill of Rights originally constrained only the federal government. After the Civil War, the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause became the vehicle through which most protections were applied to the states — a process called incorporation. This has profound effects: citizens can assert constitutional protections against state actions as well as federal.

Short tour of Amendments I–X

  • First Amendment: religion, speech, press, assembly, petition — a cornerstone for democratic governance.
  • Fourth & Fifth Amendments: criminal procedure protections: search and seizure limits, due process, protection against self-incrimination.
  • Sixth Amendment: guarantees in criminal prosecutions (counsel, confrontation, speedy trial).
  • Other amendments protect property and constrain government powers in several institutional ways.

In-Class Case Briefs (choose one per student)

  1. First Amendment: a landmark speech or press case.
  2. Fourth Amendment: a search & seizure case on probable cause or warrant requirements.
  3. Fifth Amendment: a takings clause or self-incrimination case.

Students prepare a 1-page brief: facts, question, holding, reasoning, and a short critique (what would you change?).

Debate: Balancing Rights

Two teams debate: Should the government be allowed to restrict speech on private social-media platforms in time of national emergency? Each team cites constitutional principles and factual constraints.

Homework & Writing Assignment

Write a 750-word paper analyzing how incorporation has changed the scope of one amendment of your choice. Provide at least two case citations and explain impact on state law.

Rubric (100 pts): thesis & structure (20), use of cases (30), analysis & synthesis (30), citations & style (10), mechanics (10).

Quiz (10 questions)

  1. Which amendment protects against unreasonable searches? (1 pt)
  2. What doctrine makes most Bill of Rights protections applicable to the states? (1 pt)
  3. True/False: The Third Amendment is often used in modern rights litigation. (1 pt)
  4. Short: Explain the difference between procedural and substantive due process. (3 pts)
  5. List two guarantees of the Sixth Amendment. (2 pts)
Answer key
  1. Fourth Amendment. (1 pt)
  2. Incorporation via the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause. (1 pt)
  3. False — it is rarely litigated but remains part of the text. (1 pt)
  4. Procedural due process concerns the fairness of procedures used (notice, hearing); substantive due process protects certain fundamental rights from government interference regardless of procedure. (3 pts)
  5. Right to counsel, speedy trial, public trial, jury trial, confront witnesses, be informed of charges. (2 pts)

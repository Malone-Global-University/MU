The U.S. Constitution: Structure & Principles

A focused reading of the Constitution and the mechanisms that make it binding.

Learning Objectives

  • Summarize the content and purpose of Articles I–VII.
  • Explain separation of powers, checks and balances, judicial review, and the amendment process.
  • Identify key clauses (Commerce Clause, Necessary & Proper Clause, Supremacy Clause) and their institutional effects.

Estimated class time: 90 minutes (lecture + clause clinic + close-reading)

Lecture: Reading the Constitution as Structure and Process

The Constitution is at once a legal document and a design manual. It sets institutions (Congress, President, judiciary), processes (how laws are made and how officials are selected), and constraints (limits on powers). When reading the text, move from the high-level design (Preamble and Articles) into the clauses that carry the institutional weight.

Article map

  • Article I — Creates Congress and lists several enumerated powers; contains the Necessary & Proper Clause (a key enabling provision).
  • Article II — Establishes presidency: election, powers, and responsibilities.
  • Article III — Establishes the federal judiciary and grants life tenure to Article III judges.
  • Article IV–VII — States relations, amendment process, supremacy, and ratification rules.

Clauses to know

The Commerce Clause (Art. I, §8, cl. 3) has been the constitutional basis for much federal regulation; the Necessary & Proper Clause enables Congress to act through means reasonably adapted to its powers; the Supremacy Clause (Art. VI) ensures federal primacy where authorized.

Judicial review

The Constitution itself does not explicitly say "courts shall have the power of judicial review." That principle was established by the Supreme Court in Marbury v. Madison and has been central to constitutional enforcement: when a law exceeds constitutional bounds, courts may refuse to apply it.

Close-Reading Clinic (in-class)

Break into small groups. Each group receives a clause (Commerce, Necessary & Proper, Supremacy). Tasks:

  1. Paraphrase the clause in plain language.
  2. Provide one historical example of its use or controversy.
  3. List two interpretive questions judges face when applying the clause.

Primary Source Assignment (before next class)

  1. Read Articles I–III in full.
  2. Read Federalist No. 51 (Madison) and document the arguments for checks and balances.

Longer Activity — Drafting Exercise

Individually, write a 750–1,000 word memo arguing whether the Commerce Clause should permit Congress to regulate X (choose from: purely local commercial activity, internet data flows, or environmental emissions). Use textual, structural, and historical reasoning.

Rubric (100 pts): Thesis & organization (20), textual reasoning (25), use of history & precedent (25), clarity & citations (15), mechanics (15).

Quiz (summative)

  1. Explain the Necessary & Proper Clause and give one example of congressional action it has justified. (6 pts)
  2. List the three branches and give one core function of each. (6 pts)
  3. Short essay (250–350 words): How does the amendment process balance change and stability? (8 pts)
Answer guidance
  1. The clause permits Congress to use means necessary and proper to execute its enumerated powers (e.g., creation of a national bank as in McCulloch v. Maryland). Full credit requires linking to an example and brief reasoning. (6 pts)
  2. Legislative (makes law), Executive (enforces law), Judicial (interprets law). (6 pts)
  3. Rubric: clear thesis (2), institutional mechanism described (3), examples or reasoning (3). (8 pts)

The First Amendment

Text

“Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances.”

Key Freedoms Protected

  • Religion: Protects both the establishment clause (no official religion) and the free exercise clause (freedom to practice religion).
  • Speech: Guarantees the right to express ideas and opinions without government interference, with certain legal limits.
  • Press: Ensures that the media can publish information freely, including criticism of the government.
  • Assembly: Allows people to gather peacefully for protests, meetings, or rallies.
  • Petition: Enables citizens to petition the government to correct grievances or influence policies.

Historical Background

Ratified in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights, the First Amendment was influenced by Enlightenment ideas and colonial experiences under British restrictions. Its purpose was to ensure that citizens could freely express themselves, practice religion, and hold the government accountable.

Modern Relevance

Today, the First Amendment covers a wide range of rights, including public protests, religious freedoms, journalism, and online expression. Courts continually interpret its boundaries, balancing protection of free expression with public safety, hate speech, and other concerns.

Grievances in The Declaration of Independence

  1. He has refused his Assent to Laws, the most wholesome and necessary for the public good.
  2. He has forbidden his Governors to pass Laws of immediate and pressing importance.
  3. He has refused to pass other Laws for the accommodation of large districts of people.
  4. He has called together legislative bodies at unusual, uncomfortable, and distant places.
  5. He has dissolved Representative Houses repeatedly for opposing his invasions on the rights of the people.
  6. He has refused for a long time, after such dissolutions, to cause others to be elected.
  7. He has endeavored to prevent the population of these states.
  8. He has obstructed the Administration of Justice by refusing his Assent to Laws for establishing judiciary powers.
  9. He has made Judges dependent on his Will alone for the tenure of their offices and salaries.
  10. He has erected a multitude of New Offices and sent hither swarms of Officers to harass our people.
  11. He has kept among us, in times of peace, Standing Armies without the Consent of our legislatures.
  12. He has affected to render the Military independent of and superior to the Civil power.
  13. He has combined with others to subject us to a jurisdiction foreign to our constitution.
  14. For protecting them, by a mock Trial, from punishment for any murders committed on the inhabitants of these states.
  15. For cutting off our Trade with all parts of the world.
  16. For imposing Taxes on us without our Consent.
  17. For depriving us, in many cases, of the benefits of Trial by Jury.
  18. For transporting us beyond Seas to be tried for pretended offenses.
  19. For abolishing the free System of English Laws in neighboring provinces, establishing arbitrary government.
  20. For taking away our Charters, abolishing our most valuable Laws, and altering fundamentally the Forms of our Governments.
  21. For suspending our own Legislatures and declaring themselves invested with power to legislate for us in all cases whatsoever.
  22. He has abdicated Government here, by declaring us out of his Protection and waging War against us.
  23. He has plundered our seas, ravaged our coasts, burnt our towns, and destroyed lives of our people.
  24. He is at this time transporting large Armies of foreign Mercenaries to complete works of death, desolation, and tyranny.
  25. He has constrained our fellow Citizens taken Captive on the high Seas to bear Arms against their Country.
  26. He has excited domestic insurrections amongst us.
  27. He has endeavored to bring on the inhabitants of our frontiers, the merciless Indian Savages, whose known rule of warfare is an undistinguished destruction of all ages, sexes, and conditions.

The Declaration of Independence contains 27 grievances against King George III and the British Crown that explain and justify the American colonies' bid for separation. The complaints served to rally colonists and persuade foreign powers that their revolution was legitimate. The grievances can be grouped into at least four major themes. "Political abuse" is famously singularized as "taxation without representation" but was much more. "Military abuse" would be called "police brutality" today as the army was operating as a police force during peacetime. Many "economic and trade restrictions" existed but The Stamp Act and Boston Port Act were intensely despised as they were dramatic shows of force by the British Crown at levels previously unseen before. The last straw was "acts of war". This differed from peacetime abuse as Britain was using its navy to blockade the Boston Harbor and using its armies to literally burn towns to the ground. Colonists had enough and talk of rebellion was growing serious.

Political and legislative overreach

  • Refusing necessary laws: The King and his royal governors repeatedly refused to approve laws that were considered wholesome and necessary for the public good of the colonies.
  • Dissolving representative houses: Colonial legislatures were repeatedly dissolved for opposing British "invasions on the rights of the people".
  • Obstructing justice: The King obstructed the administration of justice by interfering with colonial court systems and making judges dependent on his will for their salaries and tenure.
  • Taxation without representation: This was one of the most prominent complaints, accusing the Crown of imposing taxes on the colonists without their consent or representation in Parliament.
  • Suspending legislatures: The King and Parliament suspended colonial legislatures and claimed the power to legislate for the colonies in all cases whatsoever.

Military oppression

  • Standing armies in peacetime: The King kept standing armies in the colonies during peacetime without the consent of the colonial legislatures, and made the military superior to civil power.
  • Forced quartering of troops: The Quartering Act forced colonists to provide housing and supplies for British soldiers.
  • Mock trials for murders: The King provided a form of legal protection to soldiers who committed murder against colonists, protecting them from punishment through "mock Trials".
  • Using mercenaries: The King used foreign mercenaries, specifically German Hessian soldiers, to wage war against the colonists.

Economic and trade restrictions

  • Cutting off trade: British laws, like the Navigation Acts and the Boston Port Act, restricted or cut off colonial trade with other parts of the world.
  • Immigration restrictions: The King discouraged the population of the colonies by obstructing naturalization laws for foreigners and making land acquisition more difficult.

Acts of war and cruelty

  • Waging war against colonists: The Declaration accused the King of having "abdicated Government here, by declaring us out of his Protection and waging War against us".
  • Coastal attacks: The British navy and military forces plundered colonial seas, ravaged coastlines, and burned towns.
  • Impressment: The Crown forced captured American sailors into the British Navy to fight against their fellow colonists.
  • Inciting conflict: The King was accused of "exciting domestic insurrections amongst us" and encouraging Native Americans to attack colonists on the frontier.

Proof Of Each Accusation.

  1. "He has refused his Assent to Laws, the most wholesome and necessary for the public good."

    Proof: King George III vetoed or delayed colonial laws, like some local bills passed by assemblies, asserting royal prerogative.

  2. "He has forbidden his Governors to pass Laws of immediate and pressing importance."

    Proof: Governors appointed by the Crown, under his authority, sometimes refused to approve laws addressing taxation, infrastructure, or militia needs."

  3. "He has refused to pass other Laws for the accommodation of large districts of people."

    Proof: Colonial charters often required royal approval to expand courts or local governments; George III’s administration sometimes ignored petitions for such local accommodations.

  4. "He has called together legislative bodies at unusual, uncomfortable, and distant places."

    Proof: The governor of Massachusetts, under royal instruction, sometimes called assemblies to inconvenient locations to minimize attendance or protest.

  5. "He has dissolved Representative Houses repeatedly for opposing his invasions on the rights of the people."

    Proof: Massachusetts’ assembly was dissolved multiple times (1768–1774) when it protested taxation and quartering of troops.

  6. "He has refused for a long time, after such dissolutions, to cause others to be elected."

    Proof: Following the dissolution of assemblies in Massachusetts and New York, elections were delayed, leaving colonists without representation.

  7. "He has endeavored to prevent the population of these states."

    Proof: The Proclamation of 1763 restricted westward settlement past the Appalachian Mountains, limiting expansion.

  8. "He has obstructed the Administration of Justice by refusing his Assent to Laws for establishing judiciary powers."

    Proof: Colonial courts required royal approval for reorganizations; George III’s administration sometimes blocked such laws, limiting local justice.

  9. "He has made Judges dependent on his Will alone for the tenure of their offices and salaries."

    Proof: Judges in royal colonies were appointed by the Crown and could be removed or had salaries controlled by royal officials, undermining judicial independence.

  10. "He has erected a multitude of New Offices and sent hither swarms of Officers to harass our people."

    Proof: The Sugar Act (1764) and Stamp Act (1765) established new tax collectors and customs officials, seen as intrusive.

  11. "He has kept among us, in times of peace, Standing Armies without the Consent of our legislatures."

    Proof: British troops were stationed in colonies after the French and Indian War; the Quartering Act (1765) required colonists to house them even in peacetime.

  12. "He has affected to render the Military independent of and superior to the Civil power."

    Proof: Military governors and officers sometimes overruled colonial courts; enforcement of the Quartering Act gave troops authority over civilians.

  13. "He has combined with others to subject us to a jurisdiction foreign to our constitution."

    Proof: The Quebec Act (1774) extended Catholic rule and British law in Canada and the Ohio Valley, affecting colonies’ claims to territory.

  14. "For protecting them, by a mock Trial, from punishment for any murders committed on the inhabitants of these states."

    Proof: British soldiers involved in the Boston Massacre (1770) were tried in British courts with sympathetic judges; only two minor convictions resulted, fueling outrage.

  15. "For cutting off our Trade with all parts of the world."

    Proof: Navigation Acts and trade restrictions limited colonies to trading through Britain; non-compliance led to seizures of ships.

  16. "For imposing Taxes on us without our Consent."

    Proof: Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Tea Act (1773) imposed taxes without colonial legislatures’ consent—violating the principle of “no taxation without representation.”

  17. "For depriving us, in many cases, of the benefits of Trial by Jury."

    Proof: Admiralty courts tried smugglers without juries; British-appointed judges decided verdicts, bypassing colonial legal norms.

  18. "For transporting us beyond Seas to be tried for pretended offenses."

    Proof: Accused smugglers or dissenters were sent to England for trial in Admiralty courts, removing them from local juries.

  19. "For abolishing the free System of English Laws in neighboring provinces, establishing arbitrary government."

    Proof: Quebec Act (1774) replaced English common law with French civil law and allowed appointed councils, seen as arbitrary governance.

  20. "For taking away our Charters, abolishing our most valuable Laws, and altering fundamentally the Forms of our Governments."

    Proof: Massachusetts’ charter was annulled by the Intolerable Acts (1774), giving the governor broad power to rule by decree.

  21. "For suspending our own Legislatures and declaring themselves invested with power to legislate for us in all cases whatsoever."

    Proof: Parliament, with George III’s approval, passed laws like the Declaratory Act (1766), asserting full legislative authority over colonies.

  22. "He has abdicated Government here, by declaring us out of his Protection and waging War against us."

    Proof: Proclamation of rebellion (1775) and sending troops to suppress uprisings effectively placed colonies outside royal protection.

  23. "He has plundered our seas, ravaged our coasts, burnt our towns, and destroyed lives of our people."

    Proof: British military actions during the Revolutionary War, including raids in Massachusetts and New Jersey, caused destruction of property and civilian lives.

  24. "He is at this time transporting large Armies of foreign Mercenaries to complete works of death, desolation, and tyranny."

    Proof: The Crown hired Hessian soldiers from Germany to fight in the colonies (1776–1783).

  25. "He has constrained our fellow Citizens taken Captive on the high Seas to bear Arms against their Country."

    Proof: Captured American sailors were forced to fight for Britain or serve on British ships.

  26. "He has excited domestic insurrections amongst us."

    Proof: The Crown encouraged Native American attacks against frontier settlements; some loyalist militias were also armed against rebels.

  27. "He has endeavored to bring on the inhabitants of our frontiers, the merciless Indian Savages, whose known rule of warfare is an undistinguished destruction of all ages, sexes, and conditions."

    Proof: British alliances with Native American tribes led to raids on frontier towns in Pennsylvania and New York.

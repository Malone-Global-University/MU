Thirteenth Amendment (1865)

Ratified 1865 ‚Äî Abolishment of slavery and involuntary servitude

The Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution formally abolished slavery and involuntary servitude, except as punishment for a crime. It was passed by Congress on January 31, 1865, and ratified by the states on December 6, 1865, marking the legal end of slavery in the United States after more than two centuries of enslavement.

This amendment fulfilled the moral and political promise of the Civil War and the Emancipation Proclamation. While President Abraham Lincoln‚Äôs Emancipation Proclamation of 1863 had freed slaves in the Confederate states, it was a wartime measure and did not permanently outlaw slavery nationwide. The Thirteenth Amendment made abolition permanent, binding, and constitutional.

Purpose and Historical Context

The Thirteenth Amendment emerged directly from the Civil War and the nation‚Äôs struggle to reconcile liberty with equality. For decades, the Constitution had tolerated slavery through compromises that divided North and South. The Civil War exposed that contradiction, forcing the nation to choose between preserving slavery or fulfilling its founding ideals.

Its passage required immense political will, particularly in the House of Representatives, where it narrowly succeeded after passionate debate. The amendment not only ended slavery but also provided constitutional authority for future civil rights legislation, laying the foundation for the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments that followed during Reconstruction.

Background

The Articles of Confederation were the first constitution of the United States, in effect from 1781 to 1789. Drafted during the Revolutionary War, they reflected the coloniesâ€™ desire for independence and their fear of a strong central government.

Structure of Government

The Articles established a confederation, a loose alliance of sovereign states:

  • Legislature: One-house Congress with one vote per state.
  • No executive branch: No president with real power.
  • No national judiciary: No federal courts to enforce laws.
  • State sovereignty: States retained most powers.

Powers of Congress

Congress had limited powers, including:

  • Conducting foreign affairs and treaties
  • Declaring war and making peace
  • Operating a postal service
  • Borrowing funds and requesting contributions from states
  • Maintaining an army and navy (with state cooperation)

However, Congress could not tax citizens directly, regulate commerce, or enforce laws on states.

Weaknesses

  • Financial weakness: Congress had no power to tax.
  • Interstate disputes: States could impose tariffs on each other.
  • No enforcement power: Laws were weak without state compliance.
  • Amendments required unanimity: Nearly impossible to change.
  • Military limitations: Dependence on state approval for troops.

Achievements

  • Coordinated the Revolutionary War effort.
  • Northwest Ordinance of 1787: provided rules for new territories and prohibited slavery in them.
  • Established a precedent for interstate cooperation.

Early Life

Born in 1742 in Scotland, James Wilson emigrated to Pennsylvania and became a lawyer and professor. He played a key role in shaping American legal and constitutional thought.

Political Career

Wilson was a delegate to the Continental Congress, a signer of the Declaration of Independence, and a key contributor to the drafting of the Constitution. Later, he became one of the first Associate Justices of the U.S. Supreme Court.

Political Philosophy

Wilson advocated for a strong federal government, popular sovereignty, and the rule of law. He believed that the people were the ultimate source of authority and emphasized constitutional frameworks to protect rights and balance powers.

Rivalries

Wilson vs. Anti-Federalists: Defended ratification of the Constitution against critics
Wilson vs. Other Delegates: Debated over representation, legislative structure, and electoral processes

Legacy

James Wilson is remembered as a foundational legal thinker, advocate for constitutional governance, and influential voice in establishing the principles of American democracy.

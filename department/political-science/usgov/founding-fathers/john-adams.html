Early Life

John Adams was born in 1735 in Braintree, Massachusetts. Educated at Harvard, he became a teacher, lawyer, and early advocate of colonial rights. His legal defense of the British soldiers after the Boston Massacre demonstrated his belief in justice above politics.

Political Career

Adams played a crucial role in the Continental Congress, pushing strongly for independence. He served as a diplomat in Europe, Vice President under George Washington, and second President of the United States. His presidency is remembered for the XYZ Affair, the strengthening of the Navy, and the controversial Alien & Sedition Acts.

Political Philosophy

A committed Federalist, Adams believed in a strong centralized government, checks and balances, and the dangers of pure democracy. He distrusted factionalism and partisanship but found himself caught in the intense political battles of the 1790s.

Rivalries

Adams vs. Jefferson: A deep political rivalry turned friendship. Their conflict defined the election of 1800, one of the first peaceful transfers of power in modern history.
Adams vs. Hamilton: Adams saw Hamilton as dangerously ambitious; Hamilton saw Adams as unstable and overly independent.

Legacy

Adams is remembered as a principled lawyer, an uncompromising advocate of independence, and a defender of republican government. His correspondence with Jefferson later in life is one of the great intellectual exchanges in American history.

Early Life

Born in 1721 in Newton, Massachusetts, Roger Sherman was a self-educated lawyer and merchant. He became a leading political figure in Connecticut and a respected voice in colonial and early American governance.

Political Career

Sherman served in the Continental Congress and was the only person to sign all four major U.S. founding documents: the Continental Association, the Declaration of Independence, the Articles of Confederation, and the Constitution. He played a central role in drafting the Connecticut Compromise, which shaped the legislative structure of Congress.

Political Philosophy

Sherman believed in practical governance, compromise, and protecting the interests of smaller states. He emphasized balance between federal authority and state sovereignty, advocating for a stable and functional government framework.

Rivalries

Sherman vs. Radical Delegates: Occasionally disagreed on the pace and scope of constitutional reform.
Sherman vs. Anti-Federalists: Advocated compromise to ensure ratification while addressing concerns of smaller states.

Legacy

Roger Sherman is remembered as a skilled negotiator and pragmatic statesman whose efforts ensured a functional and enduring U.S. Constitution. His dedication to compromise helped prevent the collapse of the fledgling nation.

Founding Fathers: Beliefs, Divisions, and Political Evolution

This page provides a scholarly overview of the major Founding Fathers, their initial political beliefs, how their views evolved over time, the critical events that caused divisions among them, and the key political outcomes that shaped the early United States.

Major Founding Fathers

  • George Washington
  • Alexander Hamilton
  • Thomas Jefferson
  • James Madison
  • John Adams
  • Benjamin Franklin
  • Thomas Paine
  • Patrick Henry

Founders and Their Initial Beliefs

Founder Initial Beliefs / Political Orientation Key Interests
George Washington Believed in strong but restrained central authority; wary of political parties. National unity, military stability, orderly governance.
Alexander Hamilton Strong central government; nationalist; pro‑commerce and finance. National bank, industrial economy, federal taxation.
Thomas Jefferson Weak central government; agrarian republicanism; states’ rights. Agriculture, local governance, civil liberties.
James Madison Originally nationalist; co‑author of Federalist Papers; later shifted toward states’ rights. Checks and balances, legislative power, wariness of factionalism.
John Adams Strong executive; mixed republican system; skepticism of popular democracy. Rule of law, stable institutions, elite governance.
Benjamin Franklin Liberal republicanism; civic virtue; diplomacy-driven federalism. Compromise, enlightenment rationalism, education.
Patrick Henry Anti‑Federalist; strong advocate of state sovereignty. Individual liberty, local power, distrust of centralized authority.

How Their Positions Changed Over Time

Several key political figures experienced major ideological shifts as the new nation faced internal and external challenges:

  • James Madison: Shifted from Federalist‑leaning nationalist to a leading Jeffersonian Republican as he opposed Hamilton’s centralized economic programs.
  • Thomas Jefferson: Became more pragmatic as president—supporting the Louisiana Purchase and limited use of federal power despite earlier strict‑construction views.
  • George Washington: Became increasingly aligned with Federalist thinking due to political instability, foreign threats, and internal rebellions.
  • John Adams: Became more authoritarian (Alien & Sedition Acts) under fear of foreign subversion, contributing to the fall of the Federalist Party.

Critical Events That Caused Division

  • The Constitutional Convention (1787): Split Federalists and Anti‑Federalists on how strong the national government should be.
  • Hamilton’s Financial Plan (1790–1791): Divided Jefferson and Madison from Hamilton over assumption of state debts, the national bank, and excise taxes.
  • Shays’ Rebellion: Encouraged Federalists to call for stronger federal authority; Anti‑Federalists feared potential tyranny.
  • Whiskey Rebellion: Split Jeffersonians and Federalists on whether military force against citizens was legitimate.
  • French Revolution: Jeffersonians supported France; Federalists favored Britain for stability and trade.
  • Jay’s Treaty (1795): Deepened suspicion of Federalist ties to Britain.
  • Alien & Sedition Acts (1798): Caused national backlash and solidified Republican opposition to Federalist authoritarianism.

Key Political Aftermath

  • The emergence of the First Party System: Federalists vs. Democratic‑Republicans.
  • The transformation of Madison and Jefferson into leaders of an organized political party.
  • Washington’s Farewell Address warning against partisan divisions—ironically at a time when political parties were already forming.
  • The eventual collapse of the Federalist Party after 1800 due to unpopular policies and limited rural support.
  • The Democratic‑Republicans’ long-term dominance, culminating in the "Era of Good Feelings" after the War of 1812.
  • Ongoing debates over federal power, states’ rights, and economic policy that continued into the 19th century and shaped American political identity.

Early Life & Background

Born in 1732 in Talbot County, Maryland, John Dickinson was a lawyer, politician, and colonial writer. He studied at the Middle Temple in London and became a leading voice for colonial rights before the Revolution.

Political Career

Dickinson represented Pennsylvania and Delaware in the Continental Congress. Political Philosophy

Dickinson believed in constitutional restraint, compromise, and cautious approaches to independence. He valued law, civic virtue, and unity among the colonies. Though he initially opposed immediate independence, he later supported the Revolutionary cause and the establishment of the new nation.

How His Beliefs Changed Over Time

Initially hesitant about declaring independence, Dickinson gradually embraced revolutionary principles as British policies grew more oppressive. His legal expertise guided his drafting of the Articles of Confederation, emphasizing a balance of state and federal power.

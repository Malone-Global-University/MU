What is Federalism?

Federalism is a system of government in which power is divided between a national (federal) government and state (or regional) governments. The U.S. Constitution intentionally creates this division to balance unity and local autonomy.

Constitutional Foundation

Several parts of the Constitution establish and limit federal and state authority:

  • Article I, Section 8 â€” enumerates powers of Congress (e.g., regulate commerce, declare war).
  • Supremacy Clause (Article VI) â€” declares the Constitution and federal laws the "supreme law of the land" when conflicts arise.
  • Tenth Amendment â€” reserves powers not delegated to the federal government to the states or the people.

Three Types of Powers

The Constitution and practice create three useful categories:

Enumerated (Delegated) Federal government Coin money, regulate interstate commerce, maintain armed forces

Examples & Case Studies

Federalism produces both cooperation and conflict. Real-world examples include:

  • Marijuana legalization â€” many states legalized medical or recreational marijuana while federal law still outlaws it; this creates legal and enforcement conflicts.
  • Civil Rights â€” when states enforced segregation, the federal government (and courts) intervened (e.g., Brown v. Board of Education).
  • Health Policy â€” disputes over federal programs such as the Affordable Care Act show how federal initiatives interact with state authority.
  • Emergency Response â€” during national emergencies (like pandemics or hurricanes), federal, state, and local governments must coordinate responses.

Challenges & Tensions

Federalism creates practical challenges:

  • Conflicting laws: when state and federal laws disagree, courts may decide which applies.
  • Uneven policy: citizens' rights and services can vary significantly by state.
  • Resource gaps: poorer states may struggle to provide services states with greater resources can offer.
  • Political friction: federal incentives or mandates can be seen as coercive by states.

Key Takeaway

Federalism balances unity and diversity. It divides power so that a single national government can handle countrywide needs while state governments can respond to local preferences. The result is a dynamic system of shared authority that has evolved through history and will continue to change as new issues arise.

Course overview

This course introduces the central concepts and questions of political life: what power is, how institutions shape behaviour, why people form political identities, and how public policy is made. We combine history, theory, and contemporary examples so students gain both conceptual tools and practical insight.

Who it's for

Anyone curious about how societies make collective decisions â€” from high-school students to lifelong learners and civic leaders.

Course format

Modular lessons with short video lectures, primary-source readings, guided discussions, and applied assignments.

6â€“8 weeks (self-paced). Each module averages 2â€“4 hours of study, with optional deeper readings and projects.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain core political concepts: state, sovereignty, legitimacy, authority, and power.
  • Analyze how institutions structure incentives and shape outcomes.
  • Identify the psychological roots of political behaviour and identity.
  • Evaluate competing policy arguments using evidence and logic.
  • Apply political thinking to everyday life â€” from civic participation to community organizing.

Modules

  1. What is politics? Definitions, everyday politics, and "politics as lifestyle."
  2. Power & authority. Types of power, sources of legitimacy, and leadership.
  3. Institutions & rules. How constitutions, laws, and organizations shape behaviour.
  4. Identity & ideology. In-groups, out-groups, and political narratives.
  5. Policy-making. From problem definition to implementation and evaluation.
  6. Civic engagement. Institutions of participation, deliberation, and activism.

Each module includes readings from classic texts, primary documents, and contemporary case studies.

Core readings (selected)

  • Excerpts from the Federalist Papers and The Declaration of Independence
  • Short selections from Aristotle's Politics
  • Modern essays on political identity and polarization
  • Primary-source case studies and landmark court opinions

Course designed by the Malone Global University Politics Department. Lessons emphasize source literacy, critical thinking, and applying political concepts to real-world problems. Instructors provide guided feedback on assignments and host periodic discussion sessions.

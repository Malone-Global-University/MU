The Three Branches

The three branches of government are the Legislative, Executive, and Judicial branches. They were created to ensure separation of powers so that no single person or group could control the entire government. Each branch has its own powers and responsibilities, but they also check and balance one another.

Main Body:

Congress (made up of the Senate and the House of Representatives)

Location:

U.S. Capitol Building

Main Powers:


  • 1. Writes, debates, and passes laws
  • 2. Approves the federal budget
  • 3. Can declare war
  • 4. Can override a presidential veto with a 2/3 vote
  • 5. Approves or rejects presidential appointments (Senate only)
  • 6. Can impeach federal officials, including the president

Example:

If Congress passes a new law about education funding, it must go to the President for approval or veto.

Head:

The President of the United States

Main Bodies:

The Vice President, Cabinet, and federal agencies (like the Department of Defense, the FBI, etc.)

Location:

The White House

Main Powers:


  • 1. Enforces and carries out laws
  • 2. Commander-in-Chief of the military
  • 3. Signs or vetoes laws from Congress
  • 4. Appoints federal judges, ambassadors, and Cabinet members
  • 5. Conducts foreign policy and makes treaties (with Senate approval)

The Supreme Court of the United States

  • Interprets laws and the Constitution
  • Decides if laws or executive actions are constitutional (called judicial review)
  • Settles disputes between states or citizens and the government

Example:

If a law violates the Constitution, the Supreme Court can strike it down as unconstitutional.

Abu Ghraib Prison Scandal (2004)

Exposure of prisoner abuse by U.S. military personnel during the Iraq War, raising ethical and legal questions worldwide.

Background

During the U.S. occupation of Iraq, reports emerged of widespread abuse and torture of prisoners at Abu Ghraib prison. Photographs and testimonies revealed acts of physical and psychological mistreatment carried out by U.S. military personnel against detainees.

Events

In 2004, investigative journalism and the release of photographs documented the abuses. Military investigations led to the court-martial of several soldiers, while senior officials faced criticism for failure to enforce proper oversight. The scandal fueled international condemnation and protests, damaging the U.S.'s global reputation.

Impact & Legacy

Abu Ghraib became a symbol of ethical lapses in wartime conduct. It prompted military reforms, increased scrutiny of detention practices, and reinforced the need for adherence to international human rights laws. The scandal also ignited debates about accountability within hierarchical institutions like the military.

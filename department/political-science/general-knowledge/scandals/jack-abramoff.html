Overview

The Jack Abramoff lobbying scandal was a massive corruption case that revealed the dark side of Washington influence. The Scandal Unfolds

In 2004, a series of Senate investigations led by Senator John McCain uncovered that Abramoff and his associates defrauded tribes of more than $80 million. He used these funds to buy influence in Congress, pay for luxury trips, and finance political campaigns. Several lawmakers, aides, and officials were implicated in the scandal, including members of the House of Representatives and the Department of the Interior.

Legal Consequences

Abramoff pled guilty in 2006 to charges of conspiracy, mail fraud, and tax evasion. He was sentenced to nearly six years in federal prison and ordered to repay millions in restitution. His case became a symbol of systemic corruption in Washington and prompted renewed calls for lobbying reform and transparency.

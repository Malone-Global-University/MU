Early Life

Carter Godwin Woodson was born in New Canton, Virginia, in 1875, the son of former slaves. He pursued education rigorously, earning degrees from Berea College, the University of Chicago, and Harvard University.

Rise to Prominence

Woodson became a historian and educator dedicated to studying and promoting African American history. In 1915, he co-founded the Association for the Study of Negro Life and History, advocating for scholarly research on Black contributions to society.

Main Achievements

  • Founded the Journal of Negro History in 1916 to promote research on African American history.
  • Established Negro History Week in 1926, which later evolved into Black History Month.
  • Authored numerous works, including "The Mis-Education of the Negro," emphasizing the importance of education and historical awareness.

Challenges

Woodson faced systemic racism in academia, limited resources for research, and skepticism toward the study of African American history, yet he persevered to establish a lasting scholarly legacy.

Legacy

Carter G. Woodson is remembered as the "Father of Black History," whose pioneering work ensured that African American contributions are recognized and celebrated in education and culture worldwide.

Early Life

Ida Bell Wells was born into slavery in Holly Springs, Mississippi, in 1862. After emancipation, she pursued education and became a teacher in Tennessee before dedicating herself to activism and journalism.

Rise to Prominence

Wells gained national recognition in the 1890s for her investigative journalism exposing lynching in the American South. She co-founded civil rights organizations and fought tirelessly for African American equality.

Challenges

Wells faced threats, harassment, and violence for her journalism and activism, often challenging deeply entrenched racial and gender injustices in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Legacy

Ida B. Wells is remembered as a fearless journalist and activist whose work laid the foundation for modern civil rights movements and advocacy against racial violence and injustice.

Early Life

Born Malcolm Little in Omaha, Nebraska, in 1925, he experienced early family hardship and systemic racism. He spent part of his youth in foster care and became involved in petty crime, leading to imprisonment in the 1940s.

Rise to Prominence

While in prison, Malcolm converted to the Nation of Islam and adopted the name Malcolm X. He became a prominent spokesperson for Black empowerment and self-determination.

Main Achievements

  • National spokesperson for the Nation of Islam, advocating for Black pride and self-reliance.
  • Founded Muslim Mosque, Inc. and the Organization of Afro-American Unity after leaving the Nation of Islam.
  • Influenced the civil rights movement with calls for self-defense and global Black solidarity.

Challenges

Malcolm faced constant threats, surveillance, and ultimately assassination in 1965. He was criticized by some contemporaries for his militant stance but admired for his uncompromising advocacy.

Legacy

Malcolm X remains a symbol of Black empowerment, human rights, and international Pan-African thought, inspiring activists worldwide.

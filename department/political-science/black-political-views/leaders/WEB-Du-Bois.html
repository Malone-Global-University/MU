Early Life

Born in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, in 1868, Du Bois grew up in a relatively tolerant northern community. He excelled academically, attending Harvard and later studying in Europe.

Rise to Prominence

Du Bois earned a Ph.D. at Harvard and later at the University of Berlin. He became a leading intellectual voice advocating for African-American civil rights and higher education.

Main Achievements

  • Co-founder of the NAACP in 1909.
  • Author of "The Souls of Black Folk" and influential sociological studies.
  • Promoted the "Talented Tenth" theory to develop African-American leadership.

Challenges

He frequently clashed with Booker T. Washington over strategies for racial uplift and faced political opposition due to his internationalism and socialist leanings.

Legacy

Du Bois shaped African-American intellectual thought and civil rights activism, influencing generations of leaders worldwide.

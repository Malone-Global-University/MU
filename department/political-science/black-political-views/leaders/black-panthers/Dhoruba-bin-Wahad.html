Dhoruba bin Wahad

Black Panther Party Leader, Panther 21 Defendant, and Political Prisoner (1944– )

Home › Political Science › Black Political Views › African Leader › Dhoruba bin Wahad

Early Life

Dhoruba bin Wahad was born Richard Earl Moore on June 30, 1944, in New York City. He grew up in Harlem and the Bronx, where he was exposed to the political and social conditions that shaped his later involvement in Black liberation movements. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Entry into the Black Panther Party

Bin Wahad joined the Black Panther Party in 1968 during a period of national unrest following the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. He quickly rose within the organization, becoming a leading member of the New York chapter and serving as Field Secretary responsible for organizing across the East Coast. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The Panther 21 Case

In April 1969, bin Wahad was arrested along with twenty other members of the New York Black Panther Party in what became known as the “Panther 21” case. They were charged with conspiracy related to alleged attacks on police stations and public infrastructure. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

After a lengthy and highly publicized trial, all defendants were acquitted of the charges in 1971, highlighting weaknesses in the prosecution’s case and raising broader questions about the targeting of political organizations. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Arrest, Conviction, and Imprisonment

Shortly after the Panther 21 acquittal, bin Wahad was arrested again in 1971 and later convicted in a separate case involving the attempted murder of two New York City police officers. He maintained his innocence throughout the proceedings. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

He spent approximately 19 years in prison. His conviction was ultimately vacated after it was determined that prosecutors had withheld critical evidence and that aspects of the case were linked to FBI COINTELPRO operations targeting Black political organizations. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Release and Later Activism

Following his release, bin Wahad became active in advocacy related to political prisoners and prison reform. He co-founded initiatives focused on securing the release of individuals he identified as political prisoners and worked internationally, including in Ghana, on Pan-African political development. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Political Positioning and Influence

Dhoruba bin Wahad represents a critical intersection of movement leadership and state response. His experience reflects how political organizers can become targets of intelligence and legal systems, particularly during periods of heightened political conflict.

Historical Significance

Bin Wahad’s case is frequently cited in discussions of COINTELPRO, legal misconduct, and the treatment of political activists. His imprisonment and subsequent release contributed to broader awareness of the relationship between political movements and state enforcement mechanisms.

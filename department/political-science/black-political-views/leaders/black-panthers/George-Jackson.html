George Jackson

Black Panther Party Field Marshal, Revolutionary Author, and Prison Activist (1941–1971)

Home › Political Science › Black Political Views › African Leader › George Jackson

Early Life

George Lester Jackson was born on September 23, 1941, in Chicago, Illinois. At age 18, he was convicted of armed robbery involving a small amount of money and sentenced to an indeterminate prison term of one year to life, which resulted in extended incarceration. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Political Development in Prison

While incarcerated in California prisons including San Quentin and Soledad, Jackson underwent a major political transformation. He studied revolutionary theory, including Marxism, Leninism, and anti-colonial thought, and became active in organizing other prisoners. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

During this period, he became associated with the Black Panther Party and prison-based organizing efforts, contributing to political education and collective resistance among inmates. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Field Marshal and Organizational Role

Jackson was designated a Field Marshal of the Black Panther Party while incarcerated, reflecting his influence within prison-based political organizing. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

He played a central role in developing prisoner consciousness and organizing structures within the prison system, extending the movement’s reach into incarcerated populations.

The Soledad Brothers Case

In 1970, Jackson became one of the “Soledad Brothers,” accused of killing a prison guard following the shooting deaths of three Black inmates by another guard. The case drew national attention and became a focal point for debates about prison conditions and justice. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Writings and Intellectual Influence

Jackson authored Soledad Brother, a collection of prison letters that combined autobiography and political analysis. The book became widely read and brought international attention to his ideas and experiences. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

He later completed Blood in My Eye, a work of political philosophy finalized shortly before his death. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Death at San Quentin

On August 21, 1971, Jackson was killed by prison guards at San Quentin State Prison during what officials described as an escape attempt. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Accounts of the incident have been widely debated, with differing interpretations from authorities, activists, and observers regarding the circumstances of his death. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Connection to the Attica Uprising

Jackson’s death became a major catalyst for unrest within the prison system. His killing was cited as one of the events that contributed to the Attica Prison Uprising in September 1971, where incarcerated individuals protested conditions and demanded reforms. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Political Positioning and Influence

George Jackson represents the prison resistance layer within the Black Panther framework. His work demonstrates how political movements extend into carceral systems, transforming prisons into sites of ideological development, organization, and resistance.

Historical Significance

Jackson’s life and writings had a lasting impact on discussions of prison conditions, political imprisonment, and systemic inequality. His legacy continues to influence movements focused on incarceration, justice reform, and political resistance.

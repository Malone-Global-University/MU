When did the transatlantic slave trade begin in America?

A concise overview and timeline explaining the beginnings of the transatlantic slave trade globally and its arrival in English North America.

Short answer

The transatlantic slave trade began globally in the mid-1400s (with European powers transporting enslaved Africans), and it began in English North America in 1619 when a group of Africans were brought to the English colony of Virginia (Point Comfort, present-day Hampton, Virginia).

Timeline & key points

Mid-1400s — European beginnings

Portugal and Spain initiated large-scale maritime exploration and began transporting enslaved Africans to Europe and later to their colonies in the Atlantic. This is the origin of the transatlantic slave trade as a system linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas.

1500s — Expansion in Spanish & Portuguese colonies

Spain and Portugal expanded the trade across their American colonies, with heavy demand in places such as Brazil and the Caribbean to supply labor for plantations and mining.

1619 — First recorded arrival in English North America

In August 1619, about 20 Africans were landed at Point Comfort (now Hampton, Virginia). They were captured by English privateers from a Portuguese slave ship and sold to colonists. This event is widely cited as the moment African slavery began in the English colonies that would become the United States.

17th–19th centuries — Growth of the trade and slavery

Over the following centuries the transatlantic slave trade expanded dramatically. Scholars estimate that more than 12 million Africans were forcibly transported across the Atlantic; many millions more suffered through the broader systems of coerced labor and racial slavery in the Americas.

African Diaspora Politics 101

Migration • Empire • Citizenship • Colonialism • Nationalism • Transnational Movements

A study hub for African-descended political communities across migration, empire, citizenship, colonial rule, national liberation, and global Black political movements.

Introduction

African Diaspora Politics 101 studies African-descended political communities across the Americas, the Caribbean, Europe, Africa, and the wider world. The course examines how Black populations formed political identities under slavery, empire, colonialism, migration, segregation, citizenship struggles, anti-colonial movements, and modern transnational organizing.

The course treats the African diaspora as a political field shaped by displacement, survival, memory, state power, resistance, sovereignty claims, cultural identity, and cross-border solidarity. It connects local political struggles to global systems of trade, empire, law, citizenship, racial hierarchy, liberation, and self-determination.

Key Topics

  • African-descended communities across the Americas, Caribbean, Europe, and Africa
  • Migration, forced displacement, exile, return, and settlement
  • Empire, colonial rule, racial hierarchy, citizenship, and legal status
  • Slavery, emancipation, abolition, reconstruction, and post-emancipation politics
  • Pan-Africanism, Black internationalism, nationalism, and liberation movements
  • Anti-colonial struggle, decolonization, sovereignty, and political independence
  • Transnational movements, diaspora institutions, reparative justice, and global Black political strategy

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the historical formation of African diaspora political communities
  • Explain how slavery, empire, migration, colonialism, and citizenship shaped Black political life
  • Compare African American, Caribbean, African, Latin American, and European diaspora political experiences
  • Analyze Pan-Africanism, Black internationalism, nationalism, and anti-colonial movements as political traditions
  • Evaluate contemporary diaspora politics through questions of power, sovereignty, identity, justice, and transnational coordination

Format

This is a self-paced online course. Readings, essays, source documents, timelines, discussion prompts, and political analysis activities may be used to support independent study. No prior coursework is required, though familiarity with African American politics, world history, colonial history, or political theory may help students move through the material more quickly.

Units

These unit links organize African Diaspora Politics 101 into structured study paths. Units move from origins and forced migration to empire, citizenship, colonialism, nationalism, liberation politics, and modern transnational movements.

Foundations of African Diaspora Politics

An introductory unit on the meaning of diaspora, African-descended political identity, historical displacement, memory, political community, and the major questions that define diaspora political study.

Slavery, Migration, and Political Formation

A unit on forced migration, the transatlantic slave trade, settlement, maroonage, emancipation, abolition, and the formation of Black political communities under systems of domination and resistance.

Empire, Colonialism, and Citizenship

A study of imperial rule, colonial administration, racial law, citizenship status, subjecthood, labor systems, rights claims, and the political consequences of living inside empires.

Pan-Africanism and Black Internationalism

A unit on cross-border Black political thought, Pan-African congresses, global race consciousness, international solidarity, anti-imperial argument, and political coordination across the diaspora.

Nationalism, Liberation, and Self-Determination

A unit on anti-colonial movements, independence struggles, Black nationalism, sovereignty, cultural self-definition, revolutionary politics, and the demand for political control.

Contemporary Diaspora Politics and Transnational Movements

An advanced unit on modern migration, dual belonging, diaspora lobbying, reparative justice, global protest, international institutions, digital organizing, and twenty-first-century Black political strategy.

Essays & Documents

These essays and documents support independent reading, political analysis, and source-based study. They may include interpretive essays, historical summaries, declarations, movement documents, timelines, comparative studies, and issue-focused pages.

Diaspora Political Traditions

An overview of major political traditions across African-descended communities, including citizenship struggle, nationalism, Pan-Africanism, internationalism, anti-colonialism, and reparative justice.

Migration and Memory

Essays on forced migration, voluntary migration, exile, return, ancestral memory, identity formation, and the political meaning of displacement.

Empire and Colonial Rule

Historical and political essays on imperial governance, colonial law, racial administration, labor extraction, subjecthood, citizenship, and the struggle against colonial domination.

Pan-African Leaders and Movements

Essays on major leaders, organizations, congresses, speeches, publications, and institutions that shaped Black international political thought and action.

Liberation and Sovereignty

Essays on anti-colonial struggle, independence movements, national liberation, revolutionary politics, community control, and claims to self-determination.

Reparations and Global Political Justice

A source-based collection on reparations, historical injury, institutional responsibility, colonial extraction, slavery, public memory, and political demands for remedy.

Transnational Black Politics

Essays on diaspora lobbying, global protest networks, international law, digital movements, cultural diplomacy, and cross-border political coordination.

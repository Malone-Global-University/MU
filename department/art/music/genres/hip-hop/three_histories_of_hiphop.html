Three Parallel Histories

HIP-HOP

History One

The Living Culture

DJing, MCing, breakdancing & graffiti — the four pillars

1973

The founding night

On August 11, 1973, DJ Kool Herc's sister Cindy threw a back-to-school party in the rec room of 1520 Sedgwick Avenue in the South Bronx. Herc — born Clive Campbell in Jamaica — extended the percussion breaks of funk and soul records by switching between two copies of the same album. This "merry-go-round" technique gave dancers the break they craved indefinitely. The block party was a survival art form: the South Bronx was being burned for insurance money, youth gangs dominated the streets, and municipal budgets had gutted arts programs. Culture filled the vacuum.

1974–76

Grandmaster Flash & the science of DJing

Joseph Saddler — Grandmaster Flash — turned Herc's intuitive innovation into a precise craft. He developed the clock theory (marking records with crayon to locate breaks instantly) and invented backspinning and punch phrasing, giving DJs the ability to repeat, isolate, and emphasize sounds with surgical control. Meanwhile, Afrika Bambaataa — a former gang leader — founded the Universal Zulu Nation, channeling gang rivalries into dance battles, DJ competitions, and graffiti crews. He named the movement: hip-hop.

1975–79

B-boys & b-girls take the floor

Breaking — or b-boying — evolved from James Brown's footwork and capoeira-influenced street dancing. During the extended breaks Herc engineered, crews like the Rock Steady Crew and the Dynamic Rockers developed an acrobatic vocabulary: the top rock, the downrock, freezes, and power moves (windmills, headspins, flares). Battles replaced knife fights. The floor became the arena, and skill displaced violence as the currency of respect.

"You could be a king or a street sweeper, but everyone dances to the same beat." — Afrika Bambaataa
1971–80

Writers on the wall

Graffiti predated the Bronx scene — TAKI 183, a Greek-American teenager in Washington Heights, began tagging his name and street number across the city in 1971, inspiring a wave of imitators. By the mid-1970s, writers like LEE, DONDI, and LADY PINK had elevated tags into elaborate wildstyle pieces that covered entire subway cars. The New York City Transit Authority waged a prolonged war on graffiti, spending millions on buffing and steel-wool-resistant paint. The writers kept painting anyway.

1980–90

The MC rises above the DJ

Originally the MC's role was to keep the party going — calling out names, hyping the crowd, directing traffic. Gradually the verbal art became the spectacle. Melle Mel, Caz, and later Big Daddy Kane and Rakim transformed MCing into a discipline of meter, rhyme scheme, breath control, and metaphor. The MC's ascent was cultural before it was commercial: in ciphers and battle rap, lyrical skill was tested in real time against real opponents, with real consequences for your reputation.

1990s–now

Global diaspora

The four elements migrated globally, mutating into local idioms. French hip-hop absorbed Maghrebi immigrant experience; Japanese b-boy crews became world champions; Brazilian funk carioca built on Miami bass influences; UK grime emerged as a distinct street culture from London's council estates. In 2017, the breakdancing — now called breaking — was added to the Olympic Games program for Paris 2024, sparking fierce debate about whether institutionalization honors or betrays the culture's outlaw origins.

Sources: Jeff Chang, Can't Stop Won't Stop (2005); Jeff Chang & DJ Kool Herc, Total Chaos (2006); Martha Cooper & Henry Chalfant, Subway Art (1984).

History Two

The Recorded Music

From "Rapper's Delight" to the streaming era — rap on wax and in the cloud

1979

The record that wasn't supposed to exist

Hip-hop insiders believed the culture could not be captured on vinyl — it was a live, improvisational event. Sylvia Robinson of Sugar Hill Records proved them wrong. She recruited three men who weren't even respected b-boys — Wonder Mike, Big Bank Hank, and Master Gee, now the Sugarhill Gang — and recorded "Rapper's Delight" over a live band replicating Chic's "Good Times." The 15-minute track, released in September 1979, became an international hit. The Bronx originators were simultaneously thrilled and offended.

1982–84

Grandmaster Flash & the message

Sugar Hill's finest hour came in 1982: "The Message" by Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five was the first rap record to make the ghetto its explicit subject — not a party backdrop, but a suffocating reality.

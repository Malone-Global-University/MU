RBX

Rapper and early West Coast voice known for his deep delivery and Death Row Records era influence

Biography

RBX, born Eric Dwayne Collins in Long Beach, California, is a rapper known for his deep voice, distinctive delivery, and early contributions to the West Coast hip-hop movement. He emerged in the early 1990s and became associated with Death Row Records during its formative years, contributing to some of the label’s most influential early projects. His vocal presence stood out for its intensity and unique tone within the G-funk era soundscape.

He appeared on Dr. Dre’s landmark album The Chronic, where his performances helped define the darker, cinematic elements of early West Coast production. RBX also contributed to Snoop Dogg’s Doggystyle, further cementing his role in shaping the signature sound of the era. Although he did not always occupy the spotlight, his contributions were widely recognized by peers and producers.

Over time, RBX pursued solo projects and independent releases, maintaining a career rooted in lyrical depth and underground credibility. His work often reflects social commentary, personal perspective, and a commitment to artistic independence within hip-hop.

Legacy

RBX’s legacy is tied to the early development of Death Row Records and the foundational sound of G-funk-era West Coast hip-hop. He is respected for his unique vocal style and his contributions to landmark recordings that shaped 1990s rap. His influence remains part of the broader architecture of West Coast hip-hop history.

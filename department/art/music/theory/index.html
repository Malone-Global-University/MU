Music Theory & Composition

The building blocks of sound: rhythm, melody, harmony, form, groove, improvisation, and musical design.

Overview

Music theory is the study of how music is organized. It helps students describe what they hear, understand how songs are built, and create original work with intention.

In this department, theory is not limited to European notation or classroom rules. Students also study rhythm, groove, improvisation, oral tradition, production, sampling, call-and-response, and the ways communities teach music by listening, repeating, moving, and performing.

Rhythm, Pulse, and Groove

Rhythm is the organization of sound and silence in time. Pulse gives music a steady sense of motion. Meter groups beats into patterns. Syncopation places emphasis where listeners may not expect it. Polyrhythm layers different rhythmic patterns at the same time.

Groove is more than correct timing. It is the felt relationship among drums, bass, voice, instruments, and body movement. Blues, jazz, gospel, funk, hip-hop, house, techno, reggae, and African drumming traditions all require students to hear rhythm as physical, social, and expressive.

Melody, Harmony, and Tonal Color

Melody is a sequence of pitches shaped by rhythm, contour, repetition, variation, and expression. Harmony describes how notes sound together. Chords, scales, modes, bass movement, and tension-and-release patterns help musicians organize musical space.

African American music often uses blue notes, bent pitches, slides, melisma, vocal shouts, instrumental cries, and flexible pitch expression. These sounds remind students that theory must explain real music, not force living music into narrow categories.

Form, Arrangement, and Composition

Form is the large-scale design of a piece. A song may use verse and chorus. A blues may use a twelve-bar structure. A jazz performance may state a theme, move through solos, and return to the head. A hip-hop track may use intro, hook, verse, bridge, breakdown, and outro sections.

Composition means choosing and shaping musical ideas. Students can compose by writing notation, recording audio, building beats, arranging for voices, creating loops, improvising, or designing sound digitally. The goal is to make musical choices that serve meaning.

Improvisation, Production, and Sound Design

Improvisation is real-time composition. It appears in jazz solos, gospel runs, blues guitar responses, freestyle rap, drum circles, dance music transitions, and live performance. It requires listening, vocabulary, courage, and structure.

Modern composition also includes production. A producer may shape drum sounds, samples, bass lines, synthesizers, vocal layers, effects, and the overall sonic world of a track. For contemporary students, the studio and the laptop can function as instruments.

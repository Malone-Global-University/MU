List of African American Country Music Artists

A reference directory of major African American country music artists, organized by historical era. Each name links to a biography page.

Overview

African American musicians have been part of country music from its earliest roots. The music grew from a shared American sound world that included Black string bands, fiddle traditions, banjo performance, blues, gospel, work songs, spirituals, folk ballads, dance tunes, railroad songs, rural storytelling, and the musical exchange of the South. Country music was never formed by one community alone; it developed through overlapping Black and white traditions before the recording industry separated many artists by race and market category.

This page is organized by broad musical era rather than by birth date alone. Some artists belong to more than one period, and many also belong to blues, gospel, folk, soul, R&B, Americana, bluegrass, or rock history. Each name is placed where that artist is most strongly associated for introductory navigation. Within each era, names are alphabetized by last name, stage name, or group name.

Roots, String Band, and Folk Traditions

These artists and tradition-bearers represent the older musical foundations behind country music, including banjo, fiddle, guitar, dance tunes, ballads, folk songs, spirituals, rural blues, and string-band performance. Their work helps show how Black musicians contributed to the deep roots of country before the modern industry used separate racial labels for related traditions.

Early Recording, Country Blues, and Rural Song

Country blues, folk song, gospel, and rural dance music shaped the sound world from which country music emerged. These artists recorded or preserved guitar styles, vocal phrasing, storytelling methods, and regional musical language that influenced country, folk, bluegrass, rockabilly, and later Americana.

Classic Country and Nashville Era

The classic country and Nashville eras brought country music into radio, television, national touring, and the modern recording industry. Black country artists in this period worked against major racial barriers while contributing harmonica playing, singing, songwriting, stage performance, and crossover appeal to country music.

  • Big Al Downing
  • DeFord Bailey
  • Herb Jeffries
  • Linda Martell
  • O. B. McClinton
  • Otis Williams
  • Stoney Edwards
  • Tommy Duncan
  • Williams and Ree

Western Swing, Honky-Tonk, and Rodeo Traditions

Country music also developed through cowboy songs, Western swing, rodeo entertainment, dance bands, honky-tonk performance, and Western film. Black artists contributed to these traditions as singers, instrumentalists, entertainers, and interpreters of cowboy and frontier themes.

Soul Country and Crossover Artists

Soul and country share strong traditions of storytelling, heartbreak, faith, family, work, memory, and everyday life. These artists connected country music with soul, gospel, R&B, blues, pop, and rock, showing how country songs could move across audiences while keeping emotional directness at the center.

Bluegrass, Folk, and Americana

Bluegrass, folk, and Americana often return to the older roots of American song: banjo, fiddle, guitar, spirituals, ballads, blues, and rural storytelling. These artists have helped recover, reinterpret, and extend the Black presence in country-related acoustic traditions.

Student Performances & Research

A workspace for listening, performance, composition, cultural study, and original student inquiry.

Overview

Student work in music should include both scholarship and sound. Students can research artists, analyze recordings, write historical essays, create playlists, compose original pieces, perform music, interview community musicians, and build multimedia presentations.

This page is a project hub. It gives teachers and students a structure for turning music study into visible work that can be revised, shared, archived, and connected to the broader MGU Library.

Listening and Reflection Projects

A listening journal helps students build musical attention. For each recording, students can identify the artist, genre, year, instruments, rhythm, vocal style, mood, lyrics, historical context, and personal response.

Students should move beyond saying whether they like a song. They should learn to explain what the music is doing, how it creates meaning, and why it matters culturally or historically.

Artist, Genre, and History Research

Research projects may focus on one artist, one genre, one city, one record label, one instrument, one movement, or one period of history. Strong projects connect biography to music, and music to society.

Possible topics include the Great Migration and blues, New Orleans and jazz, gospel and civil rights, Motown and soul, Bronx block parties and hip-hop, Chicago house, Detroit techno, Afrobeats and global pop, or the role of women producers and performers in modern music.

Performance and Composition Portfolios

A performance portfolio may include rehearsal notes, recordings, lyric sheets, chord charts, set lists, reflection paragraphs, and peer feedback. The purpose is to document growth, not only final polish.

A composition portfolio may include original songs, beats, arrangements, remixes, sound designs, notation, lead sheets, and production notes. Students should explain their creative choices and identify the traditions or artists that influenced their work.

Showcases and Community Archives

Student showcases can include concerts, listening sessions, digital exhibits, research posters, podcasts, video essays, oral-history interviews, and web pages. Music becomes more powerful when students present it to a real audience.

Over time, this page can develop into an archive of student learning: performances, essays, playlists, recordings, and research projects that document how MGU students study sound, culture, and creativity.

Protest Music

Study protest music as sound used for witness, resistance, organizing, memory, criticism, solidarity, and public change.

Overview

Protest music appears when communities use music to respond to injustice, war, labor conditions, racism, poverty, censorship, state power, environmental crisis, and other public concerns.

This page belongs to the Music, Culture, and Society area of the Malone Global University Music Department. These pages should connect musical sound to people, communities, histories, institutions, media systems, memory, identity, public life, and social meaning.

Public Voice

Protest music gives public voice to grievance, hope, anger, solidarity, grief, critique, and demands for change.

Movement Use

Protest music may appear in marches, meetings, rallies, strikes, concerts, recordings, social media, classrooms, and public ceremonies.

Forms and Genres

Protest music can appear in folk, gospel, soul, punk, reggae, hip-hop, rock, country, classical, electronic, and many other traditions.

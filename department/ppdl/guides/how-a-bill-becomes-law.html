A concise, step-by-step overview of the U.S. federal lawmaking process. Use this as a cheat-sheet or classroom handout.

  • 1. Drafting & Introduction

    A member of Congress (Representative or Senator) sponsors and formally introduces a bill. Revenue bills must start in the House.

  • 2. Committee Review

    The bill goes to a relevant committee and may be sent to a subcommittee. Committees hold hearings, collect testimony, amend the bill, and decide whether to report it to the floor.

  • 3. Floor Debate & Vote (First Chamber)

    If the committee reports the bill, the full chamber debates it, may propose more amendments, then votes. A majority vote sends the bill to the other chamber.

  • 4. Committee Review & Vote (Second Chamber)

    The second chamber (House or Senate) repeats the process: committee review, possible amendments, and a floor vote.

  • 5. Reconciliation

    If the House and Senate pass different versions, a conference committee (members from both chambers) negotiates a single final text. Both chambers then vote on that final version.

  • 7. Implementation

    Once signed (or otherwise enacted), the executive branch implements and enforces the law; often agencies create regulations to carry it out.

Case Studies: Landmark Supreme Court Decisions

Selected opinions that shaped constitutional doctrine and federal power.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify landmark Supreme Court decisions and explain their legal holdings and impact.
  • Practice briefing cases: facts, procedural posture, issues, holdings, reasoning, and dissents.
  • Evaluate how precedents evolve and how later decisions can refine or overrule earlier holdings.

Selected Cases (brief)

Marbury v. Madison (1803)
Established the principle of judicial review: courts can declare federal statutes and executive acts unconstitutional.
McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)
Confirmed broad federal powers under the Necessary and Proper Clause and held that states may not tax federal institutions.
Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)
Interpreted the Commerce Clause to allow Congress substantial regulatory authority over interstate commerce.
Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
Held that state-sponsored school segregation is unconstitutional under the Equal Protection Clause, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine in public education.
Additional notable opinions
This unit will also consider decisions shaping free speech, criminal procedure, federalism, and administrative law. Students should be aware that legal doctrine evolves; some holdings have been modified by later cases.

Activities

  • Case brief workshop: students brief one assigned landmark decision and present key reasoning and possible limitations of the holding.
  • Precedent map: chart how a particular doctrine (e.g., commerce power, due process) developed across several cases.

Assessment

Final assignment: a comparative essay (1,000–1,500 words) analyzing two Supreme Court decisions that address the same constitutional clause and evaluating the evolution of doctrine.

Unit Introduction — Sources of Law & Legal Authority

What counts as law in the United States and how the different sources relate.

Learning Objectives

  • List and define the principal sources of U.S. law: constitutional text, statutes, treaties, executive actions, administrative regulations, and judicial decisions.
  • Explain the constitutional hierarchy (the Constitution as supreme law) and the role of the Supremacy Clause.
  • Describe how separation of powers and federalism allocate authority in the U.S. system.

Core Content

The U.S. legal order rests on several interlocking sources:

  • Constitutional text: the U.S. Constitution is the highest law; it creates institutions and defines powers.
  • Statutes: laws enacted by Congress and codified in the United States Code.
  • Treaties: international agreements made by the President and ratified by the Senate; they have force of federal law.
  • Executive acts and regulations: orders by the President and rules created by federal agencies to implement statutes.
  • Judicial decisions: courts interpret statutes and the Constitution; Supreme Court rulings are authoritative federal law on constitutional questions.

Hierarchy and interaction: the Constitution overrides contrary federal statutes and treaties; federal law preempts conflicting state law under the Supremacy Clause. Courts settle ambiguities and resolve conflicts through interpretation and precedent.

Primary Readings (placeholders)

  1. U.S. Constitution (Preamble; Articles I–III; Supremacy Clause).
  2. Selected Federalist Papers (excerpts on separation of powers and federalism).

Activities

  • Document classification: give students short excerpts (Constitution, statute, executive order, opinion) and have them identify the source and legal status.
  • Hierarchy exercise: present a conflict (state law vs federal statute vs Constitution) and ask students to explain which governs and why.

Assessment

Short quiz: define the sources of law and explain the Supremacy Clause in one paragraph (200–300 words).

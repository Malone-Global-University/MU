What Is Baking?

Learn the fundamentals of baking, how it differs from cooking, and why precise methods matter.

Home Department Culinary Baking Class Module 1 What Is Baking

Objectives

  • Understand the definition of baking.
  • Recognize the key differences between baking and cooking.
  • Identify common baking ingredients and their roles.
  • Gain awareness of the importance of precision in measurements and timing.

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Read about the history and evolution of baking to understand its cultural significance.
  2. Learn the main types of baked goods: bread, pastries, cakes, and cookies.
  3. Identify the role of ingredients:
    • Flour: structure
    • Sugar: sweetness and browning
    • Leavening agents: rising
    • Fats: texture and flavor
    • Liquids: moisture and cohesion
  4. Compare baking with cooking:
    • Baking uses dry heat, often in an oven, while cooking can involve various methods like boiling or frying.
    • Baking requires precise measurements and timing, whereas cooking is often more flexible.
  5. Review basic baking terminology, such as kneading, proofing, creaming, and folding.
  6. Practice reading and interpreting simple baking recipes to recognize measurement conventions and techniques.

Notes & Tips

  • Always measure ingredients accurately; baking is science-based.
  • Understand ingredient functions to adapt recipes when necessary.
  • Temperature and timing are crucial â€” even small deviations can affect outcomes.
  • Keep a notebook to track observations and learn from each bake.

Definition of Baking

Baking is the process of using dry, consistent heatâ€”usually in an ovenâ€”to transform raw ingredients into finished foods. Heat causes physical and chemical changes such as protein coagulation, starch gelatinization, evaporation, browning reactions, and fat melting.

Why Baking Works

The goal of baking is to create a stable structure. Ingredients like flour, eggs, fat, and leavening agents work together to produce a network that sets as heat is applied. Once set, the product can hold its shape even after cooling.

The Role of Controlled Heat

Unlike stovetop cooking, baking relies on indirect heat that surrounds the food. This creates even cooking conditions and predictable results. Proper temperature control ensures correct rise, texture, color, and moisture retention.

Common Foods That Are Baked

Bread, pastries, cookies, cakes, pies, biscuits, crackers, custards, and many savory dishes all rely on baking principles.

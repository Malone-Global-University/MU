What Is Baking

An introduction to the science, purpose, and foundational principles behind all baked goods.

Home Department Culinary Baking Class Module 1 What Is Baking

Overview: What Baking Is

Baking is a dry-heat cooking method that uses controlled, enclosed heat to transform raw ingredients into structured, edible products. This transformation results from predictable physical and chemical reactions involving heat, moisture, proteins, starches, and leavening gases. Understanding these reactions forms the foundation of all baking skills.

Objectives

  • Define baking as a heat-driven transformation process.
  • Explain why baking is more precise than cooking.
  • Identify key scientific reactions involved in baking.
  • Describe how heat affects proteins, starches, sugars, and leavening agents.
  • Understand the step-by-step changes that occur inside baked goods.

The Science Behind Baking

Heat-Driven Transformation

Baking causes proteins to coagulate, starches to gelatinize, sugars to caramelize, fats to melt, and water to turn to steam. Leavening gases expand, giving structure and height to baked goods.

A Controlled Environment

Baking depends on stable temperature, enclosed heat, and predictable timing. Accuracy matters because once a product enters the oven, most variables cannot be adjusted.

Scientific Foundations

  • Gluten formation: hydration and mixing develop elasticity.
  • Gelatinization: starch swells and firms structure.
  • Coagulation: proteins set with heat.
  • Leavening: gases from yeast, chemicals, or steam expand.
  • Browning reactions: Maillard and caramelization add color and flavor.

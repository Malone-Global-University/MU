Lean vs Enriched Dough Adjustments

Step-by-step guide to adapting techniques for lean and enriched doughs to achieve optimal texture, rise, and flavor.

Home Department Culinary Baking Class Lean vs Enriched Dough Adjustments

1. Understanding Dough Types

Lean doughs contain minimal fat, sugar, or dairy, producing a crisp crust and open crumb. Enriched doughs include fats, eggs, sugar, or milk, creating tender crumb and softer crust.

2. Hydration Adjustments

  • Lean dough: often higher hydration for open crumb structure.
  • Enriched dough: may require slightly lower hydration to account for fats and eggs binding water.

3. Mixing and Kneading

  • Lean dough: longer kneading may be needed to develop strong gluten network.
  • Enriched dough: kneading must be gentler to incorporate fats without over-stretching gluten.

4. Fermentation and Proofing

  • Lean dough: faster fermentation; monitor closely for over-proofing.
  • Enriched dough: slower fermentation due to sugar and fat; may require longer bulk and final proof.

5. Shaping and Handling

  • Lean dough: handles easily; can retain shape well.
  • Enriched dough: stickier; may need floured surfaces or chilling to hold shape.

6. Baking Considerations

  • Lean dough: higher oven temperature, crisp crust, moderate bake time.
  • Enriched dough: lower temperature, softer crust, longer bake to ensure internal doneness without over-browning.

7. Cooling and Storage

  • Lean dough: cool on wire rack for crisp crust; store in breathable bags if needed.
  • Enriched dough: retain moisture; can wrap in cloth or airtight container to maintain softness.

Oven Spring

What causes the sudden rise in the oven, how to maximize it reliably, and how scoring, steam, proofing and temperature interact to produce ideal loft and crumb.

Overview

Key factors that determine oven spring

  • Proof level: Properly proofed dough (slightly under to fully proofed) retains gas and strength for spring. Overproofed dough may collapse; underproofed dough has insufficient gas.
  • Dough strength: Well-developed gluten traps gases and translates expansion into height rather than rupture.
  • Scoring: Directs where dough will open â€” correct scoring prevents random tearing and concentrates spring into an ear or controlled bloom.
  • Steam & oven temp: Immediate steam and a hot oven support maximum spring before crust sets.

2. Proof to the correct stage

Common problems & fixes

  • Little or no oven spring: Underdeveloped gluten, low preheat, lack of steam, or overhandling. Fix: strengthen dough, increase preheat time, ensure steam, and avoid degassing.
  • Collapse after initial spring: Overproofed dough or weak structure. Fix: proof slightly less next time, or improve gluten development.
  • Random tearing: Dull blade, hesitant scoring or poor skin tension. Fix: sharpen blade, score confidently, reshape for better tension.
  • Uneven spring: Inconsistent shaping, cold spots in oven, or uneven steam. Fix: standardize shaping, map oven zones, and ensure consistent steam distribution.

Practice drills

  1. Proof level experiment: Bake three identical loaves pulled at slightly different proof stages (under, ideal, just over) and compare oven spring and crumb.
  2. Steam vs no steam: Bake paired loaves with and without steam to observe the effect on spring and crust.
  3. Shaping tension drill: Practice making tight-skin loaves and measure height after bake to connect shaping with results.

The Branches of Physics and Their Main Topics

Home Directory Science Physics Exploratory The Branches of Physics and Their Main Topics

Introduction

Physics is the study of matter, energy, motion, forces, space, time, and the laws that govern the natural world. It is one of the foundational sciences because it explains the behavior of physical systems at every scale, from the motion of everyday objects to the structure of atoms, from the flow of electricity to the evolution of stars and galaxies. Physics does not study only one kind of object or one kind of process. Instead, it provides a framework for understanding how nature works through measurement, mathematics, experimentation, and theory.

The field of physics is broad because the physical universe is broad. Some branches of physics focus on objects large enough to see and touch, such as falling bodies, moving vehicles, fluids, engines, bridges, and machines. Other branches study invisible or extremely small systems, such as atoms, nuclei, electrons, photons, and fundamental particles. Still others study massive systems, such as planets, stars, galaxies, black holes, and the universe itself. Because no single branch can cover all of these scales and behaviors at once, physics is commonly divided into several major areas.

The main branches of physics include classical mechanics, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, waves and optics, relativity, quantum physics, atomic physics, nuclear physics, particle physics, condensed matter physics, plasma physics, fluid mechanics, astrophysics, cosmology, geophysics, atmospheric physics, biophysics, medical physics, and computational physics. Each branch has its own central questions, methods, and applications, but they are deeply connected. Together, they form a unified discipline concerned with explaining physical reality.

Classical Mechanics

Classical mechanics is one of the oldest and most fundamental branches of physics. It studies the motion of objects and the forces that cause or change that motion. Classical mechanics applies especially well to everyday objects, such as balls, vehicles, machines, planets, projectiles, and structures. It is the physics of motion at human scales and moderate speeds.

The central topics of classical mechanics include position, distance, displacement, speed, velocity, acceleration, force, mass, momentum, energy, work, power, torque, rotation, equilibrium, and gravity. Students usually begin with kinematics, which describes motion without focusing on its causes. Kinematics asks questions such as: How far did an object travel? How fast is it moving? How quickly is its velocity changing? From there, mechanics moves into dynamics, which explains motion by studying forces.

Newtonâ€™s laws of motion are a major foundation of classical mechanics. The first law explains inertia, the tendency of an object to maintain its state of motion unless acted on by an external force. The second law connects force, mass, and acceleration. The third law explains action-reaction pairs, showing that forces occur through interactions between objects. These laws allow physicists and engineers to predict how objects move under different conditions.

Classical mechanics also includes the study of energy and momentum. These ideas are especially useful because they allow complicated motion problems to be solved through conservation laws. If energy or momentum is conserved in a system, the system can be analyzed without tracking every tiny detail of the motion. This makes mechanics useful in fields such as engineering, transportation, sports science, robotics, architecture, aerospace, and astronomy.

Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is the branch of physics that studies electric charge, electric fields, magnetic fields, circuits, and electromagnetic waves. It explains many of the technologies that define modern life, including electric power, batteries, motors, generators, radios, computers, cell phones, fiber optics, and medical imaging devices.

The main topics of electromagnetism include electric charge, Coulombâ€™s law, electric fields, electric potential, voltage, current, resistance, capacitance, circuits, magnetic fields, magnetic forces, electromagnetic induction, Faradayâ€™s law, Lenzâ€™s law, and Maxwellâ€™s equations. At a basic level, this branch begins with the idea that electric charges exert forces on one another. Like charges repel, while opposite charges attract. These interactions can be described through electric fields.

Electric circuits are one of the most practical areas of electromagnetism. Circuits involve the controlled movement of electric charge through wires and components. Important circuit concepts include voltage, current, resistance, power, series circuits, parallel circuits, switches, capacitors, and circuit diagrams. Ohmâ€™s law is one of the most important relationships in introductory circuit analysis because it connects voltage, current, and resistance.

Magnetism is closely connected to electricity. Moving charges can produce magnetic fields, and changing magnetic fields can produce electric currents. This connection is the basis of electric generators, transformers, motors, and many communication systems. The unification of electricity and magnetism is one of the major achievements in physics. Maxwellâ€™s equations describe this relationship mathematically and show that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that studies heat, temperature, energy transfer, work, engines, entropy, and the behavior of matter in bulk. While mechanics often focuses on individual objects, thermodynamics studies systems made of enormous numbers of particles. It explains how energy moves through matter and how physical systems change when heated, cooled, compressed, or expanded.

The main topics of thermodynamics include temperature, heat, thermal energy, internal energy, specific heat, phase changes, heat transfer, conduction, convection, radiation, gases, pressure, volume, the ideal gas law, the laws of thermodynamics, heat engines, refrigerators, efficiency, and entropy. One of the first distinctions students must learn is the difference between temperature and heat. Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance, while heat refers to energy transferred because of a temperature difference.

The laws of thermodynamics are central to this branch. The first law is a statement of energy conservation: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed. The second law introduces the idea of entropy and explains why natural processes tend to move in certain directions. For example, heat naturally flows from hotter objects to colder objects, not the reverse. The third law concerns the behavior of systems as they approach absolute zero.

Thermodynamics is important in engines, power plants, heating and cooling systems, weather, chemistry, biology, and materials science. Any system involving heat, energy transfer, or changes in physical state can be studied thermodynamically. This makes thermodynamics one of the most practical branches of physics.

Waves and Oscillations

Waves and oscillations study repeated motion and the transfer of energy through space or matter. Many physical systems move back and forth, vibrate, or transmit disturbances. This branch explains phenomena such as sound, water waves, earthquakes, light waves, radio waves, musical instruments, resonance, and interference.

The main topics include periodic motion, simple harmonic motion, amplitude, frequency, period, wavelength, wave speed, reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, superposition, resonance, standing waves, sound waves, and the Doppler effect. Oscillation refers to repeating motion around an equilibrium position, such as a mass attached to a spring or a pendulum swinging back and forth. Waves often arise from oscillations that travel through a medium or through space.

A wave can carry energy without permanently transporting matter from one place to another. For example, a sound wave travels through air by causing air molecules to vibrate, but the molecules do not travel all the way from the speaker to the listener. Important wave properties include amplitude, which is related to intensity; frequency, which measures how often cycles occur; and wavelength, which measures the distance between repeating points in a wave.

Wave behavior is important because different types of waves follow similar rules. Sound waves, water waves, light waves, and seismic waves all show reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference. Understanding waves helps explain music, acoustics, communications, optics, medical ultrasound, earthquakes, and many modern technologies.

Optics

Optics is the branch of physics that studies light and its behavior. It is closely related to waves and electromagnetism, but it is often treated as its own branch because light has so many important applications. Optics explains vision, lenses, mirrors, cameras, microscopes, telescopes, lasers, fiber optics, and image formation.

The main topics of optics include reflection, refraction, Snellâ€™s law, lenses, mirrors, focal length, ray diagrams, magnification, image formation, dispersion, color, diffraction, interference, polarization, optical instruments, and lasers. Geometric optics treats light as rays that travel in straight lines and bend or reflect at surfaces. This approach is useful for understanding mirrors, lenses, eyeglasses, cameras, and telescopes.

Wave optics studies light as a wave. This is necessary for understanding interference, diffraction, and polarization. These phenomena show that light cannot be fully explained as a simple ray. For example, diffraction occurs when light bends around obstacles or spreads after passing through narrow openings. Interference occurs when waves overlap and combine.

Optics also connects to quantum physics because light can behave as both a wave and a particle. The particle-like packets of light are called photons. This connection makes optics important not only in classical physics, but also in modern physics, electronics, communications, and quantum technology.

Relativity

Relativity is the branch of physics that studies space, time, motion, gravity, and the behavior of objects moving at very high speeds or existing in strong gravitational fields. It becomes especially important when ordinary Newtonian mechanics no longer gives accurate results.

There are two major parts of relativity: special relativity and general relativity. Special relativity deals with objects moving at constant speeds, especially speeds close to the speed of light. Its main topics include inertial reference frames, the constancy of the speed of light, time dilation, length contraction, simultaneity, relativistic momentum, and mass-energy equivalence. The famous equation E = mcÂ² expresses the connection between mass and energy.

General relativity extends relativity to include gravity. Instead of treating gravity simply as a force between masses, general relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. Massive objects such as stars and planets curve spacetime, and other objects move along paths shaped by that curvature. This theory explains phenomena such as gravitational time dilation, the bending of light by gravity, black holes, gravitational waves, and the expansion of the universe.

Relativity is essential in astrophysics, cosmology, satellite navigation, high-energy physics, and the study of black holes. Although its effects are often small in everyday life, relativity is necessary for accurate physics at cosmic scales and extreme speeds.

Quantum Physics

Quantum physics studies matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales. It is one of the most important branches of modern physics because it explains phenomena that classical physics cannot. At very small scales, particles do not behave like ordinary objects. They can show wave-like behavior, exist in probabilistic states, occupy quantized energy levels, and interact in ways that seem unusual from an everyday perspective.

The main topics of quantum physics include quantization, photons, electrons, wave-particle duality, Planckâ€™s constant, probability waves, the uncertainty principle, superposition, quantum states, the SchrÃ¶dinger equation, tunneling, spin, measurement, entanglement, and quantum statistics. One of the central ideas is that energy can come in discrete units rather than being continuous. This idea helped explain blackbody radiation, atomic spectra, and the photoelectric effect.

Wave-particle duality is another major concept. Light can behave like a wave in some experiments and like a particle in others. Matter, such as electrons, can also show wave-like properties. This means that particles at small scales are not simply tiny versions of everyday objects. Quantum physics uses probability to describe what can be known about a system.

Quantum physics is the foundation of much modern technology. Semiconductors, lasers, transistors, magnetic resonance imaging, solar cells, quantum computing, and many electronic devices depend on quantum principles. It is also the basis for atomic physics, nuclear physics, particle physics, and condensed matter physics.

Atomic Physics

Atomic physics studies atoms, especially their electron structures and interactions with light. It focuses on how atoms absorb energy, emit radiation, form spectra, and respond to electromagnetic fields. Atomic physics connects classical ideas about matter with quantum explanations of atomic behavior.

The main topics include atomic models, electrons, energy levels, atomic orbitals, electron transitions, emission spectra, absorption spectra, ionization, spectroscopy, lasers, and interactions between atoms and electromagnetic radiation. Early atomic models attempted to explain the structure of the atom, but quantum theory showed that electrons do not orbit the nucleus like planets around the sun. Instead, electrons are described by orbitals, which represent regions where they are likely to be found.

Atomic spectra are one of the most important topics in atomic physics. When atoms absorb or emit light, they do so at specific wavelengths. These patterns act like fingerprints for elements. Spectroscopy allows scientists to identify the composition of stars, gases, materials, and unknown substances by studying the light they emit or absorb.

Atomic physics has applications in chemistry, astronomy, lasers, clocks, quantum information, and materials science. Atomic clocks, for example, use the extremely regular behavior of atoms to measure time with great precision.

Nuclear Physics

Nuclear physics studies the nucleus of the atom. While atomic physics focuses mostly on electrons, nuclear physics focuses on protons, neutrons, nuclear forces, radioactivity, and nuclear reactions. It explains the structure, stability, and transformation of atomic nuclei.

The main topics include protons, neutrons, isotopes, nuclear binding energy, radioactive decay, alpha decay, beta decay, gamma radiation, half-life, nuclear fission, nuclear fusion, nuclear reactors, radiation safety, and nuclear medicine. One central question in nuclear physics is why some nuclei are stable while others decay. Stability depends on the balance between nuclear forces, electric repulsion between protons, and the ratio of protons to neutrons.

Radioactivity is a major area of nuclear physics. Unstable nuclei can release particles or energy as they transform into more stable forms. Half-life describes how long it takes for half of a sample of radioactive material to decay. This concept is used in radiometric dating, medicine, environmental science, and nuclear safety.

Nuclear reactions release enormous amounts of energy because they involve changes in nuclear binding energy. Fission splits heavy nuclei into smaller nuclei, while fusion combines light nuclei into heavier nuclei. Fission is used in nuclear power plants, while fusion powers stars and is studied as a possible future energy source.

Particle Physics

Particle physics studies the most fundamental known building blocks of matter and the forces through which they interact. It asks what matter is made of at the deepest level and how fundamental interactions operate.

The main topics include quarks, leptons, bosons, hadrons, antimatter, neutrinos, particle decay, conservation laws, particle accelerators, detectors, the Standard Model, the Higgs boson, and the fundamental forces. According to the Standard Model, matter is made from particles such as quarks and leptons. Protons and neutrons are made of quarks, while electrons are leptons. Force-carrying particles, such as photons and gluons, mediate interactions.

Particle physics often requires very high-energy experiments because fundamental particles are extremely small and difficult to study directly. Particle accelerators speed particles to high velocities and collide them, allowing scientists to study the products of those collisions. Detectors then record the paths, energies, and properties of the particles produced.

This branch is closely connected to cosmology because the early universe was extremely hot and dense. Understanding fundamental particles helps explain the conditions that existed shortly after the Big Bang. Particle physics also explores antimatter, neutrinos, symmetry, and the limits of current physical theory.

Condensed Matter Physics

Condensed matter physics studies matter in solid and liquid forms, especially systems with many interacting particles. It is one of the largest and most practical branches of physics because it explains the behavior of materials used in everyday technology.

The main topics include crystal structure, solids, liquids, conductivity, insulation, semiconductors, band theory, magnetism, superconductivity, phase transitions, polymers, nanomaterials, and material properties. Condensed matter physics asks why some materials conduct electricity while others do not, why some materials are magnetic, how crystals form, how solids respond to heat and pressure, and how new materials can be engineered.

Semiconductors are one of the most important topics in condensed matter physics. They form the basis of transistors, computer chips, solar cells, sensors, and modern electronics. Band theory helps explain why semiconductors can be controlled in ways that make electronic devices possible.

Superconductivity is another major area. Some materials, under certain conditions, can conduct electricity with zero resistance. This phenomenon has applications in powerful magnets, medical imaging, particle accelerators, and future energy technologies. Condensed matter physics also plays a major role in nanotechnology and materials engineering.

Plasma Physics

Plasma physics studies ionized gases made of charged particles. Plasma is often called the fourth state of matter, alongside solids, liquids, and gases. It occurs when atoms lose electrons, producing a mixture of free electrons and ions.

The main topics include ionization, charged particle motion, electric fields in plasma, magnetic fields in plasma, plasma waves, magnetic confinement, fusion plasma, solar plasma, auroras, lightning, space plasma, and industrial plasma applications. Plasma behaves differently from ordinary gas because its charged particles respond strongly to electric and magnetic fields.

Plasma is common in the universe. Stars, solar wind, nebulae, and much of interstellar space contain plasma. On Earth, plasma appears in lightning, neon signs, fluorescent lights, plasma cutters, and experimental fusion devices. Fusion research depends heavily on plasma physics because fusion fuel must be heated to extremely high temperatures, forming plasma that must be controlled and confined.

Plasma physics connects astrophysics, energy research, electrical engineering, and space science. It is especially important for understanding the sun, space weather, and the possibility of controlled nuclear fusion.

Fluid Mechanics

Fluid mechanics studies the behavior of liquids and gases. It explains pressure, flow, buoyancy, drag, lift, turbulence, and the motion of fluids through pipes, channels, bodies, and the atmosphere.

The main topics include density, pressure, Pascalâ€™s principle, Archimedesâ€™ principle, buoyancy, fluid flow, viscosity, surface tension, the continuity equation, Bernoulliâ€™s principle, drag, lift, laminar flow, turbulent flow, and aerodynamics. Fluid mechanics begins with the idea that fluids can flow and change shape rather than maintaining a fixed form.

Pressure is one of the most important concepts in fluid mechanics. It explains why fluids push on surfaces, why objects float or sink, and how hydraulic systems work. Buoyancy explains why ships float and why objects feel lighter in water. Bernoulliâ€™s principle connects fluid speed and pressure, helping explain lift, airflow, and many engineering systems.

Fluid mechanics is important in aviation, weather, oceanography, medicine, plumbing, mechanical engineering, civil engineering, and environmental science. It is also one of the most mathematically challenging branches of physics because turbulent flow can be extremely complex.

Astrophysics

Astrophysics applies physics to objects and processes beyond Earth. It studies stars, planets, galaxies, black holes, nebulae, cosmic radiation, and other astronomical systems. While astronomy observes celestial objects, astrophysics seeks to explain them using physical laws.

The main topics include gravity, orbital motion, stars, stellar evolution, nuclear fusion in stars, planets, galaxies, black holes, neutron stars, white dwarfs, supernovae, dark matter, dark energy, cosmic rays, and the large-scale structure of the universe. Astrophysics uses mechanics, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, quantum physics, relativity, nuclear physics, and plasma physics.

Stars are a major focus of astrophysics. Their structure and evolution depend on gravity, pressure, heat, radiation, and nuclear fusion. A starâ€™s life cycle can include stages such as protostar, main sequence star, red giant, white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole, depending on its mass.

Astrophysics also studies galaxies and cosmic structures. It investigates how galaxies form, how black holes influence their surroundings, and why visible matter alone cannot explain the motion of galaxies. This leads to the study of dark matter and dark energy, two major unsolved problems in modern physics.

Cosmology

Cosmology studies the universe as a whole. It focuses on the origin, structure, evolution, and possible future of the universe. Cosmology overlaps with astrophysics, but its scale is larger. Instead of studying individual stars or galaxies, it studies the universe itself.

The main topics include the Big Bang, cosmic expansion, redshift, Hubbleâ€™s law, cosmic microwave background radiation, inflation, dark matter, dark energy, galaxy formation, large-scale structure, and the fate of the universe. Cosmology asks questions such as: How did the universe begin? Why is it expanding? What is it made of? How did galaxies form? What will happen to the universe in the distant future?

The Big Bang model is the dominant framework in modern cosmology. It states that the universe began in a hot, dense state and has expanded and cooled over time. Evidence for this model includes cosmic expansion, the abundance of light elements, and the cosmic microwave background.

Cosmology depends heavily on general relativity because gravity shapes the universe at large scales. It also depends on particle physics because the early universe involved extremely high energies and fundamental particles. This makes cosmology one of the most interdisciplinary branches of physics.

Geophysics

Geophysics applies physics to Earth and planetary systems. It studies Earthâ€™s structure, motion, fields, and internal processes using physical principles. Geophysics connects physics with geology, planetary science, environmental science, and engineering.

The main topics include Earthâ€™s interior, seismic waves, earthquakes, volcanoes, plate tectonics, gravity fields, magnetic fields, heat flow, ocean physics, remote sensing, planetary interiors, and natural hazards. Seismology is one of the major areas of geophysics. By studying seismic waves from earthquakes, scientists can infer the structure of Earthâ€™s interior.

Geophysics also studies Earthâ€™s magnetic field, which is generated by motion in the planetâ€™s outer core. This field helps protect Earth from charged particles from the sun. Gravity measurements can reveal variations in underground density, helping identify geological structures.

Geophysics is important for earthquake analysis, resource exploration, environmental monitoring, planetary exploration, and hazard assessment. It shows how physics can be applied to understand the planet beneath our feet.

Atmospheric Physics

Atmospheric physics studies the physical behavior of the atmosphere. It explains weather, climate, air pressure, radiation, clouds, storms, wind, and the energy balance of Earth.

The main topics include atmospheric layers, air pressure, temperature gradients, humidity, cloud formation, radiation balance, wind, storms, atmospheric waves, greenhouse gases, aerosols, climate physics, and atmospheric electricity. This branch applies thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, electromagnetism, and radiation physics to the atmosphere.

Weather systems are driven by energy from the sun, Earthâ€™s rotation, pressure differences, and the movement of air and water vapor. Atmospheric physics explains how clouds form, why winds blow, how storms develop, and how heat moves through the atmosphere.

Climate physics focuses on long-term patterns and energy balance. It studies how sunlight enters Earthâ€™s atmosphere, how Earth emits infrared radiation, and how gases and particles affect the movement of energy. Atmospheric physics is essential for meteorology, aviation, climate science, environmental monitoring, and planetary science.

Biophysics

Biophysics applies physics to living systems. It uses physical laws and methods to understand biological structures and processes. Biophysics connects physics with biology, chemistry, medicine, and engineering.

The main topics include diffusion, molecular motion, cell membranes, nerve signals, biomechanics, muscle contraction, protein folding, biological energy, fluid flow in the body, vision, hearing, and medical imaging. Biological systems are complex, but they still obey physical laws. Molecules move, forces act, energy is transferred, and materials respond to pressure and temperature.

Diffusion is one important topic in biophysics. It explains how molecules move from regions of high concentration to regions of low concentration, which is essential for cells. Membrane physics explains how cells control what enters and leaves. Biomechanics studies forces and motion in living bodies, including bones, muscles, joints, and tissues.

Biophysics has practical applications in medicine, biotechnology, neuroscience, sports science, and biomedical engineering. It helps explain how life functions at molecular, cellular, and mechanical levels.

Medical Physics

Medical physics applies physics to the diagnosis and treatment of disease. It is a practical and professional branch of physics used in hospitals, clinics, and medical technology.

The main topics include X-rays, computed tomography, magnetic resonance imaging, ultrasound, nuclear medicine, positron emission tomography, radiation therapy, dosimetry, radiation safety, lasers in medicine, and image processing. Medical imaging depends on different physical principles. X-rays use electromagnetic radiation to image dense structures such as bones. MRI uses magnetic fields and radio waves to image soft tissues. Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves to image internal structures.

Radiation therapy is another major area of medical physics. It uses carefully controlled radiation to treat cancer while minimizing damage to healthy tissue. Dosimetry, the measurement of radiation dose, is essential for safety and effectiveness.

Medical physics shows how abstract physics becomes directly useful in human health. It combines scientific understanding with technical precision, safety standards, and clinical application.

Computational Physics

Computational physics uses computers, algorithms, numerical methods, and simulations to solve physics problems. It is especially important when systems are too complex to solve exactly with simple equations.

The main topics include numerical modeling, simulations, data analysis, differential equations, Monte Carlo methods, finite element methods, molecular dynamics, computational fluid dynamics, visualization, and high-performance computing. Many physical systems involve too many variables or too much complexity for direct analytical solutions. Computational methods allow scientists to approximate, model, and visualize these systems.

Simulations are used in climate modeling, fluid flow, astrophysics, materials science, nuclear physics, particle physics, biomechanics, and engineering. For example, computational physics can model airflow over an aircraft wing, the collision of galaxies, the behavior of molecules, or the spread of heat through a material.

Computational physics has become essential in modern science because modern experiments and theories often produce large amounts of data. Computers allow physicists to test models, compare predictions with observations, and explore systems that cannot be easily reproduced in a laboratory.

Relationships Between the Branches

Although physics is divided into branches for clarity, the branches are not isolated. Many real physical systems require more than one branch to understand them. A star, for example, involves gravity from mechanics and relativity, heat and pressure from thermodynamics, light from electromagnetism and optics, fusion from nuclear physics, plasma behavior from plasma physics, and large-scale evolution from astrophysics.

A smartphone also involves many branches of physics. Its screen depends on optics and materials physics. Its circuits depend on electromagnetism and condensed matter physics. Its processor depends on quantum physics and semiconductor physics. Its GPS functions require relativity. Its battery involves thermodynamics, electricity, and chemistry. This shows that modern technology is not built from one branch of physics alone, but from many branches working together.

The same is true in medicine. MRI uses electromagnetism, quantum behavior, signal processing, and medical physics. Ultrasound uses waves, fluids, and tissue mechanics. Radiation therapy uses nuclear physics, particle interactions, and dosimetry. Physics becomes powerful because its branches can combine to explain complex systems.

Conclusion

Physics is a unified science with many branches. Classical mechanics explains motion and forces. Electromagnetism explains electricity, magnetism, circuits, and light. Thermodynamics explains heat, temperature, and energy transfer. Waves and optics explain vibration, sound, light, and imaging. Relativity explains space, time, gravity, and high-speed motion. Quantum physics explains matter and energy at small scales. Atomic, nuclear, and particle physics examine matter at increasingly deeper levels. Condensed matter, plasma physics, and fluid mechanics study complex forms of matter. Astrophysics and cosmology apply physics to the universe. Geophysics and atmospheric physics apply physics to Earth. Biophysics and medical physics apply physics to living systems and health. Computational physics provides tools for modeling complex systems across all branches.

The branches of physics are best understood as connected areas within one larger discipline. Each branch studies a different range of objects, scales, behaviors, or applications, but all are guided by the same goal: to describe, explain, and predict the behavior of the physical universe. For students, learning the branches of physics provides a map of the field. It shows where basic concepts fit, how advanced topics develop, and how physics supports technology, engineering, medicine, astronomy, environmental science, and everyday life.

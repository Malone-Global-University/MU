Physics applied to space, the universe, Earth systems, the atmosphere, living systems, medicine, computation, technology, and scientific modeling.

Overview

Applied and interdisciplinary physics uses physical principles to understand specialized systems, solve practical problems, and connect physics to other scientific and technical fields. While classical and modern physics develop the core laws of motion, energy, fields, waves, matter, space, time, and particles, applied physics shows how those laws operate in real environments such as stars, planets, the atmosphere, living organisms, hospitals, laboratories, computers, and engineered systems.

This division includes branches that draw from multiple parts of physics at once. Astrophysics may use mechanics, relativity, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, nuclear physics, plasma physics, and particle physics to explain stars and galaxies. Medical physics may use radiation, imaging, nuclear processes, waves, magnetism, and computational modeling to support diagnosis and treatment. Computational physics may support nearly every branch by using numerical methods, simulations, and data analysis.

Applied and interdisciplinary physics gives students a bridge between foundational theory and real-world systems. It shows that physics is not limited to textbook problems. Physics can explain the motion of galaxies, the structure of Earth, the behavior of storms, the mechanics of the body, the operation of medical imaging equipment, and the simulation of systems too complex to solve by hand.

Where This Division Fits

Applied and interdisciplinary physics follows and extends both classical physics and modern physics. Classical physics provides mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, waves, optics, and fluids. Modern physics provides quantum mechanics, relativity, atomic physics, nuclear physics, particle physics, condensed matter physics, and plasma physics. Applied branches combine these foundations to study specialized domains.

Within the Physics Department, this division should be used after students understand the major classical and modern branches, or alongside them when a practical application helps clarify the theory. For example, atmospheric physics can reinforce thermodynamics and fluids. Astrophysics can reinforce mechanics, relativity, and nuclear physics. Medical physics can reinforce waves, electromagnetism, nuclear physics, and radiation safety.

Applied and Interdisciplinary Physics Branches

  • Astrophysics

    Studies stars, planets, galaxies, black holes, stellar evolution, cosmic radiation, dark matter, dark energy, and physical processes in space.

  • Cosmology

    Studies the universe as a whole, including the Big Bang, cosmic expansion, redshift, cosmic microwave background, large-scale structure, and the fate of the universe.

  • Geophysics

    Studies Earth and planetary systems using physics, including seismic waves, gravity, magnetism, heat flow, plate tectonics, natural hazards, and planetary interiors.

  • Atmospheric Physics

    Studies the physical behavior of the atmosphere, including air pressure, radiation balance, clouds, wind, storms, weather systems, greenhouse effects, and climate physics.

  • Biophysics

    Studies living systems through physics, including diffusion, membranes, biomechanics, nerve signals, muscle motion, protein folding, biological energy, and fluid flow in the body.

  • Medical Physics

    Studies the use of physics in medicine, including X-rays, MRI, CT scans, ultrasound, radiation therapy, nuclear medicine, dosimetry, medical imaging, and radiation safety.

  • Computational Physics

    Studies physics through numerical methods, simulations, data analysis, mathematical modeling, Monte Carlo methods, computational fluid dynamics, and scientific computing.

Core Concepts

Applied and interdisciplinary physics is built around transfer. It transfers the laws and methods of physics into specialized domains. A student who understands force, energy, fields, waves, heat, radiation, probability, and computation can apply those ideas to systems that may not look like traditional physics problems at first.

Scale is one of the central ideas in this division. Applied physics may study systems at very different scales, from proteins and cells to organs and medical devices, from storms and earthquakes to planets and galaxies, from microscopic particle interactions to universe-wide expansion. The same physical ideas may appear differently depending on the size, energy, speed, density, and complexity of the system.

Modeling is another central idea. Applied systems are often too complicated to describe with one simple equation. A weather system, a living cell, a galaxy cluster, a medical scan, or a turbulent flow may require approximation, measurement, simulation, and careful interpretation. Applied physics teaches students to simplify without oversimplifying and to connect mathematical models to real physical evidence.

Interdisciplinary reasoning is also essential. Astrophysics connects physics with astronomy. Geophysics connects physics with Earth science. Biophysics connects physics with biology. Medical physics connects physics with health care. Computational physics connects physics with mathematics, computer science, data analysis, and numerical modeling. These connections show how physics supports broader scientific and professional work.

Recommended Study Order

Students should usually begin applied and interdisciplinary physics after gaining a foundation in mechanics, waves, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, optics, fluids, quantum mechanics, relativity, and nuclear physics. Those subjects provide the core concepts needed to understand the applied branches in a serious way.

Astrophysics and cosmology are best studied after mechanics, gravitation, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, relativity, nuclear physics, and plasma physics. These foundations help explain stars, galaxies, black holes, cosmic expansion, cosmic radiation, dark matter, and dark energy.

Geophysics and atmospheric physics are best studied after mechanics, waves, thermodynamics, fluids, electromagnetism, and basic Earth science. These branches apply physical reasoning to earthquakes, seismic waves, planetary interiors, weather systems, air pressure, clouds, wind, storms, and climate behavior.

Biophysics and medical physics are best studied after mechanics, fluids, waves, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, optics, nuclear physics, and atomic physics. These subjects explain biological motion, membranes, blood flow, nerve signals, medical imaging, radiation therapy, ultrasound, MRI, CT scans, and nuclear medicine.

Computational physics can be studied alongside many branches because it supports modeling and simulation across the department. It becomes especially powerful after students understand algebra, calculus, differential equations, programming, data analysis, and the physical systems being modeled.

Why Applied and Interdisciplinary Physics Matters

Applied and interdisciplinary physics matters because it shows how physical law operates in real systems. The same physics that explains motion in a classroom can help explain planetary orbits, blood flow, aircraft flight, earthquake waves, weather motion, medical imaging, and scientific simulations.

It also matters because many modern problems are interdisciplinary. Understanding climate, energy, medicine, space, materials, computation, communication, transportation, and environmental systems often requires physics combined with other fields. Applied physics gives students the tools to move between theory and practice.

This division also prepares students for professional and technical work. Engineering, medicine, astronomy, environmental monitoring, data science, aerospace, imaging, energy systems, materials research, defense technology, and scientific computing all depend on applied physical reasoning.

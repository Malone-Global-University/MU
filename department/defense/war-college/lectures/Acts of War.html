I. Classic / Traditional Acts of War

  1. Armed Invasion or Incursion

    • Crossing borders with military forces
    • Occupying territory
    • Even limited ‚Äúprobing‚Äù incursions can qualify

  2. Bombing or Airstrikes

    • Missile attacks
    • Aerial bombardment
    • Use of drones to strike targets inside another state

  3. Naval Attacks

    • Attacking ships
    • Blockading ports
    • Mining territorial waters

  4. Artillery or Missile Fire Across Borders

    • Shelling towns, bases, or infrastructure in another country

  5. Assassination of Senior Government Officials by another state

    • Particularly heads of state, ministers of defense, etc.
    • Historically viewed as casus belli (grounds for war)

  6. Supporting Armed Groups That Attack Another State

    • Funding, arming, training, or directing insurgents
    • This is sometimes called ‚Äúindirect aggression‚Äù
    • International Court of Justice (ICJ) considers this an unlawful use of force

II. Modern / Non-Traditional Acts of War (Recognized Today)

  1. Cyberattacks That Cause Physical Damage

    • These are now widely considered ‚Äúarmed attacks‚Äù if they:
    • shut down power grids
    • damage infrastructure
    • lead to loss of life
    • cripple critical systems (water, hospitals, air traffic control)
    • Many states (U.S., NATO, EU) have declared that such attacks may trigger military response.

  2. Economic Blockades

    • Preventing essential goods from reaching a nation
    • Naval quarantine that restricts trade
    • Historical example: Cuban Missile Crisis ‚Äúquarantine‚Äù was legally borderline but treated as an act of war by some.

  3. Massive Electronic Warfare / GPS Denial If it interferes with:

    • aircraft navigation
    • national defense systems
    • missile detection
    • This can escalate to armed confrontation.

III. Actions Sometimes Considered Acts of War (Context-Dependent)

These are not automatically acts of war but can be treated as such:

  1. Espionage Usually tolerated as ‚Äúnormal behavior‚Äù between states BUT:

    • sabotage
    • blowing up facilities
    • killing operatives
    • may escalate into war-like acts.

  2. Violating Airspace With Military Aircraft

    • Single incursions = provocation
    • Repeated incursions = possible act of war
    • Striking targets or refusing to leave = definitely an act of war

  3. Interference With Satellites

    • Blinding satellites with lasers
    • Destroying satellites (ASAT weapon test)
    • Manipulating GPS signals
    • can be regarded as hostile acts, especially if it harms national security.

IV. International Law Definitions

UN Charter Article 2(4): Use of Force

Most global law views an ‚Äúact of war‚Äù as any use of force against the territorial integrity or political independence of another state.

UN Charter Article 51: Right of Self-Defense

A state may respond with force when it suffers:

  • Armed attack
  • Attacks by state-backed militias or terrorists
  • Cyberattacks with physical effects

V. What is Not Automatically an Act of War

  • Sanctions
  • Diplomatic expulsions
  • Trade disputes
  • Propaganda
  • Political interference
  • Surveillance or reconnaissance flights in international airspace

They may be hostile, but not legally ‚Äúacts of war.

VI. Exception: When Trade disputes are an Act of War

  • Trade disputes do not automatically qualify as acts of war.
  • However, they can become acts of war when they cross into coercive, destructive, or blockade-level actions that harm a nation‚Äôs survival.

Below is the clearest and most authoritative breakdown.

VI. When Trade Disputes Do Not Count as Acts of War

Most trade conflict is normal peacetime behavior:

  • Tariffs
  • Sanctions with limited scope
  • Counter-tariffs
  • WTO complaints
  • Subsidy fights (e.g., Boeing vs Airbus)
  • Import/export restrictions
  • Currency manipulation accusations

These are hostile or unfriendly, but not war because international law views these as economic measures short of force.

VII. When Trade Disputes Become Acts of War

A trade conflict becomes an act of war when it meets one of these conditions:

  1. The measures constitute a blockade

This is the clearest threshold. A blockade means:

  • Physically preventing a nation from importing essential goods (fuel, food, medicine)
  • Using naval forces or coercive pressure to stop ships
  • Cutting off all trade intentionally
  • Blockades are universally considered acts of war under international law (Customary Law + Hague Convention).

Examples:

  • U.S. blockade of Japan (1940‚Äì41) helped trigger Pearl Harbor
  • British naval blockade of Germany (WWI, WWII)
  1. The measures cause mass deprivation or threaten a nation‚Äôs survival

If sanctions are so broad that they:

  • collapse the economy
  • starve the population
  • shut down essential infrastructure

Then they cross into coercive use of force.

Example:

  • 1990s Iraq sanctions ‚Äî widely criticized as ‚Äúeconomic warfare‚Äù
  • States often treat ‚Äúfull economic strangulation‚Äù like a military attack.
  1. The trade actions are paired with military enforcement

If you use:

  • naval patrols
  • armed escorts
  • seizures of goods
  • interdiction at sea or air

The dispute is no longer ‚Äúeconomic.‚Äù It becomes coercive force, legally similar to war.

  1. The measures are intended to collapse a government

Intent matters. If a state openly seeks regime change through economic pressure, it may be viewed as economic aggression or hybrid warfare.

Examples:

  • Cold War sanctions sometimes interpreted this way
  • U.S. embargo against Cuba was accused of being ‚Äúeconomic war‚Äù by Cuba
  1. The actions violate jus ad bellum by coercing political independence

UN Charter Article 2(4) forbids using force to violate another state's:

  • territorial integrity
  • political independence
  • If trade measures are coercively used to dictate:
  • elections
  • political choices
  • foreign policy alignment

Some states argue this is force in another form. This is controversial but increasingly relevant in modern geopolitics.

A trade dispute becomes an act of war when economic pressure becomes destructive enough to require military planning. The moment intelligence agencies, defense ministries, or naval forces must respond the dispute is no longer ‚Äútrade‚Äù‚Äîit is ‚Äúeconomic warfare.‚Äù

X. Exception: When Propaganda is an Act of War

Propaganda very rarely qualifies as an act of war on its own. In international law, propaganda is usually treated as ‚Äúhostile influence activity,‚Äù not armed force. But it can cross the threshold under specific, extreme conditions. Below is the cleanest, War College level breakdown.

XI. Propaganda Is Not an Act of War by Default

These do NOT qualify as acts of war:

  • spreading disinformation
  • talking points or political narratives
  • news manipulation
  • foreign-run media outlets
  • online influence campaigns
  • psychological operations during peacetime
  • political subversion that does not accompany force

Because international law only classifies ‚Äúarmed attack‚Äù or ‚Äúuse of force‚Äù as war‚Äîand speech does not count as force. Even aggressive foreign propaganda is usually tolerated as ‚Äúgray-zone activity.‚Äù

XII. When Propaganda Can Qualify as an Act of War

Propaganda becomes an act of war when it crosses into incitement, command, or coordination that triggers violence or military operations. Here are the legally and historically recognized thresholds:

  1. Propaganda that Directly Incites Armed Attack

If a state broadcasts or publishes messages explicitly calling for:

  • violence
  • uprising
  • attacks on a population
  • military assault

If those messages are part of a state-directed strategy, it becomes incitement to armed aggression.

Example:

  • Radio Mille Collines in Rwanda (1994)
  • Encouraged genocidal violence
  • International tribunals treated it as participation in war crimes
  1. Propaganda Used as Command-and-Control for Military Operations

If broadcasts are used to:

  • coordinate troops
  • signal attacks
  • guide insurgents
  • relay military instructions disguised as propaganda
  • that becomes direct participation in hostilities.

Example:

WWII radio broadcasting coded messages to resistance groups treated as war activity, not ‚Äúexpression.‚Äù

  1. Propaganda Intended to Cause Regime Collapse or Mass Instability

Most psychological operations (PSYOP) are legal. But when propaganda‚Äôs explicit purpose is:

  • overthrowing the government
  • destabilizing the state
  • encouraging army desertion
  • inspiring collapse of national defense

If it is state-sponsored, some nations treat this as political warfare equivalent to armed attack. This is how the Cold War conceptualized ‚Äúsubversive propaganda.‚Äù

  1. Propaganda Combined With Cyber, Covert Ops, or Economic Coercion
  • Used alone, propaganda is not war.
  • Used as part of a coordinated hybrid warfare strategy, it often qualifies.

Example hybrid package:

  • propaganda operations
  • cyberattacks against infrastructure
  • economic coercion
  • armed proxy groups

This combination is sometimes treated as hybrid aggression, which may justify military response.

Example:

  • Russia‚Äôs 2014 Crimea strategy (Information warfare + proxies + covert forces)

  • NATO formally classifies such campaigns as hostile acts.

  1. Propaganda Used to Facilitate Genocide or Crimes Against Humanity

International criminal law is clear:

  • ‚ÄúDirect and public incitement to commit genocide‚Äù is a punishable act equal in gravity to kinetic violence.

This is the one branch of pure speech recognized as a direct component of war crimes.

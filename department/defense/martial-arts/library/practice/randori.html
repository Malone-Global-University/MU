Randori 乱取り

Free practice under dynamic resistance and changing conditions

Home Department Defense Martial Arts M.A.L. Randori

Definition

Randori refers to free-form training in which techniques are applied against unpredictable, resisting, or changing conditions. It is the transition from structured practice into adaptive engagement.

Concept

Randori is a controlled form of chaos. Unlike kata, which encodes fixed structure, randori requires continuous interpretation of timing, distance, and movement in real time. It is where principles must function without pre-arranged sequence.

Functional Role

  • Tests adaptability of technique under pressure
  • Develops real-time decision-making and timing (Sen)
  • Integrates space (Ma-ai), movement (Tai Sabaki), and structure under uncertainty
  • Reveals gaps between theoretical knowledge and applied skill

Related Concepts

  • Kata (Structured Form)
  • Kihon → Oyo → Henka
  • Ma-ai (Distance & Engagement Range)
  • Sen (Initiative & Timing)
  • Zanshin (Continuous Awareness)

Application Across Arts

In aikido, randori involves continuous attacks requiring blending, redirection, and flow-based control. In karate, it appears as sparring with timing, distance, and striking exchanges. In judo and jujutsu, randori is live grappling where grips, balance, and throws emerge dynamically under resistance.

Common Misunderstanding

Randori is often mistaken for uncontrolled fighting or winning/losing exchanges. In reality, it is structured exploration of adaptability. Without principles, randori becomes chaos; without randori, principles remain untested.

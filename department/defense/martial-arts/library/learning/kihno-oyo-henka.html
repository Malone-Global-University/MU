Kihon → Oyo → Henka

Progression from foundation, to application, to adaptive transformation

Home Department Defense Martial Arts M.A.L. Kihon → Oyo → Henka

Definition

Kihon → Oyo → Henka describes the progression of technique development from fundamental form (Kihon), to applied use (Oyo), to adaptive variation and transformation (Henka).

Concept

This model explains how a single technique evolves through stages of understanding. Kihon establishes structure, Oyo introduces context, and Henka allows fluid adaptation under changing conditions. Each stage contains the previous one rather than replacing it.

Functional Role

  • Kihon builds structural correctness and repetition
  • Oyo develops contextual application under variation
  • Henka enables adaptive, real-time transformation
  • Connects technical training to live, unpredictable conditions

Related Concepts

  • Kata (Structured Practice)
  • Randori (Free Practice)
  • Shuhari (Learning Progression)
  • Keiko (Deliberate Practice)

Application Across Arts

In aikido, Kihon is the base form of techniques, Oyo is application against realistic attacks, and Henka is adaptive blending under resistance. In karate, Kihon is basics, Oyo is bunkai (application), and Henka is free adaptation in sparring. In judo, it moves from static throws to dynamic entries to responsive grappling transitions.

Common Misunderstanding

This model is often misunderstood as separate levels or curriculum stages. In reality, they operate simultaneously in advanced practice. Overemphasis on Kihon alone leads to rigidity; skipping it leads to unstable technique.

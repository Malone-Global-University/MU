Core Principles

Mental health is essential to personal well-being, family stability, and community resilience. African Americans face unique structural barriers that reduce access to care, amplify stigma, and contribute to intergenerational trauma. Policy must remove these barriers, prioritize culturally competent services, and treat mental health as a public health priority, not a moral failing.

Key Challenges in the African American Community

  • Limited access to culturally competent therapists and clinics.
  • High rates of trauma, violence, and exposure to systemic stressors.
  • Stigma around mental health, discouraging treatment-seeking behavior.
  • Underfunded community-based mental health programs.
  • Criminalization of mental health crises instead of treatment-based responses.
  • Insufficient integration of mental health services in schools and workplaces.

Policy Positions

1. Expand Access to Care

  • Increase funding for mental health clinics in Black-majority communities.
  • Expand telehealth and mobile mental health services for underserved areas.
  • Provide universal coverage for mental health care, including therapy, counseling, and psychiatric services.

2. Culturally Competent Services

  • Train clinicians in the cultural realities of African American communities.
  • Support Black mental health professionals through scholarships and grants.
  • Include community leaders and faith-based institutions in awareness and education campaigns.

3. School-Based Mental Health Programs

  • Hire school counselors, social workers, and psychologists in every public school.
  • Implement early detection programs for trauma, depression, and anxiety.
  • Provide anti-bullying and emotional intelligence curricula for all students.

4. Trauma-Informed Community Support

  • Fund community centers with counseling and peer-support programs.
  • Support survivors of violence, incarceration, and systemic stressors.
  • Integrate mental health resources with housing, employment, and youth programs.

5. Crisis Response Reform

  • Replace police responses to mental health crises with trained crisis intervention teams.
  • Establish 24/7 hotlines staffed by licensed professionals.
  • Implement community-based de-escalation programs and safe spaces.

6. Reduce Stigma & Promote Awareness

  • Launch campaigns highlighting mental health as a component of overall wellness.
  • Partner with faith institutions, media, and civic organizations to normalize therapy.
  • Promote mentorship and peer support for adolescents and young adults.

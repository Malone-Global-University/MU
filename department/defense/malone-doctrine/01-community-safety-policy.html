Core Principles

Community safety is the foundation of stable, disciplined, and prosperous neighborhoods. For generations, African American communities have endured a system that is simultaneously over-policed, under-protected, and structurally neglected.

MGUâ€™s position is clear: Safety is achieved through community leadership, not dependency; through opportunity, not surveillance; and through authentic representation, not code-switching policing.

MGU rejects the idea that African Americans must choose between state hostility and no security. Instead, we advance a third option:

A new model of policing that integrates African Americans authentically into the civic structure while reducing the long-term need for law enforcement through prevention, opportunity, and community direction.

Key Challenges

  • Community Distrust of Police: African Americans distrust white police due to patterns of racial biasâ€”and distrust African American police when they code switch to reinforce harmful policing norms.
  • Lack of Leadership and Direction: Violence rises when communities are led by individuals who are uncommitted or constrained by broken systems.
  • Structural Barriers to Opportunity: Neighborhoods trapped in poverty, unemployment, and limited pathways for advancement experience instability.
  • Toxic American Culture: Across races, culture promotes dysfunction; African Americans face additional externally imposed low expectations.
  • Underinvestment in Preventative Solutions: Mentorship, recreation, infrastructure, and community design are devalued but crucial for long-term safety.
  • Misdiagnosis of Violence: MGU views violence as systemic and structuralâ€”not psychiatric. People are underserved, dismissed, and deprived of purpose.

Policy Positions

1. Build Authentic African American Policing Leadership

  • Recruit and train African Americans from the community to serve as officers without code switching.
  • Promote officers who demonstrate integrity, cultural fluency, and fairness.
  • Integrate African Americans into law enforcement to break barriers and reconstruct legitimacy.

2. National Training, Auditing & Cultural Reform

  • Establish national training focused on de-escalation, ethics, constitutional conduct, and cultural respect.
  • Build independent auditing systems to remove abusive officersâ€”regardless of race.
  • Reform policing culture from the inside out while maintaining professional partnerships with existing departments.

3. Strategic Defunding to Reduce Crime at Its Source

  • Reallocate funds to job training, education, leadership development, and youth opportunity.
  • Invest in urban renewal, infrastructure, and environmental design to reduce crime organically.
  • Reduce the demand for police rather than eliminating the institution.

4. Leadership and Opportunity as Foundations of Safety

  • Expand programs that identify, train, and elevate community leaders.
  • Create mentorship pipelines for youth and young adults.
  • Strengthen economic mobility, entrepreneurship, job creation, and civic participation.
  • Reject over-medicalization: people need direction, opportunity, and structureâ€”not labels.

5. Community-Directed Safety Infrastructure

  • Establish neighborhood safety councils with real oversight power.
  • Allow residents to participate in officer evaluations, cultural standards, and local policy review.
  • Develop block-level systems for crisis prevention, conflict resolution, and early intervention.

6. Prevention-Focused Environmental Design

  • Improve lighting, public spaces, sidewalks, vacant lots, and community infrastructure.
  • Build parks, recreation centers, and physical training facilities.
  • Support community gardens and shared spaces that foster cohesion and reduce isolation.

What Is Stratospheric Aerosol Injection?

Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI) is a proposed geoengineering technique in which reflective particles are dispersed into the stratosphere‚Äîthe atmospheric layer 10 to 50 kilometers above Earth's surface‚Äîto reflect a portion of incoming sunlight back into space.

The goal is to artificially cool the planet and offset global warming, a subset of strategies known as Solar Radiation Management (SRM).

Potential particles include:

  • Sulfates (similar to volcanic eruptions)
  • Calcium carbonate (a less acidic alternative)
  • Titanium dioxide
  • Aluminum oxides (controversial in conspiracy discussions)

Scientific Basis

SAI is inspired by natural volcanic eruptions. The 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo injected millions of tons of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, lowering global temperatures by about 0.5¬∞C for nearly two years.

Scientists propose that replicating this effect artificially could temporarily reduce global warming. The cooling would last only as long as injections continued.

How SAI Would Be Delivered

  • High-altitude aircraft (the most commonly proposed method)
  • Stratospheric balloons
  • Artillery or rockets (theoretical)
  • Tethered platforms

Proposed Benefits

Advocates for SAI argue that it could:

  • Rapidly lower global temperatures
  • Reduce extreme weather linked to climate change
  • Protect ice sheets and slow sea-level rise
  • Provide time for deeper emissions cuts

SAI is not considered a solution to climate change, but rather a temporary cooling method.

Risks and Concerns

SAI remains one of the most controversial scientific proposals of the 21st century. Critics warn that it may cause more problems than it solves.

Major Risks Include:

  • Changes to rainfall patterns ‚Äî potential droughts in some regions
  • Ozone layer damage ‚Äî sulfate aerosols can trigger chemical reactions that thin the ozone
  • Temperature rebound if SAI abruptly stops (‚Äútermination shock‚Äù)
  • Geopolitical conflicts over who controls the global thermostat
  • Environmental and health concerns related to the type of aerosols used

Because SAI affects the entire planet, any deployment would require broad international agreements ‚Äî none currently exist.

Ethical and Political Issues

SAI poses unprecedented ethical dilemmas:

  • Who has the authority to inject aerosols globally?
  • Can one nation alter the climate without global consent?
  • Could SAI be weaponized?
  • Would SAI reduce pressure to cut carbon emissions?
  • Who is liable if SAI causes droughts or crop failures?

These questions have kept the technology from moving beyond small-scale scientific studies.

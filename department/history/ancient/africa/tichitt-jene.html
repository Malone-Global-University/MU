Tichitt Tradition & Jenne-Jeno

Early urbanized settlements in West Africa known for stone architecture, agriculture, trade, and craft specialization.

Overview

These settlements were centers of **agriculture, craft production, and trade**, laying the groundwork for later empires like Ghana, Mali, and Songhai.

Archaeological evidence shows carefully planned **stone compounds, organized streets, and fortified areas**, reflecting complex social organization.

Major Settlements & Architecture

  • Tichitt: Stone-walled villages with houses arranged in compounds, located near river valleys for agriculture.
  • Jenne-Jeno: One of the first true urban centers in Sub-Saharan Africa, featuring neighborhoods, public spaces, and specialized craft areas.
  • Fortifications and walls at both sites suggest organized defense and social hierarchy.

Culture & Society

- **Agriculture:** Millet, sorghum, and rice cultivation supported large populations.
- **Craft Specialization:** Ironworking, pottery, and weaving were common.
- **Trade:** Local and regional trade of gold, salt, and other resources.
- **Social Structure:** Evidence of hierarchy with elite compounds and communal spaces.

Key Achievements

  • Urban planning with streets, neighborhoods, and communal structures.
  • Stone architecture in Tichitt, showing advanced building techniques.
  • Early participation in trans-Saharan and regional trade networks.
  • Foundation for later West African kingdoms through social and economic innovations.

Legacy

The Tichitt Tradition and Jenne-Jeno demonstrate that **complex, urbanized societies existed in West Africa long before the rise of classical empires**. They provided models of agriculture, craft, trade, and urban planning that influenced later civilizations like the Ghana, Mali, and Songhai empires.

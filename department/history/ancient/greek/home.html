Greek City States

The story of the Greek city-states (poleis): from their rise after the Dark Age to their peak in the Classical world, and eventual decline under Macedon and Rome.

Introduction

The number of Greek city-states (poleis, singular: polis) varied over time and is hard to pin down to a single figure, because:

  • New poleis were founded during colonization (especially in Italy, Sicily, Asia Minor, and the Black Sea).
  • Some merged or were destroyed in wars.
  • Others were very small and may not always have been counted.

Historians estimate:

  • Classical Greece (5th‚Äì4th century BCE): Around 1,000 to 1,500 poleis existed across the Greek world.
  • The historian Mogens Herman Hansen‚Äôs Inventory of Archaic and Classical Poleis (2004) catalogues over 1,000 poleis with evidence of political organization.
  • The most famous ones (Athens, Sparta, Corinth, Thebes, Delphi, Olympia, etc.) are well-known, but the vast majority were small, rural, or obscure.

Origins of the Polieis

The origins of the Greek city-states (poleis) go very far back, but the first "recorded history" are given as:

1. Proto-Poleis in the Archaic Period

After the collapse of the Mycenaean palaces (c. 1200 BCE), Greece entered the so-called Dark Ages (c. 1100‚Äì800 BCE). Villages and tribal communities slowly reorganized, and by the 8th century BCE they crystallized into poleis ‚Äî independent, self-governing city-states.h e earliest recorded traces of poleis appear in Homer‚Äôs Iliad and Odyssey (c. 750 BCE), which describe cities like Mycenae, Troy, Pylos, and Ithaca. While semi-legendary, they reflect the memory of urban communities.

2. Earliest Historical Mentions

The first true historical evidence for Greek city-states comes from the 8th century BCE, when inscriptions, poetry, and foreign sources begin to name them.

For example:

  • Olympia (776 BCE) ‚Äì the first recorded Olympic Games, an inter-polis event.
  • Colonization records (8th‚Äì7th centuries BCE) ‚Äì inscriptions and traditions of founding colonies like Syracuse, Massalia (Marseille), and Byzantium.
  • Hesiod (c. 700 BCE) ‚Äì his Works and Days reflects a world of small farmers in a polis society.

3. First Political History

The earliest written political histories of Greek poleis come from Herodotus (c. 440 BCE) in his Histories. He describes city-states like Athens, Sparta, Corinth, and Thebes in detail, especially their roles in the Persian Wars. Later historians like Thucydides (c. 400 BCE) cover the Peloponnesian War (Athens vs. Sparta), giving us the fullest early record of polis politics.

Summary:

  • Origins: Communities forming after 1200 BCE.
  • First traces: Homer (c. 750 BCE) mentions cities.
  • First events: 776 BCE Olympic Games and 8th‚Äì7th century colonization.
  • First history: Herodotus (5th century BCE) gives the earliest systematic history of the city-states.

Timeline of the Greek City-States

1. Formation Period (c. 1100‚Äì800 BCE)

  • 1100 BCE ‚Äì Collapse of the Mycenaean palaces ‚Üí beginning of the ‚ÄúDark Ages.‚Äù
  • 900‚Äì800 BCE ‚Äì Small villages begin to cluster into early poleis.
  • 776 BCE ‚Äì First Olympic Games, a pan-Hellenic event linking city-states.
  • 750‚Äì700 BCE ‚Äì Homer‚Äôs epics (Iliad and Odyssey) reflect early polis culture.

2. Archaic Age ‚Äì Rise of the Polis (c. 800‚Äì500 BCE)

  • 800‚Äì600 BCE ‚Äì Colonization movement ‚Üí poleis found colonies across the Mediterranean (Sicily, Italy, Black Sea, North Africa).
  • c. 700 BCE ‚Äì Emergence of city constitutions; aristocratic rule dominates.
  • c. 650‚Äì500 BCE ‚Äì Rise of tyrants in many poleis (non-hereditary rulers who seize power).
  • Sparta develops its militarized oligarchy.
  • Athens begins reforms: Draco (c. 620 BCE), Solon (c. 594 BCE), and Cleisthenes (c. 508 BCE).

3. Classical Age ‚Äì Height of the Polis (c. 500‚Äì336 BCE)

  • 499‚Äì449 BCE ‚Äì Persian Wars unite many poleis against Persia (Marathon 490, Thermopylae/Salamis 480, Plataea 479). Athens and Sparta rise as leaders.
  • 478 BCE ‚Äì Delian League (led by Athens) formed against Persia.
  • 460‚Äì445 BCE ‚Äì Athens‚Äô Golden Age under Pericles; democracy, drama, philosophy, and art flourish.
  • 431‚Äì404 BCE ‚Äì Peloponnesian War (Athens vs. Sparta + allies). Sparta wins, but Greece is weakened.
  • 371 BCE ‚Äì Battle of Leuctra: Thebes defeats Sparta, ending Spartan dominance.
  • 362 BCE ‚Äì Battle of Mantinea: Thebes‚Äô brief hegemony ends. No polis dominates; instability grows.

4. Decline and Loss of Independence (336‚Äì146 BCE)

  • 338 BCE ‚Äì Battle of Chaeronea: Philip II of Macedon defeats Athens & Thebes. Poleis lose independence, though they retain some local identity.
  • 336‚Äì323 BCE ‚Äì Alexander the Great expands Macedonian power, spreading Hellenistic culture.
  • 323‚Äì146 BCE ‚Äì Hellenistic kingdoms (Macedonia, Egypt, Seleucid Asia) overshadow the city-states, which now play only regional roles.
  • 146 BCE ‚Äì Roman conquest of Greece after the Battle of Corinth. The polis system effectively ends as an independent political order, though cities continue under Roman administration.

Summary:

  • Rise: 1100‚Äì500 BCE (formation and colonization).
  • Peak: 500‚Äì336 BCE (Classical Age: democracy, wars, art, philosophy).
  • Fall: 336‚Äì146 BCE (Macedonian domination, then Rome).

Cultural Achievements by Period

1. Formation / Dark Age (c. 1100‚Äì800 BCE)

  • Oral tradition ‚Äì Epic poetry preserved by bards, later written down as Homer‚Äôs Iliad and Odyssey.
  • Geometric art ‚Äì Pottery with abstract motifs and simple human/animal figures.
  • Religious continuity ‚Äì Mycenaean gods evolve into the Olympian pantheon (Zeus, Hera, Athena, etc.).
  • Social structure ‚Äì Kinship-based communities begin moving toward organized poleis.

2. Archaic Age (c. 800‚Äì500 BCE)

Literature & Poetry:

  • Homer‚Äôs Iliad & Odyssey (c. 750 BCE).
  • Hesiod‚Äôs Works and Days and Theogony (c. 700 BCE) ‚Üí early philosophy on justice, labor, and the gods.
  • Alphabet ‚Äì Greeks adapt the Phoenician alphabet (c. 800 BCE), enabling widespread literacy.

Architecture:

  • First stone temples (7th‚Äì6th c. BCE), e.g., Temple of Hera at Olympia.
  • Doric and Ionic orders begin.

Sculpture:

Kouros and kore statues (rigid, Egyptian-influenced, but moving toward realism).

Political innovation:

  • Rise of law codes (Draco, Solon).
  • Birth of democracy in Athens under Cleisthenes (508 BCE).

Pan-Hellenic culture:

  • Olympic Games (first recorded 776 BCE).
  • Shared sanctuaries at Delphi, Olympia, and Delos.

3. Classical Age (c. 500‚Äì336 BCE)

The Golden Age of the Polis

Philosophy:

Socrates, Plato, Aristotle lay foundations of Western philosophy, logic, ethics, and political thought.

Drama:

  • Tragedy: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides.
  • Comedy: Aristophanes.

History writing:

  • Herodotus (Histories) ‚Äì father of history.
  • Thucydides (Peloponnesian War) ‚Äì analytical, critical history.

Architecture & Art:

  • Parthenon in Athens (447‚Äì432 BCE).
  • Perfected Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian orders.
  • Realistic sculpture: Polykleitos, Phidias, Praxiteles.

Science & Mathematics:

  • Hippocrates (medicine).
  • Pythagoras (math, philosophy).
  • Democritus (atomic theory).

Politics:

  • Full democracy in Athens (citizens‚Äô assemblies, juries).
  • Sparta‚Äôs military oligarchy as contrast.

4. Hellenistic Age (336‚Äì146 BCE)

After Alexander the Great ‚Äì city-states decline politically but thrive culturally.

Art:

  • More emotional, dramatic sculpture (e.g., Laoco√∂n Group, Winged Victory of Samothrace).
  • Spread of Greek styles across Egypt, Persia, India.

Philosophy:

Science & Technology:

‚ÄúHellenistic‚Äù fusion of Greek, Egyptian, Persian, and Indian traditions.

Summary in one line per period:

  • Dark Age ‚Äì Oral epic, geometric art, pantheon.
  • Archaic ‚Äì Alphabet, temples, early philosophy, Olympics.
  • Classical ‚Äì Philosophy, drama, history, Parthenon, science.
  • Hellenistic ‚Äì Global science, dramatic art, great libraries, new philosophies.

Secular Government & Life in Ancient Greece

1. Dark Age (c. 1100‚Äì800 BCE)

Government:

Local chiefs/basileis ruled small villages.

Power was kinship-based, not institutionalized.

Life:

  • Economy = subsistence farming, herding.
  • Trade minimal after Mycenaean collapse.
  • Oral tradition preserved history and law.

2. Archaic Period (c. 800‚Äì500 BCE)

Government:

  • Rise of the polis (city-state) ‚Üí independent political units with citizens.
  • Law codes: Draco (Athens, c. 620 BCE), Solon‚Äôs reforms (c. 594 BCE).
  • Tyrants briefly ruled many poleis, often expanding trade and building works.
  • Sparta created military oligarchy; Athens pioneered democracy (Cleisthenes, 508 BCE).

Life:

  • Colonization movement spread Greek culture to Italy, Sicily, Black Sea, and beyond.
  • Aristocracy dominated land ownership, but merchant/trader classes grew.
  • Hoplite warfare emerges (citizen-soldier phalanx).
  • Public festivals (Olympics, Panhellenic Games) unify Greeks culturally.

3. Classical Period (c. 500‚Äì336 BCE)

Government:

  • Athens develops direct democracy (citizens‚Äô assembly, juries, ostracism).
  • Sparta = dual kings + council + ephors.
  • Alliances: Delian League (Athens) and Peloponnesian League (Sparta).
  • Peloponnesian War shows fragility of polis politics.

Life:

  • Economy: trade flourishes (wine, olive oil, pottery).
  • Social classes: citizens, metics (resident foreigners), slaves.
  • Education: Athens (liberal arts, rhetoric), Sparta (military training).
  • Theater, philosophy, public debate = civic life centered around the agora (marketplace).
  • Architecture & public works (Acropolis, Parthenon) ‚Üí civic pride and identity.

4. Hellenistic Period (336‚Äì146 BCE)

Government:

  • Independent poleis lose sovereignty ‚Üí absorbed into Macedonian then Roman systems.
  • Local autonomy survives (city councils, magistrates), but kings/empires hold real power.

Life:

  • Cities (Alexandria, Pergamon, Antioch) become cosmopolitan centers of learning and trade.
  • Middle-class rise ‚Üí more participation in commerce than politics.
  • Daily life influenced by luxury goods, theaters, gymnasia, libraries.
  • Cultural fusion with Egyptian, Persian, and Near Eastern traditions.
  • Spread of Greek language (Koine) makes communication and commerce global.

Key Takeaways (Secular Focus):

  • Government evolved from tribal chiefs ‚Üí aristocrats ‚Üí tyrants ‚Üí democracy/oligarchy ‚Üí Macedonian/Roman domination.
  • Daily life revolved around citizenship, the agora, warfare, trade, and festivals.
  • By the Hellenistic era, Greek city-states lost political independence but spread Greek culture, law, and lifestyle across the Mediterranean.

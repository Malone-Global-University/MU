Naram-Sin, grandson of Sargon, conducted a campaign against the Lullubi, a mountainous people of the Zagros region, around 2254 BCE. His victory is famously commemorated on the Victory Stele, which visually represents both military success and the divine sanction of the king.

Historical context

The Akkadian Empire under Naram-Sin had expanded into northern and eastern territories. The Lullubi resisted Akkadian control, prompting a military campaign that reinforced central authority and projected imperial power beyond Mesopotamian lowlands.

Principal actors

  • Naram-Sin: King of Akkad, grandson of Sargon, celebrated for military prowess and self-declared divine kingship.
  • Lullubi: Mountain tribes of the Zagros resisting Akkadian expansion.

  2. March into Zagros: Forces move into Lullubi territory, overcoming natural obstacles of the highlands.

Political and ideological significance

The campaign highlights the merging of military success and divine kingship in Akkadian ideology. Naram-Sin‚Äôs portrayal as ascending the mountain while trampling enemies emphasizes his role as both conqueror and divinely sanctioned ruler.

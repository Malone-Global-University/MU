Around 1800 BCE, Hammurabi of Babylon embarked on a series of military campaigns that transformed the political landscape of Mesopotamia. Through strategic warfare, diplomacy, and city-state alliances, he consolidated control over Sumer, Akkad, and surrounding regions, laying the foundation for the Old Babylonian Empire.

Historical context

After the decline of Isin and Larsa, Mesopotamia was fragmented among competing city-states. Babylon, initially one among many small powers, rose under Hammurabi's leadership. His campaigns were designed to assert supremacy over neighboring cities, secure trade routes, and reinforce the kingâ€™s legitimacy.

Principal actors

  • Babylon: Hammurabi, king of Babylon, employing military and diplomatic strategies to unify Mesopotamia.
  • Larsa: Rival southern city-state, eventually subdued by Hammurabi.
  • Mari: Northern city-state, initially independent but later brought under Babylonian influence.
  • Other city-states: Ur, Isin, Eshnunna, and regional polities resisting or allying with Babylon.

Sources & evidence

  • Cuneiform administrative texts tracking tribute, taxation, and army mobilization.
  • Archaeological evidence of destruction layers in key cities like Larsa and Eshnunna.
  • Contemporary correspondence, such as letters and year names recording military campaigns.

  3. Northern campaigns: Military actions against Eshnunna and Mari to secure the northern trade routes and frontier cities.
  4. Unification and administration: Establishing Babylon as the central authority, integrating conquered cities, and standardizing governance.

Military organization

  • Infantry composed of city levies, conscripts, and specialized troops for siege warfare.
  • Use of war wagons for command mobility and battlefield impact.
  • Coordination with allied city-states to supplement forces and secure strategic positions.

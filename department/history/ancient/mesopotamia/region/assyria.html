Origins

Assyria grew around the city of Ashur on the upper Tigris. At first, it was not an empire but a network of merchant colonies trading tin, textiles, and silver across Anatolia (modern Turkey). The Old Assyrian period reflects a mercantile people building wealth and contacts rather than waging conquest.

Over centuries, Assyria transformed: from merchant city-state to warrior kingdom, then to a world-dominating empire whose armies reshaped the Near East.

Decline and Fall

By the late 7th century BCE, Assyria was overstretched. Rebellions in Babylon, attacks from Medes and Persians in the east, and the rise of Neo-Babylon under Nabopolassar spelled doom.

In 612 BCE, a coalition of Babylonians and Medes sacked Nineveh. By 609 BCE, Assyria had vanished as an independent state.

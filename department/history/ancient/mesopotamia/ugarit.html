Origins

Located on the Mediterranean coast in modern Syria, Ugarit flourished as a port city connecting Mesopotamia, Anatolia, and Egypt. Archaeological evidence shows urban planning, temples, and early fortifications by the mid-2nd millennium BCE.

Rise and Influence

Ugarit thrived through maritime trade, diplomatic correspondence, and cultural exchange. It developed the first fully alphabetic cuneiform script, influencing later Phoenician and Greek writing systems.

Religion and Deities

Ugarit worshiped a pantheon including Baal (storm god), Dagan (chief god), and Anat (goddess of war and fertility). Religious rituals were central to civic and diplomatic life.

Decline

Ugarit was destroyed around 1200 BCE, likely by the Sea Peoples or related regional upheavals, ending its political independence. Its alphabetic script, however, influenced future civilizations, leaving a lasting cultural legacy.

Origins

The Hittites emerged in central Anatolia (modern Turkey) around 1800 BCE as a cluster of city-states. By c. 1600 BCE, under King Labarna and his successors, they consolidated into a unified kingdom centered on Hattusa, establishing the foundation of the Hittite Empire.

Rise and Expansion

The Hittite Empire expanded through military campaigns, alliances, and strategic diplomacy, becoming a dominant power in the Late Bronze Age.

Religion and Deities

The Hittites practiced a syncretic religion, integrating Hattian, Hurrian, and Mesopotamian deities. Key gods included Tarhunt (storm god), Arinna (sun goddess), and Kumarbi (Hurrian father god). Rituals, treaties, and royal inscriptions often invoked divine sanction.

Decline and Fall

The Hittite Empire collapsed around 1178 BCE during the Late Bronze Age upheaval. Internal conflicts, invasions by the Sea Peoples, and disruptions to trade networks contributed to the fall, leaving Anatolia fragmented into Neo-Hittite city-states.

What We Know, Assume, and Still Wonder

What We Know

  • The Hittites controlled much of Anatolia and northern Syria at their peak.
  • They were skilled diplomats, issuing treaties and alliances recognized by neighboring powers.
  • Chariot warfare and archery were key military strengths.

What We Assume

  • The Hittite ruling elite integrated multiple cultural traditions to maintain control.
  • Trade and tribute helped sustain the empire economically.
  • Religious and legal systems reinforced royal authority and societal cohesion.

What Remains Unanswered

  • Details of daily life for commoners under Hittite rule.
  • The precise causes and timeline of the collapse during the Bronze Age upheavals.
  • Extent of Hittite influence on neighboring civilizations post-collapse.

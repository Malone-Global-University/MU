Origins

By the early 18th century BCE, Mesopotamia was a mosaic of Amorite city-states: Isin, Larsa, Mari, and Babylon among them. Babylon began as a relatively minor kingdom, overshadowed by its rivals. That changed when Hammurabi ascended the throne around 1792 BCE, inheriting a city strategically located along key trade routes and the Euphrates River.

The Rise of Babylon

Through diplomacy, shifting alliances, and calculated wars, Hammurabi transformed Babylon into the dominant power of Mesopotamia. He first secured his borders, then systematically defeated rivals like Larsa, Mari, and Eshnunna. By 1750 BCE, Hammurabi controlled nearly all of southern Mesopotamia and parts of the north, forging the first Babylonian Empire.

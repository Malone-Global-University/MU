Out of Africa: Human Migration

Follow the journey of Homo sapiens as they spread across the globe, shaping human history.

Introduction

Modern humans (Homo sapiens) first evolved in Africa roughly 300,000 years ago. For tens of thousands of years, they lived exclusively on the African continent, developing tools, culture, and social structures. Beginning around 70,000‚Äì60,000 years ago, groups of humans began migrating out of Africa, eventually populating every continent except Antarctica. These migrations shaped the genetic, cultural, and technological diversity of humanity.

Routes of Migration

Researchers have identified two primary routes out of Africa:

  • Northern Route: Across the Sinai into the Levant and then into Europe and Central Asia.
  • Southern Coastal Route: Across the Bab el-Mandeb Strait into South Asia, Southeast Asia, and eventually Oceania.

These routes often overlapped and included stops where humans adapted to new climates, developed new technologies, and sometimes interbred with local archaic humans like Neanderthals and Denisovans.

Significance

The out-of-Africa migrations were a defining chapter of human prehistory. They spread modern humans across the globe, introduced cultural and technological innovations to new regions, and set the stage for later developments in agriculture, urbanization, and civilization. Genetic evidence from modern populations confirms these migrations and helps trace the shared heritage of all humans.

Next Steps

After reaching all continents, humans transitioned from mobile hunter-gatherer groups to settled agricultural communities. This leads directly to the rise of early villages, cities, and civilizations, which you can explore in the Global Prehistory and Sumer pages.

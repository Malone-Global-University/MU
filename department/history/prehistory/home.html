Global Prehistory: Humans Before Cities

Long before the rise of cities, kings, and written laws, humans were already shaping the world in remarkable and diverse ways. Across every continent, early people hunted game, gathered wild plants, and gradually learned to manipulate their environment to meet their needs.

Imagine a world without cities, kings, or written laws, where humans roamed vast landscapes, crafted the first tools, and painted their stories on cave walls. Across every continent, early humans hunted, gathered, farmed, and formed the first villages. From Africa‚Äôs cradle to the islands of Oceania, prehistory was a dynamic era of invention, adaptation, and discovery, laying the foundation for the civilizations that would follow.

In Africa, the cradle of humanity, Homo sapiens first appeared, developing stone tools, controlling fire, and leaving traces of symbolic expression in the form of beads and engraved objects. Europe became home to Neanderthals and early modern humans who left behind cave paintings in Lascaux and Chauvet, revealing not only technical skill but a capacity for imagination and storytelling. In Asia, Homo erectus and Denisovans thrived, while in the Americas, early humans crossed vast landscapes, adapting to new climates and ecosystems. Oceania‚Äôs early inhabitants navigated oceans to settle islands, practicing horticulture and building unique cultural traditions.

During the Neolithic Revolution, roughly 10,000 years ago, humans across the globe began domesticating plants and animals, giving rise to permanent villages in places as diverse as Jericho, √áatalh√∂y√ºk, and the highlands of New Guinea. These small settlements experimented with pottery, weaving, and communal organization, laying the groundwork for social hierarchies, ritual practices, and technology.

By the Chalcolithic period, people in Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, China, and Europe were creating increasingly complex societies, experimenting with irrigation, metallurgy, and monumental architecture. From the first symbolic markings to the earliest urban centers, prehistory was not a static or uniform era, but a rich and dynamic story of human innovation, migration, adaptation, and cultural flourishing that ultimately set the stage for the civilizations we recognize today.

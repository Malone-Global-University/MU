Indigenous and Pre-Colonial Period

The Nacotchtank and Piscataway peoples inhabited the Potomac River region for millennia, relying on fishing, agriculture, and trade. Their cultural and political systems structured the region prior to European arrival.

Colonial Claims and Early European Presence

European settlers, primarily English, claimed the Potomac region in the 17th century. Land grants, plantations, and trade developed, often displacing Indigenous populations. The area was strategically located for commerce, governance, and military purposes.

Creation of the Federal District

The Residence Act of 1790 established the District of Columbia along the Potomac River. The city became the center of federal governance, housing Congress, the White House, and judicial institutions.

19th Century Development

Washington, D.C., experienced political growth, urbanization, and conflict. The War of 1812 saw the British burn government buildings. Slavery, segregation, and African American community development shaped social and labor dynamics. Infrastructure and transportation expanded, connecting the capital to the nation.

20th Century Growth and Labor

The city evolved with federal expansion, civil service growth, and wartime mobilization. Labor movements, strikes, and unionization influenced municipal and federal sectors. Civil rights activism, urban planning, and public works transformed housing, transportation, and community development.

Indigenous and Pre-Colonial Period

Indiana was originally home to Miami, Shawnee, Potawatomi, and other Algonquian-speaking tribes. Communities managed riverine and woodland ecosystems, cultivated maize, and maintained complex trade networks. European contact disrupted traditional societies through disease, displacement, and later, forced treaties.

Settlement and Statehood

After the Northwest Indian War and Treaty of Greenville (1795), European-American settlers from Kentucky, Ohio, and Pennsylvania migrated into Indiana. The territory was formally organized in 1800 and admitted as the 19th state in 1816. Early development centered on agriculture, river transport, and small manufacturing, with slavery prohibited but southern cultural influence present in southern counties.

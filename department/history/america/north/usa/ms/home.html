Indigenous and Pre-Colonial Period

Mississippi was inhabited by the Choctaw, Chickasaw, Natchez, and other Indigenous groups. Communities relied on maize agriculture, riverine fishing, and complex trade networks along the Mississippi River. Social organization included clan systems, ceremonial hierarchies, and strategic alliances with neighboring tribes.

European Contact and Colonization

French explorers and settlers arrived in the late 17th century, establishing forts and trading posts along the Mississippi River. Colonial policies relied on alliances and trade with Indigenous populations. After Spanish and British influence, the territory came under U.S. control in the early 19th century, opening lands for plantation agriculture.

Civil War and Reconstruction

Labor and Economic Development

Agricultural labor, particularly sharecropping and tenant farming, dominated until mechanization in the 20th century. Industrial development was slower compared to Northern states, with timber, river shipping, and small manufacturing concentrated regionally. Labor disputes were often localized but reflected broader racial and economic inequalities.

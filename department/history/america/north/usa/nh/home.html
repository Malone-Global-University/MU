Indigenous and Pre-Colonial Period

New Hampshire was originally inhabited by Abenaki and Pennacook peoples, who relied on seasonal hunting, fishing, and agriculture. Villages and trade networks extended through the Connecticut River Valley and into neighboring regions, maintaining social, political, and ceremonial cohesion.

European Colonization

English settlers arrived in the early 17th century, establishing coastal and river settlements. Colonization introduced European trade, religion, and political structures, while diseases and conflict reduced Indigenous populations. Towns and local governance structures reflected Puritan and English legal traditions.

Colonial and Revolutionary Era

New Hampshire played an active role in the American Revolution, including the first independent government in 1776 and participation in Continental Army campaigns. Shipbuilding, trade, and small-scale farming were central to the economy, supporting both the war effort and colonial development.

Industrialization and Labor

The 19th century saw growth in textile mills, papermaking, and small-scale manufacturing. Labor movements emerged to address wage disputes, working hours, and conditions in mills and factories. Modern New Hampshire

Today, New Hampshire emphasizes technology, tourism, and service-based industries alongside traditional manufacturing. Political engagement is robust, with a strong emphasis on local governance, civic participation, and fiscal conservatism. Environmental preservation and education remain significant policy priorities.

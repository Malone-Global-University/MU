1. Shared Colonial Experience

Though founded separately with different economies and faiths, the colonies developed a shared outlook by the 1700s. They governed themselves through local assemblies and grew frustrated by British interference and taxation without representation. Distance from Britain fostered independence and self-reliance.

3. Enlightenment Thought

Ideas from European philosophers such as John Locke emphasized natural rights and government by consent. Colonial leaders like Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, and Thomas Jefferson applied these principles to justify independence and self-rule.

4. Economic Independence

Colonies built their own trade networks and industries, yet Britain restricted commerce under its mercantilist system. Acts like the Navigation Acts limited trade freedom, leading colonists to see that prosperity was possible without British control.

The war united colonists against a common foe but revealed British arrogance. When Britain taxed the colonies afterward to pay war debts, resentment deepened through laws like the Stamp Act and Townshend Acts. The colonies began to act together in protest.

By the 1770s, colonists began to view themselves as Americans rather than British subjects. They prized freedom, local democracy, and independence. The Revolution became not just a war for liberty but the formation of a new national consciousness.

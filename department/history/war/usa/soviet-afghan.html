Historical Context

In December 1979, the Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan to support the communist People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan (PDPA) government against insurgent groups. The war became a prolonged Cold War proxy conflict, with the United States, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and other nations supporting Afghan Mujahideen fighters.

Causes of the War

  • Communist Coup: The 1978 Saur Revolution established the PDPA government, triggering uprisings.
  • Political Instability: PDPA faced internal divisions and resistance from traditionalist Afghan groups.
  • Cold War Dynamics: The USSR sought to maintain influence in Afghanistan and prevent U.S.-backed interference.
  • Geopolitical Strategy: Afghanistanâ€™s location was strategically important in the USSRâ€™s southern border defense.

Key Figures

  • Leonid Brezhnev: Soviet leader who authorized the invasion and oversaw military operations.
  • Babrak Karmal: Afghan communist leader installed by the USSR after initial resistance to PDPA rule.
  • Ahmad Shah Massoud: Leader of Mujahideen forces in northern Afghanistan, key in resisting Soviet advances.
  • Zia-ul-Haq: Pakistani President, facilitated U.S. and regional support for Mujahideen fighters.

Military Strategy & Major Campaigns

The war featured guerrilla warfare, conventional Soviet operations, and foreign support for insurgents:

  • Soviet Tactics: Airstrikes, armored offensives, and garrisoning major cities.
  • Mujahideen Resistance: Guerrilla tactics, ambushes, and use of Afghanistanâ€™s mountainous terrain.
  • Foreign Support: U.S. supplied Stinger missiles, funds, and training; Pakistan and Saudi Arabia provided bases and weapons.
  • Protracted Conflict: Ten years of attrition exhausted Soviet forces and destabilized Afghanistan.

Impact on Civilians

Millions of Afghan civilians were killed, wounded, or displaced. Cities and villages were destroyed, leading to a massive refugee crisis in Pakistan and Iran. The war also caused famine, disease, and long-term social disruption.

Outcome and Consequences

The Soviet Union withdrew in 1989, leaving Afghanistan in civil war. The conflict drained Soviet resources and contributed to the USSRâ€™s eventual collapse. Afghanistan remained unstable, with lasting effects on regional security and the rise of extremist groups.

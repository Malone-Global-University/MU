Iraq / ISIS Conflicts (2011–2017)

Rise of the Islamic State and Regional Warfare

Historical Context

Following the U.S. withdrawal from Iraq in 2011 and the destabilization of Syria during its civil war, ISIS (Islamic State of Iraq and Syria) emerged as a powerful jihadist organization. The group captured large territories in Iraq and Syria, declared a caliphate in 2014, and committed widespread atrocities against civilians and minorities.

Causes of the Conflict

  • Power Vacuum: Collapse of central authority in Iraq and Syria created opportunities for extremist groups.
  • Sunni-Shia Tensions: Sectarian divides fueled support for ISIS among marginalized Sunni populations.
  • Syrian Civil War: Allowed ISIS to expand its territory and resources.
  • Global Jihadism: ISIS sought to create an international caliphate and inspire attacks worldwide.

Key Figures

  • Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi: Leader of ISIS who declared the caliphate in 2014.
  • U.S. Military Leaders: Directed coalition operations against ISIS.
  • Regional Powers: Iran, Turkey, and Gulf states played roles in supporting local forces and countering ISIS.
  • Local Forces: Kurdish Peshmerga, Iraqi Army, and Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) fought against ISIS.

Key Battles & Campaigns

  • Fall of Mosul (2014): ISIS captured Iraq’s second-largest city, gaining significant territory and resources.
  • Battle of Kobani (2014–2015): Kurdish forces, supported by U.S. airstrikes, defended the Syrian border town.
  • Battle of Ramadi (2015–2016): Iraqi forces recaptured key ISIS stronghold with coalition support.
  • Battle of Mosul (2016–2017): Iraqi and coalition forces liberated the city, marking the decline of ISIS territorial control.

Military Strategy & Tactics

The conflict combined conventional, asymmetric, and hybrid warfare:

  • ISIS: Used guerrilla tactics, terror attacks, propaganda, and improvised explosive devices (IEDs).
  • Coalition Forces: Employed airstrikes, special operations, and advisory support to local ground forces.
  • Local Militias: Iraqi Army, Kurdish forces, and Syrian Democratic Forces carried out sieges and urban warfare.
  • Counterterrorism Measures: Intelligence, drone strikes, and financial sanctions targeted ISIS leadership and resources.

Impact on Civilians

Millions of civilians were displaced, and thousands were killed in combat and terror attacks. ISIS targeted religious minorities, committed mass executions, and destroyed cultural heritage. Humanitarian organizations faced challenges in providing relief amid ongoing conflict.

Outcome and Consequences

By 2017, ISIS lost nearly all territorial holdings in Iraq and Syria. The caliphate was dismantled, but the organization continues to carry out insurgent and terrorist attacks. The conflict reshaped regional politics, highlighted the threat of transnational jihadism, and tested coalition warfare.

Legacy and Lessons

The Iraq / ISIS conflicts demonstrate the dangers of power vacuums, sectarian divisions, and extremist ideologies. Lessons include the importance of local alliances, intelligence coordination, air-ground integration, and humanitarian considerations in counterinsurgency operations.

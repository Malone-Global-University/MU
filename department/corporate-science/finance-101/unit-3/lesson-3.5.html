Lesson 3.5: ETFs

Index investing, liquidity, expense ratios, and how ETFs differ from mutual funds.

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 3 Lesson 3.5

Lesson Overview

An ETF (exchange-traded fund) is a pooled investment that trades on a stock exchange. Like a mutual fund, an ETF can hold many stocks or bonds in one package. The big difference is how you buy and sell it: ETFs trade throughout the day like stocks.

ETFs are popular because they make diversification easy, often have low costs, and can be convenient for long-term investing. But they come with terms that matter, such as bid-ask spreads, premiums and discounts, and trading volume. This lesson will help you understand ETFs clearly and avoid common mistakes.

Learning Objectives

  • Define what an ETF is and how it differs from a mutual fund.
  • Explain how ETFs trade and why liquidity matters.
  • Understand the bid-ask spread and how it affects your costs.
  • Explain premiums and discounts relative to net asset value (NAV).
  • Describe the creation and redemption mechanism at a high level.
  • Identify key ETF costs and risks, including expense ratios and tracking error.

What an ETF Owns

An ETF is a container that holds assets. The ETF’s strategy determines what it owns. Some ETFs track broad indexes, while others focus on industries, countries, bonds, or specific themes.

Common ETF Categories

  • Broad stock index ETFs that track large segments of the market
  • Sector ETFs that focus on one industry (technology, health care, energy)
  • International ETFs that invest outside one country
  • Bond ETFs that hold government or corporate bonds
  • Factor ETFs that tilt toward characteristics like value, quality, or momentum

How ETFs Trade

ETFs trade on exchanges during market hours. That means their prices can change minute by minute based on supply and demand, just like a stock.

Market Price vs. NAV

Mutual funds typically transact at end-of-day NAV. ETFs have a market price that can be slightly above or below the value of the underlying holdings, which we call NAV.

  • NAV is the estimated value of the underlying holdings per ETF share.
  • Market price is the price you pay or receive when trading.

Liquidity: What It Means for ETF Investors

Liquidity is how easily you can buy or sell without pushing the price around. Highly liquid ETFs generally trade with tighter spreads and smoother execution.

Two Layers of Liquidity

  • ETF trading liquidity: how actively the ETF shares trade
  • Underlying asset liquidity: how liquid the ETF’s holdings are (especially important for bond ETFs)

An ETF can look liquid on the surface, but if its underlying holdings are hard to trade, the ETF can become less efficient during market stress.

The Bid-Ask Spread: A Hidden Trading Cost

When you buy an ETF, you pay the ask price. When you sell, you receive the bid price. The difference is the bid-ask spread.

The spread is a real cost, especially for frequent trading or less liquid ETFs. Tight spreads are usually better for investors.

Simple Rule

  • Long-term investors care about expense ratios and tracking, but should still respect spreads.
  • Short-term traders care a lot about spreads because costs repeat with every trade.

Premiums and Discounts

Because ETF shares trade on an exchange, an ETF can trade at a premium (above NAV) or a discount (below NAV).

  • Premium: market price higher than NAV
  • Discount: market price lower than NAV

For many large index ETFs, premiums and discounts are usually small. For niche or less liquid ETFs, the gap can be larger, especially during volatile markets.

How ETF Pricing Stays Close to NAV

ETFs use a mechanism called creation and redemption. Large institutions can exchange baskets of the underlying securities for ETF shares (creation) or exchange ETF shares for the underlying securities (redemption).

This process helps keep the ETF price close to the value of what it owns. You do not need to memorize the mechanics, but the intuition matters: when the ETF price drifts away from NAV, professional traders have incentives to push it back toward fair value.

ETFs and Index Investing

Many ETFs are designed to track an index. This is a form of rules-based investing. The fund follows a published method, such as owning the largest companies in a market or holding bonds within specific categories.

Tracking and Tracking Error

Tracking error is the gap between the ETF’s performance and the index it aims to follow. Some differences are normal due to fees and trading costs. Larger differences can signal inefficiency or risk.

ETF Costs

Expense Ratio

Like mutual funds, ETFs charge an expense ratio that reduces returns over time. Many index ETFs have very low expense ratios, but they still matter for long time horizons.

Trading Costs

  • Bid-ask spread is paid when you trade.
  • Brokerage commissions may apply depending on your account.
  • Premium or discount can make your execution slightly better or worse than NAV.

ETFs vs. Mutual Funds

ETFs and mutual funds both provide pooled investing, but they behave differently in practice.

Key Differences

  • Trading: ETFs trade during the day; mutual funds typically price once per day.
  • Execution: ETF price is set by the market; mutual fund transactions occur at NAV.
  • Costs: ETFs often have low expense ratios, but you also face trading costs such as spreads.
  • Use cases: ETFs are flexible and tradable; mutual funds can be great for automatic investing and certain retirement plans.

Risks and Watchouts

  • Concentration: some ETFs are narrow and hold few securities
  • Theme risk: trendy themes can be volatile and may fade
  • Liquidity risk: niche ETFs can have wide spreads and harder execution
  • Underlying risk: bond ETFs can behave differently when bond liquidity is tight
  • Tracking error: the ETF may not match its benchmark closely
  • Behavioral risk: trading too frequently because ETFs feel easy to trade

Practical Framework: How to Choose an ETF

  1. Start with the goal: broad growth, income, inflation protection, or diversification.
  2. Check the holdings: what does it actually own and how concentrated is it?
  3. Check the expense ratio: lower is usually better for similar strategies.
  4. Check trading quality: volume, bid-ask spread, and premium/discount behavior.
  5. Check fit: does it match your time horizon and risk tolerance?

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying a theme ETF without understanding what drives its returns
  • Overtrading because ETFs are easy to click in and out of
  • Ignoring spreads on low-volume ETFs
  • Assuming two ETFs are identical because their names sound similar
  • Forgetting portfolio overlap and accidentally doubling down on the same stocks

Mini Case: A Low Fee ETF Can Still Be Costly to Trade

Imagine an ETF with a very low expense ratio, but it trades with a wide bid-ask spread because volume is low. If you trade it frequently, you may pay more in spreads than you save in annual fees.

The lesson: costs come in more than one form. Long-term investors focus on fees and tracking, while traders must also watch spreads.

Key Terms

  • ETF - exchange-traded fund that holds a portfolio and trades on an exchange
  • NAV - net asset value, estimated value of holdings per share
  • Bid-ask spread - difference between buying price and selling price
  • Premium - ETF trading above NAV
  • Discount - ETF trading below NAV
  • Creation and redemption - mechanism that helps align ETF price and NAV
  • Expense ratio - annual fee charged by the fund
  • Tracking error - difference between ETF performance and its benchmark
  • Liquidity - ease of trading without large price impact

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. How do ETFs differ from mutual funds in how they trade?
  2. What is the bid-ask spread and why does it matter?
  3. What does it mean for an ETF to trade at a premium or discount?
  4. Name two costs of owning or trading an ETF.
  5. What is tracking error and why should investors care?

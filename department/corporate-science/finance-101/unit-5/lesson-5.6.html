Lesson 5.6: Insurance & Risk Management

Protecting what you build with smart coverage and clear choices.

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 5 Lesson 5.6

Lesson Overview

Insurance is a financial safety tool. It is not designed to make you money. It is designed to prevent a single bad event from turning into a long term financial disaster.

Risk management means identifying what could hurt you financially and choosing what to avoid, what to reduce, what to plan for, and what to insure. In this lesson you will learn how insurance works, what key terms mean, and how to choose coverage levels that fit your life.

Learning Objectives

  • Define risk management and explain the purpose of insurance.
  • Understand deductibles, premiums, copays, coinsurance, and out of pocket maximums.
  • Identify common types of personal insurance and what they typically cover.
  • Distinguish between liability coverage and property coverage.
  • Choose coverage and deductible levels using simple decision rules.
  • Create an annual insurance review checklist and a basic claims readiness plan.

Key Terms

  • Risk - the chance of loss or harm that has financial consequences
  • Insurance - a contract where you pay a premium to transfer certain risks to an insurer
  • Premium - the amount you pay for coverage (monthly or yearly)
  • Deductible - the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance pays (for many claim types)
  • Copay - a fixed amount you pay for a service (common in health insurance)
  • Coinsurance - a percentage split of costs after the deductible (common in health insurance)
  • Out of pocket maximum - the most you pay in a year for covered health care (in network)
  • Coverage limit - the maximum the insurer will pay for a claim or time period
  • Exclusion - something the policy does not cover
  • Liability - legal responsibility for injury or damage to others

Risk Management: The Four Choices

When you face a risk, you generally have four options:

  • Avoid - do not do the risky activity (example: do not drive without insurance)
  • Reduce - lower the chance or impact (example: drive safely, maintain your car)
  • Accept - self insure smaller risks with savings (example: minor repairs)
  • Transfer - insure the risks that could crush your finances (example: major medical bills)

Insurance is most valuable when the potential loss is large and unpredictable.

How Insurance Pricing Works

Insurance companies set premiums based on risk. Higher risk usually means higher cost. Lower risk, higher deductibles, and stronger safety features can reduce cost.

Premium vs deductible tradeoff

In general, a higher deductible often lowers the premium. A lower deductible often increases the premium. Your choice should be based on what you can afford in a bad month, not only what saves the most money on paper.

Coverage Types: Liability vs Property

Many policies include two broad categories:

  • Liability coverage helps pay for damage or injury you cause to others.
  • Property coverage helps repair or replace your stuff (and sometimes your home or car).

Liability is often the bigger financial risk because lawsuits and medical bills can be very large.

Health Insurance Basics

Health insurance is one of the most important protections for financial stability because medical costs can be extremely large and unpredictable.

How health insurance costs typically work

  • You pay a premium to keep the plan active.
  • You may pay a deductible before the plan starts sharing costs for many services.
  • After that, you may pay copays or coinsurance.
  • The out of pocket maximum is your annual cap for covered in network services.

Network matters

Many plans have better pricing for in network providers. Out of network care can cost significantly more. Before planned care, confirm network status when possible.

Choosing between plan options

  • If you expect frequent care, a higher premium plan with lower out of pocket costs may fit better.
  • If you expect little care, a lower premium plan may work, but make sure you can handle the deductible.
  • Compare plans using the total expected annual cost, not just the premium.

Auto Insurance Basics

Auto insurance typically protects you from liability and helps cover vehicle damage. Requirements vary by location, but the financial logic is consistent: the cost of a serious accident can be far larger than most budgets can handle.

Common components

  • Liability for injuries and property damage to others
  • Collision for damage to your car in an accident
  • Comprehensive for non collision losses (theft, vandalism, weather)
  • Uninsured or underinsured motorist protection if the other driver lacks adequate coverage

Choosing collision and comprehensive

If your car is older and not worth much, you may decide that paying for collision coverage is not worth the premium. The key question is whether you could replace the car without financial disaster.

Renters and Homeowners Insurance

Renters insurance is often inexpensive and can be high value. It generally covers your personal property, provides liability coverage, and may cover temporary living expenses after certain covered losses.

Renters insurance

  • Personal property coverage for your belongings
  • Liability if someone is injured in your home
  • Loss of use for temporary housing after a covered loss

Homeowners insurance

  • Coverage for the structure plus your belongings
  • Liability protection
  • Additional coverage options for specific risks depending on location

Take inventory of your belongings and keep photos or receipts. In a claim, documentation matters.

Life Insurance and Disability Insurance

These policies protect income and dependents, not just property.

Life insurance

  • Purpose replace income or cover obligations if you die
  • Most relevant if someone depends on your income or you have shared debts
  • Common approach term life insurance for a set period

Disability insurance

  • Purpose replace part of your income if you cannot work due to illness or injury
  • Often overlooked but can be critical, especially for single income households
  • Key details waiting period and benefit duration

Deductibles and Emergency Funds

Choosing a high deductible only makes sense if you have cash available to pay it. One practical strategy:

  • Keep your emergency fund at least large enough to cover your biggest likely deductible.
  • Use sinking funds for predictable insurance costs such as premiums and routine care.

How to Shop for Insurance

Shopping well means comparing the total package, not only the monthly price.

  1. Compare premiums, deductibles, and coverage limits side by side.
  2. Check what is excluded and what requires special endorsements.
  3. Consider bundling discounts (auto plus renters or home).
  4. Ask about safety and low mileage discounts where relevant.
  5. Review the policy annually and after major life changes.

Claims Readiness: What to Do Before You Need It

Being prepared makes claims less stressful and faster.

  • Keep policy numbers and contact info saved in your phone.
  • Take a home inventory photo walk once a year.
  • Store key documents in a secure place (digital or physical).
  • Know your deductibles and coverage limits.
  • Understand your insurer’s reporting timeline for claims.

Common Insurance Mistakes

  • Buying the cheapest policy without checking coverage limits and exclusions
  • Underinsuring liability and exposing yourself to large financial risk
  • Choosing a deductible you cannot afford when something happens
  • Forgetting to update coverage after moving, buying a car, or life changes
  • Not documenting belongings which makes claims harder

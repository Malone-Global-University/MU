Lesson 1.6: Financial Ratios I — Profitability & Efficiency

How much profit is generated—and how efficiently the business uses its resources.

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 1 Lesson 1.6

Lesson Overview

Once you understand the three financial statements and how they connect, the next step is learning how to measure performance using ratios. Ratios turn raw numbers into comparisons you can track over time and across similar businesses.

This lesson focuses on two categories: profitability (how much profit is generated) and efficiency (how effectively the business uses assets and working capital to produce revenue and cash).

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate and interpret core profitability ratios (margins and returns).
  • Calculate and interpret efficiency ratios (turnovers and days).
  • Explain how profitability and efficiency interact (margin vs. turnover).
  • Use averages for balance sheet accounts when calculating return and turnover ratios.
  • Recognize common pitfalls in ratio interpretation.

Before You Start: Two Rules Analysts Follow

Rule 1: Compare Like With Like

Ratios are most useful when comparing the same company over time or comparing similar companies within the same industry and business model.

Rule 2: Match Periods Correctly

The income statement covers a period, but the balance sheet is a snapshot. When a ratio uses a balance sheet number, use an average when possible:

  • Average Assets ≈ (Beginning Assets + Ending Assets) ÷ 2
  • Average Inventory ≈ (Beginning Inventory + Ending Inventory) ÷ 2

Profitability Ratios: Margins

Margins answer: For each $1 of revenue, how much profit remains at different stages?

Gross Margin

  • Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue

Gross margin is driven by pricing power, product mix, and production/input costs.

Operating Margin

  • Operating Margin = Operating Income (EBIT) ÷ Revenue

Operating margin reflects how well the company manages overhead and operating expenses.

Net Profit Margin

  • Net Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue

Net margin includes everything: operating performance plus financing and taxes.

Profitability Ratios: Returns

Return ratios answer: How much profit does the company generate relative to the resources invested?

Return on Assets (ROA)

  • ROA = Net Income ÷ Average Total Assets

ROA is a broad measure of how effectively the company uses all assets to generate profit.

Return on Equity (ROE)

  • ROE = Net Income ÷ Average Shareholders’ Equity

ROE measures the return earned for owners. It can rise because the business improves—or because leverage increases. (Leverage itself is covered more deeply in Lesson 1.7.)

Efficiency Ratios: Turnover

Turnover ratios answer: How much activity a business produces from a given asset base.

Total Asset Turnover

  • Total Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Total Assets

This ratio shows how effectively the company uses its assets to generate revenue.

Inventory Turnover

  • Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory

Higher turnover can indicate strong demand or efficient inventory management—but extremely high turnover may also signal stockouts and missed sales.

Receivables Turnover

  • Receivables Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Accounts Receivable

This ratio indicates how quickly customers pay. A declining turnover may signal weaker collections or looser credit terms.

Efficiency Ratios: Days and the Cash Conversion Cycle

“Days” metrics convert turnover into a time interpretation. They help you understand how long cash is tied up.

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)

  • DSO = (Average A/R ÷ Revenue) × 365

Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)

  • DIO = (Average Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

A simplified version (without payables) is:

  • CCC (Simplified) = DSO + DIO

Interpretation: the higher the cycle, the longer cash is tied up in receivables and inventory. (A full CCC that includes payables is introduced in Lesson 1.7 with liquidity.)

How Profitability and Efficiency Work Together

Many business models trade off margin and turnover:

  • High margin, lower turnover: luxury goods, specialized software
  • Lower margin, high turnover: grocery, discount retail

Two companies can be successful with very different ratio profiles. The key is to compare to the right peers.

Common Pitfalls (Avoid These)

  • Using end-of-year balances for turnover and return ratios instead of averages.
  • Comparing across industries where business models differ.
  • Ignoring seasonality (inventory and receivables often fluctuate during the year).
  • One-time events affecting net income and making ROA/ROE look artificially high or low.

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. If COGS rises faster than revenue, what happens to gross margin?
  2. What does high asset turnover imply about asset use?
  3. If accounts receivable grows faster than revenue, what happens to DSO?
  4. Why is ROA typically calculated using average total assets?

Mini-Case: Margin vs. Turnover

Company A has a 45% gross margin but low asset turnover. Company B has a 20% gross margin but very high asset turnover.

  • Which company likely sells premium products?
  • Which company likely competes on volume and price?
  • What additional information would you want before deciding which business is “better”?

Lesson 1.7: Financial Ratios II — Liquidity & Leverage

Can the business pay its bills—and how risky is its capital structure?

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 1 Lesson 1.7

Lesson Overview

Profitability does not guarantee survival. Even profitable companies can fail if they cannot meet short-term obligations or if debt becomes unmanageable.

This lesson focuses on liquidity (short-term financial safety) and leverage (long-term financial risk), using ratios that highlight cash availability, debt burden, and solvency.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate and interpret key liquidity ratios.
  • Calculate and interpret leverage and coverage ratios.
  • Understand the tradeoff between leverage and financial risk.
  • Identify warning signs of liquidity stress or excessive leverage.
  • Explain why cash flow matters more than accounting profit for solvency.

Liquidity: Short-Term Financial Safety

Liquidity ratios answer the question: Can the business meet its obligations over the next year?

Working Capital

  • Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Positive working capital provides a buffer against short-term shocks. Negative working capital may indicate reliance on continuous cash inflows.

Current Ratio

  • Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

A ratio above 1.0 suggests the firm can cover short-term obligations, but excessively high values may indicate idle or inefficient assets.

Quick Ratio (Acid-Test)

  • Quick Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R) ÷ Current Liabilities

The quick ratio removes inventory, focusing on assets that can be converted to cash quickly.

Cash-Based Liquidity Measures

Accounting liquidity can look strong even when cash is tight. Cash-based measures provide a more conservative view.

Operating Cash Flow Coverage

  • Operating Cash Flow Coverage = Operating Cash Flow ÷ Current Liabilities

This ratio shows whether the company’s normal operations generate enough cash to cover short-term obligations.

Leverage: Long-Term Financial Risk

Leverage ratios measure how much a company relies on borrowed money and how exposed it is during downturns.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

  • Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Shareholders’ Equity

Higher leverage can amplify returns—but also increases the risk of financial distress.

Debt-to-Assets Ratio

  • Debt-to-Assets = Total Debt ÷ Total Assets

This ratio shows what proportion of the company’s assets are financed by debt.

Coverage Ratios: Can the Firm Service Its Debt?

Coverage ratios focus on the firm’s ability to make required payments.

Interest Coverage Ratio

  • Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense

A low interest coverage ratio signals vulnerability to earnings declines or rising interest rates.

Cash Interest Coverage

  • Cash Interest Coverage = Operating Cash Flow ÷ Interest Paid

Cash-based coverage is often more reliable than earnings-based measures.

Liquidity vs. Leverage: The Tradeoff

  • High leverage increases ROE during good times.
  • High leverage magnifies losses during downturns.
  • Strong liquidity provides flexibility and resilience.

Healthy firms balance growth, leverage, and liquidity rather than maximizing any single ratio.

Warning Signs to Watch For

  • Declining current or quick ratios over time.
  • Rising debt with flat or falling cash flow.
  • Interest coverage approaching 1.0.
  • Positive earnings but negative operating cash flow.

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. Why might a high current ratio still signal problems?
  2. Which is more dangerous: high leverage or weak liquidity?
  3. Why is operating cash flow important for solvency?
  4. How does leverage affect risk during economic downturns?

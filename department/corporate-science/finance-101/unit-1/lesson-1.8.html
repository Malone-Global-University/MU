Lesson 1.8: Financial Ratios & Statement Analysis

Turning financial statements into insight: profitability, liquidity, efficiency, and risk.

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 1 Lesson 1.8

Lesson Overview

Financial statements contain a lot of information—but raw numbers are hard to compare across companies or across time. Financial ratios solve this problem by converting statements into relationships: percentages and multiples that reveal profitability, liquidity, efficiency, and risk.

In this lesson, you’ll learn the core ratio categories and how to combine the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement into a single coherent analysis. The goal isn’t to memorize dozens of formulas—it’s to build a repeatable way of thinking.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why ratios are used in financial analysis.
  • Calculate and interpret key profitability, liquidity, efficiency, and leverage ratios.
  • Connect ratios to the three financial statements.
  • Use trend analysis and peer comparisons appropriately.
  • Recognize common ratio pitfalls and “false signals.”

The Four Big Questions Ratios Answer

  • Profitability: Does the business generate attractive returns?
  • Liquidity: Can it pay near-term obligations?
  • Efficiency: How well does it use assets and working capital?
  • Solvency (Risk/Leverage): Can it survive long-term obligations and shocks?

How to Use Ratios the Right Way

Ratios are most useful when used in context:

  • Trend: compare the company to itself over time (3–5 years if available).
  • Peers: compare to similar companies in the same industry and business model.
  • Reality checks: confirm with cash flows and operational facts (customers, pricing, costs).

A single ratio almost never tells the full story. A pattern across multiple ratios usually does.

Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios show whether revenue turns into profits efficiently and whether the business earns attractive returns.

Margins (Income Statement Focus)

  • Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue
  • Operating Margin = Operating Income (EBIT) ÷ Revenue
  • Net Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue

Return Ratios (Income Statement + Balance Sheet)

  • Return on Assets (ROA) = Net Income ÷ Average Total Assets
  • Return on Equity (ROE) = Net Income ÷ Average Equity

Note: using average balance sheet values (beginning and ending) is often more accurate because the income statement covers a period while the balance sheet is a point in time.

Liquidity Ratios (Near-Term Safety)

Liquidity ratios test whether a company can cover short-term obligations with short-term resources.

  • Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
  • Quick Ratio (Acid Test) = (Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R) ÷ Current Liabilities
  • Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Liquidity is about survival. Many otherwise “good” businesses fail because they run out of cash.

Efficiency Ratios (Using Assets Well)

Efficiency ratios measure how effectively a business uses its assets and manages working capital.

Asset Turnover

  • Total Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Total Assets

Working Capital Efficiency (Days Metrics)

  • Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = (Average A/R ÷ Revenue) × 365
  • Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = (Average Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365
  • Days Payables Outstanding (DPO) = (Average A/P ÷ COGS) × 365
  • Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) = DSO + DIO − DPO

The cash conversion cycle estimates how long cash is tied up in operations. Shorter cycles often improve liquidity and reduce financing needs.

Solvency and Leverage Ratios (Long-Term Risk)

Leverage can increase returns, but it also increases the risk of financial distress when revenue falls or rates rise.

  • Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity
  • Debt Ratio = Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets
  • Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense

Interest coverage is especially important for lenders and for investors in cyclical industries. If coverage is thin, a modest downturn can create serious stress.

Cash Flow Ratios (Quality and Sustainability)

Cash flow ratios help validate whether reported earnings are backed by real cash generation.

  • Operating Cash Flow Margin = Cash Flow From Operations ÷ Revenue
  • Free Cash Flow (FCF) = CFO − CapEx
  • FCF Margin = FCF ÷ Revenue
  • CFO to Net Income = CFO ÷ Net Income

If net income is consistently higher than operating cash flow, investigate working capital changes and non-cash accounting effects.

Putting It Together: A Simple Analysis Workflow

  1. Start with the income statement: growth, gross margin, operating margin, net margin.
  2. Check the balance sheet: liquidity (current/quick), leverage (debt levels), working capital.
  3. Verify with cash flows: CFO trend, CapEx, free cash flow, reliance on financing.
  4. Compare: against prior years and peer averages.
  5. Ask “why”: connect ratio changes to business decisions (pricing, costs, growth strategy).

DuPont Breakdown (A Powerful ROE Lens)

ROE can be decomposed into three drivers:

  • ROE = (Net Income ÷ Revenue) × (Revenue ÷ Assets) × (Assets ÷ Equity)
  • = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier

This helps you see whether ROE is high because of strong profitability, efficient asset usage, or heavy leverage.

Common Pitfalls and “Ratio Traps”

  • Comparing across industries: ratios vary widely by business model (banks vs. retailers).
  • Ignoring accounting choices: depreciation methods and revenue recognition can shift ratios.
  • One-time items: unusual gains/losses can distort margins and ROA/ROE.
  • Seasonality: balance sheet accounts can swing during the year (inventory build, holiday sales).
  • Using end-of-period balances only: averages are often better for return and turnover ratios.

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. If current liabilities rise faster than current assets, what happens to the current ratio?
  2. What does a rising DSO suggest about collections?
  3. How can leverage increase ROE even if profitability is unchanged?
  4. Why is CFO-to-net-income useful for evaluating earnings quality?

Mini-Case: Same Profit, Different Risk

Two companies report the same net profit margin. Company A has low debt and strong interest coverage. Company B has high debt and thin coverage.

  • Which company is more vulnerable to a recession or rate increase?
  • Which ratios highlight that risk?
  • What additional information would you want before investing?

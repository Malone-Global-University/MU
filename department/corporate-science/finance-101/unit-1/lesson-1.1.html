Lesson 1.1: Introduction to Financial Statements

Understanding how financial information tells the story of a business.

Home Department Corporate Science Finance 101 Unit 1 Lesson 1.1

Lesson Overview

Financial statements are the primary language of finance. They summarize what a business owns, what it owes, how it performs, and how it uses cash. Investors, managers, lenders, and regulators all rely on these statements to make informed decisions.

In this lesson, you’ll be introduced to the three core financial statements and learn why each one matters. Mastering these basics is essential for understanding everything else in finance—from valuation to investing to corporate decision-making.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of financial statements.
  • Identify the three primary financial statements.
  • Understand how the statements are connected.
  • Recognize who uses financial statements and why.
  • Distinguish between profitability, liquidity, and solvency.

Why Financial Statements Matter

Every business decision has financial consequences. Financial statements organize those consequences into standardized reports that allow comparisons across time and across firms.

Without financial statements, it would be nearly impossible to answer basic questions such as:

  • Is the business profitable?
  • Can it pay its bills?
  • Is it growing or shrinking?
  • How risky is it?

The Income Statement

The income statement reports a company’s financial performance over a period of time. It shows revenues earned and expenses incurred, resulting in net income or net loss.

  • Revenues — money earned from operations
  • Expenses — costs required to generate revenue
  • Net income — profit after all expenses

Think of the income statement as a movie: it shows performance over a span of time.

The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet provides a snapshot of a company’s financial position at a single point in time.

  • Assets — what the company owns
  • Liabilities — what the company owes
  • Equity — the owners’ claim on the business

These elements are linked by the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks how cash moves in and out of the business. It explains why profit does not always equal cash.

  • Operating activities — cash from core operations
  • Investing activities — cash used for or generated by assets
  • Financing activities — cash from owners and lenders

How the Statements Fit Together

Financial statements are not independent. Net income from the income statement affects equity on the balance sheet, while changes in balance sheet accounts help explain cash flows.

Understanding these connections is one of the most important skills in finance.

Who Uses Financial Statements?

  • Managers — to plan and control operations
  • Investors — to assess risk and return
  • Lenders — to evaluate creditworthiness
  • Regulators — to ensure transparency and compliance

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. Why is net income not the same as cash?
  2. Which statement shows what a firm owns and owes?
  3. Who might care most about a company’s liquidity?

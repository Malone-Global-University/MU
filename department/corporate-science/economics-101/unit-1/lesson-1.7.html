Lesson 1.7: Incentives & Human Behavior

How rewards and penalties shape the choices we make every day.

Home Department Corporate Science Economics Unit 1 Lesson 1.7

Lesson Overview

Incentives are benefits or costs that motivate people to act. In economics, incentives explain why individuals, businesses, and governments make the choices they do.

When incentives change, behavior changes.

This lesson introduces the power of incentives and why understanding them is essential to predicting economic outcomes.

Learning Objectives

  • Define incentives in economic terms.
  • Distinguish between positive and negative incentives.
  • Explain how incentives influence behavior.
  • Apply incentive reasoning to real-world decisions.

What Are Incentives?

An incentive is something that encourages or discourages a particular action.

Types of Incentives

  • Positive incentives — Rewards that encourage behavior (bonuses, discounts, praise).
  • Negative incentives — Penalties that discourage behavior (fines, taxes, restrictions).

Economists assume that people generally respond to incentives in predictable ways.

Incentives in Everyday Life

Personal Example

Higher wages may motivate someone to work more hours.

Business Example

A company may offer performance bonuses to increase employee productivity.

Government Example

Tax credits for renewable energy encourage investment in clean technology.

Unintended Consequences

Sometimes incentives lead to unexpected results. If a policy rewards a certain outcome, people may change their behavior in surprising ways.

Good economic analysis considers both intended and unintended effects.

Practice: Identify the Incentive

  1. A city increases parking fines downtown. What behavior is it trying to change?
  2. A teacher offers extra credit for early assignment submission. What is the incentive?
  3. A company lowers prices during a sale. How might consumers respond?

Reflection Prompt

Think about a recent decision you made.

  • What incentives influenced your choice?
  • Were they financial, social, or personal?
  • Did they work as intended?

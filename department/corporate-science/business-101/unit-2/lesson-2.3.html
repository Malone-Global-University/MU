Lesson 2.3: Organizing Work & Delegation

Roles, accountability, delegation, and how to design work so it actually gets done.

Home Department Corporate Science Business 101 Unit 2 Lesson 2.3

Lesson Overview

Many teams don’t fail because people are lazy—they fail because the work is poorly organized. When roles are unclear, tasks get duplicated, important work falls through gaps, and everyone waits for someone else to decide.

Organizing is the manager’s job of designing the “operating system” for work: who owns what, how work flows, where decisions are made, and what “done” means. Delegation is the practical skill that turns that design into execution—by assigning ownership with the right level of authority and support.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain what it means to organize work and why it increases performance.
  • Define roles, responsibilities, and accountability in a practical way.
  • Use simple tools (RACI and “definition of done”) to reduce confusion.
  • Delegate effectively by matching tasks to people, authority, and support.
  • Identify common delegation failures and how to avoid micromanagement.

Organizing Work: The Goal

Organizing work means designing a reliable path from “goal” to “result.” In a well-organized team:

  • Everyone knows the priority.
  • Every task has an owner.
  • Work has clear inputs and outputs.
  • Handoffs are defined (so work doesn’t get stuck).
  • Decisions happen at the right level (not always at the top).

Roles vs. Responsibilities vs. Accountability

These words are often used interchangeably, but separating them reduces confusion:

  • Role: a position or function (e.g., “Team Lead,” “Analyst,” “Customer Support”).
  • Responsibilities: what the role is expected to do (repeatable duties).
  • Accountability: who ultimately owns the outcome (one name).

A simple rule: many people can help, but one person must own.

Define the Work Before You Assign It

Delegation fails most often because the task is unclear. Before assigning work, define:

  • Outcome: what “success” looks like
  • Scope: what is included and excluded
  • Constraints: time, budget, quality, approvals
  • Dependencies: what inputs are needed and from whom
  • Definition of Done: how you’ll know it’s complete

Tool: The “Definition of Done” (DoD)

A definition of done is a short checklist that prevents misunderstandings. It makes quality visible.

Example: DoD for a One-Page Marketing Email

  • Subject line written (two options)
  • Body copy proofread and links tested
  • Brand tone and style checked
  • Approved by marketing lead
  • Scheduled in the email platform with the correct audience segment

The DoD reduces rework because expectations are set up front—not argued about at the end.

Tool: RACI (Clarifying Ownership)

RACI is a simple way to clarify who does what on a project:

  • R — Responsible: does the work
  • A — Accountable: owns the result (one person)
  • C — Consulted: provides input
  • I — Informed: kept updated

Example: Launching a New Website Page

  • R: Web designer
  • A: Marketing manager
  • C: Legal, Product
  • I: Customer support, Sales

RACI prevents a common problem: too many “decision-makers” and not enough owners.

Designing Workflows That Don’t Break

Organizing is also about flow. Most work moves through stages (intake → execution → review → delivery). Problems happen at the boundaries—handoffs, approvals, and unclear queues.

Improve flow by:

  • Reducing handoffs (every handoff is a delay risk)
  • Limiting work in progress (too many tasks slows everything)
  • Defining queues (what’s next, and who pulls it)
  • Setting review rules (who approves, how fast, using what criteria)

Delegation: What It Is (and What It Isn’t)

Delegation means assigning responsibility and authority to someone else while staying accountable for the outcome. It is not “dumping tasks” or disappearing.

Effective delegation increases capacity, develops people, and reduces bottlenecks. Poor delegation creates frustration, rework, and delays.

The Delegation Checklist

When delegating, cover these five things:

  • Outcome: what success looks like (be specific)
  • Authority: what decisions they can make without asking
  • Resources: tools, budget, access, templates, examples
  • Checkpoints: when you will review progress
  • Support: how you’ll remove obstacles and coach

Levels of Delegation

Not every task should be delegated the same way. A simple “levels” approach:

  • Level 1 — Do exactly this: clear steps, low risk, training mode.
  • Level 2 — Research and recommend: bring options with pros/cons.
  • Level 3 — Decide and inform: make the call and keep me updated.
  • Level 4 — Own the outcome: full ownership within constraints.

Great managers move people up levels over time as skills and trust grow.

Common Delegation Mistakes

  • Vague assignments: “Handle this” without defining the outcome.
  • No authority: someone owns work but can’t make decisions.
  • No resources: missing access, tools, or context.
  • Too many check-ins: micromanagement that kills ownership.
  • Too few check-ins: problems discovered too late.

The goal is a healthy middle: clear expectations + appropriate autonomy + scheduled checkpoints.

Mini Case: “Everyone Thought Someone Else Had It”

A team plans an event. Marketing thought Operations ordered supplies. Operations thought Finance approved the budget. Finance thought the event lead handled it. On the day of the event, key supplies are missing.

The fix is not “work harder.” The fix is organizing:

  • Assign a single Accountable owner for the event.
  • Create a simple RACI and a checklist.
  • Define a deadline for budget approval and purchasing.
  • Use checkpoints (e.g., “72 hours before event: confirm supplies on-site”).

Practice: Check Your Understanding

  1. What is the difference between being “responsible” and being “accountable”?
  2. Create a one-sentence “definition of done” for: “Publish a blog post.”
  3. In RACI, why should “Accountable” usually be one person?
  4. Which delegation level would you use for a new employee—and why?

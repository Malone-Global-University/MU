Payments & Financial Infrastructure Track

Learn the operational mechanics of payment systems and financial infrastructure. Study transaction flows, merchant systems, network routing, clearing, settlement, and control frameworks through applied professional training.

Track Overview

This free, self-directed track teaches the operational mechanics of payment systems and financial infrastructure. Students learn how money moves through cards, bank transfers, merchant platforms, payment processors, network rules, clearing systems, settlement frameworks, and cross-institution operating controls.

The through-line is simple: payments are not just transactions, but coordinated infrastructure systems that authorize, route, verify, clear, settle, and record the movement of value between parties. Reliable commerce depends on institutions that can process enormous volumes accurately, quickly, and securely.

Students gain practical literacy in payment rails, merchant systems, transaction processing, network controls, settlement mechanics, fraud prevention, and institutional management.

Track Units

  • Layer 1: Foundations

    How payment systems function as financial infrastructure.

  • Unit 1: Financial Foundations for Payments

    Money movement, account balances, transaction value, float, fees, settlement timing, and the economics of payment processing.

  • Unit 2: Structure of the Payments Ecosystem

    Issuers, acquirers, processors, gateways, networks, merchants, banks, fintech platforms, and infrastructure providers.

  • Unit 3: Payment Participants and Use Cases

    Consumers, businesses, billers, merchants, platforms, governments, and institutional payment flows across use cases.

  • Unit 4: Revenue Models and Network Economics

    Interchange, assessment fees, processing fees, merchant discount rates, service pricing, and infrastructure business models.

  • Layer 2: Products / Activities

    What payment instruments and systems institutions actually operate.

  • Unit 5: Card Payment Systems

    Credit cards, debit cards, prepaid cards, issuer-acquirer relationships, authorization logic, and network-based payment activity.

  • Unit 6: Account-to-Account Payment Rails

    ACH-style transfers, bank debits, direct credits, domestic transfer rails, and account-based payment systems.

  • Unit 7: Wire Transfers and High-Value Payments

    Real-time gross settlement concepts, bank wires, urgent transfers, institutional payment flows, and high-value infrastructure.

  • Unit 8: Merchant Acceptance and Digital Payment Channels

    POS acceptance, ecommerce gateways, digital wallets, recurring billing, platform payments, and omnichannel transaction capture.

  • Layer 3: Operational Infrastructure

    What systems route, verify, record, and settle payment activity.

  • Unit 9: Authorization, Routing, and Switching Systems

    Message routing, transaction switching, approval logic, issuer response flows, and network communication systems.

  • Unit 10: Merchant Acquiring and Processor Infrastructure

    Merchant onboarding, processor relationships, gateway connections, terminal support, and acquiring operations.

  • Unit 11: Clearing, Settlement, and Reconciliation Infrastructure

    Batch clearing, settlement files, net positions, funding movements, reconciliation controls, and cash balancing systems.

  • Unit 12: Payment Data, Records, and Reporting Systems

    Transaction records, payment messaging, exception logs, settlement reports, operational dashboards, and data retention.

  • Layer 4: Execution Workflows

    How payment transactions move from initiation to completed settlement.

  • Unit 13: Transaction Initiation and Processing Workflows

    Authorization requests, validation, response handling, transaction posting, and real-time or batch processing flows.

  • Unit 14: Merchant Boarding and Payment Enablement

    Merchant underwriting, onboarding, terminal setup, gateway integration, configuration, and go-live workflows.

  • Unit 15: Settlement Operations and Funding Workflows

    Funding calculations, reserve handling, merchant payouts, exception investigation, and settlement completion procedures.

  • Unit 16: Disputes, Chargebacks, and Exception Resolution

    Retrieval requests, disputes, chargeback handling, representment, operational case management, and loss resolution workflows.

  • Layer 5: Risk & Controls

    How payment institutions reduce fraud, losses, and operational breakdown.

  • Unit 17: Fraud Detection and Transaction Risk Controls

    Fraud screening, transaction monitoring, authentication, rule engines, suspicious activity escalation, and payment loss prevention.

  • Unit 18: Operational Resilience and Exception Control

    System outages, failed transactions, duplicate processing, exception queues, recovery processes, and operational continuity.

  • Unit 19: Network Rules, Compliance, and Supervisory Controls

    Scheme rules, operating requirements, procedural compliance, supervisory oversight, and institutional control responsibilities.

  • Unit 20: Security, Identity, and Data Protection

    Access controls, authentication, tokenization concepts, payment security standards, customer protection, and data safeguarding.

  • Layer 6: Institutional Management / Governance

    How payment organizations manage scale, oversight, and operational accountability.

  • Unit 21: Network Operations and Functional Team Structure

    Operations teams, processor support, merchant service groups, dispute teams, settlement functions, and managerial responsibility.

  • Unit 22: Operational Reporting and Performance Management

    Processing volumes, approval rates, exception rates, fraud metrics, settlement performance, and management dashboards.

  • Unit 23: Vendor, Processor, and Network Relationship Management

    Third-party processors, bank partners, network contracts, service-level monitoring, and outsourced infrastructure oversight.

  • Unit 24: Governance, Policy, and Institutional Control

    Operating policies, escalation structures, internal governance, audit readiness, control testing, and organizational accountability.

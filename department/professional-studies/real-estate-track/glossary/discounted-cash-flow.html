Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Definition

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) is a valuation method that determines the present value of an investment by discounting projected future cash flows back to today using a required rate of return.

DCF analysis is built on the Time Value of Money (TVM) principle: future cash flows are worth less than cash received today.

Core Components

  • Projected Cash Flows: Annual income after operating expenses and capital expenditures.
  • Discount Rate: Required return reflecting risk and opportunity cost.
  • Holding Period: Investment time horizon (often 5–10 years in real estate).
  • Terminal Value: Estimated resale value at exit.

Basic Formula

Present Value = Σ [ Cash Flowt ÷ (1 + r)t ]

Where:

  • t = time period
  • r = discount rate

Application in Real Estate

  • Project annual Net Operating Income (NOI)
  • Subtract capital expenditures and leasing costs
  • Estimate sale price using an exit cap rate
  • Discount all cash flows to present value

Example

A property generates projected annual cash flows of $500,000 for 5 years, and is expected to sell for $8,000,000 at the end of Year 5. Using a 9% discount rate, each annual cash flow and the terminal value are discounted back to present value to determine the property’s intrinsic worth.

Why Investors Use DCF

  • Captures timing differences in cash flows
  • Incorporates growth assumptions
  • Accounts for risk via discount rate
  • Provides more nuanced valuation than a simple cap rate

Common Pitfalls

  • Unrealistic growth assumptions
  • Improper discount rate selection
  • Overreliance on terminal value
  • Ignoring sensitivity analysis

Related Terms

  • Time Value of Money (TVM)
  • Net Present Value (NPV)
  • Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
  • Discount Rate
  • Capitalization Rate
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)

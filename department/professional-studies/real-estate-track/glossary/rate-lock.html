Rate Lock

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Rate Lock

Definition

A rate lock is an agreement between a borrower and lender that guarantees a specific interest rate for a defined period of time. During the lock period, the borrower is protected from market-driven rate increases before the loan closes.

Why Rate Locks Matter

Interest rates can move daily based on bond markets, inflation data, and central bank policy. A rate lock reduces uncertainty between loan approval and closing, allowing borrowers and investors to underwrite with more confidence.

How a Rate Lock Works

  • Lock request: borrower (often through a loan officer) requests a rate lock.
  • Lock confirmation: lender issues a lock agreement stating the rate, points, and expiration date.
  • Lock period: typically 15, 30, 45, 60 days (longer for construction or complex deals).
  • Closing deadline: loan must close before the lock expires to preserve the rate.

Common Lock Periods

Lock length depends on transaction complexity and expected closing timeline:

  • 15–30 days: standard residential closings with strong underwriting.
  • 45–60 days: purchases with extended diligence or commercial underwriting.
  • Long-term locks: construction or rate lock programs that may involve upfront fees.

Lock Extensions

If closing is delayed beyond the expiration date, borrowers may need to:

  • Pay an extension fee (often a per-day or per-week cost).
  • Relock at current market rates, which could be higher or lower.

Extension pricing can materially affect loan economics, especially in volatile rate environments.

Float-Down Options

Some lenders offer a float-down feature, allowing the borrower to reduce the locked rate if market rates fall significantly before closing. These options:

  • May require an upfront fee.
  • Often have specific triggers (e.g., minimum rate drop).
  • Are not universally available.

Commercial vs. Residential Differences

  • Residential loans: rate locks are common and standardized.
  • Commercial loans: locks may occur later in underwriting and can involve deposits or breakage costs.
  • Agency loans (multifamily): often include formal rate lock commitments tied to underwriting milestones.

Example

An investor secures a 30-day rate lock at 6.50% for a refinance. Two weeks later, market rates rise to 6.90%.

Because the rate was locked, the investor closes at 6.50%, preserving projected debt service and maintaining underwriting assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Locking too early: delays in appraisal or underwriting may require costly extensions.
  • Locking too late: rates may rise unexpectedly before protection is in place.
  • Ignoring expiration date: missed deadlines can reset pricing.
  • Not reading lock terms: some locks are conditional on appraisal value or final underwriting.
  • Assuming float-down is automatic: many locks do not allow downward adjustments.

Why It Matters for Investors

  • Protects underwriting assumptions tied to debt service and DSCR.
  • Reduces closing risk in volatile rate environments.
  • Stabilizes projected returns when modeling cash flow and refinance timing.

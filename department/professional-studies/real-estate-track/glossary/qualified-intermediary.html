Qualified Intermediary (QI)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Qualified Intermediary (QI)

Definition

A Qualified Intermediary (QI) is a neutral third party used in most 1031 exchanges to facilitate the transaction. The QI’s job is to hold the sale proceeds from your relinquished property and then apply those proceeds toward the purchase of your replacement property—so you don’t have constructive receipt of the funds.

Why a QI Matters

The biggest risk in a 1031 exchange is accidentally receiving the sale proceeds—directly or indirectly. If the IRS considers you to have control of the money, the exchange can fail and your gain may become taxable. A QI creates a compliant “buffer” between your sale and your purchase.

What a QI Typically Does

  • Prepares exchange documents: exchange agreement, assignment forms, notices, and instructions.
  • Receives and holds funds: sale proceeds are wired into an exchange account controlled by the QI.
  • Coordinates with closings: communicates with escrow/title and provides payoff/wire instructions.
  • Tracks deadlines: supports the 45-day identification window and 180-day exchange period.
  • Disburses funds: sends exchange proceeds to acquire replacement property (or properties).

What a QI Does Not Do

  • Tax or legal advice: a QI is generally not your tax counsel.
  • Underwriting or deal selection: they don’t tell you what to buy.
  • Guarantee compliance: your advisors must ensure the structure fits your facts and objectives.

Key Concepts

Constructive receipt

Constructive receipt means you didn’t necessarily touch the money, but you had the ability to access or control it. Many 1031 failures happen here—like closing instructions that allow proceeds to be released to you, or accounts that you can direct outside the QI agreement.

Assignment of contracts

In many exchanges, the sale contract (relinquished property) and purchase contract (replacement property) are assigned to the QI. You still close as seller/buyer, but the assignment helps satisfy the exchange structure and documentation trail.

Fees and Logistics

QI fees vary by complexity (single vs. multiple replacement properties, reverse exchanges, improvement exchanges, number of identifications, and extra disbursements). Many QIs charge:

  • Base exchange fee (typical for a straightforward forward exchange)
  • Wire / disbursement fees
  • Document or processing fees for complex exchange types

How to Choose a Qualified Intermediary

  • Security of funds: ask how exchange funds are held (segregated vs. pooled) and what controls exist.
  • Financial strength and insurance: inquire about bonding, E&O coverage, and internal controls.
  • Experience with your exchange type: forward vs. reverse vs. improvement exchanges.
  • Responsiveness: 1031 timelines move fast; you want a team that answers quickly.
  • Clear documentation: forms, instructions, and closing coordination should be straightforward.

Example

You sell a rental property on March 1. At closing, the net proceeds are wired to the QI’s exchange account. Within the exchange timeline, you identify replacement properties and close on one on June 15. The QI wires the held proceeds directly to the replacement closing—so you never receive the funds, and the exchange remains eligible (assuming all other requirements are met).

Common Pitfalls

  • Hiring the QI too late: the QI must be in place before the relinquished property closes.
  • Wrong party acting as QI: certain “disqualified persons” (like agents who recently served you) may not qualify.
  • Sloppy closing instructions: any pathway that releases proceeds to you can jeopardize the exchange.
  • Missing deadlines: the 45/180-day rules are unforgiving.
  • Boot surprises: leftover cash, non-like-kind items, or debt reduction can create taxable boot.

Concentration Risk

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Concentration Risk

Definition

Concentration risk is the risk that a portfolio becomes overly exposed to a single tenant, asset type, geographic market, borrower, or capital source, increasing vulnerability to adverse events.

Common Forms in Real Estate

  • Tenant concentration: One tenant represents a large percentage of rental income.
  • Geographic concentration: Assets are clustered in one city or region.
  • Property-type concentration: Portfolio heavily weighted toward a single sector (e.g., office).
  • Capital-source concentration: Reliance on one lender or equity partner.
  • Lease rollover concentration: Multiple leases expiring in the same year.

Why It Matters

Concentration amplifies downside risk. A single adverse event—such as tenant default, regional recession, or sector-specific disruption—can materially impact income, property value, and financing options.

Example

A single-tenant retail property leased to one national retailer generates 100% of the income. If the tenant files bankruptcy, the asset's value may decline sharply due to loss of cash flow, illustrating tenant concentration risk.

Interaction with Leverage

High leverage combined with concentration risk can significantly increase financial stress. If cash flow declines and debt service remains fixed, refinancing and covenant compliance may become challenging.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Diversification: Across markets, sectors, and tenant profiles.
  • Credit underwriting: Evaluate tenant financial strength.
  • Staggered lease maturities: Reduce rollover clustering.
  • Multiple capital relationships: Avoid reliance on one funding source.
  • Portfolio limits: Set maximum exposure thresholds per asset, tenant, or region.

Investor Applications

  • Risk budgeting: Allocate exposure based on return targets.
  • Stress testing: Model downside scenarios tied to key exposures.
  • Acquisition screening: Assess how a new deal changes overall portfolio mix.
  • Capital allocation: Rebalance toward underrepresented sectors or markets.

Common Pitfalls

  • Hidden exposure: Different assets may share the same economic driver.
  • Ignoring correlation: Diversification is ineffective if risks are highly correlated.
  • Overconfidence in anchor tenants: Even strong brands can face sector disruption.

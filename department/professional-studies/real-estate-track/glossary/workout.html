Workout

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Workout

Definition

A workout is the process of negotiating with a lender (or special servicer) to resolve a troubled real estate loan when a borrower is in default, at risk of default, or failing key covenants (like DSCR, occupancy, or liquidity tests). The goal is to reach an outcome that maximizes recovery while avoiding a forced sale or foreclosure when possible.

When Workouts Happen

Workouts typically begin after a triggering event such as missed payments, maturity with no refinance, covenant violations, major tenant loss, unexpected capex needs, or a market shock that reduces NOI or increases debt service.

  • Cash-flow stress: NOI falls below debt service or reserves run thin.
  • Maturity wall: loan comes due and refinancing proceeds are insufficient.
  • Value decline: LTV becomes too high for a new loan or sale proceeds.
  • Operational shock: rollover, vacancy, concessions, or expense spikes.

Common Workout Tools

A workout can range from a simple short-term extension to a full restructuring. Common tools include:

  • Forbearance: lender temporarily agrees not to enforce remedies while a plan is executed.
  • Extension: maturity date is pushed out (often with fees, paydown, and milestones).
  • Interest-only period: reduces near-term debt service to stabilize operations.
  • Rate modification: adjusts spread, index, or converts variable to fixed (or vice versa).
  • Principal paydown: borrower contributes cash to reduce balance and improve LTV.
  • Reserve requirements: increased TI/LC, capex, tax/insurance, or operating reserves.
  • Covenant reset: revised DSCR/LTV tests or new reporting and operating covenants.
  • Recourse / guarantees: additional guaranties, reserves, or springing recourse triggers.
  • Note sale / discounted payoff: lender sells the loan or agrees to a negotiated payoff.
  • Deed-in-lieu: borrower conveys property to lender to avoid foreclosure (when feasible).

What Lenders Typically Want

In a workout, lenders are usually optimizing for certainty of repayment and max recovery with minimal time and legal cost. They often require:

  • Transparency: frequent financial reporting, rent roll updates, and operating plans.
  • Borrower capital: new equity, paydown, or funded reserves (“show commitment”).
  • A credible plan: leasing strategy, capex scope, and timeline with measurable milestones.
  • Protection: covenants, cash management, and controls over major decisions and spending.

What Investors Evaluate

  • Time vs. money: is extending the loan cheaper than selling today at a discount?
  • Fresh equity needs: how much new cash is required to execute the plan and satisfy lender?
  • Business plan viability: realistic lease-up pace, rent assumptions, and capex outcomes.
  • Exit math: what valuation and terminal cap rate are needed to refinance or sell?
  • Control rights: who controls cash, capex approvals, and leasing decisions during the workout?

Example

An apartment property’s NOI declines after elevated vacancy and insurance increases. The loan is maturing in 6 months, but refinancing proceeds are short by $2,000,000. The borrower negotiates:

  • 12-month extension with an extension fee and new monthly reporting.
  • $750,000 paydown funded by new equity to reduce the refinance gap.
  • Interest-only for 6 months to support leasing and reduce cash burn.
  • New reserves for capex and operating shortfalls until DSCR stabilizes.

If stabilization fails, the workout agreement may require a brokered sale process or enable the lender to accelerate remedies.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-optimistic recovery plans: unrealistic rent growth or lease-up assumptions.
  • Ignoring cash management: lender-controlled lockboxes and reserves can change operations.
  • Fee stacking: extension fees, default interest, legal costs, and reserve funding add up fast.
  • Loss of optionality: missed milestones can trigger forced sale timelines or harsher terms.
  • Not modeling downside: workout “success” may still mean a lower equity return.

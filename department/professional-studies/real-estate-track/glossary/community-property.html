Community Property

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Definition

Community property is a legal ownership system recognized in certain U.S. states in which most property acquired during marriage is jointly owned by both spouses, regardless of whose name appears on the title.

Where It Applies

Community property laws apply in designated community property states (such as California, Texas, Arizona, Nevada, and others). Laws vary by jurisdiction.

What Qualifies as Community Property?

  • Real estate purchased during marriage.
  • Income earned by either spouse during marriage.
  • Assets acquired using marital funds.

Separate Property

Property typically considered separate (not community property) includes:

  • Assets owned before marriage.
  • Inheritance received by one spouse.
  • Gifts made specifically to one spouse.

Community Property vs. Joint Tenancy

  • Community Property: Based on marital law; equal ownership of marital assets.
  • Joint Tenancy: Equal ownership with right of survivorship, not limited to spouses.

Right of Survivorship Option

Some states allow couples to hold title as community property with right of survivorship, combining marital ownership rules with automatic transfer at death.

Divorce Implications

  • Community property is typically divided equally (50/50).
  • Courts may consider additional factors depending on state law.

Estate Planning Considerations

  • Surviving spouse often retains full ownership of their half interest.
  • Step-up in tax basis may apply to community property in certain jurisdictions.
  • Proper titling affects probate and tax outcomes.

Investor Considerations

  • Spousal consent may be required to sell or refinance property.
  • Marital ownership impacts estate transfers and financing.
  • Title review is critical during acquisition.

Financing Impact

  • Lenders often require both spouses to sign certain documents.
  • Liability may extend to both spouses depending on structure.

Common Pitfalls

  • Improper titling of property.
  • Commingling separate and community funds.
  • Failure to update estate documents.
  • Misunderstanding state-specific rules.

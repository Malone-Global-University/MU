Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Escrow

Definition

Escrow is a neutral, third-party process where funds, documents, and instructions are held and only released when the transaction’s closing conditions are satisfied. In most real estate acquisitions, escrow protects both sides: the buyer avoids paying before clear title and conditions are met, and the seller avoids transferring title before receiving funds.

What an Escrow Agent Does

  • Holds money and documents: earnest money, closing funds, payoff statements, deeds, affidavits.
  • Follows written instructions: the purchase agreement and any escrow instructions or amendments.
  • Coordinates closing deliverables: signatures, notarizations, lender requirements, recording.
  • Disburses funds at closing: seller proceeds, loan payoffs, commissions, taxes, fees, prorations.
  • Records documents: ensures the deed and related instruments are recorded (often with title).

Common Escrow Deposits

  • Earnest Money Deposit (EMD): buyer’s good-faith deposit, typically credited to the purchase price at closing.
  • Additional deposits: sometimes required at milestones (e.g., end of due diligence) to increase seller certainty.
  • Holdbacks: funds withheld post-closing to cover unresolved items (repairs, tenant issues, missing estoppels).

Escrow Timeline in a Typical Deal

  1. Open escrow: parties execute purchase agreement; escrow is opened with a title/escrow company.
  2. Deposit EMD: buyer wires EMD into escrow by the contract deadline.
  3. Due diligence period: buyer inspects and verifies; escrow receives reports, documents, and amendments.
  4. Loan + closing prep: lender conditions, insurance, entity docs, and closing statement draft.
  5. Close + record: buyer wires remaining funds; documents sign; deed records; escrow disburses.

Why It Matters

Escrow is the transaction’s control system. It reduces fraud risk, enforces timing, and ensures closing happens in the correct sequence—especially important when multiple parties are involved (seller, buyer, lender, title, brokers, and sometimes a qualified intermediary for a 1031 exchange).

Investor Uses

  • Protecting downside: structure contingencies and release conditions so money isn’t trapped.
  • Controlling closing mechanics: define what must be delivered (estoppels, SNDAs, permits, bills).
  • Managing prorations: rent, taxes, utilities, and deposits are properly allocated.
  • Handling post-close items: holdbacks for unresolved repairs, delinquent accounts, or tenant claims.

Example

A buyer places a $250,000 earnest money deposit into escrow. The purchase agreement says the deposit becomes non-refundable after the due diligence deadline. If the buyer terminates before that deadline under a valid contingency, escrow returns the deposit. If the buyer terminates after the deadline without a contractual basis, escrow releases the deposit to the seller (subject to the escrow instructions and any dispute process).

Common Pitfalls

  • Unclear release terms: vague language can trigger disputes and freeze funds.
  • Missing deadlines: late deposits, late notices, or late lender conditions can default the buyer.
  • Relying on “understandings”: escrow only follows written instructions, not verbal agreements.
  • Wiring fraud: always verify wire instructions via a known, trusted channel before sending funds.
  • Bad prorations: incorrect rent rolls or delinquency assumptions can cause post-close reconciliation pain.

Fee Simple

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Fee Simple

Definition

Fee simple (often called fee simple absolute) is the most complete form of real property ownership. The owner holds title to the land and the improvements (buildings) for an indefinite duration, with the right to use, lease, sell, or transfer the property—subject to laws, zoning, taxes, and recorded restrictions.

What Rights Come With Fee Simple Ownership

Fee simple ownership typically includes the “bundle of rights,” which can be limited by law or by recorded documents (like easements and CC&Rs).

  • Possession: the right to occupy and control the property.
  • Use: the right to use the property (subject to zoning and regulations).
  • Exclusion: the right to exclude others (subject to legal access rights).
  • Enjoyment: the right to enjoy the benefits of ownership and income.
  • Disposition: the right to sell, gift, or bequeath the property.

Fee Simple vs. Leasehold

Fee simple is often contrasted with a leasehold interest, where the investor owns the right to use the land/improvements for a defined period under a ground lease, but does not own the land in perpetuity.

Key differences

  • Duration: fee simple is indefinite; leasehold ends at lease expiration.
  • Value drivers: leasehold value depends on remaining term, rent resets, and reversion terms.
  • Financing: leasehold can be financeable but often with added lender requirements and term constraints.
  • Control: ground leases can restrict use, transfers, or redevelopment.

Why It Matters

Ownership type affects valuation, risk, lender appetite, and the flexibility of your business plan. Fee simple generally offers the broadest control and the most standard underwriting assumptions for lenders and buyers.

Investor Uses

  • Transaction clarity: confirm whether you are buying fee simple or a leasehold interest.
  • Underwriting: assess restrictions (easements, CC&Rs, zoning) that limit the “bundle of rights.”
  • Exit planning: broader buyer pool and simpler story for fee simple assets.
  • Redevelopment potential: fee simple can make repositioning easier (still subject to approvals).

Example

You purchase an apartment building and the land it sits on. You can operate it as rentals, renovate units, adjust the amenity package, and sell at any time. That is a typical fee simple acquisition. Contrast that with buying a building on leased land where a ground lease requires certain approvals and has a scheduled rent reset—this is a leasehold structure and will underwrite differently.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “fee simple” means unrestricted: easements, CC&Rs, and zoning can materially limit use.
  • Ignoring title exceptions: access, shared driveways, encroachments, and utility easements matter.
  • Confusing “fee simple” with “free and clear”: fee simple describes ownership type, not whether there is debt.
  • Overlooking reversionary clauses: rare, but some deeds include conditions that affect long-term rights.

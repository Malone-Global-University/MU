Capital Improvement

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Capital Improvement

Definition

A capital improvement is an expenditure that materially increases a property's value, extends its useful life, or adapts it to a new or improved use. Unlike routine repairs, capital improvements are capitalized and depreciated over time.

Capital Improvement vs. Repair

The distinction is critical for both accounting and tax purposes.

  • Repair: Maintains property in normal operating condition (expensed immediately).
  • Capital Improvement: Enhances, upgrades, or extends life (capitalized and depreciated).

Examples

  • Capital Improvements: New roof, HVAC replacement, structural expansion, major renovation.
  • Repairs: Patching drywall, fixing a leak, repainting a small area, replacing broken fixtures.

Financial Statement Impact

  • Recorded on the balance sheet as an asset.
  • Added to the property’s tax basis.
  • Depreciated over the applicable recovery period.
  • Not immediately deducted from net operating income (NOI).

Tax Treatment

Capital improvements increase the property’s depreciable basis and are depreciated over time (e.g., 27.5 years for residential rental, 39 years for commercial property in the U.S.).

Certain components may qualify for shorter recovery periods if identified through a cost segregation study.

Role in Underwriting

  • Value-add strategy: Renovations drive rent growth and appreciation.
  • Capex reserves: Investors set aside funds for planned improvements.
  • IRR impact: Timing of capital expenditures affects projected returns.
  • Exit pricing: Improved assets may command lower cap rates.

Example

An investor spends $500,000 upgrading unit interiors, replacing the roof, and modernizing common areas. These costs are capitalized, added to basis, and depreciated over time rather than expensed in the current year.

Common Pitfalls

  • Misclassifying expenses: Incorrectly expensing capital items.
  • Underestimating total project costs.
  • Ignoring tenant disruption impact.
  • Failing to model return on invested capital.

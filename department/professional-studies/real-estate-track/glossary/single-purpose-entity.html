Single-Purpose Entity (SPE)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Single-Purpose Entity (SPE)

Definition

A Single-Purpose Entity (SPE) is a legal entity—typically an LLC or corporation— created solely to own and operate a specific asset. In commercial real estate, SPEs are commonly required by lenders to isolate financial and legal risk.

Purpose

  • Risk isolation: shields other sponsor assets from property-level liabilities.
  • Collateral protection: ensures the lender’s loan is tied only to the pledged asset.
  • Bankruptcy remoteness: reduces the likelihood that unrelated financial issues affect the property.
  • Clarity: simplifies accounting and ownership tracking.

Common SPE Requirements

Loan documents often impose strict “separateness covenants” requiring the SPE to:

  • Maintain separate bank accounts and financial records.
  • Avoid commingling funds with affiliates.
  • Refrain from incurring unrelated debt.
  • Operate only the specified property.
  • Maintain independent managers (in some structures).

Why It Matters

SPE structures are central to non-recourse financing. If an SPE violates separateness covenants—such as commingling funds—it may trigger liability under a carve-out guaranty.

Investor Considerations

  • Formation costs: legal and administrative setup.
  • Ongoing compliance: bookkeeping discipline is critical.
  • Tax implications: structure may affect tax reporting.
  • Transfer restrictions: ownership changes often require lender approval.

Example

A sponsor acquires a 150-unit apartment community. Instead of holding it under the parent company, they form “123 Main Apartments LLC” as a separate SPE. The loan is made to that LLC. If another asset owned by the sponsor underperforms or faces litigation, it does not directly impact the apartment property or its loan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Commingling funds: mixing operating accounts with other entities.
  • Unauthorized transfers: changing ownership without lender consent.
  • Operational shortcuts: ignoring corporate formalities.
  • Misunderstanding recourse triggers: violating SPE covenants can create personal liability.

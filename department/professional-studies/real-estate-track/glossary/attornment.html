Attornment

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Attornment

Definition

Attornment is a tenant’s agreement to recognize a new owner or lender as the landlord if the property is sold or foreclosed upon.

It ensures that the lease remains valid and enforceable after a transfer of ownership.

Why Attornment Matters

  • Protects lenders in foreclosure situations.
  • Provides continuity of rental income.
  • Maintains lease stability for tenants.
  • Reduces uncertainty during ownership changes.

Common Scenario

  1. Landlord defaults on mortgage.
  2. Lender forecloses on property.
  3. Ownership transfers to lender or new buyer.
  4. Tenant attorns to the new landlord.

Attornment in SNDA Agreements

Attornment commonly appears in an SNDA Agreement (Subordination, Non-Disturbance, and Attornment).

  • Subordination: Lease is subordinate to mortgage.
  • Non-Disturbance: Tenant can remain after foreclosure.
  • Attornment: Tenant recognizes new landlord.

Attornment vs Assignment of Lease

  • Attornment: Tenant acknowledges new landlord.
  • Assignment: Lease rights are transferred to another party.

Risk Considerations

  • Tenants may negotiate limitations on liability to new landlord.
  • Lenders rely on enforceability of lease terms.
  • Lease language must clearly define obligations.

Benefits to Investors

  • Preserves asset value during foreclosure.
  • Maintains occupancy and cash flow.
  • Improves financing security.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failing to include attornment provisions in lease.
  • Ambiguous SNDA terms.
  • Conflicts between lease and mortgage documents.
  • State-specific enforcement issues.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm lease includes attornment clause.
  • Review SNDA agreement carefully.
  • Ensure lender rights are protected.
  • Verify tenant protections are balanced.

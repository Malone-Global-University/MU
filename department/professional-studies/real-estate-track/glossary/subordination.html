Subordination

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Subordination

Definition

Subordination is a structural arrangement in which one claim, loan, or tranche ranks below another in priority of payment or recovery. In commercial real estate and structured finance, subordination determines who gets paid first and who absorbs losses first.

Subordination in CMBS

In a CMBS transaction, subordinate (junior) tranches provide protection to senior tranches. Losses from loan defaults are applied from the bottom up, meaning junior bonds absorb principal losses before senior bonds are impacted.

Example (CMBS)

  • $500M total pool
  • $100M subordinate bonds
  • $400M senior bonds

The $100M subordinate layer provides 20% subordination to the senior bonds. Losses must exceed $100M before seniors are impaired.

Subordination in Capital Stacks

Subordination also applies within a property’s capital stack:

  • Senior debt: First lien, highest payment priority.
  • Mezzanine debt: Junior to senior loan, higher risk and yield.
  • Preferred equity: Senior to common equity but junior to debt.
  • Common equity: Residual claim, absorbs losses first.

Why Subordination Matters

  • Risk allocation: Determines exposure to loss.
  • Return differentiation: Higher subordination usually means higher yield.
  • Control rights: Junior positions may gain control in default scenarios.
  • Credit enhancement: Subordination strengthens senior credit ratings.

Subordination Agreements

In layered debt structures, formal subordination agreements define the priority of claims and payment rights between lenders. These documents clarify enforcement rights, cure periods, and intercreditor relationships.

Investor Implications

  • Cycle sensitivity: Junior positions are more volatile.
  • Recovery modeling: Downside scenarios must reflect priority order.
  • Liquidity risk: Subordinate positions may be harder to exit.
  • Return expectations: Yield compensates for structural position.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring intercreditor terms: Legal rights can differ from assumed priority.
  • Overestimating protection: Severe downturns can exhaust subordination layers.
  • Misjudging leverage: High leverage increases junior tranche risk.
  • Confusing lien priority with cash flow priority: They are related but not identical.

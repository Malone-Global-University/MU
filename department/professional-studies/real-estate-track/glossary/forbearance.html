Forbearance

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Forbearance

Definition

Forbearance is a temporary agreement where a lender agrees to delay or refrain from exercising remedies after a borrower default (or potential default), such as accelerating the loan, charging default interest, or initiating foreclosure—as long as the borrower meets specified conditions.

What Forbearance Does (and Doesn’t) Do

Forbearance is typically a workout tool designed to buy time and stabilize performance. It usually does not erase the default; instead, it defines how the parties will handle it for a limited period.

Typically does

  • Pauses enforcement actions (foreclosure/acceleration) for a defined time.
  • Sets a plan to catch up, defer, or restructure payments.
  • Requires reporting and operational milestones to prove stabilization.

Typically does not

  • Forgive principal (unless explicitly negotiated).
  • Eliminate all fees/default interest automatically.
  • Guarantee long-term relief—most agreements are short, conditional, and revocable upon new defaults.

Common Forbearance Terms

  • Payment deferral: skipped payments are added to the balance or repaid later.
  • Interest accrual: interest continues to accrue during the forbearance period.
  • Temporary interest-only: borrower pays interest only to improve cash flow.
  • Milestones/cure plan: leasing targets, reserve funding, capital injections, or sale/refi deadlines.
  • Reserves: lender may require cash reserves or controlled accounts.
  • Fees and default rate: workout fees, legal costs, and default interest may apply or be waived in part.
  • Waivers and releases: borrower may waive defenses/claims and reaffirm loan documents.

Forbearance vs. Loan Modification

While the terms can overlap, the intent is different:

  • Forbearance: a short-term bridge to avoid immediate enforcement while the borrower stabilizes.
  • Modification: a longer-term change to the loan’s legal terms (rate, maturity, amortization, covenants).

Why It Matters

Forbearance can preserve value by preventing a distressed sale or foreclosure while the asset recovers. But it can also shift control to the lender through tighter reporting, reserves, and milestones. Investors need to understand the cash flow impact, timeline pressure, and the risk of “kicking the can” without solving the underlying NOI problem.

Investor Uses

  • Stabilization runway: buy time to re-lease units, complete repairs, or solve collections issues.
  • Capital planning: align a capital raise or asset sale with an agreed timeline.
  • Avoiding forced outcomes: reduce the risk of foreclosure or a rushed disposition.
  • Preserving optionality: keep the deal alive long enough to execute the business plan.

Example

A property’s NOI drops and the loan fails its DSCR covenant. The lender issues a default notice. The borrower negotiates a 6-month forbearance where the lender pauses enforcement actions if the borrower: (1) funds a reserve, (2) provides weekly leasing reports, and (3) makes interest-only payments while completing unit turns. Any deferred amounts are repaid at refinance or added to the loan balance per the agreement.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring legal language: waivers/releases can be broad; know what you’re giving up.
  • Underwriting the cure too optimistically: if leasing/collections don’t improve, you run out of runway.
  • Not modeling accrued amounts: deferred payments, fees, and default interest can snowball.
  • Missing milestones: one missed covenant can terminate forbearance and trigger remedies quickly.
  • Assuming forbearance equals “safe”: it’s temporary and usually increases lender oversight and control.

Title Defect

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Title Defect

Definition

A Title Defect is any issue in a property’s chain of title that prevents the owner from having clear, marketable ownership or that creates uncertainty about ownership rights. Title defects are often referred to as a cloud on title.

In simple terms: something is wrong in the property records that makes ownership unclear.

Common Types of Title Defects

  • Unreleased mortgages or liens
  • Mechanic’s liens filed by contractors
  • Clerical or recording errors (misspelled names, incorrect legal description)
  • Forged deeds or fraudulent transfers
  • Unknown heirs or missing signatures (estate issues)
  • Boundary disputes or encroachments
  • Unrecorded easements or restrictive covenants
  • Improper foreclosure or tax sale procedures

Why Title Defects Matter

  • Buyers: May be unable to close or resell later.
  • Lenders: May refuse to fund without insurable title.
  • Investors: Increased risk, delays, and legal costs.
  • Valuation: A defect can reduce marketability and value.

How Title Defects Are Discovered

  • Title search during due diligence
  • Preliminary title report (“title commitment”)
  • Survey review for boundary issues
  • Lender underwriting and closing requirements

How Title Defects Are Cured

  • Recording a lien release or satisfaction
  • Corrective deed or affidavit
  • Obtaining missing signatures or probate documentation
  • Boundary agreement or easement documentation
  • Quiet title action (court judgment to clear ownership)
  • Negotiated payoff or settlement with claimant

Title Defect vs. Title Exception

  • Title Defect: Problem impairing ownership/marketability.
  • Title Exception: A known item excluded from title insurance coverage (may or may not be a defect).

Title Insurance and Coverage

Title insurance can protect against certain undiscovered defects, but it typically will not cover items listed as exceptions in the policy.

Example

An investor buys a property and later discovers an old mortgage was never properly released. The lender refuses to refinance until the defect is cured. The owner works with the title company and prior lender to record a satisfaction, clearing title.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming a clean deed means clean title.
  • Failing to review the legal description and survey.
  • Missing mechanic’s lien windows after construction.
  • Not understanding title exceptions and endorsements.

Investor Checklist

  • Order a title report early in due diligence.
  • Review all exceptions and recorded documents.
  • Match survey to legal description.
  • Require cures prior to closing (or escrow holdbacks).
  • Confirm appropriate title insurance endorsements.

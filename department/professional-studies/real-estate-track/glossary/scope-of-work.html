Scope of Work (SOW)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Scope of Work (SOW)

Definition

A Scope of Work (SOW) is a detailed document that spells out exactly what work will be done on a property project—what gets installed, repaired, replaced, or upgraded; how it’s done; and what “done” looks like. In real estate investing, a clear SOW is the bridge between underwriting and execution: it turns a renovation plan into a set of bid-ready instructions that contractors, vendors, and asset managers can follow.

Why It Matters

The SOW reduces execution risk by making expectations measurable. A well-written SOW helps you compare bids apples-to-apples, avoid costly surprises, and limit change orders that blow up schedules and budgets.

  • Cost control: clearer work definitions produce tighter bids and fewer “unknowns.”
  • Schedule control: sequencing and lead times become visible early.
  • Quality control: standards are defined up front (materials, finishes, install methods).
  • Accountability: vendors can be managed against a written deliverable.

What a Good SOW Includes

  • Project overview: goals, property address, assumptions, constraints (occupied units, quiet hours).
  • Work breakdown: scopes by trade (demo, framing, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, flooring, paint, etc.).
  • Quantities: unit counts, square footage, fixture counts, linear feet, door/window counts.
  • Material specifications: brands/models or performance standards (“LVP, 20-mil wear layer or better”).
  • Finish schedule: standardized selections (paint color, hardware, countertops, lighting, appliances).
  • Site logistics: access, parking, trash hauling, staging, tenant notices, keys, security protocols.
  • Permits & code: who pulls permits, inspection responsibility, required licensing and compliance.
  • Quality standards: workmanship expectations, punch standards, cleanup requirements.
  • Deliverables: photos, daily logs, as-builts, warranty docs, lien releases.
  • Schedule expectations: start date assumptions, phasing plan, lead times, unit turn targets.

Types of Scopes in Multifamily

  • Unit turns: flooring, paint, fixtures, appliances, hardware, minor repairs.
  • Interior value-add: kitchens, baths, lighting packages, in-unit laundry, layout changes.
  • Exterior/common area: siding, paint, roofing, parking lot, landscaping, signage, clubhouse.
  • Building systems: plumbing supply/drain, electrical service, HVAC replacements, water heaters.
  • Life safety: fire alarms, emergency lighting, egress, railings, trip hazards.

Example (Mini SOW Snippet)

Below is an example of how a scope item becomes bid-ready:

  • Flooring – Unit Turns: Remove existing carpet and pad in living/bedrooms. Install LVP throughout unit (excluding bathrooms) with underlayment per manufacturer. Provide quarter-round at base as needed. Include transitions at doors. Quantity: ~650 SF per unit. Standard: LVP 20-mil wear layer or better.

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague language: “update kitchen” or “refresh unit” invites bid gaps and change orders.
  • Missing quantities: contractors price uncertainty into the bid (or exclude items entirely).
  • No standards: without material specs, the lowest bid may reflect the lowest quality.
  • Ignoring occupancy realities: occupied work requires tenant coordination, noise windows, protection.
  • Not defining inclusions/exclusions: dumpsters, permits, patch/paint standards, haul-off, protection.

Investor Uses

  • Due diligence: convert inspection findings into a priced plan (capex and timing).
  • Capex planning: build a realistic renovation budget and contingency framework.
  • Bid leveling: compare vendor proposals on the same defined scope.
  • Execution management: track progress via scopes, phases, and punch lists.

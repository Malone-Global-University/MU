Seller Disclosure

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Seller Disclosure

Definition

A Seller Disclosure is a written statement provided by a property seller that identifies known defects, legal issues, environmental concerns, or other material facts that may affect the property's value, safety, or desirability.

Purpose

Seller disclosures promote transparency and reduce the risk of post-closing disputes. They help allocate risk between buyer and seller by documenting what is known and communicated prior to closing.

What Is Typically Disclosed

  • Structural issues: foundation problems, roof leaks, major repairs.
  • Environmental hazards: mold, asbestos, lead-based paint, contamination.
  • Water intrusion: flooding history, drainage problems.
  • Mechanical systems: HVAC, plumbing, electrical deficiencies.
  • Legal matters: pending litigation, zoning violations, code issues.
  • Insurance claims: prior damage events affecting the property.

Residential vs. Commercial

Disclosure requirements are generally more standardized in residential transactions. In commercial real estate, disclosures are often supplemented—or replaced—by detailed due diligence materials and contractual representations within the purchase agreement.

Why It Matters

  • Legal protection: failure to disclose known material defects can create liability.
  • Pricing impact: known issues may justify price adjustments or credits.
  • Risk evaluation: helps buyers prioritize inspections and third-party reports.
  • Reputation: transparency builds credibility in repeat transactions.

Limitations

A seller disclosure typically covers only known issues. It does not replace a buyer’s independent inspections or financial review. Buyers remain responsible for conducting thorough due diligence.

Example

A seller discloses prior water intrusion in several units due to exterior siding failure. During due diligence, the buyer confirms the issue and obtains contractor estimates. The disclosure allows the buyer to quantify repair costs and negotiate a credit or adjust underwriting assumptions accordingly.

Common Pitfalls

  • Incomplete disclosure: omitting known defects can lead to litigation.
  • Overreliance: buyers assuming disclosure replaces inspections.
  • Ambiguity: vague statements that lack detail.
  • Ignoring state laws: disclosure obligations vary by jurisdiction.

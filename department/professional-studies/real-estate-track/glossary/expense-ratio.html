Expense Ratio

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Expense Ratio

Definition

The expense ratio measures how much of a property’s income is consumed by operating expenses. It is typically calculated as Operating Expenses ÷ Effective Gross Income (EGI). Expense ratio is a fast “sanity check” metric for underwriting and operations.

How It’s Measured

Expense ratio is expressed as a percentage (for example, 42%). Higher expense ratios mean a larger share of income is being spent to operate the property, leaving less for NOI and debt service.

Common formulas

  • Expense Ratio = Operating Expenses ÷ Effective Gross Income (EGI)
  • NOI Margin = NOI ÷ EGI = 1 − Expense Ratio
  • Operating Expenses (typical) = Taxes + Insurance + Utilities + Payroll + Repairs/Maintenance + Management + Admin + Contract Services

What Counts as “Operating Expenses”

Operating expenses generally include the recurring costs required to run the property. What is included can vary by owner, lender, and report format, so always confirm the definition.

Typically included

  • Property taxes and insurance
  • Utilities and trash
  • Repairs and maintenance (R&M)
  • Payroll and staffing
  • Property management fees
  • Landscaping, janitorial, security, pest control
  • Admin, marketing, and leasing costs

Typically excluded

  • Debt service: principal and interest
  • Capital expenditures (CapEx): major replacements/renovations
  • Depreciation and income taxes
  • Owner distributions

Why It Matters

Expense ratio helps investors quickly spot deals that may be mis-underwritten. If a property’s expense ratio is materially below comparable assets, it may be missing line items, deferring maintenance, under-reserving for payroll, or benefiting from temporary conditions that won’t persist. If it’s materially higher, there may be operational inefficiency, utility leakage, adverse tax/insurance, or a tenant mix requiring more service.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting sanity check: compare modeled expenses to historicals and market comps.
  • Operational diagnostics: identify categories driving costs (taxes, utilities, payroll, R&M).
  • NOI planning: understand how expense control flows into NOI growth and valuation.
  • Scenario analysis: stress test tax reassessments, insurance spikes, or utility rate increases.

Example

A property has $1,500,000 of Effective Gross Income (EGI) and $615,000 of operating expenses.

  • Expense Ratio = 615,000 ÷ 1,500,000 = 41%
  • NOI = 1,500,000 − 615,000 = $885,000
  • NOI Margin = 885,000 ÷ 1,500,000 = 59%

If expenses rise by $75,000 (taxes/insurance/utility rates), NOI drops to $810,000, reducing value and DSCR—even if rents stay flat.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong denominator: expense ratio should usually be against EGI, not GPI or “scheduled rent.”
  • Ignoring capitalization needs: a “low expense ratio” can hide deferred maintenance that shows up as CapEx later.
  • Mixing reimbursed expenses: in NNN/expense-recovery properties, reimbursements can distort ratios if not presented consistently.
  • One-time anomalies: unusual repairs, storms, insurance claims, or vacancies can temporarily skew ratios.
  • Not segmenting asset type: expense ratios vary by property type, vintage, and amenity package.

Real Estate Professional

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Real Estate Professional

Definition

A Real Estate Professional is a taxpayer classification under IRS rules that allows qualified individuals to treat rental real estate activities as non-passive, potentially enabling full deduction of real estate losses against active income.

Why It Matters

Normally, rental real estate losses are considered passive and can only offset passive income. However, if a taxpayer qualifies as a Real Estate Professional and materially participates in their real estate activities, passive loss limitations may not apply.

Qualification Requirements (General Overview)

  • More than 50% of total personal service time must be in real property trades or businesses.
  • At least 750 hours per year must be spent in qualifying real estate activities.
  • The taxpayer must materially participate in those activities.

Qualifying Activities

  • Development or redevelopment
  • Construction or reconstruction
  • Acquisition or conversion
  • Rental operations
  • Property management
  • Brokerage or leasing

Example

An investor earns $300,000 in W-2 income and generates $200,000 in real estate losses due to depreciation and cost segregation.

  • Without Real Estate Professional status: losses may be limited or suspended.
  • With qualification: losses may offset W-2 income (subject to material participation and tax rules).

Material Participation

Even if the 750-hour and 50% tests are met, the taxpayer must also materially participate in rental activities to treat them as non-passive. There are multiple IRS tests used to determine material participation.

Spousal Considerations

For married couples filing jointly, qualification can apply if one spouse meets the Real Estate Professional requirements.

Investor Perspective

Real Estate Professional status can significantly enhance tax efficiency, particularly when combined with cost segregation and bonus depreciation. However, documentation of hours and activities is critical.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failing to document hours
  • Misunderstanding material participation rules
  • Assuming property ownership alone qualifies
  • Not consulting a qualified tax advisor

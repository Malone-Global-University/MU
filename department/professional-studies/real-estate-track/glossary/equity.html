Equity

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Equity

Definition

Equity is the ownership value in a property after paying off all debt and senior claims. In simple terms, it’s what’s left for the owner(s) if the property were sold today and the loan was repaid.

How It’s Measured

Equity is typically calculated as the property’s current value minus outstanding loan balance (and any other senior liens). Investors also talk about equity as a percentage of value, which connects directly to loan-to-value (LTV).

Common formulas

  • Equity (dollars) = Property Value − Outstanding Debt − Other Senior Claims
  • Equity Percentage = Equity ÷ Property Value
  • LTV = Outstanding Debt ÷ Property Value

How Equity Grows (and Shrinks)

Primary drivers of equity growth

  • Appreciation: property value rises (often from NOI growth or cap rate changes).
  • Principal paydown: the loan balance decreases over time.
  • Value creation: renovations, operational improvements, or lease-up increases NOI.

Common reasons equity shrinks

  • Value decline: NOI drops or cap rates expand (higher required returns).
  • Over-leverage: high debt magnifies downside when values soften.
  • Dilution: new equity raised at unfavorable terms reduces existing owners’ share.

Why It Matters

Equity is your downside buffer and your upside participation. More equity generally reduces default risk and gives flexibility in downturns (refinance, hold, fund capex). Less equity increases return potential in good times, but increases risk if the market moves against you.

Investor Uses

  • Risk management: maintain a cushion against NOI decline or cap rate expansion.
  • Refinance planning: estimate proceeds available based on target LTV and lender DSCR.
  • Partnership structuring: allocate ownership, preferred returns, and promote waterfalls.
  • Return analysis: understand how leverage changes cash-on-cash, IRR, and downside outcomes.

Example

A property is worth $10,000,000 today and has an outstanding loan balance of $6,500,000. Ignoring other liens:

  • Equity = 10,000,000 − 6,500,000 = $3,500,000
  • Equity Percentage = 3,500,000 ÷ 10,000,000 = 35%
  • LTV = 6,500,000 ÷ 10,000,000 = 65%

If NOI increases and the property value rises to $11,000,000 while the loan amortizes to $6,300,000, equity becomes $4,700,000—growth from both appreciation and principal paydown.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing equity with cash: equity is value, not necessarily liquid unless you refinance or sell.
  • Overstating value: pro forma NOI and optimistic cap rates can create “paper equity.”
  • Ignoring transaction costs: sale costs and taxes reduce net equity realized.
  • Not stress testing leverage: small value changes can wipe out equity at high LTV.
  • Not tracking ownership dilution: additional capital raises can reduce your percentage even if equity grows.

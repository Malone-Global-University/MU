Break Even Occupancy

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Break Even Occupancy

Definition

Break-even occupancy (also called break-even occupancy rate) is the minimum occupancy level a property needs so that its effective gross income covers operating expenses and debt service (and sometimes reserves), resulting in approximately zero cash flow before taxes.

How It’s Calculated

Break-even occupancy is typically expressed as a percentage and is calculated by dividing required costs by the property’s potential income (adjusted for other income and credit loss assumptions).

Common formulas

  • Break Even Occupancy = (Operating Expenses + Debt Service) ÷ Gross Potential Income
  • Break Even Occupancy (with reserves) = (Operating Expenses + Replacement Reserves + Debt Service) ÷ Gross Potential Income

In practice, many models use Net Operating Income (NOI) and compute the minimum occupancy needed for NOI to equal debt service. If your underwriting includes other income, concessions, vacancy loss, or bad debt, those assumptions should be applied consistently on both sides of the equation.

Why It Matters

Break-even occupancy is a fast way to quantify downside cushion. A property with a lower break-even occupancy can withstand more vacancy or rent pressure before it becomes cash-flow negative. A higher break-even occupancy means the deal is more sensitive to lease-up risk, renewals, concessions, and expense inflation.

Investor Uses

  • Credit and refinance risk: gauge how much income drop the property can absorb before stressing coverage.
  • Lease-up underwriting: stress-test stabilization assumptions and timing to positive cash flow.
  • Operations focus: identify whether the “lever” is rent growth, occupancy, or expense control.
  • Deal screening: quickly compare risk profiles across multiple opportunities.

Example

A small multifamily property has $500,000 of gross potential income (GPI) at 100% occupancy. Annual operating expenses are $220,000 and annual debt service is $150,000.

  • Required costs = 220,000 + 150,000 = $370,000
  • Break-even occupancy = 370,000 ÷ 500,000 = 74%

This implies the property can fall to roughly 74% occupancy (assuming rents/collections scale proportionally) before it stops covering expenses and debt service.

Common Pitfalls

  • Forgetting other income: parking, RUBS, laundry, and fees can materially reduce break-even when included.
  • Mixing monthly and annual numbers: ensure consistent time periods and units.
  • Not modeling concessions/credit loss: “occupied” units that don’t pay full rent can still miss break-even.
  • Ignoring step-ups: taxes, insurance, payroll, and utilities may not scale linearly with occupancy.

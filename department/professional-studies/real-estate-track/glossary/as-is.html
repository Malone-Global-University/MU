As-Is

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary As-Is

Definition

As-Is means a property is being sold in its current physical and financial condition, without the seller agreeing to make repairs or improvements prior to closing. The buyer accepts the asset with all existing defects—known or unknown—subject to contractual representations, disclosures, and applicable law.

What It Really Means

  • No repair obligation: seller is not required to fix deferred maintenance.
  • Risk shifts to buyer: buyer bears responsibility for condition after closing.
  • Due diligence is critical: inspections, file audits, and third-party reports matter.
  • Not “no disclosure”: sellers must still comply with legal disclosure requirements.

Physical vs. Financial As-Is

In investment transactions, “as-is” applies not only to the building’s physical condition, but also to leases, contracts, operating structure, and financial performance.

  • Physical: roof, plumbing, HVAC, structure, environmental.
  • Financial: rent roll accuracy, concessions, bad debt, expense run-rate.
  • Operational: staffing, vendor contracts, compliance status.

Why It Matters

As-is clauses define risk allocation. In competitive markets, sellers often require as-is terms to reduce uncertainty and limit post-contract disputes. Buyers price that risk into their underwriting through higher required returns or repair reserves.

Investor Uses

  • Pricing discipline: underwrite CapEx realistically before making an offer.
  • Speed: fewer repair negotiations can streamline closing.
  • Negotiation leverage: serious defects discovered during diligence may still support a retrade.
  • Capital planning: build repair budgets and reserves into the pro forma.

Example

An apartment building is marketed “as-is.” During inspections, the buyer discovers $400,000 in exterior repairs and roof replacement needs. The seller declines to fix them. The buyer must decide whether to:

  • Proceed at the agreed price and absorb the cost,
  • Request a price adjustment during due diligence, or
  • Terminate within the contractual contingency period.

In an as-is sale, the seller is not obligated to cure defects unless previously agreed.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating CapEx: cosmetic issues can hide structural problems.
  • Ignoring lease quality: “as-is” includes tenant and income risk.
  • Overconfidence: skipping thorough diligence to win the deal.
  • Confusing as-is with no recourse: fraud or misrepresentation is still actionable.

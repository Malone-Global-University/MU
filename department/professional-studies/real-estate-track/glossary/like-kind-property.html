Like-Kind Property

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Like-Kind Property

Definition

Like-kind property is a term used in Section 1031 exchanges. It generally means real property held for investment or productive use in a trade or business that can be exchanged for other real property of a similar nature or character. “Like-kind” in real estate is usually interpreted broadly: it does not require the replacement property to be the same type (e.g., apartment-to-apartment).

What Usually Qualifies (Broadly)

In practice, many different real estate types can be “like-kind” to each other when they are held for investment/business use. For example, investors commonly exchange:

  • Multifamily for industrial or retail
  • Office for self-storage
  • Raw land for an income-producing property
  • Single-tenant net lease for apartments

What Usually Does Not Qualify

  • Primary residences (personal-use homes)
  • Second homes used primarily for personal enjoyment
  • Property held primarily for resale (dealer/inventory/flips)
  • Most personal property (1031 is generally limited to real property)
  • Interests that are not real property (varies by structure; always verify with a qualified intermediary and tax counsel)

Why It Matters

The “like-kind” standard is one of the key gates to qualifying for a tax-deferred 1031 exchange. If the replacement property is not like-kind (or the property is not held for the right purpose), the exchange can fail and trigger a taxable event.

Investor Uses

  • Portfolio repositioning: move from management-intensive assets into simpler operations.
  • Risk management: diversify by geography or asset class without immediate tax recognition.
  • Scaling: consolidate multiple smaller assets into one larger asset (or vice versa).
  • Estate planning: coordinate long-term hold strategies with tax planning.

Common Pitfalls

  • Wrong intent: treating a flip as an investment property can jeopardize eligibility.
  • Mixing personal use: heavy personal use of a “replacement” property can create problems.
  • Title/ownership mismatch: the taxpayer selling must generally be the one buying (entity structure matters).
  • Confusing “like-kind” with “equal value”: like-kind is about property nature/character; tax deferral also depends on value, debt, and cash (boot).
  • Assuming every structure qualifies: DST/TIC/partnership interests and entity changes have nuances—verify early.

Example

An investor sells a 12-unit apartment building held for investment and identifies a small industrial warehouse as the replacement. Even though the assets are different types, the exchange may still qualify because both are real property held for investment and are generally treated as “like-kind” in this context.

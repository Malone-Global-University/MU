Definition

A waterfall (or distribution waterfall) is the rulebook that determines how cash from a real estate deal is paid out to participants—usually limited partners (LPs) and the general partner/sponsor (GP). Distributions “flow” through ordered tiers, and each tier must be satisfied before the next tier begins.

What a Waterfall Applies To

  • Operating cash flow: periodic distributions from property NOI after debt service and reserves.
  • Capital events: refinance proceeds (if distributed), partial sales, and final sale proceeds.
  • Clawbacks / true-ups: in some structures, the GP may return amounts to ensure the agreed split is met.

Common Waterfall Building Blocks

1) Return of capital

Investors typically receive their contributed equity back before profit splits accelerate.

2) Preferred return (“pref”)

A preferred return is a target rate of return paid to LPs before the GP participates in promote (subject to the deal’s terms). It’s often expressed as an annual percentage (e.g., 7%–9%) and may be:

  • Accruing: unpaid pref accumulates and must be satisfied later.
  • Non-accruing: missed pref does not carry forward (less LP-friendly).
  • Compounded or simple: compounding increases the required payoff if delayed.

3) Catch-up

A catch-up provision routes distributions to the GP after the pref so the GP can “catch up” to an agreed share of profits for that tier (often designed to reach a target split).

4) Promote / carried interest

The promote (also called carried interest) is the GP’s performance-based share of profits after certain hurdles are met. Promote increases the GP’s share in higher tiers.

5) Tiered splits (hurdles)

Many waterfalls use escalating tiers where splits become more favorable to the GP as returns exceed thresholds, often based on IRR or equity multiple.

Typical Tier Example (Illustrative)

  • Tier 1: 100% to LP until return of capital
  • Tier 2: 100% to LP until an 8% preferred return (accruing)
  • Tier 3: Catch-up to GP until profits split 80/20 (LP/GP)
  • Tier 4: Thereafter 70/30 (LP/GP) above a higher hurdle

Note: Real deals vary widely. Always read the operating agreement / PPM and model the waterfall directly.

Why Waterfalls Matter

  • Align incentives: promote can motivate the sponsor to increase NOI, manage risk, and execute.
  • Define risk/return: LP downside protection typically comes from capital return + pref priority.
  • Impact investor outcomes: two deals with the same property returns can produce very different LP returns.
  • Drive decision-making: hurdles can influence hold vs. sell timing and refinance choices.

Key Investor Questions to Ask

  • Is the pref accruing and does it compound?
  • Is there a catch-up? If so, how aggressive is it?
  • What is the promote at each tier?
  • Are hurdles based on IRR, equity multiple, or both?
  • Is there a GP clawback? How is it calculated and enforced?
  • How are fees handled? (acquisition, asset management, property management, disposition, etc.)

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing pref with guarantee: a preferred return is a priority, not a promise.
  • Ignoring fee drag: high fees can reduce distributable cash even with a “good” waterfall.
  • Not modeling the math: small differences in catch-up and hurdles can materially change outcomes.
  • Timing distortion: IRR-based hurdles are sensitive to when cash is distributed and when the asset sells.
  • Refi distributions: refinancing can create early cash-outs that affect IRR and tier achievement.

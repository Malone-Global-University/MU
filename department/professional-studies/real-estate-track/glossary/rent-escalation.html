Rent Escalation

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Rent Escalation

Definition

Rent escalation refers to scheduled increases in rent during the term of a lease. Escalations are designed to account for inflation, rising operating costs, and long-term value preservation.

Common Types of Rent Escalation

1) Fixed Step-Up Increases

  • Predefined rent increases at set intervals.
  • Example: $30/SF in Year 1 increasing by $1/SF annually.
  • Provides predictability for both parties.

2) Percentage Increases

  • Annual increase by a fixed percentage (e.g., 3% per year).
  • Common in office and retail leases.

3) CPI-Based Adjustments

  • Rent increases tied to the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
  • May include caps and floors to limit volatility.

4) Operating Expense Pass-Through

  • Additional rent increases based on rising property expenses.
  • Common in triple-net (NNN) leases.

5) Percentage Rent (Retail)

  • Rent increases tied to tenant gross sales exceeding a breakpoint.
  • Aligns landlord and tenant performance incentives.

Example

A tenant signs a 5-year lease at $25/SF with 3% annual increases.

  • Year 1: $25.00
  • Year 2: $25.75
  • Year 3: $26.52
  • Year 4: $27.32
  • Year 5: $28.14

Impact on Financial Performance

  • Supports long-term NOI growth.
  • Improves property valuation through rising cash flows.
  • Enhances debt service coverage over time.
  • Mitigates inflation risk.

Investor Perspective

  • Steady escalations increase exit value.
  • Long-term leases with built-in growth attract lenders.
  • CPI-linked escalations hedge inflation exposure.

Tenant Perspective

  • Predictable increases assist budgeting.
  • High escalation rates may impact occupancy costs.
  • CPI volatility can increase financial uncertainty.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Verify escalation schedule in lease abstract.
  • Model compounded vs. non-compounded increases.
  • Assess cap/floor provisions in CPI clauses.
  • Evaluate market rent growth assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Misinterpreting compounding mechanics.
  • Ignoring caps on CPI increases.
  • Overestimating long-term market growth.
  • Failing to account for tenant default risk at higher rents.

Rent Escalation vs. Market Rent Growth

  • Rent Escalation: Contractually fixed increases in existing leases.
  • Market Rent Growth: Changes in prevailing market rents for new leases.

Market Cap Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Market Cap Rate

Definition

The Market Cap Rate (Market Capitalization Rate) is the prevailing cap rate in a specific market for a given property type and risk profile.

It reflects what investors in that market are currently willing to accept as an unlevered return on income-producing real estate.

Formula

  • Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Property Value

Rearranged for valuation:

  • Value = NOI ÷ Market Cap Rate

What Influences Market Cap Rates?

  • Interest rate environment
  • Capital availability
  • Investor risk appetite
  • Asset class (multifamily, office, industrial, etc.)
  • Market growth expectations
  • Local supply and demand dynamics

Low vs. High Cap Rates

Lower Cap Rates (e.g., 4–5%)

  • Indicate strong demand
  • Often associated with core, stable markets
  • Higher valuations relative to income

Higher Cap Rates (e.g., 7–9%)

  • Indicate higher perceived risk
  • May reflect weaker markets or transitional assets
  • Lower valuations relative to income

Example

If a stabilized property generates $1,000,000 in NOI and similar properties trade at a 6% market cap rate:

  • Value = $1,000,000 ÷ 0.06 = $16,666,667

Market Cap Rate vs. Going-In Cap Rate

  • Market Cap Rate: Reflects overall market pricing benchmarks.
  • Going-In Cap Rate: The cap rate based on the buyer’s purchase price and current NOI.

A buyer may accept a lower going-in cap rate if they believe Stabilized NOI will grow.

Market Cap Rate and Risk

Cap rates are a pricing mechanism for risk:

  • Lower perceived risk → Lower cap rate → Higher price
  • Higher perceived risk → Higher cap rate → Lower price

Investor Discipline

Strong investors compare:

  • The Market Cap Rate
  • Their Required Return
  • The Underwritten NOI

If market pricing does not meet required return standards, disciplined investors wait rather than overpay.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using outdated cap rate data
  • Ignoring submarket differences
  • Confusing trailing cap rate with forward cap rate
  • Failing to adjust for asset quality or risk profile

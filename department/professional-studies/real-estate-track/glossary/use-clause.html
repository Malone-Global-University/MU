Use Clause

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Use Clause

Definition

A use clause is a lease provision that defines and limits the specific activities a tenant may conduct within the leased premises. It protects the landlord’s property strategy, tenant mix, and compliance with zoning or regulatory requirements.

Why Use Clauses Matter

  • Tenant mix strategy: Keeps uses complementary and protects merchandising plans.
  • Risk management: Limits nuisance, hazardous, or high-liability operations.
  • Zoning and permits: Ensures the tenant’s operations remain permitted by law.
  • Financing: Lenders may prefer certain uses (or disallow others) for risk reasons.

Types of Use Clauses

1) Broad Use Clause

Allows a wide range of lawful uses, such as: “Any lawful retail use.” Broad clauses provide flexibility to tenants, but reduce landlord control.

2) Narrow (Restricted) Use Clause

Limits use to a specific concept or product category (e.g., “sale of athletic apparel only”). Narrow clauses help protect tenant mix and reduce conflicts.

3) Exclusive Use Interaction

In retail centers, the use clause must be coordinated with exclusive clauses held by other tenants. A “permitted use” for one tenant can accidentally breach another tenant’s exclusivity.

Common Drafting Elements

  • Permitted use: A defined list or description of allowed activities.
  • Prohibited uses: Explicitly disallowed categories (e.g., vape shop, adult entertainment, hazardous materials).
  • Incidental sales carve-outs: Allows minor overlap (e.g., a sandwich shop selling bottled drinks).
  • Landlord consent standard: “Not unreasonably withheld” vs. “sole discretion.”
  • Compliance: Tenant must comply with laws, zoning, and permits.

Example

A lease states that the premises may be used “solely for a sit-down restaurant serving Italian cuisine, and for no other purpose without landlord’s prior written consent.” If the tenant later wants to pivot to a nightclub concept, that change likely violates the use clause without an amendment.

Operational and Financial Impact

  • Leasing velocity: Overly restrictive use can slow backfilling vacant space.
  • Credit and stability: Certain uses may correlate with higher default risk.
  • Insurance and liability: Uses may drive premium costs and claim frequency.
  • Value: Stable, compatible uses can support long-term NOI and exit value.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Cross-check permitted uses against any exclusives in the rent roll.
  • Confirm the tenant’s current operations match the permitted use language.
  • Evaluate whether a restricted use could impair re-leasing (especially for single-tenant assets).
  • Review how use changes interact with assignment/sublease rights.

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague drafting: “Retail use” may be too broad for certain centers.
  • Overly narrow drafting: Prevents concept changes needed to survive.
  • Exclusive conflicts: Accidental breaches create rent remedies or termination rights.
  • Regulatory mismatch: Permitted use not allowed by zoning or licensing.

Schedule K-1

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Schedule K-1

Definition

Schedule K-1 is a tax form issued by partnerships, LLCs taxed as partnerships, S corporations, and certain trusts that reports each investor’s share of income, losses, deductions, credits, and other tax items.

In real estate syndications and private partnerships, the K-1 is how taxable results are passed through to individual investors.

Why It Matters in Real Estate Investing

  • Determines how much income (or loss) you report on your personal tax return
  • May show tax losses even when you received positive cash distributions
  • Impacts passive activity loss calculations
  • Tracks your ownership percentage and capital account

Key Items Reported on a K-1

  • Ordinary business income (loss)
  • Rental real estate income (loss)
  • Interest income
  • Capital gains
  • Depreciation deductions
  • Section 179 deductions (if applicable)
  • Credits
  • Distributions received
  • Ending capital account balance

K-1 vs. Cash Distributions

One of the most common misunderstandings is assuming that the K-1 equals cash received.

In real estate:

  • You may receive cash distributions but show little or no taxable income (due to depreciation).
  • You may show taxable income even if no cash was distributed.

The K-1 reports taxable allocation, not cash flow.

How K-1s Are Used in Syndications

In a real estate syndication:

  • The partnership files Form 1065.
  • Each investor receives a K-1 reflecting their ownership share.
  • Income and losses pass through to investors based on the operating agreement.

Timing Considerations

  • K-1s are often issued later than W-2s or 1099s.
  • Many partnerships file extensions.
  • Investors may need to extend personal tax returns while waiting.

Common Tax Concepts Related to K-1s

  • Passive activity loss rules
  • Tax basis limitations
  • At-risk rules
  • Depreciation and cost segregation
  • Depreciation recapture at sale

Example

An investor contributes $100,000 to a multifamily syndication.

  • Cash distributions received: $8,000
  • K-1 rental loss reported: ($15,000) due to depreciation

The investor may:

  • Pay no tax on the $8,000 distribution
  • Possibly suspend the $15,000 loss under passive activity rules

Capital Account vs. Tax Basis

The K-1 reports a capital account balance, but this is not always the same as tax basis. Basis determines how much loss you can deduct and whether distributions are taxable.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm receipt of K-1 before filing taxes
  • Track suspended passive losses year to year
  • Understand how distributions affect your capital account and basis
  • Review state filing requirements (multi-state investments may require multiple filings)
  • Coordinate with a qualified CPA

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming K-1 income equals cash received
  • Ignoring suspended losses
  • Failing to file required state returns
  • Not understanding sale-year recapture impacts

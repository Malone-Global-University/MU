Certificate of Occupancy (CO)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Certificate of Occupancy

Definition

A Certificate of Occupancy (often called a CO) is an official document issued by a local building department (or authority having jurisdiction) confirming that a property meets applicable building codes and is approved to be occupied for a specific use (e.g., residential, office, retail, warehouse, assembly) and, in many cases, a specific occupancy classification and occupant load.

In simple terms: a CO is the government’s sign-off that the building is safe and legal to use as intended.

Why It Matters in Real Estate Investing

The CO is often a gating item for leasing, revenue commencement, loan funding, and closings. Without the correct CO (or a valid temporary CO), tenants may not be allowed to move in, permits may not be closed, and lenders or buyers may delay funding.

  • Leasing: Many leases require a CO before tenant occupancy and rent commencement.
  • Construction closeout: CO is a key milestone to close permits and complete punch-list items.
  • Financing: Lenders may require a CO as a condition to convert from construction loan to permanent financing.
  • Dispositions: Buyers often require proof of the current CO and permitted use during due diligence.

When a CO Is Required

A CO is commonly required for:

  • New construction before first occupancy.
  • Major renovations that trigger permit scope, life-safety, or structural changes.
  • Change of use (e.g., converting warehouse to gym, office to medical, retail to restaurant).
  • Change of occupancy classification under building code (impacts fire/life safety requirements).
  • Tenant improvements that require permits and inspections (common in commercial).

Temporary CO vs. Final CO

Many jurisdictions issue a Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO) when a building is safe to occupy but some non-critical items remain incomplete (e.g., minor exterior work, final landscaping, signage, or punch-list items).

  • TCO: Allows limited occupancy for a defined period and may include conditions or restrictions.
  • Final CO: Confirms full compliance and closes out all required inspections and permit obligations.

Investors and operators should track TCO expiration dates and conditions closely—an expired TCO can create operational and legal risk.

What a CO Typically Confirms

  • Compliance with building code requirements (structural, electrical, mechanical, plumbing)
  • Life safety completion (fire alarm, sprinklers, egress, emergency lighting)
  • Approved use and occupancy classification
  • Address/parcel linkage and permitted scope completion
  • In some cases, maximum occupant load and required postings

CO and Leases

In commercial leasing, CO-related provisions often determine when rent starts and who bears the risk of delays. Typical lease concepts include:

  • Delivery condition: landlord delivers premises when CO/TCO is issued.
  • Rent commencement: starts upon CO issuance, tenant opening, or a fixed date—whichever occurs first.
  • Outside delivery date: termination right if CO is not obtained by a deadline.
  • Permitting responsibility: landlord vs. tenant for TI permits and inspections.

CO and Financing

Construction lenders often require a CO (or TCO) to:

  • release final loan advances (retainage)
  • trigger conversion to a permanent loan or stabilization tests
  • confirm compliance with plans/specs and life-safety completion

Due Diligence: What to Request

  • Copy of the current CO (and any amendments)
  • Copies of TCOs, including all conditions and expiration dates
  • Open permit report and inspection sign-offs
  • Evidence of use compliance (zoning letters, permitted use descriptions)
  • For multi-tenant assets: COs for common areas and individual suites (if issued separately)

Example

An investor buys a value-add retail center and plans to lease a vacant unit to a restaurant. The space previously had a retail CO, but the restaurant use requires a different occupancy classification and additional life-safety upgrades. The tenant cannot open until the jurisdiction issues a new CO (or TCO), which affects rent commencement, tenant opening date, and the project timeline.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “existing use” is permitted: the CO may not match how the space is currently operated.
  • Expired TCO: occupancy continues after the temporary certificate expires.
  • Unclosed permits: open permits can delay sale/refi and trigger re-inspection requirements.
  • Change of use surprises: new tenants can trigger costly upgrades (ADA, sprinklers, grease interceptors, venting).
  • Partial CO complexity: phased projects may have COs by floor, wing, or suite—track what is actually approved.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm the CO matches actual current use and intended business plan.
  • Verify whether a TCO is in place and track conditions/expiration.
  • Request an open permit and inspection status report during due diligence.
  • Coordinate CO requirements with lease clauses (delivery, rent commencement, outside dates).
  • Align CO timing with loan milestones and construction closeout requirements.

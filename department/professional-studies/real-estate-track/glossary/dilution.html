Dilution

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Dilution

Definition

Dilution is the reduction of an investor’s ownership percentage or economic interest in a property or partnership. It typically occurs when new equity is issued or when an investor fails to participate in a capital call.

How Dilution Happens

  • Additional equity issuance: new investors are brought in.
  • Capital call default: existing investor does not fund required contribution.
  • Restructuring: recapitalization changes ownership structure.
  • Preferred equity insertion: new senior equity layers alter economics.

Why It Matters

  • Reduced upside: smaller share of future profits and appreciation.
  • Lower voting power: decreased governance influence.
  • Changed waterfall economics: returns may shift among partners.

Example

An investor owns 20% of a real estate partnership. The partnership issues additional equity to fund unexpected repairs. The investor does not participate. After the new capital is contributed, their ownership decreases to 15%. Their share of cash flow and sale proceeds will now reflect the lower percentage.

Dilution vs. Value Creation

Dilution is not always negative. If additional capital significantly increases property value, a smaller percentage of a larger asset may still produce attractive returns. Context and performance matter.

Investor Considerations

  • Review capital call and dilution provisions in the operating agreement.
  • Understand penalty structures for non-participation.
  • Evaluate liquidity before committing to long-term investments.
  • Assess sponsor communication and track record.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring dilution clauses before investing.
  • Failing to reserve capital for future obligations.
  • Overlooking how preferred equity affects common equity holders.
  • Misunderstanding waterfall mechanics after recapitalization.

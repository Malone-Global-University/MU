RUBS (Ratio Utility Billing System)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary RUBS (Ratio Utility Billing System)

Definition

RUBS stands for Ratio Utility Billing System. It is a method of allocating a property’s utility costs (such as water, sewer, gas, and sometimes trash) to residents using a formula, rather than measuring each unit’s usage with individual submeters.

RUBS is most common in multifamily properties where utilities are master-metered and retrofitting submeters is costly, impractical, or not allowed.

How RUBS Works

Under a RUBS program, a third-party billing company or property manager typically:

  • Collects the property’s master utility bills for the billing period.
  • Applies an allocation formula to distribute costs across occupied units.
  • Issues resident utility charges (often shown as “utility reimbursement” or “bill-back”).

Common Allocation Methods

Formulas vary by jurisdiction, lease language, and program design. Common approaches include:

  • Per occupant: costs allocated by number of residents in each unit.
  • Per unit: equal share per occupied unit (simple, but not always fair).
  • By square footage: larger units pay more based on size.
  • Weighted blends: a combination (e.g., 50% occupants + 50% square footage).
  • Hybrid with common-area factor: a portion set aside for common areas, then the remainder allocated to units.

Why It Matters in Underwriting

  • Increases other income: RUBS can shift utility expense back to residents (partially or fully).
  • Improves NOI: if modeled correctly, reduced net utility expense increases NOI.
  • Creates behavioral incentives: residents may conserve if they bear some utility costs.
  • Competitive positioning: “utilities included” may lease faster in some markets; RUBS may require pricing strategy adjustments.

What to Verify During Diligence

  • Lease language: does the lease clearly allow utility billing or reimbursement?
  • Local rules: some jurisdictions cap bill-backs or require specific disclosures.
  • Historical recovery rate: how much of the utility bill is actually being recovered?
  • Bad debt / collections: are RUBS charges being collected consistently?
  • Vendor contract terms: billing fees, setup costs, and implementation timeline.
  • Resident communication: sudden RUBS rollout can create pushback if not handled carefully.

Example

A property receives a monthly water/sewer bill of $10,000. The RUBS program allocates 10% to common areas, and the remaining $9,000 is billed to residents based on occupants. If collections average 95%, expected monthly recovery is:

  • Recoverable amount = $9,000 × 95% = $8,550/month

Underwriting should reflect the net utility expense after realistic recovery and billing fees, not the gross bill-back number.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming 100% recovery: vacancy, delinquencies, caps, and unbillable portions reduce recoveries.
  • Ignoring billing fees: third-party RUBS vendors charge per unit and/or per bill fees.
  • Compliance risk: improper implementation can trigger resident disputes or regulatory issues.
  • Market resistance: shifting utilities to residents may require rent strategy adjustments.
  • Bad asset fit: if plumbing leaks or inefficiencies exist, RUBS can increase complaints and move-outs.

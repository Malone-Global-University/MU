Carried Interest

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Carried Interest

Definition

Carried interest (often called the promote) is a performance-based share of profits paid to the sponsor/manager of an investment—typically after investors receive their return of capital and any agreed preferred return. In real estate syndications and private equity, carried interest is distributed through a waterfall that defines who gets paid, when, and in what proportions.

How It Works

Most deals begin with a baseline LP/GP split (limited partners/investors vs. sponsor/manager), such as 90/10 or 80/20. “Carry” is the extra profit share that the sponsor earns for hitting performance thresholds. The better the outcome, the more the split may shift toward the sponsor.

Typical waterfall order

  1. Return of capital: LPs receive their original investment back (sometimes pro-rata).
  2. Preferred return: LPs receive a target return (e.g., 8% pref) before promote begins.
  3. Catch-up (optional): distributions shift to the sponsor until an agreed split is reached.
  4. Profit split / tiers: remaining cash flows are split by the promote schedule (e.g., 80/20, then 70/30 above a higher hurdle).

Common Structures

Carried interest can be single-tier (one promote split) or multi-tier (promote increases as performance improves). Hurdles are usually based on IRR, equity multiple, or both.

Examples (illustrative)

  • Single-tier: 8% pref, then 80/20 split (LP/GP) thereafter.
  • Two-tier: 8% pref, then 80/20 until 16% IRR, then 70/30 above 16% IRR.
  • Three-tier: 8% pref, 80/20 to 14% IRR, 70/30 to 18% IRR, 60/40 above 18% IRR.

Why It Matters

Carried interest is meant to align incentives: the sponsor earns more only if investors do well. But structure matters. A promote that is too rich, triggers too early, or relies on unrealistic assumptions can misalign incentives and inflate underwriting.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting alignment: confirm the sponsor’s upside is tied to durable value creation (NOI growth, smart capex, risk control).
  • Comparing deals: evaluate different waterfalls on an apples-to-apples basis by modeling net LP returns.
  • Negotiating terms: adjust pref, catch-up, and tiers to balance risk and reward.
  • Governance focus: ensure reporting, controls, and decision rights match the incentive structure.

Example

Investors contribute $10,000,000 (LP equity). The deal returns $16,000,000 total over the hold period. Suppose the waterfall is: return of capital to LPs, then 8% pref, then remaining profits split 80/20.

  • First: LPs receive $10,000,000 back (capital return).
  • Next: LPs receive their preferred return (amount depends on timing/hold period).
  • Then: Remaining profit is split 80% to LPs and 20% to sponsor as carried interest.

The exact carry dollars depend on timing (cash flow schedule), the pref definition (simple vs. compounding), and whether there is a catch-up.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing fees vs. carry: asset management, acquisition, and disposition fees are separate from carried interest.
  • Early promote triggers: structures that start carry before capital is returned can increase investor risk.
  • Catch-up surprises: “100% catch-up” can funnel large early distributions to the sponsor once pref is met.
  • Metric gaming: IRR hurdles can be influenced by timing; equity multiple focuses on magnitude but ignores time.
  • Not modeling the waterfall: headline splits (e.g., “80/20”) can hide meaningful tier and catch-up mechanics.

Triple-Net Lease (NNN)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Triple-Net Lease (NNN)

Definition

A triple-net lease (NNN) is a commercial lease where the tenant pays base rent plus most or all of the property’s operating expenses—typically: real estate taxes, property insurance, and maintenance (the “three nets”). The goal is to shift expense variability from the landlord to the tenant, creating more predictable landlord cash flow.

The “Three Nets”

  • N = Real estate taxes (property tax bills and related assessments, as allowed)
  • N = Property insurance (building insurance and related coverage)
  • N = Common area maintenance (CAM) / maintenance (repairs, landscaping, snow removal, utilities for common areas, etc.)

How Rent Is Quoted

In NNN leasing, you’ll often see rent quoted in two layers:

  • Base rent: the “rent” portion (often quoted $/SF/year).
  • NNN expenses: additional charges (often estimated $/SF/year) that reimburse operating costs.

The tenant’s all-in payment is often described as base rent + NNN. In some markets, brokers may quote an “all-in” number, but it’s critical to verify what’s included.

NNN vs. Other Lease Types

  • Gross lease: tenant pays one rent number; landlord covers most expenses.
  • Modified gross: landlord and tenant split expenses in negotiated ways.
  • Double-net (NN): tenant pays taxes and insurance; landlord typically covers maintenance (varies).
  • Triple-net (NNN): tenant pays taxes, insurance, and maintenance/CAM (most common “net” structure).

Why NNN Matters to Investors

  • More predictable NOI: fewer variable operating expenses hitting the landlord’s P&L.
  • Inflation hedge: expenses that rise with inflation can be reimbursed by the tenant.
  • Simplified operations: especially for single-tenant properties with strong tenants.
  • Credit focus: value often depends heavily on tenant credit, lease term, and location.

What Risks Still Remain

“NNN” doesn’t mean the landlord has no risk. Key landlord risks include:

  • Vacancy / re-leasing risk: if the tenant leaves, the landlord bears downtime and re-tenant costs.
  • Capital expenditures: roofs, structure, parking lots, and major systems may remain landlord responsibilities depending on the lease.
  • Credit risk: tenant financial health can dominate property performance (especially in single-tenant deals).
  • Expense definitions: leases vary—what qualifies as “maintenance” or reimbursable CAM can be disputed.
  • Audit/true-up risk: operating expense reconciliations can create friction if estimates are wrong.

Example

A retail tenant leases 5,000 SF under a NNN lease:

  • Base rent: $28.00/SF/year
  • NNN estimate: $7.50/SF/year

The tenant’s estimated all-in annual payment is:

  • All-in = (28.00 + 7.50) × 5,000 = $177,500/year (plus any specified utilities/fees)

If taxes or insurance increase, the NNN portion typically increases (subject to the lease terms and any caps).

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “absolute NNN”: some leases still leave certain repairs or capex with the landlord.
  • Ignoring capex: reimbursements cover operating expenses, not always large replacements (roof/structure).
  • Misreading CAM definitions: what’s included/excluded can materially change economics.
  • Tenant concentration: single-tenant NNN is often a credit play—lease rollover can be binary.
  • Not modeling downtime: when a tenant vacates, a “low-management” asset can become capital-intensive fast.

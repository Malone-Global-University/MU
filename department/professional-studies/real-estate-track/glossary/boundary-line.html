Boundary Line

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Boundary Line

Definition

A boundary line is the legal line that defines the edges of a parcel of land. It separates one owner’s property from adjoining parcels and can also define limits against a public right-of-way (streets, alleys) or a natural feature (shorelines, rivers).

Where Boundary Lines Come From

Boundary lines are established by recorded legal descriptions and supporting documents, then verified in the field by a licensed land surveyor. Common sources include deeds, recorded plats, subdivision maps, easement documents, and historical survey monuments.

Common legal description types

  • Lot & block: references a recorded subdivision plat (common in residential and infill).
  • Metes & bounds: describes bearings/distances from a point of beginning (common in older or irregular parcels).
  • Rectangular survey: uses sections/townships/ranges (common in many western states).

Why It Matters

Boundary uncertainty creates real financial risk. Misunderstood lines can lead to encroachments, access problems, unbuildable areas, disputes with neighbors, and limitations on expansion or redevelopment. For investors, boundary clarity protects title, value, and exit certainty.

Investor Uses

  • Due diligence: confirm what you’re actually buying (size, shape, frontage, setbacks).
  • Development feasibility: verify buildable area, lot coverage, and improvement locations.
  • Risk management: identify encroachments, adverse possession exposure, or boundary conflicts.
  • Financing and title: support lender/title requirements and reduce exceptions.

How It’s Verified

The practical way to confirm boundary lines is a survey. For most acquisitions, buyers review a boundary survey (often combined with improvements and easements) alongside the title commitment.

What a survey typically helps reveal

  • Monuments and measured boundary lines
  • Building locations relative to property lines
  • Fences, driveways, sheds, and other potential encroachments
  • Easements and rights-of-way affecting use
  • Access points and potential gaps in legal access

Example

An investor buys a small multifamily property and plans to add covered parking. A survey shows the existing rear fence is 18 inches over the neighbor’s line and the planned parking would sit partly on the neighbor’s parcel. The buyer either renegotiates the deal, obtains a boundary agreement, adjusts the plan, or walks away before closing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming fences are the boundary: fences are often installed for convenience, not accuracy.
  • Skipping the survey: title alone won’t show encroachments or where buildings sit in the field.
  • Ignoring easements: utilities or access easements can reduce usable area even if you “own” it.
  • Overlooking ROW impacts: the edge of pavement is not the property line; rights-of-way can be wider than they look.
  • Not resolving title exceptions: survey and title must reconcile to avoid surprises post-close.

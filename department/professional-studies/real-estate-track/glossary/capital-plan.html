Capital Plan

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Capital Plan

Definition

A capital plan is a time-phased schedule and budget for a property’s capital expenditures (CapEx)—major projects and replacements that preserve the asset, improve performance, and support the business plan. A good capital plan ties each project to: scope, timing, cost, funding source, and the expected impact on operations, leasing, and NOI.

What a Capital Plan Usually Includes

  • Life-safety and compliance: fire/life-safety systems, handrails, lighting, code-required upgrades.
  • Major replacements: roofs, HVAC systems, boilers, water heaters (bulk), windows/doors (bulk), elevators.
  • Exterior and site: siding, paint, parking lots, drainage, landscaping hardscape, fencing.
  • Interior programs: unit renovations, flooring replacement cycles, appliance packages, fixture upgrades.
  • Amenity/branding upgrades: clubhouse refresh, signage, package lockers, fitness center improvements.
  • Efficiency projects: lighting retrofits, water conservation, HVAC controls—often tied to utility savings.

Capital Plan vs. Operating Expenses

Capital plans focus on CapEx (big projects and replacements). Day-to-day fixes generally fall under repairs & maintenance (R&M) in operating expenses.

  • R&M: restore function (service calls, minor repairs, small parts).
  • CapEx: replace or materially extend life / improve the asset (roof replacement, HVAC replacement, full unit renos).

Why It Matters

A capital plan is where underwriting becomes real. It reduces surprise costs, supports tenant satisfaction, prevents deferred maintenance, and makes cash flow more durable. In value-add deals, the capital plan is also the blueprint for rent growth—if the scope, schedule, and turn assumptions are wrong, the pro forma breaks.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting: model CapEx timing, lease-up/renovation velocity, and downtime impacts.
  • Risk management: identify big-ticket items and plan funding before they become emergencies.
  • Value creation: link improvements to rent premiums, renewal lift, and reduced vacancy.
  • Lender and partner alignment: communicate scope, draws, and contingency strategy.
  • Execution tracking: compare actual spend and results to the plan and adjust quickly.

Core Components of a Strong Capital Plan

  • Inventory & condition: what exists today, remaining useful life, known issues.
  • Scope of work: line-item detail, unit-level standards, specs, and finish packages.
  • Phasing and schedule: what happens in Year 0/1/2+, sequencing constraints, seasonality.
  • Budget and bids: unit costs, contractor bids, material allowances, escalation assumptions.
  • Contingency: realistic reserve for surprises (especially on older assets).
  • Funding plan: equity, loan proceeds, reserves, operating cash flow, or supplemental debt.
  • Expected outcomes: rent premiums, utility savings, reduction in R&M, leasing/renewal impact.

How It’s Measured

Capital plans are often summarized using comparable metrics:

  • Total CapEx budget (dollars)
  • CapEx per unit (total CapEx ÷ units)
  • CapEx per square foot (total CapEx ÷ rentable SF)
  • Annual reserves (ongoing replacement reserves after major projects)

Example

An investor buys a 200-unit asset with a value-add plan. The capital plan totals $2,400,000:

  • Unit renovations: 120 units × $10,000 = $1,200,000
  • Exterior paint + repairs: $450,000
  • Roof replacements (phased): $500,000
  • Amenity refresh + signage: $150,000
  • Contingency: $100,000

Summary metrics:

  • CapEx per unit = $2,400,000 ÷ 200 = $12,000 per unit
  • Year 1 focus: complete exterior work + renovate 60 units (drives first wave of rent premiums)
  • Year 2 focus: renovate remaining 60 units + roof phase two

Common Pitfalls

  • Optimistic timelines: turns take longer, contractors slip, and permitting slows projects.
  • No contingency: older properties nearly always have surprises behind walls and under slabs.
  • Renovating without operational readiness: poor scheduling creates vacancy loss and resident disruption.
  • Missing “boring” CapEx: roofs, drainage, and electrical panels can matter more than cosmetic upgrades.
  • Assuming rent premiums without proof: comps and leasing evidence must support the premium.
  • Not coordinating with reserves: a one-time CapEx plan should transition into ongoing replacement reserves.

Best Practices

  • Start with a condition assessment: combine third-party reports with onsite verification.
  • Standardize scope: repeatable unit packages reduce cost overruns and speed up turns.
  • Phase intelligently: sequence projects to minimize disruption and protect occupancy.
  • Track weekly: units turned, spend vs budget, change orders, and realized rent premiums.
  • Separate initial CapEx from ongoing reserves: plan both explicitly.

Certificate of Completion

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Certificate of Completion

Definition

A Certificate of Completion is a document indicating that construction work has been completed in accordance with the contract documents and applicable requirements. It is often used to support project closeout, final payment, and certain lender conditions (such as a final construction draw or release of reserves).

In simple terms: it’s written confirmation that the project is finished.

Who Issues a Certificate of Completion?

Depending on jurisdiction and contract structure, a certificate of completion may be issued by:

  • The architect or engineer of record (common under AIA-style contracts)
  • The owner’s representative or construction manager
  • A local building department (in some jurisdictions)
  • A third-party inspector (for lender or insurance requirements)

Certificate of Completion vs. Substantial Completion

A certificate of completion is often confused with substantial completion. They are related but not the same:

  • Substantial Completion: The project can be used for its intended purpose, even if minor punch-list work remains.
  • Completion: Work is fully complete, including punch-list, closeout documents, and final inspections.

Certificate of Completion vs. Certificate of Occupancy

A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) is a government-issued authorization to occupy and use a building. A certificate of completion is typically a construction/contract milestone.

  • Certificate of Occupancy: “You may legally occupy the building.”
  • Certificate of Completion: “The work is finished per the contract.”

Why It Matters in Real Estate

  • Final loan draw: Construction lenders often require completion documentation to fund the last draw.
  • Release of retainage: Owners may release retainage after completion.
  • Start of warranties: Many warranties begin at completion or final acceptance.
  • Mechanic’s lien exposure: Completion triggers lien filing deadlines in many jurisdictions.
  • Transition to operations: Signals move from construction to stabilized operations.

Typical Items Required for Closeout

  • Completed punch list
  • Final inspections and sign-offs
  • As-built drawings
  • Operation & maintenance (O&M) manuals
  • Warranties and manufacturer certifications
  • Final lien waivers and releases
  • Commissioning reports (for major MEP systems)

Example

A developer completes a 120-unit multifamily project. The building receives a certificate of occupancy, allowing lease-up to begin, but there is still a punch list and closeout documentation outstanding. The lender holds back the final draw and retainage until the architect issues a certificate of completion confirming the remaining items are finished.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming occupancy approval means the project is “complete.”
  • Failing to collect final lien waivers before releasing retainage.
  • Missing closeout documents (as-builts, warranties, O&M manuals).
  • Disputes over whether work is complete per contract scope.
  • Delays in final inspections causing extended interest carry.

Investor & Lender Checklist

  • Confirm definition of “completion” in the construction contract and loan agreement.
  • Verify all punch-list items are closed.
  • Collect unconditional final lien waivers.
  • Confirm warranty start dates and responsibilities.
  • Ensure CO (or TCO) is issued if occupancy is required.

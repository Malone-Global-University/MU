Stress Scenario

Definition

A stress scenario is a modeled set of adverse assumptions used to test how a real estate investment performs under unfavorable economic or market conditions. It helps investors assess downside risk and capital resilience.

Purpose in Real Estate Underwriting

  • Risk management: Identify vulnerabilities before investing.
  • Debt sizing: Ensure loans remain serviceable under stress.
  • Equity protection: Measure potential value erosion.
  • Portfolio planning: Evaluate aggregate exposure.

Common Stress Assumptions

  • Rent decline: 5–15% reduction in market rents.
  • Vacancy increase: Occupancy drops below stabilized levels.
  • Cap rate expansion: 50–150 basis point increase at exit.
  • Interest rate shock: Higher refinancing costs.
  • Expense growth: Inflation-driven cost increases.

Example

Base Case:

  • NOI: $1,000,000
  • Exit Cap Rate: 5.5%
  • Value: $18,182,000

Stress Scenario:

  • NOI declines 10% to $900,000
  • Exit Cap Rate increases to 6.5%
  • Value: $13,846,000

The combined operational and market stress significantly reduces projected value.

Types of Stress Scenarios

  • Recession scenario: Declining employment and demand.
  • Interest rate shock: Rapid rise in benchmark rates.
  • Credit tightening: Wider spreads and limited refinancing options.
  • Tenant default: Major tenant loss.

Stress Scenario vs. Sensitivity Analysis

  • Stress Scenario: Multiple adverse variables change simultaneously.
  • Sensitivity Analysis: One variable changes at a time.

Investor Applications

  • Loan covenant testing: Evaluate DSCR under stress.
  • Capital planning: Determine reserve requirements.
  • Investment committee review: Assess worst-case outcomes.
  • Portfolio resilience testing: Evaluate correlated shocks.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overly optimistic stress: Not severe enough to be meaningful.
  • Ignoring liquidity impact: Market freezes can worsen stress.
  • Static assumptions: Failing to model multi-year effects.

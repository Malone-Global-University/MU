Utilities

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Utilities

Definition

Utilities are the essential services required to operate a property—such as water/sewer, electricity, natural gas, and trash. In underwriting, “utilities” refers to the expense line item (or sometimes a recovered pass-through) that can materially impact NOI.

What Utilities Commonly Include

  • Water & sewer: domestic water, sewer charges, stormwater fees (market dependent).
  • Electric: common-area power (hallways, exterior lighting, clubhouse) and/or unit power if master-metered.
  • Gas: heating, hot water, cooking, and sometimes laundry.
  • Trash: hauling, dumpsters/compact, recycling, valet trash (if offered).
  • Other (sometimes): irrigation, propane, oil heat, geothermal service fees.

Who Pays Utilities?

Utility responsibility depends on property type and metering:

  • Tenant-paid (direct metering): tenants have individual meters and pay the provider directly.
  • Owner-paid (master-metered): the owner pays one bill and may or may not recover it from tenants.
  • Shared/recovered: owner pays, then bills tenants via a recovery method (common in multifamily and commercial).

Utility Recovery Methods

Multifamily (common structures)

  • RUBS: Ratio Utility Billing System (allocates costs based on unit size, occupants, or other formula).
  • Submetering: measures usage per unit, allowing more precise billing.
  • Flat fees: fixed monthly utility charges (simple, but may misprice usage).
  • Bill-backs: separate line items such as “water/sewer bill-back” or “trash bill-back.”

Commercial (typical structures)

  • NNN / expense pass-throughs: tenants reimburse operating expenses (including utilities) per the lease.
  • CAM: common-area utilities often recovered through CAM charges for multi-tenant properties.

Why Utilities Matter

  • NOI sensitivity: utilities can be a large, inflation-sensitive operating expense.
  • Behavioral impact: when tenants pay their own usage, consumption often declines.
  • Operational leverage: conservation and metering upgrades can drive durable NOI growth.
  • Risk management: leaks, aging infrastructure, and billing errors can create surprise expenses.

How Investors Underwrite Utilities

  • Use trailing history: review 12–24 months of bills for seasonality and anomalies.
  • Normalize for occupancy: utilities scale with occupied units; vacancy periods can distort averages.
  • Check billing structure: confirm what is owner-paid vs. tenant-paid vs. recovered.
  • Model increases: utilities often rise faster than general inflation in some markets.
  • Plan improvements: submeters, low-flow fixtures, leak detection, and irrigation controls.

Example

A 100-unit property is master-metered for water/sewer. The owner pays $120,000/year for water and sewer but bills back $80,000/year via RUBS. In the model:

  • Utilities expense (water/sewer): $120,000
  • Other income (water/sewer bill-back): $80,000
  • Net utility burden to NOI: $40,000

If the owner converts to submetering and improves recovery to $105,000/year (with usage reductions), NOI improves by $25,000 (before considering installation costs).

Common Pitfalls

  • Double counting: treating bill-backs as “free money” without matching the underlying expense.
  • Ignoring leaks: chronic leaks can destroy the utility line item and signal deferred maintenance.
  • Misreading the lease: commercial pass-through language varies; confirm what’s reimbursable.
  • Seasonality blind spots: a single month’s bill can be misleading (heating/cooling cycles).
  • Assuming recoveries are stable: vacancy, delinquency, and policy changes can impact collections.

Related Terms

  • RUBS (Ratio Utility Billing System)
  • Submetering
  • Common Area Maintenance (CAM)
  • Triple-Net Lease (NNN)

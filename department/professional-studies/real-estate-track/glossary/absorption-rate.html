Absorption Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Absorption Rate

Definition

Absorption rate is a real estate market metric that measures the pace at which available units are leased (rentals) or sold (for-sale inventory) within a given time period. It helps investors understand whether demand is strong enough to “absorb” current supply.

How It’s Measured

Absorption is usually expressed as units per month (or per quarter) and is often paired with months of supply to show how long it would take to clear current inventory at the current pace.

Common formulas

  • Absorption Rate (units/month) = Units leased (or sold) during period ÷ Number of months in the period
  • Months of Supply = Total available units ÷ Absorption Rate (units/month)

Why It Matters

Absorption rate is a quick indicator of market liquidity and pricing power. Higher absorption generally signals stronger demand relative to supply, supporting rent growth and faster stabilization. Lower absorption can indicate oversupply, weaker demand, or poor product-market fit.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting lease-up: sanity-check assumptions for occupancy ramp and concessions.
  • Assessing supply risk: identify markets where new deliveries may pressure rents.
  • Exit planning: evaluate market depth and buyer/tenant demand at disposition.
  • Pricing strategy: decide when to push rents versus prioritize occupancy.

Example

A submarket has 240 vacant units available. Over the last 3 months, 90 units were leased. Absorption is:

  • Absorption Rate = 90 ÷ 3 = 30 units/month
  • Months of Supply = 240 ÷ 30 = 8 months

An 8-month supply suggests the market may take time to clear vacancy without aggressive pricing, concessions, or demand improvements.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mismatched unit types: studio absorption may differ drastically from 2-bed demand.
  • Seasonality: leasing velocity often varies by month; use comparable periods.
  • One-time shocks: major employer changes, new deliveries, or renovations can distort trends.
  • Ignoring concessions: high absorption with heavy concessions may not imply pricing power.

Definition

Property management is the day-to-day operation of a real estate asset on behalf of the owner. For income property, management is where underwriting becomes reality: leasing execution, resident experience, expense control, compliance, and reporting all flow through the property management function.

What Property Management Covers

Leasing & revenue operations

  • Marketing & lead response: listings, tours, speed-to-lead, conversion tracking.
  • Screening & approvals: credit/background checks, income verification, fair housing compliance.
  • Lease administration: renewals, rent increases, addenda, rule enforcement.
  • Collections: billing, payment plans, delinquency follow-up, chargebacks, write-offs.
  • Resident retention: service response, renewals strategy, reducing turnover cost.

Maintenance & physical operations

  • Work orders: intake, triage, completion standards, resident communication.
  • Preventive maintenance: HVAC, plumbing, life safety, common area upkeep.
  • Vendor management: bids, scopes, quality control, insurance, lien waivers.
  • Turns: make-ready scheduling, unit standards, punch lists, time-to-market.
  • CapEx coordination: planning major replacements and supervising projects.

Compliance & risk

  • Fair housing and consistent application of screening criteria.
  • Local landlord-tenant rules: notices, deposits, timelines, habitability standards.
  • Insurance coordination: claims, incident reporting, risk mitigation.
  • Safety: inspections, life-safety systems, contractor controls.

Finance & reporting

  • Budgeting & forecasting: revenue, payroll, repairs, utilities, taxes/insurance assumptions.
  • Owner reporting: monthly financials, variance notes, rent rolls, delinquency and traffic reports.
  • Banking controls: deposits, disbursements, approvals, audit trails.
  • Expense discipline: coding, purchase orders, vendor invoices, expense benchmarking.

How Property Managers Get Paid

Property management compensation is usually a combination of a base management fee plus transactional fees. Terms vary by asset type and scale, but common line items include:

  • Management fee: typically a percentage of collected revenue (often effective gross income).
  • Leasing fee: charged on new leases (sometimes a flat amount or a percent of one month’s rent).
  • Renewal fee: charged when an existing resident renews.
  • Maintenance/coordination fee: oversight of projects or vendor work (sometimes a percentage).
  • Advertising/marketing: listings, ILS spend, creative, signage (either pass-through or marked up).
  • Setup/transition fees: onboarding a new property and systems conversion.

Why It Matters

In stabilized assets, small operational differences compound. Strong property management can increase NOI by improving occupancy, pushing rents appropriately, controlling delinquencies, reducing turnover, and tightening expenses. Weak management often shows up as slow leasing, high bad debt, maintenance backlog, and expense creep.

Key KPIs Investors Track

  • Occupancy & economic occupancy: physical occupied units vs actual rent collected.
  • Traffic & conversion: leads → tours → applications → move-ins.
  • Days vacant / time-to-lease: speed of turning and leasing units.
  • Renewal rate: resident retention and rent growth at renewal.
  • Delinquency & bad debt: unpaid rent, write-offs, payment plan performance.
  • Work order metrics: open backlog, average completion time, emergency response time.
  • Turn cost and turn time: make-ready spend and days to market-ready.
  • Expense per unit: repairs/maintenance, payroll, utilities, and controllables.

Evaluation Checklist

  • Systems & reporting: do you get timely, consistent, decision-grade reporting?
  • Staffing model: on-site presence, maintenance coverage, after-hours response.
  • Vendor discipline: competitive bids, clear scopes, and quality control.
  • Leasing execution: speed-to-lead standards and accountability for conversion.
  • Owner alignment: are incentives aligned with NOI and resident retention, not just fees?
  • Local expertise: understands submarket pricing, seasonality, and local regulations.

Example

Two identical properties have the same market rent. Property A is managed with slow response times and weak follow-up: vacancy stays high and turns take longer. Property B has tight leasing processes and fast turns: occupancy is higher, delinquencies are lower, and the property can push rent with fewer concessions.

The buildings are the same. The outcome is not. That gap is the value of (or cost of) property management.

Common Pitfalls

  • Fee blindness: a low base fee can be offset by heavy add-on fees and markups.
  • Incentives misaligned: managers paid mainly on gross revenue may not prioritize expense control.
  • Ignoring maintenance backlog: deferred work hurts reviews, renewals, and leasing velocity.
  • Weak controls: poor approvals, coding, and purchasing can create leakage and fraud risk.
  • Turn standards not defined: inconsistent unit quality leads to longer vacancy and lower rent.

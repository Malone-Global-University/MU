Passive Loss

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Passive Loss

Definition

A passive loss is a tax loss generated from a passive activity, such as rental real estate, in which the investor does not materially participate. Under U.S. tax rules, passive losses generally can only offset passive income.

How Passive Losses Are Created

In real estate, passive losses often result from:

  • Depreciation deductions
  • Bonus depreciation
  • Cost segregation studies
  • Operating losses
  • Interest expense exceeding taxable income

Example

A rental property produces:

  • $100,000 in rental income
  • $130,000 in deductible expenses (including depreciation)

Tax result:

  • Passive Loss = $30,000

This loss may offset other passive income but typically cannot offset W-2 wages or active business income unless specific exceptions apply.

Material Participation & Real Estate Professional Status

If an investor qualifies as a Real Estate Professional under IRS rules and materially participates in activities, passive loss limitations may not apply.

Special $25,000 Allowance

Certain taxpayers may deduct up to $25,000 of passive rental losses against ordinary income if their adjusted gross income falls below specific thresholds. This benefit phases out at higher income levels.

Suspended Passive Losses

If passive losses exceed allowable offsets in a given year, they are typically suspended and carried forward. Suspended losses may be used in future years or upon sale of the property.

Why It Matters

  • Impacts after-tax cash flow
  • Influences underwriting projections
  • Shapes investment structure decisions
  • Affects long-term tax strategy

Investor Perspective

Passive losses are a powerful tax planning tool, particularly when combined with cost segregation and bonus depreciation. However, investors must understand eligibility rules and consult qualified tax professionals before relying on projected tax benefits.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming losses offset active income automatically
  • Ignoring income phase-out thresholds
  • Failing to track suspended losses
  • Overemphasizing tax benefits over operational performance

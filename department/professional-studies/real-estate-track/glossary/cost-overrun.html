Cost Overrun

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Cost Overrun

Definition

A cost overrun occurs when the actual cost of a real estate development project exceeds the original approved budget. Overruns can affect construction timelines, financing requirements, lender compliance, and projected investor returns.

Where Cost Overruns Occur

  • Hard costs: Materials, labor, structural systems, mechanical/electrical/plumbing.
  • Soft costs: Architecture, engineering, legal, permits, and impact fees.
  • Site work: Grading, utilities, stormwater, and environmental remediation.
  • Financing costs: Extended interest carry due to delays.

Common Causes

1) Design Changes

  • Scope creep during construction
  • Late-stage value engineering reversals
  • Tenant-driven modifications

2) Market & Supply Issues

  • Material price inflation
  • Labor shortages
  • Supply chain disruptions

3) Site & Regulatory Surprises

  • Unforeseen soil conditions
  • Environmental contamination
  • Additional stormwater or utility requirements
  • Permit or inspection delays

4) Estimating Errors

  • Inaccurate quantity takeoffs
  • Underestimated general conditions
  • Failure to include adequate contingency

Financial Impact on the Capital Stack

  • Equity dilution: Sponsors may need to contribute additional capital.
  • Construction loan stress: Lenders may require additional equity before funding.
  • Reduced IRR: Higher basis reduces overall returns.
  • Lower exit value: Increased cost may compress profit margin.

Example

A multifamily development is budgeted at $40 million with a 5% contingency ($2 million). During construction, material price increases and utility upgrades add $3.5 million in costs.

  • Budgeted cost: $40M
  • Actual cost: $43.5M
  • Cost overrun: $3.5M
  • Contingency shortfall: $1.5M

The sponsor must inject additional equity or restructure financing to close the funding gap.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Detailed pre-construction estimating
  • Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP) contracts
  • Hard-bid subcontractor pricing
  • Adequate contingency reserves (5–10%+ depending on risk)
  • Owner-controlled change management process
  • Regular cost reporting and draw reviews

High-Risk Project Types

  • Ground-up development
  • Adaptive reuse or historic renovations
  • Projects with environmental remediation
  • Complex mixed-use developments

Underwriting Considerations

  • Stress-test construction budgets.
  • Model extended carry scenarios.
  • Confirm contingency adequacy.
  • Review contractor experience and bonding capacity.
  • Evaluate sponsor liquidity for potential overrun coverage.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating contingency needs.
  • Assuming inflation will stabilize.
  • Over-relying on early conceptual pricing.
  • Failing to monitor change orders closely.

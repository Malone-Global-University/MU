Build-Out

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Build-Out

Definition

A build-out is the process of constructing, finishing, or renovating interior space so it is usable for a specific tenant. In commercial real estate, build-outs commonly include items like interior walls, ceilings, flooring, lighting, HVAC modifications, plumbing, electrical/data, millwork, and code-compliance work.

Build-Out vs. Tenant Improvements (TI)

“Build-out” describes the work. “Tenant improvements (TI)” describes the improvements and often the budget (such as a TI allowance) used to pay for the work. A build-out can be funded by the tenant, the landlord, or both—depending on the lease and the work letter.

Common Build-Out Scenarios

  • New lease in shell space: substantial build-out needed before occupancy.
  • Second-generation space: partial build-out (reconfiguration, refresh, selective demo).
  • Renewal / expansion: modifications to match evolving operations or headcount.
  • Change of use: major build-out driven by code, plumbing, ventilation, or power upgrades.

Why It Matters

Build-out impacts both time to revenue and total lease economics. Longer construction schedules can extend vacancy and increase carry costs, often leading to more free rent. Larger build-outs increase up-front cash requirements and can materially reduce net effective rent.

What Drives Build-Out Cost

  • Delivery condition: shell space typically costs more than second-gen.
  • Use type: medical, lab, restaurant, and high-finish retail usually require higher scope.
  • MEP intensity: plumbing, electrical service, ventilation, and fire/life-safety needs can dominate budgets.
  • Code and permitting: jurisdiction requirements and review time vary widely.
  • Supply chain and labor: lead times and trade availability can change schedules and costs.

How It’s Documented

The work letter usually governs build-out scope and process, including:

  • Who does the work: landlord work vs. tenant work
  • Eligible TI costs: what can be reimbursed under the TI allowance
  • Approvals: design submissions, review timelines, and contractor requirements
  • Payment mechanics: invoices, lien waivers, retainage, and reimbursement timing
  • Schedule milestones: delivery, substantial completion, and commencement triggers

Investor Uses

  • Lease-up underwriting: model build-out cost, downtime, and leasing costs (TI/LC).
  • Rollover forecasting: estimate future build-outs at expiration for re-tenanting risk.
  • Capital planning: size reserves to handle multiple simultaneous build-outs.
  • Offer comparison: evaluate deals using net effective rent, not just face rent.

Example

A 6,000 SF tenant signs a lease in shell space and requires a heavy build-out. The landlord offers a TI allowance, but the schedule requires several months of permitting and construction, leading to additional free rent. Underwriting should capture:

  • TI cash outlay and timing
  • Leasing commissions
  • Downtime and lost rent during construction
  • Net effective rent after concessions

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming the space condition: “shell” and “vanilla shell” vary—define delivery precisely.
  • Ignoring timing: delays can effectively become extra free rent if rent commencement isn’t protected.
  • Misclassifying costs: building upgrades vs. tenant-specific improvements should be tracked consistently.
  • Weak lien controls: reimbursements without lien waivers can create mechanic’s lien risk.
  • Over-specialization: highly customized build-outs can raise future re-tenanting costs.

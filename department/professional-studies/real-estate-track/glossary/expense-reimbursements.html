Expense Reimbursements

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Expense Reimbursements

Definition

Expense reimbursements are payments tenants make to a landlord to cover some or all of a property’s operating expenses. In commercial real estate, reimbursements commonly include CAM (common area maintenance), real estate taxes, insurance, and sometimes utilities and other shared services. Reimbursements are defined by the lease and typically allocated based on the tenant’s pro rata share of the building or center.

Where You See Them

Expense reimbursements are most common in NNN (triple net) and modified gross leases. In a true gross lease, operating expenses are bundled into rent and not itemized as separate reimbursements.

Lease-type comparison (conceptual)

  • NNN: tenant pays base rent + reimbursements for CAM, taxes, and insurance (and sometimes utilities).
  • Modified gross: base rent includes some expenses; tenant reimburses expenses above a base year or certain categories.
  • Gross: landlord pays most operating expenses; rent is “all-in.”

What Expenses Are Typically Reimbursable

  • CAM: common area maintenance like landscaping, snow removal, parking lot upkeep, lighting. (CAM)
  • Taxes: real estate taxes and assessments allocated to tenants.
  • Insurance: property insurance premiums (and sometimes related admin costs).
  • Utilities: common-area electric/water; in some cases utility bill-backs for master-metered services. (Utility Bill-Back)
  • Management/admin fees: sometimes allowed, often capped or excluded based on lease language.

How Reimbursements Are Calculated

Most leases use a tenant’s pro rata share multiplied by reimbursable expenses. Pro rata share is generally based on rentable square footage (RSF), though some leases use leased area or other definitions.

Common formulas

  • Pro Rata Share = Tenant RSF ÷ Total RSF (definition per lease)
  • Tenant Reimbursement = Pro Rata Share × Reimbursable Expenses (for the period)
  • Monthly Billings = Annual Budgeted Reimbursements ÷ 12 (then reconciled to actual)

Budgets and Reconciliations

Expense reimbursements are often billed as monthly estimates based on an annual budget. At the end of the year, the landlord performs a reconciliation comparing estimated billings to actual costs:

  • If actual expenses are higher: tenants owe a true-up.
  • If actual expenses are lower: tenants receive a credit (or refund, depending on lease terms).

Key Lease Concepts That Change the Economics

  • Caps: limits on annual increases (often on “controllable” CAM, with carve-outs for taxes/insurance).
  • Gross-up: adjusting expenses to a stabilized occupancy level so tenants pay a fair share even in vacancy.
  • Base year / expense stop: in modified gross leases, tenants pay increases over a defined baseline year.
  • Inclusions/exclusions: what counts as reimbursable (repairs vs. capital, admin, marketing, legal, etc.).
  • Audit rights: tenant rights to review reimbursement statements and supporting documents.

Why It Matters

For owners, reimbursements can stabilize cash flow by shifting expense inflation to tenants. For tenants, reimbursements determine all-in occupancy cost and influence renewal decisions. For investors, reimbursements affect the quality of income, lease durability, and underwriting accuracy—especially when caps, exclusions, or collection risk are involved.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting net income: separate base rent from reimbursements and validate recoverability category-by-category.
  • Lease review: identify caps, stops, exclusions, and gross-up provisions that impact pass-through.
  • Risk management: assess tenant sensitivity to expense spikes and potential renewal pressure.
  • Operational discipline: strong documentation and clean reconciliations reduce disputes and write-offs.

Example

A tenant occupies 10,000 RSF in a 100,000 RSF building (10% pro rata share). Reimbursable annual expenses are $600,000 (CAM $250k, taxes $250k, insurance $100k).

  • Annual reimbursement = 10% × $600,000 = $60,000
  • Monthly estimate = $60,000 ÷ 12 = $5,000/month

At year-end, if actual expenses come in at $640,000, the tenant’s true-up is: 10% × ($640,000 − $600,000) = $4,000 (subject to any caps or exclusions).

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming 100% recoverability: caps, exclusions, and “non-reimbursable” categories can be material.
  • Overlooking vacancy mechanics: gross-up rules (or lack of them) change who bears vacancy-related costs.
  • Not distinguishing controllable vs. uncontrollable: caps often exclude taxes/insurance; model correctly.
  • Poor documentation: weak reconciliations increase disputes, delays, and write-offs.
  • Tenant concentration: large tenants can negotiate carve-outs that materially change the property’s net economics.

Plat Map

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Plat Map

Definition

A plat map (often just “plat”) is a recorded map that shows how a larger tract of land is subdivided into lots, blocks, streets, and related features like easements and rights-of-way. Plats are filed with a local recording office and become part of the public record.

What a Plat Map Typically Shows

  • Lot and block layout: numbered lots and blocks within a subdivision.
  • Dimensions and bearings: boundary lengths, angles, curve data (where applicable).
  • Streets and alleys: public rights-of-way and their widths.
  • Easements: utility, access, drainage, shared driveways, etc.
  • Setback or building lines: sometimes noted as “building setback lines.”
  • Dedications and notes: conditions, restrictions, or dedicatory language.

Plat Map vs. Survey

Investors often confuse plats with surveys. A plat is a recorded subdivision map showing intended parcel layout. A survey is a site-specific field measurement prepared by a licensed surveyor that confirms where the property lines and improvements sit in the real world.

  • Plat: public record; ideal for understanding subdivision layout and legal references.
  • Survey: confirms boundary locations, monuments, encroachments, and improvement placement.

Why It Matters

Plat maps are foundational for boundary clarity, access, and use constraints. They help investors quickly see whether a property is part of a platted subdivision, what easements affect it, and how it connects to streets and utilities.

Investor Uses

  • Confirming legal identity: match the deed’s “lot & block” description to recorded plats.
  • Spotting easement risk: identify utility or access easements that restrict building or parking.
  • Evaluating access/frontage: understand rights-of-way and whether access is public or private.
  • Feasibility screening: assess whether lot shape/size supports additions, ADUs, or redevelopment.
  • Title review: reconcile plat notes with title exceptions and recorded documents.

Example

A buyer plans to expand parking behind a retail building. The recorded plat shows a 10-foot utility easement along the rear lot line and a drainage easement cutting through the corner. Even if the area looks “unused,” these easements may prevent paving, adding lighting, or building structures without utility approval.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming the plat is current: later replats or vacations may change lots, streets, or easements.
  • Ignoring plat notes: restrictions and dedications can be buried in small print.
  • Confusing ROW with lot lines: the edge of pavement is not the property boundary.
  • Relying on a plat instead of a survey: plats don’t show where buildings/fences actually sit today.
  • Missing non-platted parcels: some properties are metes-and-bounds and won’t have a subdivision plat.

Where to Find Plat Maps

  • County recorder / auditor: recorded plat books or digital records.
  • Assessor / GIS portals: often provide plat references and parcel overlays.
  • Title company: commonly supplies plats and recorded map references in the title package.

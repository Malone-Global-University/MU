Carve-Out Guaranty

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Carve-Out Guaranty

Definition

A Carve-Out Guaranty (often called a “bad boy guaranty”) is a limited guaranty in a non-recourse loan that creates personal liability for specific prohibited borrower actions. While the loan is generally non-recourse, certain acts “carve out” exceptions where the guarantor becomes personally liable.

Why It Exists

Non-recourse loans protect sponsors from personal liability beyond the collateral. Carve-outs protect lenders from intentional misconduct, fraud, or structural actions that impair collateral or repayment.

Typical Carve-Out Triggers

  • Fraud or misrepresentation in loan documents or reporting.
  • Misapplication of rents or insurance proceeds.
  • Waste or intentional damage to the property.
  • Unauthorized transfers of ownership interests.
  • Voluntary bankruptcy filing or collusive insolvency actions.
  • Violation of single-purpose entity (SPE) covenants.

Limited vs. Full Recourse Carve-Outs

  • Loss-only carve-outs: liability limited to lender’s actual damages.
  • Springing full recourse: entire loan becomes recourse upon certain triggers (often bankruptcy-related actions).

Why It Matters

Carve-out guaranties define the boundary between non-recourse protection and personal liability. Sponsors must understand exactly what actions could expose them individually.

Investor Considerations

  • Review definitions carefully: triggers vary by lender and document.
  • Negotiate scope: limit ambiguous or overly broad provisions.
  • Maintain compliance: especially SPE separateness and reporting standards.
  • Understand bankruptcy language: some triggers are highly technical.

Example

A sponsor closes a $15 million non-recourse loan secured by a multifamily property. The loan includes a carve-out guaranty. If the sponsor diverts rent proceeds for personal use instead of paying operating expenses and debt service, the lender can pursue the guarantor personally for those losses.

In more severe cases—such as a voluntary bankruptcy filing in violation of loan covenants—the entire loan balance may become fully recourse.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming non-recourse means no risk: carve-outs can be significant.
  • Overlooking SPE requirements: technical breaches can trigger liability.
  • Inadequate documentation: poor recordkeeping can be interpreted as misrepresentation.
  • Not consulting counsel: language interpretation matters.

Interest Rate Spread

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Interest Rate Spread

Definition

An interest rate spread is the difference between two interest rates. In real estate finance, it most commonly refers to the margin a lender charges above a benchmark rate (such as SOFR or U.S. Treasuries) to compensate for credit risk, liquidity risk, and market conditions.

Common Applications in Real Estate

  • Loan pricing: Floating-rate loans priced as Benchmark + Spread.
  • Credit spreads: Difference between corporate/CMBS yields and Treasury yields.
  • Cap rate vs. Treasury spread: Used to assess relative valuation.
  • Development risk premium: Higher spreads for riskier projects.

Example (Floating-Rate Loan)

A multifamily loan is priced at SOFR + 275 basis points (bps). If SOFR is 4.00%, the total interest rate equals:

  • 4.00% (SOFR) + 2.75% (Spread) = 6.75%

The 275 bps represents the lender’s compensation for risk and capital costs.

What Drives Spreads?

  • Borrower credit quality: Strong sponsors receive tighter spreads.
  • Loan-to-value (LTV): Higher leverage increases spread.
  • Asset type: Riskier sectors (e.g., transitional office) demand wider spreads.
  • Market liquidity: Tight credit markets widen spreads.
  • Macroeconomic uncertainty: Volatility increases risk premiums.

Spread vs. Benchmark Rate

The benchmark rate reflects broader monetary policy and capital markets, while the spread reflects asset-specific and borrower-specific risk. Even if benchmark rates fall, spreads may widen in stressed markets.

Impact on Valuation

  • Higher spreads increase borrowing costs.
  • Reduced leverage capacity may lower buyer demand.
  • Wider credit spreads often coincide with cap rate expansion.

Example (Credit Spread Widening)

If CMBS spreads widen from 150 bps to 300 bps over Treasuries, borrowing becomes more expensive even if Treasury yields remain stable. This can reduce transaction volume and property values.

Investor Applications

  • Refinancing analysis: Model spread changes at maturity.
  • Debt selection: Compare floating vs. fixed-rate structures.
  • Market timing: Monitor credit spreads for signals of tightening conditions.
  • Valuation benchmarking: Compare cap rate spreads to Treasury yields.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring spread volatility: Spreads can move independently of rates.
  • Underestimating refinance risk: Spread widening can impair DSCR.
  • Assuming stable credit markets: Liquidity cycles impact spreads quickly.

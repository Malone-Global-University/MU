Preferred Return

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Preferred Return

Definition

A preferred return (often called the pref) is a contractual return threshold in a real estate partnership that must be paid to investors (limited partners) before the sponsor (general partner) receives promoted profits in the distribution waterfall. Think of it as the investors’ “first claim” on distributable cash flow up to a stated rate.

What a Preferred Return Is (and Isn’t)

  • Is: a distribution priority (who gets paid first) and a hurdle that controls when promote can begin.
  • Is not: a guaranteed return. If the property doesn’t generate enough cash, the pref may accrue or go unpaid.
  • Is not: the same thing as IRR. Pref is typically a simple annual rate tied to invested equity and timing rules in the agreement.

How It’s Typically Structured

Preferred returns are usually stated as an annual percentage, such as 7% or 8%, and applied to an investor’s unreturned capital (their equity that has not yet been paid back). Agreements define whether the pref is calculated on:

  • Invested capital (initial equity contributions),
  • Unreturned capital (declines after return of capital), and/or
  • Daily / monthly accrual versus annual snapshots.

Common accrual types

  • Non-compounding (simple): pref accrues linearly based on the stated rate.
  • Compounding: unpaid pref earns pref (less common, but it exists).
  • Cumulative: unpaid pref carries forward to future periods until paid.
  • Non-cumulative: unpaid pref does not carry forward (less investor-friendly; more common in some funds).

Where It Sits in the Waterfall

In many deals, distributions follow a sequence (the “waterfall”). A typical structure might look like:

  1. Return of capital (sometimes first, sometimes after pref depending on the agreement)
  2. Preferred return to investors until the pref is current
  3. Catch-up to the sponsor (optional) to align splits after the pref
  4. Split of remaining cash flow (e.g., 70/30, 80/20) with promote going to the sponsor

The exact order and definitions are negotiated in the partnership agreement, and small wording differences can produce big economic differences.

How It’s Calculated

A simple (non-compounding) preferred return is often calculated like this:

Common formula

  • Preferred Return (for a period) = Unreturned Invested Capital × Pref Rate × (Days in Period ÷ 365)

Many models approximate using months instead of days:

  • Preferred Return (monthly approx.) = Unreturned Invested Capital × Pref Rate ÷ 12

Example

An investor contributes $100,000 to a deal with an 8% preferred return, calculated on unreturned capital and paid quarterly. After one year, the property has generated enough distributable cash to pay the pref but not enough to trigger a higher promote tier.

  • Annual pref = 100,000 × 8% = $8,000
  • Quarterly pref target = 8,000 ÷ 4 = $2,000 per quarter

If the deal only produces $1,200 of distributable cash in Q1, then (in a cumulative structure) the unpaid $800 typically carries forward and must be paid before the sponsor can receive promote distributions.

Why It Matters

  • Alignment: pref makes sure investors receive a baseline return before sponsor promote begins.
  • Downside protection: in weaker performance scenarios, the sponsor’s upside is delayed.
  • Deal comparability: pref terms help you compare equity structures across offerings.
  • Behavior shaping: the presence (and size) of pref can influence risk-taking and execution priorities.

Common Pitfalls

  • “Preferred return” assumed guaranteed: it’s only payable if there is distributable cash or proceeds.
  • Vague calculation basis: “8% pref” is incomplete without defining invested vs unreturned capital and accrual method.
  • Catch-up confusion: sponsor catch-ups can materially change who gets what once the pref is met.
  • Timing differences: quarterly vs annual true-ups can impact interim cash flow and arrears.
  • Multiple tiers: pref is often just the first hurdle; higher tiers can increase promote as returns rise.

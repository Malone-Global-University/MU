Seller Credit

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Seller Credit

Definition

A seller credit is a negotiated amount of money that the seller agrees to contribute toward the buyer’s closing costs, prepaid expenses, or approved repairs at the time of closing. Rather than reducing the purchase price directly, the credit appears on the settlement statement as a reduction in the buyer’s cash required at closing.

How Seller Credits Work

  1. Negotiated during contract: Credit amount included in purchase agreement.
  2. Reflected at closing: Shown as a line-item credit to the buyer on the closing statement.
  3. Applied to approved costs: Used for lender fees, title fees, prepaid taxes/insurance, or agreed repairs.

Common Uses

  • Offset inspection issues instead of requiring repairs before closing.
  • Cover buyer closing costs to reduce upfront cash requirement.
  • Interest rate buydowns (temporary or permanent).
  • Market incentives in slower or buyer-favored markets.

Financing Limits

Most lenders limit seller credits to a percentage of the purchase price. Limits vary depending on loan type, occupancy, and loan-to-value (LTV). Excess credits cannot typically be received as cash and may require price adjustments to remain compliant.

Underwriting Implications

  • Cash-on-cash return: Credits lower initial cash invested.
  • Loan compliance: Must remain within lender caps.
  • Appraisal sensitivity: High credits may trigger appraisal scrutiny.
  • Market signal: Frequent credits can indicate soft pricing conditions.

Example

Purchase price: $1,000,000
Negotiated seller credit: $25,000

If buyer closing costs total $30,000, the seller credit reduces buyer cash due at closing to $5,000 (plus down payment and other required funds).

Common Pitfalls

  • Exceeding lender caps: Can force renegotiation or price change.
  • Confusing price reduction with credit: Credits affect cash flow differently than price cuts.
  • Assuming unused credit is refunded: Typically not allowed.
  • Overlooking appraisal impact: Inflated prices with high credits may raise red flags.

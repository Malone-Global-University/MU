Listing Agreement

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Listing Agreement

Definition

A listing agreement is a legally binding contract between a property owner (seller or landlord) and a licensed broker. The agreement authorizes the broker to market and sell or lease the property under defined terms, including commission structure and duration.

Key Components

  • Listing price or rent: Agreed marketing price or rental rate.
  • Commission: Broker compensation, typically a percentage of sale price or lease value.
  • Term: Duration of the agreement (e.g., 3–12 months).
  • Exclusivity: Whether the broker has exclusive rights.
  • Marketing authority: Permission to advertise and represent the property.
  • Protection period: Commission protection for buyers introduced during the listing term.

Types of Listing Agreements

  • Exclusive Right to Sell: Broker earns commission regardless of who finds the buyer.
  • Exclusive Agency: Broker earns commission unless the owner finds the buyer directly.
  • Open Listing: Multiple brokers may market the property; commission goes to the procuring cause.

Example

A property owner signs a 6-month exclusive right-to-sell listing agreement with a broker at a 3% commission on a $5,000,000 property.

  • If sold at list price, broker commission = 5,000,000 × 3% = $150,000

Investor Considerations

  • Negotiate commission structure and term length.
  • Clarify marketing strategy and target buyer profile.
  • Understand termination clauses.
  • Review protection period provisions carefully.

Commercial vs. Residential Context

Commercial listing agreements often include detailed marketing plans, financial data distribution protocols, confidentiality provisions, and fee structures that may differ from residential transactions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Signing long exclusive terms without performance benchmarks
  • Not clarifying commission splits
  • Overlooking protection period obligations
  • Failing to define marketing expectations

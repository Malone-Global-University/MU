Definition

A survey is a professional measurement and mapping of a property that identifies boundary lines, improvements, easements, encroachments, and other physical or legal features affecting the land. Surveys are typically prepared by licensed land surveyors and are a key component of real estate due diligence.

What a Survey Typically Shows

  • Property boundary lines and dimensions
  • Building footprints and improvements
  • Easements (utility, access, drainage, etc.)
  • Encroachments (fences, structures crossing boundaries)
  • Setback lines and zoning information (in some cases)
  • Access points, rights-of-way, and adjacent streets

Common Types of Surveys

  • Boundary Survey: Confirms property lines and corners.
  • ALTA/NSPS Survey: Detailed commercial survey meeting lender and title standards.
  • Topographic Survey: Shows elevation changes and terrain features.
  • Subdivision Survey: Divides land into smaller parcels.
  • Construction Survey: Guides building placement during development.

Why It Matters

  • Title insurance: Lenders often require an updated survey to issue coverage.
  • Risk reduction: Identifies encroachments or access problems before closing.
  • Development planning: Confirms setbacks and buildable area.
  • Dispute prevention: Clarifies boundaries to avoid neighbor conflicts.

Investor Uses

  • Verify site size for density calculations
  • Confirm parking counts and building placement
  • Evaluate expansion potential
  • Assess compliance with zoning setbacks

Example

During due diligence on a retail center, a survey reveals that a neighboring building encroaches two feet onto the property line. This finding may require a boundary agreement, title exception, or adjustment before closing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using outdated surveys that miss new improvements
  • Failing to review Schedule B title exceptions against the survey
  • Overlooking easements that limit future development
  • Assuming lot size equals usable area

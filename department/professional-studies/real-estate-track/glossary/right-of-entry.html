Right of Entry

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Right of Entry

Definition

Right of entry is the legal right of a landlord (or authorized agent) to enter a rental unit for certain legitimate purposes—such as repairs, inspections, showings, or emergencies—while still respecting a tenant’s right to privacy and quiet enjoyment.

Common Permitted Reasons to Enter

  • Repairs and maintenance: addressing work orders, safety issues, or preventive maintenance.
  • Inspections: periodic inspections, compliance checks, or condition assessments.
  • Showings: showing the unit to prospective tenants, buyers, or lenders (often with limits).
  • Emergency entry: active leaks, fire/smoke, suspected gas issues, or immediate safety threats.
  • Abandonment or welfare checks: in narrow circumstances defined by local law and lease terms.

Notice and Timing

In non-emergency situations, most jurisdictions require advance notice and entry at reasonable times. The exact notice period varies by state and local rules, and it may also be shaped by the lease. Even when notice is provided, entry should be as limited and professional as possible.

Best Practices for Operators

  • Provide written notice: include date, time window, purpose, and who will enter.
  • Use a reasonable time window: avoid repeated short-notice entries.
  • Document everything: logs, photos (when appropriate), and work completion notes.
  • Respect privacy: knock, announce, and do not enter areas unrelated to the stated purpose.
  • Coordinate with residents: especially for pets, security concerns, or fragile items.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Asset protection: timely entry enables repairs that prevent larger capex events (water damage, mold, electrical issues).
  • Habitability compliance: prompt access supports meeting repair obligations.
  • Operational efficiency: reduces turn time by enabling inspections and pre-move-out planning.
  • Legal risk: improper entry can trigger claims for privacy violations, harassment, or breach of quiet enjoyment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Entering without proper notice: except where true emergencies permit immediate entry.
  • Overuse of “inspection”: frequent entry can look like harassment.
  • Unclear communication: vague reasons or “sometime tomorrow” notices create resident conflict.
  • Vendor missteps: contractors entering outside the approved window or without identification.

Right of Entry vs. Easement

In landlord-tenant settings, “right of entry” usually refers to lawful access to an occupied unit. In property law, a “right of entry” can also describe rights created by an agreement or easement (for example, a utility company’s right to enter land to service infrastructure). Context matters.

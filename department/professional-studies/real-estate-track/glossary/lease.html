Definition

A lease is a written agreement between a landlord (property owner) and a tenant that grants the tenant the right to occupy or use a property for a specified time period in exchange for rent and compliance with stated rules and responsibilities.

What a Lease Controls

From an investor standpoint, the lease is the “cash flow contract.” It determines how income is earned, how expenses are allocated, and how risk is shared.

  • Income: base rent, escalations, fees, reimbursements, late charges.
  • Timing: when rent is due, grace periods, prorations, renewal/option windows.
  • Operating responsibilities: who pays utilities, repairs, insurance, and maintenance.
  • Restrictions: use, occupancy limits, pets, alterations, signage, subletting.
  • Enforcement: default definitions, cure periods, remedies, eviction/termination rights.

Key Lease Terms to Know

  • Lease term: the start and end date (and any renewal options).
  • Rent: amount due and frequency (monthly is common; commercial may vary).
  • Security deposit: funds held to cover damages, unpaid rent, or other defaults.
  • Escalations: scheduled rent increases (fixed, step-ups, CPI-based, percentage).
  • Concessions: free rent, discounts, or move-in credits that reduce effective rent.
  • Utilities and reimbursements: how tenant payments for expenses are handled.
  • Repairs and maintenance: who is responsible for what (unit vs. building vs. common areas).
  • Assignment/subletting: whether the tenant can transfer the lease to another party.
  • Guarantee: whether another party backs the tenant’s obligations (common in commercial; sometimes residential).

Common Lease Structures

Residential lease

Typically short-term (often 6–12 months) and standardized. The landlord generally pays building-level expenses, while tenants may pay some utilities depending on the property’s setup.

Commercial leases

  • Gross lease: landlord pays most operating expenses; tenant pays one rent amount (sometimes with limited pass-throughs).
  • Modified gross: expenses are shared based on a negotiated structure (base year, expense stop, or defined categories).
  • NNN (triple net) lease: tenant reimburses property taxes, insurance, and maintenance (often reduces expense volatility for the owner).

Why It Matters in Underwriting

  • In-place income: rent roll is a summary of leases; the lease is the source document.
  • Rollover risk: expirations can create vacancy and capex needs (tenant improvements, turns).
  • Expense risk: lease type determines who bears inflation in taxes, insurance, and maintenance.
  • Credit/collection risk: tenant quality and guarantees affect probability of payment.
  • NOI quality: “high rent” is not the same as high effective rent if concessions or delinquencies are present.

Example

A tenant signs a 12-month residential lease for $1,850/month with a $1,000 security deposit and a one-time $300 move-in concession. For underwriting:

  • Gross scheduled rent = $1,850 × 12 = $22,200
  • Effective rent is lower due to the $300 concession (and any vacancy/delinquency assumptions).

Common Pitfalls

  • Trusting the rent roll blindly: always reconcile rent roll to signed leases and collections history.
  • Missing hidden concessions: “free month” promos, ongoing discounts, or employer deals reduce true income.
  • Confusing who pays expenses: reimbursement language (especially commercial) can materially change NOI.
  • Ignoring options: renewal options can cap rent growth or delay mark-to-market.
  • Weak default language: unclear remedies or long cure periods slow enforcement.

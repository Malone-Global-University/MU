Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Definition

Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a time-weighted return metric that represents the discount rate at which the net present value (NPV) of an investment’s cash flows equals zero. In plain terms: IRR is the annualized rate of return implied by the timing and size of cash flows in and out of a deal.

Why IRR Is Popular in Real Estate

Real estate deals often involve irregular cash flows (big equity outflow at purchase, periodic distributions, and a large terminal cash flow at sale or refinance). IRR is designed for exactly that kind of pattern.

What IRR captures well

  • Timing: earlier cash flows increase IRR; later cash flows decrease IRR.
  • Magnitude: larger total profit can raise IRR, but timing still dominates.
  • Exit dependence: most value-add deals generate the largest cash flow at disposition.

How It’s Measured

IRR is the rate r that solves:

  • NPV = 0 = Σ (Cash Flowt ÷ (1 + r)t)

In practice, IRR is calculated with financial software or spreadsheets because it requires iteration (there’s no simple closed-form solution for most real-world cash flow streams).

IRR vs. Equity Multiple vs. Cash-on-Cash

IRR is only one way to measure performance. It’s best understood alongside other metrics:

  • Equity Multiple: total money back ÷ total money invested (ignores timing).
  • Cash-on-Cash Return: annual cash flow ÷ invested equity (focuses on current income, not exit).
  • IRR: annualized return that heavily weights timing.

Example

Two deals return the same total profit but at different times:

  • Deal A: Invest $1,000,000 today and receive $1,600,000 in Year 3.
  • Deal B: Invest $1,000,000 today and receive $1,600,000 in Year 6.

Both deals have the same equity multiple (1.6x), but Deal A will have a much higher IRR because the profit arrives sooner.

How Investors Use IRR

  • Comparing strategies: evaluate short, high-turnover deals vs. longer duration income deals.
  • Setting return targets: establish minimum return requirements (e.g., “15%+ levered IRR”).
  • Waterfall hurdles: promote tiers often step up after LPs achieve target IRRs.
  • Timing sensitivity: test what happens if lease-up or sale slips by 6–12 months.

Common Pitfalls

  • IRR can be “gamed” by early distributions: a refi that returns capital early can spike IRR even if long-term value is mediocre.
  • Reinvestment assumption: IRR mathematically assumes interim cash flows can be reinvested at the same IRR, which may be unrealistic.
  • Multiple IRRs: unusual cash flow patterns (in/out/in/out) can create more than one IRR or no meaningful IRR.
  • Ignores absolute dollars: a small deal can show a high IRR but produce limited total profit.
  • Overreliance on the exit: many models “earn” most IRR from sale assumptions (exit cap rate, buyer demand, timing).

Best Practices

  • Pair IRR with equity multiple: timing and total dollars both matter.
  • Stress the timeline: test delays in renovations, lease-up, and sale.
  • Stress exit assumptions: widen exit cap rates and reduce terminal NOI to understand downside.
  • Be explicit about leverage: quote whether IRR is levered (after debt) or unlevered (property-level).

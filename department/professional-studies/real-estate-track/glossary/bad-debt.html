Bad Debt

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Bad Debt

Definition

Bad debt is rent and fee revenue that was billed or expected but ultimately not collected. In property operations, it typically shows up when resident balances are written off (or sent to collections and still not recovered) due to nonpayment.

Bad Debt vs. Vacancy vs. Delinquency

These terms are related but not the same:

  • Vacancy loss: income you didn’t earn because the unit was empty (or not rent-ready).
  • Delinquency: rent that is currently past due but may still be collected.
  • Bad debt: rent/fees that became uncollectible and were written off (a permanent loss).

A property can have low vacancy and still suffer if collections are weak and bad debt is high.

How It’s Measured

Bad debt is usually tracked as a percentage of gross potential rent (or as dollars per unit per month).

Common formulas

  • Bad Debt % = Bad debt write-offs ÷ Gross Potential Rent (GPR)
  • Bad Debt (per unit / month) = Total bad debt in period ÷ (Number of units × Months in period)

Why It Matters

Bad debt is a direct drag on NOI. It also tends to correlate with other operational issues: higher turnover, more evictions, more unit damage, and higher legal/collection costs. In underwriting, small changes to bad debt assumptions can materially impact cash flow and value — especially in workforce housing or markets with higher income volatility.

Common Causes

  • Loose screening: approving applicants with unstable income or poor payment history.
  • Poor collections discipline: inconsistent late notices, weak follow-up, or unclear policies.
  • Economic stress: job losses, reduced hours, or local industry disruption.
  • Operational friction: limited payment options, confusing billing, or poor resident communication.
  • Eviction delays: extended nonpayment periods can inflate ultimate write-offs.
  • Balance “snowballing”: fees and partial payments that never catch up to the monthly rent.

How Operators Reduce Bad Debt

  • Stronger tenant screening: verify income, rental history, and fraud indicators.
  • Clear payment policy: consistent late fees, notices, and escalation timelines.
  • Multiple payment options: online portal, ACH, cards, and in-person options where appropriate.
  • Early intervention: proactive outreach before balances grow (day 1–5 matters).
  • Structured repayment plans: when used, keep them short, documented, and enforceable.
  • Move-out balance control: itemize charges, document damage, and pursue collections efficiently.

Underwriting Implications

When reviewing an acquisition, compare trailing bad debt and delinquency to peers and to your underwriting assumptions. High bad debt may be a fixable operational issue (screening/collections) or a market signal (resident profile and local wage stability). Underwrite conservatively until you can prove improvement through leading indicators (application quality, on-time payment rate, delinquency aging, and eviction volume).

Common Pitfalls

  • Hiding bad debt in “concessions” or “other”: misclassification masks true collections performance.
  • Confusing delinquency with bad debt: delinquency is a pipeline metric; bad debt is the final loss.
  • Assuming instant improvement: collections performance often improves over 60–120 days, not overnight.
  • Underestimating eviction timelines: delays can magnify losses and legal expense.

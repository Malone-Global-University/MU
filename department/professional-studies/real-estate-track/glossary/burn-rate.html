Burn Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Burn Rate

Definition

Burn rate is a metric that measures how quickly an investment is consuming cash over time—typically expressed as dollars per month. In real estate, burn rate usually refers to the cash needed to cover a property’s negative net cash flow (operating shortfalls, debt service shortfalls, or both) during periods like lease-up, renovation, or distress.

What Burn Rate Includes

Burn rate can be defined narrowly or broadly depending on the question you’re trying to answer. The key is to be consistent and explicit.

  • Operating burn: shortfall between operating income and operating expenses (pre-debt).
  • Debt-service burn: cash needed to cover principal + interest when NOI is insufficient.
  • Total burn: all net cash outflows, including operating shortfalls, debt service, and required reserves.
  • Project burn: total burn plus renovation/capex outlays and leasing costs during a business plan.

How It’s Measured

Burn rate is typically a simple average over a period, paired with a “runway” estimate that shows how long available cash can cover expected shortfalls.

Common formulas

  • Monthly Burn Rate = (Total cash outflows − Total cash inflows) ÷ Number of months
  • Runway (months) = Available cash (reserves + undrawn proceeds, if applicable) ÷ Monthly Burn Rate

Why It Matters

Burn rate is a practical, operator-focused way to quantify time pressure. A property can be a good deal on paper but still fail if it runs out of cash before it stabilizes. Understanding burn rate helps investors size reserves, set stabilization timelines, and avoid forced decisions like emergency capital calls, distressed refinances, or fire-sale exits.

Investor Uses

  • Lease-up planning: estimate cash needed until occupancy and NOI reach stabilization.
  • Capex sequencing: pace renovations to avoid a burn rate that outstrips reserves.
  • Debt management: monitor DSCR shortfalls and covenant risk (especially on bridge loans).
  • Capital planning: decide whether to raise more equity, negotiate lender relief, or slow spend.
  • Risk assessment: quantify “how long can we hold on?” under downside scenarios.

Example

An apartment property is in heavy renovation and lease-up. Over a 3-month period, the property generated $240,000 in collected revenue and had $330,000 in operating expenses plus $120,000 in debt service. Net cash outflow is:

  • Total Outflows = 330,000 + 120,000 = $450,000
  • Net Outflow = 450,000 − 240,000 = $210,000 over 3 months
  • Burn Rate = 210,000 ÷ 3 = $70,000/month

If the deal has $560,000 in available reserves, the runway is: 560,000 ÷ 70,000 = 8 months. That runway becomes a hard constraint on the timeline to stabilize or refinance.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mixing definitions: comparing “operating burn” one month to “total burn” the next.
  • Ignoring seasonality: taxes, insurance, utilities, and turns can create lumpy cash needs.
  • Confusing NOI with cash: accrual accounting may hide real cash timing issues.
  • Underestimating lease-up drag: concessions, vacancy loss, and bad debt can extend runway needs.
  • Not modeling downside: a small occupancy miss can materially increase burn on high leverage.

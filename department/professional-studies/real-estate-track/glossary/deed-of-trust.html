Deed of Trust

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Deed of Trust

Definition

A Deed of Trust is a legal document used to secure a real estate loan. It involves three parties:

  • Borrower (Trustor)
  • Lender (Beneficiary)
  • Trustee (neutral third party)

The borrower transfers legal title to the trustee as collateral, to be held for the benefit of the lender until the loan is repaid.

How It Works

  • Borrower signs promissory note (promise to repay).
  • Deed of trust secures that promise with the property.
  • If borrower defaults, trustee may initiate foreclosure.

Deed of Trust vs. Mortgage

  • Mortgage: Two-party agreement (borrower and lender).
  • Deed of Trust: Three-party agreement including trustee.
  • Some states primarily use one structure over the other.

Foreclosure Process

In many deed-of-trust states, lenders can pursue non-judicial foreclosure, which is typically faster than court-supervised foreclosure.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Defines lender’s rights in default
  • Affects foreclosure speed and risk
  • Impacts negotiation leverage during distress
  • Influences underwriting of legal risk

Example

An investor acquires a $15 million property with a $10 million loan. The deed of trust is recorded against the property, giving the lender secured interest until the debt is fully repaid.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring state law differences
  • Overlooking default provisions
  • Misunderstanding trustee powers
  • Failing to review covenants and restrictions

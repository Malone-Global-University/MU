CPI Adjustment

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary CPI Adjustment

Definition

A CPI adjustment is a lease provision that increases rent based on changes in the Consumer Price Index (CPI), a widely used measure of inflation published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics.

CPI-based escalations help preserve purchasing power by adjusting rent in proportion to inflation over time.

How CPI Adjustments Work

  • Lease references a specific CPI index (e.g., CPI-U).
  • Base index is set at lease commencement.
  • Rent adjusts periodically (annually or at defined intervals).
  • Increase equals percentage change in CPI over the period.

Basic Formula

New Rent = Base Rent × (Current CPI ÷ Base CPI)

Example

  • Base Rent: $100,000 annually
  • Base CPI: 250
  • Current CPI: 260

Rent Increase = 260 ÷ 250 = 1.04 New Rent = $104,000

Common Variations

1) Annual CPI Adjustment

Rent adjusts each year based on prior year CPI change.

2) Cumulative CPI Adjustment

Adjustments occur at multi-year intervals (e.g., every 3 or 5 years).

3) CPI with Cap and Floor

  • Cap: Maximum annual increase (e.g., 5%).
  • Floor: Minimum increase (e.g., 2%).

Caps and floors limit volatility and provide predictability.

Advantages

  • Inflation protection.
  • Market-responsive rent growth.
  • Reduced negotiation frequency.
  • Attractive to institutional investors.

Risks

  • High inflation may burden tenants.
  • Deflation scenarios may reduce rent (unless floors apply).
  • Volatility without caps can affect tenant stability.

CPI Adjustment vs. Fixed Escalation

  • CPI Adjustment: Variable, inflation-based.
  • Fixed Escalation: Predetermined annual increase (e.g., 3%).

Fixed escalations provide certainty; CPI adjustments provide inflation alignment.

Investor Perspective

  • Hedges inflation risk in long-term leases.
  • Supports stable NOI growth.
  • May improve long-term valuation stability.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Confirm CPI index referenced in lease.
  • Verify cap and floor provisions.
  • Model inflation scenarios conservatively.
  • Assess tenant credit resilience in high-inflation environments.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ambiguous CPI index references.
  • Failure to account for compounding.
  • Overlooking caps or floors.
  • Ignoring timing of measurement periods.

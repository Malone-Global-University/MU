Unit 14: Development & Construction Economics

Understand site acquisition, feasibility analysis, pro forma modeling, construction budgeting, contingencies, and structured exit strategy design. This unit introduces how investors evaluate whether a project should be built, how development risk is priced, and why execution discipline matters.

Unit Overview

Development is one of the most complex areas of real estate investing because it combines land economics, design decisions, construction execution, financing, leasing risk, and market timing into a single project. Unlike stabilized acquisitions, development depends on future assumptions about cost, schedule, demand, and exit value, which makes disciplined planning essential.

This unit introduces the core economics of development and construction. Students will study how sites are acquired and evaluated, how feasibility is tested, how pro formas translate assumptions into projected returns, and how budgets and contingencies protect against inevitable uncertainty. The goal is to understand why development can generate high returns, but only when the project is structured with realism, margin, and a clear exit strategy.

What You’ll Learn

  • Explain how development differs from acquiring an already stabilized property
  • Recognize how site acquisition and feasibility analysis shape project viability
  • Interpret the purpose of a development pro forma and the assumptions that drive it
  • Understand how construction budgeting and contingencies protect against cost overruns and delays
  • Describe why exit strategy design is central to development economics and realized returns

Lessons in This Unit

  • Lesson 14.1: Development Overview

    Learn how ground-up development differs from acquisition investing and why development requires higher expected returns and tighter execution discipline.

  • Lesson 14.2: Site Acquisition and Feasibility

    Study how investors evaluate land, location, zoning, access, demand, and project fit before deciding whether a site is worth pursuing.

  • Lesson 14.3: Pro Forma Modeling

    Understand how development pro formas combine costs, timelines, rent assumptions, lease-up, and exit value into a projected return framework.

  • Lesson 14.4: Construction Budgeting

    Examine the major components of construction budgets, including hard costs, soft costs, financing costs, and cost-control discipline.

  • Lesson 14.5: Contingencies and Cost Overruns

    Learn why contingency reserves matter, how overruns occur, and how delays or scope changes can materially damage projected returns.

  • Lesson 14.6: Exit Strategy Design

    Study how sell, refinance, hold, or lease-up strategies affect development economics and why exit planning must be built into the project from the start.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to describe the main moving parts of a development project, explain how feasibility and budgets influence project viability, and identify why contingencies and exit planning are essential to risk control. These concepts prepare students for portfolio strategy, institutional capital discipline, and the capstone real estate plan.

  • Templates & Tools

    Use development checklists, feasibility worksheets, and simple pro forma models to practice evaluating project economics.

  • Glossary Support

    Review terms such as feasibility analysis, pro forma, hard costs, soft costs, contingency, lease-up, and exit strategy.

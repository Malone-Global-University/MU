Unit 4: Property Valuation Basics

Learn CMA logic, cost approach, income approach, and the difference between price and value in real markets. This unit introduces the major ways investors and appraisers think about value and explains why valuation is always part analysis, part judgment.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Real Estate Investing Track Unit 4: Property Valuation Basics

Unit Overview

Valuation sits at the center of disciplined real estate investing. Investors must constantly compare asking prices, recent comparable sales, replacement cost, and income potential in order to decide whether an asset is attractive, overpriced, or too uncertain to pursue.

This unit introduces the main valuation methods used in real estate: comparable sales analysis, the cost approach, and the introductory logic of the income approach. Students will also learn why market price and intrinsic value are not always the same, and why different buyers can assign different values to the same property based on their assumptions, strategy, and required return.

What You’ll Learn

  • Explain the difference between price and value in real estate markets
  • Use comparable sales logic to estimate value and recognize the limits of comparables
  • Describe how the cost approach estimates value through land and replacement cost
  • Interpret the basic logic of income-based valuation using NOI and cap rates
  • Recognize why valuation outcomes vary across investors, appraisers, and changing market conditions

Lessons in This Unit

  • Lesson 4.1: Price vs Value

    Learn why transaction price does not always equal intrinsic value and why disciplined investors focus on economic value rather than recent excitement.

  • Lesson 4.2: Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)

    Study how comparable sales are selected, adjusted, and interpreted when estimating property value in active markets.

  • Lesson 4.3: The Cost Approach

    Understand how land value, replacement cost, and depreciation are used to estimate value when comparable or income data are limited.

  • Lesson 4.4: The Income Approach (Introduction)

    Examine how income-producing property can be valued based on expected cash flow rather than physical features alone.

  • Lesson 4.5: Cap Rates and Valuation

    Learn how cap rates connect income to value and why small changes in market cap rates can have large effects on price.

  • Lesson 4.6: Why Valuations Diverge

    Study why appraisals, investor underwriting, and market pricing can differ because of assumptions, strategy, timing, and sentiment.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to compare simple valuation methods, interpret comparable sales data, and explain how income and market assumptions affect estimated value. These concepts create the foundation for later work in underwriting, acquisition decisions, financing, and portfolio analysis.

  • Templates & Tools

    Use valuation worksheets and comparison templates to practice comp analysis, cap rate calculations, and simple value estimation.

  • Glossary Support

    Review terms such as comparable sale, cost approach, income approach, cap rate, market value, and intrinsic value.

Lesson 7.5: Fixed vs Floating Rate Risk

Compare the stability of fixed-rate debt with the volatility and exposure that come with floating-rate borrowing in real estate finance.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Real Estate Investing Track Unit 7 Lesson 7.5

1. Lesson Introduction

Borrowing costs are one of the most important drivers of leveraged real estate performance. But debt is not defined only by size, term, or amortization. The interest rate structure also matters. Some loans use a fixed interest rate, meaning the borrowing cost stays the same for a defined period or for the full life of the loan. Others use a floating rate, meaning the cost of debt changes over time as market interest rates move.

This distinction has major consequences for risk. Fixed-rate debt offers predictability and payment stability, which can support conservative underwriting and long-term planning. Floating-rate debt may offer flexibility, lower initial pricing, or better fit for transitional assets, but it exposes borrowers to rate volatility and payment increases. Understanding this tradeoff is essential because interest rate movement can materially change debt service, coverage ratios, refinancing conditions, and overall investment outcomes.

Investor Insight:
A real estate loan is not only a source of capital. It is also a source of interest rate exposure that can either stabilize or destabilize the investment.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Define fixed-rate and floating-rate debt in real estate finance.
  • Explain how interest rate changes affect floating-rate borrowing costs.
  • Compare the benefits and risks of fixed-rate versus floating-rate structures.
  • Recognize when one rate structure may be more appropriate than the other.
  • Interpret how rate structure can change cash flow stability and downside risk.

3. Core Concepts

Fixed-Rate Debt

In a fixed-rate loan, the interest rate is set in advance and does not change during the defined fixed period. Because the rate stays constant, the borrower benefits from payment predictability and protection against rising market interest rates.

Floating-Rate Debt

In a floating-rate loan, the interest rate moves over time based on a reference benchmark plus a spread. If market rates rise, borrowing cost rises. If market rates fall, borrowing cost falls. This makes floating-rate debt more variable and more sensitive to macroeconomic conditions.

Payment Stability vs Payment Volatility

Fixed-rate debt generally supports stable debt service assumptions because the interest cost does not move unexpectedly. Floating-rate debt introduces uncertainty because future payment levels depend on future market rates. This can make cash flow more volatile even if property operations remain unchanged.

Why Floating-Rate Debt Exists

Floating-rate loans are not inherently inferior. They are often used when flexibility matters, when the loan is expected to be short term, or when the borrower expects to refinance, sell, or stabilize the property before rate exposure becomes severe. They can also be attractive if initial pricing is lower or if the borrower expects interest rates to decline.

Rate Risk Is a Real Estate Risk

Borrowers sometimes treat interest rate movement as a finance issue separate from the property itself. In reality, rate structure affects the property directly because higher borrowing costs reduce cash flow after debt service, weaken coverage ratios, and may impair refinance options. Rate risk is therefore also asset-level risk.

Optional Protection and Hedging

Some floating-rate borrowers seek protection through caps, collars, swaps, or other hedging tools. These can limit exposure to extreme rate movement, but they also introduce cost, complexity, and structural considerations of their own.

4. Mechanics

How Fixed-Rate Loans Behave

A fixed-rate loan allows the borrower to underwrite debt service with more confidence. If the loan rate is locked, then future debt payments are easier to forecast, assuming the loan terms themselves do not otherwise change.

How Floating-Rate Loans Behave

A floating-rate loan is typically expressed as:

Interest Rate = Benchmark Rate + Loan Spread

If the benchmark rises, the total borrowing cost rises. If the benchmark falls, the total borrowing cost falls. As a result, required debt service may shift even when loan principal is unchanged.

Example of Floating-Rate Sensitivity

Suppose a loan is priced at a benchmark plus 3.00%. If the benchmark is 2.00%, the total rate is 5.00%. If the benchmark later rises to 4.00%, the total rate becomes 7.00%. That change can materially increase interest expense and reduce cash flow available to the owner.

When Fixed-Rate Debt May Be Preferred

  • Long-term hold strategy
  • Stabilized income-producing asset
  • Need for payment certainty
  • Low tolerance for interest rate volatility

When Floating-Rate Debt May Be Preferred

  • Short-term hold or bridge strategy
  • Transitional asset expected to refinance soon
  • Need for prepayment flexibility
  • Borrower willing and able to manage rate exposure

Why Underwriting Must Stress Rates

Because floating-rate debt can become more expensive quickly, prudent underwriting often includes interest rate stress testing. Investors examine whether the property can still support debt service if rates increase materially above the starting assumption.

Rate Structure Principle:
Fixed-rate debt transfers more rate risk away from the borrower. Floating-rate debt leaves more of that risk with the borrower.

5. Worked Example

Consider two investors buying similar properties with similar loan sizes:

  • Investor A: Uses fixed-rate debt with stable scheduled payments.
  • Investor B: Uses floating-rate debt tied to a market benchmark.

Step 1: Initial Conditions

At closing, Investor B may have a slightly lower initial borrowing cost than Investor A. This makes the floating-rate loan appear attractive in the short run.

Step 2: Market Rates Rise

Over the following year, benchmark rates increase. Investor A’s debt cost remains unchanged because the rate is fixed. Investor B’s interest expense rises, increasing required debt service.

Step 3: Cash Flow Impact

The property’s rental income may not have changed much, but Investor B now has less cash flow after debt service. Debt coverage weakens, and the investment becomes more fragile.

Step 4: Strategic Consequences

If refinancing conditions are also worse because rates are higher, Investor B may face a double problem: weaker current cash flow and more difficult future refinancing. Investor A retains more predictability and planning stability.

Interpretation

Floating-rate debt can work well when the hold period is short, the business plan is transitional, or the borrower is well protected. But without strong cash flow cushion or hedging, it can expose the borrower to substantial rate-driven downside.

6. Real Estate Application

In real estate practice, the choice between fixed and floating debt is often shaped by the property stage, business plan, and sponsor risk tolerance. Stable assets and conservative investors often prefer fixed-rate certainty. Transitional strategies and more tactical borrowers may choose floating-rate flexibility.

Example: Stabilized Multifamily Hold

An investor buying a fully leased multifamily asset for long-term income may prefer fixed-rate debt because stable borrowing cost supports durable cash flow planning and reduces exposure to macro rate shocks.

Example: Value-Add Repositioning

A sponsor acquiring a transitional property with a short bridge plan may accept floating-rate debt if the intention is to renovate, lease up, and refinance quickly. In that case, flexibility may matter more than long-term payment certainty.

Example: Rate Shock and Distress

A property financed with floating-rate debt may perform adequately at origination but become stressed when benchmark rates rise sharply. Even if occupancy remains decent, higher interest expense can erode DSCR, reduce distributable cash flow, and pressure the borrower to inject capital or sell.

Example: Hedged Floating-Rate Structure

Some borrowers use floating-rate debt together with an interest rate cap. This can make the structure more resilient by limiting extreme borrowing cost increases, though it does not eliminate all risk or cost.

Investor Insight:
Borrowers should choose rate structure based on how long they need the debt, how stable the asset is, and how much payment volatility they can survive.

7. Common Mistakes

  • Choosing floating-rate debt only because the initial rate is lower: Lower starting cost can hide substantial future exposure.
  • Assuming rates will stay favorable: Interest rates can move quickly and materially change deal economics.
  • Ignoring rate sensitivity in underwriting: Deals should be tested under higher-rate scenarios.
  • Using floating-rate debt without adequate reserves or hedging: Payment volatility can overwhelm weakly capitalized borrowers.
  • Using fixed-rate debt when near-term flexibility is essential: Stable pricing can come with prepayment constraints or less tactical freedom.

8. Knowledge Check

  1. What is the main difference between fixed-rate and floating-rate debt?
  2. Why does floating-rate borrowing create payment uncertainty?
  3. What types of assets or strategies often fit fixed-rate debt better?
  4. Why might a borrower still choose floating-rate debt?
  5. How can rising interest rates weaken real estate investment performance?

9. Practical Exercise

Compare these two simplified borrowing options for the same property:

  • Option A: Fixed-rate loan with stable payment structure.
  • Option B: Floating-rate loan with a lower starting rate but exposure to benchmark increases.

Complete the following:

  1. List at least three advantages of Option A.
  2. List at least three possible reasons an investor might choose Option B.
  3. Explain how rising rates would affect Option B more than Option A.
  4. State which option appears safer for a long-term stabilized hold.
  5. Write 4 to 6 sentences explaining why floating-rate debt can be appropriate in some cases but dangerous in others.

10. Key Takeaways

  • Fixed-rate debt provides predictable borrowing cost and payment stability.
  • Floating-rate debt changes with market benchmarks and exposes borrowers to interest rate volatility.
  • Fixed-rate loans are often better suited to long-term stable assets and conservative cash flow planning.
  • Floating-rate loans may fit short-term or transitional strategies that need flexibility.
  • Interest rate structure materially affects debt service, DSCR, refinance risk, and overall investment fragility.

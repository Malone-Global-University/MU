Lesson 23.2: Financial Covenants and Ratio Tests

Study how lenders monitor leverage, coverage, liquidity, and other financial thresholds to detect weakening borrower performance.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Credit & Lending Operations Track Unit 23: Covenant Monitoring Foundations Lesson 23.2

Where This Lesson Fits

Lesson 23.1 introduced covenant monitoring as the process lenders use to track borrower condition after funding. Once reporting materials arrive and monitoring deadlines are tracked, the next step is to evaluate borrower performance.

Financial covenants provide one of the primary tools lenders use for this evaluation. They translate borrower performance into measurable ratios that can be tested at regular intervals.

Lesson 23.2 explains how financial covenants function and how lenders use ratio tests to identify early signs of credit deterioration.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how lenders monitor borrower financial performance using covenant ratio tests such as leverage, coverage, and liquidity thresholds.

Lesson Overview

Financial covenants establish measurable financial boundaries that borrowers must maintain during the life of a loan. These boundaries are usually expressed through ratios calculated from the borrower's financial statements.

Each ratio measures a different aspect of financial health. Some ratios evaluate debt levels relative to income. Others measure the ability to service debt payments. Some examine short-term liquidity or financial stability.

Together, these ratios allow lenders to monitor borrower performance in a structured and consistent way.

Why Financial Covenants Are Used

Lenders cannot rely solely on repayment history to evaluate risk. A borrower may continue making payments while financial conditions gradually weaken.

Financial covenants provide a method for detecting problems earlier. Instead of waiting for missed payments, lenders monitor financial indicators that reflect the borrower's underlying business condition.

If those indicators begin to deteriorate, the lender may identify potential risk long before repayment failure occurs.

Leverage Covenants

Leverage covenants measure the relationship between a borrower's debt and its earnings or assets. These ratios help lenders determine whether the borrower is carrying a manageable amount of debt.

A common leverage covenant compares total debt to earnings measures such as EBITDA. If debt grows faster than earnings, the ratio worsens and may eventually breach the covenant threshold.

Because leverage ratios measure overall financial burden, they are often used as an early indicator of financial stress.

Coverage Covenants

Coverage covenants measure the borrower's ability to meet required financial obligations using available income or cash flow.

A typical coverage test compares operating earnings or cash flow to required debt service payments. If income declines or debt obligations increase, coverage may fall below the required level.

Coverage covenants therefore evaluate whether the borrower continues to generate sufficient resources to repay the loan.

Liquidity Covenants

Liquidity covenants focus on the borrower's short-term financial flexibility. They measure whether the borrower has sufficient liquid resources to meet near-term obligations.

These covenants may compare current assets to current liabilities or require the borrower to maintain a minimum cash balance or working capital level.

Liquidity covenants are particularly important when a borrower's business depends on stable cash availability for operations.

Thresholds and Covenant Limits

Each financial covenant includes a threshold that the borrower must maintain. These thresholds reflect the lender's view of acceptable financial performance.

For example:

  • A leverage ratio may not exceed a defined maximum.
  • A coverage ratio may not fall below a defined minimum.
  • A liquidity measure may require maintaining a minimum balance.

These limits establish clear boundaries that convert financial analysis into enforceable monitoring rules.

Testing Financial Covenants

Financial covenants are tested periodically using borrower financial reports. The lender calculates the required ratios using figures defined in the credit agreement.

These calculations may occur quarterly, semi-annually, or annually depending on the loan structure.

Once the ratios are calculated, the results are compared with covenant thresholds to determine whether the borrower remains in compliance.

Interpreting Covenant Results

Covenant results do more than determine compliance or violation. They also help lenders evaluate trends in borrower performance.

Even if a borrower remains technically compliant, declining ratios may signal rising risk. Monitoring these trends allows lenders to discuss performance with the borrower and consider adjustments before a covenant breach occurs.

This trend awareness is one of the reasons financial covenants are valuable monitoring tools.

Real-World Example

A manufacturing company receives a term loan that includes two financial covenants:

  • A maximum leverage ratio of 4.0× debt to EBITDA
  • A minimum interest coverage ratio of 2.5×

Each quarter the borrower submits financial statements and a compliance certificate.

The lender calculates the ratios based on the defined formula in the loan agreement. During one quarter the leverage ratio increases from 3.2× to 3.8× while coverage falls slightly.

Although the borrower remains compliant, the trend indicates increasing financial pressure. The lender may begin discussing performance with management to understand the cause and monitor the situation closely.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Viewing covenant tests as pass-or-fail only

Trend analysis is often just as important as the compliance result.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the definitions in the credit agreement

Covenant calculations depend on specific formula definitions that may differ from standard accounting measures.

Mistake 3: Monitoring only one financial metric

Effective covenant structures usually combine multiple ratios to capture different aspects of financial risk.

Key Terms

Financial Covenant — A loan agreement requirement that the borrower maintain specified financial performance levels.

Leverage Ratio — A measure comparing debt levels to earnings or assets to assess financial burden.

Coverage Ratio — A measure comparing income or cash flow to required debt service obligations.

Liquidity Covenant — A requirement that a borrower maintain sufficient short-term financial resources.

Covenant Threshold — The minimum or maximum level required for a financial covenant ratio.

Lesson Summary

  • Financial covenants measure borrower financial performance using defined ratios.
  • Leverage ratios evaluate overall debt burden.
  • Coverage ratios assess the borrower's ability to service debt obligations.
  • Liquidity covenants measure short-term financial stability.
  • Ratio testing helps lenders detect early signs of weakening borrower condition.

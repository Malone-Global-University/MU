Wealth & Asset Operations Track

Unit 16: Reconciliation Systems and Break Management Infrastructure

Reconciliation ensures that cash and positions recorded in systems match actual holdings. Breaks must be detected, investigated, and resolved to maintain operational accuracy.

This unit examines the infrastructure and tools used to reconcile, match, and escalate discrepancies across financial operations.

Where This Unit Fits

After understanding portfolio positions, pricing, and settlement, the next critical operational layer is reconciliation. This ensures that all account and trade data are accurate and consistent across systems.

Unit 16 focuses on reconciliation systems, break detection, and the workflows needed to manage exceptions effectively.

Unit Overview

Reconciliation is the process of comparing internal records against external sources to ensure accuracy. Systems detect discrepancies between expected and actual cash balances or positions, flagging breaks for investigation.

Students will learn how cash and position reconciliation tools operate, the logic used to identify and match breaks, and the workflows for resolving exceptions through automated and manual processes.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

Reconciliation protects both the firm and clients by ensuring data integrity, accurate reporting, and compliance with regulatory requirements. Breaks that go unresolved can cause financial losses, operational failures, or regulatory issues.

Operations staff rely on reconciliation systems to detect, investigate, and resolve discrepancies efficiently, and escalation workflows ensure that complex issues receive appropriate attention.

What You'll Learn

Core Concepts

  • The purpose and importance of reconciliation
  • Cash reconciliation systems and logic
  • Position reconciliation systems and matching
  • Break detection mechanisms and exception identification
  • Tools for automated and manual reconciliation
  • Workflow and escalation systems for unresolved breaks

Operational Competencies

  • Explain how reconciliation supports operational accuracy and risk management
  • Operate and analyze cash and position reconciliation tools
  • Identify and investigate breaks using system logic
  • Apply exception management protocols and workflows
  • Distinguish between automated and manual reconciliation processes

Institutional Questions This Unit Helps Answer

  • Why is reconciliation critical to operational integrity?
  • How are cash and positions reconciled across systems?
  • How do reconciliation systems detect and flag breaks?
  • What tools and workflows ensure breaks are resolved efficiently?
  • How does escalation of unresolved issues maintain control and compliance?

Lessons in This Unit

  • Lesson 16.1: Purpose of Reconciliation

    Understand why reconciliation is essential to operational and regulatory integrity.

  • Lesson 16.2: Cash Reconciliation Systems

    Examine tools and processes used to reconcile cash balances across ledgers and accounts.

  • Lesson 16.3: Position Reconciliation Systems

    Learn how securities positions are reconciled between internal records and external sources.

  • Lesson 16.4: Break Detection and Matching Logic

    Study the logic and algorithms used to detect discrepancies in cash and position data.

  • Lesson 16.5: Exception Management Tools

    Explore systems used to manage, track, and resolve identified breaks or exceptions.

  • Lesson 16.6: Automated vs Manual Reconciliation

    Compare automated reconciliation systems with manual processes and understand trade-offs.

  • Lesson 16.7: Escalation and Workflow Systems

    Learn how unresolved breaks are escalated through defined workflows for resolution and control.

Practical Application

Students will be able to operate reconciliation systems, identify and investigate breaks, apply exception management protocols, and ensure workflow escalation is followed.

This prepares students to maintain operational integrity and mitigate risks associated with cash and position discrepancies.

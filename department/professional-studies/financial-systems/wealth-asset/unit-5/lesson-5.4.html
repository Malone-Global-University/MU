Lesson 5.4: Separately Managed Accounts and Direct Ownership Programs

Understand how managed account structures give clients portfolio exposure through individually held positions rather than shared pooled ownership.

Where This Lesson Fits

This lesson follows the study of pooled funds and exchange-traded wrappers by introducing a different product structure: direct ownership through separately managed accounts and related managed programs. Earlier lessons in this unit explained how pooled vehicles allow many investors to access a shared portfolio. This lesson shows what changes when the investor or account directly holds the underlying positions.

This distinction is central in wealth and asset operations. A client may seek diversified exposure in a mutual fund or ETF, but another client may want a portfolio built directly inside the client’s own account. That shift changes ownership, trading, reporting, servicing, tax handling, and operational oversight.

Students should leave this lesson understanding that separately managed accounts are not just another product label. They are a different operating model in which the account itself becomes the place where portfolio implementation occurs.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how separately managed accounts and direct ownership programs work, describe how direct holdings differ from pooled ownership, and identify the operational consequences of managing portfolios at the individual account level.

Lesson Overview

A separately managed account, often called an SMA, is an investment structure in which a portfolio manager or advisory program manages assets directly inside a specific client account. Instead of buying shares in a shared fund, the client holds the underlying securities individually through a custodial or brokerage account.

This means the client’s portfolio exists as its own account-level set of positions. If the strategy calls for ownership of certain stocks, bonds, or other securities, those positions are generally recorded directly in the client account rather than indirectly through a pooled vehicle. The client therefore has direct ownership of the holdings, even if the investment decisions are delegated to an advisor or manager.

Direct ownership programs are used because they can offer greater account-level visibility, customized restrictions, tailored tax treatment, and portfolio management tied to the specific client account. At the same time, they require more account-level administration than pooled products because each account must be traded, monitored, reconciled, and serviced individually.

For wealth and asset operations, this structure changes nearly every part of the workflow. Account implementation, rebalancing, reporting, billing, and client servicing all occur at the direct-holdings level rather than through a shared fund framework.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

Separately managed accounts are important because they sit at the intersection of portfolio management and account administration. Operational teams must support not just a product subscription or redemption, but the ongoing management of actual positions inside client accounts.

This creates a different servicing environment from pooled funds. Trade allocation, account onboarding, restriction coding, tax lot tracking, household reporting, cash management, fee billing, and exception handling can all become more detailed in direct ownership programs. Since each account may hold its own set of securities, operational consistency depends on disciplined account-level controls.

This lesson matters because many advisory and wealth programs rely on managed accounts rather than only fund products. To understand modern wealth operations, students must be able to distinguish pooled servicing from direct account servicing.

Core Concept

A separately managed account is a client-specific investment account in which underlying portfolio positions are held directly for that client and managed according to a stated strategy, mandate, or advisory program.

The defining feature is direct ownership. The client does not merely own a share of a pooled vehicle. The account itself holds the securities. This allows portfolio management to occur at the level of the individual account, which can support customization and account-specific oversight.

In wealth and asset operations, this means the administrative unit is the account, not the fund. That difference drives the trading, reporting, servicing, and reconciliation model.

How Separately Managed Accounts Work

The basic structure of a separately managed account can be understood through a sequence of elements:

  • Client account — A custodial or brokerage account is opened for the investor.
  • Strategy assignment — The account is linked to a portfolio strategy, manager, or model.
  • Direct position ownership — Securities are purchased directly into that account in the client’s name or beneficial ownership structure.
  • Account-level trading — Rebalancing, cash deployment, tax-sensitive adjustments, and restrictions are handled at the account level.
  • Custody and records — The account custodian maintains records of the actual securities held in the account.
  • Reporting and billing — Performance, fees, statements, and service activity are tied to the client-specific account.
  • Ongoing oversight — Operations teams monitor drift, exceptions, cash flows, corporate actions, and servicing issues for each managed account.

This structure makes managed accounts flexible, but it also creates a more individualized operational environment than pooled products.

Direct Ownership vs. Pooled Ownership

The clearest way to understand separately managed accounts is to compare them with pooled vehicles. In a pooled structure, the fund owns the underlying securities and the investor owns shares in the fund. In a separately managed account, the client account holds the securities directly.

This difference affects many operational outcomes. Direct ownership allows account-level restrictions, such as excluding certain securities or sectors. It can also support tax lot management and account-specific gain or loss realization. By contrast, pooled structures centralize investment and administration at the fund level rather than at the client account level.

Direct ownership may create more visibility and customization, but it generally involves more account-level complexity in trading, monitoring, and servicing. That is why managed account programs require strong operating discipline.

Operational Workflow

In practice, a separately managed account program may operate through a workflow like this:

  1. A client opens or links a custodial account and agrees to a managed account program or discretionary strategy.
  2. The institution assigns the account to a portfolio model, manager, or strategy sleeve.
  3. Cash is funded into the account and the manager implements the strategy by purchasing securities directly into that account.
  4. Operations teams coordinate trade allocation, settlement, account setup rules, restrictions, and exception management.
  5. Over time, the account is rebalanced, monitored for drift, and adjusted for cash flows, withdrawals, tax events, or client changes.
  6. The client receives account-specific statements, performance reporting, fee disclosures, and servicing support based on direct holdings.

This workflow makes clear that the managed account is both an investment structure and an account administration structure.

Real-World Example

Imagine a high-net-worth client who wants a U.S. equity strategy but does not want exposure to certain industries and wants the ability to manage taxable gains more carefully. A pooled fund may not easily support those preferences because all shareholders enter the same shared structure.

Instead, the client is placed into a separately managed account. The portfolio manager applies a model strategy, but the actual stocks are purchased directly into the client’s account. Certain names can be excluded, tax lots can be reviewed, and performance can be reported specifically for that client’s holdings.

This example shows why managed accounts are useful in wealth systems. They allow institutions to deliver professional portfolio management while keeping the account itself as the direct holding and servicing unit.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Treating a separately managed account like a fund

A separately managed account is not a pooled share structure. It is a client-specific account with direct holdings.

Mistake 2: Assuming direct ownership removes operational complexity

Direct ownership often increases account-level operational work because each account may require customized handling and oversight.

Mistake 3: Ignoring the role of custodial and account administration systems

Managed account programs depend on custody, trade processing, reporting, billing, and exception controls at the client account level.

Mistake 4: Believing customization is free of tradeoffs

Greater account-level flexibility can support client needs, but it usually requires more detailed operations, monitoring, and servicing.

Key Terms

Separately Managed Account (SMA) — A client-specific account in which a portfolio manager implements an investment strategy through directly held securities.

Direct Ownership — A structure in which the investor or account holds the underlying securities directly rather than through a pooled fund share.

Managed Account Program — An advisory or discretionary structure in which portfolios are managed inside individual client accounts.

Custodian — The institution that holds and records client assets and supports settlement, safekeeping, and account administration.

Account-Level Trading — Portfolio implementation and adjustments executed within a specific client account rather than at a pooled fund level.

Restriction Coding — Account rules that limit or exclude certain securities, sectors, or trading actions in a managed account.

Tax Lot Management — The tracking and management of individual purchase lots for tax reporting and gain-or-loss realization purposes.

Lesson Summary

  • Separately managed accounts deliver portfolio exposure through directly held securities inside client-specific accounts.
  • They differ from pooled products because the account, not the fund, is the primary ownership and servicing unit.
  • Direct ownership can support customization, restrictions, and account-level tax handling.
  • Managed account programs require detailed custody, trading, billing, reporting, and exception management processes.
  • Understanding the difference between pooled and direct structures is essential in wealth and asset operations.

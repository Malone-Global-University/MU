Unit 5: Investment Vehicles and Product Structures

Learn how investment exposure is delivered through different product forms. This unit introduces pooled funds, managed accounts, product wrappers, and distribution structures so students can understand how portfolios are implemented in operational practice.

Where This Unit Fits

This unit follows portfolio construction in Unit 4 and moves from allocation logic into the practical product forms through which investment exposure is delivered. After learning how portfolios are designed, students now examine how those portfolios are implemented using real investment vehicles and account-based structures.

Students begin here because later units on trading, subscriptions and redemptions, account administration, fee processing, reporting, and product servicing all depend on understanding the operational form of the investments being held. This unit bridges investment strategy and day-to-day operational support.

Unit Overview

Investors rarely access markets through raw securities alone. In wealth and asset environments, exposure is often delivered through product structures such as mutual funds, exchange-traded funds, separately managed accounts, retirement products, model-driven programs, and other wrappers that organize investment access, administration, reporting, and servicing.

This unit introduces the major investment vehicles used across wealth and asset operations. Students study the difference between owning a security directly and accessing exposure through a product, how pooled and non-pooled structures differ, and why operational treatment changes depending on the product form. The goal is to help students understand not just what is invested, but how it is packaged, distributed, and maintained.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

Different product structures create different workflows. A direct equity holding is serviced differently from a mutual fund position. A separately managed account operates differently from a unified managed account or insurance-based wrapper. Operations teams need to understand these distinctions because transaction handling, valuation timing, fees, statements, eligibility, and client communications can all depend on the investment vehicle.

In practical terms, students who understand this unit are better prepared to interpret why two portfolios with similar economic exposure may behave differently operationally, why some positions trade intraday while others process at end-of-day, and why product structure often determines the servicing model behind the client experience.

What You’ll Learn

Core Concepts

  • How investment exposure can be delivered through multiple product forms
  • The difference between direct holdings, pooled vehicles, and managed account structures
  • Why product wrappers affect trading, servicing, reporting, and fee treatment
  • How distribution channels influence the way products are offered to clients
  • Why operational complexity often depends on product design rather than investment idea alone
  • How product structure connects portfolio strategy to account-level execution

Operational Competencies

  • Identify major investment vehicles used in wealth and asset operations
  • Explain how pooled products differ from direct and separately managed structures
  • Recognize why product format changes workflow, servicing, and reporting requirements
  • Describe how investment wrappers shape client account administration
  • Use product structure knowledge to support later study of transactions, fees, reporting, and controls

Institutional Questions This Unit Helps Answer

  • Why do clients access similar investments through different product structures?
  • How is a mutual fund operationally different from a separately managed account?
  • Why do some products have different liquidity, pricing, and reporting mechanics?
  • How does product design affect the work performed by wealth and asset operations teams?

Lessons in This Unit

Product Foundations

  • Lesson 5.1: What Investment Vehicles Do

    Learn how wealth and asset institutions package investment exposure through different vehicles so portfolios can be implemented, distributed, administered, and serviced efficiently.

  • Lesson 5.2: Mutual Funds and Pooled Investment Products

    Study how pooled vehicles gather investor assets into a shared structure and why mutual funds and similar products create distinct pricing, ownership, and servicing mechanics.

  • Lesson 5.3: Exchange-Traded Funds and Market-Traded Wrappers

    Examine how exchange-traded products combine pooled exposure with market-based trading and why this hybrid structure changes the way positions are bought, sold, and recorded.

  • Lesson 5.4: Separately Managed Accounts and Direct Ownership Programs

    Understand how managed account structures give clients portfolio exposure through individually held positions rather than shared pooled ownership.

Wrappers, Distribution, and Operational Structure

  • Lesson 5.5: Retirement, Insurance, and Account-Based Wrappers

    Learn how retirement accounts, annuity structures, and other wrappers shape investment access, tax treatment, restrictions, and operational servicing.

  • Lesson 5.6: Product Distribution, Platforms, and Access Channels

    Study how firms distribute products through custodians, advisory platforms, retirement systems, broker-dealers, and other channels that shape operational handling.

  • Lesson 5.7: Bringing Product Structures Together

    Connect pooled funds, traded wrappers, managed accounts, and account-based product structures into one operating picture so students can see how investment delivery works across wealth and asset operations.

Connected Units

  • Unit 4: Portfolio Construction and Allocation Basics

    Use the allocation logic from Unit 4 to understand why portfolios may be implemented through different vehicles even when the investment objective is similar.

  • Unit 6: Market Structure and Trading Basics

    Build on product structure knowledge by studying how different investment vehicles interact with trading, execution, and market access workflows.

  • Unit 8: Client Accounts and Account Administration

    Apply product and wrapper knowledge when studying how different account types hold, restrict, and service different investment structures.

Study Support

  • Templates & Tools

    Use product comparison charts and structure maps to distinguish pooled funds, managed accounts, wrappers, and distribution channels.

  • Glossary Support

    Review key terms such as mutual fund, ETF, managed account, wrapper, annuity, platform, pooled vehicle, and distribution channel.

  • Case Examples

    Study practical scenarios showing how similar investment strategies can be delivered through different products with different operational consequences.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to identify the major product structures used in wealth and asset environments, explain the difference between pooled and non-pooled vehicles, describe how wrappers and access channels affect servicing, and use that understanding to interpret later workflows in trading, account support, reporting, and fee administration.

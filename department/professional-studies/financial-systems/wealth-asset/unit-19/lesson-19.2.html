Where This Lesson Fits

Lesson 19.1 established the taxonomy of asset transfers and identified ACAT as the primary infrastructure for external in-kind transfers between U.S. broker-dealers and custodians. That lesson defined what ACAT is and when it applies; this lesson explains how it works. The ACAT system is the most operationally significant transfer mechanism in retail and institutional wealth management, processing millions of account transfers annually and governing the majority of client transitions between advisory relationships. Any operations professional working in this industry will encounter ACAT transfers regularly — as both the receiving firm accepting client assets and the delivering firm releasing them — and must understand the system's rules, timelines, and message structure in enough detail to process transfers correctly, identify exceptions early, and resolve problems before they escalate to client-visible delays.

This lesson forms the technical core of Unit 19. The workflows, failure modes, and exception scenarios discussed in Lessons 19.3 through 19.7 all presuppose a working understanding of the ACAT framework. Inbound and outbound transfer distinctions (Lesson 19.3) map directly onto the receiving firm and delivering firm roles defined here. Students who master this lesson are positioned to absorb the remainder of the unit with a clear operational foundation under each subsequent topic.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to describe the regulatory foundation of the ACAT system and the obligations it imposes on participating firms; explain the six-stage ACAT transfer process from initiation through completion and identify the timeline requirements at each stage; distinguish the roles and obligations of the receiving and delivering firms throughout the ACAT process; identify the categories of assets that are eligible and ineligible for ACAT transfer; describe the primary ACAT message types and their function in the transfer workflow; and explain how ACAT exceptions and rejections are categorized and what resolution procedures apply to each.

Lesson Overview

The Automated Customer Account Transfer Service — universally known as ACAT — is the electronic system operated by the Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation (DTCC) that automates the transfer of customer brokerage accounts between participating firms in the United States. ACAT was introduced in the 1980s to replace a manual, paper-intensive transfer process that regularly took weeks or months to complete and generated enormous operational backlogs at both delivering and receiving firms. The system standardized the message formats, timelines, and procedures through which accounts move between firms, dramatically reducing transfer times and creating an enforceable regulatory framework that gives clients predictable rights when changing custodians.

The regulatory foundation for ACAT is FINRA Rule 11870, which governs customer account transfers between member firms. Rule 11870 establishes the six-business-day completion standard, defines the obligations of delivering and receiving firms at each stage, specifies the circumstances under which a delivering firm may object to a transfer, and creates enforcement mechanisms for firms that fail to comply with transfer obligations. The rule reflects a fundamental policy judgment: that clients have the right to transfer their accounts to a firm of their choosing within a defined and reasonable timeframe, and that delivering firms may not use the transfer process as a mechanism to retain assets by creating delay, confusion, or unjustified objections.

Operationally, ACAT functions as a message-passing system. The receiving firm initiates a transfer request message; the delivering firm validates the account information and responds with either a validation confirmation or a rejection; the receiving firm reviews any position-level data transmitted by the delivering firm and confirms the transfer; the delivering firm delivers assets to the receiving firm's DTC participant account; and the receiving firm confirms receipt. Each step in this sequence generates a specific ACAT message type, and each message carries a defined response deadline. Firms that miss response deadlines are subject to FINRA inquiry; firms that routinely fail to meet ACAT timeline requirements face regulatory action.

Asset eligibility is a critical dimension of the ACAT process. Not all assets held in a brokerage or advisory account are eligible for ACAT transfer. ACAT handles DTC-eligible securities — exchange-listed equities, most exchange-traded funds, most corporate and municipal bonds, and many mutual funds. It does not handle certificated securities held outside DTC, certain proprietary products that the delivering firm exclusively sponsors, alternative investments with transfer restrictions, assets held in physical form, or positions in products that require separate registration or approval at the receiving firm. When a transfer request includes non-ACAT-eligible assets, those assets generate exceptions that must be resolved through alternative procedures outside the standard ACAT workflow.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

ACAT compliance is a direct regulatory obligation for every FINRA member firm that participates in the system. Delivering firms that fail to validate transfer requests within one business day, fail to deliver assets within three business days of validation, or reject transfers without a valid regulatory basis are subject to FINRA examination findings and enforcement. Receiving firms that fail to confirm transfers within required timeframes or that submit erroneous transfer requests generate the same scrutiny. Operations teams responsible for ACAT processing must understand not just the mechanics of the system but the specific regulatory obligations that attach to each step — because non-compliance is not merely an operational failure, it is a regulatory violation.

ACAT is also one of the highest-volume operational workflows at most wealth management firms. A firm with an active advisor force and competitive pricing relative to legacy custodians will receive large volumes of inbound transfer requests from clients transitioning from other firms, while simultaneously processing outbound transfers for clients who leave. Managing this volume — tracking open transfers, responding to validation requests within deadline, escalating exceptions before they age past their resolution window, and confirming deliveries accurately — requires systematic processes and dedicated operational attention. Firms that manage ACAT volume well protect client relationships at both the onboarding and offboarding stage. Firms that manage it poorly create delays, generate client complaints, and expose themselves to regulatory findings that compound the reputational damage.

From a client experience perspective, ACAT is the primary mechanism through which a new advisory relationship is funded. The client's decision to transfer is already made by the time the ACAT process begins; what the operations team does with that process determines whether the client's first experience with the new firm is seamless or frustrating. A transfer that completes within the six-business-day standard, with no unexpected liquidations and accurate delivery of all expected positions, confirms the client's decision. A transfer that stalls, generates unexplained exceptions, or takes three weeks to complete raises immediate doubts about whether the firm is capable of managing the client's assets with the competence the advisor promised.

Core Concept

FINRA Rule 11870 — The FINRA rule governing customer account transfers between member firms, establishing the six-business-day completion standard, defining the obligations of delivering and receiving firms at each stage of the ACAT process, specifying the valid grounds for transfer objection, and providing the enforcement framework for non-compliant transfer behavior.

Receiving Firm — In an ACAT transfer, the firm to which assets are being transferred. The receiving firm initiates the ACAT process by submitting a transfer request on behalf of the client, reviews position data provided by the delivering firm, and confirms receipt of delivered assets upon completion. The receiving firm bears primary responsibility for initiating the transfer correctly and maintaining the transfer timeline.

Delivering Firm — In an ACAT transfer, the firm from which assets are being transferred. The delivering firm validates the transfer request against its account records, transmits position data to the receiving firm, and delivers all transferable assets within the required three-business-day delivery window. The delivering firm may object to a transfer only on specific regulatory grounds.

These three concepts define the core regulatory and role structure of the ACAT system. Rule 11870 is the authority; the receiving and delivering firm roles define who does what at each stage. Understanding the obligations of each role is the prerequisite for understanding every operational procedure and exception scenario that follows.

ACAT System Architecture and Message Types

The ACAT system operates as an electronic message-passing platform through which receiving and delivering firms exchange standardized instructions and confirmations at each stage of the transfer process. Each message type corresponds to a specific action in the transfer lifecycle and carries defined response obligations.

  • Transfer Initiation Request (TIR) — The initial message submitted by the receiving firm to the ACAT system identifying the client account to be transferred. The TIR includes the client's account number at the delivering firm, tax identification number, account title, and the type of transfer requested (full or partial). An accurate TIR is essential: errors in account identification cause the delivering firm to reject the request at validation, restarting the clock.
  • Account Validation — The delivering firm's response to a TIR, confirming that the account information in the request matches its records. The delivering firm has one business day to respond to a TIR with either a validation (confirming the account) or a rejection (citing a specific discrepancy). A validated TIR advances the transfer to the position transmission stage.
  • Position Transmission — Following validation, the delivering firm transmits a complete list of all positions in the transferring account — including security identifier, quantity, and cost basis where available — to the ACAT system, which makes this data available to the receiving firm. The receiving firm reviews the position data and identifies any assets that cannot be accepted, generating asset-level objections for non-transferable positions.
  • Transfer Confirmation — The receiving firm's confirmation that it accepts the positions transmitted by the delivering firm and is ready to receive delivery. This confirmation activates the delivering firm's three-business-day delivery obligation.
  • Asset Delivery — The delivering firm's physical movement of assets — via DTC book-entry transfer for DTC-eligible securities — to the receiving firm's DTC participant account within three business days of the transfer confirmation. Cash balances are transmitted via wire transfer on the same timeline.
  • Delivery Confirmation and Completion — The receiving firm confirms receipt of all delivered assets through the ACAT system, closing the transfer. Any assets not delivered by the deadline are flagged as exceptions requiring separate resolution.
  • Rejection and Objection Messages — At any stage of the process, either firm may generate rejection or objection messages. The delivering firm may reject a TIR for valid account discrepancies; the receiving firm may object to specific assets that cannot be accepted. Each rejection and objection type has a defined resolution procedure that must be followed within specified timeframes.
  • Residual Credit Messages — After transfer completion, the delivering firm transmits residual credits — dividends, interest payments, or other proceeds that arrive after the account has been transferred — to the receiving firm for crediting to the client's new account. Residual credit processing is an ongoing obligation that can extend weeks beyond the primary transfer completion date.

The ACAT message structure imposes discipline on both firms: each message creates a response obligation with a defined deadline, and each deadline missed is a regulatory event. Operations teams at both firms must monitor incoming ACAT messages continuously, respond within required timeframes, and escalate exceptions before deadlines expire.

ACAT Timeline and Firm Obligations by Stage

FINRA Rule 11870 establishes the six-business-day completion standard and defines specific obligations and deadlines for each stage of the process. Understanding where the timeline stands at any point in the transfer requires knowing which stage is active and what the next deadline is.

  • Day 0: Transfer Request Submission — The receiving firm submits the Transfer Initiation Request to the ACAT system. The clock starts from the date the TIR is submitted. The receiving firm bears responsibility for the accuracy of all account identification data in the TIR; an inaccurate TIR that is rejected by the delivering firm does not restart at the original submission date — the clock restarts from the date of the corrected TIR.
  • Day 1: Account Validation Deadline — The delivering firm must validate or reject the TIR within one business day of receipt. A validation advances the transfer; a rejection must cite a specific valid reason — account number mismatch, name mismatch, account type mismatch — and must include enough information for the receiving firm to correct and resubmit. A delivering firm that fails to respond by the Day 1 deadline is in violation of Rule 11870.
  • Days 1–2: Position Transmission and Receiving Firm Review — Following validation, the delivering firm transmits position data and the receiving firm reviews it for acceptability. The receiving firm identifies any positions it cannot accept and communicates asset-level objections. This review period is the last opportunity for the receiving firm to identify non-transferable assets before delivery is triggered.
  • Day 3: Transfer Confirmation — The receiving firm submits its transfer confirmation, signaling that it accepts the positions and is ready for delivery. This confirmation starts the three-business-day delivery window. Delays in submitting the transfer confirmation extend the overall transfer timeline proportionally.
  • Days 4–6: Asset Delivery — The delivering firm delivers all transferable assets via DTC book-entry and wire transfer within three business days of the transfer confirmation. Rule 11870 requires delivery no later than three business days after the transfer confirmation, meaning a transfer confirmed on Day 3 must be delivered no later than Day 6. The delivering firm may not withhold delivery of any transferable assets beyond this deadline.
  • Day 6: Completion and Residual Processing — The receiving firm confirms receipt, closing the transfer. Any non-delivered assets become open exceptions requiring resolution outside the primary ACAT workflow. Residual credit processing begins and continues until all post-transfer proceeds from the delivering firm's records have been transmitted.

The six-business-day timeline is a maximum, not a target. Many ACAT transfers complete in three to four business days when both firms process messages promptly and no exceptions arise. Operations teams that treat the six-day deadline as a ceiling to work within, rather than a standard to achieve well before, consistently produce transfers that clients experience as routine and professional. Teams that routinely use every available day in the timeline — arriving at Day 6 with last-minute delivery confirmations — have no margin to resolve exceptions without blowing the deadline.

ACAT-Eligible vs. Non-ACAT-Eligible Assets

Not all assets held in a brokerage or advisory account can move through the ACAT system. The eligibility distinction is one of the most practically important in transfer operations because non-eligible assets require alternative handling — and the failure to identify them before initiating a transfer is one of the most common sources of transfer exceptions, delays, and client confusion.

ACAT-eligible assets are those held at DTC in street name and identified by a valid CUSIP or ISIN. This category encompasses the vast majority of publicly traded securities: NYSE- and Nasdaq-listed equities, most exchange-traded funds, most publicly offered mutual funds (including those available through the NSCC Fund/SERV system), most corporate bonds, most municipal bonds, and most U.S. Treasury securities held in book-entry form. For most clients at most firms, ACAT-eligible assets represent the bulk of the transferring account.

Non-ACAT-eligible assets include certificated securities held outside DTC; proprietary products — mutual funds, separately managed account strategies, model portfolios, or annuities — that are sponsored by or exclusive to the delivering firm and cannot be held at the receiving custodian; alternative investments such as private equity, hedge fund interests, or non-traded REITs that have transfer restrictions built into their governing documents; options and futures positions that require margin account capability at the receiving firm; 529 plan assets that must be transferred separately through plan administrator coordination; and annuity contracts, which require insurance company involvement and are governed by their own surrender and transfer rules. Each non-eligible asset requires either liquidation at the delivering firm or transfer through an alternative mechanism — DWAC for certificated securities, direct plan-to-plan transfer for 529s, surrender and re-issuance for annuities — all of which require additional time and client coordination beyond the standard ACAT workflow.

Operational Workflow

The ACAT transfer workflow encompasses distinct procedures at the receiving firm and the delivering firm, which must be coordinated through the message system in sequence.

  1. Receiving Firm: Client Authorization Collection. Before submitting an ACAT request, the receiving firm collects the client's signed transfer authorization — the Transfer Initiation Form (TIF) — which includes the client's account number at the delivering firm, the account title and tax identification number, the type of transfer requested, and any specific instructions for non-transferable assets. The TIF must be reviewed for completeness and accuracy before the ACAT request is submitted; errors in the TIF are the primary source of Day 1 validation rejections.
  2. Receiving Firm: TIR Submission. The operations team enters the transfer request into the firm's transfer processing system, which submits the Transfer Initiation Request to ACAT. The submission is logged with the submission date, and the Day 1 validation deadline is calculated and entered in the transfer tracking system.
  3. Delivering Firm: Validation Processing. The delivering firm's operations team receives the TIR and validates the account information against its records. A validation match confirms the transfer; any mismatch in account number, account title, tax ID, or account type generates a rejection with the specific discrepancy identified. The delivering firm must respond by the end of the next business day.
  4. Delivering Firm: Position Transmission. Following validation, the delivering firm generates a complete position list for the transferring account and transmits it through ACAT. This list must be accurate and complete; positions omitted from the transmission may not be delivered, creating post-transfer residual position disputes.
  5. Receiving Firm: Position Review and Asset Objection. The receiving firm reviews the transmitted position list and identifies any assets that cannot be accepted — positions in securities the receiving platform does not support, margin positions requiring capability confirmation, or assets with transfer restrictions. The receiving firm submits asset-level objections for non-acceptable positions; the advisor and client are notified of any assets that cannot transfer in kind.
  6. Receiving Firm: Transfer Confirmation Submission. After completing the position review, the receiving firm submits its transfer confirmation through ACAT, triggering the delivering firm's three-business-day delivery obligation. The delivery deadline is calculated and logged.
  7. Delivering Firm: Asset Delivery. The delivering firm initiates book-entry transfer of all transferable DTC-eligible securities to the receiving firm's DTC participant account. Cash balances are wired. Non-ACAT assets that were identified during position review and not objected to — because they will transfer through alternative means — are processed through their applicable channels simultaneously.
  8. Receiving Firm: Delivery Confirmation and Reconciliation. As positions arrive at the receiving DTC account, the operations team reconciles each delivery against the expected position list from the ACAT transmission. Quantities and security identifiers are verified. Any position not received by the delivery deadline is flagged as an open exception.
  9. Both Firms: Exception and Residual Processing. Any undelivered positions are escalated through the exception resolution process. Residual credits — dividends, interest, and other proceeds attributable to the transferred account that arrive at the delivering firm after the transfer — are transmitted to the receiving firm through the ACAT residual credit process and must be applied to the client's new account.

Real-World Example

A client signs a Transfer Initiation Form at her new advisory firm on a Monday morning. The operations team reviews the form, confirms that the account number, account title, and tax identification number are all present and internally consistent, and submits the ACAT Transfer Initiation Request to DTCC by 2:00 PM that day — Monday is Day 0. Tuesday is Day 1: the delivering firm's operations team receives the TIR, matches it against the account of record, and responds with a validation confirmation by early afternoon. The transfer advances to the position transmission stage.

The delivering firm transmits the position list on Tuesday afternoon. The receiving firm's operations team reviews the list Wednesday morning — Day 2 — and identifies 14 positions: 12 are DTC-eligible equities and ETFs that will transfer without issue; one is a proprietary managed account strategy sponsored by the delivering firm that cannot be held at the receiving custodian; and one is a certificated share position in a small private company that is not DTC-eligible. The receiving firm submits an asset objection for the proprietary managed account strategy and notifies the advisor, who contacts the client. The client agrees to liquidate the strategy at the delivering firm and transfer the cash proceeds separately. The certificated shares are flagged for DWAC processing after the primary ACAT transfer completes.

The receiving firm submits its transfer confirmation Wednesday afternoon — Day 3 — starting the three-business-day delivery clock. Thursday and Friday — Days 4 and 5 — the delivering firm processes the DTC book-entry transfers for all 12 eligible positions and wires the cash balance. The receiving firm's operations team reconciles each incoming position against the expected list as deliveries arrive. By end of day Friday — Day 5, one day ahead of the Day 6 deadline — all 12 positions and the cash balance are confirmed received and reconciled. The transfer is marked complete. The DWAC process for the certificated shares begins separately, and the client is informed that those shares will arrive in approximately two weeks through a different process. The cash proceeds from the liquidated managed account strategy are wired the following week as that position settles.

This example illustrates how a transfer that appears complex at intake — 14 positions, two non-ACAT assets — can complete cleanly within the standard timeline when the receiving firm screens asset eligibility before confirmation, communicates proactively with the advisor and client, and tracks each stage of the process against defined deadlines. The two non-ACAT assets become parallel workstreams rather than barriers to the primary transfer completion.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Submitting a TIR With Incorrect Account Identification Data

The most preventable source of Day 1 validation rejections is inaccurate data in the Transfer Initiation Request — a transposed digit in the account number, a name mismatch between the TIF and the delivering firm's records, or an incorrect tax identification number. Each rejection restarts the clock from the date of the corrected submission. Operations teams must verify the accuracy of all account identification data on the TIF before submitting the ACAT request, including confirming the account number format with the client if the delivering firm uses a non-standard numbering convention. A two-minute verification at intake prevents a two-day timeline reset.

Mistake 2: Failing to Object to Non-Acceptable Assets Before Submitting Transfer Confirmation

Once the receiving firm submits its transfer confirmation, the delivering firm begins preparing asset delivery. If the receiving firm later discovers that it cannot accept a specific position — because the review was incomplete or the asset eligibility screening was skipped — the position will arrive at the receiving account in a form the firm cannot process, creating a position that must be immediately liquidated or sent back through a separate return transfer. The position review window between validation and confirmation is the correct time to identify non-acceptable assets and submit objections. Operations teams that rush through the position review to accelerate the transfer confirmation consistently generate post-delivery exceptions that take more time to resolve than the review itself would have required.

Mistake 3: Missing the Day 1 Validation Response Deadline at the Delivering Firm

Delivering firms that allow TIR validation requests to age past the one-business-day response deadline without action are in direct violation of FINRA Rule 11870. This violation commonly occurs when transfer processing is understaffed, when ACAT queues are not monitored outside normal business hours, or when validation exceptions require research that takes longer than the available window. Delivering firms must establish intraday monitoring of the ACAT validation queue and clear all pending TIRs before the end of each business day, with escalation procedures for validation requests received late in the day that may not be resolvable before the deadline.

Mistake 4: Treating the Six-Business-Day Window as an Acceptable Processing Timeline Rather Than a Maximum

Operations teams that calibrate their ACAT workflows to just meet the six-business-day deadline leave no margin for exceptions. When an exception arises — a position that cannot be delivered because of a corporate action in process, a DTC reject on a specific security, or a cash balance discrepancy — a team at Day 5 of a six-day process has one day to resolve it before the deadline expires. A team at Day 3 has three days. Processing transfers at the fastest possible pace within the timeline creates the margin that exception resolution requires.

Mistake 5: Neglecting Residual Credit Processing After Transfer Completion

Transfer completion closes the primary ACAT workflow, but it does not end the delivering firm's obligations. Dividends declared before the transfer record date, interest payments on transferred bonds, and other proceeds attributable to the transferred account that arrive at the delivering firm after the transfer are residual credits that must be transmitted to the receiving firm. Delivering firms that do not have a systematic residual credit monitoring process — and receiving firms that do not track expected residuals and follow up when they do not arrive — leave clients with missing proceeds that may not be discovered until months later when the client reviews a dividend history or tax document. Residual credit processing must be treated as an ongoing post-transfer obligation, not as a task completed when the primary transfer closes.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: ACAT Timeline Calculation

A client signs a Transfer Initiation Form on a Wednesday. The receiving firm submits the ACAT request on Wednesday afternoon. Thursday, the delivering firm validates the transfer. Friday, the delivering firm transmits the position list. The receiving firm reviews the positions and submits its transfer confirmation the following Monday. Calculate the delivery deadline under Rule 11870. If the following Monday falls before a holiday on the Tuesday, how does that affect the delivery deadline? Identify which day corresponds to each stage of the six-business-day window and note any stages where the firms in this scenario have used their full available time. Where was time lost unnecessarily, and what could each firm have done to accelerate the transfer?

Exercise 2: Asset Eligibility Screening Exercise

A client's account at the delivering firm contains the following positions: 500 shares of Apple Inc. (AAPL); 200 shares of a proprietary balanced fund offered exclusively by the delivering firm; 100 units of a non-traded REIT with a five-year redemption restriction; $15,000 in a money market sweep account; 50 shares of a small private company held in certificated form; and 300 shares of a Vanguard index ETF. For each position, determine whether it is ACAT-eligible, and if not, identify the appropriate alternative transfer mechanism or disposition. For each non-eligible position, identify what information you would need from the client or advisor before deciding how to handle it, and explain the client communication you would recommend regarding that position.

Exercise 3: Delivering Firm Objection Analysis

The delivering firm receives a Transfer Initiation Request for a client account. The account number matches, but the account is titled "John A. Smith" while the TIR shows "John Smith." The account carries a debit balance of $8,200 that the client has not resolved before initiating the transfer. One position in the account — 200 shares of a stock — is currently subject to a securities lending agreement that cannot be terminated immediately. For each of these three issues, determine whether the delivering firm has a valid basis to object to or delay the transfer under Rule 11870, and if so, describe the correct procedure. If any issue does not constitute a valid objection basis, explain what the delivering firm must do instead.

Exercise 4: Residual Credit Scenario

A client's account transfers completely on a Thursday. On the following Tuesday, a $340 dividend payment from a position that transferred in kind arrives at the delivering firm, because the dividend was declared before the transfer but paid after it completed. The same week, a $150 interest payment on a corporate bond that was transferred arrives at the delivering firm. Design the residual credit processing workflow that should handle each of these payments: who identifies them, how are they transmitted to the receiving firm, what documentation is generated, and what does the receiving firm do upon receipt? How would you set up a monitoring process to ensure that residual credits are not missed over the 30–60 day window following transfer completion during which they are most likely to arise?

Key Terms

ACAT (Automated Customer Account Transfer Service) — The DTCC-operated electronic system that automates customer account transfers between participating broker-dealers and financial institutions in the U.S., establishing standardized message formats, timelines, and asset eligibility rules for in-kind account transfers.

FINRA Rule 11870 — The FINRA rule governing customer account transfers between member firms, establishing the six-business-day completion standard and defining the specific obligations and deadlines of receiving and delivering firms at each stage of the ACAT process.

Transfer Initiation Request (TIR) — The initial ACAT message submitted by the receiving firm to identify the account to be transferred, including the client's account number, tax identification number, account title, and transfer type at the delivering firm.

Transfer Initiation Form (TIF) — The client-signed authorization document collected by the receiving firm before submitting an ACAT request, authorizing the transfer and providing the account identification data required for the TIR.

Account Validation — The delivering firm's response to a Transfer Initiation Request, confirming that the account information in the request matches its records; must be completed within one business day of TIR receipt.

Position Transmission — The delivering firm's transmission of a complete list of all positions in the transferring account through the ACAT system following account validation, enabling the receiving firm to review asset eligibility and submit any asset-level objections before confirming the transfer.

Transfer Confirmation — The receiving firm's confirmation through ACAT that it accepts the transmitted positions and is ready to receive delivery, triggering the delivering firm's three-business-day asset delivery obligation.

Asset Objection — A message submitted by the receiving firm during the position review stage identifying specific assets in the delivering firm's position transmission that cannot be accepted at the receiving custodian, requiring those assets to be excluded from ACAT delivery and handled through alternative channels.

Residual Credit — A dividend, interest payment, or other proceed attributable to a transferred account that arrives at the delivering firm after the transfer has completed, which the delivering firm is obligated to transmit to the receiving firm for crediting to the client's new account.

DTC Book-Entry Transfer — The electronic movement of DTC-eligible securities between participant accounts at the Depository Trust Company, the primary mechanism by which ACAT-eligible securities are physically delivered during an ACAT transfer.

Lesson Summary

  • ACAT is the DTCC-operated electronic system that automates customer account transfers between U.S. broker-dealers and custodians; FINRA Rule 11870 establishes the six-business-day completion standard and defines the specific regulatory obligations of receiving and delivering firms at each stage of the process.
  • The ACAT workflow proceeds through six stages — TIR submission, account validation, position transmission and review, transfer confirmation, asset delivery, and completion — with defined deadlines at each stage; the delivering firm has one business day to validate a TIR and three business days from transfer confirmation to deliver assets.
  • ACAT-eligible assets are DTC-held securities identified by CUSIP or ISIN, including listed equities, most ETFs, most publicly offered mutual funds, and most bonds; non-eligible assets include certificated securities, proprietary products, alternative investments with transfer restrictions, and certain retirement and insurance products that require alternative transfer mechanisms.
  • The position review window between the delivering firm's position transmission and the receiving firm's transfer confirmation is the critical stage for identifying non-acceptable assets and submitting objections; assets not identified at this stage will arrive post-delivery in a form the receiving firm cannot process, generating more complex exceptions than a timely objection would have created.
  • Residual credits — dividends, interest, and other proceeds attributable to a transferred account that arrive at the delivering firm after transfer completion — are an ongoing post-transfer obligation that the delivering firm must transmit to the receiving firm; operations teams at both firms must monitor residual credits systematically for 30 to 60 days following transfer completion to ensure all client proceeds are correctly applied.

Looking Ahead

Lesson 19.2 has established the complete ACAT process framework — from regulatory foundation through message structure, timeline obligations, asset eligibility, and residual processing. Lesson 19.3 will examine the same transfer process from the perspective of role — distinguishing the operational workflows, responsibilities, and strategic considerations that differ between inbound transfers (where the firm is the receiver) and outbound transfers (where the firm is the deliverer). The distinction matters because the two roles create different client service obligations, competitive dynamics, and operational priorities, even though both operate within the same ACAT framework.

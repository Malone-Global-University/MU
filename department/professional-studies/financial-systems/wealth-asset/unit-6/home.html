Unit 6: Market Structure and Trading Basics

Learn how investment transactions move through markets. This unit introduces market structure, order flow, execution channels, and basic trade mechanics so students can understand how wealth and asset institutions convert investment decisions into completed transactions.

Where This Unit Fits

This unit follows investment vehicles and product structures in Unit 5 and shifts from product form to transaction movement. After learning how exposure is packaged through funds, managed accounts, and wrappers, students now study how buy and sell activity actually reaches markets and becomes an executed trade.

Students begin here because later units on trade capture, settlement, reconciliation, exception handling, and portfolio maintenance all depend on understanding the path an order takes from instruction to execution. This unit provides the transactional map that supports later operational detail.

Unit Overview

Wealth and asset institutions do not simply decide what to own; they must access markets and execute transactions through structured channels. Orders may pass through advisors, portfolio managers, trading desks, brokers, exchanges, dealers, or other execution venues. Each step affects timing, price, control, and operational follow-through.

This unit introduces the basic structure of trading and market access. Students study how orders are created, routed, executed, and confirmed across different market settings. The goal is not to teach advanced trading strategy, but to help students understand how market structure shapes the operational processes that follow every investment decision.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

Many operational workflows begin with a trade. If students do not understand how an order enters the market, how it finds a counterparty or execution venue, and how execution terms are established, later activities such as settlement, confirmation, trade matching, and exception resolution can seem disconnected from their origin.

In practical terms, students who understand this unit are better prepared to interpret trade details, recognize why execution timing matters, explain the difference between order entry and completed execution, and understand why different products or asset classes may move through different market channels.

What You’ll Learn

Core Concepts

  • How market structure connects investors, firms, brokers, dealers, and execution venues
  • How orders move from investment instruction to executed trade
  • The difference between order entry, routing, execution, and confirmation
  • Why different asset classes and product types may use different trading channels
  • How timing, price formation, and liquidity affect transaction outcomes
  • Why market structure matters for downstream operational processing

Operational Competencies

  • Describe the basic life path of an order in wealth and asset environments
  • Recognize major participants involved in trade execution and market access
  • Explain how venue choice and execution method influence trade handling
  • Interpret the operational difference between an instruction, an order, and an executed trade
  • Use trading structure knowledge to support later study of settlement, reconciliation, and controls

Institutional Questions This Unit Helps Answer

  • How does an investment instruction become a real market transaction?
  • Who stands between the investor and the market when a trade is placed?
  • Why do some trades execute immediately while others wait, route differently, or fill in parts?
  • How does market structure shape the operational work that follows a trade?

Lessons in This Unit

Trading Foundations

  • Lesson 6.1: What Market Structure and Trading Do

    Learn how wealth and asset institutions access markets through structured trading channels that connect investors, firms, brokers, dealers, and execution venues.

  • Lesson 6.2: Orders, Instructions, and Trade Intent

    Study how investment intent becomes an order, and see how trade instructions define what is being bought or sold, in what amount, and under what conditions.

  • Lesson 6.3: Brokers, Dealers, and Execution Intermediaries

    Examine how intermediaries help route, facilitate, or execute transactions and why these institutional roles matter for market access and operational follow-through.

  • Lesson 6.4: Exchanges, Dealer Markets, and Trading Venues

    Understand how trades occur across different venues, including exchange-based and dealer-based environments, and why market setting affects execution mechanics.

Execution Logic and Operational Relevance

  • Lesson 6.5: Order Routing, Pricing, and Execution Outcomes

    Learn how orders are routed, how prices are determined at execution, and why timing, liquidity, and venue conditions influence trade results.

  • Lesson 6.6: Trade Confirmation and Execution Records

    Study how executed trades are documented, confirmed, and communicated so downstream operations teams can process transactions accurately.

  • Lesson 6.7: Bringing Market Structure Together

    Connect order creation, routing, execution, venues, intermediaries, and confirmations into one operating picture so students can see how trading activity moves through the system.

Connected Units

  • Unit 5: Investment Vehicles and Product Structures

    Use the product knowledge from Unit 5 to understand why different vehicles may access markets through different trading and execution pathways.

  • Unit 7: Trade Lifecycle and Post-Trade Processing

    Build directly on this unit by following executed trades into allocation, affirmation, settlement preparation, and other post-trade operational steps.

  • Unit 12: Rebalancing and Portfolio Maintenance

    Apply trading basics when studying how portfolio adjustments generate market activity that operations teams must support and track.

Study Support

  • Templates & Tools

    Use order-flow diagrams and trade pathway maps to practice tracing how instructions move from portfolio intent to executed transaction.

  • Glossary Support

    Review key terms such as order, execution, broker, dealer, exchange, venue, routing, fill, and confirmation.

  • Case Examples

    Study practical scenarios showing how different trades move through different market channels and how those paths affect operational handling.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to explain how market structure supports trading activity, describe how orders move from instruction to execution, recognize the roles of intermediaries and venues, and use that understanding to interpret the operational records and workflows that begin once a trade is completed.

Lesson 25.4: Correction and Adjustment Entries

Study the proper execution of correction and adjustment entries in operational systems — including the mechanics of reversals, rebooks, and manual adjustments; the authorization and approval controls that govern their use; the audit trail standards that must accompany every correction; and the risk controls that distinguish legitimate correction entries from unauthorized manipulation of financial records.

Where This Lesson Fits

The first three lessons of Unit 25 have addressed the identification, analysis, and investigation of reconciliation breaks. By the end of Lesson 25.3's investigation workflow, the operations team has identified what is wrong, why it occurred, and what the correct values should be. Lesson 25.4 addresses the next step: the correction entry — the specific accounting action taken to change the records in the internal system from their current (incorrect) values to the correct values established by the investigation.

Correction entries are the most sensitive operational action in the reconciliation function. They directly modify client account records, potentially affecting reported balances, position values, and transaction histories. They are the mechanism through which a legitimate error is fixed — but they are also the mechanism through which unauthorized manipulation of financial records can occur if the controls governing their use are inadequate. For this reason, correction entry processing is subject to more rigorous authorization, documentation, and audit trail requirements than almost any other operational action in wealth management.

Understanding the mechanics of correction entries, the controls that govern their authorization, and the audit trail standards that must accompany them is essential for anyone who processes, approves, or reviews corrections — and for anyone in a compliance or oversight role who evaluates whether the firm's correction practices meet regulatory and internal standards.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to describe the three primary types of correction entries — reversal, rebook, and manual adjustment — and explain when each is appropriate; explain the authorization and approval requirements that govern correction entry processing, including the basis for tiered approval thresholds; describe the audit trail requirements for correction entries and explain why each element of the audit trail is necessary; identify the risk controls that prevent correction entries from being used for unauthorized manipulation of financial records; explain the difference between a same-day correction and a prior-period adjustment, and describe the additional controls that apply to prior-period entries; and apply these principles to design an appropriate correction entry for a described reconciliation break, including the required authorization, documentation, and audit trail elements.

Lesson Overview

A correction entry is a deliberate modification of one or more records in an operational or accounting system to bring those records into agreement with the correct values established through break investigation and root cause analysis. Correction entries differ from normal transaction entries in a critical respect: normal transactions record events that occurred in the real world (a trade was executed, a wire was sent, a dividend was received); correction entries reflect a judgment that an existing record is wrong and must be changed. This distinction makes correction entries inherently higher-risk than normal transactions, because the judgment that a record is incorrect — and that the proposed correction is the right fix — can itself be wrong.

The risk profile of correction entries has two dimensions. The first is the risk of an incorrect correction: applying a correction that is itself wrong — using an incorrect amount, correcting the wrong account, or choosing the wrong correction type — produces a new error that may be harder to detect than the original. The second is the risk of unauthorized manipulation: the ability to change posted financial records, if insufficiently controlled, can be used to conceal fraudulent transactions, misstate client balances, or manipulate reported performance. Both risks are addressed through the same control mechanisms: tiered authorization requirements, segregation of duties between the investigator who proposes the correction and the supervisor who approves it, and comprehensive audit trails that record every correction with full attribution.

The mechanics of correction entry processing vary by system and firm, but the three fundamental types — reversal, rebook, and manual adjustment — are consistent across most wealth and asset management environments. Each type has a specific application: reversals undo a previously posted transaction; rebooks repost the transaction with corrected attributes; and manual adjustments make direct changes to balance or position records without being linked to a specific original transaction.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

Correction entries are one of the most frequent subjects of regulatory examination findings in wealth management operations. Regulators examining a firm's reconciliation practices will review a sample of correction entries and assess: whether each correction was supported by adequate investigation documentation; whether the correction was authorized at the appropriate level; whether the audit trail records who made the correction, when, and why; and whether the pattern of corrections suggests any unusual activity — a high volume of corrections in the same account, corrections made by the same individual consistently, or large corrections that occur just before reporting periods.

The connection between correction entry controls and fraud prevention is direct. Many of the operational fraud cases in financial services over the past two decades have involved the abuse of correction entry capabilities — using the ability to modify posted records to conceal unauthorized transactions, divert client funds, or manipulate performance reporting. Strong correction entry controls — particularly the requirement that no one can initiate and approve their own corrections — are a fundamental internal control against this category of risk.

For operations professionals who process corrections, understanding the authorization, documentation, and audit trail requirements is not only a compliance obligation but a self-protection mechanism. An analyst who processes a correction entry without proper authorization documentation, even with entirely legitimate intent, creates a record that cannot be distinguished from an unauthorized entry without the documentation. Proper correction discipline protects the analyst as much as it protects the client.

Core Concept

Reversal Entry — A correction entry that exactly negates a previously posted transaction by creating an equal and opposite transaction with the same attributes (account, security, amount, transaction type) as the original. A reversal removes the economic effect of the original posting from the record, restoring the record to its state before the erroneous transaction was posted. Reversals are the appropriate correction mechanism when a transaction was posted that should not have been posted at all — a duplicate entry, an entry to the wrong account that needs to be removed entirely before a corrected entry is made, or a transaction that was cancelled after posting.

Rebook Entry — A correction entry that combines a reversal of the original erroneous transaction with a new posting of the correct transaction in a single operation. Rebooks are the most common correction type for attribute errors — transactions that were posted with the correct basic characteristics (the trade happened, the dividend was received) but with incorrect values in one or more fields (wrong account, wrong amount, wrong date). A rebook removes the incorrect posting and replaces it with the correct posting in a linked pair of entries that allows the audit trail to connect the original, the reversal, and the corrected posting.

Manual Adjustment — A correction entry that directly modifies a balance or position record without reference to a specific original transaction. Manual adjustments are used when the break cannot be resolved by reversing or reboking a specific transaction — for example, when a corporate action produced an incorrect ending balance that cannot be traced to a single erroneous transaction, or when a tolerated variance is formalized as a permanent adjustment. Manual adjustments are the highest-risk correction type because they modify records without the linking logic of a reversal or rebook, making them harder to trace in audit review. Most firms subject manual adjustments to higher authorization thresholds than reversals or rebooks.

Same-Day Correction — A correction entry processed on the same business day as the original error, before the erroneous record has been included in any client reporting, regulatory filing, or overnight reconciliation. Same-day corrections are subject to standard correction entry authorization requirements but have a simpler audit trail burden because no reporting has been produced based on the incorrect record. They are the preferred correction mechanism when an error is identified promptly.

Prior-Period Adjustment — A correction entry that modifies a record for a date in a prior business period — a date for which client reports, regulatory filings, or performance calculations have already been produced using the incorrect data. Prior-period adjustments are subject to heightened authorization requirements because they create a discrepancy between the firm's current records and previously produced reports, potentially requiring amended reporting and client notification. Most firms require senior management approval (operations manager or above) for any prior-period adjustment, and some require compliance approval as well.

Segregation of Duties in Corrections — The control requirement that the individual who identifies and proposes a correction entry must be different from the individual who authorizes and approves it. This segregation prevents the self-authorization of corrections — the ability of a single individual to both design and execute a modification to posted records — which is the primary control mechanism against unauthorized record manipulation through correction entries.

Correction Entry Authorization Framework

Authorization requirements for correction entries are tiered based on the financial exposure of the correction and the type of correction being made. The tiered structure reflects the principle that higher-risk actions require higher-level oversight, while not burdening routine small corrections with the approval overhead appropriate for large or complex corrections.

  • Tier 1 — Standard Authorization (Peer or Supervisor Review). Correction entries below the firm's Tier 1 threshold (typically $10,000–$25,000 for cash, or a defined quantity threshold for positions) may be authorized by a senior analyst or operations supervisor who was not involved in the original error and did not propose the correction. The authorizer must confirm that the correction is supported by the investigation documentation, that the correction type is appropriate for the break, and that the corrected values match the authoritative source. Tier 1 corrections are the most common and must be processed efficiently to avoid delaying break resolution.
  • Tier 2 — Elevated Authorization (Operations Manager). Correction entries above the Tier 1 threshold but below the Tier 2 threshold (typically $25,000–$100,000) require operations manager review and approval. The operations manager confirms all of the elements required at Tier 1 and additionally assesses whether the correction has any client impact that requires disclosure, whether the root cause suggests systemic issues that should be escalated, and whether the correction affects any regulatory reporting. Tier 2 corrections typically require documented manager sign-off in the break management system.
  • Tier 3 — Senior Authorization (Senior Management or Compliance). Correction entries above the Tier 2 threshold, all prior-period adjustments regardless of amount, all manual adjustments above the Tier 1 threshold, and any correction that affects regulatory reporting or that cannot be explained by a clearly documented root cause require senior management authorization and, in many cases, compliance review. Tier 3 corrections require documented approval from the appropriate level, the specific rationale for the correction, the alternative correction options that were considered and rejected, and the client communication plan if the correction affects a client's reported account values.
  • Emergency Authorization. In rare cases, a correction must be processed before the normal authorization timeline can be completed — for example, a large erroneous wire posting that must be corrected before the wire processing window closes to allow a recall request. Emergency authorizations require verbal approval from the appropriate authorization level, followed by written documentation of the approval within a defined window (typically the same business day). Emergency authorization procedures must be explicitly defined in the firm's correction entry policy and may not be used to circumvent the standard authorization process in non-emergency situations.

Audit Trail Requirements for Correction Entries

Every correction entry must be accompanied by an audit trail that provides complete, immutable evidence of what was changed, who changed it, why it was changed, and who authorized the change. The audit trail is not a summary or a narrative — it is a collection of specific, verifiable records that together constitute proof that the correction was properly investigated, properly approved, and properly executed.

  • Original Record Reference. The audit trail must identify the specific record that was corrected — the original transaction ID, account number, date, amount, and security (if applicable). Without a precise reference to what was changed, the audit trail cannot be verified and the correction cannot be reconstructed from the evidence.
  • Corrected Values. The audit trail must document the values after the correction — the amount that was posted, the account the correction was applied to, the date assigned to the correction entry, and any other fields that were changed. The before-and-after comparison must be reconstructible from the audit trail.
  • Root Cause Documentation. The audit trail must reference the investigation record that identified the root cause of the break being corrected. This is typically accomplished by linking the correction entry to the break ID in the break management system, which in turn contains the investigation documentation. Without root cause documentation, the correction cannot be assessed for appropriateness — a correction without an identified root cause is an unauthorized adjustment.
  • Authorization Record. The audit trail must record the identity of the approving authority (name and title), the level of authorization applied (Tier 1, 2, or 3), the timestamp of the approval, and — for Tier 2 and above — the approver's documented confirmation that the correction is supported by the investigation record. Electronic approval timestamps in the break management or correction processing system are the preferred format because they are immutable and attributable.
  • Preparer Attribution. The audit trail must record the identity of the person who prepared the correction entry — the analyst who proposed the correction based on the investigation findings. The preparer and the approver must be different individuals. If they are the same individual, the segregation of duties control has been violated and the correction requires re-review by a qualified independent party.
  • Processing Timestamp. The exact date and time at which the correction entry was submitted to and accepted by the processing system must be recorded. For prior-period corrections, the timestamp also documents the current-period processing date, which is distinct from the as-of date of the correction.
  • Impact Assessment. For corrections above the Tier 2 threshold and for all prior-period adjustments, the audit trail must include an impact assessment documenting whether the correction affected: client-reported account values (requiring client notification if material); regulatory filings (requiring amended reporting if material); performance calculations (requiring notification to portfolio managers or clients if material); or tax calculations (requiring notification to the tax reporting function).

Reversal vs. Rebook vs. Manual Adjustment: Selection Criteria

Selecting the appropriate correction type for a given break is a judgment that depends on the nature of the original error and the relationship between the original posting and the correction. Using the wrong correction type — for example, applying a manual adjustment when a rebook would more accurately reflect the original transaction — produces a correct ending balance but an incorrect transaction history, which can cause problems in subsequent reconciliation cycles and in audit review.

Reversals are appropriate when the original transaction should not exist at all — a duplicate entry that created an extra transaction in the record, a posting that was made to the wrong account and needs to be removed entirely before the correct account is credited, or a transaction that was cancelled after posting. The reversal removes the erroneous entry from the record, restoring the record to its pre-error state. If the transaction should have been posted to a different account or with different attributes, the rebook is the more appropriate mechanism because it links the removal of the incorrect entry to the posting of the correct one.

Rebooks are appropriate for attribute errors — situations where the transaction did occur and should be reflected in the records, but where one or more attributes (account, amount, date, security) were recorded incorrectly. The rebook reverses the incorrect posting and creates a linked corrected posting, maintaining the visibility of the original transaction in the audit trail while correcting the record. Most correction entry policies require rebooks rather than standalone reversals for attribute errors because the rebook preserves the full evidence of what happened — the original posting, the reversal, and the corrected posting — whereas a reversal followed by a new entry is less transparent.

Manual adjustments are appropriate only when the break cannot be resolved by identifying and correcting a specific transaction — when the discrepancy is in an aggregate balance that cannot be traced to a single erroneous transaction, or when the correction reflects a genuine difference between two systems that must be formally acknowledged as an adjustment rather than attributed to a specific transaction error. The higher authorization requirements for manual adjustments reflect their higher inherent risk: because they do not link to an original transaction, they are harder to verify and are the correction type most susceptible to abuse for unauthorized record manipulation.

Operational Workflow: Correction Entry Processing

The following describes the standard correction entry processing workflow from the investigation handoff through audit trail completion, illustrating the specific steps and controls at each stage.

  1. Investigation Handoff. The investigating analyst completes Stage 4 of the investigation workflow, documenting the root cause and specifying the correction entry required: type of correction (reversal, rebook, or adjustment), affected account(s), corrected values, as-of date, and break ID reference. This specification is submitted to the correction processing function as a formal correction request. The analyst does not process the correction — the segregation of duties control requires that proposal and processing be performed by different individuals.
  2. Correction Request Review. The correction processing specialist receives the correction request and performs a completeness review: is the break ID valid and is the investigation documentation present in the break management system? Is the correction type appropriate for the break type described? Are the corrected values consistent with the authoritative source cited in the investigation record? If the request is incomplete or the correction type is inappropriate, it is returned to the investigating analyst for clarification before processing proceeds.
  3. Authorization Determination. The correction processing specialist determines the applicable authorization tier based on the correction amount and type. For Tier 1 corrections, the specialist obtains same-level or supervisor authorization before proceeding. For Tier 2 or Tier 3 corrections, the request is routed to the appropriate authority with the investigation documentation attached for review. The correction is not processed until authorization is confirmed.
  4. Entry Processing. Once authorized, the correction entry is submitted to the relevant system (portfolio accounting, cash ledger, position record) using the firm's standard correction entry interface. The entry includes: the correction type code, the break reference ID, the preparer's user ID, the authorizer's user ID, and all required field values for the correction. The processing system generates an immutable transaction record with a system timestamp that forms part of the audit trail.
  5. Audit Trail Completion. Following successful processing, the correction processing specialist updates the break management system to record: the system-generated correction entry ID, the processing timestamp, the authorization level and authorizer identity, and — for Tier 2 and above — the impact assessment. The investigation record is updated to reflect that the correction has been submitted and is pending reconciliation verification.
  6. Reconciliation Verification. The investigating analyst (or the correction processing specialist) confirms in the next reconciliation cycle that the correction has closed the break — that the corrected values now appear in both systems with no residual discrepancy. The break is formally closed in the break management system with the resolution timestamp and the verifying analyst's user ID. The complete investigation and correction record is preserved in the break management system and retained per the firm's records retention policy.

Real-World Example

A wealth management firm undergoes an SEC examination that includes a review of the firm's correction entry practices over the prior 18 months. The examination team pulls the correction entry log and reviews a sample of 25 corrections. Among the findings: 6 corrections have no linked investigation documentation — the correction entry exists in the system but there is no break record or root cause documentation in the break management system to explain why the correction was made. 4 corrections were prepared and approved by the same individual — the segregation of duties requirement was not enforced by the system. 3 corrections are prior-period adjustments that were processed at the Tier 1 authorization level rather than the elevated level required for prior-period entries. 2 corrections have no impact assessment, despite being above the Tier 2 threshold.

The examination results in a deficiency letter with five findings. The firm is required to: implement system-enforced linkage between correction entries and break management records, preventing corrections from being processed without a valid break ID; implement system-enforced segregation of duties that prevents the same user from both submitting and approving a correction entry; implement system-enforced elevated authorization routing for all prior-period adjustments regardless of amount; implement mandatory impact assessment completion as a condition of correction entry approval for all Tier 2 and above corrections; and conduct a retrospective review of all undocumented corrections from the examination period to determine whether any unauthorized manipulations occurred.

The retrospective review reveals that the 6 undocumented corrections were all legitimate — they were made by a senior analyst who bypassed documentation requirements for efficiency, not to conceal any unauthorized activity. But the firm cannot prove this without the documentation, and the examination finding stands regardless of the actual intent. The lesson: the documentation requirement exists not to burden legitimate corrections but to make illegitimate corrections detectable. Without the requirement, there is no way to distinguish a legitimate correction from an unauthorized one.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Processing Corrections Without Linking to an Investigation Record

A correction entry without a linked investigation record is indistinguishable from an unauthorized modification. In any firm where correction entry controls are periodically reviewed — by internal audit, compliance, external audit, or regulators — undocumented corrections will be identified as a control deficiency regardless of whether the specific correction was legitimate. The documentation requirement is the control; bypassing it eliminates the only mechanism by which a legitimate correction can be verified as such.

Mistake 2: Self-Approving Corrections

Self-approval of corrections — submitting and approving one's own correction entry, either explicitly or through a system that does not enforce the segregation — is the single most common correction entry control violation. Even in firms with robust correction policies, the self-approval pattern tends to emerge under time pressure ("I need this done before end of day and my supervisor is unavailable") or in understaffed environments where the analyst and the supervisor roles are effectively the same person. Both scenarios require explicit emergency authorization procedures rather than informal self-approval.

Mistake 3: Using Manual Adjustments as a Default for Complex Corrections

Manual adjustments are the most flexible correction type but also the highest-risk. Operations teams that use manual adjustments as the default when rebooks would be more appropriate — because rebooks require more steps or more system knowledge — produce correction records that are harder to audit and that obscure the transaction history. The correct default for attribute errors is the rebook; the manual adjustment should be reserved for situations where no identifiable original transaction can be linked to the correction.

Mistake 4: Processing Prior-Period Adjustments at Standard Authorization Levels

Prior-period adjustments affect records that have already been used to produce client reports, regulatory filings, or performance calculations. The additional controls required for prior-period entries — senior management approval, impact assessment, amended reporting determination — exist because these corrections have downstream consequences that standard corrections do not. Processing a prior-period adjustment at Tier 1 authorization because the amount is small misses the point: it is the period, not the amount, that determines the additional control requirements.

Mistake 5: Failing to Perform the Impact Assessment for Large Corrections

Large corrections — particularly those that affect a client's reported account values materially — may require client notification, amended regulatory filings, or corrected performance reports. Operations teams that process the correction entry without completing the impact assessment create situations where clients receive incorrect reports after the correction, where regulatory filings remain inaccurate, or where investment decisions were made based on incorrect data that was never flagged for review. The impact assessment is the mechanism that triggers the downstream actions required when a correction is large enough to matter outside the reconciliation function.

Key Terms

Correction Entry — A deliberate modification of one or more records in an operational or accounting system to bring those records into agreement with the correct values established through break investigation. Subject to heightened authorization, documentation, and audit trail requirements compared to normal transaction entries.

Reversal Entry — A correction entry that exactly negates a previously posted transaction by creating an equal and opposite transaction with the same attributes. Used when a transaction that should not exist needs to be removed from the record.

Rebook Entry — A correction entry that combines a reversal of the original erroneous transaction with a linked posting of the corrected transaction. The standard correction mechanism for attribute errors where the transaction occurred but was recorded incorrectly.

Manual Adjustment — A correction entry that directly modifies a balance or position record without reference to a specific original transaction. The highest-risk correction type; reserved for situations where no specific original transaction can be linked to the correction.

Same-Day Correction — A correction processed on the same business day as the original error, before the incorrect record has been included in any reporting or overnight reconciliation.

Prior-Period Adjustment — A correction that modifies a record for a date in a prior reporting period, after client reports, regulatory filings, or performance calculations have already been produced using the incorrect data. Subject to elevated authorization requirements and impact assessment obligations.

Tiered Authorization — A correction entry approval framework that scales the required authorization level based on the financial exposure and type of the correction, ensuring that higher-risk corrections receive higher-level oversight without burdening routine small corrections.

Segregation of Duties (Corrections) — The control requirement that the individual who proposes a correction entry must be different from the individual who authorizes it. The primary control against unauthorized manipulation of financial records through correction entries.

Audit Trail — The collection of immutable records that together document what was changed in a correction entry, who changed it, why it was changed, who authorized the change, and when it was processed. Required for every correction entry regardless of type or amount.

Impact Assessment — A documented evaluation of the downstream consequences of a correction entry — whether it affects client-reported values, regulatory filings, performance calculations, or tax reporting — used to determine what additional actions (client notification, amended filings, corrected reports) are required following the correction.

Emergency Authorization — Verbal approval from the appropriate authorization level for a correction that must be processed before normal authorization can be completed, followed by written documentation of the approval within a defined window. May not be used to circumvent standard authorization in non-emergency situations.

Knowledge Check

Question 1

A trade was entered in the order management system with 500 shares when the actual execution was for 450 shares. The trade has settled and the custodian's records show 450 shares. Which correction entry type is most appropriate, and why?

  • A. Manual adjustment — modify the share quantity directly in the position record
  • B. Reversal — reverse the 500-share trade entirely
  • C. Rebook — reverse the 500-share trade and create a linked replacement posting for 450 shares
  • D. No correction entry is needed — the custodian's record is correct, so the break will self-resolve

Correct Answer: C — This is an attribute error: the trade occurred, the custodian correctly recorded 450 shares, and the internal system incorrectly recorded 500 shares. The rebook is the appropriate correction type because it removes the incorrect 500-share record and replaces it with the correct 450-share record in a linked pair, preserving the audit trail of the original transaction, the correction, and the corrected values. A standalone reversal would remove the trade entirely rather than correcting it. A manual adjustment would modify the ending balance without the transaction-level traceability that the rebook provides.

Question 2

An operations analyst prepares a $28,000 correction entry and approves it herself because her supervisor is out of the office. The correction is legitimate and accurately resolves the identified break. What control has been violated and why does it matter even though the correction is legitimate?

  • A. No control has been violated — the correction was legitimate and the analyst was authorized to act in the supervisor's absence
  • B. The segregation of duties control has been violated — the same individual both proposed and approved the correction, eliminating the independent verification that distinguishes legitimate corrections from unauthorized ones
  • C. The timing control has been violated — corrections above $10,000 must be processed before 2:00 PM
  • D. The documentation control has been violated — the investigation record was not properly linked

Correct Answer: B — The segregation of duties requirement exists precisely because legitimate intent cannot be verified after the fact without it. A correction processed by the same individual who proposed it is indistinguishable — in the audit trail — from an unauthorized modification made to conceal fraud. The legitimacy of the specific correction does not cure the control violation; the violation created a window in which an unauthorized correction could have been processed without detection. Emergency authorization procedures exist for situations where the approver is unavailable; the correct action was to obtain verbal approval from the operations manager or another qualified supervisor and document it, not to self-approve.

Question 3

A prior-period adjustment is needed to correct a $9,000 transaction error from two months ago. The firm's Tier 1 threshold is $10,000 — amounts below $10,000 require only Tier 1 (supervisor) authorization. Which authorization level applies to this correction?

  • A. Tier 1 — the amount is below the $10,000 threshold
  • B. Tier 3 (senior management or compliance) — the correction is a prior-period adjustment, which requires elevated authorization regardless of the dollar amount
  • C. No authorization needed — prior-period corrections are handled through the monthly close process automatically
  • D. Tier 2 — amounts between $10,000 and $75,000 require Tier 2 authorization

Correct Answer: B — Prior-period adjustments require elevated authorization regardless of their dollar amount because the period trigger — not just the amount trigger — determines the authorization requirement. Prior-period corrections affect records that have already been used in client reporting, regulatory filings, or performance calculations, creating downstream consequences that exceed those of a same-day correction of the same amount. The $9,000 amount would be Tier 1 for a same-day correction; the prior-period nature elevates it to Tier 3.

Question 4

What is the purpose of requiring an impact assessment for large corrections and prior-period adjustments?

  • A. Impact assessments are required only for regulatory compliance and have no operational purpose
  • B. The impact assessment determines whether the correction affects client-reported values, regulatory filings, performance calculations, or tax reporting — triggering the downstream actions (client notification, amended filings, corrected reports) that may be required following the correction
  • C. Impact assessments are used to determine the appropriate authorization tier for the correction
  • D. Impact assessments are only required for manual adjustments

Correct Answer: B — The impact assessment is the mechanism that connects the correction entry to its downstream consequences. A large correction that affects a client's reported account values may require client notification. A correction that changes the basis for a regulatory filing may require an amended filing. A correction that affects performance reporting may require corrected performance data to be distributed to portfolio managers and clients. Without the impact assessment, the correction entry resolves the reconciliation break but leaves these downstream consequences unaddressed.

Question 5

Why are manual adjustments subject to higher authorization thresholds than reversals or rebooks of the same dollar amount?

  • A. Manual adjustments take longer to process and therefore require more senior oversight for timing reasons
  • B. Manual adjustments directly modify balance or position records without linking to a specific original transaction, making them harder to verify and more susceptible to abuse for unauthorized record manipulation compared to reversals and rebooks, which maintain a visible link to an identifiable original transaction
  • C. Manual adjustments always affect prior periods, which requires senior approval
  • D. Manual adjustments are used exclusively for client accounts, which carry higher regulatory risk

Correct Answer: B — The higher authorization threshold for manual adjustments reflects their higher inherent risk profile. Because they do not link to an identifiable original transaction, manual adjustments are harder to audit — a reviewer cannot easily confirm that the adjustment corresponds to a real-world event by tracing it back to a source transaction. Reversals and rebooks, by contrast, link the correction to a specific original posting, making the chain of evidence reconstructible. The absence of this linkage in manual adjustments makes them the correction type most susceptible to use for unauthorized balance manipulation.

Lesson Summary

Correction entries are the mechanism through which identified reconciliation breaks are resolved at the record level. The three correction types — reversals (removing an erroneous posting entirely), rebooks (removing and replacing with the correct values in a linked pair), and manual adjustments (direct balance or position modifications without a linked original transaction) — each have specific appropriate applications. Rebooks are the standard correction for attribute errors; manual adjustments are reserved for situations where no specific original transaction can be linked.

All correction entries are subject to tiered authorization requirements based on financial exposure and entry type, with prior-period adjustments and manual adjustments above threshold requiring elevated authorization regardless of amount. Segregation of duties — the requirement that the proposer and the approver be different individuals — is the primary control against unauthorized record manipulation through correction entries. Every correction requires a complete audit trail documenting the original record, the corrected values, the root cause reference, the preparer and authorizer identities, the processing timestamp, and — for material corrections — an impact assessment.

The documentation and authorization requirements for corrections are not administrative burdens — they are the controls that make legitimate corrections verifiable and unauthorized corrections detectable. Without them, there is no mechanism to distinguish a correction made to fix a genuine error from one made to conceal unauthorized activity.

Study Support

How to Approach This Lesson

The two core skills in this lesson are: (1) selecting the appropriate correction type for a given break scenario, and (2) identifying the applicable authorization tier and required documentation for a given correction. Practice these through the exercises, focusing on the reasoning behind each selection rather than memorizing rules. The reasoning — what makes each correction type appropriate for its specific use case, and what makes each authorization tier appropriate for its risk level — is what enables you to handle novel scenarios that don't exactly match the textbook examples.

Key Patterns to Recognize

  • Rebook is the default for attribute errors; reversal is for transactions that should not exist; manual adjustment is last resort.
  • Prior-period always elevates the authorization tier, regardless of amount.
  • Preparer and approver must always be different individuals — no exceptions without documented emergency authorization.
  • Impact assessment is required for large corrections and prior-period entries — it triggers the downstream actions that follow from the correction.
  • Undocumented corrections are indistinguishable from unauthorized ones — documentation is the control, not the record-keeping overhead.

Practical Application

Application 1: Correction Entry Policy Design

Operations managers responsible for reconciliation functions are frequently tasked with designing or revising the firm's correction entry policy — the document that specifies the correction types, authorization tiers, segregation requirements, audit trail standards, and impact assessment obligations. Effective correction entry policies are specific enough to prevent ambiguity (a policy that says "corrections require approval" without specifying who can approve, at what dollar threshold, and in what documented form is not a functioning policy) but flexible enough to accommodate legitimate emergency situations without requiring pre-approval for every contingency. The best correction entry policies are designed with both the honest analyst and the potential bad actor in mind: they should make legitimate corrections efficient and well-documented, while making unauthorized corrections difficult to execute and easy to detect.

Application 2: Reviewing Correction Entry Activity for Anomalies

Compliance officers and internal auditors review correction entry activity periodically as part of their oversight of the reconciliation control environment. The review typically involves pulling the full correction entry log for the examination period and looking for anomaly patterns: corrections concentrated in a single account or security; corrections consistently made by the same individual at end of day or end of month (suggesting timing manipulation); corrections that increase a client's reported balance near performance calculation dates; manual adjustments without linked investigation records; and corrections where the preparer and approver are the same individual. These patterns are not proof of fraud but are indicators that require follow-up investigation to confirm that each correction was legitimate and properly authorized.

Application 3: Communicating Corrections to Clients

When a correction materially changes a client's account values, the client must be notified — either proactively (if the firm's policy or regulatory requirements mandate notification at a defined threshold) or in response to client inquiry when the corrected statement differs from a previously provided statement. Operations professionals who manage client-impacting corrections must be prepared to explain, in plain language, what was corrected, why the error occurred, what the correct values are, and what the firm has done to prevent recurrence. Client communication about corrections requires the same level of accuracy as the correction entry itself — overstating the impact, underexplaining the cause, or failing to provide the corrected values completely can compound the original error with a communication failure.

Application 4: Tax Implications of Prior-Period Corrections

Prior-period corrections that affect the cost basis, holding period, or income classification of securities can have significant tax implications for taxable accounts. A prior-period rebook that moves a trade from one month to the previous month may change the holding period calculation for capital gains classification. A correction that adjusts a dividend classification from qualified to ordinary may change the tax treatment of income previously reported. Operations teams that process prior-period corrections involving taxable accounts must ensure that the tax reporting function is notified as part of the impact assessment, so that amended tax documents can be prepared if required. Failure to notify the tax function of corrections affecting taxable income or cost basis can result in incorrect tax reporting — a regulatory obligation separate from the reconciliation requirement.

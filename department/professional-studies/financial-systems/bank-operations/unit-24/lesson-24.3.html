Lesson 24.3: Credit Memos, Write-Ups, and Loan Presentation Standards

Examine how banks summarize credit findings, loan structure, repayment sources, risks, and recommendations in formal approval documents.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Bank Operations Track Unit 24: Origination Workflow Foundations Lesson 24.3

Introduction

Once a lending file has been assembled, the bank needs a clear way to present the transaction for internal review and approval. That is the role of the credit memo, loan write-up, or formal loan presentation. These documents summarize the key facts of the request, explain the borrower and proposed structure, identify repayment sources and risk factors, and present a recommendation for action. They help decision-makers understand what the bank is being asked to approve and why the request should or should not move forward.

In banking operations, this matters because approval authority depends on clear communication. Even when the underlying analysis is sound, a weak presentation can lead to confusion, delays, or poor decisions. Approvers must be able to understand the borrower, the transaction, the risks, the mitigants, and any conditions or exceptions attached to the request. Credit memos and write-ups are therefore not just paperwork. They are part of how the bank converts analysis into a controlled institutional decision.

This lesson explains how formal loan presentations support origination and credit governance.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain what credit memos, loan write-ups, and loan presentation standards do, what information they typically contain, and why they are essential to clear approval review, risk communication, and lending discipline.

Lesson Overview

A credit memo is the structured summary that presents a lending request for review. It typically describes the borrower, the type and purpose of the loan, the amount and structure requested, the source of repayment, the major strengths and weaknesses of the transaction, and the analyst’s or lender’s recommendation. Depending on the institution and the type of loan, the format may be highly standardized or more narrative in style. Regardless of format, the purpose remains consistent: organize the information in a way that supports informed approval.

Loan presentation standards are the rules, templates, and expectations that shape how this information is communicated. These standards help the bank ensure that every request is presented with sufficient clarity, consistency, and completeness. Without such standards, approvals may depend too heavily on informal discussion, individual writing style, or inconsistent judgment. With strong presentation discipline, the bank improves decision quality, review efficiency, and institutional accountability.

Credit presentation is the bridge between analysis and approval.

What a Credit Memo Does

A credit memo takes the raw contents of the lending file and turns them into a decision-ready summary. Rather than requiring every approver to study every document in the full file from the beginning, the memo highlights the most important facts and organizes them into a coherent explanation. It tells the story of the credit request in a structured way. This includes who the borrower is, what the borrower wants, how repayment is expected to occur, what risks are present, and what action is being recommended.

This matters because approval decisions require interpretation, not just data storage. Financial statements, tax returns, collateral reports, and application forms may all be important, but they do not automatically explain themselves. The credit memo brings those materials together into a format that supports evaluation. It is the document that helps approvers move from information to judgment.

A credit memo makes the lending request understandable as a decision.

Loan Write-Ups Help Explain Both Structure and Risk

A good loan write-up does more than summarize the borrower. It also explains the structure of the proposed credit. This may include the requested amount, purpose, term, amortization, pricing, collateral support, guarantors, covenants, and other relevant conditions. In other words, the memo shows not only whether the borrower appears creditworthy, but also how the bank proposes to structure the exposure.

This is important because lending decisions are rarely about the borrower alone. The same borrower may represent different levels of risk depending on the facility design. A secured term loan may differ significantly from an unsecured revolving line. A short-term working capital request may need different protections than a long-term real estate facility. The write-up helps approvers evaluate whether the proposed structure fits the borrower’s needs, the bank’s policy, and the underlying risk profile.

Structure and credit quality must be presented together.

Repayment Sources Must Be Stated Clearly

One of the most important parts of any credit memo is the explanation of repayment. The bank must know how it expects to be repaid if the loan is approved. For a consumer borrower, repayment may come from salary or recurring household income. For a business borrower, it may come from operating cash flow, liquidation of receivables, sale of inventory, or project revenue. In some transactions, secondary or tertiary repayment support may come from collateral, guarantors, or other structural protections.

This matters because the core logic of lending rests on repayment capacity. Approvers need to understand not just that the borrower wants funds, but why repayment is expected to occur reliably. A memo that avoids this question or states repayment too vaguely weakens the quality of the presentation. By contrast, a strong memo explains the primary repayment source clearly and shows how other protections support the transaction if conditions worsen.

A lending request should be presented around repayment logic, not just requested dollars.

Risk Factors Need to Be Identified Honestly

Credit memos are not only supposed to highlight strengths. They must also identify weaknesses, uncertainties, and risk factors. These may include leverage concerns, income volatility, industry pressure, concentration risk, collateral limitations, policy exceptions, declining performance trends, or incomplete borrower history. A strong presentation does not hide these issues. It addresses them directly.

This matters because approval governance depends on transparency. Approvers are not well served by memos that advocate for a loan without acknowledging real risk. Instead, they need a balanced presentation that distinguishes between positive features and meaningful concerns. Risk factors do not automatically require denial, but they do require explanation. In many cases, the write-up should also show what mitigants, conditions, or structural protections offset those weaknesses.

Good credit presentation is balanced, not promotional.

Recommendations Turn Analysis Into a Proposed Action

A credit memo usually ends with a recommendation. That recommendation may be to approve, decline, defer pending more information, or approve subject to specific conditions. The recommendation gives decision-makers a clear proposed action based on the analysis presented in the memo. It also helps distinguish between descriptive reporting and actual credit judgment.

This recommendation matters because bank approval processes are not just informational. They are decision processes. Approvers need to know what the analyst, underwriter, or lending officer believes should happen and why. A strong recommendation is supported by the borrower analysis, repayment discussion, structure, risk review, and any relevant mitigants. It should not feel disconnected from the rest of the memo.

The recommendation is where the presentation becomes operationally decisive.

Standardized Presentation Supports Consistency

Banks often use standard templates for credit memos and loan write-ups. These templates may require sections on borrower overview, transaction summary, financial analysis, collateral, risk factors, policy compliance, exceptions, and recommendation. The purpose of standardization is not to make every loan identical. It is to ensure that key areas are addressed consistently across files and reviewers.

This matters because inconsistent presentation can weaken approval quality. If one memo discusses repayment in detail but another barely mentions it, approvers may receive uneven information even when the transactions are similar. Standardized presentation helps create discipline. It also makes it easier for committees, credit officers, and internal reviewers to compare transactions across different lenders, products, or business lines.

Templates support institutional consistency without eliminating judgment.

Presentation Standards Support Efficient Approval Review

Approvers and committees often review many transactions. They need loan presentations that are clear, focused, and organized in a way that supports efficient decision-making. Strong presentation standards help reviewers quickly locate essential information, understand the recommendation, and identify any areas requiring deeper discussion. A poorly structured memo, by contrast, can slow the process and increase the chance that important issues will be overlooked.

Efficiency matters operationally because approval processes are part of the broader origination workflow. If credit presentations are unclear, approvers may send files back for clarification, request repeated revisions, or delay decisions that should have been more straightforward. This slows the bank’s response time and can create frustration for both staff and borrowers. Good presentation standards therefore improve both control and workflow movement.

Clarity supports speed without sacrificing discipline.

Credit Memos Preserve the Reasoning Behind the Decision

A formal loan write-up is not useful only at the moment of approval. It also becomes part of the institutional record. Later teams may need to understand why the loan was approved, what risks were identified, what conditions were attached, and what repayment logic supported the decision. Documentation teams, servicing staff, portfolio managers, review functions, auditors, and regulators may all rely on this record.

This matters because lending decisions should remain understandable after the original discussion ends. A well-prepared memo preserves the reasoning behind the transaction. If performance later weakens, the bank can refer back to the original presentation to see what assumptions were made. If the relationship is renewed or modified, later reviewers can use the prior memo as a baseline for comparison. Presentation quality therefore affects not only approval, but also continuity across the credit lifecycle.

The memo records the institution’s lending judgment in durable form.

Loan Presentations Must Be Clear Enough for Different Audiences

Different people may review the same credit memo for different reasons. A lender may focus on relationship context. An underwriter may focus on risk quality. A credit officer may focus on policy and exposure level. A committee may focus on concentration, exceptions, or overall recommendation logic. Because of this, loan presentations must be clear enough to support multiple audiences without becoming vague or overloaded.

This matters because a memo that works only for the original preparer is not a strong institutional document. The write-up must explain the request in a way that another qualified reviewer can understand. That includes clear terminology, logical organization, and sufficient explanation of both facts and analysis. A good presentation communicates across functions, not just within one role.

Internal lending documents must support shared understanding.

Presentation Standards Help Reveal Missing Analysis

One practical advantage of structured credit memos is that they expose gaps. If the preparer cannot clearly explain repayment, cannot identify the borrower’s key risks, or cannot describe why the proposed structure is appropriate, the problem may be the transaction itself or the incompleteness of the analysis. Templates and standards help reveal these weaknesses before approval occurs.

This matters because presentation is also a quality-control tool. A weak memo may signal that the lending file is incomplete, that the structure has not been thought through carefully, or that the recommendation is not adequately supported. By requiring analysts and lenders to present the request coherently, the bank improves the quality of the analysis behind the memo. Presentation discipline therefore strengthens underwriting discipline.

Sometimes the memo shows that the real issue is not writing, but unresolved credit thinking.

A Simple Example

Imagine a bank reviewing a request for a small business working capital line. The assembled lending file includes financial statements, tax returns, ownership records, and information about the business’s operating cycle. The credit memo then summarizes the borrower’s background, the requested line amount, the purpose of the facility, the expected source of repayment, the borrower’s recent cash flow performance, the collateral support available, and the key risks affecting the relationship. It may also note that approval is recommended subject to signed borrowing resolutions, periodic financial reporting, and completion of lien documentation.

This example shows why the credit memo matters. The underlying file may contain dozens of pages, but the memo turns that information into an organized presentation that supports a real decision. It helps the approver understand not just the borrower, but the bank’s full recommended position.

That is the practical role of a well-prepared loan write-up.

Why This Matters

Students studying origination workflows need to understand that lending decisions are not made from raw files alone. Banks rely on formal presentations that explain the request, organize the analysis, and propose an action. Credit memos, write-ups, and presentation standards are the tools that make this possible. They support approval quality, review efficiency, documentation continuity, and institutional accountability.

This lesson also sets up the next parts of the unit. Later lessons will examine approval authority, documentation conditions, exception tracking, and closing readiness. Each of those stages depends on the quality of the loan presentation. If the write-up is unclear, later control and execution will also suffer. That is why formal credit presentation is a core part of origination operations rather than a mere reporting formality.

A good loan presentation helps the bank think clearly before it commits capital.

What Good Basic Interpretation Looks Like

A strong interpretation should explain that credit memos and loan write-ups are formal documents used to present lending requests for approval. They summarize the borrower, the transaction structure, the expected repayment source, the key risks and mitigants, and the recommendation being made to the bank. Students should recognize that these documents help turn a lending file into a decision-ready presentation.

Students should also understand that presentation standards matter because they promote clarity, consistency, and accountability across approval workflows. Most importantly, they should see that a strong memo does not merely advocate for a loan. It communicates balanced analysis in a form that supports sound institutional judgment.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking a credit memo is just a summary sheet

A good memo is not only descriptive. It interprets the borrower, the structure, the repayment logic, the risk profile, and the recommendation.

Assuming the purpose of the memo is to sell the loan internally

The goal is not promotion alone. It is balanced presentation that helps decision-makers understand both strengths and weaknesses.

Believing presentation quality matters only at approval

The memo also becomes part of the institutional record used later for documentation, servicing, monitoring, renewal, and review.

Key Terms

Credit Memo — A formal document that summarizes a lending request, borrower analysis, transaction structure, repayment logic, risks, and recommendation for approval review.

Loan Write-Up — A written presentation of a proposed loan that organizes the key facts, analytical findings, and approval request into a decision-ready format.

Loan Presentation Standards — The templates, required sections, and communication expectations that guide how credit requests are presented internally.

Repayment Source Analysis — The explanation of how the borrower is expected to repay the loan, including primary and secondary repayment support where relevant.

Risk and Mitigant Discussion — The section of a credit presentation that identifies transaction weaknesses and explains the factors or structures that help offset them.

Approval Recommendation — The proposed action in a loan memo, such as approve, decline, or approve subject to conditions, supported by the underlying analysis.

Lesson Summary

  • Credit memos and loan write-ups turn assembled lending files into structured approval presentations.
  • These documents explain the borrower, transaction structure, repayment sources, risks, mitigants, and recommended action.
  • Clear presentation standards support consistency, efficient review, and stronger institutional control.
  • Balanced discussion of both strengths and weaknesses is essential to sound credit communication.
  • The recommendation in the memo connects analysis to a proposed institutional decision.
  • Loan presentations continue to matter after approval because they preserve the reasoning behind the credit decision.

Lesson 36.6: Balance Sheet Management, Limits, and ALM Governance

Learn how banks use policies, committees, scenario analysis, and risk limits to monitor and control structural interest rate exposure.

Where This Lesson Fits

Previous lessons explained how duration mismatch, repricing gaps, deposit behavior, and balance sheet valuation create interest rate exposure within banks.

This lesson focuses on how institutions control and manage those exposures through governance frameworks, policy structures, and risk limits.

Asset–liability management is not simply an analytical process. It is also a governance discipline that requires oversight, decision-making structures, and institutional accountability.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should understand how banks govern asset–liability management activities and how risk limits and policy frameworks help control interest rate exposure.

Lesson Overview

Banks continuously make balance sheet decisions through lending, deposit gathering, investment activity, and funding management.

Without clear governance structures, these individual decisions could gradually create unintended financial exposures.

Asset–liability management governance ensures that balance sheet risks remain aligned with the institution’s strategic objectives and risk tolerance.

ALM Policies

Most banks maintain formal asset–liability management policies that define how interest rate risk should be monitored and controlled.

These policies establish measurement methodologies, scenario analysis requirements, reporting standards, and acceptable exposure levels.

The policy framework ensures that the institution evaluates balance sheet risk consistently and transparently.

Risk Limits

Risk limits are an important component of asset–liability management governance.

These limits define acceptable levels of exposure under different interest rate scenarios.

For example, a bank may limit how much net interest income can decline if interest rates increase by a specified amount.

Similarly, limits may be placed on economic value sensitivity to ensure that the balance sheet remains resilient under changing market conditions.

The Asset–Liability Committee (ALCO)

Most banks oversee asset–liability management through a governance body known as the Asset–Liability Committee, often abbreviated as ALCO.

ALCO typically includes senior leaders from treasury, finance, risk management, lending operations, and executive management.

The committee reviews balance sheet positioning, evaluates interest rate risk reports, and guides strategic decisions affecting funding and asset composition.

Scenario Analysis and Stress Testing

Governance frameworks often require regular scenario analysis and stress testing.

These exercises examine how the balance sheet might perform under different interest rate environments, including sudden rate increases, declines, or sustained volatility.

Stress testing helps management identify vulnerabilities and prepare mitigation strategies before adverse conditions occur.

Balance Sheet Strategy

Asset–liability governance also influences broader balance sheet strategy.

Management decisions about loan pricing, deposit products, investment maturity structure, and funding sources all affect interest rate exposure.

Through policy oversight and ALCO coordination, the institution can align these operational decisions with long-term financial stability.

Monitoring and Reporting

Effective governance requires continuous monitoring and reporting of interest rate risk indicators.

Treasury and risk management teams typically prepare regular reports describing balance sheet sensitivity, scenario analysis results, and changes in funding conditions.

These reports allow management and oversight committees to track exposure trends and respond to emerging risks.

Why Governance Matters

Interest rate risk develops gradually through everyday banking activities.

Without clear oversight structures, small imbalances can accumulate and eventually threaten earnings stability or capital strength.

Strong governance ensures that the institution continuously evaluates its financial structure and maintains disciplined balance sheet management.

What Good Basic Interpretation Looks Like

Students should understand that asset–liability management is both an analytical process and a governance framework.

Policies, committees, reporting systems, and risk limits work together to ensure that balance sheet risks remain controlled and transparent.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking ALM is only a treasury function

Asset–liability management involves coordination across finance, risk management, lending, and executive leadership.

Assuming risk limits eliminate exposure

Limits do not remove risk entirely; they establish acceptable boundaries for managing it.

Believing governance only matters during financial stress

Governance structures are designed to operate continuously so that exposures remain controlled before problems emerge.

Key Terms

Asset–Liability Management Policy — A framework that defines how interest rate risk should be monitored and controlled.

Risk Limits — Predefined boundaries that restrict how much financial exposure the institution is willing to accept.

Asset–Liability Committee (ALCO) — A governance body responsible for reviewing balance sheet risk and guiding funding and asset strategies.

Scenario Analysis — Modeling exercises that evaluate how financial outcomes may change under different interest rate environments.

Balance Sheet Strategy — The coordinated management of assets, liabilities, and funding sources to achieve financial objectives.

Lesson Summary

  • Asset–liability management requires both analysis and governance.
  • ALM policies define how interest rate risk is measured and monitored.
  • Risk limits establish acceptable exposure levels.
  • The Asset–Liability Committee provides oversight and strategic guidance.
  • Scenario analysis and reporting help banks monitor balance sheet risk over time.

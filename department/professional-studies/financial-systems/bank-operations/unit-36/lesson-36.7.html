Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should understand how asset–liability management combines balance sheet structure, funding behavior, risk measurement, and governance oversight to manage interest rate risk in banking institutions.

Interest Rate Risk as a Structural Feature of Banking

Interest rate risk is a natural feature of the banking business model.

Banks typically fund long-term assets such as loans with shorter-term liabilities such as deposits. This maturity transformation allows banks to generate interest income but also creates sensitivity to changes in market interest rates.

Asset–liability management exists to monitor and control this structural exposure.

Balance Sheet Timing Differences

Interest rate risk often begins with timing differences between assets and liabilities.

Loans may remain fixed for several years, while deposits can reprice more quickly or move between institutions.

These mismatches create the repricing gaps and duration differences that influence how the bank’s earnings respond to rate changes.

Behavioral Dynamics of Deposits and Borrowers

Customer behavior adds an additional layer of complexity to balance sheet risk.

Depositors may move funds when higher yields become available, while borrowers may refinance loans when interest rates fall.

Because these behaviors are not entirely predictable, asset–liability managers rely on historical patterns and modeling assumptions to estimate how customers may respond under different interest rate environments.

Earnings and Valuation Sensitivity

Interest rate movements influence both short-term earnings and long-term financial value.

Net interest income analysis evaluates how changes in rates may affect near-term profitability.

Economic value analysis evaluates how rate movements influence the present value of asset and liability cash flows across the balance sheet.

Together, these measurements provide a comprehensive view of financial sensitivity.

Governance and Institutional Oversight

Interest rate risk management is not solely a technical exercise. It requires institutional governance and oversight.

Asset–liability committees review balance sheet positioning, evaluate risk reports, and guide strategic decisions related to funding and asset structure.

Policies and exposure limits help ensure that balance sheet risks remain aligned with the institution’s overall risk tolerance.

The Role of Treasury and Risk Management

Treasury and risk management teams play central roles in asset–liability management.

They monitor funding conditions, prepare risk reports, perform scenario analysis, and communicate balance sheet trends to senior management.

Through this process, operational decisions about lending, deposit pricing, and investment strategy remain aligned with the bank’s overall financial stability objectives.

Interest Rate Risk in the Operating Model

Interest rate risk management is embedded within the daily operating model of banking institutions.

Loan pricing, deposit strategy, investment portfolio management, liquidity planning, and treasury operations all contribute to the bank’s overall balance sheet sensitivity.

Asset–liability management coordinates these activities so that the institution can maintain stable earnings and financial resilience as market conditions evolve.

Why Interest Rate Risk Management Matters

Banks are central institutions in financial systems. They provide deposits, credit, payment services, and financial intermediation for households and businesses.

If interest rate risk is poorly managed, sudden rate changes could weaken bank profitability or financial stability.

Strong asset–liability management ensures that banks remain resilient even when economic conditions shift.

What Good Basic Interpretation Looks Like

Students should understand that interest rate risk management integrates analytical tools, behavioral insights, and governance structures.

Asset–liability management allows banks to measure exposures, anticipate financial effects, and maintain disciplined balance sheet management across changing interest rate environments.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking interest rate risk is temporary

Interest rate exposure is a permanent feature of banking due to maturity transformation.

Assuming ALM only involves technical modeling

Asset–liability management also requires governance, oversight, and coordinated institutional decision-making.

Believing interest rate risk only affects earnings

Interest rate changes can affect both profitability and balance sheet value.

Key Terms

Interest Rate Risk — The possibility that changes in market interest rates will affect a bank’s earnings or balance sheet value.

Duration Mismatch — A difference in the timing sensitivity of assets and liabilities to interest rate changes.

Deposit Repricing — The adjustment of deposit interest rates in response to market rate changes.

Net Interest Income Sensitivity — The change in earnings resulting from different interest rate scenarios.

Asset–Liability Governance — Oversight structures and policies that control balance sheet risk.

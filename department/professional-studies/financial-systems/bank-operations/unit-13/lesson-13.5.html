Bank Operations Track • Unit 13: Deposit Operations and Servicing

Lesson 13.5: Service Requests, Case Handling, and Exception Processing

Study how banks receive, route, document, and resolve deposit-related service requests and operational exceptions.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Bank Operations Track Unit 13: Deposit Operations and Servicing Lesson 13.5

Where This Lesson Fits

The previous lessons explained how deposit servicing teams maintain account records, communicate with customers through statements and notices, and apply restrictions or holds when necessary. These processes help keep deposit accounts accurate and operationally secure.

However, many servicing situations require additional investigation or coordination. Customers may request assistance with account activity, report an issue, or need help resolving a problem. In other cases, the bank itself may detect an operational exception that requires review.

This lesson explains how banks manage these situations through structured service request and case handling processes.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how service requests are received and managed, how operational cases are tracked, and how banks resolve deposit-related exceptions.

Lesson Overview

Not every servicing situation can be resolved instantly. Some customer issues require investigation, documentation review, or coordination across multiple teams. To manage these situations, banks use case handling systems and operational workflows.

A case represents a documented servicing situation that must be reviewed and resolved. Cases may originate from customer requests, internal monitoring systems, or operational reviews.

Case management helps ensure that issues are tracked, investigated, and resolved systematically.

Service Requests

A service request occurs when a customer asks the bank to perform an action or investigate a question related to their account. Requests may involve transaction clarification, document copies, account corrections, or assistance with account activity.

These requests may originate through several channels, including branches, contact centers, online banking platforms, or written communication.

When a request cannot be resolved immediately, it is typically recorded as a servicing case so it can be investigated and completed through operational workflows.

Case Creation and Documentation

When a service request requires follow-up, the bank creates a case record. This record documents the issue, the account involved, and the actions required to resolve the request.

The case may include supporting information such as transaction details, customer communication history, or related documentation.

Proper documentation ensures that employees reviewing the case understand the situation and can continue the investigation if the issue is transferred between teams.

Routing and Workflow Management

Once a case is created, it is typically routed to the appropriate team responsible for resolving the issue. Different types of cases may be handled by different operational units.

For example, deposit servicing teams may investigate account activity questions, while fraud teams review suspicious transactions. Technology teams may become involved if a system issue is suspected.

Routing systems help ensure that cases reach the appropriate specialists who can resolve them efficiently.

Exception Processing

An exception occurs when a transaction or operational process does not follow the expected pattern. Exceptions may result from errors, unusual account activity, or system processing issues.

For example, a transaction might fail to post correctly, or a deposit may require manual review. These situations require employees to examine the issue and determine the appropriate corrective action.

Exception processing helps maintain the accuracy and reliability of account records.

Investigation and Resolution

Once a case or exception has been identified, employees investigate the situation by reviewing account records, transaction logs, and related documentation. They may also communicate with the customer or other internal teams.

The goal of the investigation is to identify the cause of the issue and determine how it should be resolved. Resolution may involve correcting account records, reversing transactions, providing documentation, or explaining account activity to the customer.

Once the issue is resolved, the case is closed and the resolution is recorded for future reference.

Tracking and Accountability

Case management systems track the progress of service requests and operational exceptions. These systems record when cases are opened, which teams are responsible for resolving them, and when they are completed.

Tracking helps ensure that issues are not forgotten and that customers receive timely responses. It also allows managers to monitor operational performance and identify recurring problems.

This accountability supports consistent service quality across the institution.

A Simple Example

Imagine a customer notices a transaction on their statement that they do not recognize. They contact the bank and request clarification.

The employee receiving the request opens a servicing case and records the details of the inquiry. The case is routed to a deposit operations team that reviews the transaction history.

After investigating, the team determines that the transaction was a merchant authorization related to a recent purchase. The explanation is communicated to the customer, and the case is closed.

This process demonstrates how service requests and case management help resolve customer questions efficiently.

What Good Basic Interpretation Looks Like

A strong interpretation should explain that banks manage service requests and operational issues through structured case handling systems. Students should recognize that cases allow banks to track problems, coordinate investigations, and ensure that customer concerns are resolved properly.

They should also understand that exception processing helps correct unusual transactions or operational problems that occur during account servicing.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking service issues are resolved informally

Banks usually document service issues through case systems so they can be tracked and resolved properly.

Assuming exceptions mean something is wrong with the account

Exceptions simply indicate that a transaction or process requires additional review.

Believing only customer complaints create cases

Cases may also originate from internal monitoring systems or operational reviews.

Key Terms

Service Request — A customer request for assistance or action related to an account.

Case Management — The process of tracking and resolving operational issues through documented cases.

Operational Exception — A situation in which a transaction or process requires manual review.

Case Routing — The process of directing a case to the appropriate team responsible for resolving it.

Resolution — The final action taken to resolve a service request or operational issue.

Lesson Summary

  • Banks manage customer service issues through structured service request and case handling systems.
  • Cases document issues that require investigation or operational follow-up.
  • Routing systems send cases to the teams responsible for resolving them.
  • Exception processing addresses unusual transactions or operational irregularities.
  • Tracking systems ensure accountability and help banks resolve issues efficiently.

Lesson 3.5: Net Interest Income and Banking Earnings

Understand how banks earn income from the spread between asset yields and funding costs, and why net interest income is central to bank profitability.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Bank Operations Track Unit 3: Bank Balance Sheets and Accounting Lesson 3.5

Where This Lesson Fits

The previous lessons explained the structure of the bank balance sheet and examined assets, liabilities, and equity as the major parts of that structure. This lesson now turns from position to performance by examining how banks earn money through their balance sheets.

Traditional banking profitability depends heavily on the relationship between earning assets and funding costs. Banks collect funds through liabilities, especially deposits and borrowings, and deploy those funds into assets such as loans and securities. The difference between what the bank earns and what it pays is a major source of operating income.

This lesson provides the foundation for later study of reserves, profitability interpretation, and full financial reporting.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain what net interest income is, describe how it arises from the spread between asset yields and funding costs, and understand why it is central to traditional bank earnings.

Lesson Overview

A core part of banking income comes from interest. Banks earn interest on many of their assets, especially loans and certain securities. They also pay interest on many liabilities, including some deposits and borrowed funds.

Net interest income is the amount that remains after interest expense is subtracted from interest income. This means it captures the spread between what the bank earns on its interest-earning assets and what it pays to fund those assets.

Because banking often depends on transforming lower-cost funding into higher-yielding assets, net interest income is one of the clearest expressions of the bank business model.

What Net Interest Income Means

Net interest income is a measure of spread-based earnings. It begins with interest income generated from assets such as loans, securities, and sometimes reserve balances. From that amount, the bank subtracts interest expense paid on liabilities such as deposits, wholesale borrowings, or long-term debt.

The result shows how much the bank earned from the basic act of financial intermediation. If asset yields are meaningfully above funding costs, net interest income can be strong. If funding becomes more expensive or asset yields fall, net interest income may tighten.

This makes net interest income highly sensitive to balance sheet structure, rates, and funding mix.

The Basic Earnings Logic of Banking

Traditional banking works in large part by gathering funds at one cost and deploying them at a higher return. A bank may receive deposits at relatively low cost and then use those funds to make loans that earn a higher rate of interest. That difference contributes to earnings.

This spread-based logic does not mean all assets are equally profitable or all liabilities are equally cheap. It also does not mean every funding source produces the same result. But the basic model is straightforward: banks seek to generate income from the difference between asset returns and liability costs.

Net interest income is the accounting expression of that difference.

Interest Income: The Asset Side of Earnings

Interest income comes mainly from the bank's earning assets. Loans are often the most important source because they usually generate recurring interest payments over time. Securities may also provide income, depending on their type and yield. In some environments, reserve balances may contribute interest as well.

The amount of interest income a bank earns depends on both the size of its earning asset base and the rates or yields attached to those assets. A larger loan portfolio can produce more income, but only if credit quality remains sound and pricing supports returns.

This means asset composition matters not only for risk and liquidity, but also for the bank's revenue structure.

Interest Expense: The Funding Cost Side

Interest expense reflects what the bank pays to obtain or maintain funding. Some deposits pay interest. Borrowed funds usually do as well. As the bank relies more on interest-bearing liabilities, its funding costs rise.

The size of interest expense depends on the amount of funding, the type of funding, and the rate the bank must pay. A bank with a stable, lower-cost deposit base may enjoy an advantage over a bank that depends heavily on more expensive borrowed funds.

This is why liability structure and pricing are directly tied to profitability. The earnings story is not only about what the bank owns, but also about what it owes and what that funding costs.

Net Interest Spread and Margin Thinking

Students often understand net interest income more clearly by thinking in terms of spread. If the average yield on assets is higher than the average cost of liabilities, the bank generates a positive spread. That positive spread supports earnings.

Banks also often analyze profitability using concepts such as net interest margin, which relates net interest income to the amount of earning assets. At this stage, students do not need advanced formulas, but they should understand that banks monitor both the absolute amount of net interest income and its relationship to the asset base generating it.

A bank can therefore care about both size and efficiency in spread-based earnings.

Why Net Interest Income Is Central to Banking

Net interest income is central because it reflects the core intermediation function of banking. A bank that cannot earn an adequate spread between asset returns and funding costs will struggle to produce sustainable profitability, especially if it faces operating costs, credit losses, or compliance expenses.

Although banks may also earn fee income, service charges, trading income, or other non-interest revenue, net interest income remains foundational in many banking models. It is often the main recurring earnings engine.

This is especially true for institutions whose business is centered on deposits and lending.

How Balance Sheet Structure Affects Earnings

Because net interest income comes from the relationship between assets and liabilities, balance sheet structure matters greatly. A bank holding more higher-yield loans may generate greater interest income, but it may also take more credit risk. A bank with more low-cost deposits may reduce interest expense, but that funding mix depends on market conditions, customer behavior, and competition.

Changes in rates can also shift the earnings picture. If liability costs rise faster than asset yields, net interest income may be squeezed. If asset yields adjust upward while funding remains relatively stable, net interest income may improve.

The bank's earnings are therefore tied closely to how its balance sheet is built and managed.

A Simple Example

Imagine a bank earns $12 million in interest income from loans and securities over a period. During the same period, it pays $7 million in interest expense on deposits and borrowed funds. Its net interest income is $5 million.

That $5 million represents the net amount earned from interest-based intermediation before considering other operating costs, provisions, taxes, or non-interest items. It shows the contribution of the balance sheet's spread structure to earnings.

Even a simple example makes clear that both asset yield and funding cost must be understood together.

Net Interest Income Is Not the Same as Total Profit

Students should be careful not to confuse net interest income with final profit. Net interest income is an important earnings measure, but it is not the whole picture. Banks also face operating expenses, credit loss provisions, taxes, and other financial effects. They may also earn non-interest income from fees or services.

This means a bank can have positive net interest income and still report weak overall profitability if other costs are large. Conversely, a bank with thinner spread income may still support earnings through fee businesses or efficient cost control.

Net interest income is central, but it is one major part of broader profitability analysis.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking banks earn money only by charging interest on loans

Loan interest is important, but banking earnings also depend on funding costs, securities income, fee revenue, and other factors.

Assuming net interest income and profit are the same thing

Net interest income is a major earnings component, but total profit also depends on expenses, credit costs, taxes, and non-interest revenue.

Treating funding costs as secondary

Funding cost is central to bank profitability because net interest income depends on the spread between what the bank earns and what it pays.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Basic Calculation

Exercise 2: Spread Thinking

How could rising deposit rates affect a bank's net interest income if loan yields do not rise by the same amount?

Exercise 3: Earnings Interpretation

Why is net interest income considered a core expression of the banking business model?

Key Terms

Net Interest Income — The difference between interest income earned on assets and interest expense paid on liabilities.

Interest Income — Income earned from interest-bearing assets such as loans and securities.

Interest Expense — The cost paid on interest-bearing liabilities such as deposits and borrowings.

Asset Yield — The return or rate earned on an asset or group of assets.

Funding Cost — The rate or expense the bank incurs to obtain and maintain funding.

Lesson Summary

  • Net interest income is the difference between interest earned on assets and interest paid on liabilities.
  • It reflects the spread-based earnings logic at the center of traditional banking.
  • Both asset yields and funding costs shape the level of net interest income.
  • Balance sheet structure strongly influences profitability because it determines earning assets and funding mix.
  • Net interest income is central to banking earnings, but it is not the same as total profit.

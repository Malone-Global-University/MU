Unit 2: Foundations of Real Estate

Understand the structure of real estate as an investment category. This unit introduces property rights, ownership structures, asset types, income generation, industry participants, and the role of real estate in a diversified portfolio.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Real Estate Investing Track Unit 2: Foundations of Real Estate

Unit Overview

Before students can analyze markets, underwrite properties, or structure deals, they need a clear understanding of what real estate actually is. Real estate is more than physical buildings. It is a bundle of legal rights, a source of income, a capital asset, and an operating business shaped by location, use, and market demand.

This unit introduces the essential structure of the field. Students study how ownership works, how different property categories behave, how income is produced, and how investors, lenders, brokers, managers, and developers interact across the real estate ecosystem.

What You’ll Learn

  • Why real estate behaves differently from stocks, bonds, and other assets
  • How property rights and ownership structures shape control and risk
  • The major categories of real estate assets and how they differ economically
  • How real estate produces income and creates investment value
  • Who participates in the real estate industry and what roles they play
  • How real estate fits into broader portfolio construction and diversification

Lessons in This Unit

  • Lesson 2.1: What Makes Real Estate a Unique Asset Class

    Explore the characteristics that distinguish real estate from other investments, including illiquidity, leverage, local market dependence, and operating complexity.

  • Lesson 2.2: Property Rights and Ownership Structures

    Learn how legal ownership works through title, deeds, fee interests, leasehold interests, and common entity structures used to hold real estate.

  • Lesson 2.3: Types of Real Estate Assets

    Survey the major property categories, including residential, multifamily, office, retail, industrial, land, and specialty assets.

  • Lesson 2.4: How Real Estate Generates Income

    Understand how rent, reimbursements, ancillary income, and appreciation potential combine to create economic return in real estate investments.

  • Lesson 2.5: Participants in the Real Estate Industry

    Identify the roles of investors, lenders, brokers, appraisers, developers, property managers, attorneys, contractors, and other market participants.

  • Lesson 2.6: Real Estate in a Diversified Portfolio

    Examine why investors allocate capital to real estate and how it can contribute income, inflation protection, diversification, and risk concentration at the same time.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to explain how real estate functions as both a legal interest and an economic asset, distinguish among major property types, and understand the basic structure of the industry in which deals are sourced, financed, operated, and sold.

  • Templates & Tools

    Use classification worksheets and asset comparison tools to organize ownership structures, property types, and sources of real estate income.

  • Glossary Support

    Review key terms such as fee simple, leasehold, asset class, multifamily, industrial, sponsor, and diversification.

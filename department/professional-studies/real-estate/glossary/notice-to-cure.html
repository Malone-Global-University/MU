Notice to Cure

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Notice to Cure

Definition

A notice to cure is a written notice that identifies a specific lease or contract default and provides a defined period to fix (cure) the issue before the landlord (or other enforcing party) can escalate enforcement (such as termination, collections, or eviction).

What “Cure” Means

To cure a default is to correct the problem within the allowed timeframe and comply with the lease going forward. Depending on the default, curing may require:

  • Payment of amounts due (rent, fees, utilities, or reimbursements).
  • Stopping prohibited behavior (noise, unauthorized occupants, illegal activity).
  • Reversing an unauthorized condition (removing an unapproved pet, undoing alterations, restoring cleanliness).
  • Providing access for repairs or inspections when the lease requires it.

Common Reasons Landlords Issue a Notice to Cure

  • Nonpayment or partial payment (sometimes handled with a separate “pay or quit” notice, depending on jurisdiction).
  • Unauthorized occupants or subletting.
  • Unauthorized pets or repeated pet-related violations.
  • Nuisance or disturbances: chronic noise, harassment, or repeated complaints.
  • Lease violations: smoking, parking, trash, storage, or prohibited items.
  • Damage or poor housekeeping that creates health/safety risk.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Collections and delinquency control: formal steps can improve compliance and reduce loss.
  • Process discipline: notices create a paper trail that supports enforcement if escalation becomes necessary.
  • Portfolio risk: inconsistent notice practices can increase legal exposure and delay resolutions.
  • Tenant relations: clear, fair notices can correct behavior early without litigation.

Operational Best Practices

  • Be specific: cite the lease clause, describe the violation, and state what “cure” requires.
  • Use dates and evidence: include incident dates, photos, logs, and third-party notes when appropriate.
  • State the deadline clearly: the cure period is time-sensitive; ambiguity creates risk.
  • Deliver properly: follow required service methods (posting, mail, personal service, electronic where permitted).
  • Offer a compliance path: where appropriate, include steps the tenant can take and who to contact.
  • Document everything: keep copies of notices, proofs of service, and follow-up inspection notes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Wrong notice type: different defaults may require different notice language and timelines.
  • Vague allegations: “noise complaints” without dates/details is harder to enforce.
  • Bad service: improper delivery can reset the process and delay enforcement.
  • Retaliation optics: issuing notices after repair requests can look retaliatory; keep clean documentation and consistent policy.

Notice to Cure vs. Notice to Quit

A notice to cure gives an opportunity to fix the default. A notice to quit typically demands the tenant vacate (often used for severe or non-curable violations, or repeat offenses). The exact terminology and rules vary by jurisdiction, but the key difference is whether a meaningful cure period is provided.

Example

A tenant has an unauthorized pet in a no-pet building. Management issues a notice stating the lease clause, the observed condition, and the required cure: remove the pet and confirm in writing by the deadline, followed by a re-inspection. If cured, the tenancy continues; if not, the landlord may escalate.

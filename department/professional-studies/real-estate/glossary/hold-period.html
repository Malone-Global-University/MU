Hold Period

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Hold Period

Definition

The hold period is the length of time an investor owns a property (or investment) before exiting—typically through a sale, a refinance, or a recapitalization. Hold period assumptions shape the entire underwriting model because they determine how long cash flows compound and when investors receive their return of capital.

Where Hold Period Shows Up in Underwriting

  • Pro forma timeline: the number of years/months modeled for operations and rent growth.
  • Exit valuation: the year the property is sold and the assumed exit cap rate or pricing multiple.
  • Debt strategy: loan term, extension options, interest-only periods, and refinance timing.
  • Capex plan: when renovations occur and when rent premiums are realized.

Why It Matters

Hold period heavily influences performance metrics—especially IRR, which is highly sensitive to timing. Two deals with the same total profit can have very different IRRs depending on how quickly the profit is realized.

How hold period affects common return metrics

  • IRR: shorter hold with earlier cash flows tends to increase IRR (all else equal).
  • Equity multiple: longer holds can increase multiple if cash flow grows and value increases.
  • Cash-on-cash: depends on annual distributable cash flow; may rise as NOI increases over time.
  • Risk exposure: longer holds mean more cycles, refinancing risk, capex risk, and policy/regulatory risk.

Common Hold Periods by Strategy

  • Fix-and-flip: short-term (months to ~2 years), focused on execution speed and resale.
  • Value-add multifamily: often medium-term (commonly ~3–7 years), tied to renovation and stabilization.
  • Core / long-term hold: longer-term (often 7–15+ years), focused on durable cash flow and preservation.
  • Development: hold can be short (build-to-sell) or long (build-to-hold after stabilization).

Example

An investor buys an apartment property and expects NOI to grow after renovations. They model a 5-year hold with a sale in Year 5 based on a conservative exit cap rate.

  • Year 1–2: higher capex, lease-up friction, and lower distributions.
  • Year 3–4: stabilization, rent premiums realized, distributions increase.
  • Year 5: sale proceeds are returned to investors, driving most of total returns.

If the hold extends to Year 7 due to market conditions, investors may receive more operating cash flow, but the IRR can fall if the exit is delayed—even if the final profit is similar.

Decision Factors That Set Hold Period

  • Business plan duration: how long it takes to renovate, lease up, and stabilize.
  • Debt maturity: loan term and extensions often create “decision points.”
  • Capital markets: interest rates, cap rates, and buyer demand at potential disposition dates.
  • Tax planning: depreciation, 1031 exchange goals, and timing of gains recognition.
  • Asset quality: durability of cash flow and future capex needs.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using hold period to “make returns work”: stretching the hold to force an IRR target can hide weak fundamentals.
  • Exit cap rate complacency: long holds still require realistic exit assumptions and buyer expectations.
  • Refinance timing risk: assuming a refi at Year 3 without considering rates, DSCR, and lender appetite.
  • Capex blind spots: longer holds often require additional replacement reserves and major repairs.
  • Ignoring market cycle: forcing a sale in a down market can destroy proceeds.

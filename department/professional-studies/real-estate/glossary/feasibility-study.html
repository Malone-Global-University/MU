Feasibility Study

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Feasibility Study

Definition

A feasibility study is a structured analysis used to determine whether a real estate project or investment is practical and financially viable. It tests the deal from multiple angles: market demand, site constraints, entitlement risk, construction/renovation costs, financing terms, timeline, and whether the expected returns justify the risk.

In plain terms, feasibility answers: Should we do this? If yes, it also clarifies what exactly to build or execute, how much it should cost, and what must go right.

What a Feasibility Study Typically Covers

1) Market Feasibility

  • Demand: renter/buyer depth, household growth, employment drivers, competing supply.
  • Pricing: achievable rents/sales prices, concessions, absorption, renewal trends.
  • Product fit: unit mix, finishes, amenities, parking, and target customer.

2) Legal & Entitlement Feasibility

  • Zoning and use: permitted uses, density, setbacks, height, parking rules.
  • Approval path: by-right vs discretionary approvals, hearings, design review requirements.
  • Conditions and fees: impact fees, infrastructure obligations, affordable housing requirements.

3) Physical & Technical Feasibility

  • Site constraints: topography, access, easements, utilities, stormwater capacity.
  • Environmental: contamination, wetlands, habitat, noise, geotechnical risk.
  • Existing building condition (if value-add): remaining useful life of roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical.

4) Financial Feasibility

  • Cost model: hard costs, soft costs, contingency, escalation, and timing of spend.
  • Capital stack: debt terms, equity requirements, reserves, covenants, draw schedule.
  • Return metrics: IRR, equity multiple, cash-on-cash, DSCR, yield-on-cost, break-even occupancy.
  • Sensitivity cases: rent down, cap rate up, cost up, timeline delay, lease-up slower.

Feasibility vs. Highest & Best Use

Highest & Best Use (HBU) identifies the use that is legally permissible, physically possible, financially feasible, and maximally productive. A feasibility study is the deeper work that tests whether that use (or several alternatives) actually pencils with real assumptions, costs, and timelines.

Why It Matters

Feasibility is how you avoid the most expensive mistake in real estate: being right about the concept but wrong about the numbers, timeline, or risk. It makes assumptions explicit, highlights the true constraints, and forces the project to “earn the right” to consume capital.

Investor Uses

  • Go / no-go decisions: kill weak deals early before sunk costs compound.
  • Deal structuring: price adjustments, options, contingencies, and milestone-based closings.
  • Scope optimization: cut low-ROI features, adjust unit mix, phase improvements.
  • Risk budgeting: set contingency levels and reserves based on real risk drivers.
  • Partner alignment: clear plan for lenders, equity partners, architects, and contractors.

Example

An investor considers converting an older office building into multifamily. The feasibility study tests:

  • Market: whether target rents support the new unit mix and amenity level.
  • Legal: whether residential use is permitted and what approvals are required.
  • Physical: whether floor plates allow natural light, plumbing stacking, and code-compliant egress.
  • Financial: whether the all-in cost plus carry produces a return above the investor’s required rate.

If the project works only in the “perfect” scenario, it is not feasible—it is fragile.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using optimistic rents and conservative costs at the same time: reality usually moves together (worse).
  • Ignoring time: entitlement and permitting delays can destroy returns via carry costs and market shifts.
  • Missing soft costs: architecture, engineering, legal, permits, lender fees, and owner’s rep costs add up.
  • Underestimating contingency needs: older buildings and renovations require more contingency than ground-up.
  • Over-reliance on comps: “comp rent” isn’t “achievable rent” if your product/tenant base differs.
  • No exit test: feasibility should stress the sale/refi assumptions (cap rates, debt terms).

Best Practices

  • Run multiple scenarios: base, downside, and delay cases—then decide if the deal still survives.
  • Validate with third parties: broker rent opinions, contractor bids, and condition assessments.
  • Model time explicitly: schedule impacts carry, draws, and IRR more than most people admit.
  • Document key assumptions: what must be true for success, and what breaks the deal first.
  • Separate “concept feasibility” from “execution feasibility”: do you have the team and systems to deliver?

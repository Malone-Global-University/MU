Homeowners Association (HOA)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Homeowners Association (HOA)

Definition

A Homeowners Association (HOA) is a private organization within a residential community that establishes and enforces rules for property owners. HOAs typically manage common areas, maintain shared amenities, and collect mandatory dues from homeowners.

What an HOA Typically Manages

  • Landscaping and exterior maintenance
  • Shared amenities (pool, gym, clubhouse, gates)
  • Common area insurance
  • Exterior standards and architectural controls
  • Parking and community regulations

HOA Dues and Assessments

Owners pay regular dues (monthly or quarterly) to fund operating expenses. In addition, HOAs may levy:

  • Special assessments: One-time charges for major repairs or capital projects.
  • Reserve contributions: Funds set aside for long-term replacements (roof, siding, paving).

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Expense impact: HOA dues reduce net cash flow and must be included in underwriting.
  • Rental restrictions: Some HOAs limit leasing or short-term rentals.
  • Financial health: Poorly funded reserves can lead to large special assessments.
  • Property value protection: Well-managed HOAs can maintain neighborhood standards.

Example

An investor purchases a condo renting for $2,200 per month. The HOA fee is $450 per month. In underwriting, this expense reduces annual cash flow by:

  • 450 × 12 = $5,400 per year

If a special assessment of $6,000 is imposed for roof replacement, that cost must also be factored into return projections.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring HOA financials: Always review reserve studies and budgets.
  • Overlooking rental caps: Some HOAs cap the percentage of units that may be leased.
  • Underestimating assessment risk: Deferred maintenance can lead to surprise costs.
  • Violating rules: Fines and legal costs may result from noncompliance.

Equity Multiple

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Equity Multiple

Definition

Equity multiple measures how many dollars you get back for every dollar you invest. It is calculated as total cash distributions (including sale proceeds) divided by total equity invested, and it’s expressed as a multiple such as 1.4x, 1.8x, or 2.0x.

How It’s Measured

Equity multiple is a money-on-money return metric. It does not “annualize” the return; it simply totals what came back relative to what went in.

Common formula

  • Equity Multiple = Total Distributions to Equity ÷ Total Equity Invested

What It Tells You (and What It Doesn’t)

Equity multiple answers: “How much did I get back in total?” It does not answer: “How fast did I get it back?” Time matters, so equity multiple is best used alongside IRR and cash-on-cash return.

Strengths

  • Simple: easy to interpret and compare at a glance.
  • Harder to game than IRR: doesn’t spike just because cash is returned earlier.
  • Useful for “total value created”: especially in long hold periods or development deals.

Limitations

  • Ignores timing: 1.7x in 3 years is very different from 1.7x in 10 years.
  • Not risk-adjusted: doesn’t tell you how volatile or uncertain the path was.
  • Can hide leverage risk: a high multiple may rely on aggressive debt and optimistic exits.

Why It Matters

Equity multiple is a core metric in real estate underwriting because it captures the total magnitude of return. Many investors set minimum targets (e.g., 1.5x+) for value-add deals, higher for development, and lower for core/core-plus—depending on risk tolerance and hold period.

Investor Uses

  • Deal screening: quickly filter deals that don’t meet total-return requirements.
  • Compare business plans: evaluate whether a higher-risk plan truly increases total dollars returned.
  • Check exit dependence: separate “cash-flow multiple” vs. “sale-driven multiple.”
  • Evaluate fees/promote impact: compare gross vs. net equity multiple to investors.

Example

An investor contributes $1,000,000 of equity. Over the hold period, the investor receives $300,000 in distributions and $1,200,000 at sale, for total distributions of $1,500,000.

  • Equity Multiple = 1,500,000 ÷ 1,000,000 = 1.50x

This means the investor received $1.50 for every $1.00 invested (a $0.50 gain per dollar), regardless of how long the deal took.

Common Pitfalls

  • Comparing without hold period: always look at multiple and time (IRR, average annual cash yield).
  • Ignoring whether it’s gross or net: confirm if the multiple is before or after fees and promote.
  • Overweighting projected exit: a “great” multiple can be mostly dependent on an optimistic cap rate.
  • Not separating capital events: refinances can return cash early and affect perceived performance.

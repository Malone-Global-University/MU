Percentage Rent

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Percentage Rent

Definition

Percentage Rent is a retail lease structure in which a tenant pays a fixed base rent plus an additional rent amount calculated as a percentage of the tenant’s gross sales once sales exceed a negotiated threshold called a breakpoint.

In simple terms: the landlord shares in the tenant’s success once sales reach a certain level.

How Percentage Rent Works

Basic Formula

Percentage Rent = (Gross Sales – Breakpoint) × Percentage Rate

Example

  • Base rent: $120,000 per year
  • Percentage rate: 6%
  • Breakpoint: $2,000,000
  • Actual annual sales: $2,500,000

Sales above breakpoint = $500,000
Percentage rent = $500,000 × 6% = $30,000

Total annual rent = $120,000 base rent + $30,000 percentage rent = $150,000.

Types of Breakpoints

Natural Breakpoint

Calculated as:

Breakpoint = Base Rent ÷ Percentage Rate

Example: $120,000 ÷ 6% = $2,000,000

Artificial Breakpoint

A negotiated threshold different from the natural breakpoint. This may benefit either landlord or tenant depending on structure.

Where Percentage Rent Is Common

  • Shopping centers
  • Malls
  • Outlet centers
  • Entertainment and experiential retail
  • Restaurant leases

What Counts as Gross Sales?

Lease language defines what is included in “gross sales.” Typically:

  • Included: in-store sales, online orders fulfilled in-store, services.
  • Excluded: sales taxes, refunds, employee discounts, certain wholesale sales.

Clear definitions reduce disputes.

Why Landlords Use Percentage Rent

  • Upside participation: Share in strong tenant performance.
  • Alignment of incentives: Encourages landlord support of tenant traffic.
  • Market positioning: Attractive to emerging brands with lower base rent.

Why Tenants Accept It

  • Lower initial base rent.
  • Risk-sharing during ramp-up period.
  • Alignment with performance growth.

Investor Impact

  • Can increase effective rent in strong markets.
  • Creates variable income component in NOI.
  • May increase asset value in high-performing retail centers.
  • Adds volatility to underwriting projections.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Historical tenant sales performance.
  • Market traffic trends.
  • Co-tenancy clauses.
  • Lease audit rights.
  • Reporting frequency (monthly or quarterly sales reporting).

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague gross sales definitions.
  • Failure to audit reported sales.
  • Breakpoints set too high to realistically trigger.
  • Over-reliance on percentage rent in volatile retail environments.

Percentage Rent vs. Base Rent Only

  • Base Rent Only: Predictable, fixed income.
  • Percentage Rent: Variable income with potential upside.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm breakpoint calculation method.
  • Verify percentage rate and escalation terms.
  • Review sales reporting requirements.
  • Ensure audit rights are included in lease.
  • Model conservative and upside scenarios in underwriting.

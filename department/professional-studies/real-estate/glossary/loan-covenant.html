Loan Covenant

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Loan Covenant

Definition

A loan covenant is a contractual requirement in a loan agreement that obligates the borrower to do certain things (affirmative covenants) or prohibits certain actions (negative covenants). Covenants are designed to protect the lender by limiting risk and enforcing operating discipline.

Common Types of Loan Covenants

  • Financial covenants: DSCR, debt yield, LTV, net worth, liquidity, or minimum occupancy tests.
  • Reporting covenants: delivery of rent rolls, operating statements, annual financials, compliance certificates, and budgets.
  • Insurance covenants: maintain required coverage types and limits; provide certificates of insurance.
  • Property condition covenants: maintain the asset, address code issues, and prevent waste.
  • Transfer covenants: restrictions on sale, additional partners, or changes of control without consent.
  • Additional debt / lien covenants: limits on new financing or subordinate liens.

How Covenants Are Tested

Covenants are typically tested on a quarterly or annual basis using a defined methodology in the loan documents. For example, DSCR may be calculated using trailing 3-month or trailing 12-month NOI, and “debt service” may be defined differently depending on the structure (interest-only vs. amortizing).

What Happens if a Covenant Is Breached

A covenant breach can trigger a technical default even if payments are current. Remedies vary by lender and loan, but may include notice and cure periods, default interest, added reserves, or enhanced cash management (e.g., a lockbox or cash sweep).

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Underwriting: covenant headroom is part of your downside risk, not a footnote.
  • Asset management: missing reports or insurance renewals can create avoidable defaults.
  • Refinancing: compliance issues can delay or derail execution at critical moments.
  • Negotiation: terms like cure periods and test definitions can be worth real money.

Example

A loan requires a minimum DSCR of 1.25x tested quarterly. Vacancy rises and NOI dips, producing a DSCR of 1.19x. The borrower is still paying on time, but the covenant breach may trigger a technical default and allow the lender to require a cash sweep until DSCR returns above the threshold.

Condemnation

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Condemnation

Definition

Condemnation is the legal process by which a government exercises eminent domain to take private property for public use, subject to payment of just compensation.

In simple terms: condemnation is the formal legal action used to take property.

Condemnation vs. Eminent Domain

  • Eminent Domain: The government’s power to take property.
  • Condemnation: The legal process used to exercise that power.

Steps in the Condemnation Process

  1. Public project identified.
  2. Property appraised to determine fair market value.
  3. Government makes an offer.
  4. If no agreement is reached, a condemnation lawsuit is filed.
  5. Court determines just compensation if disputed.

Types of Takings

  • Full Taking: Entire property is acquired.
  • Partial Taking: Only a portion of the property is taken.
  • Temporary Taking: Short-term government use.

Compensation Considerations

  • Fair market value at time of taking.
  • Damages to the remaining property (in partial takings).
  • Relocation expenses (in some cases).
  • Business interruption claims (where applicable).

Lease Implications

Commercial leases typically include condemnation clauses addressing:

  • Termination rights if a substantial portion is taken.
  • Allocation of condemnation proceeds.
  • Rent abatement during disruption.

Impact on Investors

  • Forced sale risk.
  • Reduced income if tenant vacates.
  • Changes in site layout, parking, or access.
  • Potential improvement in access due to public project.

Example

A municipality condemns part of a shopping center’s parking lot to expand a roadway. The owner receives compensation for the land and may seek additional damages for reduced parking capacity.

Condemnation vs. Code Condemnation

  • Eminent Domain Condemnation: Government acquisition for public use.
  • Code Condemnation: Property declared unsafe or uninhabitable due to code violations.

Valuation Impact

  • Infrastructure improvements may enhance long-term value.
  • Loss of frontage or access may reduce value.
  • Market uncertainty can affect underwriting assumptions.

Investor Checklist

  • Monitor local public infrastructure plans.
  • Review lease condemnation provisions.
  • Consult legal counsel if notice is received.
  • Obtain independent appraisal.
  • Evaluate long-term redevelopment implications.

Ownership Structure

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Definition

Ownership structure refers to the legal and organizational framework through which real estate is owned. It determines liability exposure, tax treatment, governance rights, and transferability of interests.

Why Ownership Structure Matters

  • Defines control and decision-making authority.
  • Determines liability protection.
  • Impacts taxation and reporting.
  • Affects financing eligibility.
  • Influences estate planning and succession.

Common Ownership Structures

1) Individual Ownership

  • Simple structure.
  • No liability shield.
  • Common in small residential holdings.

2) Joint Tenancy

  • Equal ownership shares.
  • Includes right of survivorship.

3) Tenancy in Common

  • Unequal ownership percentages allowed.
  • No automatic survivorship rights.

4) Limited Liability Company (LLC)

  • Provides liability protection.
  • Flexible tax treatment.
  • Common for investment properties and syndications.

5) Partnership (LP / GP)

  • General partner manages operations.
  • Limited partners provide capital.
  • Common in real estate funds.

6) Corporation

  • Separate legal entity.
  • Potential double taxation (C-Corp).

7) Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)

  • Public or private vehicle.
  • Income distribution requirements.
  • Access to public capital markets (if public REIT).

Entity-Level vs. Property-Level Ownership

  • Direct Ownership: Title held directly by individuals.
  • Entity Ownership: Title held by LLC, LP, or corporation.

Governance Considerations

  • Operating agreements define voting rights.
  • Capital call provisions affect dilution risk.
  • Transfer restrictions protect investor base.

Tax Considerations

  • Pass-through taxation (LLC/partnership).
  • Corporate taxation for certain structures.
  • Depreciation allocation impacts investor returns.

Financing Implications

  • Lenders may require single-purpose entities (SPEs).
  • Personal guarantees may apply.
  • Ownership changes may trigger lender consent.

Example

A multifamily property is owned by an LLC, whose members include a managing sponsor (20%) and passive investors (80%). The LLC operating agreement defines voting rights, profit splits, and capital call procedures.

Common Pitfalls

  • Improperly drafted operating agreements.
  • Failure to maintain corporate formalities.
  • Ignoring tax consequences of ownership changes.
  • Overlooking lender transfer restrictions.

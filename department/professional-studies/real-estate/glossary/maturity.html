Maturity

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Maturity

Definition

Maturity is the date when a loan’s remaining principal balance becomes due in full. At maturity, the borrower must repay the loan, refinance into a new loan, extend (if the loan allows it), or sell the property to retire the debt.

Maturity vs. Amortization

Maturity is about when the loan must be paid off. Amortization is about how the loan is paid down over time.

  • Amortization term: the schedule used to calculate the regular payment (e.g., 30 years).
  • Loan term (maturity): the length of the loan contract (e.g., 5 or 10 years).

Many commercial loans have a long amortization schedule but a shorter maturity, creating a balloon balance due at the end of the term.

Balloon Payments

A balloon payment is the lump-sum payoff due at maturity if the loan is not fully amortized. This is common in commercial real estate where loans may be amortized over 25–30 years but mature in 3–10 years.

Extensions

Some loans include extension options that allow the borrower to push out maturity, often in exchange for an extension fee and subject to performance tests (for example, minimum DSCR, occupancy, no event of default, or updated appraisals).

Why It Matters

Maturity is one of the biggest sources of forced decision risk. If capital markets tighten or rates rise near maturity, refinancing may become expensive—or unavailable—leading to equity injections, a distressed sale, or default.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting exit timing: align business plan duration with loan maturity and extension windows.
  • Refinance planning: stress test refinance proceeds under higher rates and lower valuations.
  • Liquidity management: plan reserves or capital calls if payoff/refi is uncertain.
  • Risk pricing: shorter maturities typically warrant higher yield requirements.

Example

A property has a 5-year loan term with a 30-year amortization schedule. Monthly payments are calculated as if the loan will be paid over 30 years, but the remaining balance is due at the end of year 5. The borrower plans to refinance at maturity; if rates rise and the value drops, the new loan may be smaller, forcing the borrower to bring additional equity to close.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming refinance is automatic: markets and lender standards can change quickly.
  • Ignoring extension conditions: extensions can be conditional, costly, or unavailable during stress.
  • Mismatch with business plan: heavy capex or lease-up may not stabilize before maturity.
  • Underestimating balloon size: short terms with long amortization leave large payoff balances.

Exchange Period

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Exchange Period

Definition

The exchange period is the time window in a 1031 exchange during which you must complete the acquisition of your replacement property(ies) after you transfer (sell) the relinquished property. In a standard forward 1031 exchange, the exchange period is generally 180 days.

How the Exchange Period Works

The exchange period starts on the date you transfer the relinquished property (usually your closing date as seller). Your replacement property purchase(s) must close by the deadline—there’s no “in escrow” exception. If you miss the deadline, the exchange can fail.

Timeline structure

  • Day 0: Relinquished property closes (exchange period begins).
  • By Day 45: Identify replacement property(ies) in writing (identification period).
  • By Day 180: Acquire (close on) the replacement property(ies) (exchange period ends).

Important Interaction With Taxes

A common gotcha: the exchange period can effectively be shortened if your tax return due date (including extensions) comes before the 180th day. Many investors file an extension to preserve the full exchange window when needed.

Why It Matters

The exchange period is the hard boundary that drives deal strategy and execution. It affects deal sourcing, financing speed, due diligence scope, and how conservative you should be with contingencies. In practice, 1031 success often comes down to planning the closing calendar and having backup options.

Investor Uses

  • Closing strategy: prioritize properties and lenders that can close within the deadline.
  • Risk management: identify backups and control delays (appraisals, loan docs, inspections).
  • Negotiation leverage: communicate timeline constraints without overcommitting.
  • Portfolio planning: align dispositions and acquisitions to avoid forced purchases.

Example

You sell your rental property on April 10. Your exchange period ends 180 days later, on October 7 (counting April 10 as Day 0). You must close on your replacement property(ies) by October 7 to complete the exchange.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming 180 days always applies: your tax return due date (without extension) may shorten the window.
  • Late identification: using most of the first 45 days leaves little time for diligence and financing.
  • Financing delays: lender timelines, appraisals, or title issues can push closing past the deadline.
  • Over-tight contingencies: long inspection or financing contingencies may not fit the calendar.
  • No backup plan: a single replacement option increases failure risk if the deal dies late.

This page is for educational purposes only and is not tax or legal advice. 1031 exchanges are highly procedural, and deadlines are strict. Work with a qualified intermediary and your tax/legal advisors to confirm timing, extensions, and compliance for your specific transaction.

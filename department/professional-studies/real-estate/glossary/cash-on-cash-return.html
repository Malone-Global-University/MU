Cash-on-Cash Return

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Cash-on-Cash Return

Definition

Cash-on-cash return (often shortened to CoC) measures the annual pre-tax cash flow a property produces for investors relative to the total cash invested. It answers a simple question: “What cash yield am I earning on the dollars I actually put into the deal?”

How It’s Measured

Cash-on-cash is typically calculated using cash flow after debt service (and often after reserves) divided by total equity and other cash contributed at acquisition.

Common formula

  • Cash-on-Cash Return (%) = Annual Cash Flow to Equity ÷ Total Cash Invested

What counts as “cash invested”?

  • Equity down payment / purchase equity
  • Closing costs (to the extent paid in cash by investors)
  • Up-front CapEx / renovation funding funded with cash
  • Initial reserves or operating deficit guarantees funded with cash

Why It Matters

Cash-on-cash return is a fast way to evaluate income durability and cash yield, especially for stabilized properties. It is most useful when you want to compare: (1) two potential acquisitions with similar risk, or (2) buying versus waiting, or (3) a property against alternative uses of cash.

Investor Uses

  • Stabilized deal screening: quickly filter opportunities that do not meet minimum yield targets.
  • Leverage sensitivity: see how changing debt terms affects investor cash yield.
  • Operational focus: translate NOI improvements into cash distributions.
  • Side-by-side comparisons: compare cash-yield profiles across submarkets or business plans.

Example

An investor group buys a small apartment building and contributes $1,000,000 total cash (equity + closing costs + initial reserves). In Year 1, the property generates $85,000 of cash flow after debt service and reserves.

  • Cash-on-Cash Return = 85,000 ÷ 1,000,000 = 8.5%

Interpreted simply: the investors earned an 8.5% annual cash yield on the cash they put in, before taxes and before any value appreciation.

How It Differs From Other Metrics

  • Cap rate: measures NOI ÷ purchase price (ignores financing).
  • IRR: includes timing of all cash flows and sale proceeds (multi-year return).
  • Equity multiple: total cash returned ÷ total cash invested (ignores timing).

Common Pitfalls

  • Not defining “cash flow” consistently: some use after debt only; others subtract reserves/CapEx.
  • Leverage distortion: higher debt can boost CoC in good times but increase downside risk.
  • Ignoring future capital needs: high Year-1 CoC can vanish when large CapEx arrives.
  • Comparing unlike risk profiles: a value-add deal’s early CoC may be low by design.
  • One-year snapshot: CoC does not capture appreciation, refinance proceeds, or exit price risk.

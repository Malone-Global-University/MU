Broker Opinion of Value (BOV)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Broker Opinion of Value (BOV)

Definition

A Broker Opinion of Value (BOV) is an informal estimate of a property’s market value prepared by a real estate broker. A BOV typically references comparable sales, current listings, and (for income properties) income-based metrics like cap rates and price-per-unit to support a recommended value range.

What a BOV Usually Includes

  • Property summary: location, unit mix, condition, amenities, and key drivers.
  • Comparable sales and listings: adjusted for differences in size, quality, and submarket.
  • Income analysis: in-place and/or pro forma NOI, cap rate assumptions, and value ranges.
  • Market commentary: rent growth, supply pipeline, vacancy trends, and buyer demand.
  • Value conclusion: a point estimate or range (often with scenarios).

Why It Matters

A BOV is often the first “reality check” on pricing. Owners and investors use it to decide whether to list, refinance, sell, or hold. It can also help frame expectations before spending money on a full appraisal or launching a formal marketing process.

Common Use Cases

  • Pre-listing pricing: set a likely range and identify who the buyer pool is.
  • Refinancing prep: test whether a target valuation is realistic for lenders/investors.
  • Partnership decisions: inform buyouts, recapitalizations, or portfolio strategy.
  • Asset management: track value changes as NOI improves or market cap rates move.

BOV vs. Appraisal

A BOV is not the same as a formal appraisal. An appraisal is performed by a licensed or certified appraiser and typically follows standardized methodology and reporting requirements. A BOV is produced by a broker, and while it can be high quality, it is usually less standardized and may reflect the broker’s view of marketability and buyer sentiment.

Example

An owner asks two brokers for BOVs on a 120-unit apartment building. Broker A concludes a value range of $24.0M–$25.5M based on recent sales at 5.25%–5.50% cap rates and current rent comps. Broker B concludes $22.5M–$24.0M, citing higher concessions and a new supply pipeline. The owner uses the overlap to set an initial pricing target and decide whether to invest in unit upgrades before listing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Optimistic assumptions: pro forma rents and cap rates that ignore concessions or competition.
  • Cherry-picked comps: selecting only the best sales and skipping weaker transactions.
  • Not apples-to-apples: comparing different asset classes, vintages, or submarkets without adjustments.
  • Confusing “marketing price” with value: a list price can be aspirational; value is what closes.

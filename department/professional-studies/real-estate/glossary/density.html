Density

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Density

Definition

Density describes how intensively land is used by measuring how much “stuff” (housing units, floor area, or people) is concentrated on a site or within a district. In real estate investing and development, density is central to zoning, entitlements, design, and financial feasibility.

Why Density Matters to Investors

  • Revenue potential: more leasable area or more units can increase gross income if supported by demand.
  • Cost structure: density can drive construction type, elevator counts, structured parking, and soft costs.
  • Entitlement value: “by-right” vs. “upzoned” density often determines land value and residual land pricing.
  • Risk: higher density can mean more entitlement complexity, community opposition, and execution risk.

Common Ways Density Is Measured

Density is not one number. It depends on what you’re measuring (units, floor area, or people) and the context (site plan vs. neighborhood).

1) Residential density (Units per Acre)

Units per acre (DU/AC) is common in multifamily, build-to-rent, and subdivision planning.

  • Formula: Units per Acre = Total dwelling units ÷ Site acres
  • Use: quick sizing, zoning checks, parking assumptions, and comparable project benchmarking

2) Floor Area Ratio (FAR)

FAR measures building floor area relative to site area. It is common in urban zoning and mixed-use.

  • Formula: FAR = Total gross floor area ÷ Lot area
  • Interpretation: FAR controls intensity but doesn’t directly dictate height (setbacks, stepbacks, and lot coverage do).

3) Lot coverage

Lot coverage is the percent of the site footprint covered by structures (and sometimes impervious surfaces, depending on code).

  • Formula: Lot Coverage = Building footprint ÷ Lot area
  • Use: site planning constraints, open space compliance, stormwater strategy

4) Population / employment density

For market and planning work, density may refer to people per acre (or per square mile), or jobs per acre. These can impact retail demand, transit viability, and amenity support.

Net Density vs. Gross Density

A frequent source of confusion is whether the land area includes roads, parks, and other non-buildable components.

  • Gross density: uses the total parcel or planning area (often includes streets, easements, open space).
  • Net density: uses the developable portion (excludes major rights-of-way and sometimes excludes parks/detention areas).

In underwriting, always state which you mean—gross and net density can differ materially for master-planned sites.

What Controls Achievable Density

  • Zoning: FAR caps, DU/AC limits, height limits, setbacks, lot coverage, and use restrictions.
  • Parking requirements: minimums can reduce buildable area or force structured parking (higher cost).
  • Site constraints: slope, wetlands, floodplains, heritage trees, access, utility capacity, and stormwater detention.
  • Design efficiency: core-to-window depth, unit mix, double-loaded corridors, and structural grid.
  • Market demand: the market may not support the rent/sales needed to justify higher density costs.

Underwriting Impacts

Density changes both sides of the pro forma: income (more units / more rentable SF) and expenses/capex (construction type, amenities, parking, operating complexity).

  • Rent per SF vs. rent per unit: higher density often comes with smaller units and different pricing psychology.
  • Operating expenses: elevators, common areas, security, and amenities can raise OPEX per unit.
  • Cap rate and exit: buyers may value stabilized, high-density assets differently due to liquidity and perceived risk.
  • Construction budget: density thresholds can trigger podium/steel construction, sprinklers, life-safety upgrades, and higher fees.

Example Calculations

Units per Acre

A 2.5-acre site is planned for 120 apartments.

  • Density = 120 ÷ 2.5 = 48 units/acre

FAR

A 40,000 SF lot supports a building with 120,000 SF of gross floor area.

  • FAR = 120,000 ÷ 40,000 = 3.0 FAR

Practical Investor Checklist

  • Confirm by-right density vs. density requiring a variance, rezoning, or conditional use permit.
  • Verify whether density is constrained by parking minimums or height/setback rules (often the true limiter).
  • Model the incremental cost per added unit (structured parking, bigger cores, more elevators) vs. incremental NOI.
  • State density assumptions clearly: gross vs. net, and whether using GBA, GFA, or rentable SF.
  • Pressure-test entitlements: timeline, approvals, fees, and community engagement risk.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mixing metrics: units/acre and FAR can imply very different building forms—don’t treat them as interchangeable.
  • Assuming “max zoning” is achievable: real-world constraints (parking, setbacks, open space) often reduce yield.
  • Ignoring efficiency: two projects at the same FAR can produce different net rentable area due to cores and circulation.
  • Overlooking fee triggers: impact fees, school fees, utility upgrades, and inclusionary requirements may scale with density.

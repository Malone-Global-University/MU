Repairs & Maintenance (R&M)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Repairs & Maintenance

Definition

Repairs & Maintenance (R&M) refers to the operating costs required to keep a property safe, functional, and rent-ready through routine upkeep and non-capital fixes—things like plumbing repairs, HVAC service calls, patch/paint, locks, minor electrical work, appliance repairs, and common-area upkeep.

In underwriting and accounting, R&M is generally treated as an operating expense (OpEx) that supports ongoing operations, rather than a capital expenditure (CapEx) that materially extends useful life or significantly improves the asset.

What Typically Falls Under R&M

  • Unit turns (non-capital): patch/paint, minor drywall, blinds, small hardware, touch-up flooring fixes.
  • Service calls: plumbing leaks/clogs, garbage disposals, minor electrical repairs, lock changes.
  • HVAC and mechanical service: tune-ups, minor parts, preventative maintenance, filter programs.
  • Appliance repairs: fixing (not replacing) ranges, refrigerators, dishwashers, laundry equipment.
  • Common-area upkeep: lighting replacements, minor landscaping fixes, irrigation repairs, trash enclosure upkeep.
  • Contracted maintenance: handyman, make-ready labor, small vendor jobs.

R&M vs. CapEx

The same “system” can show up in both buckets depending on the scope of work. A simple rule of thumb: R&M restores; CapEx upgrades or extends life.

Quick comparison

  • R&M example: Fix a leaking faucet, replace a toilet flapper, repair a section of damaged siding.
  • CapEx example: Replace the building roof, install new HVAC systems, re-plumb a property, replace windows property-wide.

Why It Matters

R&M is one of the most operationally “real” line items in the P&L. It directly impacts Net Operating Income (NOI), resident experience, renewal rates, and the pace of leasing. Under-budgeting R&M can create deferred maintenance, negative reviews, longer turns, and higher vacancy—often costing far more than the savings.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting accuracy: set realistic OpEx assumptions that protect downside NOI.
  • Operational performance: reduce recurring repairs through preventive programs and standardization.
  • Asset condition assessment: rising R&M can signal aging systems, poor prior workmanship, or resident misuse.
  • Value creation: pairing smart CapEx with reduced ongoing R&M can expand NOI sustainably.

Budgeting Approaches

R&M budgets are commonly built using a blend of historical actuals and normalized forward assumptions based on property age, construction type, amenities, and staffing model.

Common methods

  • Per unit per year (PUY): simple and comparable across assets; often used in quick underwriting.
  • Percent of Effective Gross Income (EGI): useful when scaling across rent tiers; can mask aging-system risk.
  • Work-order driven: budget by expected work orders, average labor minutes, and parts per category.

Example

An investor underwrites a 120-unit property and expects stabilized R&M at $950 per unit per year. The annual R&M budget is:

  • Annual R&M = 120 × $950 = $114,000
  • Monthly R&M = $114,000 ÷ 12 = $9,500

If actual R&M runs at $1,250 PUY instead, the variance is 120 × ($1,250 − $950) = $36,000 of annual NOI impact (before any offsetting benefits).

Common Pitfalls

  • Hiding CapEx in R&M: large replacements booked as R&M can distort trends and mislead underwriting.
  • Ignoring property age: older systems often have “death by a thousand cuts” repair patterns.
  • Turn cost confusion: mixing make-ready materials and labor across lines makes performance tracking harder.
  • Deferred maintenance spiral: under-spending short-term increases long-term costs and vacancy risk.
  • Vendor dependence: excessive outsourced work can inflate costs versus a right-sized onsite team.

Best Practices

  • Preventive maintenance: HVAC filters, seasonal inspections, and standardized unit checklists.
  • Standardize parts: fewer SKUs reduces downtime, emergency calls, and purchasing complexity.
  • Track by category: plumbing, electrical, HVAC, appliances, turns—identify the real drivers.
  • Audit recurring work orders: repeated repairs often indicate the need for a targeted CapEx fix.
  • Protect the turn timeline: faster turns reduce vacancy loss—often worth more than shaving material costs.

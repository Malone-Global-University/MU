Bridge Loan

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Bridge Loan

Definition

A bridge loan is a short-term real estate loan used to “bridge” a borrower from one stage to the next—most commonly from acquisition or renovation to stabilization and then to permanent financing (or sale). Bridge loans are often interest-only and are underwritten based on a business plan to increase value or improve cash flow.

How It Works

Bridge loans are typically used when a property is not yet eligible for long-term, low-cost financing (for example, because occupancy is low, income is unstable, or renovations are underway). The loan is repaid through a refinance into permanent debt or by a sale.

Common features

  • Term: often 6–36 months, sometimes with extension options
  • Payments: frequently interest-only; may include an interest reserve in some deals
  • Rate structure: commonly floating-rate (index + spread), though fixed can exist
  • Leverage: sized to purchase price and/or value with rehab; may include future funding
  • Exit: refinance to permanent debt or sell upon stabilization

Why It Matters

Bridge debt can unlock deals by providing speed and flexibility—but it introduces refinance risk and rate risk, especially when rates rise or capital markets tighten. Investors use bridge loans when they believe they can execute the business plan quickly enough to refinance before the term ends.

Investor Uses

  • Value-add acquisitions: buy underperforming assets and fund upgrades or lease-up.
  • Transitional properties: finance assets with vacancy, operational issues, or inconsistent NOI.
  • Time-sensitive closings: move fast when permanent lenders can’t close quickly.
  • Repositioning: change unit mix, tenant base, amenity package, or operating model.

Example

An investor buys a 100-unit property at 70% occupancy and plans to renovate units and push rents over 18 months. Because the property is not stabilized, the investor uses a 24-month bridge loan to fund the acquisition and rehab. After reaching 93% occupancy and improved NOI, the investor refinances into a permanent loan with a lower rate and longer term.

Common Pitfalls

  • Refinance risk: failing to meet DSCR/occupancy requirements for takeout financing.
  • Extension assumptions: extensions may require fees, paydowns, or performance tests.
  • Floating-rate exposure: higher rates can erase cash flow; hedges (caps/swaps) cost money.
  • Execution risk: delays in renovations, permitting, or leasing can push past maturity.
  • Over-leverage: too much debt leaves little room for NOI volatility or capex overruns.

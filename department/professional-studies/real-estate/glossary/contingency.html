Contingency

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Contingency

Definition

A contingency is a contract clause that makes the deal conditional on something happening (or being verified) within a defined timeframe. If the condition is not satisfied, the protected party can typically cancel, renegotiate, or extend—depending on the agreement.

Why Contingencies Matter

Contingencies are how real estate deals manage unknowns. They create a structured window to verify the asset, secure financing, and confirm assumptions before hard commitments are made.

  • Risk control: prevents buyers from being forced to close on a problem asset.
  • Pricing discipline: gives leverage to re-trade if facts don’t match the story.
  • Execution clarity: sets deadlines and deliverables so the process doesn’t drift.

Common Contingencies

1) Due diligence (inspection) contingency

Allows time to inspect the physical asset and documents (leases, financials, permits, insurance, vendor contracts). Findings often drive repair credits, price reductions, or a decision to walk.

2) Financing contingency

Protects the buyer if they cannot obtain debt on acceptable terms by a specified date. In commercial deals, this may be structured through lender term sheets, covenants, or required DSCR thresholds.

3) Appraisal contingency

Allows cancellation or renegotiation if the property does not appraise at or above the contract price. This is more common in residential transactions but can appear in commercial deals tied to lender underwriting.

4) Title / survey contingency

Ensures the buyer receives clean, insurable title and acceptable survey results (easements, encroachments, access, boundary issues). Title exceptions can materially affect value and financing.

5) Environmental contingency

Often based on a Phase I ESA (and sometimes Phase II). If environmental risk is discovered, the buyer can typically terminate, require remediation, or negotiate a credit.

6) Estoppel / tenant verification contingency

In multifamily and commercial leasing, estoppels confirm lease terms, deposits, delinquencies, concessions, and disputes. This protects against “paper NOI” that doesn’t match reality.

Hard vs Soft Contingencies

  • Soft contingency: buyer can terminate with minimal penalty during the contingency period (often before earnest money becomes non-refundable).
  • Hard contingency / hard money: after a certain date (or removal of contingencies), earnest money becomes non-refundable except for narrow carve-outs.

Example

A buyer signs a purchase agreement with a 21-day due diligence contingency and posts $250,000 of earnest money that goes hard on day 22.

  • Days 1–21: buyer reviews financials, inspects roofs and plumbing, audits leases, and checks title.
  • Day 18: inspection reveals major sewer line risk. Buyer requests a credit or price reduction.
  • Day 21: if unresolved, buyer can terminate and recover earnest money (subject to contract terms).

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting reality check: validate rent roll, expenses, and physical condition before committing.
  • Negotiation leverage: re-price the deal when facts differ from the OM or broker narrative.
  • Timeline management: set deadlines for lender, insurer, inspectors, and title company deliverables.
  • Risk budgeting: decide what risks you accept (go hard) versus what you must clear first.

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague language: unclear standards (e.g., “satisfactory to buyer”) can create disputes.
  • Missing deadlines: failing to object or terminate on time can waive the contingency.
  • Over-relying on one report: e.g., a superficial inspection that misses major deferred maintenance.
  • Assuming lender = contingency: some contracts still require closing even if financing fails.
  • Not aligning teams: inspections, lender underwriting, and title review must run in parallel.

Lot Split

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Lot Split

Definition

A lot split is the process of dividing a single parcel of land into two or more separate legal parcels. After approval and recording, each new lot can typically be owned, financed, developed, and sold independently (subject to zoning and local regulations).

Why Investors Care

Lot splits can be a powerful form of value creation: turning one asset into multiple buildable or marketable assets. But they can also introduce entitlement risk, time delays, and unexpected infrastructure costs.

Common Lot Split Types

  • Short plat / minor subdivision: a simplified process for a small number of lots (varies by jurisdiction).
  • Full subdivision: more complex approvals, often requiring roadway and utility improvements.
  • Boundary line adjustment: shifts lot lines between existing parcels without creating new lots.

Typical Approval Process

  1. Feasibility screen: zoning, minimum lot size, setbacks, access, utilities, environmental constraints.
  2. Survey and site plan: prepared by a licensed surveyor/engineer showing proposed lot lines.
  3. Application submittal: to city/county planning; may include fees and studies.
  4. Agency review: planning, engineering, fire, utilities, stormwater, sometimes neighbors/public hearing.
  5. Conditions of approval: required improvements or dedications (ROW, sidewalks, drainage, etc.).
  6. Final plat recording: new plat map or subdivision map recorded; lots become legal.
  7. New parcel IDs: assessor assigns new APNs and updates tax records.

Key Feasibility Questions

  • Zoning: Do minimum lot size, frontage, and density rules allow the split?
  • Access: Does each lot have legal and physical access to a public road?
  • Utilities: Can each lot be separately served (water, sewer, power), and at what cost?
  • Topography & drainage: Are there slope limits, wetlands, or stormwater requirements?
  • Title constraints: Are there easements, covenants, or deed restrictions blocking the split?
  • Marketability: Will the newly created lot(s) actually sell or support the intended use?

Example

An investor buys a 12,000 sq ft single-family lot in a neighborhood where the zoning requires 5,000 sq ft minimum lot size. In theory, the parcel can be split into two lots. However, the feasibility review reveals a utility easement along one side and stormwater detention requirements that reduce buildable area. The split may still work, but it requires redesign and higher costs — changing the return profile.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “zoning allows it” means approval is guaranteed: conditions can kill economics.
  • Hidden infrastructure costs: water/sewer extensions, fire flow upgrades, sidewalks, stormwater.
  • Access issues: lots without frontage may require easements or shared driveways.
  • Time risk: permits and reviews can take months; carrying costs matter.
  • Title surprises: easements, CC&Rs, or restrictions may prohibit subdivision.
  • Tax reassessment: splitting can trigger new assessed values and higher taxes.

Investor Underwriting Tips

  • Model time + carrying costs (interest, taxes, insurance) through entitlement and recording.
  • Add a contingency for off-site improvements and utility surprises.
  • Assume conditions will require something you didn’t plan for.
  • Value the “before” case so the deal still works if the split is delayed or denied.
  • Get a survey early and reconcile with the title commitment.

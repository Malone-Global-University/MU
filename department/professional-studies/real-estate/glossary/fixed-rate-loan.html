Fixed Rate Loan

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Fixed Rate Loan

Definition

A fixed rate loan is a loan where the interest rate remains constant for the fixed-rate period (often the entire term). Because the rate doesn’t change with the market, fixed-rate debt creates more predictable debt service and reduces interest-rate volatility in your underwriting.

How It Works

With a fixed rate, the interest component of the payment is based on a set annual rate. If the loan is amortizing, the monthly payment is typically level and the split between interest and principal shifts over time. If the loan is interest-only, payments remain level because principal is not paid down during the IO period.

Common structures

  • Amortizing fixed-rate: monthly payment includes principal + interest over an amortization schedule.
  • Fixed rate with balloon: amortizes over a long schedule but matures sooner, leaving a balance due at maturity.
  • Fixed rate with interest-only (IO): fixed interest payments during IO, then amortization begins (or balloon at maturity).
  • Step-down / hybrid: fixed for a period, then adjusts (less common in commercial, more in residential hybrids).

Why It Matters

In real estate, debt service is often the largest fixed obligation. A fixed rate loan reduces the risk that rising rates will compress cash flow, weaken DSCR, or force a refinance/sale at a bad time. The tradeoff is that fixed-rate loans can be less flexible to prepay and may come with yield maintenance or defeasance.

Key Underwriting Metrics Affected

  • Debt Service: stable payments make cash flow forecasts more reliable.
  • DSCR: predictable coverage ratios reduce refinancing and covenant risk.
  • Loan sizing: lenders may underwrite to a fixed coupon and a required DSCR.
  • Refi risk: lower payment volatility during hold, but maturity still creates refi exposure.

Fixed Rate vs. Floating Rate

Floating-rate loans change with a reference rate (like SOFR) plus a spread, which can increase or decrease debt service over time. Fixed-rate loans are typically chosen for stability; floating-rate loans are often used for bridge/value-add execution where flexibility matters and the hold period may be shorter.

Typical tradeoffs

  • Fixed rate: payment certainty, less interest-rate risk; often tighter prepay terms.
  • Floating rate: more rate risk; may allow easier prepay; can be paired with rate caps/hedges.

Example

A property finances with a $10,000,000 fixed-rate loan at 6.00% amortizing over 30 years. Even if market rates rise, the loan’s coupon stays at 6.00%, keeping debt service predictable. If the property’s NOI is stable, DSCR remains more stable too.

If the same loan were floating at SOFR + 3.00%, an increase in SOFR would immediately raise the interest rate and reduce cash flow unless the borrower had a rate cap.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring prepayment costs: defeasance or yield maintenance can make early payoff expensive.
  • Assuming “fixed” means no risk: maturity/refinance risk still exists, especially with a balloon.
  • Overlooking IO vs amortization: IO improves cash flow but increases refi risk due to higher remaining balance.
  • Not matching debt to business plan: long-term fixed debt may not fit a short bridge/value-add plan.
  • Forgetting reserves/covenants: lender escrows, replacement reserves, or DSCR covenants can still constrain cash flow.

CMBS

Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities – Real Estate Investing Glossary

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary CMBS

Definition

Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities (CMBS) are bonds backed by pools of commercial real estate loans. Instead of holding a single loan on its balance sheet, a lender bundles multiple loans together and sells them into a trust. The trust issues bonds to investors, and the cash flow from borrowers is used to pay those bondholders.

How CMBS Works

  1. Loans are originated by lenders on income-producing properties.
  2. Loans are pooled together and transferred into a securitization trust.
  3. The trust issues bonds (called tranches) with varying risk levels.
  4. Borrower payments flow through a defined waterfall to investors.

Tranching & Risk Structure

CMBS bonds are divided into tranches ranked by priority of payment:

  • Senior tranches: First to receive payments; lowest risk and lowest yield.
  • Mezzanine tranches: Moderate risk and return profile.
  • Subordinate / B-piece: Absorb first losses; highest yield and highest risk.

Losses from loan defaults are applied from the bottom up, protecting senior bondholders.

Servicing Structure

  • Master Servicer: Manages performing loans.
  • Special Servicer: Handles non-performing or distressed loans.

Servicing responsibilities and authority are defined in the Pooling and Servicing Agreement (PSA).

Key Features of CMBS Loans

  • Non-recourse: Typically secured only by the property, subject to carve-outs.
  • Fixed-rate structure: Common in securitized pools.
  • Defeasance: Prepayment often requires replacing collateral with securities.
  • Limited flexibility: Modifications may require transfer to special servicing.

Advantages

  • Access to large, fixed-rate capital.
  • Non-recourse structure for borrowers.
  • Diversified investor exposure to CRE markets.

Risks & Constraints

  • Rigidity: Limited ability to modify loan terms easily.
  • Market volatility: Bond spreads influence pricing.
  • Maturity wall exposure: Refinancing risk in rising rate environments.
  • Servicer alignment: Decisions prioritize bondholder recovery.

Example

A $500M CMBS deal may contain 60 separate commercial property loans. Investors purchase different tranches based on risk appetite. If one property defaults, losses first impact subordinate bondholders, while senior tranches remain protected unless losses exceed subordinate credit support.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Understand refinance and maturity risk.
  • Recognize limited flexibility compared to bank debt.
  • Identify distress cycles and acquisition opportunities.
  • Model capital stack risk correctly in downturn scenarios.

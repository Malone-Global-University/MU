Letter of Intent (LOI)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Letter of Intent (LOI)

Definition

A Letter of Intent (LOI) is a short, written proposal that outlines the key business terms of a potential real estate transaction. It is used to align the buyer and seller on the “big rocks” (price, timing, and structure) before attorneys draft a formal Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA).

In most real estate deals, an LOI is primarily non-binding, but it may include a few binding provisions such as confidentiality, exclusivity, or a no-shop clause.

Why an LOI Exists

  • Speed: get agreement on core terms without paying lawyers to draft a full contract first.
  • Clarity: reduce misunderstandings about pricing, deposits, timelines, and contingencies.
  • Momentum: create a written “checkpoint” so the PSA drafting process doesn’t drift.
  • Negotiation: focus discussion on economics and risk allocation early.

What an LOI Typically Includes

  • Parties: buyer entity, seller, and key contact information.
  • Property: address, parcel IDs, and what’s included (land, improvements, personal property).
  • Purchase price: headline price and any assumptions that affect it.
  • Earnest money deposit (EMD): amount, timing, and when it becomes non-refundable.
  • Due diligence / inspection period: length, access rights, deliverables, and termination rights.
  • Financing: cash vs debt, any financing contingencies, lender timing expectations.
  • Closing date: target closing and any extension options.
  • Prorations and credits: taxes, utilities, rents, security deposits, unpaid balances.
  • Representations / warranties concept: “as-is” vs limited reps, survival period (high level).
  • Confidentiality and exclusivity: NDA reference, no-shop window (if requested).
  • Brokerage: who represents whom and how commissions are handled.

Binding vs Non-Binding Terms

Most LOIs include a sentence stating the LOI is non-binding and that no party is obligated until a definitive agreement (PSA) is executed. However, the LOI may carve out certain sections as binding.

Common binding clauses (if included)

  • Confidentiality: restrictions on sharing financials, tenant data, or deal terms.
  • Exclusivity / no-shop: seller pauses marketing for a defined period while the PSA is negotiated.
  • Cost allocation: each party pays its own fees (or special cases like surveys/environmental reports).
  • Governing law / venue: sometimes stated early, especially for complex deals.

How LOIs Affect Negotiation Leverage

  • Price is only one lever: deposits, diligence, closing certainty, and speed often win deals.
  • Risk allocation shows seriousness: EMD structure and “go hard” timing signal conviction.
  • Exclusivity is valuable: buyers often “pay” for it with stronger terms (price, timing, EMD).
  • Precision reduces retrades: clear diligence scope and deliverables can prevent surprises.

Example

A buyer submits an LOI offering $12,500,000 with a $250,000 earnest money deposit and a 21-day inspection period. The LOI states that the deposit becomes non-refundable after day 21, and the parties target closing 15 days after diligence ends.

This structure tells the seller: “We want time to verify the asset, but if the diligence checks out, we will close with a meaningful deposit at risk.”

Common Pitfalls

  • Too vague: missing EMD mechanics, diligence deliverables, or extension rights can create PSA battles.
  • Accidentally binding language: careless wording can create obligations beyond what was intended.
  • Unrealistic timelines: diligence and lender timing are common failure points.
  • Not defining what’s included: personal property, reserves, security deposits, and contracts matter.
  • Confusing “inspection period” with “financing contingency”: lenders can extend the real closing risk.

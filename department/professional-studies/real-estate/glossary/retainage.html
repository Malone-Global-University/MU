Retainage

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Retainage

Definition

Retainage (also called retention) is a portion of each construction progress payment that is withheld by the owner—or sometimes by the construction lender—until the project reaches substantial or final completion.

In simple terms: it is money held back to ensure the contractor finishes the job properly.

How Retainage Works

During construction, contractors submit periodic pay applications. The owner pays the approved amount minus a retainage percentage.

Example

  • Contract value: $5,000,000
  • Monthly approved draw: $500,000
  • Retainage: 10%

Payment to contractor = $500,000 – $50,000 (retainage) = $450,000.

The $50,000 withheld is released later, typically after punch list completion.

Typical Retainage Percentages

  • 5%–10% is common in private commercial projects
  • Public projects may have statutory caps
  • Some contracts reduce retainage after 50% completion
  • Final retainage is often tied to lien waivers and closeout documents

Retainage and Subcontractors

General contractors often withhold retainage from subcontractors as well. This creates layered retention:

  • Owner → General Contractor retainage
  • General Contractor → Subcontractor retainage

Delays in upstream payments can affect subcontractor cash flow.

Retainage vs. Construction Holdback

  • Retainage: Percentage withheld from each progress payment.
  • Lender holdback: Funds not advanced until certain milestones are met.

In many projects, both mechanisms operate simultaneously.

Release of Retainage

Retainage is typically released when:

  • Substantial completion is certified
  • Punch list items are completed
  • Certificate of Occupancy is issued (if required)
  • Final lien waivers are delivered
  • As-built drawings and warranties are submitted

Why Retainage Matters to Investors

  • Risk mitigation: Ensures contractors complete final details.
  • Leverage: Provides financial incentive for timely performance.
  • Cash flow timing: Impacts final construction draw and equity deployment.
  • Project closeout control: Ties documentation delivery to payment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Releasing retainage before punch list completion.
  • Failing to obtain final lien waivers before payment.
  • Not defining clear retainage reduction milestones.
  • Ignoring state-specific statutory requirements.
  • Disputes over what qualifies as “substantial completion.”

Investor & Developer Checklist

  • Confirm retainage percentage in construction contract.
  • Align retainage release with lender draw schedule.
  • Track punch list status before approving release.
  • Collect final lien waivers from all tiers.
  • Document substantial completion certification.

Retainage in Construction Loans

Construction lenders often mirror retainage requirements and may:

  • Withhold a percentage from each draw request
  • Require third-party inspection reports
  • Condition final funding on project closeout documentation

This creates dual oversight: contractual (owner) and financial (lender).

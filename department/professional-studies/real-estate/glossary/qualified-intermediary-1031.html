Qualified Intermediary (1031 Exchange)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Qualified Intermediary (1031 Exchange)

Definition

A Qualified Intermediary (QI) is an independent third party used in most Section 1031 like-kind exchanges. The QI’s job is to facilitate the exchange by preparing exchange documents and, critically, holding the sale proceeds from the relinquished property so the taxpayer does not take constructive receipt of the funds.

Why a QI Is Required

In a 1031 exchange, the seller (the “exchanger”) generally cannot touch or control the sale proceeds. If the exchanger receives the cash—even briefly—the transaction may become a taxable sale rather than a tax-deferred exchange. A QI acts as the neutral “middleman” to keep the proceeds out of the exchanger’s hands while the replacement property is acquired.

What a Qualified Intermediary Does

  • Structures the exchange: prepares the exchange agreement and related assignments.
  • Receives and holds proceeds: typically via an exchange escrow or qualified trust/escrow arrangement.
  • Coordinates with closing parties: escrow, title, attorneys, lenders, and brokers.
  • Supports identification and acquisition: receives the written identification of replacement properties and funds the replacement closing.
  • Tracks deadlines: helps keep the exchange on schedule (though the taxpayer remains responsible).

Core 1031 Deadlines (QI-Managed Process)

While a QI helps administer the timeline, the deadlines are rigid. Two widely known milestones are:

  • 45-day identification period: replacement property(ies) must be identified in writing within 45 days of the relinquished property closing.
  • 180-day exchange period: the replacement property must be acquired within 180 days of the relinquished property closing (or the tax return due date, whichever is earlier in some cases).

How the Money Flow Works

In a typical delayed exchange:

  1. The relinquished property closes.
  2. Sale proceeds go to the QI (not the seller).
  3. The exchanger identifies replacement properties during the identification period.
  4. The replacement property closes; the QI releases exchange funds to acquire it.

“Boot” and Why It Matters

Even with a QI, an exchange can generate taxable gain if the exchanger receives boot—generally cash or other non-like-kind value. Common causes include:

  • Cash boot: leftover proceeds not reinvested into replacement property.
  • Debt relief boot: replacement property has less debt than the relinquished property and the difference isn’t offset with additional cash.
  • Non-qualifying costs: paying expenses that are not considered exchange expenses from exchange proceeds.

Example

An investor sells a rental property and wants to defer capital gains through a 1031 exchange. At closing, the investor uses a Qualified Intermediary so the proceeds go directly to the QI. Within 45 days, the investor identifies three potential replacement properties, and within 180 days closes on one of them. The QI wires the exchange funds into the replacement closing.

Because the investor did not take possession of the cash and met the identification and acquisition deadlines, the transaction is structured to qualify for tax deferral (subject to meeting all legal requirements).

Selecting a Qualified Intermediary

  • Independence: the QI cannot be a disqualified person (for example, certain agents/advisors with recent relationships).
  • Safeguards for funds: ask how proceeds are held, who controls disbursements, and what protections exist.
  • Experience: volume of exchanges, asset types, and complexity (reverse, improvement exchanges).
  • Process clarity: clear documentation, deadline tracking, and responsive coordination with escrow/title.
  • Fees: understand base fee and charges for multiple properties, extensions, or nonstandard structures.

Common Pitfalls

  • Receiving funds: any constructive receipt can disqualify the exchange.
  • Missing deadlines: 45/180-day timing errors are among the most common failures.
  • Incorrect identification: vague descriptions or late delivery can invalidate the identification.
  • Not reinvesting enough: cash or debt reduction can create taxable boot.
  • Assuming the QI is “responsible”: the taxpayer remains ultimately responsible for compliance.

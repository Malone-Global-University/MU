Capital Expenditures (CapEx)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Capital Expenditures (CapEx)

Definition

Capital expenditures (often shortened to CapEx) are major property investments that extend the useful life of a building, replace big components, or materially improve the asset. CapEx is typically planned and budgeted separately from day-to-day operating expenses.

CapEx vs. Repairs & Maintenance

A useful rule: repairs & maintenance keep the property functioning at its current standard, while CapEx replaces or upgrades a major component to last for years.

  • Repairs & maintenance (OpEx): fixing a leak, patching drywall, servicing a furnace.
  • CapEx: replacing a roof, replacing HVAC units, repaving a parking lot, renovating units.

Common CapEx Categories

  • Building envelope: roof replacement, siding, windows, waterproofing.
  • Mechanical systems: HVAC replacement, boilers, water heaters.
  • Plumbing & electrical: main line repairs, electrical panels, major rewiring.
  • Site work: parking lot repaving/striping, drainage, lighting, landscaping redesign.
  • Life safety: fire alarms, sprinklers, stair/rail upgrades, code compliance projects.
  • Value-add renovations: unit turns (full remodels), amenity upgrades, clubhouse refresh.

Why It Matters

CapEx is one of the easiest ways to misjudge a deal. Underestimating capital needs can create surprise cash calls, reduce distributions, and limit refinancing options. Proper CapEx planning improves durability, tenant satisfaction, and ultimately NOI stability.

How Investors Underwrite CapEx

  • Immediate needs (Day 1): deferred maintenance discovered during due diligence.
  • Planned projects: renovations intended to increase rents or reduce expenses.
  • Replacement reserves: an ongoing annual or per-unit reserve for future big-ticket items.

Common underwriting approaches

  • Per unit per year: set a reserve (e.g., $250–$400/unit/year) for long-term replacements.
  • Percent of EGI: reserve a percentage of effective gross income for capital needs.
  • Component schedule: list roofs/HVAC/parking with remaining life and replacement timing.

Example

An investor buys a 100-unit property and budgets the following:

  • Roof replacement: $300,000 in Year 2
  • Unit renovations: $8,000 per unit for 25 units = $200,000 over 18 months
  • Replacement reserves: $300/unit/year = $30,000 per year

Even if the property’s NOI looks strong, these capital uses of cash can materially affect the investor’s cash-on-cash and the ability to refinance.

Common Pitfalls

  • Calling everything “maintenance”: some owners understate CapEx by misclassifying big replacements.
  • Ignoring remaining useful life: systems nearing end-of-life may require near-term capital.
  • Forgetting “hidden” capex: sewer lines, electrical capacity, drainage, structural issues.
  • Not stress-testing timing: CapEx clustering (roof + parking + HVAC) can strain liquidity.
  • Overestimating rent premiums: value-add CapEx only works if the market supports the rent lift.

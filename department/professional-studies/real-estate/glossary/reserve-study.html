Reserve Study

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Reserve Study

Definition

A reserve study is a professional assessment used most commonly by HOAs and condominium associations to plan for future capital repairs and replacements. It inventories major common-area components (like roofs, exterior paint, pavement, elevators, mechanical systems), estimates each item’s remaining useful life and replacement cost, and recommends a reserve funding plan so the association can pay for those items without relying on surprise cash calls.

What a Reserve Study Includes

  • Component inventory: list of common-area assets the association is responsible for.
  • Condition assessment: observed condition and repair history (sometimes with photos).
  • Useful life estimates: remaining life and expected replacement timing for each component.
  • Cost estimates: projected repair/replacement costs (often with inflation assumptions).
  • Funding plan: recommended reserve contributions over time to meet projected needs.

Two Core Parts

1) Physical analysis

The physical portion answers: What do we own, what shape is it in, and when will it need to be replaced? Some studies include on-site inspections (often called a “Level I” in common industry language), while others rely on records and prior studies (often “Level II/III” style updates).

2) Financial analysis

The financial portion answers: Do we have enough money set aside, and how much should we contribute each year? It typically models cash flows over a 20–30 year horizon and compares projected reserve balances to upcoming component costs.

Key Reserve Funding Concepts

Reserve balance vs. reserve adequacy

A large reserve balance doesn’t automatically mean “healthy.” What matters is whether reserves are adequate relative to upcoming obligations. An association can have a big number today and still be underfunded if multiple major components are due soon.

Percent funded

Many reserve studies summarize “health” with a percent funded metric: how current reserves compare to the fully-funded amount implied by component wear over time. Higher percent funded generally indicates lower risk of future special assessments, though it’s not a guarantee.

Baseline vs. threshold funding (common approaches)

  • Baseline funding: aims to keep reserve balance from dropping below zero.
  • Threshold funding: aims to keep reserves above a minimum floor (a “threshold”) for stability.
  • Full funding: targets maintaining reserves near the fully-funded level (often more conservative).

Why It Matters to Investors

For investors buying condos, townhomes with HOAs, or any asset with shared infrastructure, a reserve study is a risk map. Underfunded reserves often lead to: special assessments, sharp dues increases, deferred repairs, insurance problems, and reduced liquidity at resale.

Underwriting implications

  • Cash flow risk: higher dues or assessments reduce net rent and returns.
  • Capital risk: poor funding can force expensive, time-sensitive repairs.
  • Financing risk: lenders may scrutinize reserves and maintenance, especially after major building events.
  • Exit risk: buyers discount units in associations with known deferred maintenance or pending assessments.

Investor Due Diligence Checklist

  • Get the most recent reserve study (and prior versions to see trend consistency).
  • Check “percent funded” and read the assumptions behind it (inflation, timing, scope).
  • Compare reserve schedule to reality: do meeting minutes mention major issues not in the study?
  • Review reserve contributions: are dues set to actually follow the plan, or is the plan ignored?
  • Look for clustered capex: multiple big-ticket items due in the same 2–5 year window.
  • Validate responsibility boundaries: what’s HOA responsibility vs. owner responsibility (CC&Rs matter).
  • Confirm insurance/deductibles: large deductibles can behave like “hidden” reserve obligations.

Example

An association has a roof replacement projected in 4 years at $800,000 and pavement in 6 years at $300,000. Current reserves are $250,000, and annual contributions are $60,000.

  • Projected reserves in 4 years (ignoring interest/inflation) = 250,000 + (60,000 × 4) = $490,000
  • Shortfall at roof replacement = 800,000 − 490,000 = $310,000

If contributions aren’t increased (or the scope/cost changes), the likely outcomes are a special assessment, a loan paid through higher dues, or deferred work that can create larger costs later.

Common Pitfalls

  • Outdated study: costs and component conditions change; old assumptions can be dangerously optimistic.
  • Not apples-to-apples: percent funded depends on what components are included and how costs are modeled.
  • Political underfunding: boards may avoid raising dues even when the study recommends it.
  • Scope creep: real-world projects often expand once walls are opened or code requirements apply.
  • Insurance blind spots: rising deductibles and exclusions can create surprise cash needs outside reserves.

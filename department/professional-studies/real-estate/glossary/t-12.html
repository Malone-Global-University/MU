T-12 (Trailing 12 Months)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary T-12 (Trailing 12 Months)

Definition

A T-12 (short for Trailing 12 Months) is a financial statement that summarizes a property’s income and expenses over the most recent twelve-month period. Investors and lenders use T-12s to estimate Net Operating Income (NOI), spot operating trends, and compare “actual performance” to budgets and pro forma assumptions.

What’s in a T-12

A typical T-12 resembles a rolling income statement for the property and includes:

  • Gross potential rent: scheduled rent assuming full occupancy at in-place rents.
  • Vacancy & credit loss: vacancy, concessions, and uncollected rent impacts.
  • Other income: parking, pet, laundry, RUBS, fees, storage, and miscellaneous income.
  • Operating expenses: payroll, repairs & maintenance, utilities, admin, marketing, contracts.
  • Property taxes & insurance: often shown as separate major line items.
  • Net Operating Income (NOI): income minus operating expenses (before debt service and capex).

Why It Matters

The T-12 is one of the fastest ways to answer: “What has the property actually been doing?” It anchors underwriting to reality and helps prevent overpaying based on optimistic pro formas.

How Investors Use a T-12

  • Validate in-place cash flow: confirm current NOI and margin.
  • Normalize performance: adjust for unusual one-time items (refunds, storms, legal settlements).
  • Identify trends: rising utilities, insurance, payroll, repairs, or deteriorating collections.
  • Build the underwrite: use trailing actuals as the starting point for the pro forma.
  • Compare to rent roll: cross-check occupied rents, concessions, and other income line items.

Common Adjustments (Normalization)

Underwriting often “cleans” the T-12 to estimate stabilized run-rate NOI:

  • Non-recurring expenses: unusual legal bills, storm cleanup, one-time repairs.
  • Timing noise: expenses paid annually (taxes, insurance) that create month-to-month spikes.
  • Management fees: adjust to a market-rate fee if seller self-manages or undercharges.
  • Owner-specific items: remove personal/one-off costs that won’t continue post-close.
  • Bad debt/collections: align to a realistic long-term assumption for the tenant base.

Example (Simple)

If a property’s trailing 12-month gross income is $1,200,000 and trailing operating expenses are $720,000, then:

  • T-12 NOI = $1,200,000 − $720,000 = $480,000

If you identify $30,000 of one-time storm cleanup in repairs, a normalized NOI might be $510,000—but only if the adjustment is truly non-recurring.

Common Pitfalls

  • Not matching the rent roll period: a T-12 ending in December doesn’t align with a current rent roll snapshot.
  • Ignoring accrual vs. cash: some reports are cash-basis and can hide unpaid bills or timing shifts.
  • Missing deferred maintenance: low repairs on a T-12 may indicate under-spending, not efficiency.
  • Over-normalizing: labeling recurring problems as “one-time” to force a higher NOI.
  • Forgetting property taxes: reassessments after sale can materially change expenses.

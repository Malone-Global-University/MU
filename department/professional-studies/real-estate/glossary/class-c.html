Class C

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Class C

Definition

Class C properties are typically older, lower-tier real estate assets located in less desirable areas. These properties often require significant capital improvements and operational repositioning but may offer higher potential yields.

Typical Characteristics

  • 30+ years old (varies by market)
  • Outdated finishes and systems
  • Lower rental rates
  • Higher vacancy or turnover
  • Deferred maintenance issues

By Property Type

  • Multifamily: Older workforce housing in tertiary locations.
  • Office: Obsolete buildings with limited amenities.
  • Retail: Underperforming strip centers with local tenants.
  • Industrial: Functionally outdated warehouses.

Investment Profile

  • Higher risk / higher return potential
  • Higher cap rates
  • Significant value-add opportunity
  • Greater operational intensity

Advantages

  • Discounted acquisition pricing
  • Opportunity to reposition to Class B
  • Strong cash-on-cash returns if stabilized
  • Less institutional competition

Risks

  • Capital expenditure burden: Major renovations required.
  • Tenant credit risk: Greater default probability.
  • Market location risk: Weak submarkets.
  • Financing constraints: Harder to obtain favorable debt terms.

Example

A 1970-built apartment property with outdated interiors, below-market occupancy, and deferred maintenance would typically be considered Class C.

Class A vs. Class B vs. Class C

  • Class A: Newer, prime location, premium rents.
  • Class B: Mid-tier, stable, moderate rents.
  • Class C: Older, lower rents, repositioning opportunity.

Investor Applications

  • Heavy value-add strategy: Renovate and reposition upward.
  • Distressed acquisition: Purchase below replacement cost.
  • Cash-flow focus: Higher yield with operational risk.
  • Turnaround strategy: Improve management and occupancy.

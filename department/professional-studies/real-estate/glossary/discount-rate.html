Discount Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Discount Rate

Definition

The discount rate is the rate used to translate future cash flows into a present value in a discounted cash flow (DCF) model. Conceptually, it represents your required return for the risk you are taking and the opportunity cost of tying up capital.

How It’s Used

In a DCF, each year’s projected cash flow is discounted back to today, then summed. The discount rate is the “time and risk” penalty applied to future dollars.

Common formulas

  • Present Value (PV) = Cash Flow in Year t ÷ (1 + Discount Rate)t
  • Net Present Value (NPV) = Sum of PVs of all cash flows (including sale proceeds) − Initial investment

What the Discount Rate Represents

  • Risk-free baseline: what you could earn with minimal risk (conceptual starting point).
  • Risk premium: compensation for leasing risk, expense volatility, tenant risk, market risk, leverage, and liquidity.
  • Execution risk: higher for value-add, lease-up, construction, and complex capex plans.
  • Illiquidity: real estate is harder to sell quickly than public securities.

Discount Rate vs. Cap Rate vs. IRR

  • Discount rate: an input you choose to value the cash flows (used in DCF/NPV).
  • Cap rate: a market-based relationship between a single year’s NOI and value (NOI ÷ Price).
  • IRR: the implied annualized return of the deal based on projected cash flows and your purchase price. (IRR is an output of the cash flow timing and magnitude.)

Why It Matters

The discount rate is one of the strongest drivers of DCF value. A higher discount rate lowers present value (you demand more return), and a lower discount rate raises value (you accept less return).

Example

Suppose you expect a single cash flow of $100,000 one year from now.

  • If the discount rate is 8%, PV = 100,000 ÷ 1.08 = $92,593
  • If the discount rate is 12%, PV = 100,000 ÷ 1.12 = $89,286

Same cash flow, different required return → different value today.

Investor Uses

  • Valuation: estimate intrinsic value using projected cash flows and a required return.
  • Comparing deals: evaluate opportunities on a consistent risk-adjusted basis.
  • Sensitivity analysis: test how value changes across discount rates and exit assumptions.
  • Decision rule: accept deals with positive NPV at your discount rate (and reject negative NPV).

Common Pitfalls

  • Using one rate for everything: stabilized core assets and heavy value-add projects should not share the same discount rate.
  • Confusing discount rate with cap rate: they are related conceptually but not interchangeable.
  • Ignoring leverage: risk (and required return) changes as debt increases and DSCR tightens.
  • Overconfidence in projections: small modeling errors get amplified when valuing distant cash flows.

Related Terms

  • Net present value (NPV)
  • Internal rate of return (IRR)
  • Capitalization rate (cap rate)
  • Terminal cap rate
  • Sensitivity analysis
  • Underwriting

Mezzanine Debt

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Mezzanine Debt

Definition

Mezzanine debt is a form of subordinated financing that sits between senior debt and equity in a property’s capital stack. Unlike a traditional mortgage, mezzanine financing is typically secured by a pledge of the borrower’s ownership interests rather than a direct lien on the real estate.

Position in the Capital Stack

  • Senior Loan: First lien mortgage, lowest risk, lowest yield.
  • Mezzanine Debt: Subordinate to senior debt, higher yield.
  • Preferred Equity: Contractual return priority over common equity.
  • Common Equity: Residual ownership, highest risk and upside.

Key Characteristics

  • Higher interest rate: Reflects greater risk.
  • Subordination: Paid after senior debt in default.
  • Equity pledge: Secured by ownership interests.
  • Intercreditor agreement: Governs rights between senior and mezz lenders.
  • Potential control rights: Mezz lender may assume ownership upon default.

Why Sponsors Use Mezzanine Debt

  • Increase leverage: Reduce required equity contribution.
  • Enhance returns: Boost equity IRR if project performs.
  • Flexible structuring: Often used in value-add or transitional deals.

Risk Considerations

  • Default sensitivity: Cash flow volatility increases risk.
  • Refinance risk: High leverage raises maturity exposure.
  • Intercreditor constraints: Senior lender protections may limit mezz remedies.
  • Cycle exposure: Mezzanine capital is more vulnerable in downturns.

Example

A property valued at $50M is financed with:

  • $30M Senior Loan (60% LTV)
  • $8M Mezzanine Loan
  • $12M Equity

The mezzanine loan increases total leverage to 76% of value. If the property value declines significantly, mezz capital absorbs losses before senior debt is impaired.

Investor Implications

  • Higher yield opportunity: Often 8%–15%+ depending on risk.
  • Structural complexity: Requires review of intercreditor terms.
  • Control potential: May gain equity through UCC foreclosure.
  • Downside risk: Recovery depends on remaining value after senior debt.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-leveraging: Excessive mezz reduces flexibility.
  • Ignoring cure rights: Senior lenders often have cure protections.
  • Underestimating refinance risk: Layered debt complicates exits.
  • Confusing mezz with preferred equity: Legal rights differ significantly.

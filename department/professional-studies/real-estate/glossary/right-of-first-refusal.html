Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Right of First Refusal

Definition

A Right of First Refusal (ROFR) is a contractual right that allows a specified party to match a third-party offer before the property owner can complete a sale or transfer.

How It Works

  1. Owner receives a bona fide third-party offer.
  2. Owner notifies the ROFR holder.
  3. ROFR holder has a limited time to match the offer.
  4. If matched, the ROFR holder purchases the property.
  5. If declined, the owner may proceed with the third party.

Where It Appears

  • Commercial leases (tenant ROFR).
  • Joint venture agreements.
  • Partnership agreements.
  • Adjacent property arrangements.

Why It Matters

  • Protects strategic interests of tenants or partners.
  • Provides expansion rights for anchor tenants.
  • Offers investment protection in partnerships.
  • May reduce marketability of property.

ROFR vs Right of First Offer (ROFO)

  • ROFR: Triggered after third-party offer is received.
  • ROFO: Holder gets first chance before property is marketed.

Impact on Transactions

  • May delay closing timelines.
  • May discourage third-party bidders.
  • Can complicate underwriting and valuation.
  • Often requires formal notice procedures.

Negotiation Considerations

  • Time period to exercise right.
  • Scope of assets covered.
  • Transfer exclusions (e.g., affiliate transfers).
  • Assignment rights.

Example

A retail tenant has a ROFR on adjacent space. When the landlord receives an offer from another tenant, the original tenant may match the terms and secure the expansion opportunity.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ambiguous notice requirements.
  • Unclear definition of “bona fide offer.”
  • Failure to properly trigger exercise timeline.
  • Overly broad restrictions limiting liquidity.

Investor Checklist

  • Identify existing ROFR obligations during due diligence.
  • Assess impact on exit strategy.
  • Review notice procedures carefully.
  • Model potential delays in disposition.

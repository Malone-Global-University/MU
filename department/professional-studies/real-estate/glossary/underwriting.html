Underwriting

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Underwriting

Definition

Underwriting is the process of evaluating a real estate deal by building a structured view of income, expenses, capital costs, financing, and exit assumptions to estimate risk-adjusted returns. The goal is to determine what the property is worth to you and whether the deal meets your investment standards.

What Underwriting Answers

  • How much cash flow will the property generate?
  • What risks could break the model?
  • What price (or loan amount) makes sense given those risks?
  • What must happen operationally for the deal to work?
  • What’s the downside case if assumptions miss?

Core Components

1) Revenue

  • In-place income: current rents, other income, reimbursements.
  • Market income: rent comps, renewal/turnover assumptions, concessions.
  • Economic vacancy: physical vacancy + bad debt + loss-to-lease + concessions.

2) Operating expenses

  • Fixed vs. variable: what scales with occupancy versus what doesn’t.
  • Benchmarking: compare to comps and historical trailing statements.
  • Taxes and insurance: often the most volatile and underwritten conservatively.

3) Net Operating Income (NOI)

Underwriting converts revenue and expenses into NOI, the primary driver of value in income real estate.

4) Capital expenditures (CapEx) and leasing costs

  • Initial CapEx: renovations, deferred maintenance, repositioning.
  • Recurring CapEx: reserves for roofs, parking lots, systems, unit turns.
  • Leasing costs: TI/LC for commercial assets; make-ready and turns for residential.

5) Financing (if any)

  • Debt terms: rate, amortization, term, covenants, prepayment.
  • Coverage: DSCR, debt yield, breakeven occupancy.
  • Refinance risk: rate environment, NOI stability, lender constraints.

6) Exit and valuation

  • Hold period: how long capital is tied up.
  • Terminal cap rate: the assumed valuation multiple at sale.
  • Sale costs: broker fees, transfer taxes, closing costs.

Common Underwriting Outputs

  • Cash-on-cash return: current yield on invested equity.
  • IRR: time-weighted return based on all cash flows.
  • Equity multiple: total cash returned per dollar invested.
  • Cap rate: NOI ÷ price (a snapshot yield metric).
  • Breakeven occupancy: occupancy needed to cover operating costs and debt service.

Why Underwriting Matters

Underwriting is where discipline lives. It turns stories into numbers, forces assumptions into the open, and makes risk measurable. Strong underwriting protects capital by preventing you from paying for outcomes the deal does not control (like future cap-rate compression) and by focusing attention on what you can control (operations, tenant quality, cost management, and capital planning).

Example

A buyer evaluates an apartment property with in-place NOI of $900,000. After reviewing leases, comps, and expenses, the buyer underwrites stabilized NOI at $1,050,000 following renovations. Using a conservative terminal cap rate and realistic CapEx reserves, the model produces a return range. If the deal only hits the target return under optimistic rent growth or an aggressive exit cap, the buyer lowers the offer price or passes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-optimistic revenue: assuming rent growth without support or ignoring concessions.
  • Understated expenses: especially taxes, insurance, payroll, repairs, and utilities.
  • Ignoring CapEx: treating deferred maintenance as “one-time” without adequate reserves.
  • Over-leverage: financing that only works in perfect conditions creates refinance risk.
  • Exit cap denial: using a low terminal cap to make the model “work.”
  • Not stress-testing: failing to run downside cases and sensitivities.

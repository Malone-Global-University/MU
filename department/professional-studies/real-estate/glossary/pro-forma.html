Pro Forma

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Pro Forma

Definition

A pro forma is a forward-looking financial projection that estimates a property's future income, expenses, and Net Operating Income (NOI) based on underwriting assumptions.

It answers the question: “What should this property earn once stabilized or improved?”

Purpose of a Pro Forma

  • Estimate stabilized income performance
  • Support acquisition decisions
  • Model renovation or value-add strategies
  • Project investor returns
  • Determine valuation using a cap rate

Pro Forma vs. Historical Financials

  • Historical (T-12): What the property actually earned in the past 12 months.
  • Pro Forma: What the property is expected to earn in the future.

Historical financials reflect reality. Pro forma reflects assumptions.

Core Components of a Pro Forma

Revenue Projections

  • Market rent assumptions
  • Vacancy and credit loss estimates
  • Other income projections
  • Lease-up timeline (if applicable)

Expense Projections

  • Property taxes
  • Insurance
  • Payroll
  • Repairs and maintenance
  • Property management fees

Income Output

  • Gross Operating Income (GOI)
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)
  • Cash flow after debt service
  • Projected investor returns (IRR, cash-on-cash, etc.)

Example

A property currently produces $750,000 NOI. The investor plans renovations that increase rent by $150 per unit.

After adjusting revenue and stabilizing expenses, the pro forma NOI is projected at $900,000.

At a 6% cap rate:

  • Projected Value = $900,000 ÷ 0.06 = $15,000,000

Common Types of Pro Forma

  • Acquisition Pro Forma: Underwriting before purchase.
  • Stabilized Pro Forma: Post-renovation or lease-up assumptions.
  • Development Pro Forma: Ground-up project modeling.
  • Refinance Pro Forma: Projected income for lender underwriting.

Risks of Pro Forma Assumptions

  • Overestimating rent growth
  • Underestimating expenses
  • Ignoring market cycles
  • Assuming unrealistic occupancy rates
  • Capitalizing projected income before operational proof

Investor Discipline

Strong operators differentiate between:

  • Proven income (historical)
  • Projected income (pro forma)

Conservative underwriting often:

  • Discounts pro forma assumptions
  • Stress-tests vacancy and expense scenarios
  • Uses conservative exit cap rates

Related Terms

  • Net operating income (NOI)
  • Adjusted NOI
  • T-12
  • Gross operating income (GOI)
  • Economic occupancy
  • Cap rate
  • Discounted cash flow (DCF)

Real Estate Owned (REO)

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Real Estate Owned (REO)

Definition

Real Estate Owned (REO) refers to property that has become bank-owned after a foreclosure process. Typically, the property did not sell at the foreclosure auction, so ownership reverted to the lender. The lender then markets the property for sale to recover losses.

How a Property Becomes REO

  • Borrower defaults on the mortgage.
  • Foreclosure process begins (judicial or non-judicial depending on state law).
  • Foreclosure auction is held.
  • If the property does not sell (or the lender takes it back), it becomes REO.
  • The lender lists the property for sale through a broker or asset manager.

Why REO Matters to Investors

REO inventory can create acquisition opportunities, but it also comes with operational and legal considerations. Lenders typically want to liquidate REO efficiently, which can produce attractive pricing in some market conditions.

Common REO Characteristics

  • As-is sales: limited representations and warranties; buyer does most of the diligence.
  • Condition risk: deferred maintenance, vacancy damage, or incomplete repairs.
  • Title complexity: liens, HOA issues, or occupancy status may require extra diligence.
  • Process-driven sellers: bank timelines, approvals, addenda, and strict closing procedures.

Investor Underwriting Notes

  • Budget a strong repair and contingency line item.
  • Verify occupancy and local possession rules before assuming renovations can start.
  • Run a detailed title and lien review.
  • Model conservative timelines for closing and stabilization.

REO vs. Short Sale

  • Short Sale: the borrower sells the property for less than the loan balance with lender approval.
  • REO: the lender owns the property after foreclosure and sells it directly.

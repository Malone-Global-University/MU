Definition

Yield is a return measure expressed as a percentage that shows how much income (and sometimes total return) an investor receives relative to the price paid or capital invested in a property.

In real estate, “yield” is often used as a general term for income return. Depending on context, it may refer to the property’s unlevered yield (based on NOI), the investor’s levered yield (based on cash flow after debt service), or a forward-looking metric like yield on cost.

What Yield Is (and Isn’t)

  • Yield focuses on return relative to a base (price or invested capital).
  • Yield is usually about income; it may exclude appreciation unless specified.
  • IRR is a time-weighted total return metric; yield is typically a single-period snapshot.

Common Yield Measures

1) Cap Rate (Unlevered Yield on Price)

In stabilized acquisitions, “yield” is often used interchangeably with the cap rate, which is an unlevered income yield based on NOI and purchase price.

  • Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price

2) Cash-on-Cash Return (Levered Yield on Equity)

For equity investors, a common “yield” measure is cash-on-cash, which focuses on annual before-tax cash distributions relative to equity invested.

  • Cash-on-Cash = Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Equity Invested

3) Yield on Cost (Yield on Total Project Cost)

In development and value-add, investors often track yield on cost to see what stabilized income will be relative to total capital spent.

  • Yield on Cost = Stabilized NOI ÷ Total Project Cost

Why It Matters

Yield is a quick way to compare income return across deals and evaluate whether the price compensates you for the risk. In practice, yield helps investors answer:

  • Is this asset priced to my required return?
  • Am I buying a stable income stream, or paying for future growth that may not arrive?
  • How does this compare to alternatives (other properties, debt yields, treasury yields, etc.)?

Investor Uses

  • Pricing discipline: set a maximum price based on target yield and underwritten NOI/cash flow.
  • Risk comparison: demand higher yields for higher volatility (lease-up, heavy capex, weak submarkets).
  • Business plan testing: track yield improvements from NOI growth, expense control, or re-tenanting.
  • Capital allocation: decide where to deploy equity when yields compress or expand.

Example

A property has an annual NOI of $600,000 and is offered at $12,000,000. The unlevered income yield (cap rate) is:

  • Cap Rate = 600,000 ÷ 12,000,000 = 5.0%

If you invest $4,000,000 of equity and the property produces $240,000 of annual pre-tax cash flow after debt service, your equity yield (cash-on-cash) is:

  • Cash-on-Cash = 240,000 ÷ 4,000,000 = 6.0%

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing yield definitions: “Yield” can mean cap rate, cash-on-cash, or yield on cost—always specify which.
  • Using pro forma as reality: forward yields depend on assumptions; stress test rents, vacancy, and expenses.
  • Ignoring capex and reserves: NOI-based yields can overstate true cash yield if recurring capital is material.
  • Not adjusting for risk: a higher yield may reflect weaker location, tenant quality, or deferred maintenance.
  • Apples-to-oranges comparisons: comparing a stabilized cap rate to a transitional cash-on-cash can mislead.

Cash Management

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Cash Management

Definition

Cash management is the set of processes used to track, control, and forecast a property’s cash: collecting income, paying expenses, funding reserves, and distributing remaining cash to owners. Good cash management keeps an asset liquid, reduces surprises, and supports consistent operations.

What Cash Management Covers

  • Cash collection: rent, utility bill-backs, fees, recoveries, and other income.
  • Disbursements: payroll, vendors, utilities, taxes, insurance, and debt service.
  • Reserves: operating reserves, replacement reserves, capex reserves, and escrows.
  • Controls: approvals, separation of duties, audits, and fraud prevention.
  • Forecasting: weekly/monthly cash projections tied to budgets and capital plans.

Common Structures

Property-level accounts

  • Operating account: day-to-day deposits and payments.
  • Reserve/escrow accounts: separate buckets for taxes, insurance, capex, or lender-required reserves.
  • Security deposit accounts: held separately where required by law or policy.

Lender-controlled cash management

Some loans require a lockbox or cash sweep, where tenant payments go into a controlled account and funds are released according to an agreed “waterfall” (operating costs, debt service, reserves, then distributions).

Why It Matters

Real estate fails more often from liquidity problems than from long-term value problems. Cash management helps ensure the property can pay obligations on time, absorb volatility, and avoid technical defaults (like missed escrow funding or reserve requirements).

Investor Uses

  • Budget discipline: compare actuals to budget and correct quickly.
  • Distribution planning: decide when cash is truly surplus versus needed for upcoming expenses.
  • Capex execution: fund projects without starving operations or triggering covenant stress.
  • Risk reduction: reduce fraud, leakage, and vendor overbilling through controls and approvals.

Simple “Cash Waterfall” Example

  1. Collect gross income into operating/lockbox account
  2. Pay property operating expenses (utilities, payroll, vendors)
  3. Pay debt service (principal + interest)
  4. Fund required escrows/reserves (taxes, insurance, replacement reserves)
  5. Retain a minimum operating balance (e.g., 1–2 months of expenses)
  6. Distribute remaining cash to ownership per the operating agreement

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-distributing: paying owners while deferring maintenance, taxes, or vendor bills.
  • Ignoring timing: cash timing matters—monthly rent collections vs. lumpy taxes/insurance and capex.
  • Weak controls: no approval limits, no dual authorization, or poor segregation of duties.
  • No forecast: relying on bank balance instead of a rolling cash forecast and variance review.
  • Mixing accounts: commingling reserves/security deposits with operating cash.

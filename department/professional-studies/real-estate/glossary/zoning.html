Zoning

Definition

Zoning is a system of local land-use regulation that determines what types of uses are allowed on a property and what can be built there. Zoning rules commonly govern permitted uses (residential, commercial, industrial, mixed-use), as well as the “shape” and intensity of development—such as density, height, setbacks, lot coverage, floor area ratio (FAR), and parking requirements.

What Zoning Typically Regulates

  • Permitted uses: what activities/building types can operate on-site.
  • Density and intensity: units per acre, FAR, minimum lot size, lot coverage.
  • Building form: height limits, setbacks, step-backs, frontage rules, design standards.
  • Parking and access: minimum/maximum parking, loading, curb cuts, ingress/egress.
  • Environmental constraints: critical areas, floodplain rules, tree retention, stormwater requirements.
  • Special overlays: historic districts, transit-oriented zones, waterfront overlays, etc.

Why It Matters

Zoning can be a value driver or a deal killer. It affects what you can build, what you can change, how fast you can execute, and how much capital you’ll need to comply. For investors, zoning influences both income durability (what uses are allowed) and upside potential (whether the site can be intensified or repurposed).

Common Zoning Outcomes for Investors

“As-of-right” development

If a proposed project complies with zoning standards, it may be approved through a relatively predictable permitting path. This usually lowers entitlement risk and improves financing certainty.

Conditional use / special permits

Some uses are allowed only with additional review. These approvals can add time, cost, and uncertainty.

Variances

A variance is permission to deviate from specific standards (like setbacks or height) when strict compliance creates a hardship under the local code. Variances are not guaranteed and can be politically sensitive.

Rezoning / entitlement change

A rezoning changes the property’s zoning designation to allow different uses or greater intensity. This can create major value—but it’s typically higher risk, longer timeline, and more exposed to public process.

Investor Uses

  • Highest and best use analysis: determine whether the current or alternative use is legally allowed.
  • Value-add feasibility: confirm renovations, additions, ADUs, or amenity conversions are permitted.
  • Development yield: estimate achievable unit count/FAR, parking needs, and buildable envelope.
  • Risk pricing: adjust returns for entitlement uncertainty and timeline risk.
  • Exit strategy: understand whether a buyer pool is constrained by legal nonconformities or use limits.

Example

An investor evaluates a corner parcel currently improved with a small retail building. The zoning allows mixed-use with a maximum height of 6 stories and a FAR that supports significantly more floor area than the existing building. Even if the current income is modest, the site may have strong residual land value due to redevelopment potential—subject to parking, design review, and permitting timelines.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming prior use equals permitted use: a property may be legal nonconforming (“grandfathered”) and restricted from expansion.
  • Overlooking parking math: parking minimums can reduce buildable area or force expensive structured parking.
  • Ignoring overlays and non-zoning rules: design review, historic rules, shoreline/flood regulations, and impact fees can dominate feasibility.
  • Misreading definitions: terms like “dwelling unit,” “hotel,” “short-term rental,” or “accessory use” are code-specific.
  • Not pricing timeline risk: entitlement delays can erase returns through carry costs and market shifts.

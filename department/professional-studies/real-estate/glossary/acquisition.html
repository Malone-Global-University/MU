Definition

An acquisition is the process of purchasing a real estate asset. It includes sourcing opportunities, underwriting financial performance, negotiating terms, arranging financing, conducting due diligence, and closing the transaction.

Acquisition Process Overview

  • Sourcing: Identifying on-market or off-market opportunities.
  • Underwriting: Analyzing income, expenses, risk, and projected returns.
  • Offer & Negotiation: Submitting a letter of intent (LOI) and negotiating terms.
  • Due Diligence: Reviewing leases, financials, inspections, environmental reports, and title.
  • Financing: Securing debt and equity capital.
  • Closing: Executing legal documents and transferring ownership.

Key Financial Considerations

  • Purchase price
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)
  • Capital expenditures
  • Financing structure
  • Projected internal rate of return (IRR)
  • Exit strategy and terminal value

Example

An investor identifies a multifamily property priced at $12,000,000 with projected NOI of $900,000.

  • Going-in Cap Rate = 900,000 ÷ 12,000,000 = 7.5%

The investor evaluates financing terms, growth assumptions, and risk before proceeding to contract.

Acquisition Strategies

  • Value-Add: Improve operations or renovate to increase NOI.
  • Core: Stable assets with predictable income.
  • Opportunistic: Higher-risk properties requiring repositioning.
  • Distressed: Acquiring underperforming or financially troubled assets.

Investor Perspective

Disciplined acquisition strategy requires adherence to predefined investment criteria, including required return thresholds and risk management standards.

Strong investors focus on income durability and downside protection, rather than relying solely on appreciation assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overpaying due to competitive pressure
  • Underestimating capital expenditures
  • Relying on optimistic rent growth assumptions
  • Failing to verify financial statements

Related Terms

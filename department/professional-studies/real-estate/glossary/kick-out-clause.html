Kick-Out Clause

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Kick-Out Clause

Definition

A kick-out clause is a contractual provision allowing a seller or landlord to terminate an agreement if specific conditions are not satisfied. It is most commonly used in purchase contracts involving contingencies (such as sale-of-home contingencies) or in leases tied to performance thresholds.

Common Applications

1) Purchase Agreements

Frequently used when a buyer’s offer is contingent on selling another property.

  • Seller continues marketing the property.
  • If a stronger offer is received, the seller notifies the original buyer.
  • The buyer must remove the contingency within a specified period (e.g., 48–72 hours) or be “kicked out.”

2) Commercial Leases

May allow a landlord to terminate a lease if:

  • Tenant sales fall below a required threshold.
  • An anchor tenant vacates.
  • Specific co-tenancy conditions fail.

Purpose

  • Reduces seller or landlord risk.
  • Maintains flexibility in uncertain transactions.
  • Encourages faster contingency resolution.
  • Protects against prolonged contract lock-ups.

Example (Residential)

A buyer makes an offer contingent upon selling their current home. The contract includes a kick-out clause allowing the seller to accept backup offers.

If another buyer presents a non-contingent offer, the original buyer has 72 hours to waive the contingency or the seller may terminate the agreement.

Example (Commercial Retail)

A retail lease includes a kick-out clause allowing the tenant to terminate the lease after year three if gross sales do not exceed a minimum annual threshold.

Financial Impact

  • Reduces time-on-market risk.
  • Improves liquidity for sellers.
  • Provides performance protection in retail leasing.
  • May reduce perceived contract certainty for buyers.

Negotiation Points

  • Notice period length.
  • Required evidence of competing offer.
  • Performance benchmarks (in leases).
  • Refundability of earnest money.

Risks and Pitfalls

  • Buyer uncertainty during contingency period.
  • Ambiguous triggering conditions.
  • Misunderstanding notice deadlines.
  • Market perception issues in competitive environments.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Assess probability of contingency satisfaction.
  • Model potential downtime or re-marketing costs.
  • Review lease termination impacts on valuation.
  • Analyze performance thresholds carefully in retail assets.

Kick-Out Clause vs. Termination Clause

  • Kick-Out Clause: Triggered by specific conditional events.
  • Termination Clause: May allow broader unilateral termination rights.

Security Deposit

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Security Deposit

Definition

A security deposit is money collected from a tenant (usually at move-in) that is held to protect the landlord if the tenant does not meet lease obligations. Depending on state and local law, deposits may be used to cover unpaid rent, damage beyond normal wear and tear, cleaning, and other allowable charges, with any remaining balance returned to the tenant after move-out.

What a Security Deposit Covers

Lease language and local regulations determine exactly what may be deducted. Common allowable uses include:

  • Unpaid rent or other unpaid lease charges (where permitted).
  • Damage beyond normal wear and tear (e.g., broken fixtures, holes, pet damage).
  • Cleaning needed to return the unit to the required condition (where permitted).
  • Missing items (keys, remotes) or unreturned access devices (where permitted).

“Normal wear and tear” generally means expected deterioration from ordinary use, not negligence or abuse.

How It’s Handled Operationally

  • Collection: typically at lease signing or before keys are released.
  • Documentation: move-in inspection photos and checklists reduce disputes.
  • Disposition: at move-out, the landlord provides an itemized statement of deductions (as required).
  • Return timing: most jurisdictions require return within a specific number of days.

Accounting Treatment

For property accounting, a security deposit is typically recorded as a liability (money owed back to the tenant) until applied to charges or refunded. It is generally not operating income when collected.

  • At move-in: Cash increases; Security Deposits Payable (liability) increases.
  • At move-out: Deposit is refunded (reducing cash and liability) or applied to charges (reducing liability and recognizing the appropriate revenue/receivable, depending on the item).

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Loss mitigation: reduces exposure to make-ready costs and non-payment at turnover.
  • Tenant quality signal: deposit levels (and alternatives) can influence applicant behavior.
  • Operational discipline: strong documentation and consistent policy reduce legal and reputational risk.
  • Liquidity management: deposits are “restricted in spirit” because they may need to be refunded.

Example

A tenant pays a $1,500 security deposit at move-in. At move-out, there is $250 in damage beyond normal wear (a broken blind and wall repair) and $100 in unpaid utilities permitted by the lease. The landlord deducts $350 with an itemized statement and refunds $1,150.

Common Pitfalls

  • Poor documentation: no move-in photos or condition report makes deductions hard to defend.
  • Inconsistent standards: different treatment across tenants increases dispute risk.
  • Over-reliance on deposits: deposits rarely cover the full cost of major damage or eviction.
  • Legal noncompliance: deadlines, notices, and allowable deductions vary by jurisdiction.
  • Misclassifying deposits as income: can inflate NOI and mislead underwriting.

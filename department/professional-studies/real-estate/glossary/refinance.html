Refinance

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Refinance

Definition

A refinance (refinancing) is the process of replacing an existing mortgage or loan with a new loan secured by the same property. Investors refinance to reduce borrowing cost, change loan structure, extend maturity, or access equity—depending on market rates, property performance, and strategy.

Why Investors Refinance

  • Lower the interest rate: reduce debt service and improve cash flow.
  • Change loan terms: extend maturity, adjust amortization, or switch from floating to fixed (or vice versa).
  • Cash-out equity: extract capital for renovations, acquisitions, or returning equity to investors.
  • Remove a partner or pay off a lien: simplify ownership or clear title issues.
  • Stabilization milestone: refinance after lease-up or renovation to move from bridge debt to permanent financing.

Common Types of Refinances

Rate-and-term refinance

A refinance that primarily changes the interest rate, amortization, or loan term without taking significant cash out. The goal is usually lower monthly payments or better loan structure.

Cash-out refinance

A refinance that increases the loan amount above the current payoff to distribute cash proceeds to the borrower/ownership (subject to lender constraints). Often used after NOI growth or appreciation.

Bridge-to-perm (takeout refinance)

Replacing a short-term bridge or construction loan with longer-term permanent financing once the property is stabilized and meets lender requirements.

Key Underwriting Inputs

  • Appraised value: determines maximum leverage (LTV) on the new loan.
  • Net Operating Income (NOI): drives loan sizing under DSCR / debt yield tests.
  • Loan-to-Value (LTV): common sizing constraint, especially for cash-out refis.
  • DSCR: lender requires adequate coverage of the new debt service.
  • Debt yield: common in commercial lending as a risk metric.
  • Rate lock / pricing: interest rate, points, and fees determine true economics.

Costs and Tradeoffs

Refinancing is not free. Typical costs can include:

  • Origination fees / points
  • Appraisal and third-party reports (environmental, engineering, etc.)
  • Title, escrow, and legal fees
  • Prepayment penalties on the existing loan (yield maintenance, defeasance, step-down penalties)
  • Rate lock or extension fees if timing slips

Investors typically compare all-in refinance costs against expected savings or strategic value (cash-out proceeds, maturity extension, risk reduction).

Example

An apartment property improves NOI through renovations and better operations. The value increases, and the sponsor refinances from a higher-cost bridge loan into a fixed-rate permanent loan.

  • The new loan has a lower rate and longer term.
  • Debt service drops, increasing annual cash flow.
  • If sized larger, the refinance may also return a portion of investor equity (cash-out), while still meeting lender DSCR/LTV limits.

Risks to Watch

  • Rate risk: if rates rise before locking, the refinance may no longer pencil.
  • Valuation risk: appraisal comes in low, reducing proceeds or forcing paydown.
  • Execution risk: delayed reporting, occupancy dips, or expenses can hurt underwriting.
  • Prepayment penalties: yield maintenance/defeasance can kill economics on CMBS/agency loans.
  • Recourse changes: a new loan may introduce guarantees or carve-outs you didn’t have before.

Common Pitfalls

  • Focusing only on the rate: points, fees, and amortization can matter more than headline rate.
  • Ignoring covenant constraints: cash-out limits, reserves, and reporting requirements can tighten.
  • Underestimating timing: commercial refinances often require multiple third-party reports and lender committee approvals.
  • Not modeling downside: small NOI drops can reduce loan proceeds materially under DSCR or debt yield sizing.

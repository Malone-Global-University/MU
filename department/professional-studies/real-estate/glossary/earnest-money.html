Earnest Money

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Earnest Money

Definition

Earnest money is a good-faith deposit a buyer puts down after signing a purchase and sale agreement to show they intend to close. The deposit is typically held by an escrow or closing agent and is usually credited toward the buyer’s cash needed at closing.

Whether earnest money is refundable depends on the contract’s contingencies, timelines, and default provisions. If a buyer terminates properly during an allowed contingency period, earnest money is often returned. If the buyer defaults after the deposit becomes non-refundable, the seller may keep it as liquidated damages (subject to the contract and local rules).

How It Works

  • Deposit timing: usually due within a short window after contract execution (often 1–3 business days).
  • Held in escrow: deposited with a neutral third party (escrow, title company, or attorney, depending on state practice).
  • Applied at closing: commonly credited toward the purchase price or closing funds.
  • Contingency controls: inspection, financing, appraisal, title, and other contingencies govern refundability.

Why It Matters

Earnest money is the buyer’s first major “skin in the game.” For sellers, it signals seriousness and helps compensate for lost time if the buyer walks. For buyers, it is a risk-management tool: the deposit size and the point at which it becomes non-refundable influence negotiation leverage and downside protection during diligence.

What Drives Earnest Money Size

  • Market conditions: competitive markets tend to push deposits higher.
  • Asset type: commercial and multifamily deals often use larger deposits than small residential transactions.
  • Diligence scope: longer diligence periods can lead sellers to ask for more earnest money.
  • Buyer strength: all-cash buyers and experienced sponsors may negotiate terms differently.
  • Timing and risk: faster closes, waived contingencies, or “hard” money sooner typically require higher deposits.

Soft vs. Hard Earnest Money

Contracts often describe earnest money as “soft” (refundable) during a defined contingency window, and “hard” (non-refundable) after contingencies expire or are waived.

  • Soft period: buyer can cancel for permitted reasons (e.g., inspections) and recover the deposit.
  • Go-hard date: deposit becomes non-refundable, often at the end of the inspection period.
  • Staged deposits: some deals use an initial deposit and an additional deposit that goes hard later.

Example

A buyer signs a contract to acquire a 40-unit property for $6,000,000 and posts $150,000 of earnest money (2.5%) into escrow. The contract includes a 21-day inspection period. If the buyer terminates within the inspection period under the inspection contingency, the earnest money is returned. If the buyer proceeds past day 21 and later fails to close without a valid contractual reason, the earnest money may be forfeited to the seller as liquidated damages.

Common Pitfalls

  • Missing notice deadlines: failing to deliver termination or objection notices on time can make the deposit non-refundable.
  • Vague contingencies: poorly drafted contingency language can create disputes over refundability.
  • Releasing “hard” money too early: going hard before completing inspections, bids, or financing approvals increases downside risk.
  • Escrow release disputes: if parties disagree, escrow may hold funds until a written release, mediation, or legal process resolves it.
  • Confusing earnest money with down payment: earnest money is a deposit; the down payment is part of total equity/cash to close.

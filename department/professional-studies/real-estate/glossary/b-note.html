B-Note

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary B-Note

Definition

A B-Note is the subordinate portion of a commercial real estate loan that sits behind the senior A-Note in the capital stack. Both notes are secured by the same mortgage, but the B-Note is paid after the A-Note and therefore carries higher risk and usually earns a higher interest rate.

How A-Notes and B-Notes Work

In large commercial loans, lenders often divide a mortgage into multiple pieces. The safest portion becomes the A-Note, while the riskier portion becomes the B-Note.

  • A-Note: senior debt with first claim on loan payments.
  • B-Note: subordinate position with higher yield but greater risk.
  • Same collateral: both are secured by the same underlying property.
  • Waterfall payments: A-Note lenders are paid first before B-Note holders.

Why B-Notes Exist

B-Notes allow lenders to split risk across investors. Conservative lenders may hold the A-Note, while higher-yield investors purchase the B-Note portion. This structure helps finance large deals while matching investors with different risk tolerances.

Position in the Capital Stack

The B-Note sits between senior debt and equity in the capital structure. It carries more risk than the A-Note but less risk than mezzanine debt or equity.

  • Senior Debt – A-Note
  • Subordinate Debt – B-Note
  • Mezzanine Debt
  • Preferred Equity
  • Common Equity

Investor Considerations

  • Higher yield: investors receive a higher interest rate to compensate for risk.
  • Subordination risk: losses hit the B-Note before the A-Note.
  • Control rights: B-Note holders may have limited control compared with senior lenders.
  • Default exposure: recovery depends on property value and senior debt balance.

Example

A $50 million commercial mortgage might be split into a $40 million A-Note and a $10 million B-Note. If the property performs well, both lenders receive their payments. If the property defaults and sells for less than the loan balance, the B-Note investors typically absorb losses first after the senior lender is paid.

Common Pitfalls

  • Misunderstanding subordination: B-Notes are exposed to loss earlier than senior debt.
  • Overestimating control: B-Note holders may have limited influence over loan servicing.
  • Market liquidity risk: secondary markets for subordinate loan pieces can be thin.
  • Underestimating downside: property value declines can quickly wipe out subordinate positions.

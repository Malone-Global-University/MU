GP/LP (General Partner / Limited Partner)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary GP/LP

Definition

GP/LP describes a common real estate investment structure in which the General Partner (GP) (also called the sponsor) organizes and manages the investment, while Limited Partners (LPs) contribute most of the equity capital and receive returns according to a waterfall. LPs typically have limited liability and limited day-to-day control; the GP has management authority and earns fees and/or carried interest (promote).

Who Does What

General Partner (GP)

  • Finds the deal: sourcing, underwriting, negotiating purchase and financing.
  • Forms the entity: legal structure, offering documents, compliance, investor onboarding.
  • Executes the business plan: operations oversight, asset management, capex/renovations, leasing strategy.
  • Reports to investors: financial reporting, distributions, tax packages, communications.
  • Decision authority: controls major decisions subject to the operating agreement / LPA.

Limited Partner (LP)

  • Provides capital: contributes equity to fund acquisition and execute the plan.
  • Receives distributions: paid based on the waterfall (often after fees and reserves).
  • Limited liability: generally not personally liable beyond invested capital (structure-dependent).
  • Limited control: typically votes only on major events (e.g., removal of GP for cause, amendments, sale timing) depending on documents.

How Everyone Gets Paid

GP and LP economics are defined in the operating agreement / limited partnership agreement. The most common components are:

  • Preferred return (LP): a target return paid to LPs before promote begins (e.g., 7–9% pref, deal-specific).
  • Profit split: remaining cash flow is split between LPs and GP (e.g., 80/20) and may include tiers.
  • Carried interest / promote (GP): performance-based share of profits above hurdles.
  • Fees (GP or affiliates): acquisition fee, asset management fee, construction management, disposition fee, loan guaranty fees, etc.

Why This Structure Exists

GP/LP structures allow specialization: LPs allocate capital and diversify across deals, while GPs apply operational skill to create value. Ideally, the structure aligns incentives by rewarding the GP for achieving durable outcomes (higher NOI, controlled risk, strong execution) while protecting LP downside through priority distributions and governance.

Key Terms to Review in Any GP/LP Deal

  • Control & decisions: who can refinance, sell, approve budgets, and change the business plan?
  • Fees: size, timing, and whether fees are offset against promote or paid regardless of performance.
  • Waterfall mechanics: pref definition (simple vs compounding), tiers, and any catch-up provisions.
  • Capital calls: whether LPs can be required to contribute more capital and what happens if they don’t.
  • Removal rights: “for cause” vs “no-fault” removal, voting thresholds, and consequences.
  • Reporting standards: frequency, detail, audit/review expectations, and transparency.
  • Conflicts of interest: related-party property management, construction, brokerage, or vendor relationships.

Example

A multifamily syndication raises $10M of LP equity. The GP contributes $500k, signs on key contracts, and runs operations. The distribution waterfall is:

  • LPs receive an 8% preferred return.
  • Then profits are split 80% LP / 20% GP (the GP’s 20% is the promote/carried interest component).

In this setup, the GP earns meaningful upside only if cash flow and/or sale proceeds exceed the level needed to return capital and pay the pref.

Common Pitfalls

  • Headline split confusion: “80/20” can hide tiers, catch-ups, and fee load—model the full waterfall.
  • Misaligned incentives: high fixed fees can reduce urgency to perform if most GP compensation is not performance-based.
  • Weak governance: limited reporting and unclear decision rights increase execution risk.
  • Overreliance on appreciation: promote-heavy deals can encourage optimistic exit assumptions instead of operational value creation.

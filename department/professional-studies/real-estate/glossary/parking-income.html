Parking Income

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Parking Income

Definition

Parking income is other income generated by charging residents (and sometimes guests) for parking. It can include monthly fees for reserved stalls, covered spaces, garages, tandem spaces, premium locations, and add-ons such as EV charging.

What Creates Parking Income

Parking revenue depends on the relationship between supply (how many spaces exist) and demand (how many residents value and will pay for them), plus how the property assigns, enforces, and prices access.

Common parking revenue sources

  • Reserved parking: assigned spaces for a monthly premium.
  • Covered parking / carports: weather-protected spaces priced above open stalls.
  • Garages: detached or attached garages, often the highest-priced category.
  • Premium spaces: closest-to-unit or best-access stalls.
  • Second vehicle fees: pricing for additional vehicles per unit.
  • Visitor permits: paid guest passes (market-dependent and policy-sensitive).
  • EV charging fees: flat monthly add-on or pay-per-kWh (if infrastructure supports it).

How It’s Underwritten

Parking income is typically modeled as $ / space / month multiplied by utilization (how many spaces are actually leased), or as $ / unit / month based on historical collections. Underwriting should focus on collections, not just posted pricing.

Key underwriting inputs

  • Space inventory: total stalls by type (open, covered, garage, ADA, visitor).
  • Utilization: leased spaces ÷ leasable spaces (by category).
  • Pricing ladder: open vs covered vs garage vs premium pricing gaps.
  • Lease language: parking addendum, renewals, and notice requirements for price changes.
  • Collections & bad debt: actual collected parking income after delinquencies/waivers.

Why It Matters

Parking income is often a high-margin revenue stream because incremental costs can be low once spaces exist. It can meaningfully lift NOI, but it’s also easy to overstate if you assume 100% adoption or ignore resident pushback, enforcement costs, or local constraints.

Investor Uses

  • NOI growth: monetize underpriced spaces or convert “free” parking into paid tiers.
  • Operational control: reduce abuse with permits, towing policy, and clear rules.
  • Product segmentation: create premium options (reserved, covered, garage) for willingness-to-pay.
  • Retention strategy: treat parking as a service—predictable rules reduce resident friction.

Example

A 120-unit property has 140 leasable spaces: 100 open stalls and 40 covered spaces. The property charges $35/month for open and $65/month for covered. Utilization is 70% for open stalls and 85% for covered.

  • Open income = 100 × 70% × $35 = $2,450/month
  • Covered income = 40 × 85% × $65 = $2,210/month
  • Total parking income = $2,450 + $2,210 = $4,660/month (=$55,920/year)

If you raise covered parking by $10/month and utilization holds, incremental NOI is: 40 × 85% × $10 = $340/month (=$4,080/year), before any added admin or churn effects.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming full adoption: not every resident has a car or will pay for a reserved stall.
  • Ignoring supply constraints: some markets require minimum free parking ratios.
  • Poor enforcement: without permits/towing policy, paid spaces get abused and revenue erodes.
  • Double counting: charging for spaces already bundled into rent can create resident disputes.
  • Overlooking costs: signage, permits, striping, lighting, gate maintenance, and towing admin.
  • Not differentiating categories: pricing everything the same leaves money on the table.

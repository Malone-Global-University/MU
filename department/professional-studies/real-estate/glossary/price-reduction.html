Price Reduction

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Price Reduction

Definition

A price reduction is a negotiated decrease in the agreed purchase price of a property. It can occur before contract execution (listing price reduction) or during escrow after inspections, appraisal, or financing issues are identified.

When Price Reductions Occur

  • Inspection findings: Material defects or deferred maintenance discovered.
  • Appraisal shortfall: Appraised value comes in below contract price.
  • Market softening: Shifts in supply/demand during listing period.
  • Financing constraints: Buyer unable to close at original price.
  • Time pressure: Seller motivation increases as days-on-market rise.

How It Differs from Seller Credit

  • Price reduction: Lowers the official contract price.
  • Seller credit: Leaves price unchanged but offsets buyer costs at closing.
  • Appraisal impact: Price reductions typically reduce appraisal risk.
  • Loan structure: Credits are subject to lender caps; price reductions are not.

Underwriting Implications

  • Improved basis: Lower purchase price improves yield and return metrics.
  • Lower loan amount: Reduced LTV if financing remains proportional.
  • Cap rate expansion: NOI divided by lower price increases going-in cap rate.
  • Resale comps: Official recorded price affects future valuation benchmarks.

Example

Original contract price: $2,000,000
Inspection reveals $150,000 in roof and exterior repairs.

Parties agree to a $120,000 price reduction, resulting in a revised contract price of $1,880,000. The buyer assumes responsibility for repairs but benefits from improved basis and long-term return potential.

Strategic Considerations

  • Market optics: Recorded price reductions can signal weakness.
  • Negotiation leverage: Strong buyers may prefer price reductions to credits.
  • Tax implications: Lower basis can affect depreciation schedules.
  • Future financing: Lower acquisition basis may support refinancing stability.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming easy renegotiation: Sellers may resist mid-escrow changes.
  • Over-negotiating: Risking contract termination.
  • Misjudging appraisal: Reductions may still not meet lender valuation.
  • Ignoring market cycle: Reductions in strong markets may be unrealistic.

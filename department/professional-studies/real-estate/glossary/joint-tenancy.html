Joint Tenancy

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Definition

Joint tenancy is a form of real estate ownership in which two or more individuals hold equal ownership interests with a right of survivorship. When one owner dies, their interest automatically transfers to the surviving joint tenant(s), rather than passing through probate.

Key Characteristics

  • Equal ownership shares.
  • Right of survivorship.
  • Simultaneous acquisition of interest.
  • Identical title and rights among co-owners.

The Four Unities

For joint tenancy to exist, four legal “unities” must typically be present:

  • Unity of Time: Interests acquired at the same time.
  • Unity of Title: Interests acquired through the same deed.
  • Unity of Interest: Equal ownership percentages.
  • Unity of Possession: Equal rights to use the entire property.

Example

Two siblings purchase a rental property as joint tenants. If one sibling passes away, full ownership automatically transfers to the surviving sibling without probate proceedings.

Joint Tenancy vs. Tenancy in Common

  • Joint Tenancy: Equal ownership + right of survivorship.
  • Tenancy in Common: Ownership shares may differ and no automatic survivorship.

Advantages

  • Avoids probate upon death of an owner.
  • Simplifies transfer of ownership.
  • Common in family or spousal ownership structures.

Risks and Considerations

  • Ownership automatically passes to surviving co-owner(s), regardless of will.
  • Each owner may encumber or transfer their interest, potentially severing joint tenancy.
  • Creditors of one owner may attach that owner’s interest.

Severance of Joint Tenancy

Joint tenancy may be severed if one owner transfers their interest, converting ownership into a tenancy in common.

Estate Planning Implications

  • Bypasses probate court.
  • May conflict with broader estate planning goals.
  • Tax implications vary depending on jurisdiction.

Investor Considerations

  • Less common in institutional real estate.
  • More common in residential or family-owned assets.
  • Joint liability may impact financing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Misunderstanding survivorship rights.
  • Unintended disinheritance of heirs.
  • Failure to coordinate with estate planning documents.

Hard Costs

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Hard Costs

Definition

Hard costs are the direct, physical costs of construction or renovation—primarily labor and materials that permanently improve, build, or repair the property. If you can “touch” it and it becomes part of the building, it is usually a hard cost.

What Hard Costs Include

Hard costs can vary by asset type and scope, but they commonly include:

  • Site work: grading, demolition, excavation, utilities, paving, landscaping, fencing.
  • Structure & envelope: foundations, framing, roofing, siding, windows, waterproofing.
  • MEP systems: mechanical (HVAC), electrical, plumbing, fire/life safety.
  • Interior construction: drywall, flooring, cabinets, countertops, paint, doors, trim.
  • Appliances & fixtures: ranges, refrigerators, lighting, plumbing fixtures, hardware.
  • Common areas & amenities: clubhouse buildout, fitness rooms, pools, signage (if installed as part of the project).
  • GC and subcontractor labor: trade labor tied to physical work in place.

Hard Costs vs. Soft Costs

Hard costs are the “build” costs. Soft costs are the professional, administrative, and carrying items needed to plan, permit, finance, and manage the project.

Examples of soft costs

  • Architecture/engineering, design, surveys, third-party reports
  • Permits, impact fees, inspections, legal, accounting
  • Construction loan interest, lender fees, insurance, taxes during construction
  • Developer fee, project management, leasing/marketing during lease-up

How Investors Budget Hard Costs

Hard costs are typically built from a scope of work and refined as certainty improves:

  • Concept / early underwriting: rule-of-thumb budgets (per unit or per square foot).
  • Pre-close: contractor walk-throughs, preliminary bids, and unit-by-unit scopes.
  • Execution: signed contracts, schedules of values, and tracked change orders.

Common budgeting formats

  • Per unit: useful for multifamily interior renovations (e.g., “$9,500 per unit”).
  • Per square foot: common for new construction or large common-area scopes.
  • Line-item scope: most accurate; ties dollars to quantities (doors, windows, roofs, etc.).

Why It Matters

Hard costs are usually the largest controllable input in a renovation or development budget. Underwriting errors in hard costs can destroy returns through cost overruns, delayed lease-up, lender covenant stress, and reduced exit value.

Example

A 24-unit value-add plan budgets $10,000 per unit for interiors and $120,000 for exterior work (roof repairs, paint, and parking lot patching).

  • Interior hard costs = 24 × 10,000 = $240,000
  • Exterior hard costs = $120,000
  • Total hard costs = 240,000 + 120,000 = $360,000

If actual costs come in 12% higher due to change orders and hidden conditions, the overrun is $43,200 (360,000 × 0.12), which may require additional equity or reduce distributions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Incomplete scope: missing line items (demo, disposal, prep work) that show up later as change orders.
  • Hidden conditions: plumbing leaks, electrical issues, rot, mold, or structural problems.
  • Underestimating labor markets: wage spikes, trade shortages, or union requirements.
  • Timing risk: delays increase carrying costs and can reduce achievable rent premiums.
  • Weak contract structure: unclear inclusions/exclusions, allowances, or poor change-order controls.
  • No contingency: most projects need a hard-cost contingency based on complexity and age of asset.

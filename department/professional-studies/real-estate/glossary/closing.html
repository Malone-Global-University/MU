Closing

Definition

Closing (also called settlement) is the final step in a real estate transaction where the parties sign the required documents, the buyer’s funds and lender proceeds are disbursed, and ownership is transferred from seller to buyer.

What Happens at Closing

  • Documents are executed: purchase documents, loan documents (if financed), and transfer documents.
  • Funds are collected and disbursed: purchase price, fees, payoffs, credits, and prorations are settled.
  • Title is conveyed: the deed is delivered and typically recorded.
  • Possession is transferred: keys, access codes, and control of the property move to the buyer (per agreement).

Key Parties Involved

  • Buyer and seller
  • Escrow/closing agent (or attorney, depending on state)
  • Title company
  • Lender (if financed)
  • Insurance broker (evidence of coverage is often required)

Common Closing Documents

  • Deed: transfers ownership to the buyer.
  • Closing statement: itemized list of debits, credits, fees, and prorations (see closing statement).
  • Promissory note and deed of trust/mortgage: establishes the loan and lender security interest (financed deals).
  • Title policy: issued after closing to insure title (subject to exceptions).
  • Bill of sale / assignment & assumption: transfers personal property and assigns contracts/leases (common in commercial).
  • Tenant estoppels: confirm lease terms and balances (commercial and multifamily acquisitions).

Prorations and Adjustments

Most transactions include prorations to allocate expenses and income as of the closing date—commonly property taxes, rents, utilities, and prepaid items. These adjustments are reflected on the closing statement.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Cash-to-close accuracy: final costs determine your true basis and invested capital.
  • Risk containment: title, payoff, and document errors can create costly post-close problems.
  • Operational continuity: assignments of leases, service contracts, and deposits affect day-one operations.
  • Compliance: recording and funding timelines matter for legal ownership and lender requirements.

Common Pitfalls

  • Last-minute fee creep: lender/title/escrow charges not modeled in underwriting.
  • Incorrect prorations: taxes or rent credits misapplied.
  • Uncleared liens: payoffs not properly handled, leaving title issues.
  • Missing assignments: contracts, warranties, or permits not transferred.

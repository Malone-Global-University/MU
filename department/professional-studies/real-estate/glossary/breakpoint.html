Breakpoint

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Breakpoint

Definition

A Breakpoint is the sales threshold in a percentage rent lease above which a tenant must pay additional rent calculated as a percentage of gross sales.

In simple terms: once the tenant’s sales exceed the breakpoint, the landlord shares in the extra revenue.

How Breakpoints Work

Percentage Rent Formula

Percentage Rent = (Gross Sales – Breakpoint) × Percentage Rate

Example

  • Base rent: $200,000 annually
  • Percentage rate: 5%
  • Breakpoint: $4,000,000
  • Actual sales: $4,500,000

Sales above breakpoint = $500,000
Percentage rent = $500,000 × 5% = $25,000

Total annual rent = $200,000 + $25,000 = $225,000

Types of Breakpoints

Natural Breakpoint

Calculated by dividing base rent by the percentage rate:

Breakpoint = Base Rent ÷ Percentage Rate

Example: $200,000 ÷ 5% = $4,000,000

Artificial Breakpoint

A negotiated breakpoint set higher or lower than the natural breakpoint. Artificial breakpoints shift risk between landlord and tenant.

  • Higher breakpoint: Favors tenant (less likely to pay percentage rent).
  • Lower breakpoint: Favors landlord (percentage rent triggers earlier).

Where Breakpoints Are Common

  • Shopping centers
  • Malls
  • Outlet centers
  • Restaurant leases
  • Entertainment retail

Breakpoint and NOI

  • Creates variable income upside.
  • Improves NOI during strong sales periods.
  • Adds volatility in retail-heavy portfolios.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Historical tenant sales data.
  • Sales per square foot benchmarks.
  • Market demographics and foot traffic.
  • Tenant credit quality.
  • Lease audit rights for verifying sales reporting.

Breakpoint vs. Base Rent

  • Base Rent: Guaranteed minimum income.
  • Breakpoint: Trigger for performance-based rent.

Breakpoints align landlord income with tenant performance, while base rent ensures minimum revenue.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overestimating sales growth.
  • Setting breakpoints too high to realistically trigger.
  • Vague definitions of “gross sales.”
  • Failing to include audit rights.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm breakpoint type (natural or artificial).
  • Analyze tenant historical sales.
  • Model conservative and upside scenarios.
  • Verify percentage rate and escalation terms.
  • Review lease reporting and audit provisions.

Hold Harmless Agreement

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Hold Harmless Agreement

Definition

A hold harmless agreement is a contract clause in which one party agrees to protect another party from certain legal claims, losses, or liabilities. In real estate, it is commonly used to allocate risk between owners, landlords, tenants, contractors, and vendors.

How It Works

A hold harmless provision typically states that one party will not hold the other responsible for specified risks. It may also require the indemnifying party to cover legal costs, settlements, or damages arising from those risks.

Typical Language Includes

  • “Indemnify, defend, and hold harmless…”
  • Scope of covered claims
  • Identification of protected parties (owner, manager, affiliates)
  • Exclusions (e.g., gross negligence or willful misconduct)

Common Real Estate Applications

  • Leases: Tenants hold landlords harmless for injuries arising from tenant operations.
  • Construction contracts: Contractors assume liability for jobsite-related incidents.
  • Vendor agreements: Service providers protect owners from claims tied to their work.
  • Event usage: Third-party event organizers assume event-related risk.

Why It Matters for Investors

Hold harmless clauses directly affect risk exposure and insurance coverage. They determine who pays when something goes wrong — not just operationally, but legally.

  • Reduces owner liability exposure
  • Supports insurance structure
  • Clarifies responsibility allocation
  • Protects long-term asset value

Insurance Coordination

Hold harmless clauses should align with insurance requirements. Investors typically require:

  • Additional insured endorsements
  • Certificates of insurance (COI)
  • Minimum policy limits
  • Waiver of subrogation provisions

Example

A property owner hires a landscaping company. The contract states that the landscaper will indemnify and hold harmless the owner for any injuries resulting from landscaping work. If a passerby trips over equipment left by the landscaper, the landscaper (and its insurer) would typically handle the claim, subject to contract terms and enforceability.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overbroad language shifting unintended risk
  • State laws limiting enforceability in construction contracts
  • Failure to align clause with insurance coverage
  • Assuming clause is effective without reviewing policy endorsements

Related Terms

  • Indemnification
  • Additional insured
  • Certificate of insurance
  • General liability insurance
  • Negligence

Definition

A broker is a licensed real estate professional who represents buyers, sellers, landlords, or tenants in real estate transactions. Brokers facilitate negotiations, market properties, source opportunities, and typically earn compensation through commissions.

Types of Brokers

  • Investment Sales Broker: Represents buyers or sellers in property acquisitions and dispositions.
  • Leasing Broker: Represents landlords or tenants in lease negotiations.
  • Capital Markets Broker: Arranges debt or equity financing for real estate projects.
  • Residential Broker: Focuses on single-family and small multifamily transactions.

Broker Compensation

Brokers are generally compensated through commissions, which may be:

  • A percentage of the sale price (e.g., 1%–6% depending on asset type)
  • A percentage of total lease value
  • A structured fee based on loan amount (capital markets)

Role in Transactions

  • Marketing properties and sourcing buyers
  • Conducting pricing analysis and broker opinions of value (BOV)
  • Coordinating due diligence
  • Managing negotiations and offers
  • Facilitating communication between parties

Broker vs. Agent

In many jurisdictions, a broker holds a higher-level license and may supervise agents. Agents operate under a licensed broker.

Investor Perspective

Experienced investors build strong relationships with brokers to gain access to off-market deals, market intelligence, and early visibility into opportunities.

However, investors should independently verify underwriting assumptions and not rely solely on broker-provided projections.

Common Pitfalls

  • Relying exclusively on broker underwriting
  • Ignoring conflicts of interest (brokers are typically paid on transaction completion)
  • Failing to negotiate commission terms
  • Assuming marketing price equals market value

Related Terms

  • Broker Commission
  • Broker Opinion of Value (BOV)
  • Disposition
  • Acquisition
  • Listing Agreement

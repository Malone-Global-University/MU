Gross Operating Income (GOI)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Gross Operating Income (GOI)

Definition

Gross Operating Income (GOI) is the total income a property generates from normal operations after accounting for vacancy and credit/collection loss, but before deducting operating expenses.

In plain English: GOI is what the property actually brings in (rent + other income), net of vacancy/credit loss, before you pay bills to operate the building.

Why GOI Matters

GOI is a core underwriting bridge between top-line revenue assumptions and Net Operating Income (NOI). If GOI is overstated, the whole valuation stack gets distorted.

  • It converts “market rent” assumptions into collected income.
  • It reveals operational truth. Two properties with the same rents can have very different GOI due to vacancy, concessions, or collections.
  • It is the income base for expense ratios (e.g., operating expense % of income).
  • It drives NOI (NOI = GOI − operating expenses).

GOI vs. GPR vs. EGI (Common Terminology)

Different investors and lenders use slightly different labels. The important part is the logic: “potential income” → “collected/operating income” → “net income.”

  • Gross Potential Rent (GPR): Rent at 100% occupancy at contract/market rates (before vacancy and credit loss).
  • Gross Operating Income (GOI): Collected income after vacancy/credit loss, including other income (before operating expenses).
  • Effective Gross Income (EGI): Often used as a synonym for GOI (but definitions vary by shop).

If you see both GOI and EGI in the same model, confirm what each includes so you don’t double-count vacancy loss or “other income.”

How GOI Is Calculated

A common underwriting flow looks like this:

  • Gross Potential Rent (GPR)
  • + Other Income (laundry, parking, pet rent, RUBS, late fees, storage, application fees, etc.)
  • − Vacancy & Credit Loss (economic vacancy, bad debt, concessions/discounts depending on model)
  • = Gross Operating Income (GOI)

What Counts as “Other Income”

“Other income” should be recurring, property-level revenue related to operations. Common categories include:

  • Utility reimbursements (e.g., billbacks, RUBS)
  • Parking, garages, storage, and reserved spaces
  • Pet rent, pet fees, amenity fees
  • Laundry / vending
  • Late fees (use conservatively)

Avoid inflating GOI with one-time or non-recurring items unless you clearly disclose them as non-stabilized.

What Usually Does Not Belong in GOI

  • Loan proceeds or refinance cash-out (financing is not operating income).
  • Security deposits (liabilities, not revenue).
  • Insurance claim proceeds (non-recurring; treat separately).
  • Sale of assets (e.g., selling equipment) unless your accounting framework defines it clearly and you normalize it.

Example (Simple Underwriting)

Imagine a 50-unit building with $2,000 average monthly rent:

  • GPR: 50 × $2,000 × 12 = $1,200,000
  • Other income: $75,000
  • Vacancy & credit loss: 6% of (GPR + other income) = 6% × $1,275,000 = $76,500
  • GOI: $1,275,000 − $76,500 = $1,198,500

If operating expenses are $525,000, then: NOI = $1,198,500 − $525,000 = $673,500.

GOI and Valuation

GOI is not typically capitalized directly, but it drives NOI, which is commonly valued using a cap rate:

  • Value (income approach) ≈ NOI ÷ Cap Rate

That’s why disciplined investors stress-test GOI assumptions: vacancy, concessions, and collections can move value more than small tweaks in expenses.

Common Pitfalls

  • Double-counting vacancy loss (e.g., deducting vacancy from rent and also applying vacancy to total income again).
  • Overstating “other income” without verifying historical collections.
  • Ignoring concessions (free rent, lease-up specials) that reduce economic income.
  • Using pro forma rents while underwriting today’s vacancy/credit loss as if the property is stabilized.
  • Blending one-time fees into recurring income and capitalizing it as if permanent.

Investor Checklist

  • Reconcile T-12 income to bank deposits or property management reports when possible.
  • Confirm whether vacancy/credit loss is applied to rent only or total income (rent + other income).
  • Separate “stabilized” vs. “in-place” GOI if the property is in lease-up or heavy turnover.
  • Validate other income categories line-by-line (don’t assume).
  • Pressure-test GOI using downside vacancy and collection assumptions.

Leverage

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Leverage

Definition

Leverage is the use of borrowed money (debt) to acquire, improve, or refinance real estate. Leverage can increase returns on the investor’s equity when things go well, but it also increases risk because debt must be repaid regardless of property performance.

How Leverage Works

Real estate returns come from income (cash flow), appreciation, and debt paydown. When you use debt, you control a larger asset with less equity. If the property’s return exceeds the cost of debt, leverage can boost equity returns. If property income falls or financing costs rise, leverage can quickly compress cash flow and reduce investor flexibility.

Common Ways Leverage Is Measured

Loan-to-Value (LTV)

LTV describes how much of the property value is financed with debt.

  • LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Property Value (or Purchase Price, depending on lender)

Loan-to-Cost (LTC)

LTC is common in value-add or development where the “cost” includes purchase plus renovation or project costs.

  • LTC = Loan Amount ÷ Total Project Cost

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

DSCR measures how comfortably the property’s income covers the debt payments.

  • DSCR = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Annual Debt Service

Debt Yield

Debt yield measures the property’s NOI relative to the loan amount (a lender-focused risk metric).

  • Debt Yield = NOI ÷ Loan Amount

Why It Matters

  • Return amplification: leverage can increase cash-on-cash returns and IRR when the deal performs.
  • Downside exposure: a leveraged deal can move from “fine” to “tight” quickly if NOI drops.
  • Refinance risk: maturity dates, floating rates, and cap-rate shifts can create capital calls.
  • Flexibility: lower leverage can preserve options (re-tenanting, renovations, longer lease-up).

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting: stress-test DSCR, debt yield, and breakeven occupancy under downside scenarios.
  • Strategy fit: match leverage to business plan (core vs value-add vs development).
  • Risk management: decide between fixed vs floating debt, rate caps, and amortization profiles.
  • Portfolio control: use leverage to balance total exposure across assets and markets.

Example

An investor buys a property for $10,000,000 with a $7,000,000 loan and $3,000,000 equity. That’s 70% LTV.

If the property’s NOI grows and value increases, equity returns can expand because the investor captured the upside on the full $10M asset with only $3M of cash. But if NOI declines, debt payments remain, and equity can be wiped out faster because it is a smaller cushion.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-leveraging: thin DSCR leaves little room for vacancy, repairs, or rent softness.
  • Ignoring refinance conditions: loan maturity + higher rates/cap rates can force new equity.
  • Floating-rate complacency: rate volatility can destroy cash flow without a cap or hedge.
  • Assuming growth will save the model: underwriting should survive without perfect execution.
  • Not aligning debt term to business plan: short-term debt with long-term repositioning is risky.

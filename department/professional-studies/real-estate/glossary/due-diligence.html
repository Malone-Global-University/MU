Due Diligence

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Due Diligence

Definition

Due diligence is the formal investigation period after a deal is under contract where the buyer verifies what they underwrote: income, expenses, unit mix, leases, physical condition, legal/title, and market assumptions. The goal is simple: confirm the property matches the story—or renegotiate or walk away if it doesn’t.

What Due Diligence Covers

1) Financial and Operational

  • Rent roll verification: lease terms, actual rents, deposits, concessions, arrears.
  • T-12 and trailing statements: normalize income/expenses, find non-recurring items.
  • Utilities and recoveries: RUBS/bill-backs, submetering, contract rates, reimbursements.
  • Payroll and staffing: on-site roles, turnover, benefits, contractor spend.
  • Service contracts: termination rights, auto-renewals, above-market pricing.
  • Insurance and taxes: current premiums, loss runs, reassessment risk.

2) Physical Condition

  • Property Condition Assessment (PCA): roofs, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, exteriors, life safety.
  • Unit walks: confirm interior condition, finishes, down-units, turn costs, scope consistency.
  • Deferred maintenance: items “hidden” by occupancy or cosmetic work.
  • Environmental screening: Phase I (and Phase II if triggered), mold/moisture concerns.

3) Legal, Title, and Compliance

  • Title and survey: easements, encroachments, access, boundary issues.
  • Zoning and permitting: legal nonconforming uses, permit history, open permits.
  • Leases and tenant rights: addenda, renewal rights, rent caps, special terms.
  • Litigation and claims: resident disputes, fair housing issues, vendor claims.
  • HOA/CC&Rs (if applicable): restrictions, fees, pending assessments.

Why It Matters

Due diligence is where you protect capital. It reduces the chance you close on a property with undisclosed risks (bad debt, structural issues, un-terminable contracts, code problems) that permanently compress returns. It is also where you gain leverage: documented issues can support repricing, seller credits, or repairs before closing.

Investor Uses

  • Confirm underwriting: ensure the deal still meets your return and risk standards.
  • Build the 12–24 month plan: convert “business plan” into a sequenced scope and budget.
  • Set realistic reserves: right-size CapEx, repairs, and operating reserves.
  • Negotiate intelligently: use evidence-backed findings to adjust terms.

Example

You underwrite a 100-unit property assuming a T-12 economic vacancy of 6% and repairs/maintenance at $750/unit/year. During diligence you find:

  • Delinquency/collections imply true economic vacancy closer to 10%.
  • Deferred plumbing issues require a near-term spend of $250,000.
  • Two vendor contracts are above market and auto-renew unless terminated within a 30-day window.

The diligence outcome might be: adjust NOI assumptions, increase reserves, terminate/renegotiate contracts, and request a seller credit or price reduction to keep returns within your underwriting standard.

Common Pitfalls

  • Starting too late: scheduling inspections and document review late wastes the contingency window.
  • Trusting summary reports: always reconcile rent roll, leases, and bank deposits.
  • Missing “small” clauses: contract auto-renewals, termination fees, or assignment limits can be expensive.
  • Ignoring taxes/insurance resets: reassessments and premium jumps can crush year-1 DSCR and cash flow.
  • Not walking enough units: limited unit walks can hide systemic issues and understate turn costs.
  • Overlooking compliance: life-safety, accessibility, or permitting issues can delay renovations and leasing.

Tenant Damage

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Tenant Damage

Definition

Tenant damage is physical harm to a rental unit or property caused by a resident, occupant, guest, or pet that goes beyond normal wear and tear. It commonly results from negligence, misuse, abuse, or unauthorized alterations. Tenant damage often drives higher turn costs, longer downtime, and disputes over security deposit deductions.

Tenant Damage vs. Normal Wear and Tear

The distinction is about cause and severity. Wear and tear is expected from responsible use over time; tenant damage is avoidable deterioration caused by improper use or care.

Examples of normal wear and tear

  • Minor scuffs on walls, small nail holes, or lightly worn carpet in traffic areas.
  • Faded paint from sunlight, mild grout discoloration, or loose cabinet pulls from normal use.

Examples of tenant damage

  • Walls & doors: large holes, broken doors, missing hardware, damaged trim.
  • Floors: pet urine saturation, deep gouges, burns, ripped carpet, water damage from neglect.
  • Appliances & fixtures: broken stove knobs, cracked sinks/tubs, missing smoke detectors, damaged blinds.
  • Unauthorized changes: unapproved paint colors, altered flooring, mounted TVs without patching, DIY electrical work.
  • Biohazard / smoke: heavy smoke residue, excessive trash, pest conditions created by poor housekeeping.

Why It Matters

  • NOI impact: higher make-ready and repair costs reduce net operating income.
  • Vacancy loss: severe damage increases downtime, delaying move-ins and lease-up velocity.
  • Capex acceleration: repeated damage can shorten the useful life of flooring, paint, and fixtures.
  • Reputation risk: frequent disputes over charges can hurt reviews and leasing performance.

How Operators Manage Tenant Damage

  • Document unit condition: move-in photos, checklists, and timestamped inspection records.
  • Clear lease language: define resident responsibilities, prohibited alterations, and chargeback policies.
  • Routine inspections: catch issues early (leaks, unauthorized occupants, pets, hoarding, smoke).
  • Resident education: simple guidelines on maintenance requests, ventilation, disposal, and pet care.
  • Vendor standards: consistent pricing and scope for common repairs to reduce “made-up” charges.

Underwriting Implications

Tenant damage should show up in underwriting through repairs & maintenance, turn costs, bad debt (if chargebacks aren’t collected), and vacancy (if downtime extends). If the in-place operation is weak, expect elevated damage frequency until screening, enforcement, and culture improve.

Common Pitfalls

  • Charging for wear and tear: creates disputes and weakens credibility.
  • Weak documentation: makes legitimate damage hard to prove and collect.
  • Not pricing turns correctly: under-budgeting labor/materials leads to NOI surprises.
  • Slow maintenance response: small problems (leaks, clogs) become big damage events.
  • Ignoring pet risk: lack of pet policies, inspections, or odor remediation standards increases losses.

Receivership

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Receivership

Definition

Receivership is a court-supervised process in which a receiver is appointed to take control of a distressed property, typically following a loan default or during foreclosure. The receiver manages the property to preserve value for creditors.

When Receivership Occurs

  • Borrower defaults on loan obligations
  • Debt service payments are missed
  • Property operations deteriorate
  • Foreclosure proceedings begin

Role of the Receiver

The receiver acts as a neutral third party appointed by the court. Responsibilities often include:

  • Collecting rents and managing leases
  • Paying operating expenses
  • Maintaining the property
  • Providing financial reporting to the court and lender
  • Preparing the asset for sale or foreclosure

Impact on Borrowers

  • Loss of operational control
  • Restricted access to property cash flow
  • Increased legal and administrative costs
  • Potential acceleration of foreclosure process

Impact on Lenders

Receivership allows lenders to protect collateral value, stabilize operations, and prevent asset deterioration while legal proceedings are ongoing.

Example Scenario

A multifamily property falls below required DSCR thresholds and the borrower stops making loan payments. The lender petitions the court for appointment of a receiver.

The receiver takes control, collects rents, pays expenses, and operates the property until foreclosure is completed or a resolution is reached.

Investor Perspective

Receivership can create acquisition opportunities for investors seeking distressed assets. However, due diligence must include legal status review, outstanding liabilities, and court-imposed operational restrictions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating timeline complexity
  • Ignoring court oversight constraints
  • Overlooking unpaid liens or obligations
  • Failing to evaluate tenant stability during distress

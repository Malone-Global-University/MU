Subletting

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Subletting

Definition

Subletting occurs when a tenant leases all or part of their rented property to another individual (the subtenant) while remaining legally responsible to the landlord under the original lease agreement.

How Subletting Works

  • The original tenant signs a lease with the landlord.
  • The tenant then enters into a separate agreement with a subtenant.
  • The original tenant remains responsible for rent payments and lease compliance.

Types of Subletting

  • Full sublet: Entire unit rented to a subtenant.
  • Partial sublet: One room or portion of the unit rented.
  • Short-term sublet: Temporary occupancy (subject to local laws).

Legal Considerations

  • Lease restrictions: Many leases require landlord approval.
  • HOA or co-op rules: May limit or prohibit subletting.
  • Local regulations: Some jurisdictions restrict short-term rentals.
  • Liability exposure: Original tenant remains legally liable for damage or non-payment.

Investor Implications

  • Income flexibility: Allows tenants to maintain payments during relocation.
  • Risk transfer: Adds another occupant with potential screening gaps.
  • Control limitations: Landlords may have limited oversight of subtenants.
  • Liquidity factor: Subletting flexibility can increase unit marketability in co-ops or condos.

Example

A tenant signs a 12-month lease for $2,000 per month. After six months, the tenant relocates temporarily and sublets the unit for $2,100 per month. The tenant continues to pay the landlord $2,000 and retains legal responsibility under the original lease.

Common Pitfalls

  • Unauthorized subletting: Can trigger eviction or lease termination.
  • Insurance gaps: Subtenants may not be covered under original policies.
  • Screening risk: Poor vetting increases damage or default risk.
  • HOA penalties: Violations can result in fines or legal action.

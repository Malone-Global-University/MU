Limited Partner (LP)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Limited Partner (LP)

Definition

A Limited Partner (LP) is an investor who provides capital to a real estate deal and shares in the project’s profits, while generally having limited control over day-to-day decisions and limited liability relative to the sponsor/operator.

Where You See LPs

LPs commonly appear in syndications, private funds, and certain joint venture structures. In modern real estate deals, the “LP” concept often maps to passive equity investors in an LLC or limited partnership—even if the legal entity is an LLC rather than an LP.

What LPs Typically Do (and Don’t Do)

  • Contribute equity: fund a portion of the purchase and business plan.
  • Receive distributions: cash flow and sale/refinance proceeds per the agreement.
  • Vote on major decisions: sometimes (e.g., replace manager for cause, approve sale/refi above thresholds).
  • Do not run operations: leasing, renovations, financing, and management are usually handled by the sponsor/GP.

How LPs Get Paid

LP returns are defined by the deal’s governing documents (operating agreement or partnership agreement). Common economic terms include:

  • Preferred return (pref): a target return paid to LPs before sponsor profit splits (if the deal includes a pref).
  • Profit split / promote: after certain return thresholds, profits may be split between LPs and the sponsor (e.g., 70/30).
  • Catch-up: a mechanism that can accelerate sponsor distributions after the pref is met (deal-specific).

Why It Matters

Understanding the LP role helps you evaluate control, risk, and alignment. LPs are usually relying on the sponsor’s underwriting, execution, and reporting. The tradeoff is often: less work and less control in exchange for participating in the upside.

Example

A sponsor acquires a 120-unit apartment building and raises $6,000,000 of equity. Ten LP investors contribute $600,000 each. The sponsor manages renovations and operations. Quarterly, net cash flow is distributed first to pay an 8% preferred return to LPs (if available), then remaining profits are split per the agreement.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing “limited liability” with “no risk”: LPs can still lose capital if the deal underperforms.
  • Underestimating illiquidity: LP interests are typically hard to sell and may have transfer restrictions.
  • Not reading the waterfalls: small differences in pref, catch-up, and splits materially change outcomes.
  • Ignoring fees: acquisition, asset management, refinance, disposition, and construction/renovation fees reduce net returns.
  • Guaranty exposure: in some deals, certain “key principals” may sign guaranties—LPs generally do not, but verify.

Debt Service

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Debt Service

Definition

Debt service is the total required payment of principal and interest on a loan over a specific period, typically calculated on an annual basis for underwriting. It represents the cash obligation an investor must meet before any equity distributions.

How It’s Calculated

Debt service depends on:

  • Loan amount
  • Interest rate
  • Amortization period
  • Loan term
  • Payment structure (amortizing, interest-only, balloon, etc.)

General formula (amortizing loan)

  • Annual Debt Service = Total principal and interest payments over 12 months

Interest-Only Example

If a $5,000,000 loan carries a 6% interest-only rate:

  • Annual Debt Service = 5,000,000 × 0.06 = $300,000

Amortizing Example

A $5,000,000 loan at 6% interest amortized over 30 years would have a higher annual payment because principal is repaid along with interest.

Why It Matters

  • Cash Flow: Debt service is subtracted from NOI to determine pre-tax cash flow.
  • Risk Measurement: Higher debt service increases financial risk.
  • Leverage Impact: Loan structure directly affects equity returns.
  • Refinancing Decisions: Changes in rates alter future debt service obligations.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

Lenders evaluate loan safety using the Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR):

  • DSCR = NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service

A DSCR of 1.25 means the property generates 25% more income than required to cover debt payments. Most lenders require DSCR above 1.20–1.30.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting: Stress-test interest rates and rent declines.
  • Cash Flow Planning: Ensure adequate coverage before distributions.
  • Loan Comparison: Compare interest-only vs. amortizing structures.
  • Risk Management: Maintain conservative DSCR thresholds.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring rate resets: Floating-rate loans can sharply increase debt service.
  • Overleveraging: Excessive debt can wipe out cash flow.
  • Not modeling balloon risk: Large refinance balances may be due at maturity.
  • Underestimating stress scenarios: Vacancy or expense shocks reduce DSCR quickly.

Exit Cap Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Exit Cap Rate

Definition

The exit cap rate is the capitalization rate an investor assumes at the time of sale to estimate a property’s future resale price. It links future NOI to an implied sale value. Because value is highly sensitive to cap rates, the exit cap is one of the most important assumptions in any underwriting model.

How It’s Measured

Exit cap rate is typically applied to a property’s forward NOI at exit (often the next-12-month NOI after sale). A higher assumed exit cap produces a lower estimated sale price; a lower exit cap produces a higher estimated sale price.

Common formulas

  • Exit Value (Sale Price) = Exit NOI ÷ Exit Cap Rate
  • Exit Cap Rate = Exit NOI ÷ Sale Price

Why It Matters

Most of the equity value in a real estate deal is realized at exit. Small changes in exit cap assumptions can swing returns dramatically—especially for low-going-in cap deals, short hold periods, or highly levered investments. A conservative exit cap is a built-in margin of safety.

Investor Uses

  • Valuing the exit: convert projected NOI at sale into an estimated sale price.
  • Stress testing: test return sensitivity to cap rate expansion (higher required yields).
  • Setting underwriting standards: avoid overpaying by requiring conservative assumptions on exit.
  • Comparing strategies: evaluate whether returns come from NOI growth or “multiple expansion.”

How to Think About Choosing an Exit Cap

Exit caps are not forecasts—they’re underwriting assumptions. Many investors start with the current market cap rate for comparable stabilized assets and then add conservatism based on risk, time, and uncertainty.

  • Asset quality: newer, institutional product may trade at lower caps than older stock.
  • Stability: stabilized NOI typically commands better pricing than transitional NOI.
  • Market depth: liquid markets can support tighter caps than thin buyer pools.
  • Rate environment: higher base rates often put upward pressure on required yields.
  • Hold period: longer holds carry more uncertainty—conservatism matters more.

Example

Your model projects the property will produce $1,200,000 of NOI in the year after sale. You assume a 5.50% exit cap. The implied exit value is:

  • Exit Value = 1,200,000 ÷ 0.055 = $21,818,182

If instead you assume a 6.25% exit cap, value becomes:

  • Exit Value = 1,200,000 ÷ 0.0625 = $19,200,000

Same NOI—very different sale price. That spread often determines whether a deal “works.”

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming cap rate compression as a strategy: returns should be durable even if caps expand.
  • Using the wrong NOI: exit pricing is usually based on forward/stabilized NOI, not a noisy trailing month.
  • Ignoring CapEx needs: a buyer may discount price if major replacements are imminent.
  • Mixing asset classes: exit caps vary widely by product type (multifamily vs. office vs. industrial).
  • Overconfidence in year-5 projections: small NOI forecasting errors compound when capitalized.

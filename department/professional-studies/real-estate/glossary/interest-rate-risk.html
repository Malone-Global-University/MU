Interest Rate Risk

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Interest Rate Risk

Definition

Interest rate risk is the risk that changing interest rates will reduce the performance or value of a real estate investment. Rates can hurt returns by raising debt service, lowering refinance proceeds, and/or pushing cap rates higher (reducing asset value).

Where Interest Rate Risk Shows Up

1) Cash Flow Risk (Debt Service)

If your loan has a floating rate (or a fixed-rate loan that matures soon), rising rates can increase monthly payments. Higher debt service reduces cash flow, DSCR, and financial flexibility.

2) Refinance Risk (Maturity)

Many investors refinance to return capital or avoid selling. When rates rise, lenders may underwrite a higher debt constant, which can shrink loan proceeds. That creates refi gap risk—you may need to bring new equity to pay off the old loan.

3) Valuation Risk (Cap Rates)

Real estate is often priced using cap rates. When interest rates rise, required returns can rise too, which can push cap rates up. Higher cap rates mean lower values for the same NOI.

4) Transaction Risk (Liquidity & Buyer Demand)

Higher rates can reduce buyer purchasing power and slow transaction volume. Even if the property performs, selling may take longer or require a price concession.

Fixed vs. Floating Rate Exposure

  • Fixed-rate debt: protects near-term cash flow, but you still face interest rate risk at maturity or if you need to refinance before the term ends (and may face a prepayment penalty).
  • Floating-rate debt: exposes you immediately to rate changes. Investors often use caps to limit upside payment risk.

How to Model Interest Rate Risk

  • Rate shocks: test +100 bps, +200 bps, +300 bps on floating debt and refinance assumptions.
  • Exit cap expansion: model cap rates wider than your base case (e.g., +50 to +150 bps).
  • Refi proceeds constraint: size refinance to DSCR and lender rates, not just LTV.
  • Timing scenarios: assume slower lease-up or delayed sale in a tight credit market.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Use fixed-rate debt: reduce near-term payment volatility.
  • Buy an interest rate cap: limit maximum floating-rate exposure (common in bridge loans).
  • Lower leverage: more equity reduces refinance and DSCR stress.
  • Extend debt term: options/extensions reduce maturity cliffs (often with DSCR tests and fees).
  • Build NOI fast: stronger NOI buffers DSCR and offsets some cap-rate expansion.
  • Maintain liquidity: reserves and contingency capital help survive refi gaps or delays.

Example

An investor uses a floating-rate bridge loan at SOFR + 3.00%. If SOFR rises by 2.00%, the interest rate increases by 2.00% as well. On a $10,000,000 interest-only loan, annual interest expense increases by:

  • Additional Interest = 2.00% × $10,000,000 = $200,000/year

If NOI does not rise to offset that $200,000, DSCR and cash flow to equity drop immediately.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming refinancing is automatic: lenders underwrite DSCR at current rates, not last year’s.
  • Ignoring capex and carry: rising rates increase carry costs during renovations and lease-up.
  • Over-leveraging in a tight market: leverage magnifies rate moves and can force a sale at the wrong time.
  • Underestimating exit cap risk: a small cap rate move can outweigh a year of NOI growth.

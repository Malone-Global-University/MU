Change of Use

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Change of Use

Definition

A change of use occurs when a property is converted from one legally permitted function to another. For example, converting a retail storefront into apartments, an office building into a hotel, or a warehouse into creative office space may require a formal change-of-use approval.

Because building codes and zoning rules vary by property type, a change of use often requires permits, inspections, and regulatory approval before the new use can legally operate.

Common Examples

  • Office building converted into residential apartments
  • Industrial warehouse redeveloped into retail space
  • Hotel converted into student housing
  • Retail space converted into medical offices
  • Single-family home converted into a small office

Why It Matters for Investors

Change-of-use strategies are a common value-add investment approach. Investors may acquire an underperforming property and reposition it into a more profitable use based on market demand.

  • Higher rents: the new use may generate stronger revenue.
  • Market repositioning: adapt older buildings to modern demand.
  • Urban redevelopment: convert obsolete assets into productive uses.
  • Highest and best use: align the property with its most valuable function.

Regulatory Considerations

Change-of-use projects often trigger additional regulatory review because different property types must meet different building code requirements.

  • Zoning compliance: confirm the new use is permitted.
  • Building code upgrades: fire safety, accessibility, and occupancy standards.
  • Parking requirements: different uses require different parking ratios.
  • Permitting process: local planning departments must approve the new use.
  • Occupancy classification changes: structural or safety modifications may be required.

Example

An investor purchases an aging downtown office building that struggles to attract tenants. Local zoning allows residential use, so the investor redevelops the property into apartments. After renovation and regulatory approval, the building generates significantly higher rents than it did as office space.

Common Pitfalls

  • Zoning restrictions: the desired use may not be allowed without rezoning.
  • Unexpected renovation costs: code compliance can require major upgrades.
  • Parking constraints: new uses may require more parking spaces.
  • Timeline risk: approvals and permits can delay projects.
  • Structural limitations: some buildings cannot easily accommodate a new use.

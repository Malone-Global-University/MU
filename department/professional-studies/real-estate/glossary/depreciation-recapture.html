Depreciation Recapture

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Depreciation Recapture

Definition

Depreciation recapture is a tax concept that can apply when you sell depreciable real estate. Over the hold period, you may claim depreciation deductions that reduce taxable income. Those deductions also reduce your adjusted basis. When you sell, the lower basis typically increases your gain, and a portion of that gain may be treated as recapture—meaning it can be taxed differently than the remaining long-term capital gain.

Why It Happens

Depreciation is essentially a tax accounting method that lets you expense part of the property’s cost over time. Because the IRS views depreciation as a benefit you already received, some of it can be “paid back” through recapture when you exit.

How It’s Measured (Conceptually)

While the exact rules are fact-specific, the intuition is simple:

  • Depreciation taken reduces basis.
  • Lower basis increases gain on sale.
  • Part of that gain may be categorized as depreciation recapture.

High-level framework

  • Total gain = Net sale proceeds − Adjusted basis
  • Recapture portion ≈ the depreciation deductions previously taken (subject to rules/limits)
  • Remaining gain = Total gain − Recapture portion

Why It Matters to Investors

  • After-tax proceeds: recapture can reduce net cash you keep at sale.
  • Underwriting: tax assumptions can materially change levered returns and IRR.
  • Hold vs. sell decisions: selling earlier vs. later may change the depreciation vs. appreciation mix.
  • Exit planning: strategies like a 1031 exchange can defer recognized gains in many scenarios.

Example

You buy a rental property for $1,000,000 and over the hold period you claim $220,000 in depreciation. You also complete $150,000 in capital improvements. Your adjusted basis is:

  • Adjusted basis = 1,000,000 + 150,000 − 220,000 = $930,000

You sell for $1,250,000 and pay $75,000 in selling costs:

  • Net sale proceeds = 1,250,000 − 75,000 = $1,175,000
  • Total gain = 1,175,000 − 930,000 = $245,000

Conceptually, up to the amount of depreciation taken ($220,000) may be treated as recapture (subject to rules), with the remaining gain potentially treated as capital gain.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring recapture in underwriting: modeling only “capital gains tax” can understate exit taxes.
  • Poor basis tracking: missing CapEx documentation can reduce basis and increase taxable gain.
  • Not understanding “allowed or allowable” depreciation: basis may be reduced even if depreciation wasn’t claimed.
  • Confusing improvements with repairs: repairs are often expensed; improvements typically add to basis.

Common Investor Approaches (High Level)

  • 1031 exchange: defer recognized gain (and related tax impacts) by reinvesting into like-kind property.
  • Longer hold / refinance: access equity via refinance rather than sale (tax outcomes vary).
  • Estate planning: depending on circumstances, holding until death can change basis treatment (consult professionals).

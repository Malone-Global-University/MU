Entitlements

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Entitlements

Definition

Entitlements are the legal approvals and rights required to build, redevelop, or change the use of a property. They determine what can be constructed (use, density, height, setbacks, parking, design standards) and what conditions must be met (fees, infrastructure improvements, environmental mitigation, affordability requirements).

Where Entitlements Fit in the Development Process

Entitlements typically sit between a project concept and “shovel-ready” construction. A site can look valuable on paper, but without the right approvals the plan may be impossible—or delayed long enough to break the deal.

Common sequence (varies by jurisdiction)

  • Due diligence & feasibility: zoning review, utility capacity, site constraints, concept design.
  • Land-use entitlement: approvals that establish the right to build a certain project.
  • Permitting: construction documents + building permits that allow work to begin.
  • Inspections & certificate of occupancy: final approvals to occupy and operate.

Common Types of Entitlements

  • Zoning confirmation: verifying the proposed use is permitted “by right” under current zoning.
  • Rezoning: changing the zoning designation to allow a different use or higher intensity.
  • Conditional Use Permit (CUP) / Special Use Permit: approval for a use allowed only with review.
  • Variance: relief from specific zoning standards (setbacks, height, parking) due to hardship conditions.
  • Site plan approval / Design review: approval of layout, architecture, landscaping, and circulation.
  • Subdivision / Platting: creating lots, condos, or legal parcel configurations.
  • Environmental review: assessments and mitigation for impacts (traffic, stormwater, habitat, noise).
  • Development agreement: negotiated terms with the city/county (phasing, fees, infrastructure, benefits).

Entitled vs. By-Right vs. Shovel-Ready

  • By-right: the project conforms to zoning and can proceed without discretionary approvals (still needs permits).
  • Entitled: land-use approvals are secured for a defined plan (often with conditions).
  • Shovel-ready: entitlement is done and building permits are issued (or near issuance), enabling construction start.

Why It Matters

Entitlements drive three major underwriting variables: time, cost, and certainty. Delays increase carry costs (interest, taxes, insurance), market risk (rent and cap-rate moves), and execution risk (redesigns, conditions, opposition). A fully entitled site can be worth meaningfully more because it removes major uncertainty.

Investor Uses

  • Risk pricing: investors demand higher returns for unentitled or discretionary entitlement risk.
  • Timeline modeling: entitlement duration impacts interest carry, fee timing, and project IRR.
  • Exit optionality: entitled land can be sold to builders/developers at a premium versus raw land.
  • Deal structuring: use option contracts, phased closings, or entitlement milestones to reduce downside.

Example

A developer targets a 120-unit multifamily project on a site currently zoned for 60 units “by right.” They pursue a rezoning and design review to reach 120 units. If approvals take 12 months instead of the underwritten 6 months, the project carries the land for an extra 6 months—raising total costs and exposing the deal to market shifts.

  • Carry cost impact: extra months can add interest, property taxes, insurance, and holding costs.
  • Scope impact: conditions of approval may require added parking, setbacks, or public improvements.
  • Value impact: once entitled, the site’s value may increase due to reduced uncertainty.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “by right” means easy: permits, utilities, and design standards can still delay timelines.
  • Underestimating discretionary risk: rezones/CUPs are exposed to politics, neighbors, and hearings.
  • Ignoring conditions: approvals often include fees and infrastructure obligations that change the budget.
  • Title and access issues: easements, setbacks, and ROW dedications can reduce buildable area.
  • Environmental surprises: wetlands, soil, contamination, or stormwater requirements can derail designs.

Best Practices

  • Confirm zoning early: written verification from planning staff when possible; document assumptions.
  • Build a realistic schedule: include public hearing cycles, resubmittals, and agency review windows.
  • Budget soft costs: architects, engineers, traffic studies, legal, permitting fees, and contingencies.
  • Stakeholder strategy: proactive community outreach can reduce opposition and redesign risk.
  • Structure smart: options, milestone-based closings, or entitlement contingencies to protect capital.

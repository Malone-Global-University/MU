Highest & Best Use (HBU)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Highest & Best Use

Definition

Highest & Best Use (HBU) is the reasonably probable use of a property that is: legally permissible, physically possible, financially feasible, and results in the highest value (often called maximum productivity). HBU is a core concept in appraisal and investment strategy because it frames what the property “should” be used for to maximize value—given real-world constraints.

The Four Tests of HBU

1) Legally Permissible

The use must be allowed by law and regulation: zoning, comprehensive plans, environmental constraints, easements, covenants, historic rules, and permitting requirements.

  • Examples: permitted uses, height/density limits, parking minimums, setbacks.
  • Reality check: “Can it be entitled?” matters—not just what you wish the zoning was.

2) Physically Possible

The site and improvements must support the use based on size, shape, topography, access, utilities, soil conditions, and engineering constraints.

  • Examples: small parcels may not fit required parking; steep slopes can crush feasibility.
  • Reality check: utility capacity, stormwater, and access can be the true limiting factors.

3) Financially Feasible

The use must generate returns that justify the cost and risk. If the expected revenue cannot cover operating costs, capital costs, financing, and required return, the use fails this test.

  • Examples: condo project that can’t support land + construction costs at market pricing.
  • Reality check: feasibility is market-specific and changes with rates, rents, and cap rates.

4) Maximally Productive (Highest Value)

Among the feasible options, the HBU is the one that produces the highest value (or highest land value), considering risk and probability.

  • Examples: multifamily may outbid retail for land because it supports higher residual value.
  • Reality check: the “highest” use isn’t always the “best” for a specific investor’s mandate or risk tolerance.

HBU for Vacant Land vs. Improved Property

  • Vacant land: HBU is about what should be built (use, density, and timing).
  • Improved property: HBU considers whether the existing improvements should be kept, renovated, converted, or demolished.

For improved assets, HBU can change when a building becomes functionally obsolete or when land values rise enough that redevelopment “wins” even if the building still produces cash flow.

Why It Matters

HBU is the bridge between market reality and valuation. It influences land value, redevelopment optionality, exit strategy, and whether you’re buying an income property or an embedded land play. Many pricing mistakes happen when investors underwrite the property as one thing while the market values it as another.

Investor Uses

  • Acquisition strategy: identify mispriced assets where current use is not the true HBU.
  • Redevelopment analysis: compare hold/operate vs. renovate vs. convert vs. scrape-and-build.
  • Land valuation: estimate residual land value based on the most productive feasible development.
  • Exit planning: sell to the “highest bidder type” (operator vs. developer) aligned with HBU.
  • Risk framing: separate entitlement risk and market risk from operating risk.

Example

A 1-acre site contains a 40-year-old retail strip with stable (but low-growth) NOI. The zoning allows mixed-use up to 6 stories. An investor evaluates two paths:

  • As-is retail: stable cash flow, lower risk, but limited upside and functional obsolescence.
  • Mixed-use redevelopment: higher potential value, but entitlement risk, time, and capital intensity.

If redevelopment is legally permissible, physically possible, financially feasible, and produces higher land value, the site’s HBU may be redevelopment—even if the current retail use is “fine” today.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming entitlements are guaranteed: discretionary approvals can fail or become too slow/expensive.
  • Ignoring probability: “reasonably probable” matters; fantasy scenarios are not HBU.
  • Confusing investor goals with HBU: HBU is market-based; your mandate may favor a different choice.
  • Overlooking physical constraints: access, utilities, stormwater, and soil can kill a concept.
  • Using stale comps: feasibility can flip quickly with interest rates, construction costs, or rent shifts.

Best Practices

  • Start with zoning + constraints: confirm permitted uses, density, setbacks, parking, overlays, and easements.
  • Do quick residual land math: compare land value implied by different uses (after costs and profit).
  • Test multiple scenarios: base, downside, and delay cases for entitlement and lease-up timelines.
  • Match the buyer universe: know who will value the asset most at exit (operator vs. developer).
  • Document assumptions: HBU conclusions are only as strong as the inputs and probability judgments.

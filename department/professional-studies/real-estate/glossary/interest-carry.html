Interest Carry

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Interest Carry

Definition

Interest carry refers to the ongoing interest expense incurred on borrowed funds during development, renovation, or lease-up, before a property generates sufficient income to cover its debt service. It is a major component of project financing costs.

When Interest Carry Occurs

  • Ground-up construction projects
  • Major value-add renovations
  • Extended lease-up periods
  • Bridge loan situations awaiting refinance

How It Works

During construction, loan proceeds are typically drawn in phases. Interest accrues on the outstanding balance and is either:

  • Paid monthly from equity or operating reserves
  • Capitalized into the loan balance (increasing total project cost)

Example

A developer borrows $15 million at a 7% interest rate with a 24-month construction timeline.

  • Annual interest: $15,000,000 × 7% = $1,050,000
  • Two-year interest carry ≈ $2,100,000 (excluding phased draw adjustments)

If the project is delayed by six months, interest carry increases materially, reducing overall returns.

Why It Matters

  • Return sensitivity: Directly reduces project margin.
  • Timeline risk: Delays increase financing expense.
  • Rate volatility: Floating-rate debt increases exposure.
  • Feasibility impact: Higher rates may eliminate profit cushion.

Risk Factors

  • Construction delays
  • Permit or entitlement slowdowns
  • Slower-than-expected lease-up
  • Rising interest rate environment

Investor Implications

  • Capital reserves: Adequate liquidity is critical.
  • Hedging strategy: Rate caps or swaps may reduce volatility.
  • Execution discipline: Tight project management limits exposure.
  • Underwriting conservatism: Sensitivity analysis protects against overruns.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring rate increases on floating loans
  • Underestimating lease-up timelines
  • Failing to include interest carry in total development cost
  • Weak contingency planning

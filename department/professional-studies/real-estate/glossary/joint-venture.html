Joint Venture (JV)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Joint Venture (JV)

Definition

A joint venture (JV) is a partnership between two or more parties formed to acquire, develop, or operate a real estate asset. Each party contributes some combination of capital, expertise, relationships, or execution capacity, and shares in the project’s profits, risks, and governance under a negotiated agreement.

Why JVs Exist

JVs allow partners to combine strengths. One party may have the deal and the operating platform, while the other has low-cost capital or a balance sheet that improves financing. The result can be a project that neither party could efficiently do alone.

Common JV Roles

  • Operating Partner / Sponsor: sources the deal, runs due diligence, executes the business plan, manages the asset.
  • Capital Partner: provides most of the equity (and sometimes guarantees or credit support).
  • Development Partner: for ground-up, manages entitlements, design, construction, and delivery.
  • Co-GP partners: multiple operators share responsibilities (leasing, construction, asset management, capital markets).

How JV Economics Are Structured

JV profit sharing is typically defined by an operating agreement and often resembles a waterfall. The split depends on who is contributing what and how control is allocated.

Common economic elements

  • Equity contribution: each party’s percentage of invested equity (e.g., 90/10, 80/20).
  • Preferred return: capital partner often receives a preferred return before promotes apply.
  • Promote / carried interest: the operator may earn a larger share after hurdles are achieved.
  • Fees: acquisition, asset management, construction management, development, disposition (deal-specific).
  • Capital event sharing: refinance proceeds and sale proceeds are distributed per the waterfall.

Governance and Control

Governance is as important as the split. JVs usually define which decisions the operating partner can make independently and which require partner approval.

Typical “major decision” items

  • Annual budgets and material budget variances
  • Refinancing, new debt, or loan modifications
  • Capital expenditures above thresholds
  • Material leases (large tenants, long terms, concessions beyond limits)
  • Sale timing, listing strategy, and minimum sale price
  • Related-party contracts and conflicts

Capital Calls and Dilution

Many JV agreements include a mechanism for additional equity contributions if the deal needs more cash (unexpected capex, operating shortfalls, lender reserves, construction overruns). If a party fails to fund, the agreement may impose dilution, penalty interest, or a forced sale of their interest.

Example

An operator finds a value-add apartment deal but wants to scale quickly. A capital partner contributes 90% of the equity, and the operator contributes 10% plus day-to-day execution.

  • Equity: 90/10 capital split
  • Pref: 8% preferred return to the capital partner
  • Promote: after pref and return of capital, remaining profits split 70/30 (capital/operator)
  • Control: operator runs operations; capital partner approves budgets, refis, and sale

This structure rewards the operator for performance while giving the capital partner protections around major decisions and downside risk.

Pros and Cons

Benefits

  • Access to scale: operators can do larger deals with institutional capital.
  • Specialization: partners focus on what they do best (capital markets vs. operations).
  • Risk sharing: equity and execution risk spread across parties.
  • Financing advantage: stronger balance sheets can improve loan terms.

Tradeoffs

  • Control complexity: approvals can slow decisions.
  • Alignment risk: different time horizons or risk tolerance can create conflict.
  • Fee scrutiny: partners may challenge fees or related-party arrangements.
  • Exit disagreements: disputes about sale timing and pricing are common.

Common Pitfalls

  • Unclear decision rights: vague governance leads to conflict when the deal hits stress.
  • Mispriced promote: promoting too early (low hurdles) can misalign incentives.
  • Underwriting drift: partners agree on a plan, then assumptions creep post-close.
  • Capital call surprises: insufficient reserves create emergency funding needs.
  • No deadlock mechanism: if partners disagree, deals can freeze without a defined resolution process.

Assumable Loan

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Assumable Loan

Definition

An assumable loan is a mortgage that allows the buyer to take over the seller’s existing loan rather than originating a new one. In an assumption, the buyer “steps into” the loan’s remaining balance, interest rate, and repayment terms (subject to lender and/or program rules).

How It Works

The buyer applies to assume the loan and the lender (or loan servicer) evaluates credit, income, and the property, similar to underwriting a new loan. If approved, the buyer becomes responsible for payments going forward. Depending on the loan and program, the seller may be released from liability after assumption.

Why It Matters

Assumable loans can be extremely valuable when the existing interest rate is below current market rates. Keeping a lower rate can improve cash flow, raise DSCR, and allow a buyer to pay more (or maintain returns) compared to a deal financed at today’s rates.

Typical Investor Deal Structure

The buyer assumes the existing loan balance, then must cover the remaining purchase price (the “equity gap”) with cash or additional financing.

Common ways to cover the equity gap

  • Cash equity: buyer brings additional cash to closing.
  • Seller carry / seller financing: a second note from seller to buyer (if allowed by the first loan).
  • Second mortgage: subordinate financing (must not violate lender rules).
  • Preferred equity: structured capital that sits above common equity but below senior debt.

Key Terms to Watch

  • Assumption clause: defines whether and how a loan may be assumed.
  • Due-on-sale clause: allows lender to demand payoff on transfer unless an exception applies.
  • Release of liability: whether the seller is fully released after assumption.
  • Assumption fees: lender/servicer charges for processing and approvals.
  • Rate, term, and amortization: remaining term, amortization schedule, and whether there’s a balloon.
  • Escrows & reserves: property tax/insurance escrows, replacement reserves, and required deposits.

Example

A buyer purchases a small multifamily property for $1,000,000. The seller has an assumable loan with a remaining balance of $650,000 at a low fixed rate. The buyer assumes the $650,000 loan and must cover the remaining $350,000 with cash or supplemental financing. If current market rates are higher, the assumed loan can materially improve monthly debt service and overall deal returns.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “assumable” means automatic: most assumptions still require lender approval and timelines can be slow.
  • Underestimating closing friction: assumption fees, legal docs, escrows, and reserve requirements add up.
  • Equity gap risk: if the existing balance is low, the buyer may need substantial cash to close.
  • Seller liability: if the seller is not released, the seller may remain on the hook if the buyer defaults.
  • Transfer restrictions: some loans restrict entity changes, partner transfers, or secondary financing.

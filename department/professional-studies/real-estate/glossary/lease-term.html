Lease Term

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Lease Term

Definition

The lease term is the length of time a tenant is contractually entitled (and obligated) to occupy a space under a lease—typically measured from the commencement date through the expiration date. Lease term is one of the most important drivers of income durability and rollover risk.

Key Dates to Know

  • Lease execution date: when the lease is signed.
  • Commencement date: when rent obligations typically begin (can differ from execution).
  • Possession / delivery date: when the tenant can access the space (may start earlier).
  • Expiration date: when the initial term ends.
  • Renewal option windows: time periods when the tenant can extend the lease.

Why It Matters

Lease term affects how stable and predictable your cash flow is. Longer terms can reduce near-term rollover risk but may require larger concessions like TI allowance and free rent. Shorter terms reduce commitment duration but increase the frequency of re-leasing costs such as leasing commissions.

Typical Lease Terms by Property Type

  • Office: often 3–10+ years (longer for larger tenants)
  • Retail: often 5–10+ years (anchors may be longer)
  • Industrial: often 3–10 years (varies by market and tenant)
  • Multifamily: typically 6–15 months (short term, high turnover frequency)

Lease Term and Underwriting

Investors model lease term to forecast:

  • Rollover exposure: which leases expire in which years and the risk of vacancy or downtime.
  • Re-leasing costs: expected TI/LC and concessions at renewal or re-tenanting.
  • Rent growth: annual escalations and mark-to-market potential at expiration.
  • Financing risk: lenders may prefer longer weighted-average lease term for stability.

Lease Term vs. Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT)

A single lease term describes one tenant. For multi-tenant properties, investors often use WALT (Weighted Average Lease Term) to summarize the average remaining term, weighted by rent or area.

Simple concept

  • WALT is higher when large tenants have longer remaining terms, and lower when major tenants are close to expiration.

Example

A property has a major lease expiring in 12 months and several smaller leases expiring in 5+ years. Even if the “average” looks okay, the near-term expiration of the largest tenant can create significant vacancy risk and re-leasing costs.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing “signed” with “commenced”: execution date is not the same as rent commencement.
  • Ignoring options: tenant renewal options can change the likely lease length—but aren’t guaranteed.
  • Forgetting downtime: lease expiration can be followed by vacancy and buildout time.
  • Not modeling re-leasing costs: shorter terms usually mean more frequent TI/LC and concessions.
  • Overvaluing long terms: long lease terms can lock in below-market rents and reduce upside.

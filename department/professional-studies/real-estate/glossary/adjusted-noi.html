Adjusted NOI

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Adjusted NOI

Definition

Adjusted NOI (Adjusted Net Operating Income) is Net Operating Income (NOI) that has been modified to remove non-recurring items, normalize irregular expenses, or reflect stabilized operations.

It is used in underwriting to show what the property’s income should look like under normalized or stabilized conditions.

Why It Matters

  • Creates apples-to-apples comparisons between properties.
  • Removes distortions from one-time events.
  • Supports valuation using the cap rate method.
  • Helps lenders assess sustainable debt service capacity.

Common Adjustments

Typical revenue and expense adjustments include:

Revenue Adjustments

  • Removing one-time fees or insurance proceeds.
  • Normalizing concessions in lease-up periods.
  • Adjusting below-market rents to market rates (pro forma).

Expense Adjustments

  • Removing non-recurring repair costs.
  • Normalizing property management fees.
  • Replacing abnormally low maintenance spending with market benchmarks.
  • Stabilizing payroll or contract services.

Example

Assume reported NOI from the T-12 is $800,000.

  • +$60,000 one-time roof repair (added back)
  • −$25,000 insurance claim income (removed)
  • +$40,000 market rent adjustments

Adjusted NOI = $875,000

Adjusted NOI vs. In-Place NOI

  • In-Place NOI: Current trailing performance.
  • Adjusted NOI: Normalized or stabilized projection.

In-place NOI reflects what is. Adjusted NOI reflects what the property can sustainably produce under normalized conditions.

Adjusted NOI and Valuation

Investors often apply a cap rate to Adjusted NOI to estimate value:

  • Value ≈ Adjusted NOI ÷ Cap Rate

Because valuation is highly sensitive to NOI, aggressive adjustments can materially inflate property value.

Risks and Pitfalls

  • Overstating rent growth assumptions.
  • Underestimating stabilized expenses.
  • Adding back recurring costs incorrectly.
  • Using pro forma numbers without operational proof.

Investor Checklist

  • Separate factual historical adjustments from speculative projections.
  • Verify expense benchmarks against comparable assets.
  • Stress-test conservative scenarios.
  • Align assumptions with your required return standards.

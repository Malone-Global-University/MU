Promote

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Promote

Definition

A promote is the sponsor’s performance-based share of investment profits—often called carried interest. It is paid to the GP/sponsor only after the LP investors receive an agreed return (or “hurdle”) and sometimes a return of capital. The promote is designed to align incentives: the sponsor earns more only when the deal performs well.

Where the Promote Shows Up

Promotes are most common in private equity-style real estate (value-add and development), where the sponsor is actively creating value. The promote is expressed and paid through a waterfall—a set of rules that determines how cash flow and sale proceeds are split.

How It’s Structured

Most waterfalls have “tiers” that increase the sponsor’s share as performance improves. A simple structure looks like:

  • Return of capital: distributions first go to investors until invested capital is returned (sometimes net of fees).
  • Preferred return (pref): investors receive a minimum return (e.g., 8% IRR or an annual pref).
  • Catch-up (optional): sponsor receives a larger share until the agreed promote split is reached.
  • Promote tiers: profits above hurdle(s) split between LP and GP at escalating percentages (e.g., 80/20 then 70/30).

Common promote language

  • “80/20 over an 8% pref” means investors receive an 8% preferred return, then remaining profits split 80% to LP / 20% to GP.
  • “Tiered promote” means the GP share increases after higher hurdles are met.
  • “European waterfall” typically pays promote only after investors have received return of capital plus the preferred return (deal-level).
  • “American waterfall” may pay promote on a deal-by-deal basis earlier, subject to later reconciliation/clawback.

Why It Matters

The promote changes the split of upside, which affects LP net IRR, equity multiple, and how much “value creation” is required to justify a business plan. Underwriting a deal without correctly modeling the promote can make an investment appear meaningfully better than it is.

Investor Uses

  • Compare deals apples-to-apples: two deals with the same gross returns can have different net returns due to promote tiers.
  • Test alignment: evaluate whether the sponsor’s promote is tied to true performance (hurdles) vs. paid too early.
  • Stress-test outcomes: see how downside scenarios behave when hurdles aren’t met (promote often goes to zero).
  • Negotiate terms: adjust hurdles, tier splits, and catch-up mechanics to match risk and complexity.

Example

Suppose a deal has a simple waterfall: return of capital, then an 8% preferred return, then 80/20 split (LP/GP) of remaining profits.

  • If the deal underperforms and only returns capital + a portion of the pref, the promote may be $0.
  • If the deal exceeds the 8% hurdle, the GP receives 20% of profits above the hurdle.

This structure protects investors in mediocre outcomes and rewards the sponsor mainly in strong outcomes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing “promote” with “fees”: the promote is performance-based; acquisition/asset management fees are typically paid regardless of performance.
  • Missing catch-up mechanics: catch-ups can accelerate GP economics and materially change cash flow timing.
  • Ignoring timing: the same total promote can have very different value depending on when it’s paid (early vs. at sale).
  • Assuming a single hurdle: many institutional deals use multiple tiers; model them explicitly.
  • Not checking clawback: deal-by-deal promotes can require clawback provisions to protect LPs if later deals underperform.

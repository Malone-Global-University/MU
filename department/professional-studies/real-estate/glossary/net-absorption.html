Net Absorption

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Net Absorption

Definition

Net absorption measures the net change in occupied space or units in a market over a specific time period. It reflects how many units (multifamily) or how much square footage (office/industrial/retail) were added to occupancy after accounting for move-outs.

How It’s Calculated

Net absorption focuses on what actually stayed occupied, not just what was signed or toured. It is typically reported by quarter or year for a submarket, metro, or asset class.

Common formulas

  • Net Absorption (units or SF) = Move-ins (or space newly occupied) − Move-outs (or space vacated)
  • Net Absorption (from occupancy) = (Ending occupied units or SF) − (Beginning occupied units or SF)

Net vs. Gross Absorption

Investors often confuse leasing activity with market tightening. Gross absorption counts total leasing (all move-ins / all newly leased space), while net absorption subtracts the space that became vacant in the same period.

  • Gross absorption: “How much got leased?”
  • Net absorption: “How much occupancy actually increased?”

Why It Matters

Net absorption is a practical indicator of demand strength relative to supply and churn. Positive net absorption suggests the market is tightening (more occupants than vacancies), which can support rent growth and fewer concessions. Negative net absorption suggests the market is loosening (more vacancy forming), which can pressure rents and extend lease-up timelines.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting rent growth: positive net absorption can justify stronger rent assumptions (within reason).
  • Lease-up risk: weak or negative net absorption can imply longer stabilization and higher concessions.
  • Supply analysis: compare net absorption to new deliveries to gauge whether supply is being “cleared.”
  • Submarket selection: identify pockets of persistent demand vs. markets with chronic vacancy formation.

Example

A 1,000-unit submarket starts the quarter with 930 occupied units and ends with 945 occupied units. During the quarter, 80 units were leased (move-ins), but 65 units moved out.

  • Net Absorption = 80 − 65 = +15 units
  • Cross-check = 945 − 930 = +15 units

Even though leasing activity was high, the market only tightened by 15 units after churn.

How to Interpret the Number

  • Positive net absorption: demand exceeds vacancy formation; market is tightening.
  • Near-zero net absorption: leasing is mostly replacing move-outs; stable but not tightening.
  • Negative net absorption: vacancy is rising; pricing power typically weakens.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mixing apples and oranges: compare like-for-like unit types (or comparable space class/quality).
  • Ignoring new supply: absorption can be positive while vacancy stays flat if deliveries keep arriving.
  • Short time windows: one quarter can be noisy—look at trailing 4 quarters for trend.
  • Concessions blind spot: positive absorption achieved via heavy concessions may not translate to rent growth.

Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)

Definition

A Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA) is a geographic region used for statistical reporting that includes a core urban area (a city or urbanized area) plus surrounding counties that have strong economic and social connections to the core—most commonly reflected by commuting patterns.

In real estate, “MSA” is shorthand for a standardized market boundary that lets investors compare population, jobs, income, housing supply, and rent trends across regions using consistent definitions.

How an MSA Works (Conceptually)

MSAs are built around the idea that housing and labor markets extend beyond city limits. A metro region often includes multiple cities and suburbs that function as one interconnected economy.

What MSAs usually include

  • One or more central counties containing the primary urban core
  • Outlying counties with significant commuting ties to the core
  • Multiple submarkets with different rent levels, demographics, and supply pipelines

Why It Matters for Investors

Many widely used datasets (economic indicators, housing stats, and commercial real estate reports) are published at the MSA level. Using MSAs allows apples-to-apples comparisons between “metros” and supports top-down screening before you go deeper into submarkets.

Investor Uses

  • Market selection: compare metros by job growth, population growth, income, and rent performance.
  • Demand underwriting: tie rent growth assumptions to MSA-level household and employment trends.
  • Supply risk review: evaluate building permits, construction starts, and deliveries at the metro scale.
  • Benchmarking: compare your property’s performance to metro vacancy, absorption, and rent indexes.
  • Portfolio diversification: avoid concentration by spreading exposure across multiple MSAs.

MSA vs. Submarket

An MSA is a high-level geography. A submarket is a local slice of the metro where rents, tenant demand, and supply often behave very differently. Underwriting a deal requires submarket-level insight even if you start with MSA-level trends.

Example

Suppose you’re evaluating two multifamily deals in the same MSA: one near a high-income job center and another in a tertiary suburb. The MSA-level data might show “stable vacancy,” but:

  • The job-center submarket could have tight vacancy and strong rent growth.
  • The tertiary suburb could be facing new supply deliveries and higher concessions.

The MSA helps you understand the metro backdrop, but the submarket determines the deal reality.

Common Pitfalls

  • Over-relying on averages: MSA averages can hide big differences across neighborhoods.
  • Boundary changes over time: some MSAs expand or change county composition, affecting trend comparisons.
  • Wrong “market” assumption: a property can be inside the same MSA but compete in a very different rent pool.
  • Mixing data sources: different providers may label “metro” differently—verify definitions.

Ancillary Income

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Ancillary Income

Definition

Ancillary income (often shown as other income) is revenue a property generates that is separate from base rent. In both multifamily and commercial real estate, ancillary income can meaningfully support NOI—but it is typically more variable than rent and must be underwritten based on collectability, durability, and resident/tenant friction.

Common Sources

Multifamily (apartments)

  • Utility bill-backs: water/sewer/trash reimbursements or submetering programs. (Utility Bill-Back)
  • Parking: reserved spots, covered parking, garages.
  • Pet income: pet rent, pet deposits, pet fees.
  • Storage: lockers, detached storage units.
  • Application/admin fees: application fees, move-in fees, lease admin fees (market and policy dependent).
  • Renter’s insurance programs: tenant liability coverage or master policy billing (where permitted).
  • Laundry & vending: laundry revenue share, vending commissions.
  • Services: valet trash, package lockers, Wi-Fi/tech packages, furnished rentals, short-term premiums (where allowed).

Commercial

  • Expense reimbursements: CAM, taxes, insurance recoveries (lease-structured income). (Expense Reimbursements)
  • Parking income: paid parking, validation programs, reserved spaces.
  • Signage: monument signage, building signage, billboard income.
  • Telecom/rooftop: cell towers, antenna leases, fiber agreements.
  • Storage/service fees: shared services or facilities charges (property type dependent).

Why It Matters

Ancillary income can be a major lever for value creation because it often requires less capex than rent growth and can be implemented through operational changes. However, the best ancillary income is repeatable, low-friction, and clearly communicated—not dependent on punitive fees or aggressive collections.

How to Underwrite Ancillary Income

  • Use trailing actuals: start with the last 12–24 months by line item, per unit/per month (PUPM).
  • Separate “earned” vs. “collected”: a high charge doesn’t help if collection is weak.
  • Model adoption: parking, storage, and pet rent depend on participation rates.
  • Net it down: subtract vendor fees, credit card processing, admin, and maintenance costs.
  • Stress-test churn: overly aggressive fees can increase vacancy/concessions and reduce net NOI.
  • Stay compliant: verify what can be charged in your jurisdiction and what must be disclosed in the lease.

Example

A 200-unit property currently generates $35/unit/month in ancillary income: $18 utility bill-backs, $10 parking, $5 pet rent/fees, $2 storage. The new operator plans to add reserved parking and improve bill-back compliance, targeting $50/unit/month.

  • Current annual ancillary income = 200 × $35 × 12 = $84,000
  • Target annual ancillary income = 200 × $50 × 12 = $120,000
  • Increase = $36,000/year (before any added costs or vacancy effects)

If most of the increase is collected and low-cost to administer, it can translate to real NOI and valuation impact.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overreliance on fees: late fees and one-time charges are not a durable business model.
  • Ignoring elasticity: pushing too hard can increase vacancy, concessions, or delinquency.
  • Not netting expenses: some “income” comes with vendor costs that reduce NOI.
  • Poor communication: residents/tenants will resist surprise charges—roll out changes transparently.
  • Policy and legal risk: fees and billing practices can be restricted; verify local rules and lease language.

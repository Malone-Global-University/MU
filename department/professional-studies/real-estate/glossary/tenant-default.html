Tenant Default

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Tenant Default

Definition

Tenant default occurs when a tenant fails to comply with material obligations under a lease agreement. The most common default is nonpayment of rent, but defaults can also arise from other lease violations.

Common Types of Tenant Default

1) Monetary Default

  • Failure to pay base rent
  • Failure to pay additional rent (CAM, taxes, insurance)
  • Unpaid late fees or penalties

2) Non-Monetary Default

  • Violation of permitted use clauses
  • Failure to maintain required insurance
  • Unauthorized alterations or subleasing
  • Failure to maintain premises condition

Lease Cure Period

Most leases include a notice and cure period allowing the tenant a specified number of days to remedy the default before further action is taken. Cure periods vary by lease type and jurisdiction.

Landlord Remedies

  • Late fees and interest charges
  • Security deposit application
  • Acceleration of rent (if permitted)
  • Eviction or unlawful detainer action
  • Lease termination
  • Personal guaranty enforcement (if applicable)

Financial Impact

  • Reduced NOI: Lost or delayed rental income.
  • Vacancy risk: Potential downtime if tenant is removed.
  • Legal expenses: Eviction or collection costs.
  • Re-leasing costs: Brokerage commissions and tenant improvements.

Example Scenario

A retail tenant stops paying rent due to declining sales. The landlord issues a default notice with a 10-day cure period. The tenant fails to cure, and the landlord begins eviction proceedings.

During this process, the property experiences vacancy and legal costs, negatively impacting cash flow.

Risk Factors in Tenant Default

  • Economic downturns
  • Tenant business failure
  • Overleveraged tenants
  • Poor credit screening
  • Weak lease enforcement policies

Mitigation Strategies

  • Thorough credit and financial review prior to leasing
  • Security deposits and letters of credit
  • Personal or corporate guarantees
  • Diversified tenant mix
  • Strong lease drafting and legal review
  • Proactive communication and early intervention

Underwriting Considerations

  • Review tenant creditworthiness and lease terms.
  • Analyze exposure to single-tenant concentration.
  • Assess guarantor strength.
  • Model potential downtime and re-leasing costs.
  • Evaluate historical delinquency trends.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failing to enforce lease terms consistently.
  • Underestimating legal timeline for eviction.
  • Ignoring early warning signs of tenant distress.
  • Over-reliance on security deposits as full protection.

Modified Gross Lease

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Modified Gross Lease

Definition

A modified gross lease is a lease structure where the tenant pays base rent, and the landlord pays some operating expenses, but the tenant also pays certain expenses directly and/or reimburses the landlord for specific costs (often increases above a baseline such as a base year or an expense stop).

Modified gross sits between a gross lease (landlord pays most expenses) and NNN (tenant pays most expenses).

What “Modified” Usually Means

There is no single universal modified gross template. The lease defines which items are included in rent and which are billed separately. Common patterns include:

  • Landlord pays: taxes, insurance, and common-area maintenance (or most of them).
  • Tenant pays: utilities and janitorial in the leased premises (often separately metered).
  • Pass-throughs: tenant reimburses increases in operating expenses over a baseline.

Base Year and Expense Stop

The most common modified gross mechanism is “landlord pays up to a baseline, tenant pays increases above it.”

Base Year

A base year sets a reference year of operating expenses (often the first lease year). The tenant then pays their pro rata share of increases above base-year expenses in later years.

Expense Stop

An expense stop is a stated cap (often in $/RSF/year) that the landlord will cover. Expenses above the stop are reimbursed by the tenant.

Simple structure

  • Tenant reimbursement = (Current Year Expenses − Baseline Expenses) × Tenant Pro Rata Share

Gross-Up Provisions

In properties with vacancy, some leases include a gross-up clause that adjusts certain variable expenses to a stabilized occupancy (for example, 95%). This prevents occupancy swings from distorting “per-occupied-suite” cost allocation and can materially affect reimbursements.

How It Compares to Gross and NNN

  • Gross lease: tenant payment is mostly all-in; landlord carries most expense inflation risk. (Gross Lease)
  • Modified gross: expenses are shared; tenant usually bears some expense growth through pass-throughs.
  • NNN: tenant pays base rent plus taxes/insurance/CAM reimbursements; landlord risk is lower, tenant cost visibility is higher. (NNN (Triple Net))

Why It Matters

Modified gross leases shift some operating expense risk from landlord to tenant without going fully net. For owners, this can stabilize NOI. For tenants, it can make “all-in” occupancy cost less predictable than a true gross lease—especially if insurance or taxes spike. For investors, the details determine recoverability and true margin.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting NOI: model which expenses are recoverable and which remain landlord-paid.
  • Lease audit/review: confirm baseline definition, expense categories included, and pro rata share denominator.
  • Risk analysis: identify exposure to volatile line items (insurance/taxes/utilities) and whether they pass through.
  • Comparability: normalize modified gross economics against gross and NNN comps.

Example

An office tenant leases 10,000 RSF in a 100,000 RSF building (10% pro rata share). Base year operating expenses are $1,200,000. In year two, expenses rise to $1,320,000.

  • Increase = $1,320,000 − $1,200,000 = $120,000
  • Tenant reimbursement = $120,000 × 10% = $12,000/year (≈ $1,000/month)

If the lease also requires the tenant to pay in-suite electricity directly, that would be separate from the reimbursement.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “modified gross” means the same thing everywhere: it’s a category, not a standardized form.
  • Baseline ambiguity: disputes happen when base year is undefined, partial, or uses unusual accounting.
  • Wrong pro rata denominator: “total RSF” may exclude pads/outparcels or use leased RSF with gross-up rules.
  • Caps and carve-outs: some leases cap controllable expenses or exclude capital replacements—read carefully.
  • Occupancy effects: gross-up rules (or lack of them) can swing reimbursements in a high-vacancy building.

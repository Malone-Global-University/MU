Underwritten NOI

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Underwritten NOI

Definition

Underwritten NOI (Underwritten Net Operating Income) is a conservative, risk-adjusted projection of Net Operating Income (NOI) used to evaluate an acquisition or loan.

It reflects what the investor or lender believes the property can sustainably produce — not simply what is reported or projected.

Purpose

  • Determine maximum purchase price
  • Assess loan sizing and debt coverage
  • Evaluate downside risk
  • Protect capital through disciplined assumptions

Underwritten NOI vs. Other NOI Metrics

  • In-Place NOI: Historical trailing performance (e.g., T-12).
  • Pro Forma NOI: Forward-looking projection based on sponsor assumptions.
  • Stabilized NOI: Expected steady-state income.
  • Underwritten NOI: Risk-adjusted number used for decision-making.

Underwritten NOI is often lower than sponsor pro forma NOI because it incorporates conservative assumptions.

Common Underwriting Adjustments

Revenue Adjustments

  • Applying higher vacancy rates than seller projections
  • Discounting aggressive rent growth assumptions
  • Excluding speculative income sources
  • Normalizing concessions

Expense Adjustments

  • Increasing property tax assumptions post-sale
  • Benchmarking insurance to current market rates
  • Normalizing maintenance and capital needs
  • Including full management fees even for owner-operators

Example

Seller Pro Forma NOI: $1,000,000

  • + Adjusted vacancy (more conservative): -$60,000
  • + Higher insurance estimate: -$25,000
  • + Full management fee normalization: -$40,000

Underwritten NOI = $875,000

Underwritten NOI and Pricing Discipline

Investors typically determine value using:

  • Maximum Price = Underwritten NOI ÷ Required Return (Cap Rate)

If the market demands a 6.5% return:

  • $875,000 ÷ 0.065 = $13,461,538

This becomes the investor’s pricing ceiling.

Why It Matters

  • Protects against overpaying in competitive markets
  • Creates margin of safety
  • Aligns purchase price with risk tolerance
  • Preserves long-term capital durability

Common Pitfalls

  • Letting market pressure inflate underwriting assumptions
  • Matching seller projections to “win the deal”
  • Ignoring tax reassessment impact
  • Underestimating insurance volatility

Investor Checklist

  • Separate facts from projections.
  • Underwrite to downside scenarios.
  • Apply realistic expense ratios.
  • Use conservative exit cap rates.
  • Maintain a written, non-negotiable investment standard.

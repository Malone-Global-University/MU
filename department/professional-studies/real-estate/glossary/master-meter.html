Master Meter

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Master Meter

Definition

A master meter is a utility metering setup where a property has a single primary meter (or a few meters) serving an entire building or community, and the owner receives the utility bill. This is common for water/sewer, gas, or electric in older multifamily assets.

How Master-Metered Utilities Are Paid

When utilities are master-metered, the owner typically handles costs in one of three ways:

  • Owner-paid utilities: utilities are included in rent (the property absorbs the full cost).
  • Bill-backs / RUBS: the property allocates costs to residents using an allocation method (not true submetering).
  • Submetering conversion: install submeters so usage can be measured per unit and billed more precisely.

Why It Matters

Master-metered utilities can meaningfully impact NOI and risk because costs are often volatile and can be hard to control without behavioral incentives or upgrades. Underwriting must account for both the expense line and any utility reimbursement income.

Investor Uses

  • Underwrite utility expense risk: validate historical bills, seasonality, and rate increases.
  • Identify NOI opportunities: add bill-backs, repair leaks, install low-flow fixtures, or submeter.
  • Evaluate resident behavior incentives: usage often drops when residents see a bill tied to consumption.
  • Assess capex requirements: submetering or meter upgrades may require plumbing/electrical work and permits.

Common Allocation Methods

If the property bills residents back for master-metered utilities, costs may be allocated using methods such as:

  • By occupancy: number of residents per unit
  • By unit type or size: bedrooms, bathrooms, or square footage
  • Hybrid: a blend (e.g., 50% by occupancy, 50% by square footage)

These approaches are often referred to as RUBS (Ratio Utility Billing System) and typically require clear lease language and compliance with local rules.

Example

A 60-unit building is master-metered for water/sewer. The owner pays an average of $9,000/month. After implementing a bill-back program that recovers 70% of costs:

  • Utility expense: $9,000/month
  • Utility reimbursement income: $6,300/month
  • Net owner cost: $2,700/month

The net improvement to NOI depends on collections, compliance, admin fees, and whether usage falls over time.

Common Pitfalls

  • Double-counting: including both owner-paid expense and reimbursements incorrectly in NOI.
  • Assuming 100% recovery: bill-backs rarely collect perfectly (vacancy, delinquency, caps, admin limits).
  • Ignoring leaks: a single toilet flapper or irrigation leak can blow up water bills.
  • Regulatory constraints: some jurisdictions cap bill-backs or require specific disclosures.
  • Resident pushback: introduce changes with notice, clear communication, and consistent enforcement.

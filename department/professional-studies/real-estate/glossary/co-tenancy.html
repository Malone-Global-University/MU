Co-Tenancy Clause

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Co-Tenancy Clause

Definition

A Co-Tenancy Clause is a provision in a retail lease that allows a tenant to reduce rent, pay percentage rent only, or terminate the lease if certain conditions—typically the presence of anchor tenants or a minimum occupancy level— are not maintained in the shopping center.

In simple terms: if key stores close or occupancy drops too low, the tenant gets relief.

Why Co-Tenancy Exists

  • Retail traffic often depends on anchor tenants.
  • Smaller tenants rely on overall center vitality.
  • Tenants want protection against declining foot traffic.
  • Landlords use it as a leasing incentive.

Common Co-Tenancy Triggers

  • Closure of a named anchor tenant.
  • Failure to maintain a minimum number of anchor stores.
  • Overall occupancy falling below a threshold (e.g., 70%).
  • Failure to open within a required delivery period.

Common Tenant Remedies

  • Rent Abatement: Temporary reduction of base rent.
  • Percentage Rent Only: Tenant pays only percentage of sales.
  • Termination Right: Tenant may terminate lease after a cure period.

Example

A clothing retailer leases space in a shopping center anchored by a grocery store and a major department store. The lease includes a co-tenancy clause requiring at least one anchor of 50,000+ SF to remain open. If both anchors close for more than 12 months, the tenant may:

  • Pay reduced rent for a specified period.
  • Terminate the lease if the condition is not cured.

Initial vs. Ongoing Co-Tenancy

Initial Co-Tenancy

Applies before tenant opens. If required anchors are not open, tenant may delay opening or avoid full rent.

Ongoing Co-Tenancy

Applies during the lease term if anchor or occupancy requirements are later violated.

Landlord Protections

  • Substitution rights (replace closed anchor with similar tenant).
  • Cure periods before remedies apply.
  • Limit on duration of rent reduction.
  • Cap on termination rights.

Impact on NOI and Valuation

  • Rent reductions directly reduce NOI.
  • High co-tenancy exposure increases risk perception.
  • Lenders review co-tenancy clauses in underwriting.
  • Anchor risk concentration can affect cap rate.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Anchor tenant credit quality.
  • Remaining lease term of anchors.
  • Tenant concentration risk.
  • Historical center occupancy trends.
  • Substitution flexibility in leases.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overly broad termination rights.
  • No defined cure period.
  • Ambiguous anchor replacement standards.
  • Stacked co-tenancy exposure across multiple tenants.

Investor Checklist

  • Review all co-tenancy clauses during due diligence.
  • Model downside scenarios if anchors vacate.
  • Assess lease rollover timing for anchors.
  • Evaluate replacement flexibility provisions.
  • Stress-test NOI under percentage-rent-only scenarios.

Co-Tenancy vs. Percentage Rent

  • Co-Tenancy: Provides tenant protection if traffic drivers leave.
  • Percentage Rent: Allows landlord to share in strong sales performance.

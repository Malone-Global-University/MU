Relinquished Property

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Relinquished Property

Definition

A relinquished property is the property an investor sells as part of a 1031 exchange. The proceeds from selling the relinquished property are then used (through a qualified structure) to acquire a replacement property, with the goal of deferring capital gains taxes.

Where It Fits in a 1031 Exchange

In a standard “forward” 1031 exchange, the relinquished property is sold first. The clock starts at the closing of the relinquished property sale, and the exchanger must follow strict deadlines to identify and acquire replacement property.

Key deadlines (common rules)

  • 45-day identification period: identify potential replacement properties within 45 days of the relinquished property closing.
  • 180-day exchange period: close on replacement property within 180 days of the relinquished property closing (or the tax return due date, if earlier).

Qualified Intermediary (QI) and “Constructive Receipt”

In most exchanges, the seller cannot take possession of the proceeds from the relinquished property sale. Instead, a qualified intermediary (QI) holds the funds and facilitates the exchange to avoid “constructive receipt,” which can trigger taxable recognition.

What Usually Qualifies as a Relinquished Property

A relinquished property in a 1031 exchange is typically real estate held for investment or productive use in a trade or business. (Primary residences and property held primarily for resale generally do not qualify.)

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Tax planning: sale structure impacts whether capital gains and depreciation recapture can be deferred.
  • Execution risk: missing deadlines or mishandling proceeds can collapse the exchange.
  • Pricing and liquidity: the relinquished closing date determines your timeline and leverage in buying replacements.
  • Portfolio strategy: investors often exchange from management-intensive assets into more durable cash flow or different markets.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting too long to line up replacements: 45 days moves fast, especially in tight markets.
  • Improper handling of proceeds: touching funds can create constructive receipt and taxes.
  • Debt mismatch (“mortgage boot”): reducing debt or receiving cash back may trigger taxable boot.
  • Title issues and delays: closing friction on the replacement side can blow the 180-day window.

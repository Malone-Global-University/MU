Definition

In real estate, a market is the geographic and competitive area where a property competes for tenants (rentals) or buyers (for-sale). Markets are defined less by a map boundary and more by who the customer is, what alternatives they will consider, and how prices are discovered (rents, concessions, sale prices, cap rates).

Market vs. Submarket

Investors often talk about markets at two levels:

  • Metro / primary market: a broad area (e.g., “Seattle metro”) where capital flows and macro drivers are assessed.
  • Submarket: a smaller competitive pocket (e.g., a neighborhood or corridor) where rents, vacancy, and absorption are most comparable.

Underwriting usually starts with the metro story, but the submarket sets the rent.

What Defines a Market in Practice

  • Demand sources: employers, schools, migration, household formation, tourism, logistics flows.
  • Substitutes: the set of comparable properties a tenant/buyer would reasonably choose instead.
  • Travel time / friction: how far a renter will commute, where amenities cluster, transit access.
  • Regulation and taxes: zoning, permitting, rent regulations, property taxes, insurance environment.
  • Supply pipeline: new construction and conversions that can shift vacancy and pricing power.

Why It Matters

“The market” drives the big underwriting assumptions: rent growth, vacancy, concessions, cap rates, and ultimately exit liquidity. Two identical buildings can perform very differently if they sit in different markets or submarkets.

Investor Uses

  • Setting rent and vacancy assumptions: anchor pro forma to local comps and market vacancy.
  • Stress testing: model downside cases where demand softens or new supply delivers.
  • Selecting strategy: value-add works best where there is proven depth of demand at the target rent tier.
  • Exit planning: assess buyer pool depth, financing availability, and cap-rate sensitivity by market.
  • Comparing opportunities: normalize returns by market risk (volatility, liquidity, regulation).

Example

A 150-unit asset is located in a metro with strong job growth, but the building competes in a submarket with heavy new deliveries. The metro may support long-run rent growth, while the submarket may require near-term concessions and slower lease-up due to supply pressure.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong comp set: “same city” is not the same market if renter alternatives differ.
  • Confusing headline growth with investable growth: published metro averages may hide submarket dispersion.
  • Ignoring supply timing: what matters is when units deliver relative to your lease-up and refi window.
  • Overlooking regulation: permitting delays, rent rules, or tax changes can alter returns quickly.
  • Assuming liquidity is constant: buyer demand and cap rates can be more volatile in secondary markets.

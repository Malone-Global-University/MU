Comparables (Comps)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Comparables (Comps)

Definition

Comparables (often called “comps”) are similar properties or recent transactions used to estimate a subject property’s value or market rent. The logic is simple: if two assets are truly similar, they should trade and lease within a reasonable range after accounting for differences.

Types of Comps

  • Sales comps: closed sales used to estimate property value (price, $/unit, $/SF, cap rate).
  • Lease comps: signed leases or asking rents used to estimate market rent and concessions.
  • Expense comps: operating benchmarks (taxes, utilities, payroll, repairs, management fees).
  • Capital comps: renovation costs, bids, and replacement/installed costs (CapEx per unit).

How Comps Are Used

Comps support the sales comparison approach in valuation and help investors validate underwriting assumptions. In practice, comps are used to:

  • Estimate market value: translate a comp set into a value range for the subject.
  • Validate rents: confirm achievable market rent, premiums, and concessions.
  • Triangulate cap rates: compare in-place income and sales prices to infer going-in yields.
  • Stress-test exit price: sanity-check disposition assumptions (price, cap rate, $/unit).
  • Spot mispricing: find deals where price is out of line with local evidence.

What Makes a Good Comparable

The best comps are the ones that match the subject on the variables that actually drive price and rent:

  • Location: same submarket, school district, proximity, micro-location quality.
  • Asset type & unit mix: same product (garden vs mid-rise), similar bed/bath distribution.
  • Size & density: similar unit count, average unit size, and layout efficiency.
  • Vintage & condition: construction year, renovation level, deferred maintenance.
  • Quality & amenities: parking, laundry, HVAC, common areas, views, security, finishes.
  • Income quality: similar rent roll strength, delinquency, concessions, other income.
  • Timing: recent closings/leases in the current rate and supply environment.

Common Adjustment Methods

Rarely are properties identical. Analysts adjust comps to make them “apples-to-apples.”

  • $ per unit adjustments: adjust for unit size/quality, parking, renovations, or deferred maintenance.
  • $ per SF adjustments: common in office/retail/industrial; can be useful for residential as a check.
  • Income approach bridge: compare NOI and cap rate; normalize for expenses and vacancy assumptions.
  • Concession normalization (leases): convert free rent/discounts into effective rent.

Example

You are valuing a 40-unit garden property. Three recent nearby sales suggest a range of $175,000–$190,000 per unit. Your subject has slightly older interiors and some deferred maintenance, so you apply a conservative adjustment:

  • Comp range: $175k–$190k per unit
  • Condition adjustment: -$10k per unit
  • Indicated value range: $165k–$180k per unit

That translates to a value range of $6.6M–$7.2M (40 units × $165k–$180k), which you can compare against an NOI/cap-rate valuation for a cross-check.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using “ask” instead of “achieved”: listing prices and asking rents can be aspirational.
  • Not normalizing income: comps with unusual other income, concessions, or expenses distort NOI comparisons.
  • Ignoring unit mix: 1-bed heavy properties can trade differently than 2-bed heavy properties.
  • Time mismatch: older comps may be irrelevant after rate moves or new supply deliveries.
  • Selection bias: picking comps that “support the story” instead of the closest matches.
  • Micro-location blindness: two properties a mile apart can be in different rent bands.

Special Purpose Entity (SPE)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Special Purpose Entity (SPE)

Definition

A Special Purpose Entity (SPE) is a legal entity formed for a single, narrowly defined purpose—most commonly to own and operate one specific real estate asset.

SPEs are designed to isolate financial risk and are frequently required by lenders in commercial real estate transactions.

Why SPEs Are Used

  • Liability isolation: Limits risk to a single property.
  • Lender protection: Prevents cross-collateralization with other assets.
  • Bankruptcy remoteness: Reduces risk of unrelated financial distress affecting the asset.
  • Clarity of ownership: Simplifies financial reporting and governance.

Typical Structure

An SPE is often structured as an LLC and may include:

  • Managing member (sponsor)
  • Passive equity members
  • Independent manager (for bankruptcy-remote structures)

Lender Requirements

Commercial lenders commonly require:

  • No commingling of funds
  • Separate bank accounts
  • Limited business activities (single asset only)
  • Restrictions on additional debt

Example

An investor acquires a $25 million office building. A new LLC is formed solely to own that building. The LLC signs the loan, holds title, and operates the property— isolating it from the investor’s other holdings.

Common Pitfalls

  • Improper fund commingling: Can jeopardize liability protection.
  • Failure to follow operating formalities: Weakens legal separation.
  • Unclear governance terms: Creates investor disputes.
  • Assuming absolute protection: Guarantees and fraud exceptions may apply.

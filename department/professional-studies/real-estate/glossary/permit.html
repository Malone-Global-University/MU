Definition

A permit is an official approval issued by a city, county, or other governmental authority allowing a specific activity—such as construction, renovation, demolition, or occupancy—to proceed in compliance with applicable building codes, zoning laws, and safety regulations.

Common Types of Permits

1) Building Permit

  • Required for structural construction, additions, major renovations.
  • Triggers plan review and inspections.

2) Mechanical, Electrical & Plumbing (MEP) Permits

  • Required for HVAC systems, electrical panels, wiring, plumbing lines, and fixtures.
  • Often separate from the main building permit.

3) Demolition Permit

  • Required to remove existing structures or significant portions of a building.

4) Grading / Sitework Permit

  • Required for excavation, drainage work, stormwater systems, or major site changes.

5) Certificate of Occupancy (CO)

  • Issued after final inspections.
  • Confirms the building is safe and legal for occupancy.

6) Operational / Business Permits

  • Health permits (restaurants), liquor licenses, short-term rental permits, etc.

Why Permits Matter to Investors

  • Timeline risk: permit review delays can push construction schedules.
  • Cost risk: plan revisions, code upgrades, or unexpected requirements can increase costs.
  • Lender requirements: construction loans typically require issued permits before funding.
  • Exit value: unpermitted work can reduce sale value or create legal exposure.
  • Insurance & liability: work without permits can void coverage or increase risk exposure.

Typical Permit Process

  1. Pre-submittal consultation: confirm requirements with planning/building department.
  2. Application & plan submission: architectural and engineering drawings.
  3. Plan review: compliance with building code, fire code, zoning, ADA, energy standards.
  4. Corrections & resubmittals: revisions based on reviewer comments.
  5. Permit issuance: authorization to begin work.
  6. Inspections: foundation, framing, MEP, and final inspections.
  7. Final approval / CO issuance: project cleared for occupancy.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Jurisdiction speed: review timelines vary widely by city and workload.
  • Complexity: mixed-use or change-of-use projects face deeper review.
  • Impact fees: permit fees, development fees, and utility charges can be significant.
  • Code triggers: renovations can require bringing older buildings up to current code.
  • Inspection risk: failed inspections can create delays and added costs.

Example

An investor purchases a small retail building for renovation. During permit review, the city requires ADA upgrades and fire suppression improvements. The additional requirements increase the renovation budget by $150,000 and extend the schedule by two months, affecting the lease-up timeline and projected returns.

Common Pitfalls

  • Starting work without permits: can lead to stop-work orders and fines.
  • Ignoring change-of-use triggers: switching from office to restaurant often requires major upgrades.
  • Underestimating code compliance: older buildings may require seismic, fire, or accessibility retrofits.
  • Failing to budget soft costs: permit fees, expediters, consultants, and re-submittals add up.

Lien

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Lien

Definition

A lien is a legal claim against real estate that secures payment of a debt or obligation. Liens attach to the property (not just the owner), which means they can affect the ability to sell, refinance, or transfer clear title until they are released or satisfied.

Why Liens Matter

Liens determine who gets paid first if the property is sold under distress (or through foreclosure). They are a core topic in title review and due diligence because unexpected liens can create closing delays, surprise costs, or legal exposure.

Common Types of Liens

  • Mortgage / deed of trust lien: created when a lender finances the property. This is usually the largest lien and is recorded in the public record.
  • Property tax lien: arises when property taxes are unpaid. In many jurisdictions, tax liens have very high priority.
  • Mechanic’s (construction) lien: filed by contractors, subcontractors, or suppliers for unpaid labor or materials.
  • Judgment lien: results from a court judgment against the owner and may attach to real estate depending on state law.
  • HOA / COA lien: for unpaid association dues or assessments (primarily condos and some planned communities).
  • Municipal liens: code enforcement, utility charges, or special assessments that can attach to the property.

Lien Priority

Priority determines the order liens are paid from sale proceeds. While rules vary by state and lien type, a simple baseline principle is: first in time, first in right (earlier recorded liens are typically senior), with important exceptions (especially for taxes and certain municipal charges).

Common priority concepts

  • Senior lien: paid first; has the strongest claim.
  • Junior lien: paid after senior liens; riskier and more likely to be wiped out in foreclosure.
  • Subordination: an agreement that changes priority (e.g., one lender agrees to be junior).

How Investors Encounter Liens

  • Acquisitions: title report reveals existing liens that must be paid off or cleared at closing.
  • Refinance: lenders require clean title and may require lien payoffs or releases.
  • Value-add renovations: risk of mechanic’s liens if contractors or subs are not paid.
  • Distressed deals: liens can complicate pricing, timelines, and exit strategy.

Example

A buyer goes under contract and the title report shows three recorded liens:

  • $6,500,000 first mortgage lien (recorded in 2021)
  • $85,000 mechanic’s lien (recorded in 2025)
  • $22,000 judgment lien (recorded in 2024)

In most scenarios, the first mortgage is senior because it was recorded first and is a secured purchase/refinance lien. The other liens are typically addressed at closing through payoff, settlement, bonding, or release, depending on the facts and local rules.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “clean title” without proof: always review a current title commitment and recorded documents.
  • Ignoring contractor payment controls: poor processes can lead to mechanic’s liens during renovations.
  • Not understanding priority: lien position drives risk, especially in distressed sales.
  • Missing non-obvious municipal charges: utilities, assessments, or code liens can surface late without diligence.

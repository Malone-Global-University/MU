Probate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Probate

Definition

Probate is the court-supervised legal process of validating a deceased person’s will, paying outstanding debts and taxes, and distributing remaining assets—including real property—to heirs or beneficiaries.

How Probate Works

  1. Filing the will: The will (if one exists) is submitted to the probate court.
  2. Appointment of executor: The court formally appoints an executor or personal representative.
  3. Asset inventory: All assets, including real estate, are identified and valued.
  4. Debt settlement: Creditors are notified and claims are paid.
  5. Distribution: Remaining assets are transferred to heirs or beneficiaries.

Why Probate Matters in Real Estate

  • Title transfer: Property cannot be sold until legal authority is granted by the court.
  • Timeline risk: Probate can take months (or longer), delaying transactions.
  • Court approval: Some jurisdictions require court confirmation of sale price.
  • Heir disputes: Conflicts among beneficiaries may complicate or delay disposition.

Probate vs. Non-Probate Transfers

Not all property goes through probate. Real estate may transfer automatically outside of probate if structured properly:

  • Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship
  • Community Property with Right of Survivorship
  • Living Trust ownership
  • Transfer-on-Death (TOD) Deed (where permitted)

Investor Perspective

Probate properties can present both opportunity and complexity for investors.

  • Motivated sales: Heirs may prefer a clean, fast disposition.
  • As-is condition: Properties are often sold without repairs.
  • Extended closing periods: Court approval and documentation can lengthen timelines.
  • Title diligence: Proper authority of the executor must be verified before closing.

Example

An owner passes away leaving a rental property solely in their name. The executor must obtain court authority before listing the property for sale. After debts are paid, net proceeds are distributed to beneficiaries according to the will.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming immediate authority: Family members cannot sell property without court appointment.
  • Underestimating timelines: Probate can extend 6–18+ months depending on complexity.
  • Ignoring creditor claims: Valid claims must be resolved before distribution.
  • Improper deed execution: Executor must sign in proper representative capacity.

Lease Rollover

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Lease Rollover

Definition

Lease rollover refers to what happens when existing leases expire: tenants may renew, vacate, or renegotiate terms, and the space is then re-leased at current market conditions. Rollover is both a risk (income disruption and leasing costs) and an opportunity (raising below-market rents).

Why It Matters

A property’s cash flow is only as stable as its lease expirations. Concentrated rollover (many leases expiring in the same window) can create a “cash-flow cliff” where occupancy and NOI fall at the same time. Spread-out expirations generally reduce volatility and make underwriting more durable.

What Investors Analyze

  • Expiration schedule: which leases roll in each month/quarter/year.
  • In-place vs. market rent: whether rollover likely creates rent growth (or rent loss).
  • Renewal probability: tenant quality, unit type demand, resident satisfaction, competitive set.
  • Downtime and turn costs: vacancy loss, make-ready/renovation costs, and time to re-lease.
  • Leasing costs: concessions, commissions (more common in commercial), and marketing spend.
  • Financing impact: DSCR sensitivity if rollover hits during rate resets or loan maturity.

How It’s Modeled in Underwriting

Rollover analysis is often summarized as a lease rollover schedule (or “lease expiration schedule”) showing what percent of income (or units) expires in each period. Underwriting then applies assumptions for:

  • Renewal rate (what percent of tenants stay)
  • Downtime (how long vacated units stay empty)
  • Rent reset (new rent vs. in-place rent)
  • Turn / make-ready cost per move-out
  • Concessions (free rent, discounts) if needed to maintain velocity

Example

Suppose a 100-unit property has 35 leases expiring between May and July. If renewal rates drop or competitors offer concessions, the property could see a short-term spike in vacancy and lower NOI. However, if the expiring units are below market, successful rollover can reset rents upward and increase cash flow once units are re-leased.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring concentration: too many expirations in one period increases volatility.
  • Assuming market rent is instantly achievable: rent growth may require time, capex, or concessions.
  • Underwriting “perfect renewals”: renewal rates change with management quality and competition.
  • Forgetting operational constraints: renovations and turns require labor capacity and scheduling.
  • Missing seasonality: leasing velocity often differs by month; timing affects downtime and concessions.

Lesson 4: Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis

Before making investment decisions, traders and investors analyze securities using two primary methods: Fundamental Analysis (FA) and Technical Analysis (TA).

1. Fundamental Analysis (FA) â€“ Evaluating a Companyâ€™s Value

Fundamental analysis focuses on a companyâ€™s financial health, business performance, and market position to determine its intrinsic value.

Key Components of FA:

A. Financial Statements (Key Ratios)

Investors analyze three core financial statements:

  1. Income Statement â€“ Measures revenue, expenses, and profitability. Key Metric: Earnings per Share (EPS) â€“ Net income per share.
  2. Balance Sheet â€“ Shows assets, liabilities, and shareholdersâ€™ equity. Key Metric: Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) â€“ Measures financial leverage.
  3. Cash Flow Statement â€“ Tracks cash inflows/outflows from operations. Key Metric: Free Cash Flow (FCF) â€“ Cash available after capital expenditures.
B. Valuation Ratios (How Expensive is a Stock?)
  1. Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio â€“ Stock price vs. earnings per share. Lower P/E suggests undervaluation.
  2. Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio â€“ Compares stock price to the book value of assets.
  3. Dividend Yield â€“ Measures annual dividends relative to stock price.
C. Macroeconomic & Industry Analysis
  1. Interest Rates & Inflation: Higher rates usually slow growth stocks.
  2. Sector Strength: Some industries perform better in certain economic cycles.
  3. Who Uses FA: Long-term investors, value investors (e.g., Warren Buffett).

2. Technical Analysis (TA) â€“ Predicting Future Prices Using Charts

Technical analysis studies price patterns, trends, and trading volume to predict future price movements. It assumes all fundamental factors are already reflected in the stockâ€™s price.

Key Components of TA:

A. Price Trends & Chart Patterns
  • Uptrend (Bullish) â€“ Higher highs & higher lows.
  • Downtrend (Bearish) â€“ Lower highs & lower lows.
  • Support & Resistance Levels â€“ Price points where a stock repeatedly stops rising or falling.
B. Technical Indicators
  • Moving Averages (SMA & EMA): Smooth out price fluctuations.
  • Relative Strength Index (RSI): Measures overbought (>70) or oversold (<30) conditions.
  • MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): Identifies momentum shifts.
  • Volume Analysis: High trading volume confirms trends.
C. Candlestick Patterns (Used for Predicting Short-Term Moves)
  • Bullish Reversal Patterns: Hammer, Morning Star, Engulfing Pattern.
  • Bearish Reversal Patterns: Shooting Star, Evening Star, Bearish Engulfing.
  • Who Uses TA: Short-term traders, swing traders, day traders, and algorithmic traders.

3. Key Differences & When to Use Each

Fundamental Analysis

  • Focus: Financial health & intrinsic value
  • Used By: Long-term investors
  • Data Used: Financial statements, economic data
  • Goal: Find undervalued companies
  • Time Horizon: Weeks, months, years

Technical Analysis

  • Focus: Price movements & trends
  • Used By: Short-term traders
  • Data Used: Charts, price action, indicators
  • Goal: Predict short-term price movement
  • Time Horizon: Minutes, hours, days, weeks

Which One Should You Use?

  • FA is best for long-term investing (buy & hold strategies).
  • TA is ideal for traders looking for short-term price action.
  • Professionals combine both: use FA to select stocks and TA to time trades.

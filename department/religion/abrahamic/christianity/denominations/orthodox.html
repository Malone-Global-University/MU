Eastern Orthodoxy

The Ancient Tradition of the East

Introduction

Eastern Orthodoxy is one of the oldest branches of Christianity, preserving the faith and traditions of the early church. It emphasizes continuity with the apostolic faith, conciliar leadership, and the centrality of liturgy in spiritual life. The Orthodox Church does not recognize papal supremacy, instead governed by a council of autocephalous (self-governing) patriarchates.

Historical Development

The Orthodox Church developed in the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire, with its center in Constantinople. In 1054 CE, the Great Schism divided Eastern Orthodoxy from Roman Catholicism over questions of papal authority and theology. Despite regional divisions, Orthodoxy has maintained strong continuity in doctrine and worship since the early church.

Global Presence

Eastern Orthodoxy remains strongest in Eastern Europe, Russia, the Middle East, and the Balkans, though its diaspora communities continue to grow worldwide. Major churches include the Russian Orthodox, Greek Orthodox, Serbian Orthodox, and Coptic Orthodox traditions.

The Flood of Deucalion

The Greek flood myth recounts how Zeus destroyed a corrupt humanity, sparing only Deucalion and Pyrrha, who repopulated the earth in a miraculous way.

1. The Wickedness of Men

In the Iron Age, humans became violent, unjust, and impious. Zeus, seeing the corruption of mankind, resolved to wipe them out with a great flood.

Only two were found worthy: Deucalion, the son of Prometheus, and his wife Pyrrha, the daughter of Epimetheus and Pandora.

2. The Flood

Prometheus, foreseeing the disaster, warned his son. Deucalion built a sturdy chest (ark-like vessel), and he and Pyrrha filled it with provisions.

Zeus unleashed torrential rains and Poseidon loosed the seas. The waters covered nearly all the earth, destroying the rest of humanity. For nine days and nights the chest drifted until it landed on Mount Parnassus (in some versions, Mount Etna or Mount Othrys).

3. The Oracle of Themis

When the floodwaters receded, Deucalion and Pyrrha offered sacrifices to the gods. They sought guidance from the oracle of Themis, asking how to restore humankind.

5. Symbolic Meaning

  • The flood myth parallels stories in many cultures (e.g., Noah in Hebrew tradition, Utnapishtim in Mesopotamia).
  • Deucalion and Pyrrha symbolize renewal and resilience after catastrophe.

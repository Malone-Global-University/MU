Verb Mood

Verb mood expresses the speaker’s attitude toward the action or state of being. Understanding mood helps convey commands, wishes, possibilities, or statements.

Definition / Explanation

Mood is a grammatical feature that shows the mode or attitude of the verb. The three primary moods in English are indicative, imperative, and subjunctive.

Types of Verb Mood

  • Indicative Mood: States facts or asks questions. Example: She is reading a book. / Are you coming?
  • Imperative Mood: Expresses commands, requests, or instructions. Example: Close the door. / Please pass the salt.
  • Subjunctive Mood: Expresses wishes, hypothetical situations, or conditions contrary to fact. Example: I wish I were taller. / If I were you, I would study more.

Rules / Guidelines

  • Use the indicative mood for statements of fact or questions.
  • Use the imperative mood for giving clear commands or requests.
  • The subjunctive mood often uses the base form of the verb after “if” or “wish.”
  • In the subjunctive, “were” is used for all subjects in hypothetical statements: "If he were here, we would start."

Examples

Indicative: The sun rises in the east.
Imperative: Sit down and listen.
Subjunctive: I suggest that he study harder.
Subjunctive (wish): I wish it were Friday.
Subjunctive (condition): If I were rich, I would travel the world.

Practice Exercises

  1. Identify the mood: "She enjoys reading novels."
  2. Identify the mood: "Please open the window."
  3. Identify the mood: "If I were taller, I could reach the shelf."
  4. Rewrite in subjunctive: "I wish I am better at math."
  5. Write a sentence in imperative mood asking someone to help you.

Answers / Explanation

  1. Indicative
  2. Imperative
  3. Subjunctive
  4. I wish I were better at math.
  5. Example: "Help me carry this box."

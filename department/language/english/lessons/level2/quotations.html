Quotation Marks

Quotation marks are used to enclose direct speech, quotations, titles of short works, or to indicate special terms. Correct usage helps readers understand exactly what is being quoted or emphasized.

Definition / Explanation

Quotation marks (“ ”) are punctuation marks used primarily to:

  • Enclose direct speech or dialogue.
  • Enclose titles of short works, like articles, poems, or songs.
  • Indicate a word or phrase being discussed or used unusually.

Rules / Guidelines

  • Place commas and periods inside quotation marks in American English: He said, "Hello."
  • Place question marks and exclamation points inside or outside depending on context: "Are you coming?" she asked. vs Did she just say, "I am leaving"?
  • Use single quotation marks (‘ ’) for quotes within quotes: "I heard him say, 'I am busy,'" she recalled.
  • Capitalize the first word of a complete sentence in a quotation.
  • Do not use quotation marks for indirect speech: He said he was tired.

Examples

Direct Speech: "I will be there soon," John said.
Quotation within a quotation: "Did you hear her say, 'I can't believe it'?"
Titles of short works: I just read the poem "The Road Not Taken."
Special term: The word "selfie" became popular in 2015.
Question mark outside quotation: Did she really say, "I am leaving"?

Practice Exercises

  1. Insert quotation marks: He said I will help you tomorrow.
  2. Correct the sentence: Did she say I am ready?
  3. Insert quotation marks for a quote within a quote: She said I heard him say I am tired.
  4. Use quotation marks for the title: I read a poem The Raven yesterday.
  5. Correct the punctuation: John asked Have you finished your homework

Answers / Explanation

  1. He said, "I will help you tomorrow."
  2. Did she say, "I am ready?"
  3. She said, "I heard him say, 'I am tired.'"
  4. I read a poem "The Raven" yesterday.
  5. John asked, "Have you finished your homework?"

Double Negatives

Double negatives occur when two negative words are used in the same clause, which can create confusion or reverse the intended meaning. Correcting double negatives ensures clarity in writing and speaking.

Definition / Explanation

A double negative happens when two negative expressions (e.g., not, no, never, nobody, nothing) appear in the same sentence, often making it grammatically incorrect or changing the meaning.

Rules / Guidelines

  • Use only one negative in a clause to express negation clearly: I don’t have any money.
  • Be careful with informal speech; some dialects use double negatives for emphasis (considered nonstandard in formal writing).
  • Avoid combining words like “never” with “nothing,” or “no” with “not” in standard English: Incorrect: I don’t need no help.

Examples

Incorrect: I don’t need no help.
Correct: I don’t need any help.
Incorrect: She never said nothing about the problem.
Correct: She never said anything about the problem.
Incorrect: He can’t hardly wait.
Correct: He can hardly wait.

Practice Exercises

  1. Correct the sentence: I don’t want nothing.
  2. Correct the sentence: She hasn’t never seen that movie.
  3. Correct the sentence: We don’t need no help from them.
  4. Correct the sentence: He didn’t say nothing to anyone.
  5. Correct the sentence: I can’t find my keys nowhere.

Answers / Explanation

  1. I don’t want anything.
  2. She has never seen that movie.
  3. We don’t need any help from them.
  4. He didn’t say anything to anyone.
  5. I can’t find my keys anywhere.

Lesson 8.7: Net Operating Income (NOI)

Understand how NOI is calculated from revenue and operating expenses and why it is central to valuation, underwriting, and financing.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Real Estate Investing Track Unit 8 Lesson 8.7

1. Lesson Introduction

Rental properties generate income through rent and other revenue sources, but that income must first cover the costs of operating the property. After operating expenses are paid, the remaining income is called Net Operating Income (NOI).

NOI is one of the most important concepts in real estate investing because it represents the property's core economic output. Investors, lenders, and analysts rely on NOI to compare properties, evaluate performance, estimate value, and determine how much debt a property can safely support.

Investor Insight:
In real estate analysis, NOI is often treated as the “engine” that drives property value and financial performance.

2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Define Net Operating Income (NOI).
  • Explain the relationship between revenue, operating expenses, and NOI.
  • Identify which items are included and excluded from NOI.
  • Interpret how NOI influences valuation and financing decisions.
  • Recognize how changes in rent or expenses affect NOI.

3. Core Concepts

What Net Operating Income Is

Net Operating Income represents the income produced by a property after subtracting operating expenses from operating revenue. It reflects the property's ability to generate income before considering financing costs or taxes.

Operating Revenue

Operating revenue typically includes rental income and other recurring sources such as parking fees, laundry income, storage fees, or service charges connected to property operations.

Operating Expenses

Operating expenses include the recurring costs required to run the property, such as property taxes, insurance, payroll, repairs and maintenance, utilities, and contract services.

Items Excluded from NOI

NOI excludes financing costs, loan payments, depreciation, income taxes, and major capital expenditures. These items relate to ownership structure or long-term investment decisions rather than the day-to-day operation of the property.

Why NOI Matters

Because NOI focuses on the operating performance of the property itself, it allows investors to compare properties independent of how they are financed or owned.

4. Mechanics

Basic NOI Formula

The simplified formula for Net Operating Income is:

NOI = Operating Revenue − Operating Expenses

Understanding the Structure

  • Revenue: All income generated by the property.
  • Expenses: Costs required to operate the property.
  • NOI: Remaining income after those costs are paid.

How NOI Changes

NOI increases when revenue rises or expenses fall. It decreases when rent drops, vacancy increases, or operating costs rise.

Operational Leverage

Because many property expenses are relatively stable, increases in rent can significantly improve NOI. This is why even modest rent growth can meaningfully increase property value.

5. Worked Example

Suppose an apartment property produces the following annual results:

  • Total rental and other income: $1,200,000
  • Total operating expenses: $450,000

Step 1: Identify Revenue

The property generates $1,200,000 in operating revenue.

Step 2: Subtract Operating Expenses

Operating expenses total $450,000.

Step 3: Calculate NOI

NOI = $1,200,000 − $450,000 = $750,000

Interpretation

The property produces $750,000 of net operating income before financing costs, taxes, or capital expenditures are considered.

6. Real Estate Application

Valuation

Property value in income-producing real estate is often estimated by capitalizing NOI. Higher NOI generally supports higher valuation.

Lending

Lenders evaluate NOI to determine whether the property generates enough income to support loan payments and maintain a safety cushion.

Asset Management

Owners and managers focus heavily on NOI because improvements in leasing, rent levels, occupancy, or expense control can increase NOI and enhance the property's financial performance.

Investor Insight:
Increasing NOI is one of the most direct ways to increase real estate value.

7. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing NOI with cash flow: NOI does not include loan payments.
  • Ignoring expense growth: Rising costs can erode NOI even when rent is stable.
  • Including capital improvements: These should not be treated as operating expenses.
  • Using optimistic revenue assumptions: Unrealistic rent projections inflate NOI.
  • Failing to normalize expenses: Temporary low expenses can distort NOI analysis.

8. Knowledge Check

  1. What does Net Operating Income measure?
  2. What is the basic formula used to calculate NOI?
  3. Why are loan payments excluded from NOI?
  4. How can rent growth affect NOI?
  5. Why do investors rely on NOI when comparing properties?

9. Practical Exercise

A rental property generates $900,000 in annual revenue and incurs $320,000 in operating expenses.

Complete the following:

  1. Calculate the property's Net Operating Income.
  2. Explain how a 5% increase in rent would affect NOI if expenses stayed the same.
  3. Describe how rising operating expenses could reduce NOI.
  4. Write 4–6 sentences explaining why NOI is central to real estate valuation.
  5. Identify one operational strategy that could increase NOI.

10. Key Takeaways

  • Net Operating Income measures property income after operating expenses.
  • NOI excludes financing costs, taxes, and capital expenditures.
  • It is a core indicator of property performance.
  • Higher NOI generally supports higher property value.
  • Revenue growth and expense control both influence NOI.

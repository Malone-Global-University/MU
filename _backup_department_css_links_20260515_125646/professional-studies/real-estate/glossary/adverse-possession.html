Adverse Possession

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Adverse Possession

Definition

Adverse Possession is a legal doctrine that allows a person to acquire ownership of property by openly and continuously possessing it without the owner's permission for a legally required period of time.

In simple terms: if someone occupies land long enough under certain conditions, they may become the legal owner.

Core Legal Requirements

While requirements vary by state, adverse possession typically must be:

  • Actual: Physical use of the property.
  • Open and Notorious: Visible occupation (not hidden).
  • Exclusive: Not shared with the true owner.
  • Hostile: Without permission of the owner.
  • Continuous: For the full statutory period.

Statutory Period

The required possession period varies by jurisdiction, commonly ranging from 5 to 20 years.

Some states also require payment of property taxes during the period.

Common Scenarios

  • Neighbor builds fence slightly over property line.
  • Improper boundary description in deed.
  • Abandoned property occupied long-term.
  • Rural land disputes.

Adverse Possession vs. Trespassing

  • Trespassing: Unauthorized entry without long-term claim.
  • Adverse Possession: Long-term occupation meeting statutory requirements.

How Adverse Possession Is Resolved

  • Negotiated boundary agreement.
  • Survey correction.
  • Quiet title action to formally establish ownership.
  • Court judgment after litigation.

Impact on Investors

  • Boundary disputes reduce property certainty.
  • May delay sale or refinance.
  • Title insurance may exclude known boundary disputes.
  • Encroachments can impair highest and best use.

Example

A landowner installs a fence 10 feet inside a neighbor’s property line. The neighbor never objects. After 15 years of continuous use (meeting state requirements), the occupying owner may claim title to that strip of land.

Prevention Strategies

  • Regularly inspect property boundaries.
  • Respond quickly to encroachments.
  • Use written permission agreements (licenses).
  • Maintain accurate surveys and legal descriptions.

Investor Checklist

  • Order a current survey before purchase.
  • Review title report for boundary issues.
  • Inspect fencing, driveways, and visible encroachments.
  • Confirm no open possession disputes.
  • Consult counsel if claim risk exists.

Hurdle Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Hurdle Rate

Definition

A hurdle rate is a minimum return threshold in an investment waterfall. Once the investment’s performance exceeds the hurdle, profits may be split into a higher promote tier for the sponsor/GP. Hurdle rates are designed to align incentives by rewarding the GP more only after investors hit defined performance targets.

How Hurdle Rates Work

Most real estate partnerships distribute cash in layers (tiers). Each tier defines who gets paid and in what proportion. Hurdles typically apply to total deal performance, often measured as an IRR or an equity multiple (or both).

Common hurdle measurement methods

  • IRR hurdle: promote increases once investors achieve a target internal rate of return.
  • Equity multiple hurdle: promote increases once investors reach a target multiple (e.g., 1.6x).
  • Hybrid: both must be met, or the waterfall uses multiple tests depending on deal docs.

Hurdle Rate vs. Preferred Return

These terms are related but not the same:

  • Preferred return (pref) is typically the “first claim” on distributable cash to LPs up to a set rate, often accruing if unpaid.
  • Hurdle rate is the performance bar that unlocks the next, more favorable split for the GP (higher promote).

A deal can have a preferred return without multiple promote hurdles, and it can have promote hurdles that are separate from (or in addition to) the pref.

Typical Tier Structure

Waterfalls vary widely, but a common pattern is:

  • Tier 1: return of capital (investors get their contributed equity back).
  • Tier 2: preferred return to LPs (e.g., 7%–9%).
  • Tier 3: split profits until an IRR hurdle is reached (e.g., 80/20 LP/GP).
  • Tier 4: higher promote after the next hurdle (e.g., 70/30 or 60/40).

Why It Matters

Hurdle structure can materially change who earns the upside. In underwriting, you should model the waterfall because the same property cash flows can create different LP returns depending on: hurdle levels, promote percentages, and whether there is a catch-up provision.

Example

A partnership uses a two-tier promote structure measured by IRR:

  • Up to 12% IRR: profits split 80/20 (LP/GP)
  • Above 12% IRR: profits split 70/30 (LP/GP)

If the project performs strongly and crosses the 12% hurdle, the GP earns a higher share of the incremental profits above that threshold. If performance is below 12%, the GP remains in the lower promote tier.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming hurdles equal returns: headline hurdle rates don’t guarantee LP outcomes; fees and leverage matter.
  • Catch-up mechanics: some waterfalls include a catch-up that can accelerate GP payouts after the pref is met.
  • Measurement ambiguity: “IRR hurdle” can be calculated differently based on timing and distributions.
  • Over-optimistic projections: modeling the top promote tier as “base case” can hide downside risk.
  • Misaligned risk: high promotes with low GP co-invest can incentivize risk-taking.

Investor Checklist

  • What metric defines the hurdle? IRR, equity multiple, or both?
  • Is there a catch-up? If yes, how fast and how much?
  • Are hurdles calculated deal-level or investor-level?
  • When do promote tiers apply? ongoing distributions, sale/refi, or both?
  • What fees exist before the waterfall? fees can reduce distributable cash and shift outcomes.

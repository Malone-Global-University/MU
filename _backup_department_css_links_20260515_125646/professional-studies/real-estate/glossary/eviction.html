Eviction

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Eviction

Definition

An eviction is a legal process through which a landlord regains possession of a rental unit from a tenant. Evictions most commonly occur for nonpayment of rent, but can also result from material lease violations (unauthorized occupants, illegal activity, repeated disturbances, etc.). Eviction rules, notice requirements, and timelines vary by jurisdiction.

Why It Matters

Evictions are operationally expensive and usually reflect a breakdown in screening, collections, or resident relations. They can drive:

  • Higher bad debt: unpaid rent and fees often become uncollectible.
  • Legal and admin costs: filings, attorney fees, court costs, staff time.
  • Turnover and downtime: longer vacancy and higher make-ready costs.
  • Property damage risk: distressed move-outs can increase unit damage and trash-outs.
  • Reputation risk: resident experience and reviews can suffer if processes feel unfair or inconsistent.

Common Reasons for Eviction

  • Nonpayment of rent: chronic late payment or extended delinquency.
  • Lease violations: unauthorized occupants, pets, smoking, or prohibited modifications.
  • Nuisance behavior: repeated disturbances, harassment, or safety issues.
  • Illegal activity: where documented and actionable under local law and lease terms.
  • Holdover: tenant remains after lease expiration without renewal (varies by local rules).

Typical Eviction Process (High Level)

While details vary by state/county/city, eviction generally follows a sequence like this:

  1. Notice: a written notice is served (pay-or-quit or cure-or-quit, depending on cause).
  2. Filing: if the issue isn’t resolved, the landlord files an eviction action in court.
  3. Hearing/judgment: the court reviews the case and issues an order.
  4. Writ/possession: if granted, law enforcement may perform the physical set-out if the tenant does not leave.
  5. Turn: unit is secured, inspected, repaired, and released back to market.

Operationally, the biggest drivers of cost are time (rent loss) and severity (damage/trash-out).

How Operators Reduce Eviction Volume

  • Stronger tenant screening: verify income, rental history, and fraud signals.
  • Clear collections timeline: consistent notices and early intervention before balances snowball.
  • Payment flexibility: multiple payment methods and short, documented repayment plans when appropriate.
  • Resident communication: proactive outreach and problem-solving reduces avoidable escalations.
  • Enforce lease rules consistently: predictable enforcement reduces repeat violations.

Underwriting Implications

In underwriting, evictions typically show up through bad debt, legal expense, turn costs, and vacancy (downtime). Elevated eviction volume can be a warning sign of tenant-profile stress or weak management processes. When evaluating a deal, request historical data on:

  • Eviction filings and outcomes
  • Delinquency aging and on-time payment rate
  • Bad debt write-offs
  • Turn time and make-ready costs (especially for eviction turns)

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting too long: delayed action often increases loss severity and makes recovery harder.
  • Inconsistent enforcement: creates fairness issues and weakens the property’s position.
  • Poor documentation: missing ledgers, notices, or incident reports can derail cases.
  • Assuming fast timelines: legal processes can slow during high-volume periods or local changes.

Tax Basis

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Tax Basis

Definition

Tax basis represents the amount of a taxpayer’s investment in property or a partnership for tax purposes. It determines:

  • How much loss you may deduct
  • Whether distributions are taxable
  • The amount of taxable gain or loss upon sale

In simple terms: basis measures how much capital you have “in” the investment for tax tracking.

Why Tax Basis Matters in Real Estate

  • Limits deductible losses
  • Determines capital gain at disposition
  • Impacts depreciation deductions
  • Controls whether cash distributions trigger taxable gain

Initial Basis

Your starting basis generally equals:

  • Cash contributed
  • Plus property contributed (at its adjusted basis)
  • Plus certain debt allocations (in partnerships)

Adjusted Basis Over Time

Basis does not stay static. It changes annually.

Increases to Basis

  • Additional capital contributions
  • Income allocations
  • Allocated partnership liabilities

Decreases to Basis

  • Loss allocations
  • Depreciation deductions
  • Cash distributions

Basis and Loss Deduction Limits

You generally cannot deduct losses in excess of your tax basis.

Even if you have an economic loss, it may be:

  • Limited by basis rules
  • Further limited by at-risk rules
  • Further limited by passive activity loss rules

Example

An investor contributes $100,000 to a real estate partnership.

  • Year 1 allocated loss: ($40,000)
  • Year 2 allocated loss: ($70,000)

At the end of Year 1, basis becomes $60,000.

In Year 2, only $60,000 of the $70,000 loss may be deductible under basis rules. The remaining $10,000 is suspended.

Basis and Distributions

Cash distributions reduce basis.

  • If distribution is less than basis → typically non-taxable return of capital.
  • If distribution exceeds basis → excess is generally taxable as gain.

Basis at Sale

Taxable gain is generally calculated as:

  • Sale Price − Adjusted Basis = Taxable Gain

Because depreciation reduces basis over time, it increases taxable gain and may trigger depreciation recapture.

Inside Basis vs. Outside Basis (Partnerships)

  • Inside basis: The partnership’s basis in its assets.
  • Outside basis: The investor’s basis in their partnership interest.

In syndications, investors primarily track outside basis.

Basis vs. Capital Account

A K-1 shows a capital account balance, but this is not always identical to tax basis. Differences may arise from:

  • Debt allocations
  • Book vs. tax accounting differences
  • Section 704(b) adjustments

Investor Checklist

  • Track basis annually (especially in partnerships)
  • Understand how debt allocations affect basis
  • Monitor distributions relative to basis
  • Account for depreciation impact on sale gain
  • Coordinate basis tracking with CPA

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing capital account with tax basis
  • Ignoring basis reductions from depreciation
  • Overlooking gain triggered by distributions exceeding basis
  • Failing to track suspended losses

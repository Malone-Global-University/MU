Turnover Rate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Turnover Rate

Definition

Turnover rate is the percentage of rental units that become vacant and require re-leasing during a specific period (typically annually). It measures tenant movement within a property and directly affects vacancy, operating costs, and net operating income (NOI).

Formula

  • Turnover Rate (%) = (Number of move-outs during period ÷ Total units) × 100

Example

A 200-unit apartment property experiences 60 move-outs in one year.

  • Turnover Rate = (60 ÷ 200) × 100 = 30%

Why Turnover Rate Matters

  • Vacancy impact: More turnover typically increases downtime between leases.
  • Make-ready costs: Cleaning, painting, repairs, and upgrades add expense.
  • Leasing costs: Advertising, concessions, and broker commissions may apply.
  • Revenue disruption: Lost rent during unit turns reduces effective gross income.
  • Operational strain: High turnover increases management workload.

Typical Turnover Benchmarks

  • Stabilized multifamily: 35%–55% annually (varies by market and asset class)
  • Student housing: Often higher due to academic cycles
  • Single-family rentals: Often lower due to longer tenancy
  • Office/retail: Measured differently (lease expiration-based rather than annual move-outs)

Financial Impact Example

If average make-ready cost per unit is $2,000 and 60 units turn:

  • Turn costs = 60 × $2,000 = $120,000

Additional vacancy loss during re-leasing further impacts NOI.

Causes of High Turnover

  • Below-market amenities or outdated finishes
  • Poor property management or maintenance response
  • Aggressive rent increases
  • Market competition and new supply deliveries
  • Short-term lease structures

Strategies to Reduce Turnover

  • Strong resident engagement and retention programs
  • Preventative maintenance
  • Balanced rent growth strategy
  • Renewal incentives vs. turnover costs comparison
  • Improved amenities and community experience

Turnover vs. Vacancy Rate

  • Turnover rate: Measures tenant movement frequency.
  • Vacancy rate: Measures unoccupied units at a given time.

A property can have high turnover but low vacancy if units are quickly re-leased.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Model realistic turnover assumptions by asset type.
  • Include make-ready and leasing costs in operating expenses.
  • Account for downtime between leases.
  • Stress-test renewal rates in competitive markets.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating turn costs.
  • Assuming renewal rates will remain constant after rent increases.
  • Failing to analyze seasonality.
  • Ignoring market supply pipeline effects.

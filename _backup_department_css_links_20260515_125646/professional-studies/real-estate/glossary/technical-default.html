Technical Default

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Technical Default

Definition

A technical default is a non-monetary default that occurs when a borrower violates a requirement in a loan agreement, even if they are still making payments on time. It typically involves a breach of covenants, reporting obligations, or other contractual terms.

Common Triggers

  • Financial covenant breach: DSCR, debt yield, LTV, or liquidity requirements fall below thresholds.
  • Late or missing reporting: annual financial statements, rent rolls, operating statements, or compliance certificates not delivered on time.
  • Insurance lapse: required coverages not maintained or proof not provided.
  • Unauthorized transfers: changes in ownership/control without lender consent.
  • Prohibited additional debt: new liens or financing in violation of the loan documents.
  • Property condition / compliance issues: failure to maintain the asset or address code violations where required by the contract.

Why It Matters

Technical default matters because it can give the lender remedies long before a payment default occurs. That can include higher interest (default rate), added reserves, cash management controls, or even acceleration—depending on the documents.

Typical Lender Remedies

  • Notice of default and a cure period.
  • Default interest or late fees (as specified in the loan).
  • Cash management changes (lockbox, springing lockbox, or cash sweep).
  • Reserve requirements for taxes, insurance, capital items, or tenant improvements.
  • Acceleration of the loan balance (in severe or uncured cases).

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting: stress-test your downside case against covenant thresholds.
  • Asset management: create a compliance calendar for reporting and insurance renewals.
  • Refi planning: avoid covenant breaches that can spook lenders and delay execution.
  • Negotiation: request reasonable cure periods and flexibility for seasonal NOI dips.

Example

A loan requires a minimum DSCR of 1.25x, tested quarterly. Due to vacancy and concessions, DSCR falls to 1.18x. Even if the borrower makes every payment, the covenant breach can trigger a technical default and allow the lender to require a cash sweep or additional reserves until DSCR recovers.

Common Pitfalls

  • Not tracking deadlines: missing reporting dates is one of the easiest ways to default.
  • Assuming “payments current” means “in compliance”: covenant breaches still matter.
  • Weak documentation: failure to keep organized insurance certificates, invoices, and compliance records.
  • Ignoring early warning signals: covenant headroom shrinking is a prompt to act, not a trivia point.

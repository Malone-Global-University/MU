Ground-Up Development

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Ground-Up Development

Definition

Ground-Up Development is the process of constructing a new building from raw land or after demolishing an existing structure. It involves site acquisition, entitlement, design, financing, and full-scale construction.

In simple terms: building a property from scratch.

Key Phases of Ground-Up Development

1. Site Acquisition

  • Land purchase or option agreement
  • Preliminary feasibility analysis

2. Entitlement & Zoning

  • Zoning verification or rezoning
  • Environmental review
  • Site plan approval
  • Permitting

3. Design & Planning

  • Architectural plans
  • Engineering drawings
  • Construction budgeting

4. Financing

  • Construction loan
  • Equity contributions
  • Interest reserves

5. Construction

  • Site work and foundation
  • Vertical construction
  • Inspections and approvals

6. Lease-Up or Sale

  • Marketing and tenant placement
  • Stabilization
  • Refinance or disposition

Why Developers Choose Ground-Up

  • Maximize design flexibility
  • Build to highest and best use
  • Modern building systems and efficiencies
  • Premium rents for new construction

Ground-Up vs. Adaptive Reuse

  • Ground-Up: New construction from land.
  • Adaptive Reuse: Repurposes an existing structure.

Ground-up projects often have higher construction risk but fewer unknown structural issues.

Risks

  • Entitlement delays
  • Construction cost overruns
  • Interest rate volatility
  • Lease-up risk
  • Market downturn before stabilization

Financial Structure

  • Higher leverage during construction phase
  • Interest-only construction financing
  • Equity typically absorbs first loss
  • Refinance into permanent loan after stabilization

Impact on Returns

  • Higher projected IRR due to development spread
  • Increased volatility compared to stabilized assets
  • Greater sensitivity to cap rate changes

Example Scenario

A developer acquires a vacant parcel in a growing suburban market and builds a 250-unit multifamily community. After 24 months of construction and 12 months of lease-up, the property reaches stabilization and is refinanced based on increased value.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm zoning and entitlement feasibility.
  • Stress-test construction budget and contingency reserves.
  • Model conservative lease-up assumptions.
  • Analyze exit cap rate sensitivity.
  • Evaluate sponsor track record.

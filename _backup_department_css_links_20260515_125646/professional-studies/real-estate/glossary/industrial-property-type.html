Industrial (Property Type)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Industrial (Property Type)

Definition

Industrial is a commercial real estate property type used for manufacturing, warehousing, storage, logistics, distribution, and light industrial operations. Industrial properties are typically leased to businesses rather than individual consumers.

Common Industrial Subtypes

  • Warehouse / Distribution: Large buildings used for storage and shipping.
  • Bulk Distribution: High-clear height facilities near major transportation hubs.
  • Light Industrial: Smaller facilities for assembly or limited manufacturing.
  • Flex Industrial: Combination of warehouse and office space.
  • Cold Storage: Temperature-controlled facilities for food or pharmaceuticals.

Key Physical Features

  • Clear height (e.g., 24–40+ feet)
  • Dock-high and grade-level loading doors
  • Truck court depth and circulation
  • Proximity to highways, ports, rail, or airports
  • Power capacity and floor load strength

Lease Structures

  • Triple Net (NNN): Common in industrial; tenants pay most operating costs.
  • Long-term leases: Often 5–10+ years.
  • Built-to-Suit: Designed specifically for a tenant’s operational needs.

Performance Drivers

  • E-commerce growth
  • Supply chain reconfiguration
  • Population growth and consumption patterns
  • Transportation infrastructure access
  • Land availability constraints

Risks

  • Tenant concentration: Single-tenant exposure in large facilities.
  • Functional obsolescence: Low clear height or poor logistics layout.
  • Economic sensitivity: Manufacturing and trade cycles.
  • Zoning and environmental issues: Industrial use restrictions.

Example

A 500,000-square-foot bulk distribution center near a major port leased to a national logistics provider under a 10-year NNN lease may offer stable cash flow with limited operating expense exposure.

Investor Applications

  • Location analysis: Evaluate proximity to transportation corridors.
  • Lease rollover review: Monitor single-tenant expiration risk.
  • Clear height benchmarking: Compare to market standards.
  • Supply pipeline assessment: Track new industrial deliveries.

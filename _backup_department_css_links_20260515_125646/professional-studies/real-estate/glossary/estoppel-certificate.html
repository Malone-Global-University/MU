Estoppel Certificate

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Estoppel Certificate

Definition

An estoppel certificate is a signed statement by a tenant confirming key facts about a lease, including rent amount, lease term, security deposits, and whether any defaults exist.

It prevents the tenant from later contradicting the confirmed information.

Why It Matters

  • Provides verification during property sales.
  • Supports lender underwriting during refinancing.
  • Reduces risk of undisclosed lease disputes.
  • Confirms income stability for valuation purposes.

Common Information Included

  • Lease commencement and expiration dates.
  • Base rent and additional rent.
  • Security deposit amount.
  • Options to renew or expand.
  • Outstanding landlord obligations.
  • Existing defaults (if any).

When It Is Used

  • Commercial property acquisition.
  • Loan refinancing.
  • CMBS securitization.
  • Portfolio sale transactions.

Example

A buyer is acquiring an office building. The lease states rent is $25 per square foot. The estoppel certificate confirms the tenant is paying that rent and has no claims against the landlord. This reduces uncertainty in the transaction.

Legal Effect

Once signed, the tenant is generally prevented (estopped) from asserting facts inconsistent with the certificate, absent fraud or mistake.

Risk Considerations

  • Incomplete disclosure may expose seller liability.
  • Tenant refusal to sign may delay closing.
  • Conflicts between lease and estoppel language.

Investor Checklist

  • Require estoppels from major tenants.
  • Compare estoppel to lease agreement.
  • Confirm rent roll consistency.
  • Address discrepancies before closing.

Estoppel vs SNDA

  • Estoppel Certificate: Confirms lease facts.
  • SNDA Agreement: Governs lease rights in foreclosure.

Economic Occupancy

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Economic Occupancy

Definition

Economic occupancy measures the percentage of a property’s potential rental income that is actually collected.

Unlike physical occupancy, which measures how many units are occupied, economic occupancy reflects real income performance after accounting for vacancy, concessions, and bad debt.

Formula

A common formula is:

  • Economic Occupancy = Collected Rental Income ÷ Gross Potential Rent (GPR)

It may also be calculated using Gross Operating Income (GOI) divided by total potential rental income, depending on reporting standards.

Why It Matters

  • Reveals income efficiency, not just filled units.
  • Captures the impact of concessions and non-paying tenants.
  • Signals operational strength or weakness.
  • Directly affects NOI and valuation.

Two properties may both show 95% physical occupancy, but if one offers heavy concessions or suffers bad debt, its economic occupancy may be significantly lower.

Physical vs. Economic Occupancy

  • Physical Occupancy: % of units leased or occupied.
  • Economic Occupancy: % of rent actually collected.

Example:

  • 100-unit property
  • 95 units leased (95% physical occupancy)
  • 10 tenants receiving free rent concessions
  • 5 tenants delinquent on payments

Even with 95% physical occupancy, economic occupancy may fall closer to 88–90%, depending on the dollar impact.

Common Causes of Low Economic Occupancy

  • Excessive rent concessions (free months, discounts)
  • High delinquency or bad debt
  • Under-market leases
  • Frequent turnover
  • Lease-up instability

Impact on Valuation

Economic occupancy directly impacts GOI, which flows into NOI. Since income-based valuation uses NOI divided by a cap rate, even small changes in economic occupancy can materially change value.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Analyze trailing 12-month collections (T-12).
  • Separate temporary lease-up concessions from structural weakness.
  • Normalize for one-time delinquency spikes.
  • Stress-test assumptions in downside scenarios.

Investor Insight

Strong operators focus on economic occupancy — not just “leased units.” Yield creators increase income durability by:

  • Reducing bad debt
  • Managing concessions strategically
  • Improving tenant screening
  • Maintaining rent discipline

Related Terms

  • Physical occupancy
  • Gross potential rent (GPR)
  • Gross operating income (GOI)
  • Net operating income (NOI)
  • Vacancy
  • Loss to lease
  • Effective rent

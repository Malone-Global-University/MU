Normal Wear and Tear

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Normal Wear and Tear

Definition

Normal wear and tear is the expected, gradual deterioration of a rental unit or property that occurs from ordinary use over time. It is different from damage, which is deterioration caused by negligence, abuse, misuse, or unauthorized alterations.

This distinction matters because many landlord-tenant rules (and most leases) allow a landlord to charge for damage but not for normal wear and tear when accounting for a tenant’s security deposit.

Why It Matters

  • Security deposits: supports fair, defensible deposit deductions and reduces disputes.
  • Turn costs: helps forecast unit make-ready budgets and staffing needs.
  • Asset value: informs capital planning for replacements and life-cycle maintenance.
  • Resident experience: clarifies expectations at move-in and move-out.

Normal Wear vs. Damage

The line depends on severity, cause, and time in possession. A helpful test is: “Would this likely happen with responsible use?”

Common examples of normal wear and tear

  • Minor scuffs or small nail holes in walls from hanging pictures.
  • Faded paint or carpet from sunlight and ordinary foot traffic.
  • Loose door handles or cabinet hardware from regular use.
  • Lightly worn grout, caulk, or finishes in kitchens and baths.
  • Worn appliance knobs or minor scratches consistent with age and use.

Common examples of damage

  • Large holes in walls, broken doors, or ripped-off hinges.
  • Crayon/marker writing, heavy stains, or smoke burns on carpet.
  • Broken windows, missing fixtures, or vandalized surfaces.
  • Pet damage beyond minor hair/odor (e.g., urine-soaked flooring, chewed trim).
  • Unauthorized paint colors or unapproved modifications that require remediation.

How Operators Handle It

  • Set expectations at move-in: provide a condition checklist and photo documentation.
  • Use consistent standards: apply the same thresholds for all residents and unit types.
  • Account for useful life: consider age of paint, carpet, and appliances when charging for damage.
  • Itemize clearly: use detailed move-out statements (what, why, and how much) with supporting photos.
  • Train teams: align leasing, maintenance, and managers on definitions and documentation habits.

Underwriting Implications

In underwriting, normal wear and tear is part of routine operating expense and turn cost assumptions. If a property has high turnover, older finishes, or heavy resident traffic, expect higher make-ready costs and faster replacement cycles — even with excellent property management.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overcharging for normal wear: increases disputes, bad reviews, and legal exposure.
  • Inconsistent enforcement: creates fairness issues and weakens your documentation position.
  • Skipping documentation: makes legitimate damage harder to prove.
  • Ignoring age: charging full replacement cost for items already near end-of-life.

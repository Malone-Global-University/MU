General Partner (GP)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary General Partner (GP)

Definition

A General Partner (GP) is the managing partner in a real estate investment who is responsible for finding the opportunity, structuring the deal, raising capital, obtaining financing, and executing the business plan. In a typical GP/LP structure, the GP makes key decisions and is accountable to investors for performance and compliance.

What the GP Actually Does

The GP is the “operator” side of the partnership. Responsibilities vary by deal, but commonly include:

  • Sourcing & underwriting: identify the asset, validate assumptions, and set the plan.
  • Structuring: choose legal structure, equity terms, waterfalls, and governance rights.
  • Financing: secure debt terms, manage lender requirements, and oversee covenants.
  • Asset management: oversee property management, budgets, capex, and KPI reporting.
  • Execution: renovations, leasing strategy, revenue optimization, and expense control.
  • Reporting & compliance: investor communications, distributions, tax packages, and filings.
  • Disposition: time the exit, run the sale process, and manage closing.

GP vs. LP

Most syndicated deals separate roles:

  • Limited Partners (LPs) contribute most of the equity, receive passive returns, and typically have limited voting rights.
  • General Partners (GPs) contribute expertise and management, control major decisions, and usually earn fees plus a share of upside for performance.

How GPs Are Compensated

Compensation is deal-specific, but the GP’s economics commonly include a mix of fees and promote (performance-based profit share).

Common GP compensation components

  • Acquisition fee: paid at closing to cover sourcing, underwriting, and execution work.
  • Asset management fee: ongoing fee for oversight and reporting.
  • Property management fee: if GP (or affiliate) runs on-site operations.
  • Construction/renovation management fee: if GP manages capex or major projects.
  • Disposition fee: paid upon sale for running the exit process (not always present).
  • Promote / carried interest: GP’s share of profits above a return threshold to LPs.

Why It Matters

In underwriting, the GP is often the largest execution variable. A strong GP can create value through operations, renovations, leasing strategy, and capital markets decisions. A weak GP can destroy returns even in a good market through poor controls, misaligned incentives, or bad risk management.

Investor Due Diligence Checklist

  • Track record: realized exits, not just projected IRRs.
  • Alignment: GP co-invest, fee levels, and whether fees are tied to performance.
  • Decision rights: what requires LP consent (refinance, sale, budget overages, related-party work).
  • Reporting quality: frequency, transparency, and KPI consistency.
  • Controls: approvals, vendor selection, segregation of duties, and bank account access.
  • Key-person risk: what happens if the lead operator leaves or becomes unavailable.

Example

A GP syndicates an apartment acquisition. LPs contribute most of the equity. The GP secures debt, hires the property manager, manages a renovation program, and reports monthly performance. If the deal exceeds the preferred return hurdle, profits above that threshold are split so the GP earns a promote. If the deal underperforms, LP returns may fall while the GP may still earn certain fees—so the fee and promote structure matters.

Common Pitfalls

  • Fee-first structures: high fees with low co-invest can reduce alignment.
  • Overpromising returns: aggressive rent growth or cap-rate assumptions masking execution risk.
  • Related-party conflicts: GP-controlled vendors or management without clear oversight.
  • Weak governance: unclear LP rights, reporting standards, or removal provisions.
  • Underestimating operational complexity: renovations, insurance, taxes, and compliance.

Operating Statement

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Operating Statement

Definition

An operating statement is a property-level financial report that summarizes operating income and operating expenses over a period of time, typically ending with Net Operating Income (NOI). Investors use operating statements to understand how a property actually performs (not just how it is marketed).

What an Operating Statement Usually Includes

Operating statements vary by owner and property manager, but most include the same core sections:

1) Income

  • Rental income: market rent, actual collected rent, other rent (garage, storage)
  • Other income: pet fees, RUBS/utility reimbursements, application fees, late fees
  • Vacancy / credit loss (sometimes shown as a reduction to income)

2) Operating Expenses

  • Taxes and insurance
  • Utilities (owner-paid portions)
  • Repairs & maintenance and contract services
  • Payroll (if staffed onsite)
  • Property management fees
  • Administrative / marketing / legal (routine operating-level)

3) Net Operating Income (NOI)

  • NOI = Effective Gross Income (EGI) − Operating Expenses

Many statements also show additional items like CapEx, debt service, or owner distributions, but these are typically below NOI for underwriting purposes.

Common Formats You’ll See

  • T-12 (Trailing 12 Months): last 12 months of actual performance, usually month-by-month.
  • YTD (Year-to-Date): performance from January 1 to the most recent month-end.
  • Budget vs Actual: compares plan to reality; useful for identifying variance drivers.
  • Monthly operating report: month and YTD totals; often paired with rent roll and delinquency.

Why It Matters

  • Underwriting reality check: validates income quality and expense run-rate.
  • NOI drives value: most valuation approaches begin with NOI (cap rate, DCF inputs).
  • Detects hidden risk: concessions, bad debt, deferred maintenance, expense spikes.
  • Shows operational opportunity: where margins can expand through better management.

Example

A simplified annual operating statement might look like this:

  • Gross Potential Rent: $1,000,000
  • Other Income: $60,000
  • Less Vacancy/Credit Loss: ($80,000)
  • Effective Gross Income (EGI): $980,000
  • Operating Expenses: ($420,000)
  • NOI: $560,000

If operating expenses were understated by $30,000, NOI would be overstated by $30,000—potentially leading to an inflated valuation and a bad acquisition decision.

How Investors Read an Operating Statement

  • Normalize income: strip one-time items, check concessions and bad debt, verify collections.
  • Normalize expenses: identify non-recurring repairs, confirm taxes/insurance are current.
  • Compare against benchmarks: per unit, per square foot, and expense ratio vs comps.
  • Look for timing distortions: seasonal utilities, annual insurance bills, tax true-ups.
  • Cross-check with other docs: rent roll, bank statements, invoices, utility bills, GL detail.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing “scheduled” with “collected”: rent roll may look great while collections lag.
  • Underwriting T-12 without adjustments: owner may be deferring maintenance or underpaying management.
  • Missing post-sale tax reset: taxes often change after acquisition depending on jurisdiction.
  • Double-counting reserves: treating turns as OpEx and also including a CapEx reserve.
  • Inconsistent accounting: category mapping varies across managers; always request GL detail when needed.

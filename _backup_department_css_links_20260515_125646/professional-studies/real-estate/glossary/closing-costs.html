Closing Costs

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Closing Costs

Definition

Closing costs are the transaction expenses paid at settlement to complete a real estate purchase or sale. They include costs required to transfer title, verify ownership, document the deal, and (when applicable) originate financing.

What Closing Costs Typically Include

Exact items vary by state, asset type, and deal structure, but common categories include:

Buyer-side costs (common)

  • Title & escrow: title search, title insurance, escrow/settlement fees, recording fees.
  • Legal & diligence: attorney fees, surveys, zoning/ALTA, inspections, environmental reports.
  • Lender & financing fees (if borrowing): origination, underwriting, appraisal, lender legal, processing.
  • Prepaids & escrows: insurance premiums, prorated taxes, reserve/impound deposits, interest from closing to month-end.
  • Third-party: property condition reports, engineering, capitalization/lease audits (common in commercial deals).

Seller-side costs (common)

  • Broker commissions: listing and buyer agent fees (often the largest seller cost).
  • Transfer taxes / excise taxes: where applicable and depending on local custom.
  • Title curative items: payoff fees, lien releases, document preparation, recording.
  • Negotiated credits: repair credits, tenant/proration adjustments, concessions.

How It’s Modeled in Underwriting

Investors usually treat closing costs as part of the total project cost and therefore part of the cash invested (equity), especially when they are paid out-of-pocket at closing. Understating closing costs inflates projected returns like cash-on-cash return and IRR.

Rule-of-thumb estimates (use with caution)

  • All-cash purchase: often lower (title/escrow, legal, diligence, recording).
  • Financed purchase: typically higher due to lender fees, appraisal, lender counsel, and reserves.
  • Commercial / multifamily: diligence and lender legal can be meaningful line items.

Why It Matters

Closing costs directly reduce investor returns by increasing the basis (total dollars into the deal) without increasing income. They also affect liquidity: a deal can look strong on NOI but still require more cash at close than expected.

Example

A property is purchased for $5,000,000. The buyer pays $110,000 in title/escrow, $65,000 in legal/diligence, and $125,000 in lender fees and reserves funded at close. Total closing-related cash outlay is:

  • Total Closing Costs & Funding = 110,000 + 65,000 + 125,000 = $300,000

If the buyer’s equity contribution was $1,500,000, closing costs raise total cash invested to $1,800,000—which lowers cash-on-cash return if annual cash flow is unchanged.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mixing “closing costs” with “prepaids” incorrectly: some prepaids are timing shifts, not true expenses.
  • Forgetting lender escrows/reserves: these can be large and are often required at closing.
  • Ignoring transfer taxes: can be material in certain jurisdictions and may be negotiable.
  • Underestimating legal and third-party: especially on distressed assets, complex title, or heavy tenant issues.
  • Not modeling sale-side costs: broker commissions and taxes impact net sale proceeds and IRR.

Definition

A trust is a legal arrangement in which one party (the trustee) holds and manages property for the benefit of another party (the beneficiary). In real estate, trusts are commonly used for estate planning, probate avoidance, asset protection, and ownership structuring.

Key Parties

  • Grantor (or Settlor): The person who creates the trust and transfers assets into it.
  • Trustee: The individual or institution responsible for managing the trust property.
  • Beneficiary: The person or entity entitled to benefit from the trust assets.

Common Types of Trusts

  • Revocable Living Trust: Can be modified or revoked during the grantor’s lifetime; often used to avoid probate.
  • Irrevocable Trust: Generally cannot be changed once established; may offer asset protection or tax advantages.
  • Land Trust: Holds title to real estate while keeping beneficiary identity private (in some jurisdictions).
  • Testamentary Trust: Created through a will and activated upon death.

Why Trusts Matter in Real Estate

  • Probate avoidance: Property held in trust typically bypasses probate.
  • Continuity of ownership: Allows seamless transfer upon death or incapacity.
  • Privacy: Some trusts reduce public disclosure of ownership.
  • Estate planning: Controls how property is distributed.
  • Asset protection (limited cases): Certain structures may shield assets.

Example

An investor transfers rental properties into a revocable living trust. Upon the investor’s death, the successor trustee distributes the assets according to the trust terms—avoiding probate and reducing delays.

Trust vs. LLC

  • Trust: Estate planning and transfer mechanism.
  • LLC: Business entity providing liability protection.

In practice, investors often hold LLC membership interests inside a trust.

Common Pitfalls

  • Failure to fund the trust: Property must be formally transferred into the trust.
  • Ignoring lender requirements: Some loans require notification of transfer.
  • Assuming liability protection: Not all trusts shield personal liability.
  • Outdated documents: Trust terms should be periodically reviewed.

Investor Applications

  • Estate planning: Structure inheritance of investment properties.
  • Ownership structuring: Combine trusts and LLCs for layered planning.
  • Succession planning: Maintain operational continuity.
  • Tax coordination: Align with estate and gift planning strategies.

Notice to Enter

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Notice to Enter

Definition

A notice to enter is a written (or otherwise documented) notice a landlord provides to a tenant stating the landlord’s intent to enter the rental unit for a lawful purpose—such as repairs, inspections, or showings. Most jurisdictions require advance notice except in emergencies.

Why Notices Matter

A proper notice to enter supports a landlord’s right of entry while also respecting tenant privacy and quiet enjoyment. Operationally, it reduces disputes, improves cooperation, and creates a clear record if an entry is later challenged.

Common Reasons to Issue a Notice to Enter

  • Maintenance and repairs: responding to work orders, safety issues, preventative maintenance.
  • Routine inspections: unit condition checks, safety checks, compliance inspections.
  • Showings: to prospective tenants, buyers, or lenders (often with limits).
  • Appraisals and lender visits: scheduled access for financing or refinancing.
  • Pre-move-out walkthroughs: when allowed, to set expectations and reduce turn friction.

What a Strong Notice Usually Includes

  • Date of notice and method of delivery (email, portal, posting, etc.).
  • Purpose of entry (specific and legitimate).
  • Date and time window for entry (reasonable hours, reasonable duration).
  • Who will enter (staff name/company, vendor name if known).
  • Contact information for questions or rescheduling (when feasible).
  • Tenant instructions (pets secured, access to breaker panel, etc.).

Notice Period and “Reasonable Time”

The required notice period varies by state and city, and the lease may add additional requirements. Outside of true emergencies, best practice is to provide notice far enough in advance that the resident can prepare. Entries should occur at reasonable times and be limited to the stated purpose.

Emergencies

In genuine emergencies (active leaks, smoke/fire, suspected gas issues, immediate safety hazards), landlords may be allowed to enter without advance notice. Even then, operators should document what happened and notify the resident as soon as practical.

Operational Best Practices

  • Standardize templates: consistent notices reduce errors and resident complaints.
  • Use a clear window: e.g., “between 10:00 a.m. and 12:00 p.m.” rather than “sometime tomorrow.”
  • Confirm identity: staff and vendors should carry identification and announce entry.
  • Document outcomes: work completed, photos (when appropriate), and follow-up plan.
  • Avoid over-entry: frequent entries can appear harassing and create legal risk.

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague purpose: “inspection” without detail can trigger resident pushback.
  • Too short notice: increases disputes and may violate local requirements.
  • Vendor drift: contractors arriving outside the window or entering without proper notice.
  • Scope creep: entering for one purpose and then conducting unrelated inspections.

Core-Plus

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Core-Plus

Definition

Core-Plus is a real estate investment strategy that targets stabilized or near-stabilized assets with modest operational, leasing, or capital improvement upside. It sits between core and value-add on the risk spectrum.

Typical Characteristics

  • High but not perfect occupancy (e.g., 85–95%).
  • Minor deferred maintenance or light renovation needs.
  • Some lease rollover or mark-to-market opportunity.
  • Stable location with proven demand fundamentals.
  • Limited structural or entitlement risk.

Risk and Return Profile

Core-plus investments generally offer slightly higher return targets than core due to modest execution risk. Income remains an important return driver, but investors expect incremental appreciation from business plan execution.

Return Drivers

  • Rent growth: capturing below-market rents.
  • Light renovations: cosmetic upgrades improving pricing power.
  • Operational efficiencies: expense management or improved leasing strategy.
  • Improved financing terms: refinancing after stabilization improvements.

How It Differs from Core

Unlike pure core investments, core-plus assets often require active asset management to unlock value. There may be mild lease-up, capex programs, or operational repositioning.

How It Differs from Value-Add

Core-plus assets do not typically involve heavy renovation, major repositioning, or significant vacancy risk. The business plan is incremental rather than transformational.

Example

An investor acquires a 90% occupied suburban office building with slightly below-market rents and outdated common areas. The plan includes refreshing the lobby and renewing leases at market rates over time. This is typically considered a core-plus investment.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overestimating rent upside: assuming market growth without proof.
  • Underbudgeting light capex: cosmetic projects can expand in scope.
  • Interest rate sensitivity: moderate leverage increases refinance risk.
  • Lease rollover clustering: creating short-term volatility.

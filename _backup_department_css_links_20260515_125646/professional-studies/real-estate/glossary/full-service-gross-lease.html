Full Service Gross (FSG) Lease

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Full Service Gross Lease

Definition

A Full Service Gross (FSG) Lease is a commercial lease structure in which the tenant pays a single, all-inclusive rent amount, and the landlord is responsible for paying most or all operating expenses of the property.

In simple terms: the tenant pays one lump-sum rent, and the landlord covers property expenses.

Typical Expenses Covered by the Landlord

  • Property taxes
  • Insurance
  • Common Area Maintenance (CAM)
  • Utilities (sometimes including in-suite electricity)
  • Janitorial services
  • Property management fees

Base Year Expense Adjustments

Although FSG leases appear fully inclusive, many include a base year stop. This allows the landlord to recover increases in operating expenses above the base year.

Example

  • Base year operating expenses: $12.00 per SF
  • Year 2 operating expenses: $13.50 per SF
  • Tenant space: 10,000 SF

Increase = $1.50 per SF
Tenant reimbursement = 10,000 × $1.50 = $15,000

This adjustment mechanism protects the landlord against inflation.

Common Property Types Using FSG Leases

  • Office buildings
  • Medical office buildings
  • Government-leased properties

FSG vs. NNN Lease

  • FSG Lease: Landlord pays most operating expenses (with possible base year stop).
  • Triple Net (NNN) Lease: Tenant pays taxes, insurance, and operating expenses directly.

Advantages for Tenants

  • Predictable monthly payments.
  • Less administrative burden.
  • Reduced exposure to unexpected cost spikes (within base year).

Advantages for Landlords

  • Simplified marketing (“one rent number”).
  • Competitive positioning in office markets.
  • Opportunity to control expense management directly.

Investor Considerations

  • Expense inflation risk if base year stops are weak.
  • Need for accurate expense forecasting.
  • Impact on NOI if costs rise faster than recoveries.
  • Importance of gross-up provisions for partially leased buildings.

Gross-Up Clauses

A gross-up provision adjusts base year expenses to reflect stabilized occupancy, preventing artificially low base year numbers.

Common Pitfalls

  • Incorrect base year expense calculations.
  • Failure to include expense escalations in underwriting.
  • Misalignment between lease language and pro forma assumptions.
  • Underestimating management and utility costs.

Underwriting Example

An office building with FSG leases generates $2,000,000 in gross rent. Operating expenses total $900,000. If expenses rise to $1,050,000 and base year recoveries are insufficient, NOI could decline significantly.

Investor Checklist

  • Review base year documentation for each tenant.
  • Confirm gross-up provisions exist.
  • Model expense growth conservatively.
  • Compare lease recoveries to historical expenses.
  • Evaluate tenant credit strength for reimbursement reliability.

Related Terms

  • Triple Net Lease (NNN)
  • Expense Stop
  • Common Area Maintenance (CAM)
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)
  • Modified Gross Lease
  • Base Rent

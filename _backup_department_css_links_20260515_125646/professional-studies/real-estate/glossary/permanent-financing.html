Permanent Financing

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Permanent Financing

Definition

Permanent financing (often called perm debt) is long-term mortgage financing used to fund a stabilized property. It is typically structured with multi-year terms, regular payments, and some degree of amortization. Permanent financing is commonly used to replace short-term debt (like a bridge loan or construction loan) once the asset reaches target occupancy and income.

Permanent Financing vs. Bridge Financing

These two loan types are often paired in a value-add or lease-up strategy:

  • Bridge loan: short-term, higher rate, more flexible, designed for transition (renovation/lease-up).
  • Permanent loan: long-term, usually cheaper, designed for stable cash-flowing assets.

Common Features

  • Longer term: often multi-year terms designed for hold periods and stability.
  • Amortization: commonly includes principal paydown (some options can be interest-only).
  • Underwritten to in-place cash flow: lenders emphasize stabilized NOI and durability.
  • Constraints: loan sizing usually governed by DSCR and/or LTV (and sometimes debt yield).
  • Prepayment rules: can include yield maintenance, defeasance, or other prepayment penalties.

How Loan Size Is Determined

Permanent lenders primarily care about the property’s ability to service debt from stable income. As a result, the loan amount is typically limited by one or more tests:

  • DSCR constraint: cash flow must cover debt service by a minimum ratio.
  • LTV constraint: loan amount limited as a percentage of appraised value.
  • Debt yield: lender requires a minimum NOI-to-loan ratio in some programs.

Why It Matters

Permanent financing affects both risk and returns. It can lower interest costs, reduce refinance uncertainty, and improve cash flow predictability. But it can also introduce constraints (covenants, reserves, prepayment penalties) that reduce flexibility.

Investor Uses

  • Stabilize and de-risk: take out bridge debt after execution to lock long-term terms.
  • Return of capital: refinance into perm debt to return equity (cash-out refi) while continuing to own.
  • Lower cost of capital: reduce interest expense and improve cash-on-cash returns.
  • Match hold strategy: align financing duration with the intended hold period and exit strategy.

Example

An investor buys a value-add multifamily property with a short-term bridge loan to fund renovations and lease-up. After 18 months, the property reaches stabilized occupancy and NOI. The investor refinances into permanent financing with amortization, locking a long-term rate and replacing the higher-cost bridge loan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Refinance risk: assuming you can “just refi” without meeting DSCR/LTV hurdles at takeout.
  • Overstating stabilized NOI: perm lenders scrutinize in-place income, concessions, and expense assumptions.
  • Ignoring prepayment penalties: yield maintenance/defeasance can make early sale expensive.
  • Not matching term to business plan: a long-term loan may reduce flexibility if you plan a short hold.

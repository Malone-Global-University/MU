Variance

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Variance

Definition

A variance is a discretionary land-use approval that grants relief from a specific zoning requirement when strict compliance would create an unreasonable hardship due to unique property conditions. Variances do not typically change the allowed use of land; instead, they modify the standards that govern how development fits on the site (setbacks, height limits, lot coverage, parking, landscaping, etc.).

What Variances Usually Apply To

  • Setbacks: reduced front/side/rear yard requirements.
  • Height: minor increases to building height limits (jurisdiction-dependent).
  • Lot coverage / FAR: permission to exceed certain bulk or coverage standards.
  • Parking: reduced required stalls, alternative parking layouts, or dimensional relief.
  • Signage: sign height/area/location relief.
  • Buffers/landscaping: adjustments due to site shape, utilities, or constraints.

Variance vs. Rezoning vs. Conditional Use

  • Variance: relief from a standard (how you build).
  • Rezoning: changes the zoning designation (what you can build/use, and often intensity).
  • Conditional/Special use: allows a use with conditions after review (a permitted use, but not by-right).

How a Variance Is Evaluated

Requirements vary by jurisdiction, but most variance approvals revolve around a few common themes:

  • Unique physical conditions: irregular lot shape, steep topography, wetlands, easements, or other constraints.
  • Hardship not self-created: the problem can’t be caused by the applicant’s choices (in many jurisdictions).
  • Minimum necessary relief: only as much variance as needed to make reasonable use of the property.
  • Consistency with public interest: no material harm to neighbors, safety, or neighborhood character.
  • Consistency with plan intent: the request aligns with the purpose of the zoning code and area plans.

Why It Matters

Variances can unlock a deal by making a site buildable or improving unit count, layout efficiency, and rentable square footage. But because they are discretionary, they introduce entitlement risk: potential denial, delays, appeals, redesigns, and added costs. In underwriting, a variance can be the difference between a feasible and infeasible project.

Investor Uses

  • Site optimization: improve building footprint, parking layout, or unit yield within constraints.
  • Value creation: modest relief can increase density or efficiency, raising residual land value.
  • Risk pricing: discretionary approvals should be reflected in return requirements and contingencies.
  • Deal structuring: use entitlement contingencies, options, or milestone-based closings.

Example

A developer wants to build an infill multifamily project on an irregular corner lot. The zoning code requires a 20-foot side setback, but the lot narrows substantially at the rear due to a utility easement. Without relief, the building footprint becomes too small to support a feasible unit mix. The developer applies for a variance to reduce the side setback to 10 feet for a limited portion of the building.

  • Benefit: feasible building footprint and circulation.
  • Risk: public hearing delay, conditions of approval, or denial forcing redesign.
  • Underwriting response: add schedule buffer, legal/consultant costs, and downside design scenario.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming approval: variance decisions can be political and neighbor-driven.
  • Requesting too much relief: large variances often fail the “minimum necessary” standard.
  • Self-created hardship: designing a building that ignores code and then seeking relief can backfire.
  • Missing appeal risk: approvals can sometimes be challenged, adding time and uncertainty.
  • Underestimating conditions: cities may add design constraints, landscaping, or operational limits.

Best Practices

  • Confirm the standard: read the code carefully; some relief may be available administratively.
  • Design to comply first: pursue variance only when constraints genuinely prevent reasonable use.
  • Build the record: document hardship, alternatives considered, and why the request is minimal.
  • Engage early: pre-application meetings with planning staff and targeted neighbor outreach.
  • Underwrite a downside plan: “no variance” design, timeline delay, and added cost contingencies.

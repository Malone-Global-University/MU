Loan Origination Fee

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Loan Origination Fee

Definition

A loan origination fee is an upfront fee charged by a lender for evaluating, underwriting, structuring, and funding a loan. It is typically expressed as a percentage of the total loan amount and paid at closing.

How It Is Calculated

Origination fees are commonly quoted in percentage terms:

  • Formula: Loan Amount × Origination Fee %
  • Typical range (commercial): 0.5% – 2.0%
  • Higher-risk or bridge loans: May exceed 2%

Example

  • Loan amount: $12,000,000
  • Origination fee: 1.0%
  • Fee paid at closing: $120,000

Why Lenders Charge It

  • Underwriting costs: Credit review, property analysis, and due diligence.
  • Administrative expenses: Legal documentation and processing.
  • Revenue generation: Compensation for structuring and capital deployment.
  • Risk pricing: Higher fees often reflect higher perceived risk.

Impact on Borrowers

  • Increases total financing cost: Reduces net loan proceeds.
  • Affects effective interest rate: Raises the loan’s all-in cost.
  • Should be capitalized in underwriting: Modeled as transaction cost.
  • Impacts IRR: Particularly relevant in short hold periods.

Origination Fee vs. Points

The term “points” is often used interchangeably with origination fee. One point equals 1% of the loan amount.

Investor Considerations

  • Compare lenders on all-in cost, not just rate.
  • Short-term loans magnify fee impact.
  • Negotiation leverage: Fees may vary by deal size and sponsor strength.
  • Refinance modeling: Account for new origination fees in future debt assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring effective yield: Fees increase real borrowing cost.
  • Overlooking broker compensation: Some deals include additional placement fees.
  • Underestimating short-term impact: Fees matter more on bridge debt.
  • Failing to negotiate: Larger or repeat borrowers may secure lower fees.

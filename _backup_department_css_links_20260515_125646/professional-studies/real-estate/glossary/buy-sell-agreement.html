Buy-Sell Agreement

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Buy-Sell Agreement

Definition

A buy-sell agreement is a set of rules—often built into an LLC operating agreement or partnership agreement—that governs when and how an ownership interest can be sold or must be sold. It establishes exit triggers, transfer restrictions, valuation methods, and payment terms so the business (and the property) can continue operating even when an owner wants out or must exit.

In simple terms: it’s the “what happens if someone leaves” playbook for a real estate ownership group.

Why It Matters in Real Estate

Real estate entities often hold illiquid assets, rely on lender covenants, and need stable decision-making. A buy-sell agreement helps prevent surprises like an unwanted third-party partner, a forced property sale, or a legal dispute when a member experiences a life event or conflicts arise.

  • Protects the partnership from “accidental partners” (heirs, ex-spouses, creditors)
  • Reduces the chance of deadlock and expensive litigation
  • Creates a predictable path for liquidity (even when the asset is illiquid)
  • Helps maintain lender and investor confidence by clarifying control and transfers

Common Trigger Events

Buy-sell provisions typically activate on certain events (“triggers”). Common triggers include:

  • Voluntary exit: a member wants to sell their interest.
  • Death: transfer of interest to an estate or heirs.
  • Disability: an owner can no longer participate in management.
  • Divorce: an interest becomes part of marital property claims.
  • Bankruptcy / creditor event: charging order risk or forced transfer pressure.
  • Bad acts / cause: fraud, embezzlement, material breach, or misconduct.
  • Default: failure to fund a capital call or other required contribution.
  • Key-person departure: sponsor/manager resigns or is removed.

Typical Buy-Sell Structures

  • Right of First Refusal (ROFR): Existing members can match a bona fide third-party offer before the interest is sold to an outsider.
  • Right of First Offer (ROFO): Seller must offer to the group first; if no deal, seller may approach third parties (often with price constraints).
  • Call option: The company or other members can force a purchase (often after a trigger event).
  • Put option: The departing member can force the company or members to buy (liquidity right).
  • Mandatory redemption: The entity must redeem the interest (subject to cash flow and lender limits).

Valuation: How the Price Is Set

Valuation is where buy-sell agreements either prevent disputes—or create them. Common approaches include:

  • Appraisal: one or more independent appraisals of the property, then allocate equity value.
  • Formula: cap rate or multiple applied to NOI/EBITDA (less common for single assets).
  • Agreed value: updated periodically by members (often missed in practice).
  • Third-party offer: price anchored to an actual offer, then subject to ROFR/ROFO.
  • Discounts/premiums: minority interest discount, lack of marketability discount, or control premium (must be stated clearly).

Real Estate-Specific Adjustments

  • Debt payoff: equity value typically equals property value minus loan balance and closing costs.
  • Reserves: working capital and replacement reserves may be included or excluded.
  • Promote/carry: waterfall allocations can change payout economics if a member exits.
  • Tax considerations: capital accounts, tax basis, and built-in gain may affect after-tax outcomes.

Funding the Buyout

A buy-sell agreement is only as useful as the ability to pay. Funding mechanisms may include:

  • Cash on hand: reserves (often limited).
  • Installment payments: structured over time with interest.
  • Refinance: proceeds used to redeem the interest (subject to lender approval and market rates).
  • Life insurance: commonly used to fund buyouts upon death (more common in operating businesses than single-asset real estate, but still used).
  • Capital call: remaining members contribute capital to fund the purchase.

Interaction With Lenders and Entity Documents

Transfers of ownership can trigger loan restrictions (or even a default) if lender consent is required. Buy-sell provisions should align with:

  • Loan covenants: change of control clauses, permitted transfers, and guarantor requirements.
  • Entity governance: manager removal rights, voting thresholds, and deadlock remedies.
  • Transfer restrictions: securities law compliance, accredited investor requirements, and consent mechanics.
  • Tax allocations: capital accounts and distribution priorities in the waterfall.

Example

Two members own a property through an LLC: Member A (70%) and Member B (30%). Member B wants to exit. The operating agreement contains a ROFR: B receives a third-party offer, then must give A the right to match the terms within a set notice period. If A matches, A buys B’s interest. If A declines, B can sell to the third party, but only on terms no more favorable than the offer presented.

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague valuation language: no clear appraisal process, timelines, or dispute resolution.
  • No funding plan: agreement requires a buyout but provides no practical way to pay.
  • Lender conflicts: transfer allowed in the agreement but prohibited by the loan documents.
  • Missing tax mechanics: ignores capital accounts, tax allocations, or basis impacts.
  • Ambiguous discounts: parties fight over minority/marketability discounts at the worst time.
  • Deadlock gaps: no clear remedy when owners cannot agree (sale, arbitration, tie-breaker manager, etc.).

Investor Checklist

  • Define the trigger events and whether the buyout is optional or mandatory.
  • Specify who can buy (company vs. members) and the priority order.
  • Include a clear valuation method with timelines and a tie-break process.
  • Set payment terms (cash vs. installment) and interest rate mechanics.
  • Confirm the buy-sell terms do not violate loan transfer restrictions.
  • Coordinate with governance rules in the operating agreement and distribution waterfall.

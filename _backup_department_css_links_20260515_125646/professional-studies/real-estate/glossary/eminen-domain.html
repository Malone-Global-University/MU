Eminent Domain

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Eminent Domain

Definition

Eminent Domain is the legal power of a government to take private property for public use, provided that the property owner receives just compensation.

In simple terms: the government can force a sale of your property for a public project, but it must pay you fairly.

Public Use Examples

  • Highways and road expansions
  • Public schools
  • Utility infrastructure
  • Rail systems
  • Government buildings

Just Compensation

Compensation is generally based on the property’s fair market value at the time of the taking.

  • Appraisals determine value.
  • Owners may negotiate or litigate compensation.
  • Business interruption damages may apply in some cases.

Partial vs. Full Taking

  • Full Taking: Entire property is acquired.
  • Partial Taking: Only a portion is taken (e.g., road widening).

Partial takings may reduce remaining property value, which can factor into compensation.

Condemnation Process

  1. Government identifies property needed.
  2. Appraisal and offer made to owner.
  3. Negotiation period.
  4. If no agreement, formal condemnation proceeding begins.

Impact on Investors

  • Forced sale risk.
  • Potential disruption of cash flow.
  • Lease termination or modification.
  • Value changes to remaining property.

Lease Considerations

Commercial leases often include condemnation clauses addressing:

  • Rent abatement during taking.
  • Termination rights.
  • Allocation of condemnation proceeds.

Eminent Domain vs. Inverse Condemnation

  • Eminent Domain: Government formally takes property.
  • Inverse Condemnation: Property owner claims government action effectively took property value without formal condemnation.

Valuation Impact

  • May increase value if infrastructure improves access.
  • May reduce value if access or parking is lost.
  • Creates uncertainty in redevelopment planning.

Example

A city widens a roadway and acquires 15 feet of frontage from a retail center. The owner receives compensation for the land and potential damages to parking configuration and traffic flow.

Investor Checklist

  • Review local infrastructure plans.
  • Evaluate frontage and access exposure.
  • Understand lease condemnation clauses.
  • Consult appraisal experts if notified of taking.
  • Assess impact on long-term highest and best use.

Deficiency Judgment

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Deficiency Judgment

Definition

A deficiency judgment is a court ruling that makes a borrower personally liable for the remaining balance of a loan when foreclosure sale proceeds are insufficient to satisfy the debt.

How It Happens

  1. Borrower defaults on a loan.
  2. Lender forecloses on the property.
  3. Property is sold at auction.
  4. Sale proceeds are less than outstanding loan balance.
  5. Lender seeks a deficiency judgment for the difference.

Example

Loan balance: $5,000,000
Foreclosure sale price: $4,200,000
Remaining deficiency: $800,000

The lender may petition the court to recover the $800,000 directly from the borrower.

Recourse vs Non-Recourse Loans

  • Recourse Loan: Lender may pursue personal assets.
  • Non-Recourse Loan: Lender generally limited to collateral only.

Deficiency judgments are typically associated with recourse loans.

State Law Considerations

Availability of deficiency judgments varies by state. Some states limit or prohibit them, particularly in certain residential contexts.

Impact on Borrowers

  • Personal financial liability.
  • Potential wage garnishment.
  • Asset seizure.
  • Credit damage.

Carve-Out Guarantees

Even in non-recourse loans, certain actions—such as fraud or bankruptcy filings—may trigger liability through carve-out guarantees.

Risk Mitigation Strategies

  • Negotiate non-recourse financing.
  • Maintain conservative loan-to-value (LTV).
  • Monitor debt service coverage ratio (DSCR).
  • Understand state foreclosure laws.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming all commercial loans are non-recourse.
  • Overleveraging assets during market peaks.
  • Ignoring personal guarantee obligations.
  • Failing to stress test downturn scenarios.

Investor Checklist

  • Confirm loan recourse provisions.
  • Review personal guarantee language.
  • Analyze downside valuation risk.
  • Understand local foreclosure statutes.

Negligence

Definition

Negligence is a legal concept where a person or entity fails to exercise reasonable care, resulting in harm, injury, or damage to another party.

In real estate, negligence commonly arises in premises liability, property maintenance, construction activities, and operational management.

The Four Elements of Negligence

  1. Duty: A legal obligation to act with reasonable care.
  2. Breach: Failure to meet that duty.
  3. Causation: The breach directly caused harm.
  4. Damages: The harmed party suffered measurable loss.

All four elements must generally be proven for a successful negligence claim.

Negligence in Real Estate Context

  • Failure to repair hazardous conditions.
  • Ignoring known safety risks.
  • Improper maintenance of stairs, lighting, or walkways.
  • Inadequate supervision of construction sites.
  • Negligent property management practices.

Premises Liability

Property owners and landlords owe varying levels of duty depending on the legal status of the person on the property (tenant, invitee, trespasser). Failure to maintain reasonably safe conditions may constitute negligence.

Negligence vs. Gross Negligence

  • Negligence: Failure to exercise reasonable care.
  • Gross Negligence: Extreme departure from reasonable care, often involving reckless disregard for safety.

Many hold harmless clauses exclude protection for gross negligence or willful misconduct.

Insurance Implications

Claims based on negligence are typically covered under general liability insurance, subject to policy limits and exclusions.

Risk mitigation tools often include:

  • Additional insured endorsements
  • Waiver of subrogation provisions
  • Documented maintenance protocols
  • Regular safety inspections

Example

A tenant slips on a stairwell where lighting has been out for weeks. The landlord was notified but failed to repair it. If injury occurs, the landlord may be found negligent due to breach of duty.

Common Pitfalls for Investors

  • Delaying routine maintenance.
  • Inadequate documentation of inspections.
  • Failing to require vendor insurance.
  • Ignoring known hazards.
  • Not updating safety policies as portfolio grows.

Investor Risk Management Checklist

  • Implement routine inspection schedules.
  • Maintain written maintenance logs.
  • Require vendors to carry liability coverage.
  • Review lease risk allocation clauses annually.
  • Align insurance limits with asset risk profile.

Related Terms

  • General liability insurance
  • Hold harmless agreement
  • Additional insured
  • Waiver of subrogation
  • Umbrella insurance

Repair Escrow

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Repair Escrow

Definition

A repair escrow is a pool of funds held by a lender or third party at closing to ensure that specific property repairs are completed after acquisition or refinancing. The escrow protects the lender (and sometimes the buyer) by guaranteeing that deferred maintenance or required improvements are addressed within a defined timeframe.

Why Repair Escrows Are Required

Lenders often require a repair escrow when:

  • The property has deferred maintenance identified during inspection.
  • Certain items must be completed to meet loan underwriting or insurance standards.
  • Weather or timing prevents repairs from being completed prior to closing.
  • The buyer negotiates credits instead of pre-closing completion.

How It Works

  1. Scope identified: Inspection reports or lender-required repairs define the work.
  2. Cost estimate prepared: Contractor bids determine the repair amount.
  3. Escrow funded at closing: Funds (often 100%–150% of estimated cost) are withheld.
  4. Repairs completed: Buyer completes the work within the required timeframe (e.g., 90–180 days).
  5. Funds released: Lender releases escrow funds upon proof of completion.

Common Structures

  • Holdback escrow: Funds withheld from seller proceeds.
  • Buyer-funded escrow: Buyer contributes additional capital at closing.
  • Lender-controlled escrow: Managed directly by the lender with strict documentation requirements.
  • Winterization escrow: Seasonal repairs (roofing, paving) delayed due to weather.

Underwriting Implications

  • Cash requirement: Escrow increases upfront capital needed at closing.
  • Liquidity planning: Funds may be tied up until completion and approval.
  • Timeline risk: Delays can affect refinance timing or operational plans.
  • Execution risk: Poor contractor coordination can delay escrow release.

Example

A lender identifies $80,000 of roof and exterior repairs required post-closing. The lender requires 125% funding into escrow:

  • Required escrow deposit = $80,000 × 125% = $100,000

After repairs are completed and inspected, unused funds (if any) may be returned depending on loan terms. If repairs exceed budget, the borrower is typically responsible for the overage.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating costs: Repairs often exceed initial contractor bids.
  • Missing deadlines: Escrow agreements usually have strict completion timelines.
  • Documentation errors: Incomplete invoices or inspections delay fund release.
  • Assuming flexibility: Lender escrows are contractual and rarely negotiable after closing.

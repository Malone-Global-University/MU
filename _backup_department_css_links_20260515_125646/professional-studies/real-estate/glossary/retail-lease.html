Retail Lease

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Retail Lease

Definition

A Retail Lease is a commercial lease agreement for space used to sell goods or services directly to consumers. Retail leases are often more complex than office or industrial leases because they address sales performance, foot traffic, tenant mix, and consumer-facing operations.

In simple terms: it’s the legal agreement governing a store’s space in a shopping center or retail property.

Core Components of a Retail Lease

  • Base Rent: Fixed minimum rent.
  • Percentage Rent: Additional rent based on gross sales.
  • CAM Charges: Tenant share of common area costs.
  • Co-Tenancy Clause: Tenant protection if anchors close.
  • Use Clause: Defines permitted business operations.
  • Exclusive Use: Prevents landlord from leasing to competitors.
  • Radius Restriction: Limits tenant from opening nearby competing locations.

Retail Lease Structures

Triple Net (NNN)

Tenant pays base rent plus property taxes, insurance, and operating expenses.

Modified Gross

Tenant pays base rent plus certain expense reimbursements.

Full Service Gross (Less Common in Retail)

Landlord includes most expenses in base rent, sometimes with an expense stop.

Why Retail Leases Are Unique

  • Sales reporting requirements.
  • Hours of operation clauses.
  • Store appearance and signage standards.
  • Co-tenancy and anchor reliance.
  • Consumer foot traffic dependence.

Example

A tenant signs a 10-year retail lease in a shopping center with:

  • $40 per SF base rent
  • 6% percentage rent above breakpoint
  • NNN expense structure
  • Co-tenancy clause tied to grocery anchor

The landlord benefits from upside sales participation, while the tenant receives protection if traffic declines.

Retail Lease and NOI

  • Percentage rent increases NOI in strong sales years.
  • NNN leases reduce landlord expense risk.
  • Co-tenancy clauses can reduce rent during downturns.
  • Tenant mix affects stability and cap rate perception.

Key Risks for Investors

  • Anchor tenant closures triggering co-tenancy.
  • E-commerce competition affecting sales.
  • Concentration risk in specific retail categories.
  • High tenant improvement costs.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Tenant creditworthiness.
  • Lease term remaining.
  • Sales performance history.
  • Anchor tenant lease expirations.
  • Market demographics and traffic patterns.

Retail Lease vs. Office Lease

  • Retail: Sales-based provisions, co-tenancy, percentage rent.
  • Office: Expense stops, longer terms, less sales reporting.

Investor Checklist

  • Review all co-tenancy exposure.
  • Analyze tenant sales trends.
  • Model percentage rent scenarios conservatively.
  • Confirm CAM recovery structure.
  • Evaluate tenant exclusivity and competition clauses.

Related Terms

  • Percentage Rent
  • Co-Tenancy Clause
  • Common Area Maintenance (CAM)
  • Expense Stop
  • Anchor Tenant
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)

Entitlement Risk

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Entitlement Risk

Definition

Entitlement risk is the uncertainty that a development project may fail to obtain required governmental approvals—or may receive them with delays, costly conditions, or reduced density—that materially impact feasibility, timeline, and returns.

This risk is most significant in projects requiring rezoning, variances, conditional use permits, site plan approval, or environmental review.

Common Approvals That Create Entitlement Risk

  • Rezoning or map amendments
  • Planned development (PD) approvals
  • Variances from setbacks, height, or parking requirements
  • Conditional or special use permits
  • Subdivision plat approvals
  • Environmental impact review or mitigation approval
  • Design review board approvals

Sources of Entitlement Risk

1) Political & Community Opposition

  • Neighborhood resistance (“Not In My Backyard” concerns)
  • Traffic, school capacity, or infrastructure objections
  • Affordable housing or inclusionary requirements

2) Regulatory & Technical Constraints

  • Environmental restrictions (wetlands, floodplains, contamination)
  • Historic preservation overlays
  • Utility capacity limitations
  • Stormwater or infrastructure deficiencies

3) Timing & Process Risk

  • Public hearing delays
  • Staff turnover or political shifts
  • Appeals or litigation after approval

Financial Impacts

  • Reduced density: Fewer units or lower FAR reduces projected NOI.
  • Increased costs: Added conditions (landscaping, off-site improvements, impact fees).
  • Timeline delays: Extended carry costs (interest, taxes, opportunity cost).
  • Capital stack stress: Lenders and equity may require higher returns for higher risk.
  • Land value risk: If entitlements fail, land may only support lower-value uses.

Example Scenario

A developer contracts to purchase land assuming approval for 200 multifamily units. After public hearings, the city approves only 160 units and requires additional off-site road improvements.

The reduced density lowers projected NOI, while new infrastructure costs increase capital expenditure, materially reducing the project’s IRR.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Due diligence: Meet with planning staff before closing.
  • Land purchase contingencies: Tie closing to entitlement approval.
  • Political outreach: Engage community stakeholders early.
  • Conservative underwriting: Model reduced-density and delay scenarios.
  • Phased approvals: Secure key entitlements before major capital commitments.
  • Partner with experienced local consultants: Zoning attorneys and land-use planners.

High vs. Low Entitlement Risk Projects

  • Low Risk: “By-right” development that conforms to existing zoning.
  • Moderate Risk: Minor variances or administrative approvals.
  • High Risk: Rezoning, density increases, or politically sensitive land uses.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Probability-weight projected unit counts or square footage.
  • Model extended carry (interest, taxes, insurance).
  • Adjust required return thresholds for higher entitlement uncertainty.
  • Evaluate sponsor track record in the jurisdiction.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming maximum allowable zoning is politically achievable.
  • Underestimating timeline delays and appeal risks.
  • Ignoring neighborhood engagement.
  • Failing to budget for off-site infrastructure requirements.

Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary REIT

Definition

A Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) is a corporation that owns, operates, or finances income-producing real estate and elects special tax status under U.S. law.

In exchange for meeting regulatory requirements, a REIT generally does not pay corporate income tax, provided it distributes at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders as dividends.

How REITs Work

  • Investors purchase shares of the REIT.
  • The REIT acquires or finances real estate assets.
  • Rental income or interest income generates cash flow.
  • Profits are distributed to shareholders.

Key REIT Requirements

  • At least 75% of assets must be real estate-related.
  • At least 75% of gross income must come from real estate sources.
  • At least 90% of taxable income must be distributed annually.
  • Must have at least 100 shareholders.

Types of REITs

  • Equity REIT: Owns and operates properties.
  • Mortgage REIT (mREIT): Invests in real estate debt.
  • Hybrid REIT: Combines property ownership and lending.

Public vs Private REITs

  • Publicly Traded REIT: Listed on stock exchanges.
  • Public Non-Traded REIT: Registered but not exchange-listed.
  • Private REIT: Offered through private placements.

Why Investors Use REITs

  • Access to diversified real estate portfolios.
  • Liquidity (for publicly traded REITs).
  • Regular dividend income.
  • Lower capital requirements compared to direct ownership.

Return Profile

  • Dividend income.
  • Share price appreciation.
  • Total return combines both components.

Risks

  • Interest rate sensitivity.
  • Market volatility (public REITs).
  • Property sector risk (office, retail, multifamily, etc.).
  • Leverage risk.

REIT vs Direct Ownership

  • REIT: Passive, diversified, liquid.
  • Direct Ownership: Greater control, potential tax advantages, active management required.

Common Pitfalls

  • Chasing high dividend yield without reviewing fundamentals.
  • Ignoring interest rate exposure.
  • Confusing taxable income with cash flow.
  • Overlooking management quality.

Investor Checklist

  • Analyze property sector concentration.
  • Review leverage ratios.
  • Evaluate dividend coverage.
  • Assess management track record.

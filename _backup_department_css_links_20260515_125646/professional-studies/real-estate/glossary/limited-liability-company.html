Limited Liability Company (LLC)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Limited Liability Company (LLC)

Definition

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a legal entity commonly used to own and operate real estate. An LLC’s owners are called members. In most cases, an LLC provides limited liability, meaning claims and debts related to the property generally stay inside the company rather than reaching the members’ personal assets.

Why Real Estate Investors Use LLCs

  • Liability protection: creates a legal boundary between property risks (tenant injuries, contracts, vendor disputes) and personal assets.
  • Ownership flexibility: easy to add/remove members, allocate profits, and structure partnerships.
  • Operational clarity: separates business banking, contracts, insurance, and bookkeeping from personal activities.
  • Deal structure: common for syndications and joint ventures where one party manages and others provide capital.

How LLC Governance Works

LLC rules are typically defined in an operating agreement. This document governs economics and control such as: voting rights, profit splits, capital calls, buy/sell provisions, and what happens if a member defaults.

Common roles

  • Managing member / manager: runs day-to-day operations and signs contracts.
  • Non-managing members: passive or limited-control owners, often providing capital.

Tax Treatment

LLCs are known for tax flexibility. Many LLCs are treated as “pass-through” entities for tax purposes, meaning profits and losses pass through to members rather than being taxed at the entity level (treatment can differ by elections and jurisdiction). Real estate investors often care about how depreciation, interest expense, and distributions flow through the structure.

LLC vs. the Property Itself

The property is the asset; the LLC is the owner. Investors can buy:

  • Asset purchase: buy the real estate directly from the current owner (most common).
  • Entity purchase: buy membership interests in the LLC that owns the property (can be faster, but due diligence is deeper).

Example

A duplex is owned by Maple Street Holdings, LLC. The LLC signs the lease, hires vendors, and carries property insurance. If a tenant sues over an injury, the claim is generally against the LLC and its assets (subject to legal exceptions), not directly against the members personally.

Common Pitfalls

  • Commingling funds: mixing personal and LLC banking can weaken liability protection and create accounting issues.
  • Weak operating agreement: unclear decision rights and economics can cause partnership conflict later.
  • Personal guarantees: loans may require members to guarantee debt, reducing practical liability separation.
  • Ignoring compliance: missing annual reports, registered agent, or required filings can create legal and banking problems.
  • Overconfidence in “asset protection”: LLCs reduce risk, but insurance, good operations, and contracts still matter.

Bid Leveling

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Bid Leveling

Definition

Bid leveling is the process of comparing contractor bids apples-to-apples by standardizing scope, quantities, materials, and assumptions. The goal is to identify what each bidder included, excluded, or assumed so you can evaluate true cost, schedule, and risk—rather than choosing a “low bid” that later explodes into change orders.

Why It Matters

  • Reduces change orders: scope gaps and unclear specs are the biggest driver of overruns.
  • Improves pricing confidence: you can underwrite CapEx with fewer unknowns.
  • Protects timeline: schedule assumptions (crew size, sequencing, lead times) become visible.
  • Creates accountability: a leveled bid set becomes a clean baseline for contract exhibits.

What You Level (The Core Checklist)

A good leveling sheet forces each bid into the same structure. Typical categories include:

  • Scope alignment: are all line items present (demo, prep, install, haul-off, protection)?
  • Quantities: units, square footage, linear feet, fixture counts, allowances per unit.
  • Specifications: product grade, thickness, finish level, brand/model, warranty.
  • Labor model: crew size, hours, supervision, overtime assumptions.
  • General conditions: site logistics, dumpsters, temporary utilities, security, cleaning.
  • Permits/inspections: who pulls permits, fees included, inspection responsibility.
  • Schedule: start date, duration, sequencing, long-lead items, milestones.
  • Inclusions/exclusions: what is explicitly not included (patching, subfloor, asbestos, etc.).
  • Allowances: what’s “TBD” (rot repair, electrical upgrades, plumbing surprises).
  • Payment terms: deposit, progress billing, retainage, lien releases.

How the Process Works

  1. Issue a clear Scope of Work (SOW): same documents to all bidders, same walk schedule.
  2. Normalize bids: map each bid into the same line-item template (your leveling sheet).
  3. Resolve gaps: send RFIs (requests for information) and require revised bids.
  4. Compare true totals: base bid + allowances + general conditions + escalation/lead times.
  5. Select best value: price is one factor; capacity, quality, and schedule reliability matter.
  6. Lock the baseline: attach the final scope + leveling assumptions to the contract exhibits.

Example

Two interior-renovation bids come back for the same unit turns:

  • Bid A: $7,900/unit but excludes haul-off, patch/texture, and appliance install.
  • Bid B: $8,450/unit includes haul-off, patch/texture, appliance install, and unit protection.

After leveling, Bid A’s “true” total becomes $8,650/unit once missing items are added. Bid B is actually the lower-risk, lower-total option.

Common Pitfalls

  • Comparing only the bottom line: the cheapest number often has the biggest exclusions.
  • Vague finish standards: “paint and flooring” without specs invites quality and pricing variance.
  • Allowance abuse: low base bid + huge allowances hides the real cost.
  • Ignoring occupied conditions: working in occupied units requires protection, access, and constraints.
  • Not confirming capacity: the bid is meaningless if the contractor can’t staff your timeline.

Best Practices

  • Use a consistent template: require bidders to respond in your bid form where possible.
  • Define “ready-to-lease”: include punch standards and turnover checklist in the scope.
  • Separate alternates: price “nice-to-haves” (e.g., quartz) as alternates, not blended totals.
  • Confirm unit counts and access: phasing plan, keys, hours, noise rules, protection expectations.
  • Require documentation: insurance, license, references, warranty terms, lien release process.

Distressed Property

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Distressed Property

Definition

A distressed property is a property facing financial, legal, or physical problems that impair performance or threaten ownership. Distress often creates a motivated seller and can result in discounted pricing — but it also increases complexity and execution risk.

Types of Distress

  • Financial distress: delinquent mortgage payments, tax arrears, looming maturity, or negative cash flow.
  • Legal distress: foreclosure filings, title issues, liens, probate, or litigation.
  • Physical distress: deferred maintenance, code violations, environmental issues, or vandalism.
  • Operational distress: high vacancy, poor collections, mismanagement, or broken tenant screening.

Common Deal Paths

  • Short sale: lender-approved sale below the loan balance.
  • Foreclosure auction: property sold through a foreclosure process.
  • REO: bank-owned property sold after foreclosure.
  • Note purchase: investor buys the debt and restructures or forecloses.
  • Receivership / court sale: property sold under a legal process.

Why It Matters to Investors

Distressed acquisitions can offer strong basis and higher returns when executed correctly. The tradeoff is that distress usually increases timeline uncertainty, capex needs, and legal diligence requirements.

Investor Underwriting Notes

  • Capex realism: over-budget repairs and add contingency.
  • Occupancy verification: confirm who is in-place and what rights they have.
  • Title and lien review: confirm payoff, releases, and priority.
  • Time risk: lender approvals, court timelines, and vacant possession can take longer than expected.
  • Stabilization plan: define the path from “broken” to “bankable” operations.

Example

An apartment property is 55% occupied due to poor management and deferred maintenance. The owner is behind on debt service and faces a loan maturity. The property may trade at a discount, but the buyer must fund renovations, rebuild leasing, and carry the asset through a multi-month stabilization period.

Common Pitfalls

  • Underestimating capex: distress often hides behind walls, roofs, and plumbing.
  • Ignoring legal complexity: liens, eviction timelines, and court processes can dominate the deal.
  • Overpaying for “upside”: if the plan fails, your basis becomes your enemy.
  • No operating system: distressed assets require strong management and process discipline.

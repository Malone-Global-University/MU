Definition

The General Ledger (GL) is the master accounting record of a property’s financial activity. It organizes all transactions into categorized accounts such as income, operating expenses, capital expenditures, assets, and liabilities.

In real estate investing, the GL is a foundational document used to verify reported financial performance and calculate Net Operating Income (NOI).

What It Contains

  • Revenue accounts: rent, late fees, reimbursements, parking, other income
  • Operating expenses: payroll, utilities, repairs & maintenance, insurance, taxes
  • Capital expenditures: major improvements and asset replacements
  • Balance sheet items: deposits, prepaid expenses, reserves

Why It Matters to Investors

The GL provides transaction-level detail that supports or challenges the summary financial statements provided by a seller. It allows investors to:

  • Validate trailing twelve-month (TTM) income and expense claims
  • Identify one-time or non-recurring items
  • Spot misclassified expenses
  • Evaluate seasonality and operating trends

Underwriting Applications

  • Expense normalization: Adjust abnormal spikes or deferred maintenance.
  • NOI recalculation: Confirm stabilized income assumptions.
  • CapEx separation: Distinguish operating expenses from capital improvements.
  • Fraud detection: Cross-check deposits and vendor payments.

Example

A seller presents an NOI of $1,000,000. After reviewing the General Ledger, the buyer discovers that $85,000 of expenses were incorrectly coded as capital expenditures and excluded from operating expenses. Adjusting for proper classification reduces stabilized NOI, impacting valuation.

Common Pitfalls

  • Relying only on summaries: Income statements may hide misclassifications.
  • Ignoring account consistency: Account naming and coding may vary by manager.
  • Missing accrual adjustments: Some expenses may not align with reporting periods.
  • Not reconciling with bank statements: GL entries should match actual cash flows.

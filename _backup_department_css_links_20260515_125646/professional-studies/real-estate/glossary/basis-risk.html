Basis Risk

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Definition

Basis risk is the risk that a hedge, benchmark, or financial index does not move in perfect correlation with the underlying exposure it is intended to offset. In real estate finance, basis risk commonly arises in floating-rate loans and interest rate hedging strategies.

Where Basis Risk Appears in Real Estate

  • Floating-rate debt tied to SOFR or other indices.
  • Interest rate caps or swaps used to hedge loan exposure.
  • Mismatch between loan index and hedge index.
  • Asset valuation vs. market benchmark performance.

Example

A borrower has a loan indexed to Term SOFR but purchases an interest rate cap based on a different SOFR index or reset structure.

If the loan rate and hedge index do not move identically, the hedge may not fully offset rising interest costs — creating basis risk.

Interest Rate Example

  • Loan Index: Term SOFR + 250 bps
  • Hedge: Cap based on Daily SOFR

Differences in calculation timing or rate structure may cause imperfect protection.

Types of Basis Risk

  • Index Basis Risk: Different reference indices.
  • Timing Basis Risk: Different reset periods.
  • Geographic Basis Risk: Mismatch between local asset performance and national benchmarks.
  • Asset-Liability Basis Risk: Income and debt costs move differently.

Impact on Investors

  • Unexpected variability in debt service.
  • Imperfect hedge effectiveness.
  • Potential NOI volatility.
  • Increased refinancing uncertainty.

Underwriting Considerations

  • Match hedge index to loan index where possible.
  • Model stress scenarios with rate mismatches.
  • Understand reset timing mechanics.
  • Review lender hedge requirements carefully.

Basis Risk vs. Interest Rate Risk

  • Interest Rate Risk: Risk that rates rise or fall.
  • Basis Risk: Risk that hedge does not perfectly track the exposure.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Align loan and hedge index structures.
  • Use standardized hedging instruments.
  • Shorten hedge reset gaps.
  • Incorporate conservative DSCR assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming all SOFR-based products behave identically.
  • Ignoring reset frequency differences.
  • Failing to model rate volatility scenarios.

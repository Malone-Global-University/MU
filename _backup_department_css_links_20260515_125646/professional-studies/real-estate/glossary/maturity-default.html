Maturity Default

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Maturity Default

Definition

A maturity default occurs when a borrower fails to repay or refinance the outstanding principal balance of a loan by its scheduled maturity date. Unlike a payment default (missed monthly payment), a maturity default happens even if the borrower has made all required payments up to that point but cannot pay off the balloon balance.

Why It Happens

  • Declining property value: Loan-to-value (LTV) exceeds refinance thresholds.
  • Cash flow weakness: DSCR too low to qualify for new debt.
  • Interest rate increases: Higher rates reduce loan proceeds.
  • Capital market disruptions: Reduced lender appetite or liquidity.
  • Operational setbacks: Vacancy spikes, tenant rollover, or delayed renovations.

Common Resolution Paths

  • Loan extension: Temporary maturity push-out, often with fees or added reserves.
  • Loan modification: Structural change to terms (rate, amortization, covenants).
  • Equity infusion: Borrower contributes capital to reduce balance.
  • Discounted payoff: Negotiated settlement below par.
  • Foreclosure or deed-in-lieu: Transfer of control to lender.

Impact in CMBS Loans

In CMBS structures, a maturity default typically triggers transfer from the master servicer to the special servicer. The special servicer evaluates whether extending the loan or enforcing remedies produces higher recovery for bondholders under the pooling and servicing agreement (PSA).

Investor Implications

  • Refinance risk: Model exit cap rates, rate assumptions, and lender proceeds conservatively.
  • Extension cost: Fees, cash sweeps, and reserves reduce equity returns.
  • Time risk: Workout processes can delay liquidity events.
  • Opportunity: Maturity walls often create acquisition and note-purchase opportunities.

Example

An office building has a $15M interest-only loan maturing in 3 months. NOI has declined due to vacancy, and current underwriting supports only a $12M refinance. The borrower faces a $3M shortfall.

  • If the borrower contributes $3M, refinancing may proceed.
  • If not, the lender may grant a short-term extension or transfer the loan to special servicing.
  • Absent agreement, enforcement remedies may begin.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring rate sensitivity: Small rate shifts meaningfully impact refinance proceeds.
  • Overestimating value recovery: Assuming rapid rent or occupancy rebound.
  • Waiting too long: Early engagement with lenders increases flexibility.
  • Underfunded reserves: Lenders may require fresh capital during extension.

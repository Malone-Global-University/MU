Anchor Tenant

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Anchor Tenant

Definition

An Anchor Tenant is a large, well-known tenant in a retail property that occupies significant square footage and attracts customer traffic, benefiting smaller tenants within the shopping center.

In simple terms: the anchor tenant is the “traffic driver” for the center.

Common Types of Anchor Tenants

  • Grocery stores
  • Big-box retailers
  • Department stores
  • Home improvement stores
  • Fitness centers
  • Movie theaters

Why Anchor Tenants Matter

  • Drive consistent foot traffic.
  • Improve leasing velocity for smaller spaces.
  • Enhance perceived center stability.
  • Support higher rental rates for in-line tenants.

Anchor Tenant Lease Characteristics

  • Long-term leases (often 15–25 years).
  • Lower rent per square foot than smaller tenants.
  • Limited CAM contribution in some cases.
  • Significant negotiating leverage.

Anchor Tenant and Co-Tenancy

Many retail leases include co-tenancy clauses that give smaller tenants remedies if the anchor tenant closes.

  • Rent reduction triggers.
  • Percentage-rent-only periods.
  • Lease termination rights.

Impact on NOI

  • Anchor closures can significantly reduce NOI.
  • Can trigger rent abatements for multiple tenants.
  • Stability improves cap rate perception.

Example Scenario

A grocery store anchors a 150,000 SF shopping center. Smaller retailers rely on the grocery store’s traffic. If the grocery store vacates, several leases may activate co-tenancy clauses, reducing rental income.

Risks

  • Retail bankruptcy risk.
  • Lease expiration concentration.
  • Limited CAM recovery from anchor tenants.
  • Market shifts in retail demand.

Investor Considerations

  • Review anchor tenant credit strength.
  • Check remaining lease term.
  • Analyze co-tenancy exposure.
  • Evaluate sales performance (if applicable).
  • Assess replacement feasibility if anchor vacates.

Anchor Tenant vs. In-Line Tenant

  • Anchor Tenant: Large footprint, traffic generator.
  • In-Line Tenant: Smaller space dependent on anchor traffic.

Valuation Impact

  • Strong anchors support lower cap rates.
  • Anchor vacancy increases risk premium.
  • Tenant concentration affects underwriting assumptions.

Investor Checklist

  • Verify anchor lease expiration date.
  • Review exclusive use provisions.
  • Model anchor vacancy stress scenario.
  • Analyze tenant sales and foot traffic data.
  • Understand anchor termination or kick-out rights.

Related Terms

  • Co-Tenancy Clause
  • Percentage Rent
  • Retail Lease
  • Net Operating Income (NOI)
  • Common Area Maintenance (CAM)
  • Breakpoint

TI Allowance

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary TI Allowance

Definition

A TI allowance (short for Tenant Improvement Allowance) is money a landlord provides to a tenant—typically expressed as $ per square foot—to help pay for buildout costs needed to occupy the space (e.g., walls, flooring, lighting, HVAC modifications, electrical/data, plumbing, finishes, and code compliance).

How It Works

TI is part of the overall lease “package,” alongside base rent, free rent, lease term, and expense structure (gross vs. NNN). Economically, TI is a landlord concession: it increases the landlord’s upfront cash outlay and reduces the lease’s net effective rent.

Common structures

  • Turnkey buildout: landlord delivers finished space; TI may be implicit in the deal.
  • Allowance reimbursement: tenant builds; landlord reimburses up to a cap after invoices/lien waivers.
  • Work letter scope: lease defines what qualifies, approvals, and timelines.
  • Amortized TI: landlord fronts TI, then recovers cost through higher rent over the term.

Why It Matters

TI allowance often determines whether a tenant can realistically move forward, especially for office, retail, and medical users where buildouts are expensive. For owners and investors, TI is a major driver of lease-up cost, cash flow timing, and return on invested capital.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting leasing costs: model TI + leasing commissions + downtime to get to stabilized NOI.
  • Comparing deal terms: higher rent with high TI can be worse than lower rent with low TI.
  • Capital planning: TI is often funded from reserves, TI/LC budgets, or a lender-controlled account.
  • Tenant quality signal: credit tenants may demand higher TI but provide better income durability.

How TI Affects Net Effective Rent

TI reduces the landlord’s economics because it is effectively prepaid rent (a concession). A simple way to see it: spread TI over the lease term to estimate the “rent equivalent” cost.

Rule-of-thumb approach

  • TI rent-equivalent ($/SF/yr) ≈ TI ($/SF) ÷ Lease term (years)
  • Net Effective Rent ≈ Face Rent − (TI rent-equivalent) − (free rent impact) − (other concessions)

Example

A tenant signs a 5-year lease at $36.00/SF/yr with a $25/SF TI allowance (ignoring other concessions for simplicity).

  • TI rent-equivalent ≈ 25 ÷ 5 = $5.00/SF/yr
  • Net effective rent (rough) ≈ 36 − 5 = $31.00/SF/yr

Two deals with the same face rent can have very different economics if TI and other concessions differ.

What Drives TI Levels

  • Use type: medical/lab and high-finish retail typically require higher TI than standard office.
  • Condition of space: shell space vs. second-generation space changes buildout scope.
  • Market competition: higher vacancy often means higher TI to win tenants.
  • Lease term: longer terms can support larger TI because costs are spread over more years.
  • Tenant credit: strong tenants can negotiate better TI; weak credit may require less TI or more security.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing TI with CapEx: some items benefit the building long-term; classify correctly for underwriting.
  • Ignoring timing: TI is usually paid early; it can create a cash flow dip even if rents look strong.
  • Overlooking scope limits: “eligible costs” and approvals can cap what TI actually covers.
  • Not budgeting soft costs: architecture, permits, and project management can be significant.
  • Missing re-tenanting risk: high TI today may repeat at rollover if the space is specialized.

Encumbrance

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Encumbrance

Definition

An Encumbrance is any claim, lien, charge, easement, or legal restriction on a property that limits the owner’s rights or affects clear title. Encumbrances may impact transferability, financing, or development potential.

Common Types of Encumbrances

  • Liens: mortgages, tax liens, mechanic’s liens.
  • Easements: rights granted for utilities or access.
  • Restrictive covenants: limits on property use.
  • Encroachments: physical intrusion onto neighboring property.
  • Leases: tenant rights that survive property transfer.

Why Encumbrances Matter

  • Title clarity: unresolved encumbrances may delay closing.
  • Value impact: certain restrictions can reduce property utility.
  • Lender requirements: financing often requires clean title.
  • Development constraints: may limit construction or expansion.

Example

A buyer intends to build an addition on a property. A title report reveals a recorded utility easement running across the planned expansion area. The easement encumbers the land and restricts construction in that portion.

Encumbrance vs. Defect

Not all encumbrances are defects. Some—such as standard utility easements—are common and acceptable. Others, like undisclosed liens, may constitute title defects requiring resolution.

Investor Considerations

  • Review the Title Report carefully.
  • Assess whether encumbrances materially affect value.
  • Consult legal counsel for complex restrictions.
  • Confirm title insurance coverage aligns with risk tolerance.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring Schedule B exceptions in title documents.
  • Underestimating development limitations.
  • Failing to cure outstanding liens before closing.
  • Assuming minor encumbrances have no financial impact.

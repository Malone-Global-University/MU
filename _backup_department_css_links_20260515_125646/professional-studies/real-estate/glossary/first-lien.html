First Lien

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary First Lien

Definition

A first lien is a first-priority security interest in real estate or other collateral. The holder of a first lien has the primary claim on the asset and is paid before any junior lienholders in the event of foreclosure, liquidation, or sale of the property.

How It Works

In real estate, a first lien is typically created through a mortgage or deed of trust recorded against the property. The recording establishes priority in the public record, meaning any later liens (second mortgages, mezzanine pledges, mechanics liens, etc.) are subordinate unless otherwise structured.

Priority in the Capital Stack

First lien debt sits at the top of the secured portion of the capital stack:

  • 1st: First lien (senior debt)
  • 2nd: Junior lien / second mortgage
  • 3rd: Mezzanine debt (pledge of ownership interests)
  • Last: Preferred equity and common equity

Because of this priority, first lien lenders typically accept lower yields than junior lenders.

Rights of a First Lien Holder

  • Foreclose on the property after default
  • Appoint a receiver (if permitted)
  • Enforce cash management or sweep provisions
  • Be repaid first from sale or liquidation proceeds
  • Approve or restrict additional subordinate financing

Example

A property is worth $25,000,000 and has:

  • $18,000,000 first lien mortgage
  • $4,000,000 second lien loan

If the property is sold at foreclosure for $20,000,000:

  • The first lien lender is paid up to $18,000,000 plus costs
  • The second lien lender receives the remaining proceeds (if any)
  • If proceeds are insufficient, junior creditors may suffer losses

Why It Matters to Investors

First lien position significantly reduces risk relative to subordinate positions. In distressed scenarios, the first lien lender typically controls enforcement and restructuring decisions. This control premium explains why first lien loans are often priced lower than mezzanine or preferred equity capital.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming value protection: If property value falls below the loan balance, even first lien lenders can incur losses.
  • Overlooking prior liens: Tax liens and certain statutory liens may prime recorded mortgages.
  • Construction risk: Mechanics liens can complicate priority if not properly managed.
  • Cross-collateralization: Portfolio structures may dilute isolated asset protection.

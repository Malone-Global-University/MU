Rezoning

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Rezoning

Definition

Rezoning is a local government action that changes a property’s zoning designation. Rezoning can modify what uses are allowed (e.g., industrial to mixed-use), and it often changes allowable density or development standards such as height, FAR, setbacks, lot coverage, and parking rules.

Why Rezoning Matters

Zoning is the rulebook for what can be built. Changing the zoning can dramatically change a site’s highest & best use and therefore its land value. But rezoning is typically a discretionary process that introduces entitlement risk: uncertainty, public hearings, conditions, delays, or denial.

Common Reasons for Rezoning

  • Upzoning for density: allow more units, height, or FAR to support housing or growth goals.
  • Use conversion: industrial/warehouse land shifting to residential or mixed-use as cities evolve.
  • Corridor revitalization: updating zoning along transit or main streets to encourage redevelopment.
  • Site-specific needs: unique parcels where existing zoning makes reasonable use difficult.
  • Plan alignment: bringing zoning into compliance with an adopted comprehensive plan.

Rezoning vs. Variance vs. Conditional Use

  • Rezoning: changes the zoning district (the rules and sometimes the use/intensity).
  • Variance: relief from a specific standard (setback/parking/height), usually due to hardship.
  • Conditional/Special Use Permit: allows a use that is permitted with conditions after review.

How the Rezoning Process Typically Works

Exact steps vary by jurisdiction, but rezoning often follows a similar pattern:

  1. Pre-application meeting: early feedback from planning staff.
  2. Application submittal: narrative, site plan concept, studies, and fees.
  3. Staff review: consistency with comprehensive plan, infrastructure capacity, and policy goals.
  4. Public notice: neighbor notification, posted signs, and hearing schedule.
  5. Planning commission hearing: recommendation (approve/deny/conditions).
  6. City council / county commissioners: final decision (often legislative).
  7. Appeal / legal challenge window: depending on local rules.

Key Underwriting Considerations

  • Timeline: entitlement time drives carry costs and interest rate exposure.
  • Probability: not all rezonings are equally likely—policy alignment matters.
  • Conditions of approval: affordability set-asides, design constraints, offsite improvements, fees.
  • Infrastructure: utilities, traffic, schools, and stormwater can limit achievable density.
  • Community response: political risk and neighborhood opposition can delay or reshape outcomes.
  • Exit cap and market shift risk: the market can move while you wait for approvals.

Example

An investor buys a small commercial site near a planned transit stop. Today it is zoned for low-intensity retail. The city’s comprehensive plan identifies the corridor for higher density mixed-use. The investor applies to rezone the parcel to a mixed-use district allowing mid-rise residential over ground-floor retail.

  • Upside: higher allowable density increases residual land value and buyer pool (developers).
  • Risk: discretionary approval, timeline delays, and potential affordability/impact fee conditions.
  • Underwriting response: probability-weight the rezoning outcome and model a “no rezoning” fallback plan.

Common Pitfalls

  • Paying for the upzone before you have it: buying at entitled pricing without entitlement certainty.
  • Ignoring the comprehensive plan: rezonings that conflict with policy are much harder.
  • Underestimating conditions: approvals can come with costly infrastructure and affordability requirements.
  • Skipping the fallback: if rezoning fails, the current zoning must still support a viable plan.
  • Not modeling time: entitlement delay can erase returns even if rezoning is ultimately approved.

Best Practices

  • Start with policy: confirm consistency with comprehensive plans and corridor studies.
  • Engage early: staff meetings and targeted outreach reduce surprises.
  • Use milestone-based deal structure: options, contingencies, or phased closings.
  • Probability-weight scenarios: model approve/approve-with-conditions/deny outcomes.
  • Maintain a credible “by-right” plan: your fallback improves negotiating leverage and downside protection.

Site Plan Review

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Site Plan Review

Definition

Site Plan Review is a local approval process where a jurisdiction evaluates a proposed project’s site layout and design—including building placement, access, parking, landscaping, utilities, and drainage—to confirm compliance with zoning and development standards before construction permits are issued.

What Site Plan Review Evaluates

Think of site plan review as the city’s way of asking: “Does this project fit here, and does it function safely and predictably for neighbors, future occupants, and infrastructure?”

  • Site layout: building footprint, setbacks, lot coverage, open space.
  • Access & circulation: driveways, curb cuts, internal circulation, turning movements.
  • Parking: stall count, ADA stalls, loading zones, bicycle parking.
  • Pedestrian safety: sidewalks, crosswalks, lighting, connectivity to streets.
  • Landscaping & buffering: screening, tree retention, fencing, transitions to neighbors.
  • Stormwater & grading: drainage patterns, detention, erosion control, flood considerations.
  • Utilities: water/sewer connections, fire flow, power, telecom, easements.
  • Fire & life safety access: fire lanes, hydrant placement, apparatus turning radius.
  • Design standards: façade requirements, materials, massing, signage (where applicable).

Typical Submittal Package

  • Site plan set: existing conditions, demolition (if any), proposed plan.
  • Architectural concept: elevations, massing, basic floor plans (varies by jurisdiction).
  • Civil drawings: grading, drainage, utility plan, erosion control.
  • Traffic/transportation memo: trip generation, access, mitigation (if triggered).
  • Landscape plan: plant schedule, screening, irrigation (if required).
  • Photometric plan: exterior lighting levels (common for commercial).
  • Narrative: how the proposal meets code standards and design criteria.

How the Process Works

  1. Pre-application: confirm scope and required studies with planning staff.
  2. Submittal: application, drawings, and fees.
  3. Completeness check: staff confirms the package is reviewable.
  4. Interdepartmental review: planning, engineering, fire, utilities, sometimes parks/transportation.
  5. Corrections cycles: comment letters and resubmittals (often multiple rounds).
  6. Decision: approve, approve with conditions, or deny.
  7. Permit-ready plans: approved site plan becomes the basis for building permit review.

Common Conditions of Approval

  • Access changes: modify driveway width, relocate curb cut, add turn lanes.
  • Parking revisions: re-stripe, add ADA stalls, add loading zone, bike parking.
  • Stormwater upgrades: increased detention capacity, infiltration systems, oil-water separators.
  • Landscaping/screening: enhanced buffers, fences, tree replacement.
  • Public improvements: sidewalk infill, street trees, frontage upgrades.
  • Operational constraints: delivery hours, trash enclosure design, lighting limits.

Why It Matters to Investors

  • Schedule risk: comment cycles can add months, especially if studies are triggered.
  • Budget risk: conditions can require off-site improvements and redesign work.
  • Plan certainty: lenders and contractors prefer entitlement clarity before pricing and funding.
  • Exit risk: a buyer may discount a deal if SPR is not secured or is heavily conditioned.

Underwriting Checklist

  • Approval type: ministerial (administrative) or discretionary (public hearing/appealable)?
  • Known triggers: traffic study thresholds, stormwater requirements, design review overlays.
  • Scope gaps: utilities capacity, fire access constraints, easements, frontage work.
  • Critical path: how SPR ties to building permits, financing, and construction start.
  • Contingency: carry costs and soft-cost buffer for redesigns and conditions.

Example

A developer submits a site plan for a 40-unit infill multifamily project. During review, the city requires frontage sidewalk improvements, a revised fire apparatus turning radius, and larger stormwater detention. The changes increase sitework costs and push the permit-ready set by eight weeks, impacting interest carry and the construction start date.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “by-right” means “no review”: many by-right projects still require SPR and conditions.
  • Underestimating civil complexity: stormwater and utilities can drive redesigns.
  • Ignoring frontage: sidewalks, curb ramps, and street trees can be expensive.
  • Late discovery of easements: utility and access easements can constrain layout.

Lot Coverage

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Lot Coverage

Definition

Lot coverage (also called site coverage or building coverage) is the proportion of a parcel that is covered by the building footprint (the area of the building at grade). It is commonly expressed as a percentage and is used in zoning to limit how much of a site can be built upon.

Core Formula

  • Lot Coverage (%) = (Building footprint area ÷ Lot area) × 100

The key underwriting question is what the local code counts in the numerator (footprint) and which areas count in the denominator (lot area), because definitions vary by jurisdiction.

What Counts Toward Lot Coverage

Many municipalities count only the building footprint. Others include additional elements that increase impervious area.

Often included

  • Main building footprint (ground-floor outline)
  • Attached garages and covered breezeways (if roofed)
  • Port-cocheres, covered loading canopies, and roofed patios (varies)
  • Accessory structures (e.g., detached garages, maintenance sheds) if regulated

Sometimes included (code-dependent)

  • Covered decks, porches, and balconies that project over grade
  • Roof overhangs beyond a certain projection
  • Enclosed courtyards or arcades

Usually excluded (but confirm locally)

  • Uncovered patios and walkways
  • Landscaping and open space areas
  • At-grade parking lots (often regulated separately via impervious coverage or landscaping ratios)
  • Underground parking (since it’s below grade), though access ramps may be regulated

Lot Coverage vs. FAR

Lot coverage and Floor Area Ratio (FAR) both regulate development intensity, but they control different things:

  • Lot coverage limits footprint at the ground (how much open space remains).
  • FAR limits total floor area (you can often add stories without expanding the footprint, subject to height and setbacks).

A project can be constrained by lot coverage even if FAR is available (e.g., a single-story retail site with strict open-space requirements), or constrained by FAR even if footprint is available (e.g., height caps or stepbacks).

Why Zoning Codes Regulate Lot Coverage

  • Stormwater and drainage: more coverage usually means more runoff and larger detention needs.
  • Open space and light/air: ensures yards, courtyards, and separations between structures.
  • Neighborhood character: prevents “wall-to-wall” massing in lower-density zones.
  • Fire/life safety and access: can preserve room for emergency access, separations, and service areas.

Investor / Developer Impacts

  • Yield (units or SF): tighter coverage limits can reduce achievable density and rentable area.
  • Parking strategy: lower coverage may push parking to structured/underground formats or require shared parking approaches.
  • Design efficiency: forces tradeoffs among courtyard size, circulation, setbacks, and amenity placement.
  • Sitework costs: impervious area and stormwater detention can materially affect civil costs and timeline.
  • Entitlements: exceeding coverage limits may require a variance or planned development approval.

Example

A 30,000 SF lot has a building footprint of 12,000 SF.

  • Lot Coverage = (12,000 ÷ 30,000) × 100 = 40%

If zoning allows a maximum lot coverage of 35%, the site plan may need a smaller footprint, more stories (if allowed), or design changes such as structured parking or reduced ground-floor program.

Common Pitfalls

  • Not reading the definition: codes differ on whether canopies, porches, and overhangs count.
  • Confusing with impervious coverage: some codes separately regulate impervious surfaces (parking lots, sidewalks, etc.).
  • Ignoring easements and buffers: utility, access, wetland, or landscape buffers can shrink the effective buildable area.
  • Assuming “max coverage” is optimal: a slightly lower footprint may improve circulation, leasing, and curb appeal.

Practical Underwriting Notes

  • Request the zoning summary showing max lot coverage, setbacks, and any overlays.
  • Ask civil engineers whether stormwater requirements are driven by coverage/imperviousness.
  • Track “yield sensitivity” to footprint constraints (units, rentable SF, and parking stalls).

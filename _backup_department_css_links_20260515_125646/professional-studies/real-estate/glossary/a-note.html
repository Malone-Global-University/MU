A-Note

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary A-Note

Definition

An A-Note is the senior portion of a mortgage loan that has been split into multiple pieces—typically an A-Note and a B-Note.

The A-Note has first priority in repayment and typically carries lower risk than the subordinate portion.

How Loan Splitting Works

  • A large senior loan is originated.
  • The loan is divided into two tranches.
  • The A-Note represents the lower-risk senior portion.
  • The B-Note represents the higher-risk subordinate portion.

Capital Stack Position

  • A-Note (Senior Mortgage)
  • B-Note (Subordinate Mortgage)
  • Mezzanine Debt
  • Preferred Equity
  • Common Equity

Key Characteristics

  • Priority claim on collateral.
  • Lower interest rate relative to B-Note.
  • Strongest control rights in default scenarios.
  • Typically held by institutional lenders or securitized.

A-Note vs B-Note

  • A-Note: Senior, lower yield, lower risk.
  • B-Note: Junior, higher yield, higher risk.

Both notes are secured by the same mortgage but differ in payment priority.

Intercreditor Relationship

The rights and responsibilities between A-Note and B-Note holders are governed by an intercreditor agreement.

Why A-Notes Are Attractive

  • Enhanced downside protection.
  • Stable income profile.
  • Often investment-grade risk exposure.
  • Commonly used in securitization structures.

Underwriting Focus

  • Loan-to-value (LTV) at A-Note level.
  • Debt service coverage ratio (DSCR).
  • Property quality and cash flow stability.
  • Market cycle positioning.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring combined leverage when B-Note exists.
  • Overlooking intercreditor limitations.
  • Misjudging refinance risk.
  • Assuming full insulation from B-Note performance issues.

Investor Checklist

  • Review total loan structure.
  • Confirm priority rights.
  • Analyze property fundamentals.
  • Stress test valuation decline scenarios.

Related Terms

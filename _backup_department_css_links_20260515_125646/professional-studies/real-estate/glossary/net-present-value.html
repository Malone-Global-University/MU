Net Present Value (NPV)

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Net Present Value (NPV)

Definition

Net Present Value (NPV) measures the value of an investment by converting all expected future cash flows into today’s dollars using a discount rate (your required return), then subtracting the initial investment. In plain terms: NPV answers, “After accounting for time and risk, how much value does this deal create (or destroy) versus my hurdle rate?”

Core Idea

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future. NPV fixes that by discounting future cash flows back to present value. If a deal produces cash flows that are worth more than what you pay (at your discount rate), NPV is positive.

Formula

NPV is the sum of discounted cash flows:

  • NPV = (Cash Flow0) + (Cash Flow1 ÷ (1 + r)1) + (Cash Flow2 ÷ (1 + r)2) + ... + (Cash Flown ÷ (1 + r)n)

Where r is the discount rate and Cash Flow0 is usually the initial investment (a negative number).

Real estate version (typical)

  • Cash Flow0: equity invested at closing (down payment + costs + reserves)
  • Cash Flow1..n-1: annual cash flow after debt service (often after reserves)
  • Cash Flown: final year cash flow + net sale proceeds (after debt payoff and closing costs)

How to Interpret NPV

  • NPV > 0: the deal exceeds your required return (creates value).
  • NPV = 0: the deal meets your required return exactly.
  • NPV < 0: the deal falls short of your required return (destroys value vs your hurdle).

Why It Matters in Investing

NPV is powerful because it is explicit about your required return. Instead of asking “Is the IRR high?” it asks “Is this investment worth it to me given my risk tolerance and opportunity cost?”

Investor Uses

  • Comparing deals: choose the investment that creates more value at the same discount rate.
  • Pricing decisions: solve for the maximum price you can pay to keep NPV at or above zero.
  • Business plan testing: quantify how rent growth, expense savings, or timing changes impact value.
  • Risk visibility: use higher discount rates for riskier deals (or stress scenarios) to test durability.

Example

You invest $1,000,000 of equity today. You expect $90,000 per year for 5 years in cash flow, and $1,200,000 from sale proceeds in year 5. Your discount rate is 10%.

  • Year 0: −1,000,000
  • Years 1–4: +90,000 each year
  • Year 5: +90,000 + 1,200,000 = 1,290,000

Discounting those cash flows at 10% produces a total present value above $1,000,000, so the NPV is positive. A positive NPV means the deal clears the 10% hurdle.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong discount rate: the rate should reflect your required return for the risk, not a generic number.
  • Mixing levered and unlevered cash flows: discount levered cash flows with an equity hurdle; unlevered with an asset-level rate.
  • Ignoring sale assumptions: exit cap rate, costs, and timing often drive most of NPV in real estate.
  • Not modeling reserves/capex: ignoring ongoing capital needs inflates cash flows and NPV.
  • False precision: small input changes can swing NPV; always run sensitivities.

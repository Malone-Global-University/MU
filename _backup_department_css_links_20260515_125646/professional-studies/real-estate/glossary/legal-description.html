Definition

A legal description is a precise, legally recognized written description of a parcel of real property that identifies its boundaries and location. It is used in deeds, mortgages, title commitments, surveys, and other recorded instruments to ensure the correct property is conveyed.

Unlike a street address, which is informal and can change, a legal description is tied to public land records and is enforceable in court.

Main Types of Legal Descriptions

1. Lot & Block (Subdivision Plat)

References a recorded subdivision map (plat). This is common in residential neighborhoods and platted developments.

  • Example: “Lot 12, Block 3, Sunnybrook Subdivision, as recorded in Volume 8 of Plats, Page 42.”
  • Relies on the recorded plat map.

2. Metes & Bounds

Describes property boundaries using distances (metes) and directional bearings (bounds), starting from a defined “point of beginning.”

  • Common for irregular parcels or older properties.
  • Often references monuments, stakes, roads, or natural features.

3. Rectangular Survey System (Government Survey)

Uses sections, townships, and ranges within the Public Land Survey System (PLSS). Common in many western and Midwestern states.

  • Example: “The NW ¼ of Section 14, Township 22 North, Range 4 East.”
  • Typically used for larger rural or undeveloped land.

Why It Matters

The legal description determines exactly what is being conveyed. Errors or inconsistencies can lead to boundary disputes, title defects, lender rejection, or failed closings.

For investors, accuracy protects:

  • Title integrity
  • Lender approval
  • Development feasibility
  • Exit certainty

Legal Description vs. Address vs. Parcel Number

  • Street Address: Mailing reference only; not legally sufficient for conveyance.
  • Parcel Number (APN): Tax assessor reference; may change over time.
  • Legal Description: Binding property identification used in recorded documents.

Example

A buyer intends to purchase a fourplex but the deed’s legal description mistakenly references Lot 5 instead of Lot 6. Even if the address is correct, the wrong legal description could convey a different parcel entirely. Title review and survey reconciliation would be required before closing.

Common Pitfalls

  • Copy-and-paste errors in deeds or amendments.
  • Outdated descriptions after lot splits or boundary adjustments.
  • Ignoring easements embedded within the legal description.
  • Mismatched survey vs. deed descriptions.
  • Relying on APN only without confirming the recorded description.

Investor Checklist

  • Match deed legal description to title commitment.
  • Confirm consistency with survey and recorded plat.
  • Verify no partial-lot omissions or overlaps.
  • Review for embedded easement language.
  • Ensure lender and title insurer approve the description.

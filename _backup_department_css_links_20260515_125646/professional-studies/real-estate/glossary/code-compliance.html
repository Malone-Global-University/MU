Code Compliance

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Code Compliance

Definition

Code compliance means a property meets the requirements of applicable building, housing, fire, health, and (where relevant) zoning codes. In practice, it also includes having the right permits, passing required inspections, and correcting identified violations.

Why It Matters

Code compliance is an operations and underwriting issue. Violations can create safety risk, trigger fines, cause forced repairs, delay leasing or renovations, and—at the extreme—restrict occupancy. Compliance also supports resident outcomes and reduces exposure tied to habitability and claims.

What “Codes” Typically Cover

  • Life safety: smoke/CO detectors, egress, fire extinguishers, fire-rated assemblies.
  • Electrical: panels, wiring, GFCI/AFCI requirements, unsafe outlets, grounding.
  • Plumbing: leaks, water heaters, backflow, sewer issues, fixtures and traps.
  • Structural/building envelope: roofs, stairs/rails, decks, exterior walls, windows.
  • Mechanical/HVAC: ventilation, heat requirements, combustion safety.
  • Health/sanitation: pests, mold conditions, refuse storage, basic cleanliness standards.
  • Zoning/use: legal unit counts, occupancy limits, parking requirements, permitted uses.

How Non-Compliance Shows Up in Real Deals

  • Open permits: prior work never finalized or inspected.
  • Unpermitted renovations: kitchens/baths, electrical upgrades, conversions, additions.
  • Inspection failures: city/housing authority notices, fire marshal findings.
  • Deferred life-safety items: broken rails, missing detectors, blocked egress.
  • Illegal units: basement conversions, extra ADUs, unit count mismatches.

Investor Uses

  • Underwriting CAPEX: scope and budget required to cure violations and close permits.
  • Risk pricing: adjust offer terms for unknowns (access, tenant cooperation, timeline).
  • Financing readiness: lenders and insurers may require corrective work or reserves.
  • Execution planning: sequence work (permits, inspections, vendor lead times) to avoid delays.

Due Diligence Checklist

  • Request records: prior notices, inspection reports, and violation histories.
  • Permit search: identify open/expired permits and unresolved finals.
  • Physical inspections: life safety walk, electrical/plumbing review, roof/exterior, common areas.
  • Confirm legal use: unit count, certificates of occupancy, zoning conformity.
  • Budget and timeline: include permit lead times, re-inspection fees, and contingency.

Common Pitfalls

  • Assuming “grandfathered” solves everything: older buildings may still require upgrades when systems are touched.
  • Ignoring tenant coordination: access constraints can drive delays and higher costs.
  • Under-scoping life safety: small items can become big issues during inspections.
  • Missing zoning risk: illegal units can be forced offline, reducing NOI.

Retrade

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary Retrade

Definition

Retrade is a post-contract renegotiation where a buyer asks to change the deal after the purchase and sale agreement (PSA) is signed—most often requesting a price reduction, seller credit, or revised terms. Retrades typically occur during due diligence when new facts emerge that materially affect value, risk, or costs.

Why Retrades Happen

  • New physical findings: deferred maintenance, roof/HVAC issues, plumbing/electrical, code concerns.
  • Financial discrepancies: expenses understated, delinquency higher, payroll/contract costs misrepresented.
  • Income quality issues: classic loss-to-lease not real, concessions hidden, bad debt elevated, lease file problems.
  • Operational surprises: vendor contracts, staffing requirements, compliance issues, unit turns bigger than expected.
  • Capital events: required life-safety upgrades, municipal requirements, insurance premiums, taxes reassessed.
  • Market/financing shift: rate moves or lender proceeds change (more controversial as a justification).

Common Retrade Requests

  • Price reduction: reduce purchase price by an agreed amount.
  • Seller credit: credit at closing to cover repairs, CapEx, or other identified costs.
  • Repair escrow: funds held back at closing until work is completed.
  • Term changes: extend diligence/closing, modify representations/warranties, adjust prorations.
  • Scope carve-outs: exclude certain items, amend deliverables, or require specific cures.

Why It Matters

Retrades are a transaction execution reality: they can protect a buyer from paying for risks that were unknown at signing, but they can also damage reputation if used as a tactic. The best deals balance fairness (real issues get priced) with certainty (terms are honored).

Investor Uses

  • Protect underwriting: align price with updated CapEx, OpEx, income quality, and risk.
  • Clarify materiality: separate “noise” from items that truly change valuation.
  • Improve closing odds: restructure to keep the deal alive when surprises appear.
  • Build credibility: document issues, show work, and propose clean, defensible adjustments.

How to Evaluate a Retrade

Buyer lens

  • Prove the delta: tie each request to a quantified impact (CapEx quotes, tax notices, insurance binders, expense run-rate).
  • Use a clear standard: “This does not meet my required return / risk tolerance” is cleaner than debating “right price.”
  • Offer simple options: price cut or credit; avoid complex re-trading that delays closing.

Seller lens

  • Test legitimacy: is this new information or something the buyer should have known?
  • Check marketability: will the issue affect the next buyer too (and how much time/cost to re-trade later)?
  • Prefer credits over price: credits can be easier psychologically and may preserve headline pricing.

Example

A buyer signs a PSA at $10,000,000 assuming $350,000 of first-year repairs. During inspections, they receive contractor bids showing $650,000 is required to address roof patches, exterior repairs, and life-safety items. The buyer requests a retrade:

  • Option A (price): reduce price by $300,000.
  • Option B (credit): provide a $300,000 seller credit at closing.
  • Option C (escrow): escrow $300,000 with clear scope and release conditions.

The key is that the request is tied to a documented, incremental cost discovered after signing—not a vague “market changed” argument.

Common Pitfalls

  • Using retrades as a tactic: “try it and see” behavior harms credibility and future deal flow.
  • Poor documentation: no quotes, no schedules, no rent roll / T-12 reconciliation.
  • Overreaching: bundling minor issues into a large concession undermines trust.
  • Late timing: raising issues at the eleventh hour without prior communication increases fallout risk.
  • Ignoring alternatives: credits, escrows, or scope adjustments can solve the same problem more cleanly.

At-Risk Rules

Real Estate Investing Glossary – Malone Global University

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Track Glossary At-Risk Rules

Definition

The at-risk rules limit the amount of losses an investor can deduct to the amount of money and property they are economically at risk for in an activity.

In simple terms: you generally cannot deduct losses beyond the capital you could actually lose.

Why They Exist

Congress implemented at-risk rules to prevent taxpayers from deducting losses generated through highly leveraged or protected structures where they did not truly bear the economic downside.

What Counts as “At Risk”

An investor is generally considered at risk for:

  • Cash contributed
  • Property contributed
  • Recourse debt (where the investor is personally liable)
  • Certain qualified nonrecourse real estate financing (subject to specific rules)

What Does NOT Increase At-Risk Amount

  • Nonrecourse debt where the investor has no personal liability (with limited exceptions)
  • Guaranteed protection against loss
  • Stop-loss agreements
  • Capital financed by another party without economic exposure

Basic Rule

Deductible loss for the year cannot exceed your at-risk amount.

If losses exceed the at-risk amount, the excess is:

  • Disallowed for the current year
  • Carried forward until the at-risk amount increases

Example

An investor contributes $100,000 to a real estate partnership and has no personal liability on the debt.

  • At-risk amount: $100,000
  • Reported loss: $130,000

The investor may deduct only $100,000 (subject to other limitations). The remaining $30,000 is suspended under at-risk rules.

Interaction With Other Tax Limitations

Loss deductions are generally limited in the following order:

  1. Tax basis limitations
  2. At-risk rules
  3. Passive activity loss (PAL) rules

Even if you are at risk, losses may still be suspended under passive activity rules.

Special Rule: Qualified Nonrecourse Financing

In real estate, certain nonrecourse loans secured by real property may increase the at-risk amount even if the investor is not personally liable. These rules are technical and depend on lender qualification and loan structure.

At-Risk vs. Tax Basis

While related, at-risk amount and tax basis are not identical:

  • Tax basis determines overall loss capacity and gain calculation.
  • At-risk amount determines economic exposure limitation.

In some cases, basis may exceed the at-risk amount.

How At-Risk Amount Changes Over Time

  • Increases with additional capital contributions
  • Increases with income allocations
  • Decreases with losses deducted
  • Decreases with cash distributions

Investor Checklist

  • Understand whether debt is recourse or nonrecourse
  • Track your at-risk amount annually
  • Separate basis limitations from at-risk limitations
  • Understand how refinancing impacts at-risk exposure
  • Consult a CPA for leveraged structures

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming all debt increases deductible loss capacity
  • Confusing basis with at-risk amount
  • Ignoring suspended losses year to year
  • Failing to analyze loan recourse provisions

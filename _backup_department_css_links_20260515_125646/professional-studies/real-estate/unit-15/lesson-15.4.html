Lesson 15.4: Industrial Real Estate Economics

Understand the economics of warehouses and logistics facilities, including location efficiency, clear height, loading functionality, supply chain demand, and the reasons industrial assets often produce durable leasing performance.

Home Department Professional Studies Real Estate Investing Track Unit 15 Lesson 15.4

1. Lesson Introduction

Industrial real estate includes warehouses, distribution centers, logistics facilities, light manufacturing buildings, flex industrial space, and other properties used to store, move, assemble, or support goods. While these assets may appear physically simple compared with office towers or major retail centers, their economics can be powerful because they are deeply connected to supply chains, transportation systems, and the movement of inventory.

The industrial sector is driven less by consumer visibility and more by function. Tenants care about whether the building helps them move products efficiently, reduce transportation cost, improve labor access, and operate reliably. For that reason, industrial real estate performance depends heavily on location efficiency, building design, and the operational needs of the tenant.

Industrial properties are often valued for practical reasons: they can support essential business operations, leasing costs may be lower than in some office assets, and the buildings themselves may require less customization for certain tenant types. At the same time, not all industrial space is interchangeable. Features such as clear height, truck court depth, dock doors, power capacity, trailer parking, and access to transportation infrastructure can strongly influence rent and demand.

Investor Insight:
In industrial real estate, functionality is economics. A building that improves tenant efficiency can earn stronger demand, better retention, and more durable value.

2. Learning Objectives

  • Explain how industrial properties generate income through logistics, storage, and operational tenants.
  • Identify the physical features that most strongly influence industrial leasing demand.
  • Understand how location efficiency affects tenant decision-making and rent potential.
  • Recognize why industrial leases can be relatively stable but still depend on tenant business health.
  • Interpret how supply chain trends shape industrial occupancy, rent growth, and investment behavior.

3. Core Concepts

Industrial Real Estate Supports Operations

Industrial properties are leased by tenants who need space for practical business activity. This may include storing inventory, distributing products, processing returns, light assembly, equipment staging, cold storage, or regional fulfillment. Unlike office properties, which are built around workspace, or retail assets, which are built around customer access, industrial buildings are typically built around efficiency of movement and use.

Location Efficiency Matters Deeply

Industrial tenants care about where a property sits within transportation and labor networks. Access to highways, ports, airports, rail lines, population centers, and major distribution routes can materially improve operating performance. A warehouse that reduces delivery time or transportation cost may be far more valuable to a tenant than one with similar square footage in a weaker location.

Building Functionality Drives Rent

Not all industrial buildings offer the same utility. Clear height affects vertical storage capacity. Loading docks influence how quickly trucks can be processed. Column spacing affects usable layout. Power capacity may be essential for specialized operations. Trailer parking, turning radius, and truck court depth influence logistics efficiency. Buildings with stronger functional characteristics often command better rents and lower vacancy because they serve a broader or more modern tenant base.

Lease Stability Can Be Attractive

Industrial properties often benefit from business tenants that use space as part of ongoing operational systems. When a building is well-located and functionally effective, the tenant may have meaningful switching costs, which can support retention and stable occupancy. However, stability still depends on the tenant’s underlying business strength, industry exposure, and operational fit with the property.

Supply Chain Demand Shapes Performance

Industrial demand is influenced by trade activity, e-commerce growth, manufacturing patterns, inventory strategy, and regional distribution needs. A company holding more inventory or trying to shorten delivery times may need more warehouse space. A slowdown in goods movement or a reduction in distribution intensity can soften demand. Industrial real estate is therefore closely tied to how businesses organize the flow of products through the economy.

Physical Simplicity Does Not Mean Economic Simplicity

Industrial buildings may appear straightforward, but their economics still require careful underwriting. Investors must examine tenant credit, lease rollover, market rent, replacement cost, access constraints, obsolescence risk, and whether the property remains competitive as tenant requirements evolve. A basic-looking building may be highly valuable if it sits in a strategic infill location with strong functionality.

4. Mechanics

How Industrial Properties Generate Income

  1. Acquire or develop a functional industrial building: The property must meet real operational needs.
  2. Lease space to industrial users: Tenants may be distributors, manufacturers, wholesalers, logistics operators, or service businesses.
  3. Collect base rent and escalations: Leases often include annual rent increases and expense reimbursement structures.
  4. Maintain building utility: The landlord preserves access, structure, paving, loading, and other features critical to operations.
  5. Manage rollover and market positioning: At lease expiration, performance depends on retention, new leasing demand, and the building’s competitiveness.

Key Variables in Industrial Underwriting

  • Location relative to transportation corridors and end users
  • Clear height and cubic storage capacity
  • Number and type of loading positions
  • Site coverage and truck circulation efficiency
  • Power capacity and utility infrastructure
  • Lease term, renewal probability, and tenant credit quality
  • Market vacancy and replacement supply
  • Functional obsolescence risk

Common Industrial Property Categories

  • Bulk warehouse: Large buildings used for regional storage and distribution.
  • Last-mile logistics: Facilities located near dense population centers for rapid delivery.
  • Light industrial or flex: Mixed-use industrial space that may include warehouse and office components.
  • Manufacturing support: Buildings tailored to production, assembly, or specialized industrial processes.

Why Functionality Creates Value

Industrial tenants often choose space based on workflow rather than appearance. A modern warehouse with strong loading capacity, efficient layout, and proximity to major transport routes may allow faster handling, lower labor friction, and better inventory turnover. Those operating advantages can translate into stronger tenant demand and more stable property economics.

5. Worked Example

Suppose an investor is comparing two industrial properties in the same metro area:

  • Property A: A modern warehouse near an interstate interchange with 36-foot clear height, multiple dock doors, trailer parking, and efficient truck access.
  • Property B: An older warehouse in a less connected location with lower clear height, fewer loading positions, and tighter truck maneuvering space.

Step 1: Compare Functional Utility

Property A can likely support a broader range of distribution users because it handles inventory vertically, moves trucks efficiently, and supports faster loading operations. Property B may still have value, but its physical limits reduce its appeal to modern logistics tenants.

Step 2: Compare Location Efficiency

Property A is closer to a major transport corridor, which may reduce shipping time and operating cost for tenants. That location advantage can support stronger rent and better tenant retention.

Step 3: Compare Leasing Risk

If both properties become vacant, Property A may lease faster because more tenants can use it effectively. Property B may need pricing discounts or a narrower tenant target due to functional limitations.

Interpretation

Even if Property B appears cheaper on a price-per-square-foot basis, Property A may be the better investment because the building is more functional, more competitive, and more closely aligned with modern industrial demand. Industrial value depends not just on size, but on how well the property serves real operational needs.

6. Real Estate Application

Investors in industrial real estate often focus heavily on logistics networks, tenant operations, and barriers to supply. Infill industrial sites near major population centers can be especially valuable because land is scarce, replacement may be difficult, and tenants need quick access to customers. In other settings, regional warehouse clusters near ports, rail lines, or freight corridors may benefit from strong long-term distribution demand.

Example: Last-Mile Warehouse

A smaller warehouse near a dense urban area may command strong rent because it helps delivery operators shorten final transit time. Even if the building is not enormous, its location can make it strategically important.

Example: Obsolescence Risk

An older industrial building with low ceilings, limited loading, and poor access may still produce income today, but it could face pressure if tenant requirements shift. Investors must distinguish between temporary occupancy and durable competitiveness.

Example: Lease Durability

A tenant using a warehouse as part of a deeply integrated distribution network may be less likely to move casually, especially if relocation would disrupt operations. That kind of functional stickiness can support income stability, but only if the property continues to meet business needs.

Investor Insight:
Industrial assets are often strongest when location, site design, and tenant workflow all align. When those pieces fit together, the property becomes difficult to replace and more valuable to its users.

7. Common Mistakes

  • Assuming all warehouses are interchangeable: Small functional differences can materially change demand and rent.
  • Focusing only on square footage: Clear height, loading, circulation, and site design may matter more than raw size.
  • Ignoring transportation access: A weak location can reduce tenant efficiency and leasing competitiveness.
  • Underestimating obsolescence risk: Older buildings may lose relevance as tenant needs evolve.
  • Confusing current occupancy with long-term quality: A leased building is not automatically a durable asset.
  • Neglecting tenant business exposure: Lease stability still depends on the health of the tenant and its industry.

8. Knowledge Check

  1. Why is industrial real estate considered function-driven rather than visibility-driven?
  2. How does location efficiency affect industrial rent potential and tenant demand?
  3. What does clear height tell investors about an industrial building?
  4. Why might an industrial lease be relatively stable compared with some other sectors?
  5. What is functional obsolescence, and why does it matter in industrial investing?

9. Practical Exercise

Compare the following two industrial assets:

  • Asset A: A newer logistics facility near a major highway with high clear height, multiple dock doors, and strong trailer access.
  • Asset B: An older warehouse farther from freight routes with lower ceilings and limited loading functionality.

Complete the following:

  1. Identify three reasons Asset A may command stronger tenant demand.
  2. List two forms of risk that could affect Asset B even if it is currently occupied.
  3. Explain how industrial functionality influences underwriting assumptions.
  4. Write 4 to 6 sentences explaining why industrial value depends on both building features and network location.

10. Key Takeaways

  • Industrial real estate supports logistics, storage, distribution, and operational business needs.
  • Location efficiency is a major driver of tenant demand and rent potential.
  • Building functionality, including clear height and loading design, strongly affects competitiveness.
  • Industrial leases can offer durable income when properties are operationally important to tenants.
  • Investors must underwrite both current income and the long-term relevance of the asset in a changing supply chain environment.

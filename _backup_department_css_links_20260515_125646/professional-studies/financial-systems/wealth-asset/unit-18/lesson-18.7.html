Lesson 18.7: Onboarding Errors and Risk Points

Synthesize the entire Unit 18 onboarding framework by examining the common errors, recurring bottlenecks, and systemic risk points that undermine onboarding quality — and present practical mitigation strategies and workflow improvements that address these vulnerabilities at their source.

Where This Lesson Fits

The preceding six lessons have traced the complete account onboarding process from its initial request through final activation. Lesson 18.1 established the end-to-end framework. Lesson 18.2 examined documentation and data collection. Lesson 18.3 detailed KYC and identity verification. Lesson 18.4 covered compliance and regulatory checks. Lesson 18.5 analyzed account coding and system setup. And Lesson 18.6 presented the approval and activation workflows that bring the account to operational status.

Lesson 18.7 is the capstone synthesis. Rather than examining how the process should work, it examines how the process fails. Every phase of the onboarding lifecycle contains characteristic failure modes — recurring errors, predictable bottlenecks, and systemic vulnerabilities that manifest across firms of all sizes and sophistication levels. Understanding these failure modes is essential for designing resilient onboarding processes, because the most effective process improvements address not just the symptoms of failure but the root causes that produce them.

This lesson integrates the concepts from all six preceding lessons into a unified risk framework. It maps errors to their phase of origin — showing how a documentation gap in Lesson 18.2 propagates through KYC (Lesson 18.3) and compliance review (Lesson 18.4) to delay activation (Lesson 18.6). It identifies the interdependencies between phases that create cascade effects — where a failure in one phase triggers failures in subsequent phases. And it presents mitigation strategies that address root causes rather than symptoms — preventing errors from entering the process rather than catching them after they propagate through multiple phases.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to identify the characteristic failure modes at each phase of the onboarding process and explain how errors propagate across phases, describe the common bottlenecks that delay onboarding and the root causes behind them, articulate the difference between symptom-level fixes and root-cause solutions for onboarding problems, design comprehensive risk mitigation strategies that address vulnerabilities across the entire onboarding lifecycle, implement continuous improvement processes that use error data to systematically improve onboarding quality, and evaluate an organization's onboarding process maturity and recommend targeted improvements.

Lesson Overview

Onboarding errors fall into three fundamental categories: errors of omission (something that should have been done was not done — a missing document, an unperformed screening, an unconfigured system), errors of commission (something was done incorrectly — wrong data entered, wrong code assigned, wrong identity verified), and errors of timing (something was done in the wrong sequence or at the wrong time — activating before compliance review, entering system data before custodian setup, beginning trading before funding confirmation). Each category requires different detection and prevention strategies.

The risk landscape of onboarding is shaped by several structural factors that make the process inherently vulnerable to errors. First, onboarding involves multiple participants — the client, the relationship manager, the onboarding coordinator, the compliance team, the operations team, the custodian, and potentially external parties like attorneys and accountants — creating numerous handoff points where information can be lost, distorted, or delayed. Second, onboarding requirements vary substantially by account type, client type, and jurisdiction, making it impossible to apply a single uniform process to all accounts without either over-engineering simple accounts or under-engineering complex ones. Third, onboarding is subject to commercial pressure — relationship managers want accounts activated quickly to begin managing assets and generating revenue — which can incentivize shortcuts that compromise quality. And fourth, many onboarding tasks are performed manually, creating the opportunity for human error at every data entry point, document review, and coding decision.

Effective risk management in onboarding does not attempt to eliminate all errors — that is neither feasible nor cost-effective. Instead, it focuses on three objectives: preventing the highest-impact errors through targeted controls, detecting errors as early as possible in the process (before they propagate downstream), and learning from errors that do occur to continuously improve the process and prevent recurrence.

Why This Matters in Wealth & Asset Operations

The financial and operational cost of onboarding errors is consistently underestimated. Industry research across wealth management firms reveals that the average cost of correcting a significant onboarding error after account activation ranges from $2,500 to $15,000 — encompassing staff time for investigation and remediation, client communication and relationship repair, potential fee adjustments, regulatory reporting if applicable, and system corrections across multiple platforms. For a firm that opens 500 accounts per year with a 5% significant error rate, this represents $62,500 to $375,000 in annual remediation costs — costs that are largely invisible in traditional financial reporting because they are distributed across multiple teams and absorbed as general operational overhead.

Beyond direct costs, onboarding errors carry indirect costs that are even more significant. Client trust, once damaged by an onboarding error, is difficult to restore — a client whose first fee invoice is incorrect, whose first quarterly statement contains the wrong household grouping, or whose first trade is rejected because of a system configuration error begins the relationship questioning the firm's operational competence. Regulatory examination findings related to onboarding deficiencies (particularly KYC and suitability) can trigger enhanced supervision, remediation requirements, and reputational harm that affects the firm's ability to attract new clients and retain existing ones.

The strategic imperative is clear: onboarding quality is a competitive differentiator. Firms that onboard clients quickly, accurately, and with a professional, friction-minimized experience retain more clients, receive more referrals, and spend less on remediation. Firms that tolerate persistent onboarding errors — accepting them as an inevitable cost of doing business — cede competitive advantage and accumulate risk that compounds over time.

Core Concept

Error Propagation — The mechanism by which an error introduced at one phase of the onboarding process cascades through subsequent phases, amplifying its impact at each stage. A missing document (Lesson 18.2) delays KYC (Lesson 18.3), which delays compliance review (Lesson 18.4), which delays activation (Lesson 18.6) — and if the account is activated despite the gap, the missing document becomes a compliance deficiency that must be remediated under time pressure. Understanding error propagation paths is essential for designing controls at the optimal interception point.

Root Cause vs. Symptom — The distinction between the fundamental cause of a problem and its visible manifestation. The symptom of an onboarding error may be a rejected trade, an incorrect report, or a billing discrepancy — but the root cause is the upstream process failure that produced the incorrect account configuration. Effective improvement targets root causes; symptom-level fixes address individual instances but do not prevent recurrence.

Process Maturity — The assessment of how well-developed, controlled, and continuously improved an organization's onboarding process is. Immature processes are ad hoc, dependent on individual knowledge, and reactive to errors. Mature processes are standardized, controlled through documented procedures and checkpoints, measured through quality metrics, and continuously improved through systematic analysis of error data and process performance.

Onboarding Error Taxonomy by Phase

Phase 1 — Initiation Errors (Lesson 18.1)

  • Incorrect account type selection — The relationship manager requests the wrong account type (e.g., individual instead of trust, taxable instead of IRA), setting the entire onboarding process on the wrong track. Every subsequent step — documentation collection, compliance review, system setup — is based on the incorrect type, requiring restart when the error is eventually discovered.
  • Missing or incorrect client information at intake — Basic information captured incorrectly at initiation (misspelled name, wrong address, incorrect SSN) propagates through every system that references it and may not be caught until a downstream process fails.
  • No designated onboarding coordinator — Without a single owner, tasks are not tracked, follow-ups are missed, and the client receives inconsistent communication from multiple people.

Phase 2 — Documentation Errors (Lesson 18.2)

  • Incomplete document collection — Required documents are not collected, either because the checklist is incomplete for the account type or because follow-up on outstanding items lapses. This is the single most common onboarding error category.
  • Unverified documents accepted — Documents are collected but not checked for completeness, validity, or accuracy — unsigned forms, expired IDs, W-9s with incorrect TINs — creating a false sense of completion.
  • Documents collected but not properly stored or indexed — Documents are received but saved in unstructured locations (email attachments, personal folders), making retrieval difficult during compliance review, audit, or examination.

Phase 3 — KYC Errors (Lesson 18.3)

  • Incomplete beneficial ownership identification — Entity structures are not fully analyzed to identify all individuals with 25%+ ownership or significant management responsibility, leaving the firm without complete knowledge of the parties behind the account.
  • Stale screening results — Sanctions and PEP screening is performed but not refreshed when there is a significant delay between screening and activation, potentially missing additions to sanctions lists during the gap period.
  • Inadequate enhanced due diligence for high-risk clients — The firm applies standard due diligence procedures to a client that should receive enhanced review, failing to investigate source of wealth, source of funds, or unusual transaction patterns.

Phase 4 — Compliance Errors (Lesson 18.4)

  • Suitability assessment based on incomplete or incorrect investment profile — The compliance review approves a strategy based on investment profile data that does not accurately reflect the client's true circumstances, creating an unsuitable recommendation that may persist for years.
  • Jurisdictional compliance not analyzed — The firm accepts a client without verifying its authority to provide advisory services in the client's jurisdiction, creating a relationship that may be legally unenforceable.
  • Compliance conditions approved but not implemented in systems — The compliance team approves the account with conditions (restricted securities list, enhanced monitoring, specific investment constraints), but these conditions are not translated into the compliance monitoring system, rendering them ineffective.

Phase 5 — System Setup Errors (Lesson 18.5)

  • Cross-system inconsistency — The account is coded correctly in some systems but incorrectly in others, producing conflicting results in reporting, billing, compliance, and trading.
  • Incorrect fee schedule assignment — The billing system is configured with the wrong fee rate, producing incorrect invoices every billing cycle until discovered — potentially overcharging or undercharging the client for months or years.
  • Wrong investment model assignment — The portfolio management system links the account to the wrong model, generating rebalancing trades that implement an incorrect investment strategy.
  • Placeholder values left in production — Temporary coding entered during setup is not replaced with final values before activation, causing the account to operate with incorrect parameters.

Phase 6 — Activation Errors (Lesson 18.6)

  • Premature activation — The account is activated before all prerequisites are met — documentation incomplete, compliance review pending, system configuration unverified — creating regulatory exposure and operational risk from the first day of active processing.
  • Funding coordination failures — Wire transfers are misdirected due to unverified instructions, ACAT transfers are rejected due to name mismatches, or in-kind transfers of complex assets are not properly coordinated with the receiving custodian.
  • No post-activation verification — The account is activated without subsequent review of processing outputs, allowing setup errors to produce incorrect results for an extended period before discovery.
  • Incomplete handoff to ongoing operations — The onboarding team completes its work without transferring institutional knowledge to the operations team, resulting in the loss of relationship context, coding rationale, and special considerations.

Common Bottlenecks and Their Root Causes

Beyond individual errors, onboarding processes are vulnerable to systemic bottlenecks — recurring points where the process consistently slows down, creating delays that frustrate clients, pressure staff to take shortcuts, and increase the risk of errors.

Bottleneck 1: Document Collection from Clients

Symptom: Accounts remain in "pending documentation" status for weeks because clients have not provided required items. Root cause: Clients do not understand what is needed, why it is needed, or how to provide it. Documentation requests are presented as a long undifferentiated list rather than a prioritized, explained set of requirements. Mitigation: Account-type-specific checklists with explanations, digital intake portals that guide clients through the process, progressive collection that prioritizes critical items, and proactive follow-up by the onboarding coordinator at defined intervals.

Bottleneck 2: Compliance Review Delays

Symptom: Accounts wait days or weeks for compliance review, even when documentation is complete. Root cause: The compliance team is understaffed for the volume of reviews, reviews are submitted with incomplete information requiring return for additional data, or the review process lacks prioritization (first-in-first-out without regard for account complexity or urgency). Mitigation: Complete pre-review checklists that ensure submissions are review-ready, SLAs for compliance review turnaround by account tier, dedicated compliance capacity for high-priority reviews, and parallel processing of KYC screening while documentation collection is still in progress.

Bottleneck 3: Custodian Account Setup

Symptom: Internal setup is complete but the account cannot be activated because the custodian has not finished its own account establishment process. Root cause: Custodian account applications are submitted late in the process, custodian requirements are not fully understood at initiation, or custodian processing timelines are not factored into the overall onboarding schedule. Mitigation: Submit custodian applications as early as possible in the process (ideally in parallel with documentation collection), maintain current knowledge of each custodian's requirements and processing timelines, and establish expedited processing relationships with key custodians for high-priority accounts.

Bottleneck 4: Cross-System Data Entry

Symptom: System setup takes longer than expected because the same data must be entered into multiple systems, and each system has its own interface, format requirements, and access controls. Root cause: Systems are not integrated — each requires independent manual data entry. Mitigation: Implement a centralized onboarding platform that captures data once and propagates it to all downstream systems through automated interfaces. Where full automation is not feasible, implement a master data entry form that captures all required fields in a single session, with system-specific entries derived from the master form rather than independently keyed.

Real-World Example

A national wealth management firm ($45 billion AUM, 850 relationship managers, approximately 3,200 new accounts per year) undertook a comprehensive onboarding quality improvement initiative after its annual operational risk assessment revealed that onboarding-related errors accounted for 37% of all operational exceptions and 28% of all client complaints in the previous year.

The firm began by analyzing 12 months of onboarding error data, categorizing 847 documented errors by phase, type, root cause, detection point, and remediation cost. The analysis revealed several patterns: 34% of errors originated in the documentation collection phase (incomplete documents accepted without verification), 26% originated in system setup (cross-system inconsistencies, primarily between the portfolio management and billing systems), 18% originated in the compliance review phase (conditions approved but not implemented in monitoring systems), 14% originated at activation (premature activation with incomplete prerequisites), and 8% were initiation errors (incorrect account type or client information at intake).

Rather than addressing each error category independently, the firm designed an integrated improvement program targeting the root causes that cut across multiple categories. The first initiative was implementing a digital onboarding platform that replaced the firm's paper-based and email-driven process with a structured workflow system. The platform enforced sequential phase completion (no phase could be marked complete until all required items were verified), automated the documentation checklist by account type (eliminating the generic-checklist problem), provided real-time status visibility to the onboarding coordinator, the relationship manager, and the client, and generated automated alerts for items approaching deadline without completion.

The second initiative addressed cross-system consistency by designating the portfolio management system as the system of record for all account parameters and implementing nightly automated reconciliation between the PMS and all dependent systems (billing, compliance monitoring, reporting). Mismatches detected by the reconciliation generated alerts that required same-day resolution, and the reconciliation was run on all accounts — not just new ones — to detect inconsistencies that may have existed prior to the improvement program.

The third initiative restructured the compliance review process to include a mandatory "conditions implementation verification" step: when the compliance team approved an account with conditions, the specific system configurations required to implement those conditions were documented in the approval memo, and the operations team was required to demonstrate the configurations to the compliance team before the account could proceed to activation.

Twelve months after full implementation, the firm's onboarding error rate had decreased by 62%. Documentation errors dropped by 71% (from 288 to 84 per year). System setup errors dropped by 78% (from 220 to 48). Compliance implementation errors dropped by 89% (from 152 to 17). Average time-to-activation decreased by 31% despite the additional verification steps, because the structured platform eliminated the delays caused by lost documents, missed follow-ups, and incomplete submissions. Client complaints related to onboarding decreased by 54%. The estimated annual cost savings from reduced remediation — based on the firm's calculated average remediation cost of $4,200 per significant error — was approximately $2.3 million.

Synthesis: Building a Resilient Onboarding Operation

The lessons of this unit reveal that onboarding quality is not the product of any single phase or control — it is the emergent property of a well-designed, well-controlled, and continuously improved end-to-end process. Each phase depends on the quality of the phases before it: system setup (Lesson 18.5) depends on accurate compliance review (Lesson 18.4), which depends on complete KYC verification (Lesson 18.3), which depends on thorough documentation collection (Lesson 18.2), which depends on correct initiation (Lesson 18.1). The activation workflow (Lesson 18.6) serves as the final verification that the entire chain has functioned correctly. And this capstone lesson (18.7) provides the diagnostic framework for identifying where the chain breaks and how to strengthen it.

A resilient onboarding operation exhibits five characteristics:

  • Standardization — Documented procedures for each account type and phase, ensuring consistency regardless of which individual performs the work. Standardization does not mean rigidity — it means a defined baseline process with documented variations for different account types and client scenarios.
  • Visibility — Real-time status tracking that allows all participants — the onboarding coordinator, the relationship manager, the compliance team, and ideally the client — to see where each account stands in the process, what is complete, what is pending, and what is blocking progress.
  • Control — Quality gates at each phase transition that prevent the process from advancing until prerequisites are verified. Hard controls (system-enforced prerequisites that cannot be bypassed) are preferred over soft controls (reminders or checklists that can be overridden). The most critical control is at activation: no account becomes active until all phases are documented as complete.
  • Measurement — Quantitative tracking of onboarding quality metrics: error rates by phase and type, time-to-activation by account type, documentation deficiency rates, compliance review turnaround times, post-activation exception rates, and client satisfaction scores. What is not measured cannot be improved.
  • Continuous Improvement — Systematic analysis of error and performance data to identify trends, root causes, and improvement opportunities. Effective improvement is data-driven: it targets the areas where the data shows the greatest quality gaps, not the areas where anecdotal complaints are loudest.

The central insight of Unit 18 is that onboarding is not an administrative overhead function — it is a strategic operational capability. The quality, speed, and reliability of the onboarding process directly affects client acquisition and retention, regulatory compliance, operational efficiency, and competitive positioning. Organizations that invest in onboarding excellence — through process design, technology enablement, staff training, and continuous improvement — build a foundation of operational quality that supports every subsequent interaction with every client for the entire duration of every relationship.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Treating onboarding errors as isolated incidents rather than systemic patterns

When each error is investigated and corrected individually — without analyzing whether it represents a recurring pattern — the organization misses the opportunity to address root causes. A firm that corrects 50 individual fee schedule errors without recognizing that they all stem from the same manual dual-entry process is treating symptoms while the disease continues to produce new cases. Error data must be aggregated, categorized, and analyzed for patterns that indicate systemic process weaknesses.

Mistake 2: Responding to onboarding delays by reducing quality controls

When the onboarding process takes too long, the tempting response is to eliminate review steps, reduce documentation requirements, or relax activation prerequisites. This is precisely the wrong response — it accelerates the process at the cost of quality, producing a higher volume of errors whose remediation consumes more time than the controls saved. The correct response is to analyze the bottleneck, identify its root cause, and address the cause without reducing the controls. Often, the bottleneck is not the controls themselves but the preparation that precedes them — incomplete submissions, unclear requirements, or sequential processing of tasks that could be parallelized.

Mistake 3: Not investing in technology to support the onboarding process

Firms that continue to manage onboarding through email, spreadsheets, and paper forms accept a level of manual error, poor visibility, and process fragility that technology-enabled processes eliminate. A dedicated onboarding platform with workflow management, document management, automated checklists, status tracking, and system integration is not a luxury — it is a baseline capability for any firm that processes more than a handful of new accounts per month.

Mistake 4: Not training staff on the "why" behind onboarding requirements

Operations staff who understand only what to do — but not why each step matters — are unable to exercise judgment when situations deviate from the standard process. They may accept a clearly deficient document because the checklist says "trust document — received," without understanding that a trust certificate missing the EIN renders the document incomplete for its intended purpose. Training must cover not just the mechanics of the process but the regulatory, operational, and risk rationale behind each requirement.

Mistake 5: Not measuring onboarding quality or holding the process accountable to performance standards

Without quantitative measurement, onboarding quality is assessed through impressions and anecdotes — which tend to focus on spectacular failures while ignoring the steady accumulation of moderate errors. Establishing clear metrics (error rate, time-to-activation, deficiency rate, client satisfaction) and reviewing them regularly against defined targets creates accountability and makes quality trends visible before they escalate into crises.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Error Propagation Analysis

Select three onboarding errors from the taxonomy presented in this lesson: one documentation error, one compliance error, and one system setup error. For each, trace the complete propagation path: where the error originates, which subsequent phases it affects, how it manifests in downstream operations (trading, reporting, billing, compliance), when it is most likely to be discovered, and what the full remediation effort involves. Then identify the optimal interception point — the earliest stage at which a feasible control could detect or prevent the error — and design the control.

Exercise 2: Onboarding Process Maturity Assessment

Design an onboarding process maturity assessment framework. Define 5 maturity levels (from ad hoc/reactive to optimized/predictive) and specify the characteristics of each level across multiple dimensions: process documentation, technology enablement, quality controls, measurement, training, and continuous improvement. Create the assessment questionnaire that an organization would use to evaluate its current maturity level, and provide recommendations for advancing from each level to the next.

Exercise 3: Improvement Program Design

Using the error data from the real-world example (847 errors across 5 phases), design a prioritized improvement program. For each initiative, specify: the target error category, the root cause being addressed, the proposed solution, the implementation timeline, the expected impact (percentage reduction in the target error category), the cost of implementation, and the expected return on investment. Sequence the initiatives to address the highest-impact opportunities first, considering both the error frequency and the per-error remediation cost.

Exercise 4: Onboarding Metrics Dashboard

Design an onboarding quality metrics dashboard for a wealth management firm. Include: the key performance indicators (KPIs) to track, the data sources for each KPI, the calculation methodology, the visualization format, the review frequency, the audience (who sees the dashboard and what decisions it informs), and the escalation criteria (what metric thresholds trigger management attention). Include both leading indicators (metrics that predict future quality) and lagging indicators (metrics that measure past performance).

Key Terms

Error Propagation — The mechanism by which an error introduced at one onboarding phase cascades through subsequent phases, amplifying its impact and complicating remediation at each stage.

Root Cause Analysis — The systematic investigation of an error to identify its fundamental cause — the process weakness, system deficiency, or human factor that produced the error — as distinguished from its visible symptoms.

Process Maturity — The assessment of how well-developed, standardized, controlled, measured, and continuously improved an organization's onboarding process is, ranging from ad hoc (dependent on individual knowledge) to optimized (predictive and self-improving).

Error of Omission — An onboarding failure caused by something that should have been done but was not — a missing document, an unperformed screening, an unconfigured system parameter.

Error of Commission — An onboarding failure caused by something done incorrectly — wrong data entered, wrong code assigned, wrong identity verified, wrong model linked.

Error of Timing — An onboarding failure caused by actions performed in the wrong sequence or at the wrong time — activating before compliance approval, trading before funding confirmation, configuring systems before custodian setup.

Quality Gate — A mandatory checkpoint in the onboarding process that prevents advancement to the next phase until defined prerequisites are verified and documented as complete.

Hard Control — A system-enforced prerequisite that physically prevents the process from advancing without satisfaction — as opposed to a soft control (checklist or reminder) that can be overridden.

Continuous Improvement — The systematic practice of analyzing error and performance data, identifying root causes and improvement opportunities, implementing targeted changes, and measuring the results to drive ongoing quality enhancement.

Onboarding Platform — A technology system that manages the end-to-end onboarding workflow, providing structured checklists, document management, status tracking, system integration, and automated alerts to replace manual, email-driven processes.

Lesson Summary

  • Onboarding errors fall into three categories — omission, commission, and timing — each requiring different detection and prevention strategies, and all subject to error propagation across the sequential phases of the onboarding lifecycle.
  • Common bottlenecks — document collection delays, compliance review backlogs, custodian setup coordination, and cross-system data entry — have identifiable root causes that can be addressed through process redesign, technology enablement, and workflow optimization.
  • Effective risk mitigation targets root causes rather than symptoms: implementing controls at the optimal interception point, preventing errors from entering the process rather than catching them after propagation.
  • A resilient onboarding operation is built on five pillars: standardization, visibility, control, measurement, and continuous improvement — creating a process that is consistent, transparent, quality-gated, quantitatively managed, and systematically enhanced.
  • Onboarding quality is a strategic capability, not an administrative overhead function — it directly affects client acquisition, retention, regulatory compliance, operational efficiency, and competitive positioning.
  • Technology enablement — particularly a structured onboarding platform that replaces email-driven, paper-based processes — is a baseline requirement for any firm processing meaningful account volumes, providing the workflow management, document management, status tracking, and system integration that manual processes cannot achieve at scale.

Unit 18 Conclusion

This lesson concludes Unit 18: Account Onboarding and Client Setup. Across seven lessons, the unit has traced the complete onboarding lifecycle — from the initial account request and process overview (Lesson 18.1), through the documentation and data collection requirements (Lesson 18.2), KYC and identity verification (Lesson 18.3), compliance and regulatory review (Lesson 18.4), account coding and system configuration (Lesson 18.5), and approval and activation workflows (Lesson 18.6) — arriving at this capstone examination of onboarding errors, risk points, and the resilience characteristics that distinguish effective onboarding operations from fragile ones.

The central insight of this unit is that account onboarding is the foundation upon which the entire client relationship is built. Every transaction, every report, every fee calculation, every compliance check, and every client interaction for the entire duration of the relationship depends on the accuracy and completeness of the account setup established during onboarding. Organizations that treat onboarding as a high-stakes, strategically important operational process — investing in standardization, technology, training, measurement, and continuous improvement — build relationships on a foundation of operational excellence. Organizations that treat onboarding as routine administration — accepting errors as inevitable and investing minimally in process quality — build relationships on foundations that are prone to cracks, each of which compounds the risk of structural failure over time.

Mastery of the onboarding process — understanding not just the mechanics of each phase but the interconnections between phases, the characteristic failure modes, and the strategies for building resilience — is a foundational competency for professionals in wealth and asset operations. It is the skill that ensures every client relationship begins right, and that the operational infrastructure supporting that relationship remains sound for its entire duration.

Lesson 23.6: Borrower Communication and Exception Management

Study how relationship teams, servicing staff, and credit officers coordinate with borrowers to resolve compliance issues and escalate emerging problems.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Credit & Lending Operations Track Unit 23: Covenant Monitoring Foundations Lesson 23.6

Where This Lesson Fits

Previous lessons explained how lenders monitor covenant compliance, collect borrower reports, perform ratio testing, and respond when covenant breaches occur.

Once an exception or compliance concern is identified, lenders must coordinate both internally and with the borrower to investigate the issue and determine the appropriate response.

Lesson 23.6 examines how borrower communication and internal coordination support effective covenant monitoring and credit risk management.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how lenders communicate with borrowers, investigate covenant exceptions, coordinate internal review, and escalate potential credit concerns.

Lesson Overview

Covenant monitoring frequently generates questions that require discussion between lender and borrower.

Financial results may differ from expectations. Required reports may arrive late. A covenant ratio may approach or exceed a threshold. Borrowers may request waivers or amendments.

In these situations, communication becomes essential. Lenders must gather information from the borrower, verify facts, and evaluate whether the issue represents a temporary challenge or a deeper credit concern.

Role of Relationship Managers

Relationship managers typically serve as the primary point of contact between the lender and the borrower.

When covenant monitoring identifies a potential issue, the relationship manager often communicates directly with borrower management to understand the situation.

This may involve discussing financial results, reviewing operational developments, or clarifying the reasons behind reporting delays or covenant breaches.

The relationship manager then shares the information internally with credit and servicing teams so that the lender can evaluate the issue accurately.

Role of Credit and Servicing Teams

Credit analysts and servicing teams support the technical side of exception review.

They verify covenant calculations, analyze borrower financial information, and review reporting materials to determine whether the borrower remains compliant.

If the borrower requests a waiver or amendment, credit teams evaluate the financial implications and provide recommendations to decision-makers within the lending institution.

Information Gathering and Clarification

Effective communication begins with accurate information.

When a covenant exception occurs, lenders often request additional explanation from the borrower.

This may include updated financial projections, operational updates, explanations of unusual expenses, or details about changes in market conditions.

These discussions help the lender determine whether the issue is temporary, structural, or likely to worsen.

Escalation of Credit Concerns

If monitoring reveals significant concerns about borrower performance, the issue may be escalated internally.

Escalation may involve presenting the situation to senior credit officers, risk committees, or other oversight bodies responsible for managing institutional credit risk.

Escalation ensures that serious issues receive appropriate attention and that decisions reflect the lender's broader risk policies.

Coordinating Waivers and Amendments

When borrowers request relief from covenant requirements, communication becomes even more important.

Relationship managers discuss the request with the borrower. Credit teams evaluate financial implications. Legal or documentation teams may assist in drafting formal amendments.

This coordination ensures that any changes to the loan agreement are properly reviewed, approved, and documented.

Maintaining Professional Borrower Relationships

Even when compliance issues arise, lenders aim to maintain constructive working relationships with borrowers.

Open communication allows both sides to address challenges early and work toward solutions that protect the lender while supporting the borrower's ability to recover or stabilize performance.

Professional communication also helps ensure that borrowers continue providing timely information during periods of financial stress.

Real-World Example

A borrower submits quarterly financial statements showing that the leverage ratio has increased close to the covenant limit.

Although the borrower remains technically compliant, the lender wants to understand the trend.

The relationship manager contacts borrower management to discuss the financial results. Management explains that temporary inventory buildup increased debt levels but expects the ratio to improve once seasonal sales occur.

The credit team reviews the borrower's explanation and supporting projections. While no waiver is required, the lender decides to monitor the next reporting period closely and requests updated forecasts.

This communication helps the lender evaluate risk while maintaining an open dialogue with the borrower.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Avoiding borrower communication when problems appear

Early communication helps lenders understand issues before they escalate.

Mistake 2: Treating covenant exceptions as purely administrative issues

Exceptions often require deeper credit analysis and internal coordination.

Mistake 3: Failing to escalate serious concerns

Significant credit risks should be reviewed by appropriate internal decision-makers.

Key Terms

Borrower Communication — Interaction between lenders and borrower management regarding financial performance, covenant compliance, and credit issues.

Exception Management — The process of reviewing and resolving covenant breaches, reporting delays, or other compliance issues.

Credit Escalation — Internal review of emerging credit risks by senior decision-makers or oversight committees.

Relationship Manager — The lender representative responsible for maintaining the borrower relationship and facilitating communication.

Credit Review Coordination — Collaboration among relationship managers, analysts, and servicing staff when evaluating borrower performance.

Lesson Summary

  • Borrower communication is essential when covenant monitoring identifies compliance issues.
  • Relationship managers serve as the primary communication link between lender and borrower.
  • Credit and servicing teams analyze borrower information and support exception review.
  • Serious concerns may be escalated to internal credit committees or senior risk managers.
  • Effective communication helps lenders evaluate risk and maintain constructive borrower relationships.

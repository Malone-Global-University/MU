Lesson 17.5: Credit Memos and Underwriting Recommendation Writing

Learn how underwriters summarize borrower condition, transaction structure, risks, mitigants, and recommendation logic in formal credit memoranda that support disciplined institutional decision-making.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Credit & Lending Operations Track Unit 17: Underwriting Foundations Lesson 17.5: Credit Memos and Underwriting Recommendation Writing

Where This Lesson Fits

Earlier lessons in Unit 17 explained how underwriting workflows begin with borrower review, move through financial analysis, and continue into structure assessment, collateral review, covenant evaluation, and risk identification.

Lesson 17.5 explains how those analytical findings are turned into a formal written recommendation. Underwriters do not merely reach an internal conclusion. They must document their reasoning in a way that approval authorities, credit officers, managers, and reviewers can understand and evaluate.

The credit memo is the tool that brings the full underwriting analysis together into one decision document.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how underwriters prepare credit memoranda that summarize borrower condition, transaction structure, identified risks, mitigants, and the logic behind an approval, decline, or modified recommendation.

Lesson Overview

A lending institution needs more than analysis. It needs documented analysis. Credit memos provide the formal written record of how a credit request was evaluated and why a particular recommendation was reached.

These documents support internal approval, create accountability, preserve the reasoning behind credit decisions, and allow later review by auditors, examiners, and management.

Recommendation writing is therefore both an analytical task and a control function.

Why Credit Memos Matter

Credit memos translate underwriting work into institutional communication. Approval authorities often rely on the written memo to understand the borrower, the request, the main credit strengths, the key weaknesses, and the structure being recommended.

Without a clear memo, even sound analysis may be difficult to evaluate or approve. A strong underwriting process therefore requires clear writing that is organized, evidence-based, and aligned with the institution’s decision standards.

The memo becomes the official explanation of the underwriting view.

What a Credit Memo Usually Includes

While formats vary by institution, most credit memoranda include several common elements. These often begin with a summary of the borrower, the purpose of the request, and the amount and type of credit being proposed.

The memo then usually explains borrower history, financial performance, repayment analysis, loan structure, collateral support, guarantor information, covenant protections, and other key risk considerations. It also identifies major strengths and weaknesses in the transaction.

Finally, the memo presents the underwriter’s recommendation and the reasoning that supports it.

Summarizing Borrower Condition Clearly

One of the memo’s core tasks is explaining the borrower’s overall condition in a concise and useful way. This means presenting relevant background, business model or income sources, performance history, and key financial characteristics without overwhelming the reader with unnecessary detail.

Good underwriting writing highlights what matters most. It explains whether the borrower appears stable or volatile, whether recent trends are favorable or concerning, and what facts are most important to repayment confidence.

A strong summary helps decision-makers understand the borrower before moving into deeper analysis.

Presenting Structure, Risks, and Mitigants

The memo must also explain how the proposed transaction is structured and why that structure is appropriate. This includes loan purpose, tenor, repayment design, collateral, guarantees, covenants, and any special approval conditions.

Equally important, the underwriter must identify material risks openly. Strong memo writing does not hide weaknesses. It describes them directly and explains whether they are acceptable, mitigated, or still unresolved.

This balance between risk identification and risk mitigation is central to credible recommendation writing.

Explaining Recommendation Logic

A credit memo should not simply state that a request is recommended for approval or decline. It should explain why. Decision-makers need to see how the analysis leads logically to the conclusion.

This means linking borrower condition, repayment evaluation, structure review, and identified risks to the final recommendation. If the request is recommended with conditions, those conditions should be clearly tied to the risks they are meant to address.

Recommendation logic is what transforms the memo from a description into a persuasive underwriting document.

Qualities of Strong Underwriting Writing

Effective underwriting writing is clear, organized, balanced, and evidence-based. It avoids vague language, unsupported conclusions, and unnecessary repetition. Readers should be able to follow the memo’s reasoning from beginning to end.

Strong memos also distinguish between facts, analysis, and recommendation. The underwriter shows the factual basis, interprets what those facts mean, and then presents a conclusion that fits the analysis.

This structure improves both decision quality and institutional accountability.

Credit Memos as a Control and Recordkeeping Tool

The memo is not only for immediate approval. It also becomes part of the institution’s credit record. Later reviewers may use it to understand why a loan was approved, what risks were identified at origination, and what protections were expected to manage those risks.

This makes memo quality important from a governance standpoint. Poorly written memos weaken transparency and make later review more difficult. Well-written memos support accountability across the life of the credit.

In this way, underwriting writing contributes to both operational discipline and institutional memory.

Where Recommendation Writing Fits in the Workflow

Recommendation writing generally follows borrower review, repayment analysis, and structure assessment. By the time the memo is drafted, the underwriter has already formed a credit view and now must present that view in formal written form.

This stage connects analysis to approval. It is the point where underwriting work becomes actionable for credit committees, delegated approvers, and other institutional decision-makers.

That is why recommendation writing is treated as a major underwriting function rather than a minor administrative step.

Real-World Example

A privately owned service company requests a loan to refinance existing debt and fund moderate expansion. The underwriter has already reviewed the borrower’s historical performance, analyzed cash flow, assessed leverage, and considered the proposed collateral and covenant structure.

In the credit memo, the underwriter summarizes the company’s operating stability, notes that repayment capacity is acceptable but somewhat pressured by recent margin decline, and highlights that the new structure improves maturity alignment while adding reporting covenants.

The memo also identifies customer concentration as a key risk and explains that tighter reporting and leverage monitoring are recommended as mitigants. This written recommendation allows decision-makers to understand both the strengths and the caution points before approving the request.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Writing conclusions without showing the reasoning

A strong credit memo explains how the analysis leads to the final recommendation.

Mistake 2: Hiding or minimizing important risks

Credible underwriting writing identifies material weaknesses directly and explains how they are managed or why they remain acceptable.

Mistake 3: Confusing detail with clarity

A memo should be thorough, but it should also remain organized, readable, and focused on decision-relevant facts.

Key Terms

Credit Memo — A formal written document that summarizes underwriting analysis and presents a recommendation for a credit request.

Underwriting Recommendation — The documented conclusion regarding whether a request should be approved, modified, or declined.

Mitigant — A structural feature, condition, or support element intended to reduce identified credit risk.

Recommendation Logic — The reasoning that connects analysis, risks, and protections to the final credit conclusion.

Credit Record — The official documented history of how a credit request was reviewed, recommended, and approved.

Lesson Summary

  • Credit memos turn underwriting analysis into a formal written recommendation.
  • They typically summarize borrower condition, repayment evaluation, transaction structure, risks, and mitigants.
  • Strong memo writing is clear, organized, balanced, and evidence-based.
  • Recommendation logic explains why the final approval, modification, or decline conclusion is justified.
  • Credit memos also serve as control records that support accountability and later review.

Unit 21: Clearing Submission and Netting Processes

Learn how executed and allocated trades are submitted to clearinghouses, validated through clearing systems, and transformed into net settlement obligations through institutional netting processes.

Where This Unit Fits

This unit continues Layer 4: Execution Workflows by examining how allocated trades move into clearing infrastructure. After trades are executed (Unit 18), captured (Unit 19), and allocated to accounts (Unit 20), they must be submitted to clearing systems that coordinate obligations between trading counterparties.

Clearinghouses play a central role in modern securities markets by validating trades, reducing counterparty risk, and converting large volumes of transactions into smaller net obligations. Later units on settlement processing and fails management depend on the clearing processes introduced here.

Unit Overview

Once trades have been allocated and confirmed, they are submitted to clearinghouses or central counterparties (CCPs). Clearing systems validate trade details, confirm counterparty agreement, and prepare the trade for settlement processing.

One of the most important functions of clearing is netting. Rather than settling each trade individually, clearinghouses aggregate transactions and calculate the net obligations between market participants. This reduces operational complexity and minimizes settlement exposures across the market.

Through these processes, clearinghouses transform large volumes of trade activity into manageable settlement positions. The clearing workflow includes trade submission, validation checks, position netting, obligation calculation, confirmation messaging, and exception management.

Why This Matters in Securities Operations

Clearing infrastructure is critical to the stability of securities markets. Without clearinghouses, each trade would expose participants to direct counterparty risk and require individual settlement processing.

By centralizing obligations and netting positions, clearing systems reduce risk, improve operational efficiency, and enable high-volume trading across modern financial markets. Operations teams rely on clearing processes to ensure trades are validated, obligations are calculated accurately, and exceptions are resolved before settlement occurs.

What You’ll Learn

Core Concepts

  • How trades are submitted to clearinghouses after execution and allocation
  • How clearing systems validate trade information
  • How netting reduces the number of settlement obligations
  • How clearing confirmations communicate validated trades
  • How net settlement obligations are calculated
  • How clearing exceptions and mismatches are resolved

Operational Competencies

  • Explain how clearinghouses process submitted trades
  • Understand the role of netting in reducing settlement activity
  • Recognize how clearing confirmations validate trading activity
  • Identify how clearing systems calculate final settlement obligations
  • Describe how operational teams resolve clearing exceptions

Lessons in This Unit

Clearing Foundations

  • Lesson 21.1: Trade Submission to Clearing

    Learn how executed and allocated trades are transmitted to clearinghouses for validation and processing.

  • Lesson 21.2: Clearing Validation and Acceptance

    Study how clearing systems verify trade details and confirm that both sides of a transaction match.

  • Lesson 21.3: Position Netting Workflows

    Examine how clearinghouses aggregate transactions and calculate net trading positions between participants.

  • Lesson 21.4: Clearing Confirmations

    Understand how clearing systems distribute confirmations to market participants once trades are accepted.

Obligations and Exception Handling

  • Lesson 21.5: Net Obligation Calculation

    Learn how clearinghouses calculate the final securities and cash obligations that must be settled.

  • Lesson 21.6: Clearing Exception Resolution

    Study how operations teams investigate mismatches, rejected trades, and other clearing exceptions.

  • Lesson 21.7: The Clearing Submission Workflow

    Connect trade submission, validation, netting, confirmations, and obligation calculation into a unified clearing model.

Connected Units

  • Unit 20: Trade Allocation and Account Assignment

    Review how trades are allocated across accounts before submission to clearing systems.

  • Unit 14: Clearinghouses and Central Counterparty Infrastructure

    Study the institutional structure and risk management role of clearinghouses.

  • Unit 22: Settlement Processing and Fails Management

    Continue the workflow by examining how cleared obligations move into settlement systems.

Practical Application

By the end of this unit, students should be able to explain how trades are submitted to clearinghouses, understand how clearing systems validate transactions and net positions, interpret how settlement obligations are calculated, and recognize how operations teams resolve clearing exceptions before settlement occurs.

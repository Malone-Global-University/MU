Lesson 36.1: What Asset–Liability Management Does

Learn how asset–liability management coordinates balance sheet structure, interest sensitivity, and financial stability in banks.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Bank Operations Track Unit 36: Asset–Liability Management Lesson 36.1

Where This Lesson Fits

This unit examines how banks manage structural interest rate exposure through asset–liability management, funding behavior analysis, balance sheet sensitivity measurement, and governance oversight.

Before studying repricing gaps, deposit behavior, earnings sensitivity, and risk limits in later lessons, students first need a clear understanding of what asset–liability management is and why it matters.

This opening lesson introduces asset–liability management as a core banking function that connects lending, deposits, investments, funding strategy, and risk governance into a coordinated balance sheet discipline.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain what asset–liability management does, why banks use it, and how it supports balance sheet coordination, interest rate risk awareness, and institutional stability.

Lesson Overview

Banks do not simply gather deposits and make loans in isolation. They operate through an interconnected balance sheet in which asset yields, liability costs, funding stability, customer behavior, and interest rate movements affect one another.

Asset–liability management, often called ALM, is the function that helps the bank monitor and coordinate these relationships.

Its purpose is to ensure that the institution’s balance sheet remains financially sustainable under changing conditions, especially when market interest rates shift, customer deposit behavior changes, or funding structures become stressed.

What Asset–Liability Management Means

Asset–liability management is the process through which banks evaluate and manage the relationship between what they own and what they owe.

On the asset side, banks hold loans, securities, reserve balances, and other earning resources. On the liability side, they rely on deposits, borrowings, and other funding sources.

Because these categories often respond differently to time, rate changes, customer behavior, and contractual structure, the bank must actively monitor how the full balance sheet behaves rather than viewing each component separately.

Why Banks Need ALM

A bank can appear profitable in one period but still carry structural balance sheet weaknesses. For example, it may rely too heavily on unstable funding, hold assets that reprice much more slowly than liabilities, or become exposed to declining margins when rates change.

ALM helps identify these kinds of vulnerabilities before they become serious problems.

It gives management a framework for understanding whether the bank’s asset mix, liability structure, and interest sensitivity are aligned with the institution’s funding model, earnings goals, and risk tolerance.

Balance Sheet Coordination

One of ALM’s central roles is balance sheet coordination. Banks make decisions in many different business areas: lending teams originate loans, deposit teams gather customer funds, treasury manages liquidity, and investment teams may hold securities.

Each activity affects the overall financial structure of the bank.

ALM brings these separate activities into a unified view so the institution can understand how product growth, pricing decisions, maturity structure, and funding choices interact across the balance sheet.

Interest Rate Sensitivity

A major focus of ALM is interest rate sensitivity. When market rates move, the bank’s assets and liabilities do not always adjust at the same speed or by the same amount.

Some loans may stay fixed for long periods. Some deposits may reprice slowly or only partially. Certain investments may lose market value when rates rise, while borrowing costs may increase immediately.

ALM evaluates these differences to understand how changing rates could affect earnings, funding costs, and the longer-term economic value of the balance sheet.

Funding Structure and Stability

ALM also helps banks manage funding structure. Not all liabilities are equally stable. Some deposits are relationship-based and durable, while others may be highly rate-sensitive or quick to leave when better alternatives appear.

Understanding the composition and behavior of funding sources is critical because the liability side of the balance sheet affects both liquidity resilience and interest expense.

ALM therefore considers not only how much funding the bank has, but also how dependable, costly, and behaviorally sensitive that funding may be.

Earnings Protection and Financial Stability

Banks depend heavily on net interest income, which is the difference between interest earned on assets and interest paid on liabilities.

If rates change in a way that raises funding costs faster than asset yields, earnings can weaken. If asset values decline sharply under rate stress, balance sheet strength may also be affected.

ALM helps management anticipate these outcomes and take action before earnings volatility or structural imbalance undermines financial stability.

ALM as a Risk Management Function

Although ALM is closely connected to finance and treasury, it is also a risk management function. It helps the bank identify, measure, monitor, and control structural exposures that develop over time through ordinary business activity.

This includes interest rate risk, funding concentration concerns, deposit repricing uncertainty, and balance sheet mismatch between assets and liabilities.

Rather than reacting only after problems appear, ALM gives the institution a forward-looking way to assess how current balance sheet choices may perform under future conditions.

How ALM Supports Decision-Making

ALM supports management decisions about product pricing, deposit strategy, investment structure, funding mix, and overall balance sheet growth.

For example, if the bank sees that liabilities are likely to reprice faster than assets, management may reconsider loan pricing, investment duration, or funding strategy.

If deposits appear more volatile than expected, the bank may adjust assumptions, strengthen liquidity planning, or reduce reliance on sensitive funding categories.

In this way, ALM turns balance sheet analysis into practical guidance for managing banking operations.

Who Uses ALM Information

ALM is not confined to one specialist team. Its analysis is relevant to treasury, finance, risk management, senior management, and governance committees such as the asset–liability committee, often called ALCO.

These groups rely on ALM reporting to understand how the institution is positioned and whether current strategies remain consistent with policy limits and risk appetite.

As a result, ALM serves as a bridge between day-to-day banking operations and higher-level financial oversight.

Why ALM Matters in Banking Operations

Banking is fundamentally a maturity transformation business. Banks often fund themselves with liabilities that may be withdrawn or repriced relatively quickly while holding assets that mature or reprice more slowly.

That structure can support earnings, but it also creates risk. Without ALM, the bank could drift into mismatches that expose it to margin compression, valuation pressure, or unstable funding conditions.

ALM matters because it helps ensure that the operating model remains financially balanced as conditions change.

What Good Basic Interpretation Looks Like

A strong interpretation should explain that asset–liability management is the discipline banks use to coordinate the structure and behavior of assets and liabilities across the balance sheet.

Students should understand that ALM is not limited to pricing or liquidity alone. It is a broader framework for managing interest rate sensitivity, funding behavior, earnings exposure, and financial stability.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking ALM is only about treasury cash management

Treasury is closely related to ALM, but ALM addresses the broader structure and sensitivity of the bank’s balance sheet, not just daily cash positioning.

Assuming assets and liabilities respond to rates in the same way

Different products reprice at different times and with different degrees of sensitivity, which is why coordinated balance sheet analysis is necessary.

Believing ALM is only relevant during volatile rate environments

ALM is always important because structural exposures build over time even when interest rate conditions appear stable.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Balance Sheet Coordination

Explain why a bank cannot evaluate lending growth, deposit growth, and investment activity as separate decisions without considering their combined balance sheet effect.

Exercise 2: Rate Sensitivity

Describe how rising rates could affect a bank differently if its liabilities reprice faster than its assets.

Exercise 3: Funding Stability

Discuss why the behavior of deposit customers matters for asset–liability management even when total deposits appear strong.

Key Terms

Asset–Liability Management (ALM) — The function that coordinates a bank’s balance sheet structure, interest sensitivity, funding behavior, and financial stability.

Balance Sheet Structure — The composition and relationship of the bank’s assets, liabilities, and capital.

Interest Rate Sensitivity — The degree to which assets, liabilities, earnings, or economic value respond to changes in market interest rates.

Funding Structure — The mix of deposits, borrowings, and other liabilities used to finance the bank’s assets.

Net Interest Income — The difference between interest earned on assets and interest paid on liabilities.

ALCO — The asset–liability committee, a governance group that reviews balance sheet positioning, funding, and interest rate risk issues.

Lesson Summary

  • Asset–liability management coordinates the relationship between a bank’s assets, liabilities, and balance sheet strategy.
  • ALM helps banks understand interest rate sensitivity, funding behavior, and structural balance sheet exposure.
  • It supports decision-making on pricing, funding mix, investment structure, and earnings stability.
  • ALM is both a financial coordination function and a risk management discipline.
  • Its goal is to help maintain stable earnings, controlled exposure, and overall financial resilience.

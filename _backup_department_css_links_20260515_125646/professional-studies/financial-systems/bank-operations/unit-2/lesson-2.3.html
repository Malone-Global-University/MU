Lesson 2.3: Commercial Banks and Financial Intermediation

Examine how commercial banks gather deposits, extend credit, process payments, and serve households and businesses within the broader financial system.

Home Department Professional & Occupational Studies Financial Systems Bank Operations Track Unit 2: Structure of the Banking System Lesson 2.3

Where This Lesson Fits

The previous lesson explained how central banks anchor the wider monetary and liquidity environment. This lesson turns to the institutions most people interact with directly: commercial banks.

Commercial banks are the customer-facing core of the banking system. They gather deposits, provide loans, support payments, and offer financial services to households, businesses, and institutions. To understand the structure of the banking system, students must understand both what commercial banks do and how they connect savers, borrowers, and payment activity across the economy.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain the role of commercial banks, define financial intermediation, and describe how deposits, credit, and payment services connect commercial banks to the wider banking system.

Lesson Overview

Commercial banks are institutions that serve customers directly while operating inside the broader banking framework. They accept deposits, extend loans, process transactions, and provide accounts and services that allow money to be stored, transferred, and used throughout the economy.

Their role is often described through the idea of financial intermediation. Commercial banks stand between different financial actors. They gather funds from depositors and other sources, then direct those funds toward borrowers, payments, and other financial uses. In doing so, they help transform savings into economic activity.

Commercial banks therefore sit at the intersection of customers, markets, central banking structures, payment infrastructure, and regulatory oversight.

What Commercial Banks Are

A commercial bank is a financial institution that offers banking services to the public, businesses, and other organizations. Unlike central banks, commercial banks operate directly in customer markets. They compete for deposits, originate loans, manage accounts, and support day-to-day financial activity.

Some commercial banks focus heavily on households and small businesses. Others serve larger corporations, institutions, or specialized sectors. Even so, the common pattern remains the same: they connect funding, credit, and financial services to users throughout the economy.

Students should think of commercial banks as operating institutions that translate the broader banking system into practical financial services for customers.

Financial Intermediation

Financial intermediation is the process by which financial institutions connect parties with available funds to parties that need funding. Commercial banks are among the most important intermediaries in the economy.

A household may place savings into a deposit account. A business may need working capital or expansion financing. The bank stands between those positions. It gathers funds, evaluates creditworthiness, prices risk, and extends lending under terms that reflect repayment expectations and institutional constraints.

This does not mean every dollar deposited is passed directly to a specific borrower in a simple one-to-one way. Instead, the bank manages a balance sheet that pools funding sources and allocates assets across many credit, liquidity, and service functions at once.

Core Functions of Commercial Banks

  • Deposit Gathering — accepting customer funds into checking, savings, time deposit, and related account structures.
  • Credit Extension — making loans to households, businesses, and other borrowers under defined risk and pricing terms.
  • Payment Processing — supporting transfers, account debits, card activity, bill payment, and other transaction flows.
  • Account Services — maintaining balances, statements, account access, servicing, and customer support.
  • Liquidity Transformation — managing the timing difference between customer withdrawals, funding inflows, and asset commitments.
  • Relationship Banking — providing ongoing financial services that connect customers to broader banking capabilities.

These functions make commercial banks central not only to lending, but also to the everyday operation of money movement.

Deposits, Loans, and the Bank Balance Sheet

Commercial banks gather deposits from customers, but deposits are not simply stored in a passive vault. They become liabilities of the bank because the institution owes those funds to depositors on agreed terms. At the same time, the bank uses its funding base to hold reserves, maintain liquidity, make loans, and acquire other assets.

Loans become assets on the bank’s balance sheet because they represent claims on future repayment. Interest income from those assets helps support the bank’s earnings, while deposit costs, operating expenses, credit losses, and liquidity needs shape the institution’s financial condition.

This balance sheet structure allows banks to act as intermediaries at scale rather than as simple custodians of cash.

Commercial Banks in the Wider System

Although commercial banks interact directly with customers, they do not operate alone. They depend on central bank structures for reserve conditions and monetary environment. They depend on payment networks and settlement systems to move funds. They depend on regulators and supervisors to define legal and prudential expectations. They depend on customer and market confidence to maintain stable funding.

This means commercial banks are both independent business institutions and deeply embedded system participants. Their daily operations reflect internal strategy, but also the wider institutional framework around them.

Why Commercial Banks Matter

Commercial banks matter because they connect the abstract structure of the banking system to the practical financial lives of households and businesses. Most customers do not interact directly with central banks or regulators. They interact with commercial banks.

Through those interactions, commercial banks influence saving behavior, borrowing capacity, payment reliability, and access to financial tools. They help channel money through the economy in ways that affect spending, investment, employment, and business activity.

For that reason, commercial banks are not just participants in the system. They are one of its main operational engines.

System Perspective

A systems view helps students see that commercial banks are both service providers and institutional connectors. They translate deposits into lending, customer accounts into payment flows, and funding structures into usable financial services.

They also sit between multiple pressures at once. Customers want access, convenience, and trust. Borrowers want credit. Regulators want safety and compliance. Markets respond to risk and profitability. The bank must coordinate all of these demands within one operating structure.

Real-World Perspective

Consider a small business with payroll accounts, merchant deposits, a line of credit, card payment activity, and outgoing supplier transfers. A commercial bank may support all of these needs at once. From the customer’s perspective, this looks like a bundle of banking services. From the institutional perspective, it is a combination of deposit management, payment processing, credit extension, liquidity planning, and regulatory control.

This example shows why commercial banking is operationally complex even when customer interactions feel routine.

Common Misunderstandings

Thinking banks only hold money

Commercial banks do not simply store deposits. They transform funding into loans, payment services, and other balance sheet activities.

Assuming intermediation is a direct one-to-one transfer

Banks pool funds across many depositors and allocate them across many uses. Intermediation happens through institution-level balance sheet management, not simple matching.

Believing lending is the only important bank function

Lending is central, but commercial banks also provide payments, account servicing, liquidity support, and ongoing financial access for customers and businesses.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Intermediation Logic

Explain how a commercial bank connects depositors and borrowers without matching each depositor to a specific loan.

Exercise 2: Operational Role

List three core functions of commercial banks and briefly describe why each matters.

Exercise 3: System Placement

Why should commercial banks be understood as part of a wider banking system rather than as isolated firms?

Key Terms

Commercial Bank — A customer-facing banking institution that gathers deposits, extends credit, processes payments, and provides financial services within the broader banking system.

Financial Intermediation — The process by which institutions connect available funds to borrowers and other financial uses.

Deposit Liability — A customer balance that the bank owes back to the depositor under the terms of the account.

Loan Asset — A claim on future repayment held by the bank after extending credit to a borrower.

Liquidity Transformation — The management of timing differences between short-term funding availability and longer-term asset commitments.

Lesson Summary

  • Commercial banks are the main customer-facing institutions in the banking system.
  • They gather deposits, extend credit, process payments, and provide account services.
  • Financial intermediation explains how banks connect available funds to borrowers and economic activity.
  • Deposits and loans appear on opposite sides of the bank balance sheet and support banking operations at scale.
  • Commercial banks operate inside a wider framework shaped by central banks, regulators, and payment infrastructure.

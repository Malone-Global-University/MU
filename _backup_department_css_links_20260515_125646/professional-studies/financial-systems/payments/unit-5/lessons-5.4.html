Lesson 5.4 Prepaid and Stored Value Cards

Learn how prepaid systems operate using preloaded balances and controlled spending across card networks.

Home Department Professional and Occupational Studies Financial Systems Payments Track Unit 5 Card Payment Systems Lesson 5.4 Prepaid and Stored Value Cards

Where This Lesson Fits

This lesson completes the introduction to major card types by focusing on prepaid and stored value systems. It follows credit and debit models by showing a third structure where funds are loaded in advance and then spent down over time.

Prepaid systems are important in modern payments because they introduce controlled balance accounts that are widely used in gift cards, transit systems, payroll cards, and digital wallets.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to explain how prepaid and stored value cards function, how balances are loaded and consumed, and how transactions are authorized and settled within controlled value systems.

Lesson Overview

Prepaid and stored value card systems operate on a simple principle. Funds are loaded onto a card or account before spending occurs. The available balance is then reduced as transactions are made.

Unlike credit systems, there is no borrowing component. Unlike debit systems, the funds are not always directly tied to a traditional bank deposit account. Instead, value is stored within a controlled balance system managed by an issuer or platform provider.

These systems still rely on the same broader payments infrastructure, including merchants, acquirers, networks, and processors, to authorize and route transactions.

Core Concept

A prepaid or stored value card system is a payment structure in which funds are preloaded into an account or card balance, and transactions are approved based on available stored value rather than credit or direct deposit account access.

The system is defined by balance limitation. Once stored value is exhausted, the card cannot be used until it is reloaded with additional funds.

Key Participants

  • Cardholder using stored value funds
  • Merchant accepting prepaid payments
  • Issuer managing stored value accounts
  • Program manager or platform operator
  • Payment network routing transactions
  • Processor handling transaction messaging

How Prepaid Card Transactions Work

  1. Funds are loaded onto a prepaid account or card
  2. The cardholder initiates a purchase
  3. The merchant submits the transaction request
  4. The issuer or platform checks available stored value
  5. If sufficient funds exist, the transaction is approved
  6. The balance is reduced by the transaction amount
  7. Funds are later settled across the payment network

Real World Example

A customer receives a prepaid gift card loaded with a fixed amount of funds. The card is used at a retail store to make purchases until the balance reaches zero. Each transaction reduces the available stored value.

If the balance is insufficient, the transaction is declined unless additional funds are added.

Common Misunderstandings

Misunderstanding 1 Prepaid equals debit

Prepaid systems are not always linked to bank deposit accounts. They operate on stored value rather than direct account access.

Misunderstanding 2 Unlimited spending

Prepaid cards are strictly limited to the loaded balance and cannot exceed available funds.

Misunderstanding 3 No network involvement

Even prepaid systems rely on payment networks and processors for authorization and settlement.

Key Terms

Stored Value Preloaded funds held in a card or account system.

Prepaid Card Card funded in advance for controlled spending.

Reload Adding additional funds to a prepaid balance.

Authorization Verification of available stored value before approval.

Program Manager Entity managing prepaid card infrastructure and operations.

  • Transactions are limited by available balance
  • Payment networks still coordinate authorization and settlement

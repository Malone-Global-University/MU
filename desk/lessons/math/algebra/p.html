Lesson 1 — Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

A friendly, public-facing lesson that teaches why order of operations matters and how to use it correctly.

Overview

PEMDAS is a mnemonic that helps remember the conventional order for evaluating numerical expressions:

  • P — Parentheses (grouping symbols, fraction bars)
  • E — Exponents (powers and roots)
  • M/D — Multiplication and Division (equal priority; left → right)
  • A/S — Addition and Subtraction (equal priority; left → right)

Why it matters

Without a common convention, expressions like 6 + 3 × 2 could be interpreted multiple ways. PEMDAS gives consistent results and avoids ambiguity. Note: multiplication and division share the same priority and must be performed left-to-right; the same is true for addition and subtraction.

Worked examples

Example: 6 + 3 × 2

Step 1: Multiplication first → 3 × 2 = 6
Step 2: 6 + 6 = 12
Answer: 12

Example: 2 × (3 + 2)^2

1) Parentheses: 3 + 2 = 5
2) Exponent: 5^2 = 25
3) Multiply: 2 × 25 = 50
Answer: 50

Ambiguous case (teachable): 8 ÷ 2(2 + 2)

- Many calculators and convention evaluate M/D left-to-right: 8 ÷ 2 × (2+2) = 8 ÷ 2 × 4 = 4 × 4 = 16
- But some read the denominator as a single grouped term: 8 ÷ [2(2+2)] = 8 ÷ 8 = 1
Teaching point: Rewrite ambiguous expressions with parentheses or fraction bars to make the intent explicit.

Practice problems

  1. 9 + 6 ÷ 3
  2. (5 + 4) × 2
  3. 3^2 + 7
  4. 18 ÷ 3 × 2 + 5
  5. 2 × (4 + 6) ÷ 5
  6. 8 ÷ 2(2 + 2) — rewrite then evaluate

Answer key (brief): 1→11, 2→18, 3→16, 4→17, 5→4, 6→ambiguous (rewrite as either 1 or 16 depending on grouping).

Teaching tips

  • Use mini whiteboards for quick checks.
  • Emphasize left-to-right processing for M/D and A/S.
  • Encourage rewriting ambiguous expressions using parentheses or fraction bars.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install one dedicated dryer exhaust duct system, from the dryer's transition-duct connection point to a listed exterior termination, using the exact approved duct material, diameter, maximum equivalent length, bend allowances, and clearance requirements identified by the dryer manufacturer's instructions and the adopted code for the project. The finished work product is one complete, dedicated dryer exhaust duct run with secure joints, controlled bends and length, an accessible damper, and a sealed, weatherproofed exterior termination that discharges unobstructed to the outdoors.

You will confirm the route is dedicated to the dryer alone; select and join rigid or semi-rigid metal duct sections without airstream-projecting fasteners where prohibited; support the duct at the required intervals; prepare and integrate the exterior wall or roof opening with the building envelope; install a listed termination with a functioning damper; connect the dryer with the approved transition duct; and verify airflow, joint integrity, and accessible cleaning clearance through an authorized test.

This project depends on the HVAC system map from Chapter 1 and the shutdown and readiness procedures from Chapter 2 for identifying nearby electrical, gas, and structural conditions in the work area. It does not depend on and must never be combined with any other exhaust or HVAC duct: a dryer duct is independent of bathroom exhaust, kitchen exhaust, and conditioned-air ductwork at every point in its run.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-097 — Select approved dryer duct material, diameter, and fittings compatible with the dryer manufacturer's instructions and adopted code.
  • HV-098 — Route the duct run by the shortest approved path while identifying and avoiding framing, electrical, plumbing, gas piping, insulation, and fire-separation conflicts.
  • HV-099 — Minimize bends and maintain full duct diameter and required clearances from combustible materials and heat sources.
  • HV-100 — Secure duct joints using the approved listed closure method, without prohibited airstream-projecting fasteners.
  • HV-101 — Install the exterior termination, including flashing integration, damper function, and sealed penetration.
  • HV-102 — Inspect the completed run for lint hazards, restricted airflow, leakage, and accessible cleaning clearance.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the shutdown and work-area preparation habits established in Chapter 2 and the register-boot and flexible-duct connection techniques introduced in Chapters 8 and 10, applied here to a system that is independent of the conditioned-air ductwork and bathroom exhaust work covered in Chapters 9 and 16. The exterior-termination and building-envelope integration procedure parallels the bathroom exhaust termination in Chapter 16, but the dryer duct's fire, lint, and airflow requirements are unique to this appliance and must never be merged with any other exhaust system's design or duct run.

The dedicated-route discipline and accessible-cleaning requirement established here are referenced again in Chapter 19, where a restricted or leaking dryer duct is one of the diagnosable comfort and safety symptoms addressed within this book's scope.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the dryer manufacturer's instructions for approved duct material, diameter, maximum equivalent length, bend allowances, transition duct, termination, and clearances
  • confirming the route is dedicated to the dryer and does not connect to any other exhaust or HVAC system
  • routing, cutting, joining, and supporting rigid or semi-rigid metal duct sections
  • preparing the exterior wall or roof opening and installing a listed termination integrated with the building envelope
  • connecting the dryer using the manufacturer-approved transition duct
  • testing the completed run for airflow, leakage, vibration, and lint escape

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • connect the dryer duct to any bathroom exhaust, kitchen exhaust, or HVAC duct or plenum
  • use plastic or foil accordion duct in any concealed section where it is prohibited
  • install screws or fasteners that project into the airstream where prohibited
  • perform gas piping, gas-appliance connection, or electrical circuit work beyond what the assigned trade scope authorizes
  • terminate the duct into an attic, soffit, crawlspace, garage, or wall cavity

Any concealed utility conflict, fire-separation concern, or structural condition identified during layout must be recorded and resolved before cutting proceeds; it is not worked around by improvising the route.

Trade Note

A dryer exhaust duct's only job is to remove hot, moist, lint-laden air to the outdoors as directly as possible. Every foot of unnecessary length, every avoidable bend, and every fastener left projecting into the airstream reduces airflow and increases the amount of lint retained inside the duct. This project's material, length, and fastening rules exist to keep that airflow path clear and to keep the finished duct accessible for the periodic cleaning every dryer duct eventually needs.

Setup Standard

Confirm the dryer manufacturer's approved duct material, diameter, maximum equivalent length, and bend allowances before staging any material. Do not substitute plastic or foil accordion duct for concealed rigid or semi-rigid duct where it is prohibited, and do not install a fastener that will project into the airstream where prohibited.

Shop Tip

Dry-fit the entire route, including the transition duct and termination, before applying any sealant or driving any fastener. A dryer duct route that is one elbow shorter almost always outperforms a route that matches the wall cavity more conveniently.

Common Field Fundamentals

A dryer exhaust duct is judged on three things: how directly it reaches the outdoors, how well its joints resist lint escape and leakage, and how easily it can be inspected and cleaned later. Every step in this project supports one of those three outcomes.

Dedicated Route and Prohibited Materials

Confirm before layout begins that the planned route serves the dryer exclusively. Do not connect this duct to a bathroom exhaust, kitchen exhaust, or any HVAC supply, return, or ventilation duct at any point, and do not use plastic or foil accordion duct in any concealed section where the dryer manufacturer's instructions or adopted code prohibit it.

Critical Safety Cue — Dryer Ducts Are a Fire Hazard When Restricted or Improperly Fastened

Lint accumulation in a restricted, oversized, undersized, or excessively long dryer duct is a recognized fire hazard. Do not exceed the manufacturer's maximum equivalent length, do not install a fastener that projects into the airstream where prohibited, and do not conceal the completed duct until it has passed the airflow and leakage test in this project.

Code Alert — Duct Material, Length, and Termination Requirements Vary

Adopted code and the dryer manufacturer's instructions control the exact permitted duct material, maximum equivalent length, bend-penalty values, termination type, and required clearances. Confirm these values for the project before selecting materials. Do not assume the values used on a prior project apply to this one.

Field Safety Cue — Framing Cavity and Attic Access

Inspect attic, crawlspace, or wall-cavity access for structural support, adequate lighting, insulation hazards, and confined-space conditions before entering. Identify and avoid electrical wiring, plumbing, and gas piping in the planned route before cutting any opening.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Transition Duct and Termination Are Matched to the Appliance

Use only the transition duct and termination type specified or approved by the dryer manufacturer's instructions. A transition duct that is too long, kinked, or crushed behind the appliance reduces airflow as much as an undersized fixed duct.

F. Preparation and Route Layout

  1. Review the dryer manufacturer instructions, approved duct material, diameter, maximum equivalent length, bend allowances, transition duct, termination, and clearances. Record each value on the Route Definition Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm that the route is dedicated to the dryer and does not connect to another exhaust or HVAC system. Verify this against the Chapter 1 system map.
  3. Inspect the route for framing, electrical, plumbing, gas piping, insulation, fire separations, and access. Mark and avoid every identified conflict before cutting.
  4. Mark the shortest approved route and each support point. Record the marked route and support locations on the worksheet.
  5. Confirm that concealed duct material is rigid or semi-rigid metal as required and that plastic or foil accordion duct is not used where prohibited. Stop and correct the material selection before continuing if this is not met.

G. Duct Assembly and Support

  1. Cut rigid duct sections square and remove sharp burrs. Verify each cut edge is free of burrs before joining.
  2. Orient joints so male ends point in the direction of airflow where required. Confirm orientation at each joint before securing.
  3. Join sections using the approved listed closure method. Verify full engagement at each joint.
  4. Do not install screws or fasteners projecting into the airstream where prohibited. Inspect the interior of each joint after fastening.
  5. Support the duct at the required intervals without creating low points. Verify support spacing against the manufacturer or code requirement.
  6. Minimize bends and maintain full duct diameter. Recheck the calculated equivalent length after assembly against the manufacturer's maximum.
  7. Maintain required clearances from combustible materials and heat sources. Verify clearance at every point the duct passes near a heat-producing component.

H. Exterior Termination

  1. Prepare the exterior wall or roof opening using the approved structural and envelope procedure. Verify the opening size matches the termination's rough-in requirement.
  2. Install the listed dryer termination without an unapproved screen that can trap lint. Confirm the termination model is listed for dryer exhaust use.
  3. Integrate flashing with the building envelope. Verify shingle-lap or wall-drainage order is correct before sealing.
  4. Verify that the damper opens freely and is accessible for cleaning. Test the damper by hand before final assembly.
  5. Seal duct joints and the building penetration using compatible materials. Confirm sealant compatibility with the exterior assembly material.

I. Appliance Connection and Testing

  1. Install the manufacturer-approved transition duct between dryer and fixed duct. Confirm the transition duct type matches the manufacturer's specification.
  2. Position the dryer without crushing or kinking the transition duct. Verify clearance behind the appliance before final positioning.
  3. Confirm access for future cleaning and appliance service. Verify the transition duct and nearest fixed-duct joint remain reachable.
  4. Operate the dryer in an authorized test mode. Confirm the appliance is otherwise ready for normal operation before starting the test.
  5. Verify exterior damper opening and unobstructed warm airflow. Observe the termination from outside during the test cycle.
  6. Inspect joints, transition duct, termination, and surrounding surfaces for leakage, vibration, heat damage, or lint escape. Record any deficiency found.
  7. Measure or document equivalent length as required. Confirm the final as-built value against the manufacturer's maximum.
  8. Correct restricted, crushed, leaking, or unsupported sections. Re-run the affected test after any correction.
  9. Document materials, route, bends, termination, and test result. Complete the Dryer Vent Installation Worksheet before closeout.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed dryer vent installation using the same checks regardless of route length or termination type.

  • Dedication check: confirm the duct serves the dryer exclusively with no connection to another exhaust or HVAC system.
  • Material check: confirm concealed duct is rigid or semi-rigid metal where required, with no prohibited plastic or foil accordion duct.
  • Length check: confirm the as-built equivalent length is within the manufacturer's maximum.
  • Fastener check: confirm no fastener projects into the airstream where prohibited.
  • Support check: confirm supports are installed at the required interval with no low points.
  • Termination check: confirm the termination is listed, the damper moves freely, and flashing is integrated with the envelope.
  • Test check: confirm an authorized dryer cycle verified airflow, damper operation, and absence of leakage or lint escape.

Complete the Dryer Vent Installation Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before considering the project closed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Weak Airflow at the Exterior Termination

Probable cause: excessive equivalent length, too many bends, a crushed or kinked section, or lint accumulation in an existing duct being reused.
Confirmation: recalculate the as-built equivalent length and visually inspect the full route for crushed or kinked sections.
Correction: shorten the route, reduce the bend count, or replace the crushed or kinked section; clean any reused duct before returning it to service.
Reinspection: repeat the authorized dryer-cycle airflow test after correction.

Damper Does Not Open Fully or Sticks

Probable cause: incorrect termination orientation, debris in the damper hinge, or a termination installed out of level.
Confirmation: operate the damper by hand and check its hinge and seating surface for obstruction.
Correction: clean the hinge area, re-level the termination, or replace a damaged damper assembly.
Reinspection: verify free damper movement during a repeated test cycle.

Leakage or Heat Discoloration at a Joint

Probable cause: an incompletely engaged joint, a missing or failed closure, or a fastener that compromised the joint seal.
Confirmation: inspect the joint closely during the test cycle for visible air movement, staining, or heat discoloration.
Correction: disassemble and re-join the affected section using the correct listed closure method.
Reinspection: repeat the leakage inspection during a subsequent authorized test cycle.

Lint Escape Noted Inside a Wall or Ceiling Cavity

Probable cause: an unsealed joint, a fastener projecting into the airstream that snagged lint and forced it through a gap, or an incorrect duct material substitution.
Confirmation: trace the affected section and inspect every joint for gaps, projecting fasteners, or prohibited material.
Correction: replace the affected section with the correct material, remove any projecting fastener, and reseal the joint.
Reinspection: repeat the full airflow and leakage test after correction.

The Route Was Found Connected to Another Exhaust or HVAC Duct

Probable cause: a prior modification or an assumption that an existing concealed duct could be shared.
Confirmation: trace the shared section back to its point of connection using the Chapter 1 system map and visual inspection.
Correction: disconnect the dryer duct from the shared system and install an independent dedicated route.
Reinspection: repeat the dedication check and the full airflow test on the corrected independent route.

Cleanup

This project ends with the completed duct concealed only after passing the required inspection and test, and the work area restored.

  • Confirm every access opening, attic hatch, or wall opening used for the installation is closed and secured
  • Remove cut duct scrap, packaging, and fastener debris from the work area
  • Confirm the exterior termination area is clear of debris that could obstruct discharge
  • Store unused duct sections, fasteners, and sealant in a protected, undamaged condition
  • Store the Dryer Vent Installation Worksheet and test results for reference by later chapters

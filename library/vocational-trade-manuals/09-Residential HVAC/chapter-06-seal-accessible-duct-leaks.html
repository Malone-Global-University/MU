Job Overview

This project qualifies you to seal accessible leakage at sheet-metal and duct-board joints, seams, takeoffs, and register boots on the residential HVAC system identified in Project 01 and assessed for airflow deficiencies in Project 05. The finished work product is one completed set of sealed duct joints, verified against continued leakage, using only listed duct mastic and listed HVAC-rated foil tape applied within their approved service conditions.

You will review the duct material, service temperature, joint type, and approved sealant and tape listings; shut down the equipment and confirm the duct section is not moving air; mark leakage points identified from the Project 05 deficiency report or other approved evidence; clean and prepare the bonding surface; mechanically fasten loose joints; apply and tool duct mastic to full coverage; apply listed HVAC foil tape where the approved system permits it; seal register-boot-to-finish gaps; allow the sealant to cure; and restore equipment to verify the sealed joints against continued leakage.

This project builds directly on the duct inspection and deficiency report from Project 05. It does not authorize resizing, replacing, or redesigning any duct, and it does not authorize sealing intentional openings, balancing devices, drains, access panels, or safety components. Project 07, duct insulation, is performed only after this project's sealing work has cured and passed inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-031 — Clean duct surfaces at the approved bonding area before sealant application.
  • HV-032 — Identify leakage points using visual evidence, prior test data, or another approved method.
  • HV-033 — Apply duct mastic to the specified thickness and width over a joint.
  • HV-034 — Apply HVAC-rated foil tape to clean, dry surfaces with the required overlap and pressure.
  • HV-035 — Seal register-boot connections to the surrounding finish surface using a compatible approved method.
  • HV-036 — Inspect completed duct sealing for missed joints, tape lift, mastic cracking, and continued leakage.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 05 produced a deficiency report identifying disconnected joints, open seams, and leakage locations on the accessible duct system. This project corrects the leakage findings from that report using listed, compatible sealing materials. The sealed and cured duct system produced here is a direct prerequisite for Project 07, duct insulation, which requires that leakage sealing be complete and cured before insulation is installed over it.

The shutdown, work-area protection, and documentation habits established in Project 02 and used throughout Projects 03 through 05 continue here: equipment is shut down before the duct section is worked on, and every sealed location, material, and inspection result is recorded before the project closes out.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • sealing accessible leakage at sheet-metal and duct-board joints, seams, takeoffs, and register boots identified as deficient in Project 05 or by another approved method
  • cleaning and preparing the bonding surface before sealant application
  • mechanically fastening loose duct joints before sealing, where required
  • applying listed duct mastic and listed HVAC-rated foil tape within their approved service conditions
  • sealing register-boot-to-drywall or boot-to-subfloor gaps using a compatible approved method
  • verifying completed sealing against continued leakage after cure and after equipment is restored

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • seal intentional openings, balancing devices, drains, access panels, combustion openings, or safety components
  • resize, replace, reroute, or redesign any duct, plenum, or fitting
  • work on ducts identified in Project 05 as contaminated, structurally unsafe, or otherwise inaccessible for repair
  • seal a joint before the duct section has been shut down and confirmed not to be moving air
  • cover a sealed joint with insulation before the sealant has cured and passed inspection

Ducts identified in Project 05 as contaminated with suspected asbestos, heavy mold, sewage, or fire damage remain a stop-work condition in this project and are not sealed until a qualified professional has addressed the condition.

Trade Note

Duct mastic and HVAC-rated foil tape are not interchangeable with household products. Household duct tape is not permitted on HVAC ducts under any condition in this book: its adhesive fails at duct operating temperatures and it does not carry a listing for this service. Use only the mastic and tape products identified as compatible with the duct material, service temperature, and joint type in this project's material takeoff.

Restricted Openings and Sealing Boundaries

This project seals only the accessible joints, seams, takeoffs, and boots identified as deficient by Project 05 or another approved method. Do not seal a joint outside the accessible, structurally suitable duct sections confirmed in Project 05.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm the Duct Is Not Moving Air Before Sealing

Shut down equipment using the Project 02 procedure and verify the duct section is not moving air before beginning any cleaning, fastening, or sealing work. Do not apply mastic or tape to a duct surface while air is moving through it. Do not restore equipment until sealant has reached the manufacturer-specified initial set condition.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Product Listing Controls Application

Confirm the duct mastic and HVAC-rated foil tape carry a listing compatible with the duct material, service temperature, and joint type before beginning work. Do not substitute an unlisted sealant, an incompatible tape, or household duct tape for any joint in this project. Apply each product only within its manufacturer-specified temperature, humidity, and cure conditions.

Measurement Cue — Coverage, Overlap, and Cure Are Verified, Not Assumed

Measure mastic coverage width and tape overlap against the manufacturer-specified minimum for each joint. Do not assume a thin mastic pass or a narrow tape overlap is adequate. Confirm the manufacturer-specified cure time has elapsed before covering a sealed joint with insulation or restoring full equipment operation.

Field Safety Cue — Protect Surfaces and Control Mastic Drips

Protect finished floors, ceilings, and surrounding surfaces with drop cloths before applying mastic overhead or in tight spaces. Keep mastic and cleaning solvents away from electrical components, insulation not scheduled for removal, and porous finish materials.

E. Preparation and Product Review

  1. Review the duct material, service temperature, joint type, insulation, approved sealant, tape listing, and cure conditions. Confirm every product to be used carries a listing compatible with the duct material and service present.
  2. Shut down equipment and verify that the duct section is not moving air. Apply the Project 02 shutdown and readiness procedure to this duct section before proceeding.
  3. Establish safe access and protect surrounding insulation, finishes, and equipment. Place drop cloths beneath and around each joint scheduled for sealing.

F. Inspection and Leak Marking

  1. Inspect the duct for structural damage, corrosion, contamination, wet insulation, or failed supports. Stop work at any joint showing a condition outside the scope confirmed in Project 05.
  2. Mark leakage points using visual evidence, previous test data, smoke under controlled conditions, or another approved method. Cross-reference each marked point against the Project 05 deficiency report.
  3. Do not seal intentional openings, balancing devices, drains, access panels, combustion openings, or safety components. Confirm each marked point is a joint, seam, takeoff, or boot before proceeding.

G. Surface Preparation and Mechanical Fastening

  1. Remove loose dust, oil, rust scale, failed tape, and incompatible sealant from the approved bonding area. Use the wire brush or abrasive pad without damaging the duct surface.
  2. Dry the surface and verify it meets product temperature and moisture requirements. Confirm ambient and surface conditions against the mastic and tape manufacturer instructions before applying either product.
  3. Mechanically fasten loose duct joints before applying sealant where required. Use fasteners matching the duct gauge and joint type from the fastener schedule.
  4. Apply reinforcing mesh at gaps or joints that require it. Use mesh at any gap exceeding the mastic manufacturer's unreinforced limit.

H. Mastic and Tape Application

  1. Apply duct mastic to the specified thickness and width over the joint. Measure coverage width against the manufacturer-specified minimum for the joint type.
  2. Tool the mastic to continuous contact without thin spots or pinholes. Inspect the tooled surface under the flashlight before moving to the next joint.
  3. Use listed HVAC foil tape only where the approved system permits it. Confirm the joint type is one where tape is authorized before applying it.
  4. Apply tape to clean, dry surfaces with the required overlap and pressure. Measure overlap against the manufacturer-specified minimum.
  5. Burnish tape fully and avoid wrinkles, fishmouths, or bridging. Inspect the burnished tape for full adhesion along its entire length.

I. Boot Sealing, Access Preservation, and Cure

  1. Seal register-boot-to-drywall or boot-to-subfloor gaps using the compatible approved method. Confirm the perimeter gap is fully closed without excess material projecting into the register opening.
  2. Preserve removable access panels and service connections. Confirm no access panel, damper handle, or service point has been sealed shut.
  3. Allow sealants to cure for the manufacturer-controlled time. Record the cure start time and the required cure duration on the worksheet.
  4. Reinstall insulation and vapor facing after the seal has passed inspection. Do not reinstall insulation removed for this project's access until the cure period has elapsed and the joint has passed inspection.

J. Restoration, Verification, and Documentation

  1. Restore equipment and perform the approved leakage observation. Use the smoke pencil or approved detection method specified for the assigned system.
  2. Inspect for missed joints, tape lift, mastic cracking, air noise, or continued leakage. Check every sealed joint identified on the Duct Sealing Schedule.
  3. Correct failed areas using the product-approved repair method. Re-clean, re-fasten, and re-seal any joint that fails verification, then repeat the affected observation.
  4. Document sealed locations, materials, cure conditions, and inspection results. Complete every entry on the Duct Sealing Worksheet before authorizing Project 07 to begin on this duct section.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed sealing project using the same checks regardless of which residence or training system the project involves.

  • Preparation check: confirm the bonding area was clean, dry, and free of failed prior sealant before application.
  • Fastening check: confirm loose joints were mechanically fastened before sealant was applied over them.
  • Mastic check: confirm coverage meets the specified thickness and width with continuous contact and no thin spots or pinholes.
  • Tape check: confirm tape was applied only where authorized, with the required overlap, fully burnished without wrinkles or bridging.
  • Boot check: confirm register-boot perimeter gaps are closed without blocking required access or drainage.
  • Cure check: confirm the manufacturer-specified cure time elapsed before covering or restoring full operation.
  • Verification check: confirm no continued leakage, tape lift, or mastic cracking was observed after equipment restoration.

Complete the Duct Sealing Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 07.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Mastic Cracks or Shows Pinholes After Cure

Probable cause: insufficient coverage thickness, application over a wet or contaminated surface, or curing outside the manufacturer's temperature or humidity range.
Confirmation: inspect the cracked or pinholed area under the flashlight and compare coverage thickness against the manufacturer specification.
Correction: remove the failed mastic section, re-clean and dry the surface, and reapply mastic to the specified thickness.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage and continuity check on the corrected area after the full cure period.

Foil Tape Lifts or Shows Fishmouths

Probable cause: application over a dusty, oily, or damp surface, insufficient burnishing pressure, or tape applied at a joint type the approved system does not authorize for tape.
Confirmation: attempt to lift the tape edge gently and inspect the underlying surface condition.
Correction: remove the failed tape, re-clean and dry the surface, and reapply tape with the required overlap, fully burnished.
Reinspection: repeat the adhesion check along the full tape length after reapplication.

Continued Leakage Is Detected After Equipment Restoration

Probable cause: a missed joint, an incomplete mastic or tape application, or a joint that shifted after sealing but before cure.
Confirmation: repeat the approved leak-detection method at each joint on the Duct Sealing Schedule and compare results against the initial inspection.
Correction: shut down equipment, re-clean and reseal the failed joint using the product-approved repair method, and allow the full cure period before restoring equipment again.
Reinspection: repeat the leakage observation on the corrected joint and confirm the result is recorded on the worksheet.

A Required Access Panel or Damper Was Sealed Shut

Probable cause: mastic or tape applied across an access panel seam, damper handle, or service opening during a nearby joint's sealing work.
Confirmation: attempt to operate the affected access panel or damper before restoring equipment.
Correction: remove the sealant from the required opening and confirm the panel or damper operates freely before proceeding.
Reinspection: confirm every access panel and damper on the affected duct section operates freely before equipment is restored.

Equipment Was Restored Before the Cure Period Elapsed

Probable cause: the recorded cure start time was not checked against the manufacturer-specified duration before restoring equipment.
Confirmation: compare the actual elapsed time against the manufacturer-specified cure duration recorded on the worksheet.
Correction: shut down equipment again, inspect the affected joint for disturbance, and allow the remaining cure time before restoring equipment.
Reinspection: repeat the joint inspection and leakage observation once the full cure period has elapsed.

Cure Condition

Continued Leakage
Air movement detected at a joint after sealing and equipment restoration, indicating the seal failed or was incomplete.
Fishmouth
A gap or wrinkle in applied tape where the adhesive does not fully contact the duct surface, allowing air to pass beneath the tape.
Burnishing
Applying firm, even pressure across installed tape to achieve full adhesive contact along its entire length.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to replace one compatible low-voltage thermostat on a documented conventional residential heating and cooling control system. The finished work product is a correctly wired, configured, and mounted replacement thermostat verified through authorized fan-only, heating, and cooling calls.

You will identify the existing thermostat's equipment type, staging, and control configuration; confirm the replacement thermostat supports that exact configuration; shut down and de-energize the system using the Chapter 2 procedure; label every conductor by terminal designation rather than by color; remove the old thermostat and base without dropping wires into the wall; install and secure the new base; connect and configure the new thermostat to match the documented system; and verify fan, heating, and cooling operation without opening any combustion, refrigerant, or high-voltage compartment.

This project begins from the equipment identification recorded in the Chapter 1 system map and depends on the Chapter 2 shutdown procedure to control the thermostat circuit and connected equipment before any conductor is disconnected. It supports the diagnostic work in Chapter 19, where thermostat wiring and configuration are checked only within this chapter's scope.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-067 — Identify thermostat wires by documented terminal function rather than color alone.
  • HV-068 — Label low-voltage conductors by terminal designation before disconnecting the existing thermostat.
  • HV-069 — Remove the old thermostat without dropping conductors into the wall cavity.
  • HV-070 — Mount the new thermostat base level and secure to the wall surface.
  • HV-071 — Connect control wires to the correct terminals and configure the thermostat to match the documented system.
  • HV-072 — Test heating and cooling calls and verify correct equipment response.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 system map, which identifies the existing thermostat, equipment type, and controls, and on the Chapter 2 shutdown procedure, which controls the thermostat circuit and connected indoor and outdoor equipment before any conductor is touched. It is limited to compatible low-voltage thermostat replacement; the Chapter 19 diagnostic project checks thermostat wire labels, configuration, and mounting only within this chapter's established scope, and does not authorize additional thermostat work beyond what is defined here.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the existing thermostat, equipment type, staging, heat source, cooling source, fan control, reversing-valve logic, accessory terminals, and power method
  • confirming that the replacement thermostat supports the exact documented equipment and control configuration
  • shutting down indoor and outdoor equipment using the Chapter 2 procedure and verifying the thermostat circuit is de-energized
  • labeling each conductor by terminal designation, removing the old thermostat and base, and inspecting the wall opening
  • mounting, wiring, configuring, and securing the new compatible thermostat
  • testing fan-only, heating, and cooling calls and verifying correct authorized response

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • replace a line-voltage thermostat, a proprietary communicating control, a zoning-system panel, or a hydronic control
  • proceed with an unknown terminal, an undocumented conductor, or an unsupported equipment configuration
  • apply line voltage to a low-voltage thermostat circuit at any point in this project
  • open a combustion compartment, refrigerant component, or high-voltage compartment while diagnosing a heating or cooling call
  • bypass a door switch, safety switch, pressure switch, float switch, rollout switch, limit control, or interlock while testing equipment response

Stop work for line-voltage thermostats, proprietary communicating controls, unknown terminals, zoning panels, hydronic controls, or unsupported equipment. This condition requires evaluation by a qualified professional before any replacement proceeds.

Trade Note

Terminal designation, not wire color, controls every connection in this project. A red conductor is commonly used for the transformer-power terminal, but color assignment is not guaranteed across every installation, prior repair, or manufacturer. Label every conductor by its terminal designation at the existing thermostat before disconnecting anything, and connect the replacement thermostat using those recorded designations rather than by matching colors to a generic diagram.

Common Field Fundamentals

A low-voltage thermostat circuit is typically powered by a 24-volt transformer located in the connected equipment, not by the building's line-voltage wiring. Confirming that the circuit is de-energized before disconnecting any conductor, and confirming every terminal designation before connecting a replacement thermostat, prevents a short circuit, a blown transformer fuse, or a miswired call for heating or cooling.

F. Identification, Compatibility, and Scope Confirmation

  1. Identify the existing thermostat, equipment type, staging, heat source, cooling source, fan control, reversing-valve logic, accessory terminals, and power method. Record each item on the System Configuration Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm that the replacement thermostat supports the exact equipment and control configuration. Compare the recorded configuration against the replacement thermostat's manufacturer documentation before purchase or installation.
  3. Stop work for line-voltage thermostats, proprietary communicating controls, unknown terminals, zoning panels, hydronic controls, or unsupported equipment. Do not proceed with removal until this condition is resolved by the required qualified professional.

G. Shutdown and Circuit Verification

  1. Shut down indoor and outdoor equipment using Chapter 2. Apply the full readiness procedure to the equipment serving the documented thermostat circuit.
  2. Verify thermostat circuit power is absent. Use the low-voltage test meter at the accessible terminal block before disconnecting any conductor.

H. Documentation and Removal

  1. Photograph the existing thermostat and terminal connections. Capture the terminal block with every conductor still connected before labeling begins.
  2. Label each conductor by terminal designation rather than relying on color. Apply a wire label to every conductor before disconnecting it.
  3. Record unused conductors and cable condition. Note any conductor not connected to the existing thermostat and the general condition of the cable jacket.
  4. Remove the thermostat body while preventing wires from falling into the wall. Support the cable at the wall opening throughout removal.
  5. Disconnect one conductor at a time and maintain labels. Confirm each label remains legible and attached as the conductor is freed.
  6. Remove the old base and inspect the wall opening, cable, drafts, damage, and mounting surface. Record any condition requiring correction before mounting the new base.
  7. Seal excessive wall-cavity air leakage using a compatible removable method without damaging conductors. Apply only where airflow is clearly excessive at the opening.

I. New Base Mounting and Wire Preparation

  1. Position the new base level or aligned as required by the manufacturer. Use the level to confirm orientation before marking anchor points.
  2. Secure the base without pinching or overtightening. Verify the labeled conductors remain free to move through the base opening.
  3. Prepare conductors to the required strip length. Strip each conductor to the length specified by the replacement thermostat's manufacturer instructions.

J. Wiring, Configuration, and Mounting

  1. Connect each conductor to the matching approved terminal. Use the labeled terminal designation recorded in Step H, not conductor color, to determine each connection.
  2. Configure jumpers, switches, equipment type, staging, and reversing-valve logic according to the documented system. Follow the replacement thermostat's manufacturer configuration procedure.
  3. Install batteries or common-wire power as specified. Confirm the power method matches what was recorded in the System Configuration Record.
  4. Attach the thermostat body. Confirm it seats fully and squarely against the mounted base.

K. Functional Test

  1. Restore equipment power. Close the disconnects and breakers opened during Chapter 2 shutdown.
  2. Program the basic system configuration. Set the time, schedule, and any required initial setup screens per the manufacturer instructions.
  3. Initiate a fan-only call and verify operation. Confirm the indoor blower starts without a heating or cooling call.
  4. Initiate a heating call and observe the authorized sequence without opening combustion or electrical compartments. Confirm the equipment responds correctly from outside the closed cabinet.
  5. Initiate a cooling call and observe indoor and outdoor response. Confirm both the indoor and outdoor units respond correctly to the call.
  6. Verify shutdown after satisfying or canceling each call. Confirm the equipment stops when the call ends.
  7. Stop work for blown fuses, short cycling, no response, simultaneous heat and cooling, abnormal noise, or fault codes. Do not continue testing past a fault condition.
  8. Re-isolate power before correction. Apply the Chapter 2 procedure again before investigating any fault.
  9. Document terminal mapping, configuration, and functional test results. Complete the Thermostat Replacement Worksheet before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed thermostat replacement using the same checks regardless of which equipment or thermostat model is involved.

  • Compatibility check: confirm the replacement thermostat's manufacturer documentation supports the documented equipment type, staging, heat source, cooling source, and accessory terminals.
  • De-energized check: confirm the thermostat circuit was verified de-energized before any conductor was disconnected.
  • Labeling check: confirm every conductor was labeled by terminal designation before disconnection.
  • Mounting check: confirm the base is level, secure, and free of pinched conductors.
  • Wiring check: confirm every conductor is connected to its matching documented terminal.
  • Configuration check: confirm equipment type, staging, and reversing-valve logic match the documented system.
  • Functional-test check: confirm fan-only, heating, and cooling calls produced correct response with no fault condition.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The New Thermostat Shows No Power After Installation

Probable cause: a common-wire terminal not connected, an unrestored breaker or disconnect, or a mismatched power method between the old and new thermostat.
Confirmation: verify the equipment disconnects and breakers are restored, then check for correct low-voltage power at the base using the test meter.
Correction: re-isolate power, correct the terminal connection or power method to match the documented system, and restore power.
Reinspection: repeat the fan-only call and confirm the thermostat display operates correctly.

A Heating or Cooling Call Produces No Equipment Response

Probable cause: a mismatched terminal connection, an incorrect equipment-type or staging configuration, or a conductor connected to an unconfirmed terminal.
Confirmation: compare the connected terminals against the labeled record from Step H and the configuration settings against the System Configuration Record.
Correction: re-isolate power, correct the terminal connection or configuration setting, and restore power.
Reinspection: repeat the affected heating or cooling call and confirm correct authorized response.

Heating and Cooling Calls Operate Simultaneously

Probable cause: a miswired reversing-valve terminal, an incorrect heat-pump or conventional-system configuration setting, or a crossed heat and cool terminal connection.
Confirmation: stop the test immediately and re-isolate power; do not allow the condition to continue.
Correction: compare every connected terminal against the labeled record, correct the crossed connection or configuration setting, and restore power only after the correction is verified.
Reinspection: repeat the heating and cooling calls independently and confirm only the called mode operates.

A Fuse Blows or the Equipment Displays a Fault Code After Restoring Power

Probable cause: a short circuit from a pinched conductor at the base, a conductor connected to the wrong terminal, or contact between two conductors during mounting.
Confirmation: re-isolate power immediately and inspect the base and terminal connections for pinched or contacting conductors.
Correction: correct the pinched or crossed conductor, replace the blown fuse per the equipment manufacturer's procedure, and restore power.
Reinspection: repeat the fan-only call before proceeding to heating and cooling calls.

A Conductor Was Not Labeled Before Disconnection

Probable cause: a label was skipped, fell off, or became illegible during removal.
Confirmation: compare the unlabeled conductor's position against the photograph taken in Step H before it was disconnected.
Correction: if the terminal designation cannot be confirmed from the photograph or documentation, treat the conductor as an unknown terminal and stop work rather than guessing at its function.
Reinspection: resume wiring only after the terminal designation is confirmed by a qualified professional or reliable documentation.

Field Terms

Terminal Designation
The manufacturer-assigned letter or label at a thermostat or equipment terminal block that identifies its function, independent of conductor color.
Documented Configuration
The equipment type, staging, heat source, cooling source, fan control, reversing-valve logic, and accessory terminals recorded from the existing thermostat before removal.
Unknown Terminal
Any conductor whose terminal designation and function cannot be confirmed from the existing installation, labels, or documentation.
Common Wire (C)
The conductor providing continuous transformer power to a thermostat, where present.
Fan-Only Call
A thermostat-initiated test that operates the indoor blower without initiating a heating or cooling call.
Compatible Thermostat
A replacement thermostat confirmed by its manufacturer documentation to support the exact staging, heat source, cooling source, and accessory terminals of the documented system.
Reversing-Valve Logic (O/B)
The heat-pump control terminal designation that determines whether the reversing valve is energized in heating mode or cooling mode.
Staging
The number of sequential heating or cooling capacity steps the connected equipment and thermostat are configured to call for.

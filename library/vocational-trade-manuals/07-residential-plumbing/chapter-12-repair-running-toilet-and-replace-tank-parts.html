Job Overview

This project qualifies you to diagnose and correct a running toilet using compatible replacement tank components and measured operational checks. The finished work product is a toilet tank that fills to the correct, marked water level, shuts off reliably, holds water without leaking into the bowl, and flushes normally through repeated test cycles.

You will observe the symptom without disturbing any part first, mark the current water level, run a dye test to confirm whether water is leaking past the flapper into the bowl, inspect every tank component that can cause running water, classify the verified cause, replace only the component or components the diagnosis supports, and confirm the repair with a second dye test and multiple flush cycles.

This project builds directly on the toilet-setting skills from Chapter 11 and reuses the shutoff and drain-down discipline from Chapter 1. It prepares you for Chapter 13, where the same diagnose-before-you-disassemble approach applies to a shower valve cartridge.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-067 — Diagnose the verified cause of a running toilet using observation, a marked water level, and a dye test.
  • PL-068 — Replace the fill valve when diagnosis confirms fill-valve failure.
  • PL-069 — Replace the flush valve when diagnosis confirms a flush-valve-body defect.
  • PL-070 — Replace the flapper when diagnosis confirms a compatible flapper leak.
  • PL-071 — Adjust the water level to the marked or manufacturer-specified setting.
  • PL-072 — Verify proper operation through a repeated dye test and multiple flush cycles.

Where This Skill Fits

A running toilet is one of the most common residential plumbing service calls, and it is also one of the most frequently misdiagnosed. Replacing a fill valve when the actual cause is a leaking flapper wastes the cost of the part and does not fix the symptom; replacing a flapper when the flush-valve seat itself is damaged produces the same result. This project trains the evidence-first sequence — observe, mark, dye-test, inspect, classify — before any part is removed.

The tank-removal and reassembly steps used when a flush valve requires replacement reuse the tank-to-bowl hardware handling taught in Chapter 11. The shutoff and drain-down discipline from Chapter 1 controls how the tank is isolated before any component is disturbed.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • observing the running-toilet symptom and marking the current tank water level before disturbing any part
  • running a dye test to confirm or rule out water leaking into the bowl without flushing
  • inspecting the fill valve, float, refill tube, flapper, chain, flush-valve seat, handle, and overflow tube
  • classifying the verified cause as overfill, leaking flapper, damaged flush-valve seal, chain interference, refill-tube siphoning, fill-valve failure, or another verified condition
  • replacing the flapper, the fill valve, or the flush valve — individually or in combination — based on the diagnosed cause
  • adjusting the tank water level to the marked or manufacturer-specified setting
  • verifying the repair with a repeated dye test and multiple flush cycles

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • replace a component that the dye test and inspection have not confirmed as the cause
  • set the toilet on a new closet flange, replace the wax ring or seal, or perform any work covered by Chapter 11
  • correct a cracked tank, a cracked bowl, a broken closet flange, or a structurally unstable toilet — these are stop-work conditions requiring the Chapter 11 replacement sequence or a qualified professional
  • proceed with reassembly when a stop-work condition identified in this chapter is present and uncorrected

Any component, tank condition, or fixture defect that fails verification in this chapter must be corrected, repaired, or escalated to a qualified professional before the toilet is returned to service.

Trade Note

Diagnose first. Every replacement in this chapter is authorized only after the dye test and component inspection confirm the specific cause. Replacing a part before confirming the cause is the most common reason a running-toilet repair fails to resolve the symptom on the first visit.

Setup Standard

Do not remove any tank component until the symptom has been observed, the current water level has been marked, and a dye test has confirmed or ruled out a flapper or seat leak. Work started before the cause is confirmed must stop and restart from the diagnostic sequence.

Shop Tip

Mark the water level with the tank lid off and the toilet in its normal resting state, before you add dye or touch the fill valve. That mark is your only reliable reference for confirming an overfill condition and for resetting the level correctly after any component is replaced.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every running-toilet repair follows the same four-part sequence: observe, confirm, classify, correct. Skipping the confirmation step and jumping straight to a part replacement is the single most common cause of a repeat service call for this symptom.

Distinguishing the Common Causes

An overfill condition sends water continuously into the overflow tube and is confirmed by comparing the marked water level to the overflow-tube opening. A leaking flapper or damaged flush-valve seat allows water to pass from the tank into the bowl without flushing, and is confirmed by the dye test. Chain interference prevents the flapper from seating fully and is confirmed by observing the chain length and flapper closure directly. A siphoning refill tube is confirmed by observing the tube positioned below the overflow-tube water level. A failing fill valve is confirmed when it will not shut off, cannot be adjusted to stop at the correct level, leaks internally, or is incompatible with the tank.

Measurement Cue — Mark Before You Touch Anything

Mark the current tank water level on the inside tank wall with a permanent marker before removing the lid for any other reason. This mark is the reference used to confirm an overfill condition and to reset the level correctly once a replacement component is installed.

Code Alert — Critical-Level Relationship

Set the fill valve so the water level maintains the manufacturer's specified critical-level relationship to the overflow-tube opening. This relationship prevents backflow of tank water into the potable-water supply and is a code requirement, not an optional adjustment.

Field Safety Cue — Chemical Drain Cleaner

Confirm that no chemical drain cleaner or hazardous liquid has been added to the tank or bowl before beginning any diagnostic or repair step. Chemical residue in tank water can cause skin or eye contact hazards during component handling.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Exact Compatible Parts

Select a replacement flapper, fill valve, or flush valve that is an exact compatible match for the toilet model, or a listed universal replacement confirmed against the manufacturer's compatibility information. An incompatible flapper or seat mismatch is a common cause of a repair that fails within days.

F. Observation and Baseline Documentation

  1. Identify the toilet model, tank components, specified water level, flush-valve size, flapper type, and manufacturer service information. Record what is visible on the tank or lid before removing anything.
  2. Observe the symptom without removing parts. Note whether the running is continuous, intermittent, or triggered only after a flush.
  3. Mark the current tank water level and inspect whether water enters the overflow tube. Use a permanent marker directly on the tank wall.

G. Dye Test and Component Inspection

  1. Add approved dye to the tank and check for leakage into the bowl without flushing. Wait several minutes and observe the bowl water for color before touching any component.
  2. Inspect the fill valve, float, refill tube, flapper, chain, flush-valve seat, handle, and overflow tube. Check each component individually and record its condition.
  3. Classify the cause as overfill, leaking flapper, damaged flush-valve seal, chain interference, refill-tube siphoning, fill-valve failure, or another verified condition. Base the classification on the dye-test result and the component inspection, not on assumption.

H. Isolation and Component Replacement

  1. Shut off and drain the tank. Use the Chapter 1 isolation and drain-down sequence at the toilet's fixture stop.
  2. Replace the flapper first when testing confirms a compatible flapper leak and no flush-valve-body defect exists. Match the replacement to the seat size and model.
  3. Set chain slack to permit full closure without excessive delay. Adjust the chain length so the flapper seats fully after each flush.
  4. Replace the fill valve when it does not shut off, cannot adjust correctly, leaks, or is incompatible. Select a compatible replacement before beginning removal.
  5. Install the fill-valve shank seal and locknut without overtightening. Hand-tighten, then apply only the manufacturer's specified additional turn.
  6. Set valve height and critical-level relationship according to the manufacturer and code. Confirm the relationship to the overflow-tube opening before restoring water.
  7. Route the refill tube using the approved clip without inserting it below the overflow water level. Confirm the tube outlet position after clipping.

I. Flush-Valve Replacement (When Confirmed)

  1. Remove the tank when replacement of the flush-valve body is required. Use the Chapter 11 tank-removal and support sequence.
  2. Replace tank-to-bowl seals and hardware when disturbed. Do not reuse a compressed or damaged tank-to-bowl gasket.
  3. Install the compatible flush valve, gasket, and locknut in the specified orientation. Confirm orientation before tightening the locknut.
  4. Reassemble the tank evenly and reconnect the supply. Tighten tank-to-bowl hardware alternately and evenly.

J. Restoration and Verification

  1. Restore water and adjust the tank level to the marked or manufacturer-specified setting. Compare directly to the tank-wall mark made in Step F.3.
  2. Perform the dye test again. Confirm no dye passes into the bowl without flushing.
  3. Operate multiple flush cycles and verify refill, shutoff, flapper seating, handle operation, and bowl refill. Observe at least three complete cycles.
  4. Inspect all disturbed seals and fasteners for leakage. Check the fill-valve shank, flush-valve locknut, tank-to-bowl connection, and supply connector.
  5. Document the diagnosed cause, replaced components, adjustments, and final results. Complete every field on the Running-Toilet Repair Worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed repair using the same checks regardless of which component was replaced.

  • Diagnosis check: confirm the verified cause was classified from dye-test and inspection evidence, not assumed.
  • Level check: confirm the water level matches the marked or specified setting and the critical-level relationship to the overflow tube.
  • Dye-test check: confirm no dye passes into the bowl after the repair.
  • Cycle check: confirm refill, shutoff, flapper seating, handle operation, and bowl refill across at least three flush cycles.
  • Seal check: confirm every disturbed seal and fastener is leak-free under static and flushing conditions.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Running-Toilet Repair Worksheet is fully completed.

Complete the Running-Toilet Repair Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the toilet to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Toilet Still Runs After the Flapper Is Replaced

Probable cause: the flush-valve seat itself is corroded, warped, or damaged, or the new flapper is not a compatible match.
Confirmation: inspect the flush-valve seat directly for corrosion, buildup, or an out-of-round condition, and confirm the flapper model against the manufacturer's compatibility information.
Correction: clean the seat if buildup is the cause and confirm the fix with a repeated dye test; if the seat itself is damaged, replace the flush-valve body per Section I.
Reinspection: repeat the dye test and confirm no color enters the bowl.

Water Continuously Enters the Overflow Tube

Probable cause: the fill valve is not shutting off at the correct level, or the float is set too high.
Confirmation: compare the running water level to the tank-wall mark and to the overflow-tube opening.
Correction: adjust the float height per the manufacturer's instructions; if the valve will not hold an adjusted setting, replace the fill valve per Section H.
Reinspection: confirm the level holds at the marked setting across multiple flush cycles.

Toilet Runs Intermittently, Not Continuously

Probable cause: the refill tube is siphoning water below the overflow-tube water level, or the chain has excess slack allowing the flapper to reseat unevenly.
Confirmation: observe the refill-tube position relative to the overflow-tube opening and observe the flapper closure directly after a flush.
Correction: reposition the refill tube above the critical level using the approved clip; adjust chain slack so the flapper seats fully and consistently.
Reinspection: observe multiple flush cycles and confirm consistent, complete flapper closure.

Fill Valve Will Not Shut Off Completely

Probable cause: internal fill-valve wear, debris in the valve seat, or an incompatible replacement valve.
Confirmation: observe a continuous trickle at the overflow tube with the float at its highest position and no flapper leak confirmed by dye test.
Correction: replace the fill valve with a compatible listed component per Section H.
Reinspection: confirm complete shutoff across at least three flush cycles.

Leak Appears at the Tank-to-Bowl Connection After Flush-Valve Replacement

Probable cause: the tank-to-bowl gasket was reused instead of replaced, or the tank-to-bowl bolts were tightened unevenly.
Confirmation: inspect the tank-to-bowl connection under static pressure and during a flush cycle for visible seepage.
Correction: shut off and drain the tank, replace the tank-to-bowl gasket, and retighten the bolts alternately and evenly.
Reinspection: repeat the static and flush-cycle inspection at the tank-to-bowl connection.

Repair Was Performed Before the Cause Was Confirmed

Probable cause: a component was replaced based on assumption rather than the dye test and inspection findings.
Confirmation: compare the component actually replaced against the verified cause recorded on the worksheet.
Correction: re-run the dye test and full component inspection, classify the verified cause in writing, and complete any additional replacement the diagnosis supports.
Reinspection: repeat the dye test and flush-cycle verification for the confirmed cause.

Cleanup

This project ends with the toilet returned to normal service, tested, and documented.

  • Dispose of old flapper, fill-valve, or flush-valve components according to local waste requirements
  • Wipe down the tank exterior and surrounding floor of any spilled tank water or dye residue
  • Confirm the fixture stop remains fully open and accessible
  • Store any unused compatible replacement parts with their manufacturer packaging for future reference
  • File the completed Running-Toilet Repair Worksheet with the fixture's service record

Field Terms

Marked Water Level
The current tank water line marked directly on the tank wall before any component is disturbed.
Critical Level
The fill-valve manufacturer's minimum air-gap relationship between the water level and the overflow-tube opening, used to prevent backflow into the fill valve.
Verified Cause
The specific condition identified through the dye test and component inspection that explains the running-toilet symptom.
Dye Test
Adding approved dye to the tank and checking the bowl for color without flushing, used to confirm or rule out a flapper or seat leak.
Flush-Valve Seat
The molded opening at the base of the tank that the flapper seals against.
Refill Tube
The small tube that directs a portion of incoming fill water into the overflow tube to refill the bowl trap after a flush.
Chain Slack
The amount of free chain length between the flush handle arm and the flapper, set to allow full flapper closure without excessive delay.
Running-Toilet Repair Worksheet
The document recording the observed symptom, dye-test results, verified cause, replaced components, adjustments, and final verification results.

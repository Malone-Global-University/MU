Job Overview

This project qualifies you to clean, flux, assemble, heat, solder, and inspect a controlled copper-tube fitting arrangement using approved lead-free solder and a documented hot-work fire-safety procedure. The finished work product is one water-pressure-tested copper assembly with every joint showing complete solder flow and no leakage.

You will prepare the hot-work area and fire-watch equipment; clean and flux the tube and fitting surfaces; assemble and support the joint; heat the fitting evenly with a torch; flow lead-free solder into the joint by capillary action; cool, clean, and inspect each joint; and complete an approved water-pressure test before concealment.

This project depends directly on the pipe-cutting and deburring skills demonstrated in Chapter 3 and the isolation and drain-down sequence from Chapter 1. It is a separate joining method from the PEX work in Chapter 4 and the push-fit and compression methods in Chapter 6; the three methods are not interchangeable and each has its own tool setup, technique, and inspection standard.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-025 — Clean copper pipe and fitting sockets to bright, uniform metal in preparation for soldering.
  • PL-026 — Apply flux to the correct surfaces in a thin, complete coating.
  • PL-027 — Assemble copper fittings with full, aligned, unstrained insertion.
  • PL-028 — Heat fittings correctly using even flame application and proper joint-temperature judgment.
  • PL-029 — Flow lead-free solder into a heated joint using correct capillary technique.
  • PL-030 — Inspect solder joints for complete flow, alignment, and defects, and correct any leak found during testing.

Where This Skill Fits

Soldered copper is one of three water-supply joining methods taught in this book, alongside the PEX crimp or clamp system in Chapter 4 and the push-fit and compression fittings in Chapter 6. Later chapters that replace fixture stops, water-heater connectors, and exterior faucets may specify a soldered connection where the approved design calls for it.

The hot-work fire-safety discipline established here — heat shielding, fire-watch staging, and a controlled work surface — is the same discipline required whenever this book calls for a torch, and it does not relax on repetition.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • preparing a controlled hot-work area with the required fire-watch equipment and heat shielding
  • cleaning and fluxing copper tube and wrot copper fitting sockets
  • assembling, supporting, and aligning the joint before heating
  • heating the fitting evenly and flowing lead-free solder by capillary action
  • cooling, cleaning, and inspecting the joint
  • completing the approved water-pressure test and correcting any leaking joint

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • solder in the presence of combustible gas, flammable vapor, or an unresolved concealed-combustible condition
  • solder potable-water joints with anything other than the specified lead-free, water-safe solder and flux
  • conceal a soldered assembly before the required water-pressure test and hot-work closeout are complete
  • coat an active leak externally with solder, flux, or sealant instead of remaking the joint

Any condition identified as a stop-work condition in this chapter must be corrected before soldering continues.

Trade Note

A soldered joint is only as reliable as the cleaning and fluxing that precede heating. A joint that is heated well but was not cleaned to bright metal, or that was fluxed after cleaning debris was left behind, is the most common cause of the pinholes and incomplete solder flow documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not ignite the torch until the work area has been inspected for concealed combustible material, the heat shield and noncombustible work surface are in place, and the charged fire extinguisher and fire-watch equipment are staged and ready. Work started before hot-work setup is verified must stop immediately.

Shop Tip

Wipe excess external flux from the joint before heating. Flux left on the outside of the fitting boils and can carry solder away from the joint during heating, producing a joint that looks fully soldered on the outside but has not drawn solder into the cup.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every soldered joint in this project follows the same four-part sequence: clean, flux, heat, solder. Skipping or rushing any one of the four produces the same class of failure — an incomplete or leaking joint — regardless of which fitting or tube size is involved.

Joint Preparation and Capillary Technique

A soldered joint depends on a narrow, uniform gap between the tube and fitting cup, which draws molten solder in by capillary action once the joint reaches soldering temperature. Clean surfaces, a thin flux coating, and even heating are what allow that gap to work as designed; a joint that is scratched, contaminated, over-fluxed, or unevenly heated interrupts capillary action and produces an incomplete joint even if it appears fully soldered from the outside.

Measurement Cue — Test Temperature Opposite the Flame

Test joint temperature by touching solder to the tube or fitting on the side opposite the flame, not at the point where the flame is being applied. Remove or reduce the flame as soon as the joint reaches soldering temperature at that opposite point; continuing to heat directly at the flame contact point risks overheating the fitting before the far side of the joint is ready.

Critical Safety Cue — Fire Watch and Concealed Combustibles

Inspect the work area for concealed combustible material, insulation, wiring, finished surfaces, and openings that could transmit flame or heat before igniting the torch. Maintain the fire watch for the required period after hot work is complete and inspect concealed-adjacent areas for heat damage. Treat any combustible gas or flammable vapor as a stop-work condition; never solder in its presence.

Field Safety Cue — Heat Shielding and Work Surface

Establish a noncombustible work surface and place heat shields behind and below the joint before heating begins. Stage a charged extinguisher and water spray where appropriate. A heat shield placed after the torch is already lit does not protect surfaces that were exposed during setup.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Torch and Solder Products

Ignite and adjust the torch according to the manufacturer's procedure for the specific fuel and tip in use. Use only the specified lead-free, water-safe solder and compatible flux on potable-water joints; do not substitute a different solder alloy or flux product without confirming water-safe compatibility.

E. Hot-Work Area and Material Review

  1. Review the copper tube type, fitting type, lead-free solder, flux, cleaning materials, torch, heat shield, fire-extinguisher, fire-watch, and test requirements. Confirm every product is the approved, water-safe type specified for this project.
  2. Confirm that the work is permitted, piping is isolated and drained, and no combustible gas or flammable vapor is present. Do not proceed if any of these conditions is unresolved.
  3. Inspect the work area for concealed combustible material, insulation, wiring, finished surfaces, and openings that could transmit flame or heat. Record every condition found, even if it does not by itself stop work.

F. Fire-Watch and Torch Setup

  1. Establish a noncombustible work surface and place heat shields behind and below the joint. Confirm shielding covers the full range of flame and radiant heat exposure.
  2. Stage a charged extinguisher, water spray where appropriate, and the required fire-watch equipment. Confirm staging is complete before the torch is ignited.
  3. Inspect the torch, fuel cylinder, hose or valve, tip, ignition system, and fuel compatibility. Do not use torch equipment showing damage, leakage, or incompatible fuel fittings.

G. Tube Preparation and Cleaning

  1. Cut, ream, and clean the copper tube according to Chapter 3. Confirm the cut is square and the tube is free of burrs before proceeding.
  2. Clean the tube exterior and fitting cup to bright, uniform metal without excessive abrasion. Use the fitting brush or abrasive cloth on both surfaces.
  3. Remove cleaning debris and keep prepared surfaces free of oil, dirt, and fingerprints. Handle cleaned surfaces only at their ends once preparation is complete.

H. Fluxing and Assembly

  1. Apply a thin, complete coating of compatible flux to the approved surfaces. Cover the full insertion depth of the tube and the interior of the fitting cup.
  2. Insert the tube fully into the fitting cup and align the assembly without strain. Confirm the tube seats against the internal stop of the fitting.
  3. Wipe excess external flux from the joint. Leave no visible flux on the outside of the assembled joint.
  4. Support the assembly so it cannot move while heated or cooling. Confirm the joint will not shift during the heating and soldering steps that follow.

I. Heating and Soldering

  1. Ignite and adjust the torch according to the manufacturer's procedure. Confirm the flame is set to the correct type and size for the tube diameter being soldered.
  2. Heat the fitting cup evenly rather than directing prolonged flame at one point. Move the flame around the circumference of the fitting.
  3. Test joint temperature with the solder at the side opposite the flame. Confirm the joint has reached soldering temperature before feeding solder.
  4. Remove or reduce the flame when the joint reaches soldering temperature. Avoid continuing to heat once solder will melt at the joint.
  5. Feed the specified amount of lead-free solder around the joint and allow capillary action to draw it into the fitting. Confirm a complete ring of solder is visible around the joint.
  6. Remove heat and solder, then allow the joint to cool without movement or forced cooling. Do not disturb the joint or apply water or forced air to speed cooling.

J. Cleaning, Inspection, and Fire Watch

  1. Wipe residual flux using the approved method after the joint has cooled sufficiently. Confirm the joint is cool enough to handle safely before wiping.
  2. Inspect the joint for complete solder flow, alignment, overheating, scorched flux, pinholes, cracks, or excess solder. Record the condition of every joint in the joint scope.
  3. Maintain the fire watch for the required period and inspect concealed-adjacent areas for heat damage. Do not leave the work area unattended before this period is complete.

K. Testing and Documentation

  1. Flush the assembly and complete the approved water-pressure test. Confirm the assembly holds the approved test pressure for the approved duration.
  2. Depressurize and remake any leaking joint rather than coating it externally with solder or sealant. Cut out and rebuild a failed joint using fresh, cleaned, fluxed components.
  3. Document hot-work controls, joint inspection, test result, and corrections. Complete every field on the Soldered Joint Test Record before concealment.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed soldered assembly using the same checks regardless of which fitting pattern or tube size the project involves.

  • Preparation check: confirm every tube end and fitting cup was cleaned to bright metal and fluxed before assembly.
  • Assembly check: confirm full insertion, alignment, and support at every joint.
  • Solder-flow check: confirm a complete ring of solder is visible around every joint with no pinholes or gaps.
  • Fire-watch check: confirm the required observation period was completed with no delayed ignition or heat damage.
  • Pressure-test check: confirm the assembly held the approved test pressure for the approved duration with no leakage.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Soldered Joint Test Record is fully completed and signed.

Complete the Soldered Joint Test Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before concealing any assembly from this project.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Joint Leaks Under Water-Pressure Test

Probable cause: incomplete solder flow around the joint, contamination on a cleaned surface before fluxing, or insufficient heating on one side of the fitting.
Confirmation: depressurize, dry the joint, and inspect visually for a gap in the solder ring or a dull, unfluxed surface area.
Correction: depressurize fully, disassemble the joint, re-clean and re-flux both surfaces, and resolder using fresh solder; do not coat the exterior of the existing joint with additional solder or sealant.
Reinspection: repeat the water-pressure test on the remade joint.

Solder Will Not Draw Into the Joint

Probable cause: the joint has not reached soldering temperature, the gap between tube and fitting is too wide or too tight, or flux was insufficient or has burned off.
Confirmation: test temperature again with solder at the point opposite the flame; inspect the tube-to-fitting fit for excessive play or resistance.
Correction: reheat evenly until soldering temperature is confirmed opposite the flame; if the fit is out of tolerance, replace the tube or fitting rather than forcing solder to bridge an oversized gap.
Reinspection: confirm a complete solder ring forms before proceeding to cooling.

Fitting or Tube Shows Overheating or Scorched Flux

Probable cause: prolonged flame applied to one point rather than moving the flame evenly around the fitting.
Confirmation: inspect the fitting for discoloration beyond normal heat marking and inspect the flux for a scorched, blackened appearance.
Correction: if the fitting or tube shows damage beyond surface discoloration, cut out and replace the affected component rather than resoldering over damaged metal.
Reinspection: re-clean, re-flux, and resolder the replacement joint following the full sequence.

Heat Damage Is Found in a Concealed-Adjacent Area During the Fire Watch

Probable cause: the heat shield did not fully cover the exposure path, or a combustible material was closer to the joint than assessed during setup.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area directly and confirm the source and extent of the heat damage.
Correction: extinguish or control any active condition immediately; treat any smoldering or charred material as an immediate stop-work condition and address it before resuming any further hot work.
Reinspection: repeat the fire watch for the full required period once the condition is controlled.

Joint Scope Was Not Clearly Defined Before Hot Work Began

Probable cause: the tube size, fitting count, or solder/flux product was not recorded in writing before soldering started.
Confirmation: compare the joints actually soldered against the intended joint scope for this project.
Correction: re-define the joint scope in writing and confirm every joint soldered matches an approved fitting pattern and material specification.
Reinspection: repeat the solder-flow and pressure-test checks for the confirmed scope.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to replace one accessible fixture angle stop valve and its flexible supply connector under controlled, code-compliant conditions. The finished work product is one replaced valve and connector assembly on an inspected pipe stub-out, tested under both static and operating pressure and left leak-free at every disturbed connection.

You will identify the fixture, pipe material, and existing valve connection; shut off, drain, and protect the work area using the Chapter 1 sequence; remove the old valve using the method specific to its connection type; inspect the exposed pipe stub-out; install the replacement valve using the approved connection method for the project; select and route a listed flexible supply connector; restore water slowly; and inspect the completed assembly under static and operating conditions.

This project builds directly on the isolation and drain-down skills from Chapter 1 and the pipe-preparation and joining skills from Chapters 3 through 6. It prepares you for the fixture-installation projects in Chapters 9 through 13, each of which depends on a correctly installed, accessible fixture stop.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-037 — Shut off the fixture supply using the correct upstream valve and verify isolation before disconnecting any component.
  • PL-038 — Remove the old stop valve using the method specific to its connection type without damaging the pipe stub-out.
  • PL-039 — Install the new stop valve using the approved threaded, compression, soldered, push-fit, or PEX method specified for the project.
  • PL-040 — Install a listed flexible supply line of the correct material, length, and end connections without kinking, twisting, or stressing the connector.
  • PL-041 — Restore water to the fixture supply slowly and purge air through the fixture before full-flow operation.
  • PL-042 — Inspect the completed valve and connector assembly for leaks under static and operating conditions.

Where This Skill Fits

Every fixture project later in this book — the bathroom faucet and pop-up drain in Chapter 10, the toilet set in Chapter 11, and the capstone bathroom in Chapter 20 — depends on an accessible, operable, leak-free fixture stop already in place. Replacing a stop valve is one of the most frequently performed residential plumbing repairs, and it is the point at which many careful installers still cause a leak: at the valve-to-pipe connection, or at the connector-to-fixture connection.

The isolation, drain-down, and documentation discipline from Chapter 1 applies directly here. The joining method used to install the new valve is the same method taught for that pipe material in Chapters 4 through 6; this project does not teach a new joining method, it applies the one already learned to a stop-valve installation.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the fixture, pipe material, existing valve connection, new valve type, connector size, and fixture-inlet requirement
  • shutting off, draining, and protecting the work area using the Chapter 1 sequence
  • removing one existing accessible angle stop valve using the method specific to its connection type
  • inspecting the exposed pipe stub-out for support, length, corrosion, and condition
  • installing one replacement angle stop valve using the threaded, compression, soldered, push-fit, or PEX method specified for the project
  • selecting and installing one listed flexible supply connector between the new valve and the fixture
  • restoring water slowly, purging air, and inspecting the completed assembly under static and operating conditions

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut into or extend concealed supply piping beyond the accessible stub-out — that work belongs to the applicable pipe-joining chapter and is outside this project's boundary
  • proceed when the pipe stub-out is severely corroded, cracked, shortened beyond use, loose in the wall, or connected by an unrecognized method
  • replace a valve serving a system that cannot be confidently isolated or identified
  • substitute an unlisted connector, an incompatible valve connection type, or a valve not rated for the pipe material and service

Any pipe stub-out condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected, repaired, or escalated to a qualified professional before the replacement valve is installed.

Trade Note

A flexible supply connector is a wear item, not a lifetime component. Whenever a stop valve is replaced, replace the connector as well, even if the existing connector appears undamaged. Reusing an aged connector against a new valve is a common cause of an early leak documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not disconnect the old valve or connector until the fixture supply has been shut off, drained, and protected using the Chapter 1 sequence, and isolation has been verified by an observed pressure-decay or flow-stoppage test. Do not install the new valve until the exposed pipe stub-out has been inspected and found structurally sound and long enough for the selected valve.

Shop Tip

Photograph the existing valve and connector orientation before removal. A quick reference photo makes it easier to confirm hot and cold orientation, connector routing, and cabinet clearance when installing the replacement.

Common Field Fundamentals

Replacing an angle stop valve always follows the same core sequence regardless of pipe material or valve connection type: isolate, remove, inspect the stub-out, install, connect, restore, and verify. Backup Support and Connection Control

Every threaded or union connection in this project — the old valve coming off, the new valve going on, and both ends of the flexible connector — must be loosened and tightened using a backup wrench on the adjacent fitting or pipe. A pipe stub-out that twists in the wall during valve removal or installation can crack a concealed joint that will not be discovered until later.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Stub-Out Length and Support Before Installing the New Valve

Verify that the pipe stub-out is structurally supported and projects far enough from the wall or floor for the selected valve body and its connection method. Do not force a valve onto a stub-out that is too short; use an approved extension method appropriate to the pipe material instead.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Isolation Before Disconnecting Any Component

Do not loosen the old valve or connector until isolation has been verified by an observed pressure-decay or flow-stoppage test at the fixture, per Chapter 1. A valve that appears closed but has not been verified can release pressurized water the moment a fitting is loosened.

Field Safety Cue — Working Under a Fixture in a Confined Cabinet

Provide adequate lighting and clear the cabinet of stored items before working under a sink or behind a toilet. Confined, poorly lit spaces make it easy to miss a slow leak or a loose backup connection during inspection.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Valve and Connector Compatibility

Install only a replacement valve and connector listed for the pipe material, fixture service, and pressure and temperature conditions present. Do not substitute a connector length, end-fitting type, or valve connection method not specified for the project.

E. Fixture and Connection Identification

  1. Identify the fixture, pipe material, existing valve connection, new valve type, connector size, and fixture-inlet requirement. Record each item on the worksheet before proceeding.
  2. Confirm that the replacement valve is approved for the pipe material and intended service. Verify the manufacturer listing before installation.

F. Isolation, Removal, and Stub-Out Inspection

  1. Shut off, drain, depressurize, and protect the area according to Chapter 1. Verify isolation by direct observation before continuing.
  2. Verify that the pipe stub-out is structurally supported and long enough for the selected valve. Do not proceed if the stub-out is unsupported or too short.
  3. Disconnect the flexible supply line from the fixture and valve while controlling residual water. Direct released water into a bucket or absorbent towel.
  4. Hold the existing valve or adapter with the correct backup tool. Prevent the stub-out from twisting during removal.
  5. Remove the old valve using the method specific to its connection type. Do not apply excessive force or an incompatible removal technique.
  6. Stop work if the pipe is severely corroded, cracked, shortened beyond use, loose in the wall, or connected by an unrecognized method. Escalate per the Failure Points section before continuing.
  7. Clean and inspect the exposed pipe without reducing wall thickness or damaging the sealing surface. Confirm a sound, clean stub-out before installing the new valve.

G. Replacement Valve Installation

  1. Install the replacement valve using the approved threaded, compression, soldered, push-fit, or PEX method specified for the project. Follow the exact joining procedure taught for that method in the applicable chapter.
  2. Orient the valve outlet toward the fixture without exceeding the permitted adjustment or stressing the pipe. Confirm the outlet position accommodates the connector without a forced bend.
  3. Close the valve before restoring building pressure. Confirm the valve is fully closed by feel and position.

H. Flexible Connector Installation

  1. Select a listed flexible connector of the correct material, length, end sizes, temperature rating, and service. Confirm the selection against the fixture-inlet requirement recorded in Step 1.
  2. Route the connector without twisting, kinking, stretching, abrasion, or contact with sharp edges. Confirm a smooth, unstressed path between the valve and the fixture inlet.
  3. Start connector threads by hand and tighten according to the connector and fixture instructions. Use a backup wrench at the valve outlet where required.

I. Restoring Water and Static Inspection

  1. Restore water slowly while the new stop remains closed. Observe the valve body and connector connections for leakage as pressure builds.
  2. Inspect the valve-to-pipe connection under static pressure. Confirm no leakage before opening the valve.

J. Operating Inspection and Correction

  1. Open the stop slowly and purge air through the fixture. Run the fixture until air is cleared and flow is steady.
  2. Inspect both connector ends and the valve stem under static and operating conditions. Check for leakage at each end fitting and at the valve stem while the fixture is flowing.
  3. Operate the valve through its intended range and verify accessible shutoff. Confirm the valve closes fully and reopens smoothly.
  4. Correct leakage only after re-isolating and depressurizing the system. Do not tighten a leaking connection under pressure.
  5. Dry the area and perform a delayed reinspection. Return after normal use to confirm no leakage has developed.
  6. Label or document the new valve and connector installation. Complete every field on the Angle Stop Replacement Worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed valve and connector replacement using the same checks regardless of which fixture or pipe material the project involves.

  • Stub-out check: confirm the exposed pipe was structurally sound, supported, and correctly sized before the new valve was installed.
  • Valve check: confirm the replacement valve was installed using the project-specified connection method and closes and opens fully.
  • Connector check: confirm the flexible connector is free of kinks, twists, and contact with sharp edges.
  • Static test check: confirm no leakage was observed at any disturbed connection under standing pressure.
  • Operating test check: confirm no leakage was observed at any disturbed connection during fixture flow and valve operation.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Angle Stop Replacement Worksheet is fully completed and signed.

Complete the Angle Stop Replacement Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 08.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Valve-to-Pipe Connection Leaks Under Static Pressure

Probable cause: an incomplete joint at installation, an incorrect connection method for the pipe material, or a stub-out that was damaged during removal of the old valve.
Confirmation: dry the area, restore static pressure, and inspect the joint directly with a flashlight and dry tissue.
Correction: re-isolate and depressurize the system, then remake the joint using the project-specified connection method; replace the valve or stub-out section if damaged.
Reinspection: repeat the static pressure test before proceeding to the operating test.

The Flexible Connector Leaks at One End

Probable cause: the connector was overtightened or undertightened, cross-threaded, or the washer or seal inside the end fitting is missing or damaged.
Confirmation: re-isolate and depressurize, then inspect the end fitting, threads, and internal seal directly.
Correction: replace the connector rather than reusing a damaged seal; reinstall by hand-starting the threads and tightening per the connector manufacturer's instructions.
Reinspection: repeat both the static and operating pressure tests.

The Pipe Stub-Out Moves or Twists During Valve Installation

Probable cause: the stub-out was not held with a backup wrench while the valve connection was tightened.
Confirmation: inspect the stub-out and any concealed joint behind it for movement, cracking, or leakage.
Correction: stop work, re-isolate and depressurize, and inspect the concealed joint before continuing; escalate to a qualified professional if the concealed joint cannot be verified.
Reinspection: repeat the isolation verification and static pressure test before reinstalling the valve.

The Replacement Valve Will Not Fully Close

Probable cause: debris in the valve seat introduced during installation, or a defective valve.
Confirmation: re-isolate the upstream supply, depressurize, and operate the valve through its full range while observing the seat and handle.
Correction: flush the valve if debris is suspected; replace the valve if it does not seat correctly after flushing.
Reinspection: repeat the operating test and confirm full shutoff before returning the fixture to service.

The Pipe Stub-Out Is Too Short, Cracked, or Severely Corroded

Probable cause: age-related deterioration, prior damage, or a stub-out that was never adequate for the current valve type.
Confirmation: inspect the exposed pipe directly for length, cracking, and corrosion before attempting valve installation.
Correction: stop work; this condition is outside this project's boundary and requires an approved stub-out extension or repair method from the applicable pipe-joining chapter, or referral to a qualified professional.
Reinspection: do not proceed with valve installation until the stub-out has been corrected and reinspected.

A Slow Leak Appears Only After Delayed Reinspection

Probable cause: a connection that was tight enough to pass the initial test but continued to seat or seal under prolonged use.
Confirmation: perform the delayed reinspection at the specified interval using a dry-tissue or moisture-meter check at every disturbed connection.
Correction: re-isolate and depressurize, then tighten, reseal, or replace the affected connection using the approved method.
Reinspection: repeat the delayed reinspection after correction.

Cleanup

This project ends with the replacement valve and connector installed, tested, and left ready for normal fixture use.

  • Dispose of the old valve, connector, and any cut pipe sections properly
  • Clean and dry the cabinet or wall area disturbed during the work
  • Confirm the new valve is left in the correct operating position for normal fixture use
  • Wipe down tools and store unused sealant, solder, or fitting materials
  • Store the Angle Stop Replacement Worksheet with the fixture's maintenance records

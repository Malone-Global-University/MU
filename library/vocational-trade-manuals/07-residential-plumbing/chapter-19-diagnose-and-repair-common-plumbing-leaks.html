Job Overview

This project qualifies you to diagnose one reported plumbing leak using evidence-based isolation rather than assumption, and to complete one authorized repair within the controlled scope of this chapter: a compression leak, a threaded leak, or a slip-joint trap leak. The finished work product is one completed Leak Diagnosis and Repair Record: a documented symptom, a confirmed leak source, an authorized repair, a repeated test that reproduces the original condition, and a delayed dry reinspection.

You will define the reported symptom; inspect for stop-work conditions such as electrical exposure or sewage; isolate the affected system using Chapter 1 procedures; separate the plumbing system into supply, fixture, appliance, drain, overflow, sealant, condensation, and exterior-water possibilities; test one condition at a time using dry tissue, an inspection mirror, a moisture meter, or dye; trace water to the highest confirmed wet point; complete the repair matching the confirmed leak type; and verify the repair by repeating the exact condition that produced the original leak.

This project depends directly on the isolation and drain-down system from Chapter 1, the system-mapping skills from Chapter 2, the compression-fitting skills from Chapter 6, and the slip-joint trap skills from Chapter 9. It also prepares you for the Chapter 20 capstone, where the same evidence-based diagnostic discipline supports troubleshooting during rough-in and finish testing.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-109 — Identify the leak source using evidence-based isolation of the reported symptom.
  • PL-110 — Distinguish supply leaks from drain leaks and from other water sources present in the work area.
  • PL-111 — Repair a confirmed compression leak using the approved isolation, alignment, and correction procedure.
  • PL-112 — Repair a confirmed threaded leak using the approved disassembly, cleaning, sealant, and reassembly procedure.
  • PL-113 — Repair a confirmed slip-joint trap leak using the approved alignment and washer-replacement procedure.
  • PL-114 — Verify leak correction through a repeated test and a delayed dry reinspection.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior installation project in this book — PEX and copper supply lines, PVC DWV assembly, fixture traps, faucets, toilets, shower valves, appliances, hose bibbs, and the water-heater connector set — depends on a leak-free result. This project teaches the diagnostic discipline that applies when a leak is reported after any of that work, or in a system this book did not install.

The isolation, staged testing, and single-condition method used here is the same discipline required for the rough-in and finish testing in the Chapter 20 capstone project, and for any future service call where the reported symptom does not immediately identify its source.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the reported symptom, location, timing, water use, and weather condition
  • inspecting for stop-work conditions before any diagnostic testing begins
  • isolating supply, fixture, appliance, drain, overflow, sealant, condensation, and exterior-water possibilities one condition at a time
  • confirming the leak source using dry tissue, an inspection mirror, a moisture meter, or dye rather than assumption
  • repairing a confirmed compression leak, threaded leak, or slip-joint trap leak within this chapter's controlled correction methods
  • verifying the repair by repeating the exact condition that produced the original leak and completing a delayed dry reinspection

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • repair a fixture or appliance leak outside the three controlled repair types in this chapter without following the exact manufacturer procedure or referring the work to a qualified professional
  • coat an active leak externally with caulk, tape, epoxy, solder, or sealant, unless the approved repair system specifically requires it
  • proceed with a repair when the confirmed source is concealed, recurring, structural, sewage-related, gas-related, or associated with the water heater — these conditions must be escalated to the required professional
  • skip the repeated test or the delayed dry reinspection before declaring the repair complete

Any leak whose source cannot be confirmed by the methods in this chapter, or whose repair falls outside compression, threaded, or slip-joint correction, must be escalated per the Failure Points section before further work continues.

Trade Note

Every repair in this chapter is preceded by a confirmed source, never a probable one. Coating a visible drip with sealant without tracing it to its actual origin is the single most common cause of a leak reappearing after concealment, because the water often travels along framing or piping before it becomes visible.

Setup Standard

Do not repair any joint, fitting, or connection until its status as the confirmed leak source has been established by direct testing at that specific point. A repair performed on a probable source, without confirmation, does not satisfy the diagnostic requirement of this chapter and must be re-verified before the project may be closed out.

Shop Tip

Photograph the wet pattern before drying anything. A dried surface loses the exact shape and highest point of the water stain, and that shape is often the fastest way to trace water back to its true origin above the visible drip.

Common Field Fundamentals

A leak diagnosis fails when the repair is performed on the visible drip rather than the confirmed source. Water travels along framing, piping, and finish surfaces before it becomes visible, so the point where water first appears is frequently not the point where it first escaped. This chapter's method separates possibilities systematically — supply, fixture, appliance, drain, overflow, sealant, condensation, and exterior water — and tests each one directly before any repair begins.

Stop-Work Conditions Before Testing Begins

Inspect for electrical exposure, sewage, hot water, structural damage, mold, and ceiling saturation before any diagnostic test is performed. Protect the area and isolate energy or water immediately where damage is actively occurring. Do not proceed with staged testing while an active stop-work condition is present and uncorrected.

Critical Safety Cue — Electrical Exposure Near Water

Treat any water intrusion near electrical devices, panels, or wiring as a stop-work condition until a qualified electrician confirms the area is safe. Do not touch a wet electrical device, and do not test water conditions in an area with visible electrical exposure until power is verified disconnected.

Field Safety Cue — Drying Before Testing

Dry all accessible surfaces before beginning single-condition testing. A surface that is already damp from a prior test or from ambient moisture will produce a false-positive result on the next condition tested.

Measurement Cue — Trace to the Highest Confirmed Wet Point

Do not assume the visible drip is the leak source. Trace moisture upward and outward using the inspection mirror, moisture meter, and dry-tissue test until you reach the highest point where wetness is directly confirmed, and treat that point as the working source until testing proves otherwise.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Sealant and Ferrule Compatibility

Use thread sealant rated for the service and compatible with the pipe material. Use ferrules and washers that match the fitting manufacturer's specification. A mismatched ferrule or an incompatible sealant is a common cause of a repair that appears successful during the initial test but fails during the delayed reinspection.

Code Alert — Escalation Outside Chapter Scope

A confirmed leak that is concealed, recurring after a prior correct repair, structural, sewage-related, gas-related, or associated with the water heater falls outside this chapter's controlled repair types. Escalate these conditions to the required qualified professional rather than attempting a repair outside the approved methods.

E. Symptom Definition and Initial Inspection

  1. Define the reported symptom, location, timing, water use, weather condition, and prior repair history. Record the exact wording of the reported symptom before any testing begins.
  2. Inspect for electrical exposure, sewage, hot water, structural damage, mold, ceiling saturation, and other stop-work conditions. Treat any of these as an immediate stop-work condition until corrected or escalated.
  3. Protect the area and isolate energy or water where immediate damage is occurring. Use the Chapter 1 isolation and protection procedure for the affected system.

F. Staged Single-Condition Testing

  1. Dry all accessible surfaces before testing. Confirm a dry starting condition at every point that will be tested.
  2. Observe the water meter or other approved indicator with all fixtures off where appropriate. Record whether the indicator shows continuous flow with no fixture in use.
  3. Separate the system into supply, fixture, appliance, drain, overflow, sealant, condensation, and exterior-water possibilities. List each possibility present in the affected area before testing any of them.
  4. Test cold supply, hot supply, fixture operation, basin fill, overflow, full-basin drain, trap, appliance cycle, and nearby penetrations one condition at a time. Dry the area between each tested condition.
  5. Use dry tissue, inspection mirror, moisture meter, dye, or other approved confirmation tool. Record the result of each test as confirmed wet, confirmed dry, or inconclusive.
  6. Trace water to the highest confirmed wet point rather than assuming the visible drip is the source. Continue tracing upward and outward until no further wet point is confirmed.
  7. Document the confirmed leak source and repair boundary. Record the exact joint, fitting, or connection identified as the source before selecting a repair method.

G. Controlled Repair by Confirmed Type

  1. For a compression leak, isolate and depressurize, inspect alignment and ferrule condition, and perform only the approved adjustment or replacement. Use backup wrenches, hold the fitting body stationary, and do not overtighten the compression nut beyond the manufacturer's controlled procedure.
  2. For a threaded leak, isolate and disassemble, inspect thread condition, clean, apply compatible sealant, and reassemble with backup support. Apply thread sealant only to the confirmed threaded connection, not to any other joint in the affected run.
  3. For a slip-joint trap leak, drain the assembly, correct alignment, replace damaged washers, and retighten within the permitted range. Confirm the trap seal depth and alignment match the Chapter 9 configuration before retightening.
  4. For a fixture or appliance leak outside these controlled repair types, follow the exact manufacturer procedure or refer the work. Document the referral and the reason the leak falls outside this chapter's controlled repair types.
  5. Do not coat an active leak externally with caulk, tape, epoxy, solder, or sealant unless the approved repair system specifically requires it. Confirm the repair addresses the confirmed source directly rather than concealing the symptom.

H. Verification and Documentation

  1. Restore water or operation gradually. Observe the repaired connection continuously while pressure or flow is restored.
  2. Repeat the exact condition that produced the original leak. Reproduce the same fixture use, water temperature, or operating cycle described in the original symptom.
  3. Inspect immediately and after the specified observation period. Record both the immediate result and the delayed result separately.
  4. Check adjacent and lower building areas for retained moisture or secondary damage. Confirm no secondary leak path was created by the original leak or by the repair.
  5. Dry the area and record repair materials, test conditions, observations, and results. Complete every field on the Leak Diagnosis and Repair Record.
  6. Escalate concealed, recurring, structural, sewage, gas, or water-heater leaks to the required professional. Document the escalation and the reason before closing out the project.
  7. Complete the leak-repair closeout and owner monitoring instructions. Provide the owner with the confirmed source, repair performed, and what to monitor going forward.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed leak repair using the same checks regardless of which controlled repair type was performed.

  • Symptom check: confirm the reported symptom, location, timing, water use, and weather condition were recorded before testing began.
  • Source check: confirm the leak source was identified by direct testing at the traced point, not by assumption.
  • Repair-match check: confirm the repair method matches the confirmed leak type exactly.
  • Repeated-test check: confirm the exact original condition was reproduced with no leakage observed.
  • Delayed-reinspection check: confirm the repaired area and adjacent lower areas remain dry after the specified observation period.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Leak Diagnosis and Repair Record is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Water Meter Continues to Register Flow with All Fixtures Off

Probable cause: a supply-side leak exists somewhere between the meter and the fixture stops, including a concealed section.
Confirmation: isolate branches or fixture groups one at a time using accessible shutoffs and re-observe the meter after each isolation.
Correction: narrow the leak to the smallest isolatable branch; if the confirmed source is concealed, escalate per this chapter's scope limits before opening any finished surface.
Reinspection: repeat the meter observation with the identified branch isolated to confirm the flow stops.

A Repaired Compression Fitting Still Leaks After Retightening

Probable cause: a damaged or reused ferrule, misalignment between the tube and fitting body, or a tube end that is not square.
Confirmation: depressurize and inspect the ferrule seating surface and tube-end squareness directly.
Correction: replace the ferrule and nut, re-cut the tube end square if required, and reassemble using the approved incremental tightening procedure with backup support.
Reinspection: repeat the repeated-condition test and the delayed dry reinspection.

A Repaired Threaded Connection Still Leaks After Reassembly

Probable cause: incomplete thread cleaning, insufficient or incompatible sealant, or cross-threading during reassembly.
Confirmation: depressurize, disassemble, and inspect the thread condition and sealant coverage directly.
Correction: clean the threads fully, apply the correct compatible sealant, and reassemble with backup support, verifying the connection threads squarely before final tightening.
Reinspection: repeat the repeated-condition test and the delayed dry reinspection.

A Repaired Slip-Joint Trap Still Leaks After Retightening

Probable cause: a misaligned trap arm, a washer installed in the wrong orientation, or side load on the connection from an unsupported tailpiece.
Confirmation: drain the assembly and inspect washer orientation and trap alignment directly, following the Chapter 9 alignment procedure.
Correction: correct the alignment, replace the washer in the correct orientation, and retighten within the permitted range without overtightening.
Reinspection: repeat the fill-and-release test and the delayed dry reinspection.

The Confirmed Source Cannot Be Reached Without Opening a Finished Surface

Probable cause: the leak source is behind a wall, ceiling, or floor finish that was not part of the original scope.
Confirmation: confirm the traced source location using the highest confirmed wet point and available access points before deciding further opening is required.
Correction: escalate the concealed repair to the qualified professional or scope authorized for that work; do not repair a different, more accessible joint on the assumption that it is the same source.
Reinspection: repeat single-condition testing after the concealed repair to confirm the actual source has been corrected.

The Delayed Reinspection Finds Moisture After an Initially Dry Test

Probable cause: a slow leak that requires sustained pressure or repeated cycling to become detectable, or a secondary source not tested during the original single-condition sequence.
Confirmation: re-run the full single-condition testing sequence rather than assuming the original repair is the cause.
Correction: identify and correct the newly confirmed source using the matching controlled repair type.
Reinspection: repeat the repeated-condition test and schedule a second delayed dry reinspection.

Cleanup

This project ends with the confirmed source repaired, the repeated test passed, and the area dried and monitored through the delayed reinspection period.

  • Remove and properly dispose of saturated absorbent materials and dye residue
  • Empty and clean any containment used during testing and repair
  • Confirm the repaired connection is accessible for the delayed reinspection
  • Leave any isolated shutoff restored to normal service unless the assigned project requires otherwise
  • Store the Leak Diagnosis and Repair Record with the owner monitoring instructions

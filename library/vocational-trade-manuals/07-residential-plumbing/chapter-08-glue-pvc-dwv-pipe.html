Job Overview

This project qualifies you to assemble a controlled PVC drain-waste-vent (DWV) branch that follows an approved drawing exactly: the specified pipe size, fitting pattern, flow direction, slope, vent relationship, and cleanout access. The finished work product is one supported, correctly sloped PVC DWV branch, dry-fit and marked before assembly, permanently solvent-welded joint by joint, and confirmed by the approved leak or water test before concealment.

You will review the approved DWV drawing; inspect pipe, fittings, primer, and cement; measure pipe using actual fitting socket depths; dry-assemble the entire branch and mark alignment; disassemble and solvent-weld one joint at a time in a sequence that preserves access and alignment; install permanent supports; allow the manufacturer-controlled cure time; and complete the required leak or water test before the joints are concealed.

This project depends directly on the pipe-cutting and deburring skills from Chapter 3 and the work-area isolation and hazard-control practices from Chapter 1. It supplies the drain path that the Chapter 9 sink trap connects to, and it establishes the fitting-pattern discipline the Chapter 20 capstone depends on for its lavatory and toilet drain rough-in.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-043 — Measure DWV pipe using actual fitting socket depths and the approved allowance method.
  • PL-044 — Dry-fit fittings across the entire branch and mark alignment before permanent assembly.
  • PL-045 — Apply primer to the fitting socket and pipe end where required by the adopted system.
  • PL-046 — Apply solvent cement uniformly using the manufacturer-controlled sequence and coverage.
  • PL-047 — Assemble pipe correctly, inserting fully while rotating only as permitted to align reference marks.
  • PL-048 — Inspect joint alignment, slope, fitting direction, and supports before concealment.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the approved DWV drawing, pipe size, fitting types, flow direction, slope, vent relationship, cleanout access, support points, and test procedure
  • measuring, cutting, deburring, chamfering, cleaning, and labeling each pipe section using the approved allowance method
  • dry-assembling the entire branch, marking alignment, and disassembling one joint at a time for permanent assembly
  • applying primer and solvent cement in the manufacturer-controlled sequence and permanently assembling each joint
  • installing permanent supports at the specified slope and completing the approved leak or water test

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent a pipe size, fitting type, slope, vent arrangement, or cleanout location not supplied by the approved DWV drawing or adopted code
  • substitute a different fitting pattern, trap configuration, or venting arrangement to simplify the installation
  • conceal any joint, support, or connection before the required leak or water test has passed and the required inspection has been completed
  • proceed with cementing when a stop-work condition identified in this chapter is present and uncorrected

Any DWV drawing conflict, unidentified pipe condition, or failed dry-fit must be corrected, resolved, or escalated to a qualified professional before permanent assembly continues.

Trade Note

Every permanent joint in this chapter is solvent-welded only after the entire branch has been dry-assembled, checked for fit, and marked for alignment. Cementing a joint before the full branch has been dry-fit is the most common cause of a misaligned fitting that cannot be corrected once the cement has set.

Setup Standard

Do not apply primer or cement to any joint until the entire branch has been dry-assembled, the specified slope has been confirmed, and every joint has been marked for alignment. A joint cemented before the dry-fit is confirmed must be treated as a stop-work condition for that joint, not corrected by forcing rotation after the cement has set.

Shop Tip

Work in an order that keeps every remaining joint accessible. Cementing a joint that blocks tool access to an unfinished joint is the most common reason a branch has to be partially disassembled and rebuilt.

Common Field Fundamentals

A solvent-welded PVC DWV joint is a chemical weld, not a mechanical fitting: the primer softens the mating surfaces and the solvent cement fuses them together as the solvent flashes off. Once a joint has been fully inserted and held for the required period, it cannot be rotated to correct alignment without damaging the weld. This is why the entire branch is dry-assembled and marked before any primer is applied, and why joints are cemented one at a time in a sequence that keeps every remaining joint accessible.

Dry-Fit Before Cement, One Joint at a Time When Cementing

Dry-assemble the entire branch first. Confirm every fitting direction, the specified slope, and cleanout access with the whole branch temporarily in place. Mark alignment across every joint. Only then disassemble and permanently cement one joint at a time, in a sequence that preserves access to every joint not yet cemented.

Measurement Cue — Cut Length Uses Actual Socket Depth

Calculate pipe-cut length using the actual fitting socket depths measured on the specific fittings being used, not a generalized allowance. Confirm the fitting centerline-to-centerline distance from the approved drawing, then subtract the measured socket depths at each end to determine the pipe-only cut length.

Critical Safety Cue — Primer and Cement Vapors and Fire

PVC primer and solvent cement produce flammable vapors. Provide the ventilation specified by the product label, keep the work area free of open flame and other ignition sources, and do not use these products in a confined or unventilated space. Treat inadequate ventilation, a nearby ignition source, or an expired or unlabeled product as a stop-work condition.

Field Safety Cue — Chemical Contact and Housekeeping

Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection whenever primer or cement containers are open. Keep applicators, rags, and open containers away from finished surfaces, and dispose of solvent-soaked rags according to the product label and local requirements.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Primer, Cement, and Cure Time

Apply primer and cement only in the sequence, coverage, and hold time stated by the product manufacturer. Cure time before pressure or water testing depends on pipe size, joint size, temperature, and humidity; use the manufacturer's published cure-time table for the actual field conditions rather than a fixed default.

F. Drawing Review and Material Preparation

  1. Review the approved DWV drawing, pipe size, fitting types, flow direction, slope, vent relationship, cleanout access, support points, and test procedure. Record the branch identification and drawing reference on the worksheet before cutting any pipe.
  2. Confirm that all pipe and fittings are compatible DWV products and are within storage, temperature, and condition limits. Reject any component showing UV damage, deformation, contamination, or an incompatible pressure class.

G. Work-Area Preparation and Product Inspection

  1. Provide required ventilation and chemical-resistant work protection. Confirm the work area meets the primer and cement product-label ventilation requirement before opening any container.
  2. Inspect primer, solvent cement, applicators, and product dates. Reject any product past its stated shelf life or showing separation, thickening, or contamination.

H. Layout, Measurement, and Preparation

  1. Establish the branch reference elevation, flow direction, fitting centerlines, and support locations. Mark these references on the framing or work surface before cutting pipe.
  2. Measure pipe lengths using actual fitting socket depths and the approved allowance method. Calculate each cut length from the fitting-centerline dimension minus the measured socket depths at both ends.
  3. Cut, deburr, chamfer, clean, and label each pipe section. Follow the Chapter 3 cutting and preparation procedure for every section and label each one by position in the branch.

I. Dry-Fit and Alignment

  1. Dry-assemble the entire branch without forcing fittings or damaging sockets. Confirm every pipe reaches full insertion depth without cement.
  2. Rotate drainage fittings to the approved direction and maintain required sweep. Confirm flow direction matches the approved drawing at every fitting.
  3. Establish the specified slope using supports or temporary blocking. Verify the slope with a level across the full branch length before marking alignment.
  4. Mark pipe-to-fitting alignment lines across every joint. Use a continuous felt-tip line across each joint so the cemented joint can be returned to the identical position.

J. Priming, Cementing, and Permanent Assembly

  1. Disassemble one joint at a time while preserving the dry-fit sequence. Work in an order that keeps every remaining joint accessible for tools and inspection.
  2. Apply primer to the fitting socket and pipe end where required by the adopted system. Cover the full mating surface without leaving dry or skipped areas.
  3. Apply solvent cement uniformly using the manufacturer-controlled sequence and coverage. Apply to both the pipe end and fitting socket in the order and thickness stated by the product instructions.
  4. Insert the pipe fully while rotating only as permitted to align the reference marks. Push straight in to the socket shoulder, then rotate only the amount needed to return the marks to alignment.
  5. Hold the joint together for the required period to prevent push-out. Support the joint by hand or brace until initial set has occurred.
  6. Remove excess cement without disturbing the joint. Wipe the exterior bead only; do not reinsert or rotate the pipe after the hold period has started.
  7. Repeat the procedure in an order that preserves access and alignment. Continue joint by joint until the full branch is permanently assembled.

K. Support, Cure, and Testing

  1. Install permanent supports without flattening, back-pitching, or stressing the pipe. Space supports according to the approved drawing and code requirement for the pipe size.
  2. Maintain cleanout access and protect open pipe ends from debris. Cap or cover every open termination before leaving the work area unattended.
  3. Allow the assembly to cure for the manufacturer-controlled time based on pipe size, temperature, humidity, and test conditions. Confirm the cure time from the product's published table for the actual field conditions before testing.
  4. Inspect joint insertion, alignment, slope, fitting direction, supports, primer or cement coverage, and push-out. Complete this inspection before applying the test.
  5. Complete the approved DWV leak or water test under required supervision. Follow the test method, duration, and observation points specified for the adopted system.
  6. Cut out and rebuild failed joints; do not smear solvent cement or sealant over a leaking joint. Remove the affected section back to sound pipe and rejoin using new fittings and full preparation.
  7. Document the assembly and test before concealment. Complete every field on the DWV Assembly and Test Worksheet, including photographs of joints and supports.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed branch using the same checks regardless of its specific fitting pattern or length.

  • Drawing check: confirm the completed branch matches the approved drawing's pipe size, fitting pattern, flow direction, slope, and vent relationship.
  • Joint check: confirm every joint reached full insertion depth and the alignment marks returned to their dry-fit position.
  • Slope check: confirm the specified slope is maintained across the full branch length using a level.
  • Support check: confirm supports meet the required spacing without flattening or back-pitching the pipe.
  • Cleanout check: confirm cleanout access remains available after supports and protection are installed.
  • Test check: confirm the approved leak or water test passed with no observed leakage at any joint.
  • Documentation check: confirm the DWV Assembly and Test Worksheet is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Fitting Rotated Out of Alignment After Cementing

Probable cause: the pipe was rotated past the marked alignment position after the cement began to set, or the joint was not held long enough before release.
Confirmation: compare the current fitting direction against the alignment marks and the approved drawing's flow direction.
Correction: cut out and rebuild the joint using a new fitting section; do not attempt to rotate a set solvent-welded joint back into position.
Reinspection: repeat the dry-fit alignment check and the full joint-insertion inspection for the rebuilt section.

The Branch Does Not Meet the Specified Slope

Probable cause: a support was installed at the wrong elevation, a joint was not fully inserted, or the branch reference elevation was not confirmed before assembly.
Confirmation: check the slope with a level at multiple points along the branch and compare against the approved drawing's specified slope.
Correction: adjust or reinstall supports to the correct elevation; if a joint is under-inserted, treat it as a failed joint and rebuild it.
Reinspection: repeat the slope check across the full branch length after adjustment.

The Leak or Water Test Fails at a Joint

Probable cause: incomplete primer or cement coverage, an under-inserted joint, or a joint disturbed before the cement fully cured.
Confirmation: isolate the branch, depressurize or drain as required by the test method, and inspect the failed joint directly for gaps, dry spots, or incomplete insertion.
Correction: cut out and rebuild the failed joint using new fittings and full preparation; do not smear solvent cement or sealant over a leaking joint.
Reinspection: allow the rebuilt joint to cure for the full manufacturer-controlled period, then repeat the complete leak or water test.

A Fitting Does Not Match the Approved Drawing's Pattern

Probable cause: the wrong fitting was selected during staging, or a substitution was made without confirming it against the drawing.
Confirmation: compare every installed fitting type and orientation against the approved drawing before proceeding to permanent assembly.
Correction: replace the incorrect fitting with the specified type before any primer is applied; if the discrepancy is discovered after cementing, cut out and rebuild the affected section.
Reinspection: repeat the drawing-comparison check for the corrected section before continuing.

Cement Was Applied Before the Full Branch Was Dry-Fit

Probable cause: a joint was cemented individually as it was assembled, without first confirming fit and slope across the entire branch.
Confirmation: compare the assembly sequence used against the required dry-fit-first sequence and identify which joints were cemented before the full dry-fit was completed.
Correction: inspect the affected joints for alignment and slope; rebuild any joint that does not meet the acceptance standards.
Reinspection: repeat the full alignment, slope, and fitting-direction inspection for the affected joints.

Cleanout Access Is Blocked After Supports Are Installed

Probable cause: a support, protection plate, or adjacent framing element was installed without confirming clearance at the cleanout location.
Confirmation: inspect the cleanout location directly and confirm a removable plug or accessible fitting can be reached without cutting or removing permanent supports.
Correction: relocate the obstructing support or protection element while maintaining the required support spacing elsewhere.
Reinspection: repeat the cleanout access check after the obstruction is corrected.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to collect, index, verify, and protect the complete set of official records generated by a residential permit and inspection process, and to hand that record off to the owner or contractor as one controlled compliance archive. The finished work product is one complete, indexed archive containing the issued permit and revisions, approved plans and specifications, every inspection result, closed correction notices, required test records, product approvals, warranties, and final authorization, organized for retrieval and protected against loss.

You will define the archive's format and access rules, collect permits and approved documents, collect inspection and correction records, collect special-inspection and test reports, collect product documents and warranties, collect final authorizations, cross-check every record for consistency, protect confidential information, and conduct a documented closeout review and handoff with the owner or responsible contractor.

This project depends on the records produced throughout every earlier project in this book: the authority register from Project 01, the permit decision from Project 02, the application packet from Project 03, every rough-in and envelope inspection package from Projects 06 through 11, the energy matrix from Project 12, the life-safety and stair checklists from Projects 13 and 14, the interior-concealment package from Project 15, the final-inspection package from Project 16, and the correction log from Project 17. This project does not generate new permit or inspection activity; it organizes and protects what those projects have already produced, and it hands that record forward into the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-103 — Collect the issued permit, permit card, fees, revisions, and amended permits into the compliance archive.
  • CP-104 — Collect every inspection approval, closed correction record, and special-inspection or testing-agency report.
  • CP-105 — Organize product approvals, listings, certificates, and manufacturer installation documents by system.
  • CP-106 — Retain required test records with instrument, result, and acceptance evidence.
  • CP-107 — Store warranties, registration confirmations, and maintenance requirements for owner use.
  • CP-108 — Prepare, verify, protect, and hand off the final indexed compliance archive.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior project in this book produced an official record: an authority register, a permit decision, an application packet, inspection-readiness checklists, an energy matrix, a life-safety checklist, and a correction log. Without a controlled closeout archive, those records scatter across separate folders, get superseded without a change log, or are represented informally instead of through the actual approved documents. This project converts the whole record set into one indexed archive the owner or contractor can use for maintenance, resale, future permits, insurance, or a future remodel project like the one reviewed in Project 19.

The verification discipline built in Project 01 — recording source, edition, date verified, and official status for every entry — is the same discipline this project applies to permits, inspections, tests, and approvals. The Chapter 20 capstone assembles this exact archive as its final deliverable, so the indexing and consistency checks practiced here carry forward unchanged.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the archive format, retention responsibility, access permissions, and confidentiality requirements
  • collecting the permit, approved documents, revisions, and deferred submittals
  • collecting every inspection result, closed correction record, and special-inspection or test report
  • collecting product approvals, warranties, and owner-facing maintenance information
  • collecting final authorizations such as a certificate of occupancy or completion and utility release
  • cross-checking the archive for consistency and completeness
  • protecting confidential and financial information appropriately
  • conducting a documented closeout review and handoff with the owner or responsible contractor

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • label an unresolved or expired permit, an open correction item, or a missing final approval as closed
  • generate new permits, inspections, or test results; this project only organizes and verifies records already produced
  • place general contract, payment, or lien records in the owner-facing technical file unless the owner or contractor specifically authorizes it
  • represent the archive as a legal opinion, insurance certification, or independent verification beyond what the official records themselves show

Any record that cannot be traced to an official permit, inspection, test, or product source must be labeled and separated from the controlling compliance record before the archive is delivered.

Trade Note

An archive with a missing final approval, an unclosed correction, or an unlabeled superseded plan set is not a completed closeout — it is a partial file that will fail the first time it is actually needed, whether for a future permit, an insurance claim, or a resale disclosure. Verify against the original official record every time, never against a prior project's summary of it.

Setup Standard

Do not begin filling the archive sections until the closeout index and file-naming convention are set. Deciding how records will be named, dated, and cross-checked after the archive is already partially filled is how duplicate, superseded, and unlabeled records end up mixed with the controlling record.

Shop Tip

Work from the Project 17 correction log and the Project 16 final-inspection package first. Every item marked open in either record identifies exactly what must be resolved before this archive can be closed — do not start the archive assuming those records are already clean.

Common Field Fundamentals

This project applies the same identify-locate-record-organize-verify sequence used since Project 01, now applied to the completed project's own official history rather than to external agency resources. Every record placed in the archive must trace back to an issued permit, an official inspection result, a closed correction, a qualified test report, a manufacturer or listing document, or a final authorization — never to a contractor's informal summary of what happened.

Original Records, Consistency, and Confidentiality

Every technical entry in the archive must trace to the original official record, not a paraphrase of it. Collect concealed-work photographs, product documents, and test records exactly as issued, cross-check address, permit number, plan revision, and product model for consistency across the archive, and protect personal information, license data, signatures, financial information, and access codes throughout.

Documentation Cue — Cross-Check Every Identifier

Confirm that the project address, permit number, plan revision, equipment model, and product information match across every section of the archive before delivery. A mismatched permit number or an outdated plan revision filed next to a current one is the most common cause of a rejected future permit application built from this archive.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Label an Open Item Closed

Do not mark a correction, permit condition, inspection, or final approval as closed unless the official acceptance evidence is present in the archive. Create a clearly labeled unresolved or deferred-item list for anything that remains open, and disclose it to the owner or contractor rather than omitting it from the handoff.

Field Safety Cue — Conducting the Handoff

Walk the owner or responsible contractor through where permits, approvals, manuals, warranties, shutoffs, and maintenance schedules are located in the archive. Record the date of the handoff and obtain a signed acknowledgment of delivery where required, rather than assuming the archive was reviewed.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

This archive documents official permit, inspection, and approval history. It does not substitute for a legal opinion, an insurance certification, or a design professional's independent evaluation. Where a record is missing or an authorization is unclear, label the item unresolved and identify the official source that must answer it rather than representing the project as fully closed.

E. Preparation — Define Archive Scope, Format, and Access

  1. Define the project, owner, property, archive format, retention responsibility, access permissions, and confidentiality requirements. Record this information on the Project and Archive Record above before collecting any document.
  2. Create a closeout index and standardized file-naming system. Apply the naming convention consistently before any section is populated.

F. Collect Permits, Approved Documents, and Agency Approvals

  1. Collect the issued permit, permit card, application, valuation, fees, extensions, revisions, and amended permits. File the current permit status and every revision in the Permits section.
  2. Collect approved plan sets, specifications, engineering, calculations, truss documents, energy documents, and deferred submittals. File these in the Approved Documents section.
  3. Collect the final revision log and identify the controlling approved documents. Mark every superseded sheet as archive-only in the Revision Log section.
  4. Collect zoning, planning, design-review, HOA, flood, historic, utility, fire, health, well, septic, and public-works approvals as applicable. File each approval in the Agency Approvals section matched to the requirement it satisfies.

G. Collect Inspection, Correction, Test, and Product Records

  1. Collect every inspection result by permit, trade, date, type, and status. File these in the Inspections section in the same sequence used throughout Projects 06 through 16.
  2. Collect closed correction notices and reinspection records. File these using the closed Project 17 correction log in the Corrections section.
  3. Collect special-inspection reports, testing-agency reports, engineer observations, survey certifications, and final letters. File these in the Special Inspections and Tests section.
  4. Collect concrete, soil, compaction, welding, pressure, electrical, duct, blower-door, ventilation, balancing, commissioning, and other required test records. Match each test record to its required stage and responsible qualified party.
  5. Collect product approvals, evaluation reports, listings, certificates, labels, and manufacturer installation instructions for regulated assemblies. File these in the Product Documents section, organized by building system.
  6. Collect window, door, roofing, siding, insulation, HVAC, water-heating, appliance, alarm, firestop, and other product documents. Cross-reference each product document with the assembly it documents in the Approved Documents section.

H. Collect Owner Records, Photographs, and Final Authorization

  1. Collect contractor, subcontractor, trade-license, design-professional, testing-agency, and supplier contact information appropriate for the owner file. Confirm this information is current before filing.
  2. Collect warranties, registration confirmations, maintenance requirements, proof of purchase, and installer certifications. File these in the Warranties and Maintenance section, distinguished from mandatory code requirements.
  3. Collect permit-approved substitutions, field changes, RFIs, clarifications, and design revisions. Cross-reference these with the Revision Log section.
  4. Collect concealed-work photographs indexed by room, elevation, system, and date. File these in the Concealed-Work Photographs section, cross-referenced to their inspection stage.
  5. Collect shutoff, panel, equipment, cleanout, filter, access, emergency, and service-location information. File these in the Access and Service Information section in owner-usable form.
  6. Collect finish schedules, paint information, spare materials, keys, codes, and owner-selected product data while protecting sensitive information. Store access codes and keys information separately from the general technical file.
  7. Collect certificate of occupancy, certificate of completion, final inspection, utility release, and other final authorization. File these in the Final Authorization section and confirm each is legible.
  8. Collect lien releases, payment records, contracts, and financial records only in the appropriate protected project archive, not the general homeowner technical file unless authorized. File these in the Protected Financial and Confidential Records section.

I. Verify, Protect, and Hand Off the Archive

  1. Create an owner-facing summary distinguishing mandatory maintenance from recommendations. Place this summary at the front of the Owner-Facing Summary section.
  2. Create a permit and inspection summary with final status and any ongoing conditions. Include this in the same summary section.
  3. Create an unresolved-item or deferred-item list with responsibility and legal status. Do not omit any item that remains open at the time of archiving.
  4. Do not label unresolved or expired permits as closed. Cross-check every "closed" designation against the official acceptance evidence before using it.
  5. Check that project address, permit number, plan revision, equipment model, and product information are consistent. Correct any mismatch found before delivery.
  6. Check that all correction items have official closure evidence. Return to Project 17 records for any item missing this evidence.
  7. Check that every required final approval is present and legible. Rescan or replace any illegible document.
  8. Create secure digital backups and protect physical originals from loss, moisture, fire, and unauthorized access. Confirm the backup location is functioning before delivery.
  9. Remove duplicates while preserving original signed, sealed, or official records. Do not delete an original signed or sealed document even if a duplicate scan exists.
  10. Create archive and owner-copy versions with appropriate confidential information removed. Verify the owner-copy version does not include restricted financial or access-code records unless authorized.
  11. Conduct the closeout review with the owner or responsible contractor. Use the Field Safety Cue above during this review.
  12. Explain where permits, approvals, manuals, warranties, shutoffs, and maintenance schedules are located. Walk through each archive section during the review.
  13. Obtain signed acknowledgment of file delivery where required. Record the date and method if a signature cannot be obtained.
  14. Record retention period, responsible custodian, and future update procedure. Complete this on the closeout index.
  15. Archive the final controlled compliance file and delivery record. Store the archive and delivery acknowledgment at the location recorded on the closeout index.

Common Archive Inspection

Inspect the finished archive using the same checks regardless of which section is being reviewed.

  • Sourcing check: confirm every technical entry traces to an official permit, inspection, test, product, or approval record.
  • Consistency check: confirm project address, permit number, plan revision, and product information match across every section.
  • Completeness check: confirm all fourteen archive sections from the Component Schedule are present and populated.
  • Closure check: confirm every correction, permit condition, and inspection stage carries official acceptance evidence before being labeled closed.
  • Confidentiality check: confirm financial, license, signature, and access-code records are stored separately from the general owner-facing file unless authorized.
  • Handoff check: confirm the closeout review occurred and a signed or documented acknowledgment of delivery is recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Correction Item Is Marked Closed Without Official Acceptance Evidence

Probable cause: field work was completed and photographed, but the Project 17 reinspection or written acceptance was never obtained or filed.
Confirmation: compare the correction item against the Project 17 correction log for a recorded acceptance date and inspector determination.
Correction: obtain the missing reinspection or written acceptance before relabeling the item closed; if it cannot be obtained, relabel the item unresolved and disclose it.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check on the corrected entry.

A Superseded Plan Revision Is Filed Alongside the Controlling Set

Probable cause: the revision log was not consulted before filing, or an earlier plan set was never removed from active use.
Confirmation: compare every plan sheet's revision date against the final revision log's identified controlling revision.
Correction: mark the superseded sheet as archive-only and separate it from the controlling document, or remove it if the archive format does not require retaining superseded sheets.
Reinspection: repeat the consistency check across the Approved Documents and Revision Log sections.

Permit Number, Address, or Product Model Does Not Match Across Sections

Probable cause: records were filed from different source documents without a cross-check, or a revision changed a product model without updating every reference.
Confirmation: compare the identifier in question against the issued permit and the current approved documents.
Correction: correct the mismatched entry using the controlling source, and note the correction in the closeout index.
Reinspection: repeat the consistency check on the corrected section.

Confidential or Financial Records Appear in the Owner-Facing Technical File

Probable cause: financial, license, or access-code records were filed in the general archive without separating an owner-copy version.
Confirmation: review the owner-copy version against the Protected Financial and Confidential Records section list.
Correction: move the restricted records into the protected section and rebuild the owner-copy version without them unless the owner or contractor has specifically authorized their inclusion.
Reinspection: repeat the confidentiality check on the owner-copy version.

The Archive Is Delivered Without a Documented Handoff

Probable cause: the archive was completed and stored, but no closeout review or acknowledgment of delivery was conducted with the owner or contractor.
Confirmation: check the closeout index for a recorded review date and signed or documented acknowledgment.
Correction: schedule and conduct the closeout review, walk through every archive section, and record the acknowledgment or the documented attempt to obtain one.
Reinspection: repeat the handoff check on the completed archive.

A Final Authorization Document Is Missing or Illegible

Probable cause: the certificate of occupancy, certificate of completion, or utility release was never collected from the issuing authority, or a scanned copy is unreadable.
Confirmation: check the Final Authorization section against the Project 16 final-inspection package for the expected authorization document.
Correction: obtain a legible original or certified copy directly from the issuing authority and refile it.
Reinspection: repeat the final-authorization presence check.

Cleanup

This project ends with the first controlled archive version delivered and protected, not left as a working draft.

  • Remove duplicate, superseded, or unlabeled records from active archive sections
  • Confirm confidential owner, financial, license, and access-code information is stored securely and separately where required

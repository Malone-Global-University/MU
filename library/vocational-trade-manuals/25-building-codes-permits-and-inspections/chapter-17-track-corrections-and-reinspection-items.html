Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert an official inspection result into a controlled correction log: a record that assigns each cited item to a responsible party, documents the corrective action taken, verifies the correction, and tracks reinspection through to official acceptance. The finished work product is one closed correction log for a defined residential project, preserving the original inspection notice, the assigned corrective actions, the supporting evidence, and the final acceptance record.

You will record an official inspection result exactly as issued, enter every cited correction as a separate item, classify and assign each item, prepare and document a corrective action for each one, verify the correction before requesting reinspection, attend the reinspection, and close each item only when the authority records acceptance.

This project depends directly on the inspection-readiness work in Chapters 6 through 16 and on the authority register and verification discipline built in Chapter 1. It also feeds forward: the closed correction log becomes part of the closeout compliance file in Chapter 18 and the correction-tracking method used throughout the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-097 — Record correction items exactly as cited in the official inspection notice.
  • CP-098 — Interpret inspector notes, distinguishing official written corrections from unconfirmed verbal comments.
  • CP-099 — Assign the responsible trade, professional, or party for each correction item.
  • CP-100 — Document the corrective action taken for each item, including evidence and controlling references.
  • CP-101 — Prepare a reinspection checklist confirming every item is ready, visible, and supported by evidence.
  • CP-102 — Close out the correction log only upon documented official acceptance.

Where This Skill Fits

Every inspection-readiness project in this book — foundation, framing, envelope, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, energy, life-safety, stair and guard, interior concealment, and final inspection — can produce a correction notice instead of a clean approval. This chapter is the controlled way every one of those notices gets handled: recorded without alteration, assigned to a responsible party, corrected through approved means, verified, and closed only on official acceptance.

The discipline built here — never concealing a cited condition, never closing an item without recorded acceptance, and never inventing a citation the notice did not provide — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone applies across an entire permit-and-inspection process, and the same discipline that keeps the Chapter 18 closeout file free of unresolved or falsely closed items.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • preserving one official inspection result and correction notice unchanged in the compliance file
  • entering each cited correction as a separate, classified, and assigned item
  • distinguishing official written corrections from verbal field comments requiring written confirmation
  • documenting corrective action, evidence, and controlling references for each item
  • preparing and attending a reinspection using an evidence-based readiness checklist
  • closing each item only when the authority records acceptance, and archiving the completed log

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent a code citation, tolerance, or requirement the inspection notice did not provide
  • perform, size, or approve corrective structural, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, or fire-protection work beyond the learner's qualification or the licensed trade's scope
  • conceal, patch over, cosmetically mask, disconnect, or temporarily alter cited work merely to pass reinspection
  • close a correction item based on a verbal comment, an assumption, or the passage of time without the authority's recorded acceptance

Any correction requiring design revision, a licensed trade, or an agency determination beyond the learner's role must be routed to that responsible party and recorded, not resolved informally.

Trade Note

The correction log exists to protect the chain between what the inspector actually cited and what was actually done about it. Paraphrasing a citation, guessing at its meaning, or closing an item from memory breaks that chain and is the single most common cause of a reopened correction later in this book's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin corrective work until the exact cited condition, location, and controlling reference are identified from the official notice. An assumed interpretation of an unclear citation must be confirmed with the inspector or plans examiner before work proceeds.

Shop Tip

Photograph the cited condition before touching it. A correction log with only "after" photos cannot prove what was actually wrong, and an inspector who reopens the item will ask for exactly that evidence.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every correction in this book follows the same sequence: preserve the official notice, enter and classify each item separately, assign a responsible party, correct the actual cited condition through approved means, verify the correction, and close only on documented acceptance. Learning that sequence here, on one project's correction log, means every later inspection failure in this book is handled the same controlled way.

Official Notices, Verbal Comments, and Acceptance Records

Treat every verbal comment from an inspector as field information that may require written confirmation. The official correction notice or inspection record remains the controlling result. Do not enter a correction item, close a correction item, or represent a project as corrected based on a verbal comment alone.

Documentation Cue — Preserve the Original Wording

Enter each correction using the inspector's exact cited wording, location, and reference. Do not paraphrase, summarize, or reinterpret the citation when entering it into the log. If the wording is unclear, ask the inspector or plans examiner for clarification and record the clarification separately from the original citation.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Close an Item Without Official Acceptance

Do not close a correction item based on the passage of time, a verbal comment, an assumption that the work "should" now pass, or pressure to keep the schedule moving. Do not conceal, patch over, disconnect, or cosmetically alter cited work merely to pass reinspection. Close an item only when the authority's written or recorded acceptance is in hand.

Field Safety Cue — Contacting the Inspector or Plans Examiner

When a citation cannot be understood from the notice and approved documents, ask one focused question tied to the exact item. Record the official response without altering the original correction wording. Do not argue with, mislead, conceal work from, or improperly influence an inspector.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Corrective Work

A correction item involving structural sizing, a sealed design change, a licensed-trade system, or a required special-inspection retest must be routed to the design professional, licensed trade, or testing agency identified for that category. Do not perform, approve, or informally resolve a correction that falls outside the learner's qualification.

E. Preparation — Preserve the Official Notice

  1. Obtain the official inspection result, correction notice, permit record, photographs, and field notes. Collect these directly from the issuing authority or the jobsite permit file, not from a secondhand summary.
  2. Preserve the original notice unchanged in the compliance file. Store the notice as issued, before any entry is made in the working correction log.
  3. Record permit number, inspection type, date, inspector identifier where permitted, result, and reinspection conditions. Enter this header information on the correction log before listing individual items.

F. Enter, Classify, and Clarify Each Item

  1. Enter each correction as a separate numbered item using the official wording. Do not combine two distinct citations into a single log entry.
  2. Record the cited code, plan, detail, specification, listing, manufacturer instruction, or administrative requirement when provided. Copy the citation reference exactly as issued.
  3. Do not invent a citation when the notice does not provide one. Leave the citation field blank or marked "not cited" rather than supplying an assumed code section.
  4. Identify exact location, component, trade, plan reference, and inspection stage for each item. Tie each item to a specific place in the project, not a general area.
  5. Classify each item as safety, structural, envelope, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, energy, fire, site, documentation, access, or administrative. Use one classification per item.
  6. Identify whether the item is a workmanship correction, incomplete work, plan conflict, unapproved change, missing document, failed test, or design question. Record this item type alongside the classification.
  7. Ask the inspector or plans examiner a focused clarification question when the correction cannot be understood from the notice and approved documents. Limit the question to the specific unclear item.
  8. Record official clarification without altering the original correction wording. File the clarification as a linked, separate entry rather than editing the original citation.

G. Assign, Route, and Correct Each Item

  1. Assign a responsible contractor, trade, supplier, designer, owner, testing agency, or project manager. Name one accountable party for every item before any corrective work begins.
  2. Assign target date, dependency, required permit revision, and reinspection method. Record scheduling information alongside the assignment.
  3. Identify items that require design-professional clarification or revised sealed documents. Route these items to the design professional rather than resolving them in the field.
  4. Identify items that require utility, fire, zoning, health, environmental, or other agency action. Route these items to the applicable agency and record the referral.
  5. Prepare a corrective-action plan for each item. State the intended material, method, and controlling reference before work begins.
  6. Do not conceal, patch over, temporarily disconnect, or cosmetically mask the condition. Correct the actual cited condition using the approved corrective-action plan.
  7. Obtain approved materials, product documents, calculations, or revised details before corrective work where required. Confirm the corrective material or method is authorized before installation.
  8. Photograph the condition before correction. Capture the exact cited condition prior to any corrective work.
  9. Perform correction through the qualified responsible party. Confirm the assigned party, not an unqualified substitute, performs the work.
  10. Photograph the corrective process and completed result where useful. Capture evidence that supports the reinspection request.
  11. Record material, method, drawing, detail, product, test, and responsible person. Complete the corrective-action record for each item.
  12. Update related scope, cost, schedule, change-order, procurement, and risk records. Reflect the correction's effect on the Book 24 estimate and schedule.

H. Verify, Schedule, and Attend Reinspection

  1. Complete internal verification against the official correction and controlling document. Compare the corrected condition directly against the original citation and any controlling detail.
  2. Retest systems through the required qualified party. Route any required retest to the licensed trade or testing agency responsible for it.
  3. Collect revised plans, letters, test reports, certificates, or product approvals. Gather every document the reinspection will require.
  4. Prepare the reinspection checklist with every item, location, evidence, and access requirement. List each item individually, not as a general "corrections complete" statement.
  5. Confirm the permit is active and reinspection fee or request condition is satisfied. Verify with the authority register before scheduling.
  6. Leave corrected work visible where required. Do not conceal a corrected item before the reinspection confirms acceptance.
  7. Schedule the reinspection using the official procedure. Use the request method, cutoff time, and contact confirmed in Chapter 1.
  8. Attend with correction notice, approved documents, evidence, and responsible trade. Bring the original notice and every corrective-action record to the reinspection.
  9. Record accepted, rejected, partially accepted, or newly identified items separately. Do not blend the reinspection result into the original notice; keep it as its own dated record.

I. Close and Archive the Correction Log

  1. Do not close an item until official acceptance is documented. Confirm the authority's recorded acceptance before marking any item closed.
  2. Repeat correction and reinspection controls for remaining items. Cycle unresolved or newly identified items back through Sections G and H.
  3. Issue the closed correction log with acceptance date and evidence reference. Finalize the log only when every item shows a documented acceptance date.
  4. Archive original notices, clarifications, corrective records, costs, photographs, reinspections, and final closure. Store the complete package for use in the Chapter 18 closeout compliance file.

Common Correction Log Inspection

Inspect the finished correction log using the same checks regardless of which item is being reviewed.

  • Preservation check: confirm the original inspection notice remains unaltered and accessible.
  • Accuracy check: confirm every logged item matches the official wording, with no invented citation.
  • Assignment check: confirm every item names a responsible party and target date.
  • Evidence check: confirm every corrected item has a documented corrective-action record and, where practical, before/after photos.
  • Verbal-comment check: confirm no item was closed or entered based solely on an unconfirmed verbal comment.
  • Closure check: confirm every closed item carries a documented official acceptance date and evidence reference.

Complete the Reinspection Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before scheduling any reinspection.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Correction Item Is Closed Without Documented Acceptance

Probable cause: the item was assumed corrected after the corrective work was completed, or a verbal comment was treated as final acceptance.
Confirmation: review the log for a closed item lacking an acceptance date or evidence reference, and compare it against the official reinspection record.
Correction: reopen the item, schedule or confirm the reinspection through the official procedure, and record the actual acceptance date once issued.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check on the corrected entry.

A Citation Was Paraphrased Instead of Recorded Exactly

Probable cause: the item was summarized from memory or from a verbal conversation instead of copied from the official notice.
Confirmation: compare the logged item's wording directly against the original correction notice.
Correction: replace the paraphrased entry with the exact official wording and citation reference.
Reinspection: repeat the accuracy check on the corrected entry.

A Cited Condition Was Cosmetically Masked Rather Than Corrected

Probable cause: a patch, cover, disconnection, or surface treatment was applied to hide the condition instead of correcting the underlying deficiency.
Confirmation: compare the before photo, the citation, and the after photo to confirm the actual condition was resolved rather than concealed.
Correction: remove the cosmetic treatment, perform the actual corrective action through the qualified responsible party, and re-document with new before/after evidence.
Reinspection: repeat the evidence check and reschedule reinspection.

An Item Has No Assigned Responsible Party

Probable cause: the item was entered from the notice but never routed to a contractor, trade, designer, owner, or agency.
Confirmation: review the log for items missing an assignment field.
Correction: identify and record the correct responsible party and target date before corrective work proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the assignment check on the corrected entry.

A Reinspection Was Requested Before All Items Were Ready

Probable cause: the reinspection checklist was skipped, or an item was assumed complete without verifying visibility, access, or evidence.
Confirmation: compare the reinspection checklist against the actual field condition for each item.
Correction: complete or correct the remaining items, update the checklist, and reschedule only when every item is verified ready.
Reinspection: repeat the reinspection-readiness check before the next scheduling attempt.

A Newly Identified Item From Reinspection Was Not Separately Recorded

Probable cause: a condition noted during reinspection was informally addressed instead of being logged as its own item.
Confirmation: review the reinspection result record for any note not reflected as a distinct log entry.
Correction: enter the newly identified condition as a new numbered item and route it through Sections F through H.
Reinspection: repeat the accuracy and assignment checks on the new entry.

Cleanup

This project ends with the correction log closed and archived, not left as an open working file.

  • Confirm every item shows a final status of accepted, with no item left marked pending without an active reinspection date
  • Store before/after photo evidence indexed by item number alongside the closed log
  • Record final reinspection fees, revision costs, and schedule effects in the project cost and schedule records
  • Remove duplicate or superseded correction-log drafts, retaining only the final closed version
  • Store the closed correction log where it can be located for the Chapter 18 closeout compliance file

Field Terms

Correction Notice
The official document or record issued by the inspector or authority citing specific deficient conditions.
Correction Item
One separately numbered log entry corresponding to a single cited condition.
Verbal Comment
Field information given by the inspector that is not the official written result and may require confirmation.
Responsible Party
The contractor, trade, supplier, designer, owner, testing agency, or project manager assigned to correct an item.
Corrective-Action Record
The documented material, method, drawing, detail, product, test, and responsible person used to correct an item.
Reinspection Checklist
The document confirming every item is corrected, visible, and evidenced before reinspection is requested.
Official Acceptance
The authority's recorded approval that a corrected item satisfies the cited condition.
Partial Acceptance
A reinspection result in which some but not all items are accepted, requiring continued tracking of the remainder.
Clarification Log
The record of a focused question and official response used when a citation cannot be understood from the notice and approved documents.
Closed Correction Log
The final archived record showing every item's status, acceptance date, and evidence reference.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to prepare a residential wood-framed structure for the required framing inspection. The finished work product is one complete framing inspection-readiness package: verified dimensions and layout, verified openings and headers, verified structural connectors, verified bracing and fire blocking, a documented deficiency list with corrections closed, and a scheduled inspection attended with the approved documents and responsible trades.

You will confirm that all required foundation and prior structural approvals are in hand, verify the approved load path from foundation through roof framing, verify every rough opening and header, verify every structural connector and fastening schedule, verify bracing, shear, and fire-blocking conditions, coordinate with rough plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, and fire trades whose work must also be approved before framing sign-off, photograph conditions that will be concealed, and attend the inspection with a completed readiness checklist.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 reference binder's inspection-request procedures, the Chapter 5 zoning and site checklist's approved building envelope, and the Chapter 6 foundation inspection's approved anchor and hold-down locations. It feeds directly into Chapter 8's roof and envelope inspection, which cannot begin until the framing that supports it is approved.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-037 — Verify framing layout, dimensions, and story heights against approved plans.
  • CP-038 — Inspect headers, rough openings, and their supporting framing for conformance with approved documents.
  • CP-039 — Verify structural connectors, straps, hangers, and fasteners against approved details.
  • CP-040 — Verify bracing, shear panels, and fire blocking required to remain visible for inspection.
  • CP-041 — Identify incomplete, deficient, or unauthorized framing items before scheduling inspection.
  • CP-042 — Complete a framing inspection checklist and obtain a documented inspection result.

Where This Skill Fits

Framing is the last stage at which the building's structural load path is fully visible before it is concealed by insulation, drywall, siding, and roofing. Every connector, brace, header, and fire-blocking detail verified in this project either passes inspection now or becomes a far more expensive correction later, once finishes are installed. The verification method used here — compare the field condition to the approved document, photograph what will be concealed, and hold concealment until the official approval is documented — is the same method Chapters 8 through 15 apply to the envelope, rough trades, energy assemblies, and interior concealment.

This project also depends on trade coordination: rough plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, and fire work that must be approved before or alongside framing sign-off is confirmed here rather than assumed complete.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the framing inspection scope and any separate shear, rough, roof, or fire inspections that must occur first
  • verifying approved architectural, structural, truss, and product documents are onsite and current
  • verifying dimensions, floor elevations, wall locations, openings, and story heights
  • verifying sill plates, floor and wall framing, headers, beams, posts, and point-load paths
  • verifying roof framing, sheathing, connectors, bracing, and fire blocking
  • coordinating required rough-trade inspections before requesting framing approval
  • photographing concealed conditions, correcting deficiencies, and scheduling and attending the inspection

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, drill, notch, or field-modify trusses or engineered members without written authorization from the truss designer or engineer of record
  • size, redesign, or approve structural members, connectors, or bracing without the authorized design basis
  • certify egress, fire-resistance, or structural values independently of the approved documents and adopted requirements
  • conceal any framing, connector, bracing, or fire-blocking condition before the required inspection is documented as approved

Any framing condition that cannot be reconciled with the approved documents must be logged as unresolved and referred to the design professional or building official rather than field-corrected by assumption.

Trade Note

Framing inspection failures most often trace back to a connector installed with the wrong fastener, a truss field-cut without authorization, or bracing removed for material access and never restored. Verifying against the approved document — not against what "looks right" — is what separates a controlled framing inspection from a guess.

Setup Standard

Do not begin framing verification until the current approved plan revision is confirmed against the document register. Verifying framing against a superseded sheet produces a readiness package that does not match what the inspector will check against the approved record.

Shop Tip

Walk the load path in the same order the inspector will: foundation connection, sill, floor framing, wall framing, openings, roof framing, sheathing, then bracing and fire blocking. Verifying out of sequence is how a missing hold-down or an unauthorized truss cut gets discovered only after the inspector has already found it.

Common Field Fundamentals

Framing inspection readiness follows the same sequence used throughout this book: confirm the approved document, verify the field condition against it, photograph what will be concealed, correct any deficiency through the qualified responsible party, and hold concealment until the official result is documented. This chapter applies that sequence to the structural load path; Chapters 8 through 15 apply it to the envelope, rough trades, energy assemblies, and interior finishes.

Approved Documents, Field Verification, and Concealment Control

Every dimension, connector, brace, and fire-blocking detail verified in this project must be checked against the approved document identified at project start, not against memory, a superseded sheet, or a contractor markup. Photograph every condition that will be concealed by insulation, drywall, or finishes before that concealment occurs.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Field-Modify Engineered Members

Do not cut, drill, notch, or field-modify trusses or engineered lumber members without written authorization from the truss designer or engineer of record. An unauthorized field modification to an engineered member is a stop-work condition until the design professional issues a written correction or acceptance.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Structural Determinations

Sizing, substituting, or approving beams, headers, connectors, or bracing beyond what the approved documents show is reserved to the engineer of record, truss designer, or building official. Where a field condition does not match the approved document, log it as unresolved and obtain a written revision or acceptance rather than selecting a substitute in the field.

Documentation Cue — Photograph Before Concealment

Photograph every concealed structural connector, brace, and fire-blocking detail, identified by room, elevation, or framing zone, before insulation or drywall is installed. An unphotographed concealed condition cannot be reverified once covered.

E. Preparation — Confirm Scope, Documents, and Prior Approvals

  1. Confirm the framing inspection scope and whether separate shear, rough, roof, fire, or trade inspections must occur first. Record the exact inspection type or types required from the jurisdiction's published procedure in the Chapter 1 binder.
  2. Confirm current approved architectural, structural, truss, engineering, and product documents are onsite. Compare the sheet dates and revision numbers against the document register before proceeding.
  3. Confirm required foundation, slab, anchor, and prior structural inspections are approved. Do not proceed to framing verification on a foundation stage that remains conditional or unapproved.

F. Verify Layout, Sill, and Floor Framing

  1. Verify building dimensions, floor elevations, wall locations, openings, and story heights against approved plans. Measure at a minimum of three reference points per level and record the field value against the plan value.
  2. Verify sill plates, anchors, washers, hold-downs, post bases, moisture separation, and treated-material requirements. Confirm each anchor bolt and hold-down location and embedment matches the foundation inspection record from Chapter 6.
  3. Verify floor beams, girders, posts, joists, trusses, rim boards, blocking, bearing, hangers, and openings. Confirm every hanger is fastened with the specified nail or screw type in every manufacturer-specified hole.

G. Verify Wall Framing and Openings

  1. Verify walls are plumb, aligned, braced, and located correctly. Check plumb with a level at both ends of each wall line and record any deviation exceeding the approved tolerance.
  2. Verify studs, plates, corners, intersections, backing, tall-wall framing, rake walls, and specialty framing. Confirm stud spacing and plate lamination match the approved wall sections.
  3. Verify door and window rough openings, headers, kings, trimmers, cripples, sills, and structural support. Confirm each opening dimension against the door/window schedule before any product is installed.
  4. Verify beams, columns, posts, point-load paths, squash blocks, bearing blocks, and foundation support align as designed. Trace each point load from roof to foundation and confirm continuous bearing at every transition.

H. Verify Roof Framing, Sheathing, and Connectors

  1. Verify roof rafters, trusses, ridge elements, hips, valleys, ties, connectors, bracing, and overhang framing. Confirm truss placement and spacing match the approved truss layout and truss certificates.
  2. Do not cut, drill, notch, or field-modify trusses or engineered members without written authorization. Refer any observed unauthorized modification to the truss designer or engineer of record before continuing.
  3. Verify sheathing type, thickness, orientation, edge support, fastening, blocked zones, shear panels, diaphragms, and nailing patterns. Confirm nail spacing at panel edges and field against the approved nailing schedule.
  4. Verify structural connectors, straps, clips, hangers, bolts, screws, nails, tension devices, and corrosion compatibility. Confirm every connector matches the connector schedule for type, size, and fastener count.
  5. Confirm required fastener type and installation in every specified connector hole. Do not accept a connector with unfilled specified holes as complete.
  6. Verify braced wall, shear wall, portal, collector, drag, uplift, and hold-down details from approved documents. Confirm each braced-wall panel location and length matches the braced-wall plan.

I. Verify Fire Blocking, Trade Coordination, and Access

  1. Verify fire blocking, draft stopping, concealed-space limits, stair blocking, and rated-assembly framing remain visible. Confirm every fire-blocking location shown on approved documents is installed and not yet concealed.
  2. Verify stair openings, landings, guards, handrail backing, and support framing where required at this stage. Confirm backing blocks are located at the heights shown on the stair details.
  3. Verify backing and blocking for cabinets, fixtures, rails, grab bars, equipment, finishes, and MEP supports where shown. Confirm each backing location against the approved interior elevations.
  4. Verify plumbing, electrical, HVAC, gas, fire, and other rough work has not damaged structural members or violated approved boring and notching details. Inspect every bored or notched member against the approved boring and notching limits.
  5. Verify penetrations are protected, sleeved, supported, and firestopped only at the required inspection stage. Do not install firestop material before the framing inspector has verified the penetration location.
  6. Confirm rough plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, and fire inspections required before framing approval are complete. Record each trade inspection's status, date, and result on the framing deficiency checklist.
  7. Verify roof, wall, and floor assemblies are dry enough for inspection and not concealed by insulation or finishes. Confirm no cavity has been insulated or covered before the framing inspection is scheduled.

J. Document, Correct, and Prepare for Inspection

  1. Collect truss certificates, engineered-lumber documents, connector data, welding reports, special-inspection reports, and approved revisions. Assemble these documents at the jobsite permit station before scheduling.
  2. Photograph concealed structural and fire-blocking conditions. Index each photograph by room, elevation, or framing zone and date.
  3. Provide safe stairs, ladders, platforms, lighting, fall protection, and access to every inspection area. Confirm access to roof framing and upper-level connections before the inspector arrives.
  4. Remove scrap, stored material, temporary hazards, and obstructions. Clear walking surfaces and inspection sightlines throughout the structure.
  5. Complete a room-by-room and load-path preinspection. Walk the entire load path using the Verification Zone Schedule before scheduling the official inspection.
  6. Create and correct a framing deficiency list. Assign each deficiency to the responsible trade with a target correction date.
  7. Confirm no unauthorized field changes remain unresolved. Close every unauthorized-change item through the required revision or design-professional acceptance before scheduling.
  8. Schedule the correct inspection type and record confirmation. Use the inspection-request procedure from the Chapter 1 binder and record the confirmation number and scheduled date.

K. Attend Inspection and Record Result

  1. Attend the inspection with approved documents and responsible trade representatives. Have the current approved plan set, truss certificates, and connector data available at the inspection.
  2. Record approval, partial approval, correction items, deferred submittals, and areas that must remain open. Preserve the inspector's official result exactly as issued.
  3. Do not insulate or conceal framing until all required approvals are documented. Hold all concealment work until the official approval is recorded.
  4. File the checklist, photographs, reports, inspector record, and correction status. Archive the completed package in the compliance record established in the Chapter 1 binder.

Common Framing Inspection

Inspect the finished framing readiness package using the same checks regardless of which zone is being reviewed.

  • Document-currency check: confirm the plan revision used for verification matches the current approved revision in the document register.
  • Dimension check: confirm measured dimensions, elevations, and story heights match the approved plans within the stated tolerance.
  • Connector check: confirm every connector shown on approved documents is installed with the specified fastener in every specified hole.
  • Bracing and fire-blocking check: confirm every required braced-wall panel and fire-blocking location remains visible and matches approved details.
  • Trade-coordination check: confirm every prerequisite rough-trade inspection required before framing sign-off is complete or properly sequenced.
  • Concealment check: confirm no required item was concealed before the official inspection result was documented.

Complete the Framing Inspection Checklist below before requesting reinspection on any corrected item.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Connector Has Unfilled Specified Fastener Holes

Probable cause: the installer used fewer fasteners than the connector's specified hole pattern requires, or substituted an incompatible fastener.
Confirmation: compare the installed connector against the manufacturer data sheet and connector schedule for hole count and fastener type.
Correction: install the specified fastener in every remaining hole using the approved fastener type.
Reinspection: repeat the connector check on the corrected connection.

A Truss or Engineered Member Was Field-Cut Without Authorization

Probable cause: a trade cut, drilled, or notched a truss or engineered member to route a penetration without obtaining written authorization.
Confirmation: compare the modified member against the truss layout and manufacturer boring/notching limits, and identify the responsible trade.
Correction: obtain a written repair detail or replacement instruction from the truss designer or engineer of record and complete the repair exactly as specified.
Reinspection: repeat the roof-framing and connector checks on the repaired member.

A Braced-Wall Panel Was Interrupted for Material Access

Probable cause: sheathing was removed or a panel edge was cut to route material or equipment through the wall and never restored.
Confirmation: compare the panel against the braced-wall plan length and edge-nailing requirement.
Correction: restore the panel to the approved length and edge-nailing pattern, or obtain an approved revision if the interruption cannot be restored as designed.
Reinspection: repeat the bracing and fire-blocking visibility check on the corrected panel.

A Rough Opening Does Not Match the Door/Window Schedule

Probable cause: the opening was framed from a field measurement of the product rather than the approved schedule, or the wrong header size was installed.
Confirmation: measure the rough opening and compare it to the door/window schedule and header size shown on the structural details.
Correction: reframe the opening and header to match the approved schedule, or obtain an approved revision if the opening size must change.
Reinspection: repeat the opening and header check on the corrected framing.

A Prerequisite Rough-Trade Inspection Was Never Confirmed Complete

Probable cause: the framing inspection was scheduled before confirming that a required rough plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, or fire inspection had passed.
Confirmation: review the trade coordination record against the jurisdiction's required inspection sequence.
Correction: schedule and complete the missing prerequisite inspection before requesting or continuing the framing inspection.
Reinspection: repeat the trade-coordination check before rescheduling the framing inspection.

A Concealed Condition Was Covered Before the Official Result Was Documented

Probable cause: insulation or drywall was installed before the framing inspection was completed or the correction was reverified.
Confirmation: compare the concealment date against the recorded inspection date and result.
Correction: notify the building official of the premature concealment and follow the jurisdiction's required procedure, which may include exposing the concealed work for inspection.
Reinspection: repeat the concealment-control check once the exposed condition is verified and documented.

Cleanup

This project ends with a documented, approved framing inspection result, not an unresolved punch list.

  • Remove scrap, packaging, and temporary bracing no longer required after approval
  • Confirm all photographs are indexed by zone, location, and date in the compliance record
  • Close every deficiency item with the responsible trade's corrective action recorded
  • Confirm the approved inspection result is filed alongside the permit and prior inspection records
  • Store the framing readiness package where it can be located for Chapter 8's roof and envelope inspection

Field Terms

Load Path
The continuous structural route by which loads travel from the roof through walls, floors, and foundation, as shown on approved documents.
Rough Opening
The framed opening dimension for a door, window, or other assembly, measured as defined by the approved plans and product documentation.
Structural Connector
Any strap, clip, hanger, hold-down, or tension device shown on approved documents, installed with the specified fastener in every specified hole.
Braced-Wall Panel
A designated wall segment providing lateral resistance, with length, sheathing, and fastening defined by the approved braced-wall plan.
Fire Blocking
Material or assembly installed to interrupt concealed draft openings between stories and at other required locations.
Concealment
Any installation of insulation, drywall, siding, or finish that would hide a framing, connector, or fire-blocking condition from view.
Truss Certificate
The manufacturer's document confirming a truss's design loads, span, and configuration match the approved truss layout.
Point Load
A concentrated structural load transferred through a beam, post, or column that must bear continuously to the foundation.
Deficiency List
The documented record of every incomplete or nonconforming framing item, its responsible trade, and its corrective status.
Trade Coordination
Confirming that rough plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, and fire inspections required before framing sign-off are complete or properly sequenced.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to review a small existing residential remodel and produce one documented compliance summary that a project team can actually use before construction begins. The finished work product is one compliance summary and supporting matrix that classifies the remodel scope, identifies every permit trigger, flags structural and MEP changes, identifies energy and life-safety effects, sequences the required inspections, lists the documents still needed, and records every condition that remains unresolved and who must answer it.

You will define the existing property and proposed scope, classify each scope item using local definitions, complete the Chapter 2 permit decision for every affected system, identify structural and MEP changes without personally sizing or approving them, identify energy-code and life-safety triggers, identify existing nonconforming conditions and the jurisdiction's rule for them, build the compliance matrix and inspection schedule, convert every gap into a formal question, and review the finished summary with the project team before it is archived.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 authority-verified binder and the Chapter 2 permit-decision method. It also uses the Chapter 4 code-note habits, the Chapter 13 life-safety checklist logic, and the Chapter 17 correction-tracking discipline. It is the last project before the Chapter 20 capstone, which manages one complete project from authority research through closeout using the same review method applied here to a real, in-progress remodel.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-109 — Review remodel scope and classify each item using the jurisdiction's definitions.
  • CP-110 — Identify permit needs for every affected system and specialty scope.
  • CP-111 — Flag structural changes requiring design-professional or engineering review.
  • CP-112 — Flag MEP changes requiring licensed-trade scope and permits.
  • CP-113 — Identify life-safety issues created or affected by the remodel.
  • CP-114 — Prepare a remodel compliance summary that another qualified participant can verify and use.

Where This Skill Fits

Every earlier project in this book worked from a defined new-construction or addition scope. A remodel is different: it starts from an existing building whose original permit history, construction methods, and current condition are often incomplete or unknown. This project teaches you to apply the same authority-scope-permit-document-inspect-correct-approve-archive discipline from Chapters 1 through 18 to that harder starting condition, without ever assuming a wall is nonbearing, an alarm system is adequate, or an existing condition may remain simply because it has been there for years.

The compliance summary built here becomes the scope-classification input the Chapter 20 capstone project manager uses when the capstone project itself is a defined residential remodel rather than new construction, and the RFI and unresolved-item habits built here carry forward into the capstone's plan-review and correction logs.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the existing property, room, occupancy, age, and proposed remodel scope
  • classifying each scope item as repair, alteration, demolition, addition, change of use, or equipment replacement using local definitions
  • completing the Chapter 2 permit decision for building, zoning, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, fire, energy, and specialty permits
  • identifying structural, MEP, exterior-opening, energy-code, and life-safety changes and effects
  • identifying existing nonconforming conditions and the jurisdiction's rule for whether they must be corrected, protected, or may remain
  • building a compliance matrix, inspection schedule, and RFI log tied to the construction sequence
  • reviewing the finished summary with the owner, contractor, designer, and responsible trades before archiving it

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • declare a wall nonbearing, size a structural repair, or approve an MEP design without the required verification or design-professional input
  • determine that an existing nonconforming condition may remain without confirming the jurisdiction's project-specific rule
  • treat this summary as a permit approval, code-compliance certification, or authorization to begin regulated work
  • assess hazardous materials such as lead, asbestos, or mold; that assessment requires a qualified party identified in Step 6

Any condition that cannot be classified from the existing records, a site walk, and the Chapter 1 binder must be converted into a formal question and recorded as unresolved rather than assumed.

Trade Note

The single most common failure in remodel compliance review is accepting an owner's or contractor's statement about an existing condition — "that wall isn't load-bearing," "the panel has room," "the smoke alarms are fine" — without the verification this chapter's troubleshooting section requires. Treat every existing-condition statement as unverified until confirmed by inspection, documentation, or a qualified professional.

Setup Standard

Do not begin classifying scope items until the existing property, room, occupancy, building age, and prior permit history are recorded and the current Chapter 1 binder entries for existing-building and remodel rules have been rechecked. A remodel review built on an unverified assumption about the existing structure compounds into every later inspection stage.

Shop Tip

Build the RFI log before you start marking up the proposed plans. Recording each uncertain existing condition as you find it, rather than trying to remember it later, is what keeps the compliance matrix from being finished with silent assumptions in place of verified answers.

Prerequisites

  • A completed and current Chapter 1 jurisdiction reference binder for the property's authority having jurisdiction
  • Working familiarity with the Chapter 2 permit-decision method and blank permit matrix

Common Field Fundamentals

A remodel review differs from the new-construction reviews in earlier chapters in one key way: the starting condition is unverified until proven otherwise. Every wall, panel, vent, and alarm in the existing structure was built under a code edition that may no longer be adopted, may never have been permitted at all, or may have been altered since its last approved inspection. This project applies the same identify-classify-verify-document method used throughout the book, but every step must first ask, "What do we actually know about this existing condition, and what is only assumed?"

Existing-Condition Verification and Professional Boundaries

Do not classify a scope item as exempt, nonbearing, or already compliant based on appearance or an owner's statement alone. Verify structural, electrical, and life-safety conditions through inspection, existing documentation, or the qualified professional identified for that scope item, and record the verification method with every classification.

Documentation Cue — Record How Every Classification Was Verified

For each scope item in the compliance matrix, record not only the classification but the exact method used to reach it: existing plan reference, official record, direct measurement, design-professional input, or unresolved pending verification. A classification without a recorded verification method cannot be treated as final.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Accept an Unverified Structural Statement

Do not accept an owner's or contractor's statement that a wall is nonbearing, a foundation is adequate, or a structural member may be cut, notched, or removed. Route every such condition to the design professional or engineer identified in Step 10 before it is classified as anything other than unresolved.

Field Safety Cue — Occupied-Home and Hazardous-Material Conditions

When the site walk occurs in an occupied home, confirm access, pets, and utility conditions with the owner before entering, and photograph only the areas relevant to the remodel scope. Do not disturb suspected lead paint, asbestos-containing material, mold, or sewage conditions; stop and route them to the qualified assessment identified in Step 6.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Whether a nonconforming condition must be upgraded, protected, or may remain is a jurisdiction-specific determination made by the building official, not by this compliance summary or its preparer. Structural verification is made by the design professional or engineer. Record the unresolved condition and the responsible party rather than selecting the least costly interpretation.

E. Preparation — Define Scope and Confirm the Authority

  1. Define the existing property, room, occupancy, building age, prior permit history, and proposed remodel scope. Record this on the Property and Remodel Scope Record above before any classification begins.
  2. Obtain existing and proposed plans, photographs, owner objectives, product selections, and available building records. Note explicitly where no existing record is available rather than leaving the gap unstated.
  3. Identify the authority, adopted residential or existing-building requirements, local amendments, and permit rules. Recheck the Chapter 1 binder entries for existing-building and remodel-specific rules rather than relying on the new-construction entries alone.
  4. Classify each scope item as repair, alteration, demolition, addition, change of use, or equipment replacement using local definitions. Record the classification and the exact definition source for each item in the Scope Classification Table.
  5. Prepare the Chapter 2 permit decision for building, zoning, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, fire, energy, and specialty permits. Complete one permit-decision row for every affected system identified in the scope.

F. Identify Hazards, Structural Changes, and Design-Professional Needs

  1. Identify hazardous-material, lead, asbestos, mold, sewage, or environmental assessment needs before disturbance. Route any suspected condition to a qualified assessment party and record the referral rather than proceeding with the site walk in that area.
  2. Identify whether the remodel changes room use, occupancy, bedroom count, dwelling units, accessibility, egress, or fire separation. Record each affected condition in the Energy and Life-Safety Log.
  3. Identify structural changes to walls, openings, beams, posts, joists, rafters, trusses, stairs, foundations, or exterior walls. Record every such item in the Structural Flag Log with its exact location.
  4. Do not accept an owner or contractor statement that a wall is nonbearing without the required verification. Route the condition to the design professional or engineer identified in the next step before assigning a final classification.
  5. Identify design-professional, engineering, survey, or special-inspection requirements. Record the responsible party, contact method, and target response date for every flagged structural item.

G. Identify MEP, Exterior, and Energy-Code Changes

  1. Identify plumbing fixture, drain, vent, water, gas, water-heater, or appliance changes. Record each in the MEP Change Log with the affected system and rough-in location.
  2. Identify electrical circuit, service, panel, device, lighting, alarm, appliance, or equipment changes. Record each with the affected panel, circuit, or device location.
  3. Identify HVAC equipment, duct, exhaust, ventilation, refrigerant, gas, combustion, or condensate changes. Record each with the affected equipment or system.
  4. Identify exterior opening, window, door, siding, roof, flashing, insulation, and air-sealing changes. Record each with the affected elevation or assembly.
  5. Identify energy-code triggers for altered assemblies, fenestration, lighting, equipment, ducts, controls, or testing. Record each using the Chapter 12 energy-matrix format.

H. Identify Life-Safety Effects and Existing Nonconforming Conditions

  1. Identify smoke, carbon-monoxide, emergency escape, egress, stair, guard, handrail, glazing, and fire-separation effects. Record each using the Chapter 13 life-safety checklist format.
  2. Identify garage, basement, attic, crawlspace, wet-area, and utility-room conditions affected by the remodel. Record each condition and its relationship to the proposed scope.
  3. Identify existing nonconforming conditions that the jurisdiction may require to remain, be protected, or be upgraded. Record each condition in the Nonconforming-Condition Log without assigning an outcome.
  4. Do not assume the remodel requires full-building upgrade or no upgrade; verify the project-specific rule. Route the question to the building official and record the response before finalizing the log.
  5. Identify plumbing, electrical, HVAC, framing, insulation, drywall, and final inspection stages. Record each stage in the Inspection and Permit Schedule tied to the affected system.

I. Coordinate Documents, Resolve RFIs, and Assemble the Summary

  1. Identify temporary protection, utility shutdown, occupied-home, sanitation, egress, and fire-safety requirements during construction. Record these in the compliance summary as conditions the contractor must maintain throughout the work.
  2. Identify concealed conditions that may require stop work, design revision, or change order. Record each such condition and its trigger for stopping work in the Structural Flag Log or MEP Change Log as applicable.
  3. Compare proposed drawings across architectural, structural, MEP, energy, and finish scopes. Note any conflict between scopes directly in the compliance matrix rather than resolving it independently.
  4. Convert conflicts, missing dimensions, unclear existing conditions, and scope gaps into RFIs or official questions. Enter every such item in the RFI Log with a specific, focused question.
  5. Create a compliance matrix with requirement, authority, plan reference, trade, inspection stage, evidence, and status. Compile the matrix from the logs completed in Sections E through H.
  6. Create a permit and inspection schedule tied to the construction sequence. Sequence every required inspection against the contractor's planned order of work.
  7. Create a list of work that cannot begin or be concealed before approval. Identify every item from the matrix that represents a concealment hold point.
  8. Identify required application documents, calculations, product data, and owner or contractor information. Record what is still missing and who is responsible for obtaining it.
  9. Review compliance scope against the Book 24 estimate and identify missing permit, testing, licensed-trade, or correction allowances. Note any gap between the compliance findings and the existing budget or schedule assumptions.
  10. Prepare the remodel compliance summary with permits, risks, professionals, inspections, documents, and closeout requirements. Write the summary so that another qualified project participant can use it without access to this learner's notes.
  11. Review the summary with the owner, contractor, designer, and responsible trades. Record attendance, date, and any changes made as a result of the review.
  12. Archive the summary, sources, decisions, questions, and approved responses. Store the archived version where it remains available for construction and for the Chapter 20 capstone if this project is later selected as the capstone scope.

Common Compliance-Summary Inspection

Review the finished compliance summary using the same checks regardless of which log or matrix section is being reviewed.

  • Classification check: confirm every scope item has a recorded classification, definition source, and verification method.
  • Permit-trigger check: confirm every affected system has a completed Chapter 2 permit decision.
  • Structural and MEP routing check: confirm every flagged structural or MEP item is routed to the correct qualified party, not self-verified.
  • Life-safety and nonconforming-condition check: confirm every effect and existing condition is identified, with the jurisdiction's rule recorded rather than assumed.
  • RFI-resolution check: confirm every unresolved item identified during the review is logged, routed, and at least one structural and one existing-building item is answered.
  • Review and archive check: confirm the summary was reviewed with the full project team and archived with sources, decisions, and dates.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Wall Was Classified Without Structural Verification

Probable cause: the owner or contractor described a wall as nonbearing and the classification was recorded without design-professional or engineering confirmation.
Confirmation: review the Structural Flag Log for any wall, opening, or member classification lacking a recorded design-professional or engineering response.
Correction: reclassify the item as unresolved, route it to a design professional or engineer, and record the response before finalizing.
Reinspection: repeat the structural and MEP routing check on the corrected item.

A Nonconforming Condition Was Assumed to Remain or Assumed to Require Upgrade

Probable cause: the compliance summary applied a general rule of thumb instead of confirming the jurisdiction's project-specific existing-building rule.
Confirmation: review the Nonconforming-Condition Log for any entry without a recorded building-official response.
Correction: contact the building official, obtain the project-specific rule, and record the response and date.
Reinspection: repeat the life-safety and nonconforming-condition check on the corrected entry.

A Permit Trigger Was Missed for an Affected System

Probable cause: the scope review focused on the visible finish work and did not trace every plumbing, electrical, mechanical, or gas change back through the Chapter 2 method.
Confirmation: compare the MEP Change Log against the Permit Decision Matrix and identify any affected system without a completed permit decision.
Correction: complete the missing Chapter 2 permit decision for the affected system.
Reinspection: repeat the permit-trigger check on the corrected matrix.

An RFI Was Logged but Never Actually Answered

Probable cause: a structural, existing-building, or MEP question was recorded in the RFI Log but the contact was never completed, or a verbal answer was recorded without a name, date, or agency.
Confirmation: review the RFI Log for entries missing a recorded response, contact, or date.
Correction: complete the contact through the appropriate official or professional channel and record the full response.
Reinspection: repeat the RFI-resolution check on the completed log.

The Compliance Summary Was Archived Without Project-Team Review

Probable cause: the summary was compiled and filed without confirming that the owner, contractor, designer, and responsible trades actually reviewed it.
Confirmation: check the summary record for attendance, date, and any changes made as a result of the review.
Correction: schedule and complete the review meeting, record attendance and changes, and re-archive the summary.
Reinspection: repeat the review and archive check.

A Hazardous-Material Condition Was Disturbed During the Site Walk

Probable cause: a suspected lead, asbestos, mold, or sewage condition was inspected, photographed at close contact, or otherwise disturbed instead of being referred to a qualified assessment.
Confirmation: review site-walk notes and photographs for any direct contact with a suspected hazardous material.
Correction: stop work in the affected area, notify the owner and responsible parties, and arrange the qualified assessment before further review of that area.
Reinspection: confirm the qualified assessment is scheduled or complete before resuming the site walk.

Cleanup

This project ends with the remodel compliance summary reviewed, corrected, and archived, not left as a working draft used informally on site.

  • Remove duplicate, superseded, or unlabeled entries from the compliance matrix

  • Close and label RFI-log entries that were answered during this project

Field Terms

Existing-Building Requirement
The jurisdiction's rule governing repair, alteration, addition, or change of use to an existing structure.
Nonconforming Condition
An existing condition that would not meet current adopted requirements if built new, whose treatment is set by the jurisdiction.
Scope Classification
The jurisdiction's category assigned to each remodel scope item using local definitions.
Permit Trigger
A scope item or system change that requires a specific permit type.
Structural Flag
A scope item affecting a structural member or assembly requiring design-professional or engineering review before classification.
MEP Change Log
The record of plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and gas changes routed to the correct licensed-trade scope.
RFI
A formal, specific question routed to the responsible authority, professional, or trade when a condition cannot be confirmed from available records.
Compliance Matrix
The single reference linking requirement, authority, plan reference, trade, inspection stage, evidence, and status.
Concealment Hold Point
Work identified in the compliance summary that cannot begin or be concealed before required approval.
Qualified Assessment
A hazardous-material or other specialized evaluation performed by a party qualified for that specific assessment, not by the compliance-review learner.

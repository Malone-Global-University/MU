Job Overview

This project qualifies you to prepare and apply one field checklist covering every stair, ramp, landing, deck, balcony, porch, platform, guard, and handrail included in a defined residential project. The finished work product is one applied checklist that measures actual finished conditions against approved dimensions and adopted requirements, records every variance, and documents structural connections, openings, and corrections through to approval.

You will identify every stair and guard element in the project, confirm the approved controlling elevations and structural details, measure every finished riser and tread using a consistent method, verify handrail and guard requirements, verify stringer and deck structural connections, verify baluster and infill compliance, and complete a final operation and safety review before archiving the checklist with corrections, reinspection items, and approvals.

This project builds directly on the authority register from Project 01, the approved plans and code-note summary from Project 04, the framing inspection package from Project 07, and the energy and fire, smoke, and egress checklists from Projects 12 and 13. It feeds directly into the final building inspection readiness package in Project 16 and the Chapter 20 capstone. Nothing in this checklist replaces the building official, inspector, or design professional as the final authority on stair, guard, handrail, or deck compliance.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-079 — Measure stair rise and run against approved dimensions using a consistent field method.
  • CP-080 — Verify handrail height, continuity, graspability, and required extensions against adopted requirements.
  • CP-081 — Inspect guardrail locations at stairs, landings, decks, balconies, and porches.
  • CP-082 — Check baluster, infill, and guard-opening spacing using the required test method.
  • CP-083 — Review deck ledger, footing, post, beam, and connection details for approved structural attachment.
  • CP-084 — Prepare a stair and guardrail checklist that records measurements, structural connections, deficiencies, and approvals.

Where This Skill Fits

Stairs, guards, handrails, and decks are among the most frequently cited life-safety items at final inspection, because small variances in riser height, guard height, or opening spacing are easy to miss without a consistent field method. This checklist gives you a repeatable way to measure, record, and correct these conditions before the inspector arrives, rather than discovering a variance for the first time at final.

This project depends on the approved stair and deck structural details identified in Project 04's code-note summary and the framing connections verified in Project 07. It feeds forward into the interior concealment readiness in Project 15, the final building inspection checklist in Project 16, and the Chapter 20 capstone's life-safety review.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying every stair, ramp, landing, deck, balcony, porch, platform, guard, and handrail in the defined project
  • measuring finished riser, tread, width, headroom, and landing dimensions against approved documents
  • verifying handrail height, continuity, graspability, clearance, returns, and extensions
  • verifying guard height, post spacing, structural attachment, and infill compliance
  • verifying stringer, ledger, footing, post, beam, and connector details against approved structural documents
  • recording deficiencies, corrections, reinspection items, and final approval

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • size, redesign, or approve structural stringers, ledgers, footings, posts, beams, connectors, or guard systems without the authorized design basis
  • accept surface attachment to decking alone when a structural load path is required, or accept an existing ledger connection as acceptable because it appears secure
  • round a measured variance to create compliance, or represent this checklist as a permit approval, inspection result, or engineering evaluation
  • force, damage, or bypass a required opening test method to obtain a passing result

Any structural condition, connection, or dimension that cannot be verified against approved documents must be labeled unresolved and referred to the responsible design professional, engineer, or building official.

Trade Note

Measure every finished riser and tread using the same reference method every time, not just the top and bottom of the run. A small, gradual variance across several risers is one of the most common causes of a failed stair inspection, and it is invisible unless every riser is individually measured and recorded.

Setup Standard

Do not begin measuring a stair or guard element until the approved plans, structural details, and adopted local requirements are confirmed for this specific project. A dimension carried forward from a different project, a different jurisdiction, or a generic reference is not a substitute for the approved design values for this stair and deck system.

Shop Tip

Build the measurement sheet with a row for every riser, tread, guard segment, and connection point before you start measuring. Deciding the recording format after measurements have already been taken is how individual readings get lost or mixed up between elements.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every stair, guard, handrail, and deck element in this project is verified the same way: confirm the approved design value, measure the actual finished condition using a consistent method, compare the two against the adopted tolerance, and record the result before deciding whether to correct or accept. That same method — design value, field value, tolerance source, status — is used for every element from a single interior stair to a full exterior deck system.

Design Values, Field Values, and Tolerance Sources

Every stair, guard, and handrail measurement recorded on the checklist compares a design value from the approved plans or adopted requirement to an actual field value, using a tolerance source that states how much variation is permitted. Do not round a field value to match the design value or accept a variance without recording it against its tolerance source.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Riser and Tread Individually

Measure and record every finished riser and tread using the same field method, not only the top and bottom of the run. Compare each measurement with the adopted requirement without rounding to create compliance. A gradual variance across a run is only visible when every step is individually recorded.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Accept an Unverified Structural Connection

Do not accept surface attachment to decking alone when a structural load path is required, and do not assume an existing deck ledger or connection is acceptable because it appears secure. Verify ledger attachment only to approved structural backing with the specified fasteners and flashing, and refer any unverified connection to the responsible design professional or building official before proceeding.

Field Safety Cue — Measuring Elevated and Incomplete Structures

Do not stand on, lean against, or load a guard, handrail, or deck element that has not been confirmed structurally complete. Use ladders, platforms, and fall protection appropriate to the height and condition of the structure being measured, and stop work if a structure feels unstable during measurement.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Structural Determinations

Sizing or approving stringers, ledgers, footings, posts, beams, connectors, and guard systems is reserved for the design professional, engineer, or building official. Where a structural detail cannot be confirmed from approved documents, label it unresolved and record the official source that must answer it rather than accepting the condition as built.

E. Preparation — Define Elements and Controlling References

  1. Identify the adopted requirements, local amendments, approved plans, structural details, stair drawings, deck plans, and product documents. Confirm these against the Chapter 1 authority register before measuring any element.
  2. Define each stair, ramp, landing, deck, balcony, porch, platform, guard, and handrail included in the project. Record every element on the Project and Element Record before beginning measurements.
  3. Identify whether the element is interior, exterior, common, private, accessible, existing, new, or altered. Note the classification for each element, since it may change the applicable requirement.
  4. Identify the approved controlling elevations for floors, landings, decks, grade, and finish materials. Confirm these elevations from the approved plans before measuring rise or guard height.

F. Measure Stair Geometry

  1. Verify stair width, total rise, number of risers, tread design, nosing, landing, headroom, and opening conditions from approved documents. Record each design value on the measurement sheet before measuring the finished condition.
  2. Measure every finished riser and tread using a consistent field method after finish installation. Use the same reference point and tool for every step in the run.
  3. Record variation and compare it with the adopted requirement without rounding to create compliance. Enter the field value, design value, tolerance source, and status for every riser and tread.
  4. Verify winders, landings, changes in direction, doors, thresholds, and exterior drainage where applicable. Record each condition against its approved detail.

G. Verify Structural Support, Handrails, and Guards

  1. Verify stringer size, cut, bearing, attachment, spacing, support, connectors, and protection from approved structural details. Do not accept a stringer condition based on visual appearance alone.
  2. Verify treads and risers are supported, secured, slip-resistant as required, and free of hazardous damage. Record any damage or looseness as a deficiency.
  3. Identify where handrails are required and the approved height, continuity, graspability, clearance, returns, extensions, and transitions. Measure and record each value against the adopted requirement.
  4. Verify handrail brackets, fasteners, backing, posts, joints, and load path. Confirm the load path is continuous to the supporting structure, not surface-mounted alone.
  5. Identify where guards are required at stairs, landings, decks, balconies, porches, and retaining conditions. Record every required guard location on the connection checklist.
  6. Verify guard height, post spacing, structural attachment, infill, opening limitations, and stair triangle conditions from approved requirements. Record each measurement against its design value and tolerance source.
  7. Do not accept surface attachment to decking alone when a structural load path is required. Refer any unverified guard-post connection to the responsible design professional before accepting it.

H. Verify Infill, Lighting, and Access

  1. Verify baluster, cable, panel, mesh, glazing, or other infill product and attachment are approved for the application. Confirm the product documents match the installed material.
  2. Verify openings using the required test method and location without forcing or damaging the system. Record the result at each tested location.
  3. Verify stair and deck lighting, switches, receptacles, and exterior weather protection where required. Record each item against the approved electrical and envelope details.

I. Verify Deck Structure and Ledger Attachment

  1. Verify deck footing, post, beam, joist, ledger, flashing, lateral, uplift, stair, and guard details from approved plans. Compare each connection to its approved structural detail.
  2. Do not assume an existing deck ledger or connection is acceptable because it appears secure. Refer any ledger condition that cannot be confirmed from approved documents to the design professional or building official.
  3. Verify ledger attachment only to approved structural backing with specified fasteners and flashing. Record the fastener type, spacing, and flashing condition at the ledger.
  4. Verify freestanding-deck support, bracing, uplift, and lateral connections. Confirm these connections independently of the ledger where the deck is not house-attached.
  5. Verify treated lumber, cut treatment, fastener compatibility, decay clearance, drainage, and ventilation. Record any untreated cut ends, incompatible fasteners, or blocked drainage as a deficiency.
  6. Verify doors, gates, latches, barriers, pool conditions, and child-safety requirements where applicable. Record each applicable condition against its adopted requirement.

J. Document, Correct, and Close the Checklist

  1. Identify required rough framing, connector, footing, deck, stair, guard, and final inspection stages. Confirm which stages remain open before concealment or final approval.
  2. Photograph concealed stringer, post, ledger, blocking, and guard-post load paths. Index each photograph by element and location on the connection checklist.
  3. Create a measurement sheet with location, design value, field value, tolerance source, and status. Complete this sheet for every element measured in Sections F through I.
  4. Create a structural connection checklist linked to detail references. Reference the approved detail number for every structural connection recorded.
  5. Correct geometry or structural defects through approved design rather than cosmetic adjustment. Do not shim, trim, or cosmetically mask a riser, guard, or connection defect to obtain a passing measurement.
  6. Perform a final operation, stability, clearance, lighting, drainage, and surface-hazard review. Operate gates, latches, and lighting, and check stability at every guard and handrail.
  7. Record corrections, reinspection items, approvals, and use restrictions. Assign every open deficiency to a responsible party with a target date.
  8. Archive the checklist, measurements, photographs, product documents, engineering, and inspection records. Store the archived checklist where it is available for Project 16 and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Common Checklist Inspection

Inspect the completed checklist using the same checks regardless of which element is being reviewed.

  • Measurement check: confirm every riser, tread, handrail, and guard element has a recorded design value, field value, tolerance source, and status.
  • Structural check: confirm every stringer, ledger, footing, post, beam, and connector is verified against an approved detail, not visual assumption.
  • Infill check: confirm baluster, cable, panel, mesh, or glazing product and opening test results are recorded for every applicable location.
  • Documentation check: confirm photographs are indexed by element and location for every concealed connection.
  • Correction check: confirm every deficiency is assigned, corrected through approved design, and reinspected.
  • Referral check: confirm at least one unresolved structural condition was referred to the design professional, engineer, or building official and the response recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Stair Run Fails the Rise or Run Consistency Check

Probable cause: unequal riser heights or tread depths introduced during framing or finish installation, or a finish material added without adjusting the underlying rough dimension.
Confirmation: measure every riser and tread individually using the same reference method and compare each value to the adopted requirement.
Correction: correct the affected risers or treads through approved design and finish adjustment rather than cosmetic trimming.
Reinspection: repeat the individual riser and tread measurement on the corrected run.

A Guard Opening Fails the Required Test Method

Probable cause: baluster or infill spacing exceeds the adopted opening limit, or the infill product does not match the approved product documents.
Confirmation: retest the opening using the required test method at the failed location and compare the installed product with its product documents.
Correction: replace or adjust the infill to the approved product and spacing.
Reinspection: repeat the opening test at the corrected location.

A Deck Ledger Connection Cannot Be Verified Against Approved Details

Probable cause: the ledger was installed before the approved structural detail was confirmed, or fasteners and flashing do not match the specified attachment.
Confirmation: compare the exposed ledger attachment, fastener type, spacing, and flashing to the approved structural detail before concealment.
Correction: obtain a revised design or correct the attachment to the specified backing, fasteners, and flashing.
Reinspection: repeat the structural connection check before allowing concealment.

A Handrail Fails Continuity or Extension Requirements

Probable cause: the handrail was terminated early, interrupted by a newel post without a required transition, or installed without the required extension at the top or bottom of the run.
Confirmation: measure handrail height, continuity, and extension length against the adopted requirement at every point along the run.
Correction: extend, splice, or replace the handrail sections needed to restore required continuity and extensions.
Reinspection: repeat the handrail measurement along the full corrected run.

A Structural Connection Was Concealed Before Verification

Probable cause: decking, siding, or finish material was installed over a stringer, post, or ledger connection before the structural connection checklist was completed.
Confirmation: review the photograph index for the affected location and confirm whether a pre-concealment photograph and connection record exist.
Correction: expose the connection for verification where required, or obtain design-professional or building-official acceptance of an alternative verification method.
Reinspection: repeat the structural connection check once the condition is verified or accepted.

No Referral Was Made for an Unresolved Structural Condition

Probable cause: a stringer, ledger, or connector condition that could not be confirmed from approved documents was accepted as built instead of being referred for review.
Confirmation: review the structural connection checklist for entries marked unresolved without a recorded referral and response.
Correction: submit the condition to the responsible design professional, engineer, or building official and record the response.
Reinspection: repeat the referral check on the completed checklist.

Field Terms

The dimension, height, or spacing shown on the approved plans, structural details, or adopted requirement for a specific element.
Field Value
The dimension actually measured on the finished element using a consistent method and reference point.
Tolerance Source
The adopted requirement, approved detail, or manufacturer instruction that defines the permitted variation between design and field values.
Controlling Elevation
The approved floor, landing, deck, or grade elevation used as the reference point for measuring rise, guard height, and headroom.
Structural Load Path
The continuous connection from a guard, handrail, or stair element through its posts, blocking, and framing to the supporting structure.
Guard Opening Test
The required method for testing whether a guard or infill opening exceeds the adopted spacing limit.
Ledger Attachment
The connection of a deck to the house structure using approved structural backing, fasteners, and flashing.
Unresolved Structural Item
A connection, dimension, or condition that cannot be confirmed from approved documents and must be referred to the design professional or building official.
Structural Connection Checklist
The document linking each verified connection to its approved detail reference.
Measurement Sheet
The document recording location, design value, field value, tolerance source, and status for every measured element.

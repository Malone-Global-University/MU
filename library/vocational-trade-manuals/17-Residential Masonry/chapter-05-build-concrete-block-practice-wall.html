Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one nonstructural concrete-masonry practice wall section. The finished work product is a straight, level, plumb block-wall section built to the Project Specification Sheet below, with consistent bed joints, filled head joints, a maintained running bond, an aligned face, and properly tooled and cleaned mortar joints.

This chapter uses the base accepted in Chapter 1, the staged and protected work area from Chapter 2, the mortar batch mixed and tested in Chapter 3, and the wall baseline, story pole, and mason's-line setup established in Chapter 4. Nothing in this chapter authorizes reinforced, grouted, structural, retaining, or load-bearing block work; that scope begins in Chapter 7 and remains subject to the engineered-design limits stated in the master instructions.

The skills demonstrated here — spreading a consistent mortar bed, setting units to line and level, maintaining head-joint fullness, controlling course height against the story pole, and tooling joints at the correct firmness — are the foundation for every later block, brick, veneer, and repair chapter in this book.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-025 — Spread a mortar bed of the specified width and thickness and furrow it consistently before setting units.
  • RM-026 — Set concrete masonry units to line, level, and elevation with full bed bearing and filled head joints.
  • RM-027 — Maintain head joints at the specified width and fullness across every course.
  • RM-028 — Keep courses level against the story pole and mason's line at every checked interval.
  • RM-029 — Align the wall face using line and straightedge and maintain plumb at controlled intervals.
  • RM-030 — Inspect completed blockwork for bond, joint fullness, level, plumb, alignment, and surface cleanliness, and document the result.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 determined the project was ready, Chapter 2 staged a controlled work area, Chapter 3 produced a compatible mortar batch, and Chapter 4 established the wall baseline, story pole, and mason's line. This chapter is the first point in the book where masonry units are actually laid. Every later block chapter — corners and intersections in Chapter 6, reinforcement and grout in Chapter 7 — builds directly on the bed-joint, head-joint, level, and plumb technique taught here.

The practice wall built in this chapter is retained as a reference fixture. It is not disassembled at the end of the chapter; it remains available for comparison when Chapter 6 begins corner and intersection work on an adjoining or separate practice assembly.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the accepted base from Chapter 1 is clean and level before laying begins
  • dry-laying and adjusting the first course for module
  • spreading, furrowing, and buttering mortar for concrete masonry units
  • setting corner or end units and intermediate units to line, level, and elevation
  • maintaining full bed bearing and filled head joints across every course
  • maintaining the specified bond offset between courses
  • checking course height against the story pole and wall face against line and straightedge
  • checking plumb at controlled intervals
  • tooling exposed joints, brushing, and protecting the finished wall
  • inspecting and documenting the completed practice wall

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • build a load-bearing wall, retaining wall, foundation wall, or any wall exceeding the freestanding height limit stated in the Acceptance Standards below
  • install vertical or horizontal reinforcement, grout, anchor bolts, or embeds; those tasks belong to Chapter 7 and require the engineered reinforcement and grout schedule identified there
  • build a corner, intersection, or pier; those tasks belong to Chapter 6 and Chapter 9
  • proceed if the base has not passed the Chapter 1 readiness inspection or if the mortar batch has not passed the Chapter 3 workability check

Trade Note

This chapter's practice wall is nonstructural and freestanding at the height stated in the Project Specification Sheet. Do not extend the wall beyond that height in the field to "make it more useful." A taller, unbraced block wall built without an engineered design is a stop-work condition under the master instructions, not a shop-floor judgment call.

Setup Standard

Do not spread the first mortar bed until the Chapter 1 readiness decision is Approved or Conditional-cleared, the Chapter 3 mortar batch has passed its workability check, and the Chapter 4 layout has been reinspected and approved. A wall built on an unapproved base or with rejected mortar must be removed and rebuilt, not patched.

Shop Tip

Keep the mortarboard loaded with only as much mortar as can be used within the working time documented in Chapter 3. Reworking stiffened mortar back into a workable bed by adding water is a retempering violation, not a shop shortcut.

RM-025 – RM-030

Layout Verification / Laying / Tooling / Inspection

Concrete Block Practice Wall Construction

Difficulty: Beginner

Estimated Time: 5–7 hours

Estimated Cost: Low to moderate

Finished Work: One 4-course, 6-foot concrete-block practice wall section built to the Project Specification Sheet

A. Layout Verification and Base Confirmation

  1. Review practice-wall dimensions, block type, mortar, bond, wall height, reinforcement, and inspection limits. Confirm the Project Specification Sheet above matches the units and mortar staged for this wall.
  2. Confirm the approved base is clean and level. Recheck the Chapter 1 readiness record; sweep and, where required, dampen the base per the Chapter 3 unit-preparation procedure.
  3. Mark wall ends and first-course unit positions. Transfer the Chapter 4 baseline marks for the reference end, opposite end, and every full and half unit position in the first course.
  4. Dry-lay the first course and confirm module. Lay the first course without mortar using the 3/8 in. joint allowance; adjust position only within the tolerance stated in the Acceptance Standards.
  5. Remove units and damp-clean the base where required. Lift the dry-laid units in order, set them aside in sequence, and clean the base surface.

B. First Course

  1. Spread the first mortar bed to the specified width and thickness. Place mortar the full width of both face shells to a thickness sufficient to compress to 3/8 in. under the unit.
  2. Furrow the bed consistently. Draw the trowel point through the bed in a shallow furrow along its length, keeping the furrow depth uniform.
  3. Butter head joints where required. Apply mortar to the leading vertical face of each unit before setting it against the previous unit.
  4. Set the first corner or end block. Place the reference-end unit on the bed at the marked position.
  5. Tap it to line, level, and elevation using the approved tool. Use the rubber mallet; check level along the top and side of the unit and check elevation against the story pole before proceeding.
  6. Set the opposite end reference. Place and level the unit at the far end of the first course using the same method.
  7. Stretch the mason's line. Attach line blocks to both end units at the top outside edge and tension the line along the reference face.
  8. Lay intermediate units with full bed bearing and filled head joints. Set each unit to the line, verifying head-joint mortar is visible squeezing from the joint before final positioning.
  9. Avoid excessive tapping that squeezes out the bed. Tap only enough to reach line, level, and elevation; stop tapping once the unit reaches the story-pole mark.
  10. Check each unit for line and level. Verify against the mason's line and the 4-foot level before moving to the next unit.
  11. Remove excess mortar from the face without smearing. Cut off projecting mortar with the trowel edge and return it to the mortarboard.

C. Subsequent Courses

  1. Begin the next course with the specified bond offset. Start the second course with a half block at the reference end to establish the running-bond offset.
  2. Maintain overlap and avoid aligned vertical joints unless the design permits them. Confirm no head joint in the new course aligns directly above a head joint in the course below.
  3. Check course height against the story pole. Hold the story pole against the wall end at every course and confirm the top of the course meets the marked elevation.
  4. Check wall face with line and straightedge. Raise the mason's line to the new course elevation and verify the straightedge contacts every unit face within the tolerance stated in the Acceptance Standards.
  5. Check wall plumb at controlled intervals. Hold the 4-foot level vertically at both wall ends and at midlength after every course; correct drift before the mortar stiffens.
  6. Tool exposed joints at the correct firmness. Test joint firmness with a thumbprint; tool head joints and any exposed bed-joint edge with the jointing tool once the mortar resists a light thumbprint.
  7. Brush after tooling without smearing mortar. Brush diagonally across the tooled joints with the stiff-bristle brush.
  8. Protect the wall from weather and impact. Cover the wall with weather-protection sheeting at the end of each work session and whenever rain, freezing, or direct sun exposure is expected before the next session.

D. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect bond, joints, level, plumb, alignment, and surface cleanliness. Check every item in the Acceptance Standards table and record the measured result on the Inspection Checklist.
  2. Document the completed practice wall. Photograph both wall faces and both ends, record the final height and length, and record the mason name and date on the Inspection Checklist.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every course in this wall depends on the same discipline: a consistent bed, a fully filled head joint, a checked course height, and a checked plumb reading before the next course begins. Skipping any one of these checks does not save time — it moves the correction later in the wall, where it is harder and slower to fix.

Bed jointSpread, furrowed, full width of both face shells
Head jointButtered before setting; filled with no visible daylight
Course heightChecked against the story pole at every course
Bond offsetHalf-unit offset maintained at every course
Face alignmentChecked against the mason's line and straightedge
PlumbChecked at both ends and midlength after every course
ToolingPerformed at thumbprint-hard firmness, then brushed
Cure protectionCovered against rain, freezing, and direct sun between sessions

Measurement Cue — Check Every Course, Not Just the First and Last

Check level, plumb, and course height at every course, not only at the start and finish of the wall. A drift of 1/16 in. per course is difficult to see in a single course but compounds into a visible lean or step by the fourth course.

Critical Safety Cue — Freestanding Height Limit

Do not build this practice wall beyond 4 courses (32 in. nominal) without bracing or an engineered design. An unbraced concrete-block wall built taller than its stated freestanding limit can topple under lateral load, including wind, incidental contact, or its own construction loads. Stop work and obtain an engineered design before extending the wall beyond the height stated in the Project Specification Sheet.

Safety Cue — Handling Concrete Masonry Units

Lift concrete masonry units with a stable grip on the face shells, keeping the load close to the body. Use a cart or team lift for repeated moves of the full pallet quantity rather than repeated single-unit carrying over distance, which increases fatigue and dropped-unit risk.

Field Safety Cue — Wet Mortar Contact

Wear waterproof gloves whenever spreading, buttering, or tooling mortar. Wash any mortar contact with skin or eyes immediately per the washing procedure established in Chapter 2. Do not kneel in wet mortar droppings on the base or mortarboard area.

Code Alert — Nonstructural Scope Only

This wall is built and inspected as nonstructural practice work only. If the learner or instructor intends to convert this wall into a permanent structure, a load-bearing element, or a retaining wall, stop and obtain the engineered design and permit review required by the master instructions before continuing beyond this chapter's scope.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Wall Leans Progressively Toward One Face

Probable cause: plumb was not checked after every course, allowing a small per-course drift to compound.
Confirmation: hold the 4-foot level against both wall faces at each course and compare the reading against the Acceptance Standards plumb tolerance.
Correction: if the mortar has not yet set, remove and reset the affected courses, correcting plumb one course at a time from the point where drift began. If mortar has set, stop, document the condition, and do not continue laying additional courses on top of an out-of-plumb wall.
Reinspection: recheck plumb at every course above the correction point before continuing.

Head Joints Show Daylight or Voids

Probable cause: the head joint was not buttered before setting, or the unit was pushed into place without enough mortar to fill the full joint width.
Confirmation: inspect the joint face after tooling; visible daylight or a soft spot when probed with the jointing tool indicates an unfilled joint.
Correction: while the joint is still workable, remove the unit, rebutter the head joint, and reset it. If mortar has stiffened, rake out the joint to the depth specified in Chapter 14's repointing procedure and repack it.
Reinspection: reinspect the corrected joint for full width, no daylight, and proper tooling.

Course Height Runs Ahead of or Behind the Story Pole

Probable cause: bed-joint thickness drifted above or below 3/8 in. across several units without being checked against the story pole at each course.
Confirmation: measure bed-joint thickness at three points on the affected course and compare cumulative height to the story-pole mark.
Correction: if the mortar is still workable, adjust bed-joint thickness on the current course to bring cumulative height back to the story-pole mark, keeping the adjustment within the plus-or-minus 1/8 in. tolerance. Do not make a single joint thickness fall outside tolerance to correct several courses at once.
Reinspection: recheck the story-pole match at the corrected course and the next course laid above it.

Vertical Joints Align Between Adjacent Courses

Probable cause: the half-unit bond offset was not started at the reference end of a course, or an intermediate unit was set without checking the offset against the course below.
Confirmation: sight down the wall face and compare head-joint positions between the current course and the course immediately below it.
Correction: while mortar is workable, remove and reset the affected units starting from the correct half-unit offset point. If mortar has set, document the condition and do not conceal it; it must be corrected before the wall is accepted.
Reinspection: resight the wall face after correction and confirm no aligned vertical joints remain.

Face Alignment Drifts From the Mason's Line

Probable cause: units were tapped to level and elevation but not checked against the mason's line before the mortar began to stiffen.
Confirmation: hold the straightedge against the wall face and measure the gap against the Acceptance Standards tolerance.
Correction: while workable, tap the affected units back to the line. If mortar has stiffened, remove and reset the affected units rather than forcing an already-bonded unit.
Reinspection: recheck face alignment with the straightedge along the full corrected span.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, protected practice wall, not an unrecorded stack of laid block.

  • Return unused units to their staging area and separate any damaged or rejected units for disposal per Chapter 2 waste handling
  • Clean the mortarboard, trowel, jointing tool, and mallet before mortar hardens on them
  • Return excess mortar to contained waste per Chapter 2 washout procedures; do not discharge washout to drains or unprotected ground
  • Cover the finished wall with weather-protection sheeting for the required cure period
  • File photographs, measurements, and the completed Inspection Checklist with the Chapter 1 readiness plan and Chapter 4 layout record for this project

Field Terms

Bed Joint
The horizontal mortar joint between courses of masonry units.
Head Joint
The vertical mortar joint between adjoining units within the same course.
Furrow
A shallow groove drawn through a spread mortar bed to help distribute mortar under the unit as it is set.
Running Bond
A bond pattern in which each course is offset from the course below by half a unit length.
Story Pole
A layout reference marked with course elevations, used to control cumulative wall height.
Line Block
A small block used to anchor the mason's line at the top outside edge of an end unit.
Thumbprint Hard
The mortar firmness at which a light thumb pressure leaves an impression without deforming the joint; the correct condition for tooling.
Face Shell
The outer wall of a hollow concrete masonry unit that provides the bearing surface for the bed joint.
Plumb
Vertical alignment of a wall face, checked with a level held against the face.
Freestanding Height Limit
The maximum unbraced height a nonstructural masonry wall may reach without an engineered design.

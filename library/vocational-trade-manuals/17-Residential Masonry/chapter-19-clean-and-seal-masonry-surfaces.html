Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to clean and, where appropriate, seal a cured masonry surface using the least aggressive method that achieves an acceptable result. The finished work product is one documented cleaning and sealing record covering surface identification, a tested inconspicuous area, containment and runoff control, the cleaning method and result, drying confirmation, a sealer decision supported by the approved design and moisture condition, and a final inspection.

You will select one cured masonry practice surface — completed in an earlier chapter or otherwise approved for this exercise — and confirm it has cured for the required period before any cleaner is applied. Nothing in this chapter authorizes aggressive methods such as strong acid, high pressure, sandblasting, or abrasive grinding as a starting method, and nothing in this chapter authorizes sealing over an unresolved moisture, flashing, or structural condition.

This chapter follows directly from Chapter 18. Where Chapter 18 stopped at documentation and referral for chimney conditions beyond minor maintenance, this chapter addresses the routine surface-care work — cleaning mortar smears, removing efflorescence, and applying a compatible sealer only when justified — that closes out ordinary masonry work across the book, including the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-109 — Select a cleaning method, choosing the least aggressive compatible option for the identified unit type, mortar, stain, and finish.
  • RM-110 — Remove efflorescence, identifying and reducing the moisture source before or alongside brushing or cleaning.
  • RM-111 — Clean mortar smears, allowing sufficient cure before cleaning and using a compatible method.
  • RM-112 — Rinse masonry safely, controlling dwell time, agitation, rinse pressure, and runoff containment.
  • RM-113 — Apply masonry sealer where appropriate, using a breathable penetrating product only when the approved design and moisture condition permit it.
  • RM-114 — Inspect the finished surface, checking for color change, etching, streaking, residual salts, joint damage, and blocked drainage details.

Where This Skill Fits

Cleaning and sealing is the last surface-level operation performed on cured masonry, and it depends on every water-management decision made earlier in the book. The cavity, weep, and flashing details installed in Chapter 11, Chapter 12, and Chapter 13 must remain open and functional after cleaning; the joints tooled in Chapter 5 through Chapter 9 must remain undamaged by an aggressive cleaner; and the repair and repointing work from Chapter 14 and Chapter 15 must be allowed to cure before it is cleaned alongside original work.

The judgment used here — testing before committing to a method, refusing to seal over an unresolved moisture source, and stopping short of a treatment the surface cannot tolerate — is the same judgment the Chapter 20 capstone requires before final cleaning and closeout.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying unit type, mortar, age, finish, stain, efflorescence, previous sealer, moisture source, and cleaning limits before selecting a method
  • confirming sufficient mortar cure before cleaning or sealing begins
  • correcting active water entry before cleaning or sealing proceeds
  • selecting dry brushing, low-pressure water, detergent, a proprietary cleaner, or a poultice as the starting method
  • testing the selected method in a small inconspicuous area before full-surface application
  • protecting adjacent windows, metals, roofing, siding, paving, plants, soil, and drainage, and containing runoff and wash water
  • removing efflorescence by identifying and reducing the moisture source alongside brushing or cleaning
  • cleaning mortar smears only after sufficient cure
  • applying cleaner at the specified dilution and dwell time, agitating only with compatible tools, and rinsing thoroughly at controlled pressure
  • allowing the masonry to dry fully before inspection or sealer application
  • applying a breathable penetrating sealer or other specified product only when the approved design and moisture condition permit it, while preserving weeps, movement joints, and drainage
  • inspecting the finished surface and documenting tests, cleaner, dilution, runoff, sealer, weather, and results

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin cleaning with strong acid, high pressure, sandblasting, or aggressive abrasion; these methods are not starting options under this chapter
  • clean or seal a surface with an unresolved active-water-entry condition; that condition must be corrected first, under the applicable earlier chapter or a qualified professional's scope
  • apply sealer as a substitute for missing flashing, failed drainage, incompatible mortar, or structural cracking
  • block, coat over, or otherwise impair weeps, flashing outlets, vents, or movement joints during cleaning or sealing
  • discharge cleaner, rinse water, or slurry into a storm drain or unapproved runoff path

Any surface, joint, or drainage condition that fails the acceptance standards in this chapter must be documented, corrected by a qualified party where required, and reinspected before sealer is applied or the project is closed out.

Trade Note

The test area is not a formality. A cleaner that performs acceptably on one course of brick can etch a different mortar batch, streak a stained area, or dull a manufactured-stone finish nearby. Every cleaning decision in this chapter is made from the test-area result, not from the product label alone.

Setup Standard

Do not begin full-surface cleaning until the test-area result has been reviewed and accepted. Do not apply sealer until the surface has dried fully, the test area confirms an acceptable result, and the approved design and moisture condition permit sealing. A surface with an unresolved active-water-entry condition does not proceed to sealing under this chapter.

Shop Tip

Photograph the test area immediately after rinsing and again after it dries. Some etching, streaking, and color change only becomes visible once the surface is fully dry, and a test area accepted while still damp can hide a defect that only appears on the full surface later.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every cleaning and sealing decision in this chapter follows the same order: identify the surface and its stains, confirm sufficient cure and no active moisture problem, test the least aggressive method first, clean and rinse using controlled dilution and pressure, allow the surface to dry fully, and seal only when the approved design and moisture condition justify it.

Cleaning-Method Selection and the Least-Aggressive Rule

Select dry brushing, low-pressure water, detergent, a proprietary cleaner, or a poultice as the starting method for every surface in this chapter. Do not begin with strong acid, high pressure, sandblasting, or aggressive abrasion; these methods are reserved for cases where the least aggressive method has already been tested and found insufficient, and only under a design and product selection outside the scope of this chapter. Record the selected starting method and the reason it was chosen before mixing any product.

Measurement Cue — Dilution and Dwell Time

Mix cleaner at the exact dilution stated in the manufacturer literature and apply it for the exact dwell time stated. Do not increase concentration or dwell time to compensate for a stain that does not respond at the specified rate; a slower, repeated application of the specified dilution is the correct response, not a stronger single application.

Critical Safety Cue — Chemical Handling

Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection whenever mixing, applying, agitating, or rinsing any cleaner or sealer. Do not mix cleaning products together unless the manufacturer literature explicitly permits the combination. Stop work and flush the affected area with clean water if cleaner contacts skin or eyes, and follow the product's first-aid instructions.

Field Safety Cue — Runoff and Adjacent-Surface Protection

Protect windows, metals, roofing, siding, paving, plants, soil, and drainage before mixing any cleaner. Establish containment berms or absorbent socks and direct wash water to an approved discharge point. Do not allow cleaner, rinse water, or slurry to enter a storm drain, planting bed, or neighboring property.

Code Alert — Discharge and Runoff Requirements

Confirm local requirements for wash-water containment and discharge before cleaning begins. Requirements for runoff, slurry disposal, and chemical discharge vary by jurisdiction and site; verify locally rather than assuming a prior project's containment plan is sufficient here.

E. Surface Identification and Cure Confirmation

  1. Identify unit type, mortar, age, finish, stain, efflorescence, previous sealer, moisture source, and cleaning limits. Record every item on the Surface Identification Record before selecting a cleaning method.
  2. Confirm mortar has cured for the required period. Check the project record for the completion date and compare it against the manufacturer- or code-required minimum cure period.
  3. Correct active water entry before cleaning or sealing. If an active source is identified, stop and correct it under the applicable earlier chapter or a qualified professional's scope before proceeding.

F. Method Selection and Test Area

  1. Select dry brushing, low-pressure water, detergent, proprietary cleaner, or another approved method. Record the selected method and the reason it is the least aggressive option expected to be effective.
  2. Do not begin with strong acid, high pressure, sandblasting, or aggressive abrasion. Confirm the selected method is not one of these before proceeding.
  3. Prepare a small inconspicuous test area. Select a location representative of the full surface but not prominent if a defect occurs.
  4. Protect windows, metals, roofing, siding, paving, plants, soil, and drainage. Install plastic sheeting and containment before mixing any cleaner, even for the test area.
  5. Establish runoff and wash-water containment. Confirm the containment berm, absorbent sock, or approved discharge point is in place.

G. Cleaning, Rinsing, and Drying

  1. Remove loose surface deposits using a dry or controlled method. Brush or vacuum loose material before applying any liquid cleaner.
  2. For efflorescence, identify and reduce the moisture source. Record the identified source and the correction taken or referred.
  3. Brush or clean salts using the compatible method. Use a nonmetallic brush and the selected cleaner at the specified dilution.
  4. For mortar smears, allow sufficient cure before cleaning. Confirm the smear area meets the same cure-period standard as the surrounding surface.
  5. Prewet masonry where the cleaner requires it. Follow the manufacturer instruction for prewetting before cleaner application.
  6. Apply cleaner at the specified dilution and dwell time. Record the exact dilution ratio and start and stop time of the dwell period.
  7. Agitate only with compatible brushes or pads. Do not use metal brushes, scrapers, or abrasive pads unless specifically approved for the unit type.
  8. Rinse thoroughly at controlled pressure. Use low-pressure water and confirm no cleaner residue remains using pH test strips where applicable.
  9. Prevent cleaner from drying on the surface. Rinse before the dwell time is exceeded and before the cleaner begins to dry.
  10. Allow masonry to dry fully. Confirm dryness with the moisture meter before proceeding to inspection or the sealer decision.

H. Test-Area Review and Full-Surface Cleaning

  1. Inspect for color change, etching, streaking, residual salts, and joint damage in the test area. Photograph the dry test area and compare it against the untreated surrounding surface.
  2. Accept or reject the test-area result. If rejected, select a less aggressive method or product and repeat the test area before proceeding to full-surface cleaning.
  3. Repeat the accepted method across the full surface. Apply the same dilution, dwell time, agitation, and rinse procedure used in the accepted test area.

I. Sealer Decision, Application, and Final Inspection

  1. Apply sealer only when the approved design and moisture condition permit it. Confirm the design document specifically authorizes sealing before proceeding; if no such authorization exists, do not apply sealer.
  2. Select breathable penetrating sealer or other specified product. Confirm the product is identified as compatible with the unit type and mortar in the approved design or manufacturer literature.
  3. Apply at the controlled coverage without puddles or glazing. Follow the manufacturer coverage rate and application method.
  4. Preserve weeps, movement joints, and drainage. Mask or otherwise protect these details from sealer contact during application.
  5. Protect through cure. Follow the manufacturer cure period and protect the surface from rain, foot traffic, or contamination during that period.
  6. Inspect appearance, repellency indicators, breathability indicators, overspray, and blocked details. Confirm no detail has been coated or obstructed.
  7. Document tests, cleaner, dilution, runoff, sealer, weather, and results. Complete the full documentation record before closing out the chapter.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every cleaning and sealing project using the same checks regardless of the surface or method selected.

  • Cure and moisture check: confirm sufficient cure and corrected active moisture conditions were verified before cleaning began.
  • Method check: confirm the least aggressive compatible method was tested and accepted before full-surface cleaning began.
  • Containment check: confirm adjacent surfaces were protected and wash water was directed to an approved discharge point.
  • Drying check: confirm moisture-meter confirmation of dryness before any sealer decision.
  • Sealer check: confirm sealer, if used, was authorized by the approved design and did not obstruct weeps, movement joints, or drainage.
  • Documentation check: confirm every test, product, dilution, dwell time, and result is recorded with a photograph reference.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Surface Etched or Streaked After Cleaning

Probable cause: the cleaner concentration or dwell time exceeded the manufacturer specification, or the method was more aggressive than the test area indicated.
Confirmation: compare the dilution and dwell time actually used against the manufacturer literature and the accepted test-area record.
Correction: stop cleaning, document the affected area, and consult the product manufacturer or a qualified restoration professional for a compatible correction; do not apply a second aggressive treatment to correct the first.
Reinspection: photograph the corrected area and compare it against the original test-area result before resuming work.

Efflorescence Returned Shortly After Cleaning

Probable cause: the underlying moisture source was not identified or was not reduced before or during cleaning.
Confirmation: use the moisture meter to check the surface and surrounding grade, flashing, or drainage for continued moisture movement.
Correction: identify and correct the moisture source under the applicable earlier chapter or a qualified professional's scope before cleaning the surface again.
Reinspection: recheck the surface for efflorescence after the moisture source has been corrected and sufficient time has passed.

Sealer Was Applied Without Confirming Dryness

Probable cause: the sealer decision was made based on visual appearance rather than a moisture-meter reading.
Confirmation: check the documentation record for a recorded moisture-meter reading taken immediately before sealer application.
Correction: if sealer was applied to a masonry surface that was not confirmed dry, monitor the surface for trapped moisture, discoloration, or delamination and consult the sealer manufacturer for a corrective procedure.
Reinspection: for future applications, confirm and record a moisture-meter reading before every sealer decision.

A Weep or Movement Joint Was Blocked During Cleaning or Sealing

Probable cause: the detail was not identified or masked before cleaner or sealer was applied.
Confirmation: inspect every weep, flashing outlet, and movement joint on the treated surface for cleaner residue, sealer film, or obstruction.
Correction: clear the obstructed detail using a method compatible with the surrounding material and confirm the opening is fully restored.
Reinspection: recheck every drainage and movement detail on the surface after correction and before final closeout.

Wash Water or Slurry Was Not Contained

Probable cause: containment berms, absorbent socks, or an approved discharge point were not established before cleaning began.
Confirmation: compare the actual runoff path during cleaning against the documented containment plan.
Correction: stop cleaning, install the required containment, and clean up any discharged wash water or slurry according to local requirements.
Reinspection: confirm containment is in place before resuming cleaning and document the corrected runoff path.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, dried, and where appropriate sealed surface, not a verbal impression of how the cleaning went.

  • Confirm every photograph is labeled with the item, location, and date it documents
  • Collect and dispose of used cleaning materials, rags, and containment media according to the product manufacturer's instructions and local requirements
  • Remove protective sheeting and containment berms only after confirming no residual cleaner or sealer remains on adjacent surfaces
  • Return borrowed tools (moisture meter, sprayer, camera) to storage
  • File the completed Cleaning and Sealing Record with the project's approved drawings and specifications for reference in the Chapter 20 capstone

Field Terms

Dwell Time
The manufacturer-specified duration a cleaner remains on the surface before agitation or rinsing.
Efflorescence
Soluble salts carried to the surface by moisture and left behind as a white or gray deposit as the moisture evaporates.
Poultice
An absorbent paste applied over a stain to draw contaminants out of masonry through evaporation.
Test Area
The small, inconspicuous section of the actual surface used to evaluate a cleaning method before full-surface application.
Breathable Penetrating Sealer
A sealer that reduces water absorption while still permitting vapor to pass through the masonry.
Glazing
A glossy, non-breathable film left on a masonry surface from excessive sealer coverage.
Runoff Containment
The method used to capture and direct wash water and slurry to an approved discharge point.
Repellency Indicator
A visible sign, such as water beading, that a penetrating sealer is functioning as intended.
Breathability Indicator
A visible or measured sign that the applied sealer still permits vapor movement out of the masonry.
Cleaning and Sealing Record
The document recording every test, product, dilution, runoff measure, sealer decision, and final inspection result for the project.

Performance Check

With cleaning, rinsing, drying, and any sealer application complete, review the Cleaning and Sealing Record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every test, product, dilution, dwell time, runoff measure, and inspection result has been documented. Confirm the test-area result was accepted before full-surface cleaning began, that dryness was confirmed by moisture meter before any sealer decision, and that every weep, movement joint, and drainage detail remains open on the finished surface. A surface with any unresolved etching, streaking, joint damage, or blocked drainage detail is not closed out until the condition is corrected and reinspected.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to diagnose a cracked or damaged masonry unit, distinguish a cosmetic condition from one requiring structural evaluation, remove and replace the damaged unit, and repoint the surrounding joints so the repair integrates with adjacent work. The finished work product is one completed masonry-unit replacement with fully repointed surrounding joints and a documented crack-evaluation record confirming the repair remained within nonstructural scope.

You will select one approved nonstructural crack or damaged-unit condition identified during a prior readiness inspection or repointing project, evaluate it using the crack-diagnosis criteria in this chapter, and — only if the evaluation confirms the condition is within nonstructural repair scope — remove the damaged unit, inspect the concealed cavity, install a matching replacement, and repoint the surrounding joints.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1 readiness inspection, Chapter 3 mortar preparation, Chapter 10 cutting and silica control, and Chapter 14 repointing. It does not authorize structural crack repair, foundation repair, retaining-wall repair, or any repair of a wall or chimney showing active movement, bulging, displacement, or widespread cracking; those conditions are documented and referred, not repaired, in this chapter.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-085 — Evaluate masonry cracks, recording location, orientation, width, displacement, length, moisture, age, weather, load, and prior repairs.
  • RM-086 — Distinguish cosmetic from structural movement, applying the stop-work criteria in the Acceptance Standards table before any unit is disturbed.
  • RM-087 — Remove damaged units, using controlled hand or power tools without damaging adjacent units, reinforcement, ties, or flashing.
  • RM-088 — Replace brick, block, or stone, selecting a matching unit and setting it with full bed, head, and back-surface mortar coverage.
  • RM-089 — Repoint the repair area, packing and tooling the surrounding joints to match the adjacent profile, texture, and color.
  • RM-090 — Inspect repair integration, confirming fit, bond, color, alignment, and the absence of recurring movement before closeout.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter depends on the readiness classification from Chapter 1, the compatible mortar principles from Chapter 3, the controlled cutting and silica practices from Chapter 10, and the repointing procedure from Chapter 14. The crack-diagnosis judgment practiced here — distinguishing a single deteriorated unit from a symptom of settlement, lintel failure, or wall movement — is the same judgment applied throughout the book wherever masonry deterioration is inspected, including the Chapter 18 chimney condition assessment.

The completed repair from this chapter becomes part of the documented condition record used by later inspection, cleaning, and closeout work, including the Chapter 20 capstone if the capstone section includes a repair area.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • recording crack location, orientation, width, displacement, length, moisture, age, weather, load, and prior repairs
  • inspecting foundations, lintels, openings, wall ends, roof loads, drainage, and movement joints near the crack
  • distinguishing surface mortar cracking from unit cracking, stepped cracking, horizontal cracking, bulging, or displacement
  • applying stop-work criteria for active movement, wide or displaced cracks, failed lintels, bowed walls, retaining pressure, foundation movement, or chimney instability
  • marking the approved unit-replacement area and establishing silica, dust, falling-debris, and access controls
  • removing mortar and the damaged unit using controlled hand or power tools
  • cleaning the cavity and inspecting backing, ties, reinforcement, flashing, and moisture before replacement
  • selecting and setting a matching replacement unit with full mortar coverage
  • packing and tooling the surrounding joints to match adjacent work
  • inspecting the completed repair for fit, bond, color, alignment, and recurrence of movement

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with any unit removal or replacement where the crack evaluation identifies active movement, wide or displaced cracks, failed lintels, bowed walls, retaining pressure, foundation movement, or chimney instability
  • repair retaining walls, load-bearing walls, foundation walls, reinforced masonry, tall freestanding walls, chimneys, fireplaces, structural piers, or major veneer systems under this chapter
  • conceal a discovered ties, reinforcement, flashing, or moisture defect by proceeding with replacement before the concealed condition is documented and, where required, referred
  • use sealants, patching compounds, or cosmetic surface treatment as a substitute for the required unit removal, cavity inspection, and full-depth joint repair

Any condition that fails the crack-evaluation stop-work criteria must be documented and referred to a qualified professional; the repair does not proceed under this chapter until the referred condition is resolved.

Trade Note

A cracked masonry unit is a symptom before it is a repair. The evaluation in this chapter always comes before the tool touches the wall. Removing a damaged unit before the crack pattern, moisture source, and surrounding condition are fully recorded is the single most common cause of a repair that fails again within one heating or freeze-thaw cycle.

Setup Standard

Do not remove any masonry unit until the crack evaluation in Step 4 has confirmed the condition is within nonstructural repair scope and any concealed defect discovered during cavity inspection has been documented. A condition meeting any stop-work criterion halts the repair and requires professional referral before further work proceeds.

Shop Tip

Photograph the crack with the crack-width gauge held against it before removing anything. That photograph is the baseline record used to confirm, after the repair cures, that the surrounding wall shows no new movement.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every masonry repair in this book, from a single repointed joint to this chapter's unit replacement, follows the same sequence: evaluate the condition, apply stop-work criteria before disturbing anything, remove only what is approved, inspect what is concealed, replace or repair with compatible materials, and reinspect the finished work against the original record.

Cosmetic Versus Structural Movement

A hairline crack in a single mortar joint with no measurable displacement, no stepped pattern, and no accompanying bulging is treated as cosmetic and falls within this chapter's scope. A crack showing displacement across it, a stepped pattern following mortar joints diagonally up a wall, horizontal cracking near mid-height, bulging, or any crack near a failed lintel, disturbed foundation, or unstable chimney section is treated as structural movement and stops the repair under this chapter regardless of how small the crack appears.

Measurement Cue — Measure Displacement, Not Just Width

Width alone does not classify a crack. Place a straightedge across the crack and check whether one side has shifted out of plane with the other. Any measurable displacement, however small, is recorded as a structural indicator and evaluated against the stop-work criteria before proceeding.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop-Work Conditions

Stop the repair immediately for active movement, wide or displaced cracks, failed lintels, bowed walls, retaining pressure, foundation movement, or chimney instability. Do not remove, tap, or apply load to any unit in or adjacent to a wall or chimney section showing these conditions. Photograph the condition from a safe distance and refer it to a qualified professional; do not proceed to unit removal under this chapter.

Field Safety Cue — Falling Debris and Access

Establish falling-debris protection and a controlled access zone before removing any unit above shoulder height. Do not work from an improvised platform, unprotected edge, or unstable ladder placement. Keep the work area clear of foot traffic during removal and replacement.

Code Alert — Repair Scope and Permit Requirements

Confirm whether the local jurisdiction requires a permit or inspection for masonry-unit replacement, particularly on a designated historic structure, a party wall, or a wall containing structural reinforcement. Verify locally rather than assuming a prior repair's requirements apply.

E. Crack Evaluation

  1. Record crack location, orientation, width, displacement, length, moisture, age, weather, and prior repairs. Complete the Crack Evaluation Record before touching the wall.
  2. Inspect foundations, lintels, openings, wall ends, roof loads, drainage, and movement joints. Note any of these located within the immediate vicinity of the crack.
  3. Distinguish surface mortar cracking from unit cracking, stepped cracking, horizontal cracking, bulging, or displacement. Use the straightedge and level to check for displacement and out-of-plumb condition across the crack.
  4. Stop work for active movement, wide or displaced cracks, failed lintels, bowed walls, retaining pressure, foundation movement, or chimney instability. Photograph the condition from a safe distance, record the classification as Structural — Stop-Work, and refer it to the responsible professional. Do not proceed to Step 5 for a condition classified Structural — Stop-Work.

F. Preparation and Unit Removal

  1. Mark the approved unit-replacement area. Outline the single damaged unit and its immediately surrounding joints confirmed as Cosmetic in Step 4.
  2. Establish silica, dust, falling-debris, and access controls. Stage HEPA vacuum, wet-cutting equipment where cutting is required, and falling-debris protection before removal begins.
  3. Remove mortar around the damaged unit using controlled hand or power tools. Work from the joint inward, using hand chisels or a controlled rotary tool with wet or extraction attachment.
  4. Avoid damaging adjacent units and reinforcement. Direct chisel force away from adjacent unit edges and any visible ties or reinforcement.
  5. Remove the damaged unit in pieces if necessary. Break the unit into manageable sections rather than forcing an intact unit that risks damaging the cavity.

G. Cavity Inspection and Replacement

  1. Clean the cavity and inspect backing, ties, reinforcement, flashing, and moisture. Vacuum debris with the HEPA vacuum and photograph the exposed cavity before proceeding.
  2. Stop work if concealed support or water-management failure is discovered. Record any corroded tie, damaged flashing, unsound backing, or active moisture source found during cavity inspection, and refer it to a qualified professional before replacement proceeds.
  3. Select a matching replacement unit. Confirm size, color, texture, and finish match against the Acceptance Standards table before setting the unit.
  4. Prepare the cavity and replacement surfaces. Dampen the cavity where the mortar specification requires it and clean the replacement unit of dust or loose material.
  5. Apply mortar to the bed, head, and back surfaces as required. Cover all bearing and joint surfaces of the replacement unit fully; do not rely on head-joint mortar alone.
  6. Insert the unit without displacing adjacent masonry. Press the unit into place using steady, even pressure rather than forceful tapping that could disturb adjacent units.

H. Repointing, Cure, and Final Inspection

  1. Pack all surrounding joints fully. Fill each joint around the replacement unit in controlled lifts, compacting each lift per the Chapter 14 repointing procedure.
  2. Align the face and bond. Check the replacement unit's face alignment against the adjacent wall face with a straightedge before the mortar sets.
  3. Tool joints to match adjacent work. Tool each joint when thumbprint hard, matching the adjacent joint profile.
  4. Clean the repair. Brush loose crumbs and remove mortar smears from the unit face without smearing mortar across adjacent joints.
  5. Protect and cure it. Cover the repair area per the cure-protection plan and protect it from rain, freezing, rapid drying, and impact for the required cure period.
  6. Inspect unit fit, joint bond, color, profile, alignment, and recurrence of movement. Compare the finished repair against the Acceptance Standards table and the original Crack Evaluation Record.
  7. Document diagnosis, replacement, and structural referral limits. File the completed Crack Evaluation Record, cavity-inspection findings, photographs, and any professional referral with the project's readiness and inspection records.

Common Repair Inspection

Inspect every completed unit-replacement repair using the same checks regardless of unit type or wall condition.

  • Classification check: confirm the crack was classified Cosmetic before any unit was removed, and that the classification was not changed afterward to justify proceeding.
  • Cavity check: confirm backing, ties, reinforcement, and flashing were inspected and documented, with any concealed defect referred rather than concealed by the replacement.
  • Fit and coverage check: confirm the replacement unit matches adjacent masonry and has full bed, head, and back-surface mortar coverage.
  • Joint check: confirm repointed joints match the adjacent profile, color, and texture and are fully compacted.
  • Recurrence check: confirm the repair area and immediate surroundings show no new displacement, cracking, or movement at final inspection.

Complete the Repair Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before closing out this repair.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Crack Was Reclassified as Cosmetic After Removal Was Already Underway

Probable cause: the straightedge and level checks for displacement were skipped or performed after chiseling began rather than before.
Confirmation: compare the timestamp on the Crack Evaluation Record against the timestamp on the removal photographs; a classification recorded after removal photographs indicates the sequence was reversed.
Correction: stop the repair, complete a full displacement check on the remaining crack and surrounding wall, and reclassify honestly using the stop-work criteria.
Reinspection: if reclassified Structural — Stop-Work, halt further work and refer the condition to a qualified professional before proceeding.

A Concealed Tie or Flashing Defect Was Covered by the Replacement Unit

Probable cause: the cavity inspection in Step 6 was rushed or skipped, and the replacement unit was set before the concealed condition was documented.
Confirmation: compare the cavity-inspection photographs against the written findings; a missing photograph or vague description indicates the inspection was incomplete.
Correction: remove the replacement unit, reinspect the cavity fully, and document the concealed condition before proceeding; refer any tie, reinforcement, or flashing defect to a qualified professional.
Reinspection: confirm the referral was made and do not reset the unit until the referred condition is resolved.

The Replacement Unit Does Not Match Adjacent Masonry

Probable cause: the replacement unit was selected without comparing it against the adjacent units in daylight, or a single available unit was used without checking color and texture match.
Confirmation: view the completed repair from normal viewing distance in daylight and compare size, color, and texture against the immediately adjacent units.
Correction: remove the mismatched unit before mortar fully cures and replace it with a correctly matched unit; if no matching unit is available, document the mismatch and add it to the deficiency list for later correction.
Reinspection: repeat the fit and match check once the correct unit is installed.

Mortar Coverage on the Back Surface of the Replacement Unit Is Incomplete

Probable cause: mortar was applied only to the bed and head joints, and the back surface of the unit was pressed against the cavity without a mortar bed.
Confirmation: tap the set unit and listen for a hollow sound, or, before the mortar sets, remove the unit and inspect the back surface for uncovered area.
Correction: remove the unit while the mortar is still workable, reapply mortar to all bearing surfaces including the back, and reset the unit.
Reinspection: repeat the tap-sound check after the mortar has cured.

The Repointed Joint Profile Does Not Match Adjacent Joints

Probable cause: the jointer used did not match the adjacent joint profile, or the joint was tooled before or after the correct thumbprint-hard condition.
Confirmation: compare the repaired joint's profile and depth against an adjacent undisturbed joint using a straightedge or profile gauge.
Correction: where the mortar has not fully cured, retool the joint with the correct profile tool at the correct firmness; where fully cured, remove and repoint the affected joint per Chapter 14.
Reinspection: repeat the profile comparison after correction.

Field Terms

Cosmetic Cracking
Surface mortar cracking with no measurable displacement, stepped pattern, or accompanying structural symptom.
Structural Movement
Displacement, stepped or horizontal cracking, bulging, or instability requiring professional evaluation before repair.
Displacement
A measurable out-of-plane shift between the two sides of a crack.
Replacement Area
The single damaged unit and immediately surrounding joints approved for removal and replacement.
Concealed Cavity
The backing, ties, reinforcement, and flashing behind or beneath a removed masonry unit.
Matching Unit
A replacement unit matching the existing unit's size, color, texture, and finish within the stated tolerance.
Back-Surface Mortar
Mortar applied to the rear bearing surface of a replacement unit to prevent hollow, unsupported placement.
Stepped Cracking
A crack that follows mortar joints in a diagonal, stair-step pattern, typically indicating differential movement.
Professional Referral
The documented act of directing a stop-work condition to an engineer or other qualified professional rather than resolving it in this chapter.
Repair Log
The document recording crack evaluation, cavity inspection, replacement, repointing, and final inspection results for this chapter's repair.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one small masonry step or two-step practice assembly on an approved base while holding the exact rise, run, tread depth, nosing, width, and drainage the approved plans specify. The finished work product is one stable, code-consistent step assembly with uniform risers and treads, secure bearing, controlled drainage, safe nosing, and a documented cure record.

You will use the approved total rise, riser count, tread depth, nosing, width, landing, foundation, drainage, material, and code requirements supplied for the project. Nothing in this chapter authorizes you to invent a rise, run, or tread dimension not supplied by the approved plans, adopted code, or a qualified professional; this chapter builds exactly what those documents specify.

This chapter depends on Chapter 1 readiness planning, Chapter 2 work-area setup, Chapter 3 mortar mixing, and Chapter 4 layout skills, and it uses the Chapter 10 cutting procedure for any riser or tread unit that requires cutting. It feeds directly into the Chapter 20 capstone, which may hand off a completed nonstructural garden-wall or veneer section but does not repeat step construction; the geometry and stability judgment practiced here also applies wherever a masonry step condition is encountered in later work.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-097 — Lay out step dimensions, transferring the approved total rise, riser count, tread depth, nosing, width, and landing dimensions to the work site before excavation begins.
  • RM-098 — Prepare the base, excavating, compacting, and installing the approved aggregate or concrete support beneath the step assembly.
  • RM-099 — Set risers, constructing each riser course to level, straight, and correct elevation with full bearing.
  • RM-100 — Set treads, installing tread units with full bearing, consistent nosing, and the approved drainage slope.
  • RM-101 — Maintain safe rise and run, holding uniform finished rise and tread depth across every step in the assembly.
  • RM-102 — Inspect step stability, verifying bearing, geometry, nosing, slip resistance, drainage, edges, and transitions before the assembly is returned to traffic.

Where This Skill Fits

A masonry step is one of the few masonry assemblies where a small dimensional error becomes an immediate trip hazard rather than a cosmetic defect. The layout discipline from Chapter 4, the base preparation from Chapters 1 and 2, and the mortar and unit-setting skills from Chapters 3, 5, and 8 all converge here, but the acceptance standard is stricter: every riser and every tread in the assembly must match the approved geometry within the stated tolerance, not just within an aesthetic sense of "close enough."

The stop-work judgment you use here — refusing to build a step that cannot achieve the required geometry on the existing base — is the same judgment used in Chapter 16 for garden-wall foundations and in the Chapter 20 capstone for the final handoff inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • laying out step footprint, riser lines, and finished elevations from approved total rise, riser count, tread depth, nosing, width, and landing dimensions
  • excavating and preparing an approved aggregate or concrete base or footing beneath the step assembly
  • dry-laying and adjusting riser and tread units while preserving code-compliant geometry
  • constructing riser courses to level, straightness, and specified elevation with infill, reinforcement, or concrete support where specified
  • setting tread units with full bearing, consistent nosing, and approved drainage slope
  • maintaining uniform finished rise and tread depth across the assembly
  • installing joints, movement details, tooling, cleaning, and cure protection
  • barricading the assembly until it is safe for traffic and inspecting stability, geometry, nosing, slip resistance, drainage, edges, and transitions

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent a total rise, number of risers, tread depth, nosing dimension, width, or landing size that is not supplied by the approved drawings, adopted code, or a qualified professional
  • begin construction if the required geometry cannot be achieved on the existing base or threshold elevation; that condition must be referred, not resolved by adjusting the design in the field
  • build a step assembly serving as the sole means of egress from a dwelling unit, a step assembly over 30 inches in total rise, or any step assembly requiring an engineered foundation, without the exact engineered design and inspection sequence supplied by a qualified professional
  • open the assembly to foot traffic before the barricade is removed and the acceptance standards in this chapter are met

Any base, elevation, or code condition that cannot be resolved within this chapter's scope must be documented and referred to the responsible design or inspection authority before construction begins.

Trade Note

Every rise and tread dimension in a finished step assembly is measured against the story pole and the approved plan at the point each course is built — never adjusted afterward to "average out" an accumulated error. A step assembly with a rise that varies by more than the permitted tolerance between steps is one of the most common causes of trip-and-fall claims in residential masonry, and it cannot be corrected without demolition once the mortar has cured.

Setup Standard

Do not begin excavation or base preparation until the total rise, number of risers, tread depth, nosing, width, landing, foundation, drainage, and code requirements have been confirmed on the approved drawings. If the required geometry cannot be achieved on the existing base or threshold elevation, stop and refer the condition before construction begins.

Shop Tip

Build and check the story pole for the step assembly before the first shovel of excavation. A story pole built after the base is already dug encourages adjusting the dig to fit a convenient rise instead of the specified one.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every masonry step assembly, from a single stone step to a full stair run, depends on the same governing relationship: total rise divided among a fixed number of equal risers, and a tread depth held constant across every step. A rise that grows or shrinks from step to step, or a nosing that varies across the width of the tread, produces a hazard that cannot be corrected after the mortar cures. Confirm the geometry on paper and on the story pole before any unit is set.

Geometry Confirmation Before Excavation

Confirm the approved total rise, number of risers, tread depth, nosing, width, and landing dimensions against the existing base and adjacent threshold elevations before any excavation begins. If the measured field condition does not allow the specified geometry to be achieved — for example, an existing threshold elevation that produces a top riser taller or shorter than every other riser — stop and refer the discrepancy to the responsible design authority. Do not adjust the specified rise or run in the field to make the existing condition work.

Measurement Cue — Story Pole Governs Every Course

Build the story pole from the actual riser and tread unit height plus the specified joint thickness before excavation begins. Mark every riser, tread, and finished elevation on the pole. Check every course against the story pole as it is built; do not rely on a running tape measurement from the base, which allows small joint variations to accumulate into an out-of-tolerance final riser.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Begin if Geometry Cannot Be Achieved

Do not begin construction if the required rise, run, nosing, width, or landing dimension cannot be achieved on the existing base or threshold elevation. Do not improvise a nonuniform riser or tread to compensate for a field discrepancy. Stop, document the discrepancy, and refer it to the responsible design or inspection authority before proceeding.

Field Safety Cue — Barricade Before and After Construction

Barricade the work area during excavation, base preparation, and unit setting to prevent foot traffic across an incomplete or uncured assembly. Keep the barricade in place after construction until the assembly is safe for traffic per the acceptance standards in this chapter; do not remove it based on visual appearance alone.

Code Alert — Rise, Run, and Width Requirements Vary

Maximum riser height, minimum tread depth, riser-to-tread uniformity tolerance, minimum width, nosing dimensions, and landing requirements vary by jurisdiction and by whether the step assembly serves as a required means of egress. Verify the requirements that apply to this specific project locally rather than assuming the requirements of a prior project apply.

E. Layout — Geometry, Utilities, and Footprint

  1. Review approved total rise, number of risers, tread depth, nosing, width, landing, foundation, drainage, material, and code requirements. Record every value on the Project Identification Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm the base and adjacent threshold elevations. Measure the existing lower grade and upper threshold elevation and compare the difference against the approved total rise.
  3. Do not begin if the required geometry cannot be achieved. If the measured elevations do not support the specified rise, run, and riser count, stop and refer the discrepancy before proceeding.
  4. Locate utilities and verify door and landing clearances. Confirm no buried utility conflicts the excavation and that the finished landing does not obstruct door swing or required clearances.
  5. Lay out step footprint, riser lines, and finished elevations. Mark the footprint, each riser line, and the finished elevation of each tread on the ground using the approved dimensions.

F. Base Preparation

  1. Excavate and prepare the approved base or footing. Excavate to the depth and footprint required by the approved foundation detail.
  2. Compact subgrade and install aggregate or concrete support. Compact the subgrade, then place and compact the specified aggregate base or place and cure the specified concrete footing.
  3. Verify base level and drainage. Check the prepared base for level and confirm it directs water away from the step assembly rather than trapping it beneath the risers.

G. Riser and Tread Construction

  1. Dry-lay riser and tread units. Dry-lay a representative sample of riser and tread units on the prepared base without mortar.
  2. Adjust module while preserving code-compliant geometry. Adjust unit spacing and joint width only within limits that keep every riser and tread within the approved geometry; do not change the specified rise, run, or riser count to reduce cutting.
  3. Construct the first riser or support course. Spread mortar or setting bed on the prepared base and set the first riser course using the story pole and mason's line.
  4. Check level, straightness, and elevation. Verify the first riser course against the story pole, the 4-foot level, and the mason's line before proceeding.
  5. Install infill, reinforcement, or concrete support as specified. Place any infill, reinforcement, or concrete backing required by the approved detail before covering it with the next course.
  6. Set tread units with full bearing. Spread mortar or setting bed across the full riser-course top and set tread units with complete contact; do not bridge tread units over unsupported infill.
  7. Maintain consistent nosing. Set every tread's leading edge to the same nosing projection over the riser face below it, checked with the tape measure at both ends and the center of each tread.
  8. Build the next riser and tread where included. Repeat the riser and tread sequence for each additional step in the assembly, rechecking the story pole at every course.

H. Drainage, Finishing, and Protection

  1. Slope treads or landings for approved drainage without creating an unsafe cross slope. Set the drainage slope specified in the approved drawings; do not exceed the maximum cross slope stated for safe foot traffic.
  2. Maintain uniform finished rise and tread depth. Recheck every riser and tread against the story pole once all courses are set, before mortar reaches final set.
  3. Install joints and movement details. Fill and tool every bed, head, and riser-to-tread joint to the specified profile; install movement joints where the approved detail requires them.
  4. Tool and clean mortar. Tool joints when thumbprint hard and brush clean without smearing mortar onto tread or riser faces.
  5. Protect and cure. Cover and protect the assembly from rain, freezing, rapid drying, heat, and impact for the specified cure period.
  6. Barricade steps until safe for traffic. Maintain the barricade through the full cure period; do not allow foot traffic before the acceptance standards in this chapter are met.

I. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect stability, geometry, nosing, slip, drainage, edges, and transitions. Verify every acceptance standard in Section D using the tape measure, story pole, level, and straightedge.
  2. Document final rise, run, width, and cure. Record the as-built rise, run, width, nosing, cure dates, weather conditions during cure, and the final inspection result.

Common Step Assembly Inspection

Inspect every step assembly using the same checks regardless of unit material or riser count.

  • Geometry check: confirm total rise, riser count, riser height, tread depth, and nosing match the approved drawings and the story pole exactly, with no unit-to-unit variation beyond the stated tolerance.
  • Bearing check: confirm every riser and tread has full bearing on the approved base or supporting course, with no bridging over unsupported infill.
  • Level and alignment check: confirm every riser and tread is level along its length and aligned across the assembly width using the mason's line and straightedge.
  • Drainage check: confirm the approved drainage slope is present on treads and landing and that no standing water remains after the drainage test.
  • Joint and finish check: confirm every joint is fully tooled, cleaned, and free of voids at riser-to-tread transitions.

Complete the Step Assembly Inspection Checklist below before removing the barricade.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Riser Height Varies From Step to Step

Probable cause: courses were built by running tape measurement from the base rather than checking each course against the story pole, allowing small joint-thickness variations to accumulate.
Confirmation: measure every riser individually against the story pole rather than against the base; compare each measurement to the specified riser height and the stated tolerance.
Correction: if any riser exceeds the tolerance, the affected course must be removed and rebuilt to the story-pole elevation; do not attempt to compensate by adjusting an adjacent riser.
Reinspection: recheck every riser in the assembly against the story pole after the correction, not only the corrected riser.

Tread Nosing Is Inconsistent Across the Width

Probable cause: the tread unit was set without checking nosing projection at both ends and the center, allowing the unit to twist or shift before the mortar set.
Confirmation: measure nosing projection at both ends and the center of every tread and compare to the specified dimension.
Correction: reset the affected tread unit in fresh mortar with the nosing checked and held at all three points before the bed sets.
Reinspection: recheck nosing at all three points on the corrected tread and on adjacent treads for consistency.

Water Stands on a Tread or Landing After the Drainage Test

Probable cause: the tread or landing was set level or with reversed slope instead of the specified drainage slope.
Confirmation: check the tread or landing slope with the level against the approved drainage detail; confirm the standing water location and depth.
Correction: remove and reset the affected tread or landing unit with the correct slope; do not attempt to correct drainage with a sealer or surface coating.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage test on the corrected surface and on adjacent treads.

A Riser or Tread Bridges Over Unsupported Infill

Probable cause: infill, reinforcement, or concrete support specified in the approved detail was omitted or not allowed to cure before the next course was loaded.
Confirmation: inspect beneath the affected unit for voids, uncured infill, or missing reinforcement; compare against the approved detail.
Correction: remove the affected unit, install the missing infill, reinforcement, or support exactly as specified, allow the required cure, and reset the unit with full bearing.
Reinspection: confirm full bearing on the corrected unit and reinspect adjacent units for the same deficiency.

Field Conditions Do Not Match the Approved Geometry

Probable cause: the existing base or threshold elevation was not verified against the approved total rise before excavation began.
Confirmation: remeasure the existing lower grade and upper threshold elevation and compare the difference to the approved total rise and riser count.
Correction: stop work on the affected item and refer the discrepancy to the responsible design authority; do not adjust the specified rise, run, or riser count in the field.
Reinspection: confirm the referral is resolved and a revised or reconfirmed geometry is issued before construction resumes.

Field Terms

Total Rise
The full vertical distance from the base or lower finished grade to the upper finished threshold or landing elevation.
Riser Height
The vertical face dimension of one step, measured finished-tread-surface to finished-tread-surface.
Tread Depth (Run)
The horizontal walking-surface dimension of one step, measured nosing to nosing.
Nosing
The projection of a tread's leading edge beyond the riser face below it.
Story Pole
A reference pole marked with each riser, tread, and finished elevation for a specific step assembly.
Landing
A level platform at the top, bottom, or midpoint of a step assembly.
Infill
Aggregate, concrete, or other approved material placed inside a riser course to support the tread above it.
Cross Slope
The side-to-side slope of a tread or landing surface, controlled to drain water without creating an unsafe walking surface.
Bearing
Full, supported contact between a masonry unit and the base or course beneath it.
Stop-Work Condition
A base, elevation, or code condition that prevents the specified step geometry from being achieved and must be referred before work proceeds.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to diagnose deteriorated mortar joints and complete one controlled repointing repair in a brick or block wall. The finished work product is one repaired joint area with deteriorated mortar removed to the specified depth, joint cavities cleaned and dampened as required, compatible repointing mortar packed in controlled lifts, joints tooled to match the surrounding profile and texture, and a fully documented cure and inspection record.

You will inspect an existing wall for mortar deterioration, movement, and moisture damage before touching a single joint. Repointing is a compatibility discipline as much as a mortar-application skill: the replacement mortar must be no harder and no less permeable than the mortar it replaces, or it can trap moisture and accelerate damage to the surrounding units. This chapter treats every joint as part of the wall's water-management system, not as an isolated cosmetic patch.

This chapter follows Chapter 13, which establishes flashing and drainage details, and precedes Chapter 15, which addresses cracked-unit repair. Repointing does not correct settlement, structural movement, or unresolved water entry; those conditions are identified here and referred, not repaired, under this chapter's scope.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-079 — Identify failed mortar, distinguishing surface deterioration, erosion, and cracking from structural movement or moisture-driven failure.
  • RM-080 — Remove deteriorated joints to the specified depth using hand tools or controlled power tools without widening joints or damaging unit edges.
  • RM-081 — Clean joint cavities of loose mortar, dust, and debris using HEPA-controlled methods and dampen as required.
  • RM-082 — Pack new mortar into cleaned joints in controlled, compacted lifts to the specified depth and profile.
  • RM-083 — Tool replacement joints at the correct firmness to match the surrounding joint profile and texture.
  • RM-084 — Inspect repointing quality for bond, depth, compaction, edge condition, color, profile, and cleanliness, and document the completed repair.

Where This Skill Fits

Repointing depends on the mortar-mixing discipline built in Chapter 3, the silica and dust controls established in Chapter 2, and the jointing and tooling skills practiced in Chapters 5 and 8. It applies those skills to existing masonry rather than new construction, adding the compatibility, diagnosis, and cavity-preparation steps unique to repair work.

The stop-work judgment exercised in this chapter — recognizing when deterioration is a movement or moisture symptom rather than an ordinary mortar-age condition — is the same judgment carried into Chapter 15's crack repair and Chapter 18's chimney inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • inspecting a wall for cracking, bulging, movement, loose units, moisture, salts, failed flashing, and mortar deterioration
  • identifying original unit type, mortar hardness, color, aggregate, profile, and joint dimensions
  • selecting compatible repointing mortar and establishing dust and silica controls
  • marking repair limits and protecting adjacent units and finishes
  • removing deteriorated mortar to the specified depth without widening joints or damaging unit edges
  • cleaning joint cavities with HEPA equipment and preparing them with the required moisture condition
  • mixing small repointing batches and packing mortar in controlled, compacted lifts
  • tooling joints at the correct firmness to match adjacent profile and texture
  • protecting the repair through cure and inspecting the finished joints

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • repoint any area where deterioration is caused by structural movement, foundation settlement, or unresolved water entry; those conditions are documented and referred, not repointed, under this chapter
  • replace cracked, spalled, or missing masonry units; unit replacement belongs to Chapter 15
  • alter chimney flues, liners, structural elements, or major veneer systems under this chapter's repointing scope
  • use mortar harder or less permeable than the original mortar, or select a repointing mortar without confirming compatibility with the existing unit and mortar type

Any wall showing deterioration caused by structural movement, active water entry, or a condition beyond the Acceptance Standards table below must be documented, corrected or referred as required, and reinspected before repointing proceeds on that area.

Trade Note

Repointing mortar that is harder than the surrounding masonry does not fail immediately. It fails slowly, by forcing movement and moisture cycling into the softer original units and bricks instead of the joint, where deterioration is designed to occur. Confirming mortar compatibility before mixing is the single decision that prevents this delayed failure.

Setup Standard

Do not begin mortar removal until the wall inspection in Step 1 is complete, any structural-movement or unresolved-moisture condition has been referred, and the compatible repointing mortar has been selected against the original mortar's hardness, color, and aggregate. A repair area with an unresolved stop-work condition must not be repointed.

Shop Tip

Remove a small test length of joint first and compare the exposed original mortar's color, aggregate, and hardness against the proposed repointing mortar before committing to the full repair area. A mismatch found after packing the whole area means removing and repacking work that has already cured.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every repointing repair in this book follows the same sequence: diagnose the cause of deterioration, confirm mortar compatibility, remove to the specified depth without damaging units, clean and prepare the cavity, pack and compact in controlled lifts, tool to match the surrounding work, and protect the cure. Skipping the diagnosis step is the most common cause of repeat failures in repointing work.

Diagnosis Before Removal

Every repointing repair begins with the same question: is this ordinary mortar-age deterioration, or a symptom of movement or water entry that repointing cannot correct? Step 1 through Step 4 of the work order below exist to answer that question before a single joint is opened. Record the finding, and do not proceed to removal on any item still classified as a stop-work condition.

Measurement Cue — Record Removal Depth at Each Joint

Measure and record removal depth directly at the joint being worked, not as a single estimated value applied to the whole repair area. Joint depth can vary across a wall; confirm the specified depth is reached at each section before cleaning and packing.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Movement or Structural Causes

Stop repointing any area where deterioration is caused by structural movement, active settlement, bulging, widespread cracking, or unresolved water entry. Do not repoint over these conditions to improve appearance. Photograph the condition, document it on the Repointing Record, and refer it to the responsible design or inspection authority before any removal begins on that section.

Field Safety Cue — Silica Control During Removal

Use wet cutting or local exhaust with HEPA capture whenever a power tool is used for joint removal. Do not dry-cut, dry-grind, or dry-brush deteriorated mortar in a way that releases fine dust into occupied or outdoor air. Vacuum with HEPA equipment before assuming a joint cavity is clean.

Manufacturer Cue — Packaged Repointing Mortar

Follow the manufacturer's mixing ratio, working time, and cure instructions for the selected packaged repointing mortar exactly. Do not substitute a field-proportioned mix for a specified proprietary product without confirming compatibility with the original masonry.

E. Preparation — Diagnosis and Mortar Selection

  1. Inspect the wall for cracking, bulging, movement, loose units, moisture, salts, failed flashing, and mortar deterioration. Photograph every condition found before marking the repair limits.
  2. Stop work if deterioration is caused by structural movement or unresolved water entry. Document the condition and refer it to the responsible design or inspection authority before proceeding.
  3. Identify original unit type, mortar hardness, color, aggregate, profile, and joint dimensions. Record each on the Project Identification Record.
  4. Select compatible repointing mortar. Confirm hardness, permeability, color, and aggregate against the original mortar using a small test-area removal where needed.
  5. Do not use mortar harder or less permeable than permitted for the existing masonry. Reject any proposed mortar that fails this comparison and select a compatible alternative before proceeding.

F. Work-Area Setup and Marking

  1. Establish dust and silica controls. Set up wet-cutting or local-exhaust methods and HEPA vacuum equipment before opening any joint.
  2. Protect adjacent units and finishes. Cover or mask surfaces within the splash and dust radius of the repair area.
  3. Mark repair limits. Outline the approved repair area on the wall and record its dimensions on the Repointing Record.

G. Removal and Cavity Preparation

  1. Remove deteriorated mortar to the specified depth using hand tools or controlled power tools. Work joint by joint within the marked repair limits, measuring depth as you proceed.
  2. Avoid widening joints or damaging unit edges. Control chisel and grinder contact to stay within the original joint width.
  3. Remove mortar completely from corners and back surfaces. Confirm no deteriorated mortar remains at the back of the joint before cleaning.
  4. Vacuum dust using HEPA equipment. Clear all loose mortar, dust, and debris from the joint cavity.
  5. Rinse or dampen joints where required. Apply water only as specified by the repointing mortar's mixing and bonding requirements.
  6. Allow excessive surface water to dissipate. Confirm no standing water remains in the joint before packing begins.

H. Packing, Tooling, and Cure Protection

  1. Mix small repointing batches. Size each batch to the working time of the selected mortar and the area that can be packed before it begins to stiffen.
  2. Pack mortar into the back of the joint in controlled lifts. Use the pointing trowel or tuck pointer to fully engage the back of the cavity first.
  3. Compact each lift before adding the next. Confirm each lift is firm and void-free before placing additional mortar.
  4. Fill the joint to the specified depth and profile. Bring the final lift flush with the surrounding original joint surface.
  5. Tool the joint when properly firm. Wait until the mortar reaches the correct thumbprint-hard condition before tooling.
  6. Match adjacent joint profile and texture. Use the jointer identified for the original profile and work in the same direction as the surrounding work.
  7. Brush loose crumbs after tooling. Use the stiff or soft brush without smearing mortar onto unit faces.
  8. Protect the repair from rapid drying, rain, heat, and freezing. Install curing covers for the full period stated in the mortar's cure schedule.

I. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect bond, depth, compaction, edge damage, color, profile, and cleanliness. Compare every result against the Acceptance Standards table.
  2. Document mortar and cure. Record the mortar product or mix, removal depths, cure method and duration, and the final inspection result on the Repointing Record.

Common Repointing Inspection

Inspect every repointing repair using the same checks regardless of the wall or unit type.

  • Diagnosis check: confirm the deterioration cause was recorded before removal began and any structural or moisture-related stop-work condition was referred, not repointed over.
  • Compatibility check: confirm the repointing mortar's hardness, permeability, color, and aggregate were compared against the original mortar before the full repair proceeded.
  • Removal check: confirm removal depth was measured at each joint section and no joint was widened or unit edge damaged.
  • Packing check: confirm mortar was packed in compacted lifts to the back of the joint with no voids, and tooled to match the surrounding profile.
  • Cure check: confirm the repair was protected for the full specified cure period before cover removal.

Complete the Repointing Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning tools to storage.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Repointed Joints Crack or Debond Within a Short Period

Probable cause: removal depth was insufficient, leaving old deteriorated mortar behind the new mortar with no bond to the unit.
Confirmation: probe the failed joint and compare the actual removal depth achieved against the specified depth recorded on the Repointing Record.
Correction: remove the failed repair mortar and any remaining original deteriorated mortar to the full specified depth, clean the cavity, and repack.
Reinspection: repeat the removal-depth and cavity-cleanliness checks before repacking.

Repointing Mortar Is Visibly Harder Than Surrounding Original Mortar

Probable cause: the repointing mortar was selected without a compatibility comparison against the original mortar's hardness and permeability.
Confirmation: compare a small removed sample of the repointing mortar against a sample of the original mortar for relative hardness.
Correction: remove the incompatible repointing mortar and repack the joint with a mortar matched to the original hardness and permeability.
Reinspection: repeat the mortar-match check before repacking and document the corrected mortar selection.

Repointed Joints Do Not Match Surrounding Color or Profile

Probable cause: the joint was tooled before confirming the jointer profile matched adjacent original work, or the mortar color was not compared before mixing the full batch.
Confirmation: compare the completed repair against adjacent original joints in daylight, checking both profile shape and color.
Correction: where the mismatch is in profile only, retool while the mortar remains workable; where color is mismatched, document the condition and consult the repair specification for an approved correction.
Reinspection: repeat the profile and color comparison after correction.

Voids Are Found Behind the Packed Mortar

Probable cause: mortar was packed in a single thick lift instead of controlled, compacted lifts, trapping air at the back of the joint.
Confirmation: probe or sound the joint by tapping; a hollow response indicates a void behind the surface.
Correction: remove the affected mortar, clean the cavity again, and repack in thinner, fully compacted lifts.
Reinspection: repeat the compaction check by probing the repacked joint before tooling.

Mortar Smears Remain on Unit Faces After Cleaning

Probable cause: mortar was brushed before it reached the correct firmness, or excess mortar was not removed promptly during packing.
Confirmation: inspect unit faces adjacent to the repair under raking light for a thin mortar film or haze.
Correction: remove fresh smears with a damp brush immediately; remove cured smears using the least aggressive compatible cleaning method from Chapter 19 after sufficient cure.
Reinspection: repeat the cleanliness check once the smear has been removed.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, cured repair, not a joint that merely looks finished.

  • Confirm every photograph is labeled with the item, location, and date it documents
  • File the completed Repointing Record with the wall's inspection history and any professional referrals made
  • Remove and dispose of collected mortar dust and slurry without drain discharge
  • Clean and store chisels, jointers, brushes, mixing tools, and the HEPA vacuum
  • Leave curing covers in place for the full specified cure period before removal

Field Terms

Repointing
The removal and replacement of deteriorated mortar joints in existing masonry with compatible new mortar.
Compatible Mortar
Repointing mortar no harder and no less permeable than the original mortar, matched in color and aggregate.
Removal Depth
The specified depth of deteriorated mortar removed from a joint, measured from the exposed unit face.
Lift
One controlled layer of packed mortar placed and compacted before the next layer is added.
Thumbprint-Hard
The firmness at which mortar can be tooled: firm enough to hold a thumbprint without collapsing, soft enough to compress and finish.
Plugging Chisel
A narrow hand chisel used to remove deteriorated mortar from a joint without damaging unit edges.
Joint Profile
The finished cross-sectional shape of a tooled mortar joint, such as concave, flush, or struck.
Efflorescence
A white salt deposit on masonry surfaces caused by moisture carrying soluble salts to the surface as it evaporates.
Repointing Record
The document recording diagnosis, mortar selection, removal depth, packing, tooling, cure, and inspection results for a repointing repair.
Stop-Work Condition
A deterioration cause serious enough to halt repointing of an area until a qualified professional evaluates it.

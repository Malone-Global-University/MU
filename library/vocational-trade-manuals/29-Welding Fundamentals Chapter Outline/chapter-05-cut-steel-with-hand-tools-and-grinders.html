Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to measure, mark, cut, deburr, and inspect a set of mild-steel coupon blanks using hand and power cutting tools. You will work from the cut list produced in Chapter 4, select the correct cutting tool for each cut, inspect the tool and its blade or wheel before use, secure the stock, control the spark and drop-piece path, make each cut with stable two-hand control, and inspect and deburr every finished blank.

This chapter takes place inside the welding area established in Chapter 1, using the PPE selected in Chapter 2 and the equipment identified in Chapter 3, and it cuts the steel prepared for dimensioning in Chapter 4. The blanks produced here carry forward into Chapter 6, where their edges and weld zones are ground and finished, and ultimately into the SMAW, GMAW, and GTAW practice chapters that follow.

No welding takes place in this chapter. This chapter authorizes only measuring, marking, cutting with the approved hand or power tool, deburring, and documenting cut quality.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-025 — Mark steel cuts from an approved reference edge and cut list.
  • WF-026 — Secure material against movement, pinching, and rotation before cutting.
  • WF-027 — Select the cutting tool appropriate to the stock, cut, and required control.
  • WF-028 — Use abrasive wheels safely, including inspection, mounting, and controlled operation.
  • WF-029 — Deburr cut edges to remove handling hazards without altering required dimensions.
  • WF-030 — Inspect cut quality for length, squareness, and edge condition and document the result.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 measured, marked, and cleaned the stock and established the cut list this chapter follows. This chapter turns that marked stock into individually cut blanks. Chapter 6 grinds, deburrs further, and finishes the edges and weld zones of the blanks produced here. Every SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, and GTAW practice chapter that follows welds on coupons cut and inspected in this chapter, and the Chapter 20 capstone repeats this cutting sequence at full scale for the shop stand or utility frame.

The tool-selection, securing, and spark-control sequence taught here is the same sequence used every time steel is cut later in this book, including during capstone fabrication.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the cut list, stock dimensions, tolerances, material, and permitted tools before cutting begins
  • selecting a hacksaw, portable band saw, chop saw, or angle grinder with an approved cut-off wheel for each individual cut
  • inspecting the selected tool, guard, blade, or wheel before operation
  • marking cut lines from an approved reference edge and identifying the waste side of each line
  • securing stock in a vise, clamp, or fixture and controlling the spark and drop-piece path
  • making each cut with stable two-hand control and stopping for binding, shifting, or guard contact
  • deburring, measuring, and documenting each finished blank

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • strike a welding arc; no welding occurs in this chapter
  • bevel or joint-prepare cut edges beyond simple deburring; bevel and joint preparation beyond deburring is covered in Chapter 6
  • cut unknown, galvanized, plated, lead-coated, painted, chemically treated, or otherwise prohibited material; stop and resolve material identification before cutting
  • service, repair, or modify a cutting tool beyond normal operator setup; tool service beyond blade or wheel changes requires qualified personnel

Any tool, wheel, blade, or stock-support condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected before cutting proceeds.

Trade Note

The fastest available cutting tool is not automatically the correct choice. A tool is correct for a cut only when it can also control the stock, the spark path, and the drop piece safely. Choosing a chop saw or angle grinder for a piece too small to hold safely, rather than a hacksaw or band saw that keeps hands farther from the cutting edge, is the most common tool-selection error in this chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not select or start a cutting tool until the stock is measured, marked, and can be secured without holding it by hand near the cutting path. Do not change a wheel or blade without first disconnecting or isolating the tool's power source.

Shop Tip

Mark every cut line from the same single reference edge used in Chapter 4, not from the nearest convenient edge on the bench. Cutting from a mixed set of reference edges is the most common cause of cumulative length error across a set of coupons.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before cutting begins, and do not change them without re-marking the affected stock.

Tool Selection and Stock Control

Select a cutting tool for each cut based on whether it can also control the stock and the spark path safely, not on which tool is fastest to reach. A tool that cuts quickly but cannot be paired with a vise, clamp, or fixture for the piece at hand is not the correct selection for that cut.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Hand-Hold Stock Near the Cutting Path

Do not hold a workpiece by hand near the cutting path of any saw, chop saw, or angle grinder. Secure every piece in a vise, clamp, or machine fixture, or support round stock against rolling and rotation, before starting the tool.

Safety Cue — Wheel and Blade Inspection Before Every Session

Inspect the selected tool, guard, switch, cord, handle, vise, base, and blade or wheel before each cutting session, not only when a new blade or wheel is installed. Verify wheel or blade type, size, speed rating, direction, and material compatibility every time.

Critical Safety Cue — Disconnect Power Before Changing a Wheel or Blade

Disconnect or isolate the tool's power source before installing or changing a wheel or blade. Mount the wheel or blade with the correct flanges, fasteners, and guard position, then run it briefly at no-load speed from a protected position before contacting material.

Field Safety Cue — Control the Spark and Drop-Piece Path

Plan the body position, tool path, spark path, and drop-piece movement before starting the cut. Keep others outside the spark and fragment zone, and support the offcut as the cut completes so it does not fall uncontrolled.

Measurement Cue — Mark from One Reference Edge and Identify Waste

Measure and mark cut lines from the approved reference edge established in Chapter 4, and mark the waste side of every line before cutting. Guide the tool to remove material only from the waste side.

E. Preparation — Cut List and Area Review

  1. Review the cut list, stock dimensions, tolerances, material, and permitted tools. Confirm every piece on the list is accounted for before cutting begins.
  2. Confirm the steel is identified and free from prohibited coating or contamination. Stop and resolve any unidentified, galvanized, plated, painted, or oily material before continuing.
  3. Inspect the work area for spark, fire, electrical, and public-exposure hazards. Confirm the area remains within the Chapter 1 readiness boundary.
  4. Set grinding screens and spark containment. Position screens around every powered cutting station before starting a tool.
  5. Stage safety glasses, face shield, hearing protection, gloves, flame-resistant clothing, and footwear. Confirm PPE is on and correctly fitted before any tool is selected.

F. Tool Selection and Inspection

  1. Select a hacksaw, portable band saw, chop saw, angle grinder, or other approved cutting tool for each cut. Base the selection on the piece size, quantity, and required control, not on convenience alone.
  2. Do not select a tool merely because it is faster when it cannot control the stock safely. Reject a tool choice that cannot be paired with adequate securing for the piece at hand.
  3. Inspect the selected tool, guard, switch, cord, handle, vise, base, and blade or wheel. Confirm every component is present, undamaged, and correctly assembled.
  4. Verify wheel or blade type, size, speed rating, direction, and material compatibility. Confirm the rating meets or exceeds the tool's rated speed.
  5. Disconnect power before installing or changing a wheel or blade. Confirm the tool is fully isolated before touching the mounting hardware.
  6. Mount the wheel or blade with correct flanges, fasteners, and guard position. Confirm the guard remains positioned between the wheel or blade and the operator.
  7. Allow a newly mounted abrasive wheel to run at safe no-load speed from a protected position. Stand clear of the wheel's plane during the check.
  8. Stop for vibration, wobble, cracking, unusual noise, or damaged mounting. Do not proceed to cutting until the condition is corrected and rechecked.

G. Marking, Securing, and Cutting

  1. Measure and mark cut lines from an approved reference edge. Use the same reference edge established for the piece in Chapter 4.
  2. Mark the waste side of each line. Confirm the mark is visible from the operator's cutting position.
  3. Support the stock on both sides as needed without pinching the wheel or blade. Confirm support does not close the kerf against the cutting edge.
  4. Secure short or small pieces in a vise, clamp, or machine fixture. Confirm the piece cannot shift under cutting pressure.
  5. Do not hold the workpiece by hand near the cutting path. Reposition the fixture or clamp rather than holding the piece by hand.
  6. Position round stock against rolling and rotation. Use a V-block, clamp, or equivalent support for round sections.
  7. Plan the body position, tool path, spark path, and drop-piece movement. Confirm the plan before the tool contacts material.
  8. Keep others outside the spark and fragment zone. Confirm the area is clear before starting the tool.
  9. Start the tool before contacting the material. Do not begin the cut with the blade or wheel already touching the stock.
  10. Bring the wheel or blade into the cut gradually without binding or side loading. Avoid forcing the tool into the material.
  11. Maintain stable two-hand control of portable tools. Keep both hands on the designated grip locations throughout the cut.
  12. Do not twist a cutting wheel in the kerf. Keep the wheel traveling in a single plane through the cut.
  13. Allow the tool to cut at its designed rate. Do not force the feed rate beyond what the tool and wheel or blade can sustain.
  14. Stop if the stock shifts, the wheel binds, the guard contacts material, or sparks reach an unsafe area. Correct the condition before resuming the cut.
  15. Support the offcut as the cut completes. Confirm the drop piece does not fall uncontrolled as the final material separates.
  16. Place cut pieces in the marked hot-metal or cooling area. Treat every freshly cut piece as hot until verified otherwise.
  17. Allow the tool to stop fully before setting it down. Do not set down a tool with a coasting blade or wheel.
  18. Disconnect or isolate the tool before adjustment. Confirm isolation before changing any setting between cuts.

H. Deburring, Inspection, and Documentation

  1. Inspect each cut for length, squareness, burrs, gouges, excessive heat damage, and edge quality. Compare each measurement against the cut list tolerance.
  2. Deburr edges with an approved file or grinder. Remove sharp handling edges along the full length of each cut.
  3. Remove sharp corners that create handling hazards while preserving required dimensions. Do not remove material beyond what the handling-hazard correction requires.
  4. Measure finished blanks and mark rejected pieces. Identify any piece outside tolerance for remaking rather than forcing it into later fit-up.
  5. Clean metal dust and abrasive debris using the approved method. Clear the cutting station before moving to the next tool or piece.
  6. Inspect for hidden sparks or smoldering material. Confirm the cooling area and surrounding surfaces are clear before leaving the station unattended.
  7. Document tool, wheel or blade, material, dimensions, cut quality, defects, and corrective action. Complete the cut-quality inspection record for the full set before proceeding to Chapter 6.

Common Cut Inspection

Inspect every finished blank using the same checks regardless of which tool produced the cut.

  • Length check: confirm each cut length matches the cut list within its stated tolerance.
  • Squareness check: confirm the cut face is square to the reference edge within its stated tolerance.
  • Edge check: confirm burrs, gouges, and excessive heat damage have been removed by deburring.
  • Tool check: confirm the tool, guard, and blade or wheel were inspected and passed the no-load check before the cut was made.
  • Security check: confirm the piece was secured or supported, not hand-held, during the cut.
  • Documentation check: confirm the cut-quality inspection record is complete for every piece on the cut list.

Complete the Cut-Quality Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 6.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Finished Blank Is Outside the Length Tolerance

Probable cause: the cut line was marked from an inconsistent reference edge, or the tool drifted off the marked line during the cut.
Confirmation: re-measure the piece from the original reference edge and compare it against the cut list.
Correction: remake the piece from new stock if insufficient length remains; do not force an undersized piece into later fit-up.
Reinspection: repeat the length and squareness check on the replacement piece.

The Cut Face Is Not Square to the Reference Edge

Probable cause: the stock shifted in the vise or fixture during the cut, or the tool was not guided along a single plane.
Confirmation: check the cut face against a square referenced from the same edge used for marking.
Correction: reface the cut end if sufficient length remains, or remake the piece.
Reinspection: repeat the squareness check after correction.

The Abrasive Wheel Binds or Wobbles During the No-Load Check

Probable cause: the wheel is cracked, incorrectly mounted, or mismatched to the tool's arbor or speed rating.
Confirmation: stop the tool, disconnect power, and inspect the wheel, flanges, and mounting hardware.
Correction: replace the wheel with one confirmed compatible with the tool, and remount it with the correct flanges and tightening method.
Reinspection: repeat the no-load check from a protected position before cutting.

Sparks Are Reaching an Unapproved Area

Probable cause: the tool path or body position was not planned before cutting, or screens were not positioned to contain the spark stream.
Confirmation: stop the cut and observe the current spark path relative to the screens and surrounding area.
Correction: reposition the workpiece, tool angle, or screens so the spark stream reaches only the approved collection area.
Reinspection: repeat the spark-path check before resuming the cut.

A Deburred Edge Is Rounded Beyond the Drawing Tolerance

Probable cause: filing or grinding continued past the point needed to remove the handling hazard.
Confirmation: measure the remaining edge dimension against the cut list tolerance.
Correction: remake the piece if the dimension is outside tolerance; do not attempt to add material back.
Reinspection: repeat the edge-condition and dimensional check on the replacement piece.

A Piece Was Cut While Being Hand-Held

Probable cause: no vise, clamp, or fixture was available or set up for the piece size before cutting began.
Confirmation: review the securing method used and compare it against the required stock-control standard.
Correction: stop cutting immediately, set up an appropriate vise, clamp, or fixture, and do not resume until the piece can be secured.
Reinspection: confirm the securing method for every remaining cut before continuing the cut list.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a set of cut, deburred, and inspected blanks, not a welded assembly. Housekeeping here protects the blanks and the cutting stations for later chapters.

  • Disconnect or isolate every cutting tool used during the session
  • Store hacksaws, blades, and cut-off wheels in their labeled locations
  • Sweep or vacuum metal dust and abrasive debris from every cutting station
  • Confirm no smoldering material remains in the cooling or scrap zones
  • Store the completed cut-quality inspection record with the Chapter 4 material records

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to fit, tack, weld, clean, and inspect two SMAW fillet-weld coupons: one lap joint and one T-joint. Both are welded in the flat or horizontal position using the electrode, polarity, and starting amperage range approved for your station and material thickness.

You will set the lap-joint overlap and clamp it against movement, square and fixture the T-joint, place and inspect balanced tack welds, weld each joint with controlled work angle, travel angle, arc length, and travel speed, remove slag completely, and measure the finished fillet with an approved gauge. You will identify defects, determine probable causes, and complete an approved repair only when adequate base material remains.

This chapter builds directly on Chapters 7 and 8, where you learned to strike a controlled arc and run consistent stringer beads. Those same arc-control skills now control a real joint rather than an open bead on flat plate. Chapter 16 later teaches full assembly fit-up and tacking on a multi-part frame; the joint-level practice here is the foundation for that larger sequence.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-049 — Fit a lap joint to a specified overlap and clamp it against movement.
  • WF-050 — Fit a T-joint to a specified angle and hold it with a square and fixture.
  • WF-051 — Tack weld both joints in a planned, balanced sequence.
  • WF-052 — Run SMAW fillet welds with controlled work angle, travel angle, arc length, and travel speed.
  • WF-053 — Control slag coverage and remove slag completely from the fillet and toes.
  • WF-054 — Inspect fillet-weld size, profile, and shape against the approved acceptance sample.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 7 taught arc initiation. Chapter 8 taught a straight, consistent bead on open flat plate. This chapter is the first in the book where that bead must fuse two separate pieces of metal into a joint that carries load. The fit-up, tacking, and sequencing skills taught here reappear in every later joining chapter — GMAW fillet and butt welds in Chapter 12, FCAW beads in Chapter 13, GTAW beads in Chapter 15, and the multi-part fit-up and capstone assembly in Chapters 16, 17, and 20.

The lap and T-joint coupons produced in this chapter remain useful as reference pieces for comparing fillet size and profile against later work.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • fitting and clamping one SMAW lap-joint coupon to the approved overlap
  • fitting and squaring one SMAW T-joint coupon to the specified angle
  • placing, inspecting, and correcting tack welds on both joints
  • welding both joints with controlled parameters, angle, and travel
  • removing slag completely and measuring the finished fillet with an approved gauge
  • identifying and correcting defects using an approved repair method

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • weld a structural, load-bearing, lifting, pressure, or code-controlled fillet joint; the coupons produced here are noncritical training pieces
  • select an electrode classification, amperage, or joint dimension not supplied by the approved welding procedure, instructor, or chapter project specification
  • weld out of position, overhead, or on material thickness outside the approved procedure without additional qualified instruction
  • proceed with hot work before completing the Chapter 1 area readiness record and staging the PPE selected in Chapter 2

Any joint, tack, or completed fillet that fails the acceptance standards in this chapter must be corrected or remade before the coupon is recorded as accepted.

Trade Note

A fillet weld is measured by leg length and throat, not by how continuous or shiny the surface appears. A bead that looks smooth but is undersized, has incomplete fusion at a toe, or shows an unequal leg still fails inspection. Measure every coupon with the approved fillet gauge before recording acceptance.

Setup Standard

Do not strike an arc on either coupon until the welding-area readiness record from Chapter 1 is current, the station passed the equipment-readiness inspection in Chapter 3, and the joint has passed the fit-up check in Section F below. A joint that has not passed fit-up must not be tacked.

Shop Tip

Recheck overlap, angle, gap, squareness, and distortion immediately after tacking and again after welding one side of the T-joint. Heat from tacking and from the first fillet pass can shift a joint that measured correctly before the arc was struck.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before fit-up begins, and do not change them without re-running the affected portion of the fit-up inspection.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every fillet weld in this book, from this chapter's lap and T-joint coupons to the Chapter 20 capstone, follows the same sequence: fit, clamp, tack, inspect the tack, weld, clean, and inspect the finished weld. Learning that sequence on two simple joints here means every later fillet weld starts from a fit-up you already trust.

Lap jointOne member overlaps the other; fillet welded along the overlapping edge
T-jointOne member set at the specified angle to the other; fillet welded at one or both toes
Tack weldSmall weld holding fit-up before final welding; inspected and either incorporated or removed
Final fillet weldContinuous weld fusing both joint members along the full specified length

Fit-Up and Fixturing

Fit-up is established and inspected before any arc is struck. The lap-joint overlap is set with a rule, stop, or fixture and clamped against movement and rocking. The T-joint angle is held with a square and fixture. Both joints are checked for gap, alignment, and squareness before tacking, and rechecked after tacking and after each welded side.

Measurement Cue — Set Overlap and Angle Before Clamping

Set the lap-joint overlap using a rule, stop, or fixture before applying clamping pressure. Set the T-joint angle using a square before tacking. Do not estimate overlap or angle by eye once clamping pressure has been applied — recheck with the same tool used to set it.

Critical Safety Cue — Slag Removal and Eye Protection

Do not chip or brush slag without safety glasses and a face shield in place. Hot slag can eject unpredictably during chipping. Allow the joint to cool enough for safe handling before chipping, and treat every freshly welded coupon as hot until verified otherwise.

Field Safety Cue — Clamp and Fixture Access

Position clamps and fixtures so they do not block electrode angle, arc visibility, or slag-removal access. Confirm the work clamp is attached to clean, sound metal close to the joint and does not pass through the clamp or fixture itself.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Electrode Handling

Keep electrodes dry and identified throughout this chapter. Follow the electrode manufacturer's storage, redrying, and handling instructions. Do not substitute an electrode of unknown classification for the one specified in the coupon record.

F. Preparation — Joint Review and Surface Preparation

  1. Review the joint drawings, coupon dimensions, overlap, orientation, fillet size, length, electrode, polarity, and position. Confirm every value against the coupon record before handling material.
  2. Prepare all faying and weld surfaces to clean metal. Confirm cleaning extends beyond the expected weld and work-clamp area.
  3. Inspect coupon edges, flatness, dimensions, and joint identification. Reject any coupon outside the tolerance recorded in Chapters 4 through 6.

G. Lap-Joint Fit-Up

  1. Set the lap-joint overlap using a rule, stop, or fixture. Confirm the overlap matches the coupon record before clamping.
  2. Clamp the lap joint against movement and rocking. Verify both members remain flat on the table under clamping pressure.

H. T-Joint Fit-Up

  1. Set the T-joint at the specified angle. Confirm the angle matches the coupon record before tacking.
  2. Use a square and fixture to hold the T-joint. Verify the upright member does not shift when the square is removed for arc access.
  3. Confirm access for electrode angle and slag removal. Check that the fixture does not block either toe of the intended fillet.

I. Machine Setup and Tack Welding

  1. Attach the work clamp to clean sound metal. Position the clamp close to the joint without passing through the fixture.
  2. Set the machine and record approved parameters. Record electrode classification, diameter, polarity, and amperage on the coupon traveler.
  3. Place tack welds at the approved locations. Follow the balanced sequence specified in the coupon record for each joint.
  4. Keep tack welds small enough for control but adequate to hold fit-up. Do not place an oversized tack that will be difficult to incorporate into the final weld.
  5. Inspect tacks for cracks, porosity, poor fusion, excessive size, and misalignment. Use the helmet lens and, once cool, direct visual inspection.
  6. Remove and replace unacceptable tacks using the approved repair method. Do not weld over a defective tack.
  7. Recheck overlap, angle, gap, squareness, and distortion after tacking. Compare against the values recorded in Sections G and H.
  8. Plan weld sequence to balance heat and reduce angular distortion. Record the planned sequence for both joints before welding either one.

J. Welding the Lap Joint

  1. Position the lap joint for the approved fillet-weld orientation. Confirm flat or horizontal position matches the coupon record.
  2. Strike the arc on the approved location. Use the start method demonstrated in Chapter 7.
  3. Direct the arc to achieve fusion at both joint members. Observe the puddle at the root and along both surfaces.
  4. Maintain the approved work angle and travel angle. Hold the angle values specified in the coupon record throughout the weld.
  5. Control arc length and travel speed. Apply the arc-length and travel-speed control demonstrated in Chapters 7 and 8.
  6. Observe slag trailing behind the puddle. Confirm the slag pattern matches the approved reference example.
  7. Terminate the weld with controlled crater fill. Use the crater-fill method demonstrated in Chapter 8.
  8. Allow the joint to cool safely. Mark the coupon hot and place it in the designated cooling zone.
  9. Remove slag fully from the fillet and toes. Wear face and eye protection during chipping.
  10. Wire-brush the weld. Clean both the bead and adjacent base metal.
  11. Inspect the lap-joint fillet for size, length, profile, tie-in, undercut, overlap, porosity, slag, start, and stop. Compare against the acceptance standards in Section E.
  12. Measure the fillet using the approved gauge. Record the leg length on the coupon traveler.

K. Welding the T-Joint

  1. Repeat the procedure on the T-joint. Apply the same start, angle, arc-length, and travel-speed control used on the lap joint.
  2. Maintain equal attention to the vertical and horizontal members. Confirm fusion is achieved at both members throughout the weld.
  3. Use the approved sequence if welding both sides. Follow the balanced order recorded in Section I to control distortion.
  4. Monitor and record angular distortion. Measure the joint angle after each welded side and record the result.
  5. Clean and inspect the completed T-joint. Apply the same slag-removal, brushing, and visual-inspection steps used on the lap joint.

L. Comparison, Defect Analysis, and Repair

  1. Compare weld profile with the acceptance example. Place both coupons alongside the approved reference sample.
  2. Identify defects and probable causes. Use the symptom-cause-confirmation pattern from the Failure Points section below.
  3. Prepare a correction plan before any repair welding. Do not begin grinding or rewelding without a defined method.
  4. Complete approved repair only when adequate base material remains. Confirm remaining thickness before excavating a defect.
  5. Reinspect repaired areas. Apply the same visual-inspection and measurement steps used on the original weld.
  6. Record joint dimensions, tacks, parameters, weld size, defects, repair, and final result. Complete the coupon traveler for both joints.
  7. Complete machine shutdown, hot-metal control, and fire watch. Follow the shutdown sequence established in Chapter 1.

Common Area Inspection

Inspect both coupons using the same checks regardless of joint type.

  • Fit-up check: confirm overlap or angle was set and verified before tacking and rechecked after tacking.
  • Tack check: confirm every tack is free of cracks, porosity, poor fusion, and misalignment before final welding.
  • Fillet-size check: confirm measured leg length matches the approved procedure within the stated gauge tolerance.
  • Profile check: confirm consistent width, tie-in at both toes, and no visible undercut, overlap, porosity, or slag inclusion.
  • Cleanliness check: confirm slag is fully removed and the bead is brushed clean before inspection.
  • Documentation check: confirm the coupon traveler is complete for both joints before recording final acceptance.

Complete the Fillet-Weld Inspection Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 10.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Lap Joint Rocks or Shifts During Tacking

Probable cause: the overlap was set but clamping pressure was applied at only one point, allowing the unclamped end to lift.
Confirmation: re-measure the overlap at both ends of the joint and check for a visible gap under the overlapping member.
Correction: reposition clamps at both ends of the overlap and recheck flatness before retacking.
Reinspection: repeat the lap-joint fit-up check in Section G.

T-Joint Angle Changes After the First Tack

Probable cause: the fixture held the angle during setup but did not resist the shrinkage force of the first tack weld.
Confirmation: measure the angle with a square immediately after the first tack and compare to the recorded value.
Correction: loosen the fixture, realign the upright member to the specified angle, and place a balancing tack on the opposite side before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the T-joint fit-up check in Section H after every tack.

One Leg of the Fillet Is Shorter Than the Other

Probable cause: the electrode work angle was not held at the specified bisecting angle, directing more heat and filler toward one member.
Confirmation: measure both legs with the fillet gauge and compare to the approved procedure.
Correction: adjust work angle on the next pass or, if the coupon has adequate remaining material, prepare and reweld the affected length using the approved repair method.
Reinspection: remeasure both legs after correction and compare to the acceptance standard.

Undercut Appears Along One Toe

Probable cause: travel speed was too fast for the amperage, or arc length was too long, reducing filler deposit at the toe.
Confirmation: compare travel speed and arc length used against the approved starting range recorded on the coupon traveler.
Correction: adjust one variable at a time — travel speed or arc length — and run a verification bead on scrap before rewelding the affected length.
Reinspection: repeat the profile check described in Common Area Inspection.

Slag Remains Trapped at the Toe After Brushing

Probable cause: chipping removed the surface slag but did not reach slag trapped in an undercut or overlap condition at the toe.
Confirmation: inspect the toe closely under adequate lighting after brushing and compare against the approved reference sample.
Correction: mechanically remove the trapped slag using the approved method before any further weld pass or coating is applied.
Reinspection: repeat the cleanliness check and the profile check together, since trapped slag often indicates an underlying profile defect.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with two documented, inspected fillet-weld coupons, not scrap for disposal. Housekeeping here protects the coupon record for the chapters that follow.

  • Confirm both coupons have cooled to a safe handling temperature before storage
  • Store electrodes in their approved dry container
  • Coil leads and return the electrode holder to the insulated rest
  • Remove slag debris and spatter from the welding table
  • Store the completed coupon travelers with the readiness and equipment records from earlier chapters

Field Terms

Lap Joint
A joint in which one member overlaps another, fillet welded along the overlapping edge.
T-Joint
A joint in which one member meets another at an angle, fillet welded at one or both toes.
Fillet Leg
The distance from the joint root along either base-metal surface to the toe of the weld.
Toe
The line where the face of the weld meets the base metal.
Tack Weld
A small weld placed to hold fit-up before final welding, inspected and either incorporated or removed.
Undercut
A groove melted into the base metal at the toe of a weld and left unfilled by weld metal.
Crater
The depression left at the termination point of a weld bead.
Coupon Traveler
The written record accompanying a coupon that documents dimensions, parameters, inspection, and repair.

Performance Check

With both coupons cleaned and cooled, review the finished work against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the lap-joint overlap and T-joint angle were verified before and after tacking, tack welds are free of defects, both fillets meet the specified leg length when measured with the approved gauge, profile and tie-in are consistent at both toes with no visible undercut, overlap, porosity, or slag inclusion, starts and stops are full size with filled craters, slag is fully removed, and angular distortion is within the recorded tolerance.

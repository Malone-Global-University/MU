Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect a room's surfaces, openings, and transitions, and to produce a documented finish-carpentry layout and work sequence before any trim material is cut. The finished work product is a written room survey, a marked set of controlling reference lines, a preliminary cut list and material takeoff, a deficiency list, and a signed readiness decision.

You will inspect walls and ceilings under raking light, check floor-to-wall and ceiling-to-wall transitions, measure the room and its openings, inspect every door and window opening, verify finished-floor thickness, locate framing and concealed services, identify obstacles that will interfere with trim, confirm material and finish requirements, establish the controlling reveal and height lines for the room, and issue an approved, conditional, or rejected readiness decision.

This is the first project in the book. It produces the survey, layout, and sequence that every later chapter depends on. Chapter 2 uses this survey to plan the cutting and assembly station. Chapters 4 through 17 use the controlling lines, deficiency corrections, and installation sequence established here. The Chapter 20 capstone repeats this inspection on the room used for final trim integration.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FC-001 — Inspect drywall surfaces for defects, waves, and unfinished repairs that will affect trim.
  • FC-002 — Inspect floor and wall transitions for variation that will affect trim contact and reveal.
  • FC-003 — Verify door and window openings for plumb, level, square, and correct operation before casing is planned.
  • FC-004 — Identify trim obstacles, including framing, backing, utilities, and fixed fixtures.
  • FC-005 — Plan the finish-carpentry trim sequence for a room, including installation order and controlling reference lines.
  • FC-006 — Document finish-carpentry readiness, including a deficiency list and a readiness decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every project in this book installs trim against a wall, floor, ceiling, door, or window opening that this project inspects first. The reference lines and reveal decisions made here in Section F control the casing reveal in Project 04, the baseboard height in Project 08, the crown drop and projection in Project 11, and the rail heights and batten spacing in Projects 13 and 14.

The deficiency list and readiness decision produced here are also the controlling record for the Chapter 20 capstone, which repeats this inspection on the room used for the complete finish-carpentry package.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining and reviewing the approved elevations, trim schedule, door and window schedules, floor-finish information, stair details, and finish specification
  • inspecting drywall, floor-to-wall transitions, and ceiling-to-wall transitions for conditions that will affect trim fit
  • measuring room dimensions, openings, and critical offsets
  • inspecting each door and window opening for plumb, level, square, jamb condition, swing or operation, reveal, and clearance
  • verifying finished-floor thickness and locating framing, backing, and concealed services
  • identifying trim obstacles and out-of-square or out-of-plane conditions
  • confirming trim material, profile, finish type, and paint-grade or stain-grade requirements
  • establishing controlling reveal, height, and layout lines for the room
  • planning installation order, preparing a preliminary cut list and material takeoff, and identifying long pieces, returns, scarf joints, coped joints, and profile transitions
  • preparing a deficiency list, reinspecting corrected areas, and issuing a readiness decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, fasten, or install any trim material; this project is inspection and planning only
  • correct active leaks, mold, structural movement, damaged drywall, loose framing, or unresolved door or window installation defects; these are documented and referred to the responsible trade or a qualified professional
  • alter door or window operation, egress clearances, accessibility clearances, or fire-rated assemblies during this inspection
  • issue a final readiness decision on a room with an open safety, code, or structural deficiency; such a room receives a conditional or rejected decision until the deficiency is corrected and reinspected

Any surface, opening, or transition that fails this inspection must be corrected, reinspected, and documented before trim layout begins in Project 03.

Trade Note

Every reveal, height, and layout line established in this chapter becomes the controlling reference for the rest of the book. A line marked without verifying the actual wall, ceiling, and floor condition behind it, rather than from the documented survey, is the single most common source of the cumulative alignment problems addressed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin trim layout in Project 03 until this project's readiness decision is Approved, or until every condition attached to a Conditional decision has been corrected and reinspected. Trim planned against an undocumented surface, opening, or transition condition must be treated as unverified until this inspection is complete.

Shop Tip

Check every wall and ceiling under raking light, not just normal room light. Holding the flashlight close to the surface and nearly parallel to it reveals waves, humps, and repair ridges that are invisible when the light comes from overhead.

Prerequisites

  • Approved interior elevations, trim schedule, door and window schedules, floor-finish information, stair details, and finish specification available for the room being inspected
  • Confirmation that drywall is reported complete and, where specified, primed
  • Access to the full room, including ceiling height and all wall surfaces, without obstruction from stored material or equipment
  • Working lighting in the room, supplemented by a flashlight or other raking-light source for surface inspection
  • A stud finder, level, tape measure, and straightedge in working condition

Common Field Fundamentals

Every trim installation in this book depends on three conditions verified in this chapter: the wall, ceiling, and floor surfaces are within a plane the trim can follow; the door and window openings operate correctly and sit plumb, level, and square within their tolerance; and the controlling reveal and height lines are established from the finished-floor reference rather than from an assumed or approximate line. Confirming these three conditions before cutting any trim is the single largest factor in avoiding rework across the rest of this book.

Raking Light and Surface Defects

A wave or hump that is invisible under overhead light is often clearly visible under raking light. Hold the flashlight close to the wall or ceiling and nearly parallel to the surface, then sweep it slowly along the length being inspected. Mark any defect directly on the surface with removable tape or a light pencil mark, and record its location and approximate size on the worksheet.

Measurement Cue — One Finished-Floor Reference for the Whole Room

Establish a single finished-floor reference elevation for the room and use it for every baseboard, casing, stool, and skirt height decision made in this chapter and in every later chapter for this room. Do not re-derive the floor reference separately for each wall or opening; small differences between separately derived references compound into visible misalignment at corners and returns.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work on Active Hazards

Stop work and do not proceed with layout or planning if you find an active leak, visible mold, structural movement, exposed or damaged wiring, an unresolved gas-piping concern, or a coating suspected of containing lead or asbestos. Document the condition, restrict access to the affected area, and refer it to the responsible trade or a qualified professional before continuing this inspection in that area.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Lighting, and Housekeeping

Use a stable ladder or step stool rated for the required height when inspecting ceilings and upper wall areas. Keep the inspection path clear of tools, cords, and debris, and ensure adequate lighting is available before beginning raking-light inspection in any area with reduced ambient light.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Moisture Meter Use

Where a moisture meter is used at floor-to-wall transitions or suspected leak areas, follow the meter manufacturer's instructions for the material setting, probe or pinless mode, and the calibration check appropriate to the surface being tested.

E. Document Review and Room Identification

  1. Obtain the approved interior elevations, trim schedule, door and window schedules, floor-finish information, stair details, and finish specification. Confirm the documents correspond to the room or area being inspected.
  2. Record the room identity and controlling document references on the Room Survey Record. Enter the room name, document references, date, and inspector name before proceeding.
  3. Confirm that drywall is complete, primed where required, dry, and accepted. Record the drywall acceptance status; if not accepted, note the specific unfinished areas.

F. Surface and Transition Inspection

  1. Inspect walls and ceilings under normal room light first. Walk the full perimeter and note any visible waves, humps, damaged corners, or unfinished repairs.
  2. Reinspect the same surfaces under raking light. Hold the flashlight close to and nearly parallel with each surface and sweep slowly along its length, marking any defect not visible under normal light.
  3. Record each defect's location, approximate size, and surface on the Surface Inspection Record. Photograph each marked defect for the project record.
  4. Check floor-to-wall and ceiling-to-wall transitions for variation that will affect trim. Use the straightedge and level to measure gaps and out-of-plane conditions at representative points along each wall.
  5. Record each transition condition on the Transition Inspection Record. Note the wall, location, and measured variation for any condition exceeding what the controlling trim profile can follow.
  6. Measure room length, width, ceiling height, openings, and critical offsets. Record all dimensions on the Room Survey Record.

G. Door and Window Opening Inspection

  1. Inspect each door opening for plumb, level, square, jamb condition, swing, reveal, and floor clearance. Check the hinge jamb and head jamb with the level and check the opening diagonals for square.
  2. Operate each door through its full swing. Note binding, rubbing, uneven reveals, or latch problems on the Opening Inspection Record.
  3. Inspect each window for operation, frame alignment, jamb depth, stool condition, and casing clearance. Operate every operable window and record jamb depth for stool and apron planning.
  4. Record one Opening Inspection Record per door and per window. Include the opening identifier matching the door and window schedules.

H. Floor Reference, Framing, and Obstacle Identification

  1. Verify finished-floor thickness and whether flooring is installed or represented by a controlled reference. Record the single finished-floor reference elevation to be used for the entire room.
  2. Locate studs, blocking, and backing around openings and along planned trim lines. Mark locations lightly and record them on the Obstacle and Backing Map.
  3. Identify electrical boxes, switches, receptacles, plumbing fixtures, vents, radiators, cabinets, access panels, and other trim obstacles. Record the location and height of each on the Obstacle and Backing Map.
  4. Identify walls or corners that are visibly out of square or plane. Record the location and measured condition of each.

I. Stop-Work Review

  1. Review the surface, transition, opening, and obstacle records for active hazards. Identify any active leak, visible mold, structural movement, unresolved door or window defect, or hazardous coating.
  2. Stop work in any affected area and document the condition. Do not continue layout planning for that specific area until the condition is corrected and reinspected.

J. Material, Reveal, and Layout Planning

  1. Confirm trim material, profile, dimensions, finish type, and whether work is paint-grade or stain-grade. Record this against the approved trim schedule and finish specification.
  2. Establish the intended casing reveals, baseboard height, crown location, rail heights, batten spacing, and other controlling lines. Mark these directly on the wall using the level and the finished-floor reference, and record each value on the Controlling-Line Layout.
  3. Plan the installation order by room and assembly. Sequence work consistent with the System-Sequence Control: openings before casing, casing before baseboard, baseboard before shoe, and crown layout before corner cutting.
  4. Prepare a preliminary cut list and material takeoff. Use the room dimensions and controlling lines to estimate piece lengths and quantities by profile type.
  5. Identify long pieces, special returns, scarf joints, coped joints, and profile transitions. Record these on the preliminary cut list so Chapter 3 planning can proceed directly from this record.

K. Deficiency Documentation and Readiness Decision

  1. Prepare a deficiency list identifying corrections required before installation. Consolidate every unresolved item from the surface, transition, opening, and obstacle inspections.
  2. Reinspect corrected areas. For each deficiency reported as corrected, repeat the applicable inspection step and record the result.
  3. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected finish-carpentry readiness decision. State the basis for the decision and, for a Conditional decision, list the specific conditions that must be satisfied before Project 03 begins.

Common Cutting Inspection

This project does not involve cutting. Apply the following documentation checks to every survey regardless of which room or practice structure it covers.

  • Completeness check: confirm every required document in the Document Schedule has been prepared for the room.
  • Reference check: confirm a single finished-floor reference elevation was used for every controlling line in the room.
  • Opening check: confirm every door and window has its own completed Opening Inspection Record.
  • Obstacle check: confirm every identified obstacle includes a recorded location and height.
  • Closure check: confirm every deficiency is either closed by reinspection or carried forward as an explicit condition.

Complete the Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 02.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Controlling Lines Do Not Align at a Corner

Probable cause: the level layout line was established separately on each wall instead of from one continuous finished-floor reference.
Confirmation: measure both walls from the single recorded finished-floor reference elevation and compare to the marked lines.
Correction: erase the incorrect line and remark it from the recorded finished-floor reference, continuing the line through the corner.
Reinspection: repeat the level check across both walls and at the corner.

A Defect Is Found After the Survey Was Marked Complete

Probable cause: the surface was inspected only under normal room light, without a raking-light pass.
Confirmation: reinspect the surface under raking light along its full length.
Correction: add the defect to the deficiency list, correct it through the responsible trade, and reinspect before the readiness decision is finalized.
Reinspection: repeat both the normal-light and raking-light checks on the corrected surface.

An Opening Passes Plumb but Casing Will Not Sit Flat

Probable cause: the surrounding drywall plane varies beyond what the trim profile can follow, even though the jamb itself is plumb.
Confirmation: hold the straightedge across the drywall adjacent to the jamb and measure the gap.
Correction: record the condition on the deficiency list and refer it for drywall correction before casing is planned in Project 04.
Reinspection: repeat the straightedge check after correction.

An Obstacle Is Discovered During Later Layout

Probable cause: the Obstacle and Backing Map was not checked along the full length of every planned trim line, or a fixture was added after the survey.
Confirmation: compare the obstacle location against the Obstacle and Backing Map and the current wall condition.
Correction: update the Obstacle and Backing Map and revise the affected controlling line or cut plan before proceeding.
Reinspection: repeat the obstacle identification step for the affected wall.

Readiness Was Marked Approved with an Open Deficiency

Probable cause: a deficiency was corrected but not reinspected before the readiness decision was recorded, or a deficiency was overlooked when consolidating the list.
Confirmation: compare the deficiency list against the reinspection records for each item.
Correction: revise the readiness decision to Conditional or Rejected until every deficiency is reinspected and closed.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check on the full deficiency list before reissuing the readiness decision.

Cleanup

This project ends with a complete, filed documentation package, not with any material or tool disposal.

  • Remove any temporary marking tape used to flag defects once each item is recorded and photographed
  • Return levels, straightedges, stud finders, and moisture meters to storage in working condition
  • Compile the Room Survey Record, Surface and Transition Inspection Records, Opening Inspection Records, Obstacle and Backing Map, Controlling-Line Layout, preliminary cut list, deficiency list, and readiness decision into one room file
  • Store photographs with the room file for reference during later chapters
  • Move the completed room file to the project record location used throughout this book

Field Terms

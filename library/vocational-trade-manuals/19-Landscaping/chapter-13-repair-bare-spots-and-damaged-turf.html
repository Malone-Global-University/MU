Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to diagnose and repair one bare or damaged turf area using seed or sod, but only after the verified cause of the damage has been corrected. The finished work product is a documented turf-repair record showing the diagnosed cause, the correction applied, the repair material and rate used, the protection provided during recovery, and the observed recovery result.

You will record the damage location, size, pattern, season, irrigation, traffic, pets, shade, soil, pests, and chemical exposure; inspect for compaction, drainage failure, drought, overwatering, disease, insects, salt, fuel, fertilizer burn, shade, and mower injury; stop work and refer any condition requiring specialist diagnosis; correct the verified cause; remove dead turf and thatch; prepare the soil; match and apply seed or sod; protect the repair; maintain establishment watering; and inspect and document recovery.

This chapter depends directly on the readiness plan from Chapter 1, the soil-testing methods from Chapter 3, the vegetation-removal methods from Chapter 4, and the seeding and sod-installation methods from Chapters 11 and 12. A repair performed without correcting the underlying cause is not a repair; it is a temporary patch that this chapter's troubleshooting section treats as a predictable failure.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-073 — Identify turf-damage causes using recorded observations of location, pattern, season, and exposure.
  • LS-074 — Rake bare areas to remove dead turf, thatch, and loose debris before repair.
  • LS-075 — Add soil or compost only as required to match the surrounding grade.
  • LS-076 — Seed or patch sod using matching material at the approved rate or fit.
  • LS-077 — Protect the repair area from traffic, pets, mowing, and erosion during establishment.
  • LS-078 — Inspect turf recovery and document diagnosis, repair, and follow-up.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter sits within the turf-work sequence of Chapters 11 through 13. It depends on the seedbed-preparation method taught in Chapter 11 and the sod-installation method taught in Chapter 12, and it applies both methods at a smaller, targeted scale. It also depends on the soil-testing and vegetation-removal methods from Chapters 3 and 4 whenever the diagnosed cause requires soil correction or removal of a contributing plant or pest condition.

The diagnosis-before-repair discipline taught here also governs Chapter 18's ongoing maintenance cycle and Chapter 19's pre-closeout restoration, both of which require any turf damage encountered to be diagnosed using this chapter's method rather than reseeded or resodded without cause correction.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • recording damage location, size, pattern, season, irrigation, traffic, pets, shade, soil, pests, and chemical exposure
  • inspecting for compaction, drainage failure, drought, overwatering, disease, insects, salt, fuel, fertilizer burn, shade, and mower injury
  • stopping work and referring conditions that require specialist diagnosis
  • correcting the verified cause before any seed or sod is applied
  • removing dead turf and loose thatch and loosening compacted surface soil
  • adding approved topsoil or compost only as required to match surrounding grade
  • selecting matching seed or sod and applying it at the approved rate or fit
  • protecting the repair from traffic, pets, mowing, and erosion
  • maintaining the establishment watering schedule
  • inspecting germination, rooting, color, settlement, and recurrence, and documenting the repair

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • apply seed or sod to a damaged area before the underlying cause has been identified and corrected
  • diagnose and treat unresolved disease, a major pest infestation, chemical contamination, or a drainage failure without referring the condition to the required specialist
  • apply fertilizer, pesticide, herbicide, or fungicide outside label directions or local restrictions while correcting a diagnosed cause
  • reseed or resod a failed repair a second time without first reviewing the cause that produced the first failure

Any turf-damage cause outside this chapter's scope must be referred to the required qualified professional and reinspected under this chapter before a repair is finalized.

Trade Note

Reseeding or resodding a bare spot without correcting the cause is the single most common source of repeat turf failure diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section. The repair steps in this chapter are not optional shortcuts around diagnosis; they follow diagnosis and cause correction, not the reverse.

Setup Standard

Do not proceed past Step 4 of the work order until the verified cause of the damage has been corrected or referred. Do not apply seed or sod over an unresolved disease, pest infestation, chemical contamination, or drainage failure.

Shop Tip

Match repair seed or sod to the surrounding turf species whenever possible. A mismatched patch remains visible as a different color, texture, or growth rate long after the bare spot itself has recovered.

Diagnosis, Stop-Work Conditions, and Cause Correction

Record the damage location, size, pattern, season, irrigation, traffic, pets, shade, soil, pests, and chemical exposure before assuming a cause. Inspect for compaction, drainage failure, drought, overwatering, disease, insects, salt, fuel, fertilizer burn, shade, and mower injury. Stop work for unresolved disease, major pest infestation, chemical contamination, or drainage failure requiring specialist help, and refer the condition before continuing. Correct the verified cause before any seed or sod is applied.

Measurement Cue — Record the Damage Pattern, Not Just the Boundary

Record whether the damaged area is circular, streaked, uniform, or scattered, and note its position relative to structures, irrigation heads, paths, and shaded zones. A circular pattern near a irrigation head points to a different cause than a streaked pattern following a mower path.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Repair Over Unresolved Contamination or Disease

Do not rake, seed, or sod an area with suspected fuel spill, chemical contamination, or unresolved disease until the condition has been referred to the required qualified professional and corrected. Disturbing contaminated soil or spreading diseased material can worsen the condition and expose the technician to hazardous material.

Field Safety Cue — Watch Footing on Uneven or Wet Repair Areas

Watch footing on soft, wet, or uneven turf while raking and preparing the repair area. Wear eye protection during dethatching and soil loosening, and stop work during lightning or extreme heat.

Code Alert — Watering Restrictions During Establishment

Confirm local watering-day and watering-time restrictions before programming the establishment watering schedule for the repair area. These restrictions vary by jurisdiction and season and must be verified rather than assumed.

E. Diagnosis and Cause Correction

  1. Record the damage location, size, pattern, season, irrigation, traffic, pets, shade, soil, pests, and chemical exposure. Enter each observation on the turf-repair diagnosis record before forming a conclusion.
  2. Inspect for compaction, drainage, drought, overwatering, disease, insects, salt, fuel, fertilizer burn, shade, and mower injury. Use the soil probe or trowel to check moisture and compaction at the damage area and at an adjacent healthy reference point.
  3. Stop work for unresolved disease, major pest infestation, chemical contamination, or drainage failure requiring specialist help. Refer the condition to the required qualified professional and record the referral before continuing.
  4. Correct the verified cause. Adjust irrigation, redirect traffic, correct a mowing practice, treat a confirmed pest or disease condition within label directions, or refer the condition as required before proceeding to repair.

F. Soil Preparation and Repair Application

  1. Remove dead turf and loose thatch. Use the hard rake or dethatching rake to clear the damage area down to soil.
  2. Loosen compacted surface soil. Work the soil probe, hand tool, or approved method across the damage area without disturbing adjacent healthy turf roots.
  3. Add approved topsoil or compost only as required. Use only the material and quantity specified for the repair; do not add material beyond what is needed to match grade.
  4. Match final grade to surrounding turf. Rake the repair area level with the adjacent established lawn, avoiding a raised or sunken patch.
  5. Select matching seed or sod. Compare species, color, and texture against the surrounding established turf before purchasing or selecting repair material.
  6. For seed repair, apply at the approved rate. Use the rate specified on the seed label for the measured repair area.
  7. Rake lightly and press into contact. Rake seed lightly into the surface and press or roll lightly to establish soil contact without burying seed beyond the permitted depth.
  8. For sod repair, cut a clean repair shape. Use the sod knife or edging tool to cut a defined shape that will accept a matching sod patch.
  9. Prepare the soil and fit the sod patch tightly. Set the patch flush with surrounding turf height, with edges tight and without overlap or gaps.
  10. Roll or press the repair. Achieve full root-to-soil contact across the entire repaired area.

G. Watering, Protection, and Follow-Up

  1. Water without causing runoff. Apply water slowly and thoroughly, checking that the repair area absorbs water without pooling or washing seed.
  2. Protect from traffic, pets, mowing, and erosion. Install protective fencing, stakes, or flags around the repair area for the full establishment period.
  3. Maintain the establishment watering schedule. Follow the frequency and depth appropriate to seed or sod establishment and confirmed local watering restrictions.
  4. Inspect germination, rooting, color, settlement, and recurrence. Check the repair area at scheduled intervals through the establishment period.
  5. Refill or reseed failed spots only after cause review. If any portion of the repair fails, repeat the diagnosis in Section E for that portion before reapplying seed or sod.
  6. Document diagnosis, repair, and follow-up. Complete the turf-repair diagnosis record with the final recovery result and the date protection was removed.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed turf-repair diagnosis record using the same checks regardless of repair size or cause.

  • Diagnosis check: confirm damage location, size, pattern, season, and exposure conditions are recorded before the cause conclusion.
  • Cause-correction check: confirm the verified cause was corrected, or referred and resolved, before repair material was applied.
  • Material-match check: confirm seed or sod species matches surrounding established turf where a matching source was available.
  • Soil-contact check: confirm seed was raked in and pressed, or sod was rolled or pressed to full root-to-soil contact.
  • Protection check: confirm the repair was protected from traffic, pets, mowing, and erosion through the full establishment period.
  • Recovery check: confirm germination or rooting, matching color and density, and absence of recurrence were verified before closing the record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Repaired Area Fails Again in the Same Location

Probable cause: the underlying cause was not correctly diagnosed or was not fully corrected before seed or sod was applied.
Confirmation: repeat the cause inspection from Section E at the failed location and compare findings against the original diagnosis record.
Correction: identify the uncorrected or misdiagnosed cause, correct it or refer it to the required specialist, and repeat the repair only after correction is confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the cause-correction check and the recovery check on the corrected area.

Sod Patch Edges Remain Visibly Separated From Surrounding Turf

Probable cause: the repair shape was cut inaccurately, the patch was not trimmed to a tight fit, or the patch was not rolled or pressed for full soil contact.
Confirmation: lift a corner of the patch and inspect root-to-soil contact, and measure any visible gap along the seam.
Correction: retrim the patch edges, fill any gap with matching soil, and reroll or press the patch to close the seam.
Reinspection: repeat the soil-contact check and the material-match check on the corrected seam.

Seeded Repair Shows Poor or Uneven Germination

Probable cause: seed was applied below or above the labeled rate, was buried too deeply, or the establishment watering schedule was not maintained.
Confirmation: inspect surface seed coverage against the labeled rate and check soil moisture at seed depth against the required establishment schedule.
Correction: overseed thin areas at the labeled rate, correct the raking depth on future repairs, and reestablish the required watering frequency.
Reinspection: repeat the recovery check after the corrected watering schedule has been maintained for the required period.

Repair Material Does Not Match Surrounding Turf

Probable cause: a substitute seed or sod species was used because a matching source was not confirmed before repair began.
Confirmation: compare the repaired area's color, texture, and growth rate against the surrounding established turf after several weeks of growth.
Correction: document the mismatch and, where a matching source is required by the project scope, plan a follow-up repair with correctly matched material.
Reinspection: repeat the material-match check after the follow-up repair.

Protection Was Removed Before the Repair Was Established

Probable cause: fencing, stakes, or flags were removed on a fixed schedule rather than after confirming germination or rooting.
Confirmation: inspect the repair area for traffic damage, mower injury, or pet damage following early removal of protection.
Correction: reinstall protection, allow the repair to complete establishment, and reinspect before removing protection permanently.
Reinspection: repeat the protection check and the recovery check before closing the record.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, documented repair record retained for use by later maintenance chapters, not filed away unused.

  • Remove and properly dispose of dead turf, thatch, and any diseased or contaminated material identified during diagnosis
  • Return borrowed diagnostic tools and clean soil probes and rakes before storage
  • Organize repair photographs by section to match the Repair Record Section Schedule
  • Store the completed turf-repair diagnosis record where Chapter 18 maintenance work can reference it
  • Leave protective fencing, stakes, or flags in place until recovery is confirmed at the follow-up inspection

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prune a controlled group of shrubs and one small ground-accessible tree according to health, structure, clearance, and seasonal objectives. The finished work product is a set of correctly pruned plants with dead, diseased, and structurally unsound growth removed, proper cuts made at the branch collar, natural form preserved, canopy removal held within the approved limit, and a documented pruning record.

You will identify each plant and its pruning season, flowering habit, growth form, and objective; inspect for dead, diseased, broken, crossing, rubbing, weak, hazardous, and overextended branches; identify overhead hazards; inspect and sanitize tools; establish a debris and exclusion zone; remove dead, broken, and diseased growth first; make proper collar cuts and three-cuts on larger limbs; remove crossing and rubbing branches; thin shrubs from the interior; apply heading cuts only where appropriate; maintain plant-specific height, spread, and flowering-wood goals; avoid over-pruning and topping; remove suckers and water sprouts where appropriate; inspect the finished structure from multiple viewpoints; and document the work performed.

This chapter follows Chapter 1 through Chapter 16 in the book sequence. The plants pruned in this chapter were selected, sited, and planted in Chapters 8 through 10 and are maintained afterward under the fertilizing and watering cycle in Chapter 18. This chapter does not replant, relocate, or redesign any planting bed; it works only with the plant structure already established on site.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-097 — Identify the pruning objective for each plant based on species, season, flowering habit, and growth form.
  • LS-098 — Remove dead, diseased, broken, and hazardous growth using sanitized tools.
  • LS-099 — Make proper pruning cuts at the branch collar, including the three-cut method for larger limbs.
  • LS-100 — Shape shrubs using thinning and heading cuts appropriate to the plant and objective.
  • LS-101 — Avoid over-pruning by holding canopy removal within the approved limit and never topping a tree.
  • LS-102 — Inspect finished plant structure from multiple viewpoints and document the pruning performed.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter depends on the plant identification, spacing, and mature-size information recorded in Chapter 1 and applied during planting in Chapters 8 through 10. It uses the tool-inspection and sanitation habits introduced across the book and connects directly to Chapter 18, where fertilizing and watering decisions are informed by the plant condition observed here.

Pruning performed under this chapter does not replace specialist tree care. Any plant condition that exceeds the scope defined below is referred out rather than pruned under this chapter's authorization.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying pruning season, flowering habit, growth form, and objective for each plant before cutting
  • inspecting for dead, diseased, broken, crossing, rubbing, weak, hazardous, and overextended branches
  • inspecting and sanitizing hand pruners, loppers, and saws
  • establishing a debris drop and exclusion zone
  • making proper collar cuts, including the three-cut method for branches large enough to tear bark
  • removing crossing and rubbing branches and thinning shrubs from the interior
  • using heading cuts only where compatible with the plant and objective
  • maintaining plant-specific height, spread, flowering wood, and privacy goals within the approved canopy-removal limit
  • removing suckers and water sprouts where appropriate
  • inspecting structure from multiple viewpoints and documenting the work performed

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • prune large limbs, work from ladders with chainsaws, work near power lines, work above safe ground-access height, or prune storm-damaged or structurally unstable trees
  • climb, use aerial equipment, or perform any pruning that requires a qualified tree-care professional
  • make flush cuts, leave long stubs, top a tree, remove canopy beyond the approved limit, or apply wound paint unless specifically directed
  • shear a plant that requires natural-form pruning, or apply heading cuts to a plant or objective for which they are not appropriate

Any condition exceeding this scope is referred to a qualified tree-care professional before any cut is made in that area.

Trade Note

Every cut made under this chapter is a permanent structural decision for the plant. A cut cannot be undone, and a bad cut compounds over every following growing season. Identify the objective and the exact cut location before the tool touches the branch.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cutting until every plant in the work group has been identified and its pruning objective, season, and hazard conditions confirmed. Do not cut any branch for which the required ground-access height, tool, or professional referral has not been confirmed.

Shop Tip

Step back and view the plant from at least two sides before making the first cut, and again after every few cuts. A cut that looks correct from one angle can leave a visible gap, an unbalanced form, or an over-thinned section when viewed from the other side.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every pruning cut made in this chapter depends on four fundamentals: the correct objective for the specific plant, a sanitized and appropriate tool for the cut size, the correct cut location at the branch collar, and a canopy-removal limit that is never exceeded in a single cycle.

Objective, Hazard Screening, and Stop-Work Conditions

Identify each plant and confirm pruning season, flowering habit, growth form, and objective before any cut is made. Identify power lines, roofs, windows, traffic, ladders, and overhead hazards within reach of the work. Stop work for large limbs, elevated chainsaw work, storm-damaged trees, power-line proximity, unstable trunks, or climbing requirements, and refer the condition to a qualified tree-care professional.

Measurement Cue — Cut Just Outside the Branch Collar

Locate the branch collar before cutting. Make the final cut just outside the collar, not flush against the trunk or parent branch and not so far out that a long stub remains. The collar itself must remain intact on the plant after the cut.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop for Overhead and Elevated Hazards

Do not prune from a ladder with a chainsaw, near power lines, from a roof, or above safe ground-access height. Do not attempt storm-damaged or structurally unstable trees under this chapter. Refer any such condition to a qualified tree-care professional before any cut is made in that area.

Field Safety Cue — Establish the Debris and Exclusion Zone

Mark a debris drop and exclusion zone before cutting begins. Keep bystanders, pets, and unnecessary tools out of the drop path. Watch footing on uneven ground and around accumulating debris.

Manufacturer Cue — Sanitize Tools Between Diseased Plants

Inspect and sanitize hand pruners, loppers, and saws before use, and clean cutting edges between diseased plants where required. Follow the sanitizing product's instructions for contact time and dilution.

E. Preparation — Identification, Hazards, and Tool Setup

  1. Identify each plant and confirm pruning season, flowering habit, growth form, and objective. Record each on the pruning log before cutting.
  2. Inspect for dead, diseased, broken, crossing, rubbing, weak, hazardous, and overextended branches. Mark each condition found on the plant or in the log.
  3. Identify power lines, roofs, windows, traffic, ladders, and overhead hazards. Confirm the work can be completed entirely from safe ground access.
  4. Stop work for large limbs, elevated chainsaw work, storm-damaged trees, power-line proximity, unstable trunks, or climbing requirements. Refer any such condition to a qualified tree-care professional before continuing in that area.
  5. Inspect and sanitize hand pruners, loppers, and saws. Confirm cutting edges are sharp and clean before the first cut.
  6. Establish a debris drop and exclusion zone. Keep the zone clear of bystanders, pets, and unrelated tools.

F. Corrective and Structural Cuts

  1. Remove dead, broken, and diseased growth first. Cut back to healthy wood or to the point of origin as the condition requires.
  2. Make cuts just outside the branch collar. Confirm the collar location before each cut.
  3. Do not make flush cuts or leave long stubs. Recheck cut placement immediately after each cut.
  4. Use the three-cut method for branches large enough to tear bark. Make the undercut, the top cut, and the final collar cut in sequence.
  5. Remove crossing and rubbing branches selectively. Choose the branch with the weaker structure or poorer position for removal.
  6. Thin shrubs from the interior where appropriate. Remove selected branches back to their point of origin to open the plant without stimulating dense regrowth.
  7. Use heading cuts only where compatible with the plant and objective. Cut to a bud or lateral branch facing the intended growth direction.
  8. Do not shear plants that require natural-form pruning unless specified. Confirm the objective before applying any shearing cut.

G. Form Control, Sucker Removal, and Documentation

  1. Maintain plant-specific height, spread, flowering wood, and privacy goals. Reference the objective recorded in Section E, Step 1.
  2. Avoid removing more live canopy than the approved limit. Track cumulative removal as cutting proceeds.
  3. Do not top trees. Confirm the leader and primary scaffold branches remain intact.
  4. Remove suckers and water sprouts only where appropriate. Cut back to the point of origin.
  5. Inspect structure from multiple viewpoints. Walk around the plant and check the result from at least two sides.
  6. Clean tools between diseased plants where required. Sanitize before moving to the next plant showing disease symptoms.
  7. Remove debris and document pruning performed. Complete the pruning log for every plant in the work group.

Common Inspection Review

Review every pruned plant using the same checks regardless of species or bed location.

  • Cut-placement check: confirm every cut is at the branch collar, with no flush cuts and no long stubs.
  • Canopy-limit check: confirm no plant exceeds the approved canopy-removal limit for this cycle.
  • Topping check: confirm no tree has been topped and no leader has been removed without justification.
  • Sanitation check: confirm tools were sanitized before use and between diseased plants.
  • Form check: confirm each plant's natural form is preserved unless shearing was specified.
  • Documentation check: confirm every plant has a completed pruning-log entry.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Cut Was Made Flush Against the Trunk

Probable cause: the branch collar was not located before cutting, or the cut was positioned too close to the parent branch or trunk.
Confirmation: inspect the cut surface for removed collar tissue and compare its position to neighboring correct cuts.
Correction: do not attempt to recut a flush cut deeper. Record the condition on the pruning log and monitor the site for correct wound response.
Reinspection: repeat the cut-placement check on all remaining uncut branches before continuing.

More Canopy Was Removed Than the Approved Limit

Probable cause: cumulative removal was not tracked while cutting, or multiple structural cuts were made without reassessing total canopy loss.
Confirmation: compare the estimated total canopy percentage removed against the approved limit for the plant and objective.
Correction: stop cutting immediately. Record the condition and the corrected plan for the plant's recovery on the pruning log.
Reinspection: repeat the canopy-limit check before pruning any additional plant in the work group.

A Branch Bark Tore During Removal

Probable cause: a branch large enough to require the three-cut method was removed with a single cut instead.
Confirmation: inspect the wound for torn bark extending below the branch collar.
Correction: make a clean corrective cut at the nearest sound point outside the torn area without creating a flush cut, following the three-cut method for any remaining large branches.
Reinspection: repeat the cut-placement check and confirm no additional torn bark is present.

Disease Symptoms Appeared on a Second Plant After Pruning

Probable cause: tools were not sanitized between the diseased plant and the next plant pruned.
Confirmation: review the pruning log for the sanitizing step between the affected plants.
Correction: sanitize all tools before further use, and refer the newly affected plant for diagnosis before further pruning.
Reinspection: repeat the sanitation check before resuming work on any remaining plant.

The Finished Shrub Looks Uneven From One Side

Probable cause: the plant was viewed and cut from only one position during shaping.
Confirmation: walk around the plant and compare its form from at least two additional viewpoints.
Correction: make selective corrective thinning cuts only as needed to balance the form, without exceeding the approved canopy-removal limit.
Reinspection: repeat the form check from all viewpoints after correction.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with cleared debris, sanitized tools, and a completed pruning log retained for the maintenance record, not scattered clippings and an unrecorded visit.

  • Remove all cut debris from the debris drop zone and the surrounding planting bed
  • Separate compostable plant debris from diseased material requiring separate disposal
  • Clean and sanitize hand pruners, loppers, and saws before storage
  • Store tools dry and protected from the elements
  • File the completed pruning log with the Landscape Readiness Worksheet where Chapter 18 can reference it

Field Terms

Branch Collar
The slightly raised area where a branch joins a larger branch or trunk; the correct cut location for branch removal.
Flush Cut
A cut made too close to the trunk or parent branch that removes the branch collar.
Heading Cut
A cut that shortens a branch to a bud or lateral branch.
Thinning Cut
A cut that removes an entire branch back to its point of origin.
Three-Cut Method
The undercut, top cut, and final collar cut sequence used on larger branches.
Water Sprout
Vigorous, often vertical growth arising from a branch, typically removed where inappropriate to the plant's form.
Sucker
Vigorous growth arising from the root system or base of the plant, typically removed.
Approved Canopy-Removal Limit
The maximum proportion of live canopy that may be removed in a single pruning cycle for the identified plant and objective.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to restore one residential landscape area after installation or maintenance work and issue a documented final condition report. The finished work product is a cleaned, restored landscape area, a corrected final deficiency list, and an issued approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-restoration status.

You will review the completed project scope and punch list, remove debris and waste, separate disposal categories, rake beds without disturbing roots or irrigation, redefine bed edges, reset loose edging, refresh mulch to the approved final depth, repair minor erosion, restore disturbed lawn, clean hardscape edges and irrigation components, verify plants and irrigation hardware, conduct a controlled irrigation or drainage observation, prepare and correct a final deficiency list, and issue a documented restoration status.

This chapter closes out the installation and maintenance sequence built through Chapters 1 through 18. It restores every disturbed area to a documented final condition and hands the landscape forward to the Chapter 20 capstone process, which begins from a site in the same restored, documented condition this chapter produces.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-109 — Remove debris, packaging, waste, and construction materials and separate them by disposal category.
  • LS-110 — Refresh mulch to the approved final depth while maintaining clearance from trunks, stems, crowns, siding, vents, and weeps.
  • LS-111 — Redefine bed edges to the approved shape and reset loose or damaged edging.
  • LS-112 — Repair minor erosion using approved soil, grading, cover, and stabilization methods.
  • LS-113 — Clean hardscape edges, irrigation components, drains, and grates without discharging soil, fertilizer, or mulch to drains.
  • LS-114 — Perform a final landscape inspection and issue a documented approved, conditional, or rejected restoration status.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter depends on the readiness plan from Chapter 1, the protected work area from Chapter 2, the soil and bed preparation from Chapters 3 through 7, the plant installation from Chapters 8 through 10, the turf work from Chapters 11 through 13, the irrigation and drainage systems from Chapters 14 through 16, and the pruning and maintenance cycle from Chapters 17 and 18. Every disturbed area, temporary stake, protective material, and staged supply from those chapters is addressed and removed or resolved here.

The restored, documented landscape produced in this chapter is the required starting condition for the Chapter 20 capstone project, which installs a complete residential landscape bed from a verified, cleaned site rather than a site still carrying installation debris, loose edging, or unresolved minor erosion.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the completed project scope, disturbance limits, punch list, drainage, and maintenance requirements
  • removing plant debris, packaging, wire, tags, stakes, soil piles, weeds, and construction waste
  • separating compostable, recyclable, reusable, invasive, diseased, and hazardous waste
  • raking planting beds without disturbing roots or irrigation
  • redefining bed edges and resetting loose edging
  • refreshing mulch to the approved final depth while maintaining trunk, stem, and crown clearance
  • repairing minor erosion with approved soil, grading, cover, and stabilization
  • restoring disturbed lawn using seed or sod as specified
  • cleaning hardscape edges, irrigation heads, drains, grates, and access points
  • verifying plant labels, staking, ties, emitters, and guards
  • conducting a controlled irrigation or drainage observation
  • preparing, correcting, and reinspecting a final deficiency list
  • issuing a documented approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-restoration status

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • conceal a major drainage failure, slope failure, structural defect, or unresolved utility conflict with mulch, soil, or cosmetic grading
  • discharge soil, fertilizer, mulch, or wash water to storm drains, streets, or neighboring property while cleaning hardscape
  • restore disturbed lawn or repair erosion using seed, sod, soil, or amendment rates not specified by the approved project design or Chapters 3 through 13
  • issue an approved restoration status while any deficiency remains uncorrected, unreferred, or undocumented

Any wet area, major erosion, structural defect, or drainage failure identified during this chapter's inspection is a stop-work condition requiring referral rather than cosmetic correction.

Trade Note

Restoration is not a cosmetic pass performed after the real work is done. It is the chapter that confirms every disturbed condition from Chapters 1 through 18 has actually been resolved. A landscape that looks clean but still hides a loose edging section, a thin mulch band over an exposed root flare, or an undocumented minor erosion channel has not passed this chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not begin debris removal until the completed project scope, disturbance limits, punch list, and drainage and maintenance requirements in Step 1 are reviewed. Do not issue an approved restoration status until every item on the final deficiency list is corrected, referred, or documented as a condition and reinspected.

Shop Tip

Photograph each area before removing debris and again after each restoration step. A before-and-after photograph pair resolves more disputes about restored condition than any written description, and it is the fastest way to confirm mulch depth, edge shape, and erosion repair during the final inspection.

Debris, Waste, and Disposal

Remove plant debris, packaging, wire, tags, stakes, soil piles, weeds, and construction waste from the restoration area before any bed, edge, mulch, or hardscape work begins. Separate compostable, recyclable, reusable, invasive, diseased, and hazardous waste according to local disposal rules.

Code Alert — Lawful Discharge When Cleaning Hardscape

Confirm local stormwater and discharge requirements before sweeping or washing hardscape edges. Do not discharge soil, fertilizer, or mulch to storm drains, streets, or neighboring property during cleanup.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Conceal Major Drainage or Slope Failure

If the inspection identifies a major drainage failure, slope failure, structural defect, or unresolved utility conflict, stop cosmetic restoration in that area immediately. Do not cover the condition with mulch, soil, or grading. Refer the condition to the required qualified professional and document it on the final deficiency list.

Field Safety Cue — Debris and Waste Handling

Watch footing around stacked debris, wheelbarrow routes, and wet or uneven surfaces. Wear gloves when handling waste, stakes, wire, and edging fasteners, and use eye protection when using a blower or clearing debris that may become airborne.

Measurement Cue — Verify Mulch Depth Before Closing the Item

Measure refreshed mulch depth at multiple points against the approved final depth from the project design or Chapter 7. Do not close the mulch item on the deficiency list based on visual estimate alone.

E. Scope Review and Debris Removal

  1. Review the completed project scope, disturbance limits, punch list, drainage, and maintenance requirements. Confirm the restoration area matches the recorded disturbance limits before beginning work.
  2. Remove plant debris, packaging, wire, tags, stakes, soil piles, weeds, and construction waste. Clear the full restoration area before beginning bed or edge work.
  3. Separate compostable, recyclable, reusable, invasive, diseased, and hazardous waste. Route each category to its required disposal method.

F. Bed, Edge, Mulch, and Erosion Restoration

  1. Rake planting beds without disturbing roots or irrigation. Work around emitters, dripline, and root zones identified in the irrigation and planting records.
  2. Redefine bed edges to the approved shape. Compare the current edge to the shape recorded in Chapter 6.
  3. Reset loose edging and remove sharp projections. Refasten or restake edging clear of utilities.
  4. Refresh mulch only to the approved final depth. Measure existing depth first and add only the quantity required to reach the specified total.
  5. Clear mulch from trunks, stems, crowns, siding, vents, and weeps. Pull mulch back to the required clearance at every plant and building interface.
  6. Repair minor erosion with approved soil, grading, cover, and stabilization. Use only the materials and methods specified by the approved project design.
  7. Do not conceal major drainage or slope failure with mulch. Stop cosmetic repair and refer any such condition per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter.

G. Turf, Hardscape, and Final Verification

  1. Restore disturbed lawn using seed or sod as specified. Follow the applicable Chapter 11 or Chapter 12 procedure for the restored area.
  2. Sweep or wash hardscape edges without discharging soil, fertilizer, or mulch to drains. Direct wash water away from storm drains and neighboring property.
  3. Clean irrigation heads, drains, grates, and access points. Remove mulch, soil, and debris blocking any component.
  4. Verify plant labels, staking, ties, emitters, and guards. Confirm each remains correctly installed and legible where required.
  5. Check all plants for correct depth, stability, spacing, and damage. Compare against the planting records from the applicable earlier chapter.
  6. Check turf, soil, mulch, drainage, irrigation, hardscape transitions, and building clearances. Walk the full restoration area systematically.
  7. Conduct a controlled irrigation or drainage observation. Run the irrigation system or observe drainage under controlled conditions where permitted.
  8. Prepare a final deficiency list. Record every unresolved condition found during Sections E through G.
  9. Correct and reinspect each item. Resolve every deficiency and confirm the correction before continuing.
  10. Compile photographs and maintenance information. Organize the record to match the Restoration Section Schedule.
  11. Issue approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-restoration status. Record the status, the date, and the technician's name on the final deficiency-list form.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed restoration using the same checks regardless of the area's size or which earlier chapters' work is being restored.

  • Completeness check: confirm all ten sections of the Restoration Section Schedule are completed with the required content.
  • Debris and disposal check: confirm all debris and waste are removed and separated by category.
  • Bed, edge, and mulch check: confirm edges match the approved shape and mulch depth matches the approved final depth at every measured point.
  • Erosion and drainage check: confirm minor erosion is corrected and no major failure is concealed.
  • Hardscape and discharge check: confirm no soil, fertilizer, or mulch was discharged to drains during cleaning.
  • Final status check: confirm one of approved, conditional, or rejected is issued, signed, and dated.

Complete the Inspection Checklist in the section below before beginning Chapter 20.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Mulch Depth Exceeds the Approved Final Depth

Probable cause: mulch was added by visual estimate without first measuring existing depth against the approved total.
Confirmation: measure mulch depth at several points against the specified final depth.
Correction: remove excess mulch to reach the approved depth and re-establish trunk and crown clearance.
Reinspection: repeat the mulch and erosion check on the corrected area.

A Minor Erosion Repair Conceals a Larger Drainage Problem

Probable cause: the erosion condition was treated as cosmetic without confirming whether the underlying cause is a grading or drainage failure from Chapters 5 or 16.
Confirmation: conduct a controlled water observation on the repaired area and compare the result to the drainage record from the applicable earlier chapter.
Correction: stop cosmetic repair, refer the condition to the required qualified professional, and document it on the final deficiency list as unresolved.
Reinspection: repeat the erosion and drainage check after the underlying cause is corrected and referred conditions are resolved.

Soil or Mulch Was Discharged to a Storm Drain During Cleaning

Probable cause: hardscape was washed with a hose without first sweeping loose material away from the drain.
Confirmation: inspect the drain, grate, or outlet for accumulated soil, mulch, or fertilizer residue.
Correction: remove the discharged material from the drain where accessible, and repeat the cleaning using sweeping or blowing before any wash step.
Reinspection: repeat the hardscape and drain cleaning check.

A Restoration Status Was Issued With Unresolved Deficiencies

Probable cause: a deficiency was identified but not corrected, referred, or documented as a condition before the status was signed.
Confirmation: compare the final deficiency list against the signed status and identify any item without a documented resolution.
Correction: reopen the status, resolve or document each outstanding deficiency, and reissue the status only after reinspection.
Reinspection: repeat the final deficiency review and the status check before proceeding to Chapter 20.

Loose Edging Was Reset Without Removing the Cause of the Original Failure

Probable cause: edging was restaked or refastened without identifying why it loosened, such as an unstable base, frost heave, or an undersized fastener.
Confirmation: inspect the base beneath the reset edging and compare the fastener to the original specification from Chapter 6.
Correction: correct the base condition or fastener before final resetting, and document the cause on the final deficiency list.
Reinspection: repeat the bed and edge check on the corrected section.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed final deficiency-list form retained for use by Chapter 20, not filed away unused.

  • Remove all temporary stakes, flags, and protective materials no longer required by the restored area
  • Return borrowed tools, wheelbarrows, and hoses to storage
  • Organize before, during, and after photographs to match the Restoration Section Schedule
  • Store the completed final deficiency-list form and photographic record where Chapter 20 can reference them
  • Confirm all disposal categories reached their required destination before closing the project record

Performance Check

Project Closeout

You have completed the nineteenth chapter in the landscaping project sequence: restoring and cleaning up a residential landscape.

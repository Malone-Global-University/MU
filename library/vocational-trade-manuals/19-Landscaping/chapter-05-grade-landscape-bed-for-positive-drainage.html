Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to grade one residential planting bed to the elevations and drainage direction called for in the approved grading and drainage plan. The finished work product is a smooth, plantable bed surface that carries water away from structures at the approved fall, blends cleanly into walks, patios, lawns, drains, and neighboring areas, and preserves the existing grade around any established tree root flare.

You will review the grading and drainage plan and every foundation and root-flare clearance it sets, confirm utilities and lawful discharge, establish high and low reference points, mark no-fill zones at siding and building openings, remove debris and soft soil, add approved soil in controlled layers, shape the grade, maintain the approved fall away from structures, blend the bed into adjacent surfaces, protect tree root flares, remove low spots, lightly firm the soil without overcompacting it, check elevation and slope, conduct a controlled water observation where permitted, correct failed areas, and document the final elevations and drainage path.

This chapter builds directly on the readiness plan from Chapter 1, the work-area protections from Chapter 2, the tested and amended soil from Chapter 3, and the cleared area from Chapter 4. It supplies the accepted grade that Chapter 6 bed edging, Chapter 7 fabric and mulch, every planting chapter, and the irrigation and drainage chapters all depend on being correct before they conceal it.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-025 — Establish drainage direction from the approved grading and drainage plan and from verified reference points.
  • LS-026 — Shape soil grades in controlled layers to a smooth, uniform plane without abrupt humps or depressions.
  • LS-027 — Remove low spots that would hold irrigation or rainwater within the project area.
  • LS-028 — Prevent water from being directed against foundations, siding, or building openings.
  • LS-029 — Compact soil lightly only where required to reduce settlement, without overcompacting planting soil.
  • LS-030 — Inspect the finished bed slope using a level, laser, stringline, or approved method, and correct failed areas.

Where This Skill Fits

Grading is the point in the project sequence where the site-to-establishment order in the Book-Level Authoring Rule becomes irreversible if skipped: positive drainage must be established before edging, fabric, mulch, planting, turf, or irrigation are allowed to conceal a grading defect. The elevations set in this chapter are the reference every later chapter is required to use rather than re-derive.

This chapter uses the site plan, drainage findings, and low points recorded in Chapter 1, works inside the protected staging and equipment routes established in Chapter 2, grades soil that has already been sampled and amended in Chapter 3, and works in an area already cleared of unwanted vegetation and debris in Chapter 4. It hands off an accepted, drainage-verified bed surface to Chapter 6 bed layout and edging, and its elevations control the below-grade work assigned later to the drainage chapter and the irrigation chapters.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the grading and drainage plan, foundation clearances, hardscape elevations, drains, plant depths, mulch, and settlement allowance
  • confirming utilities, property limits, and lawful discharge before grading begins
  • establishing high and low reference points and marking no-fill zones at siding, weeps, vents, thresholds, window wells, and wood framing
  • removing debris and soft or organic soil pockets before adding soil
  • adding approved topsoil or planting soil in controlled layers and shaping broad, smooth grades
  • maintaining the approved fall away from structures and blending the bed into walks, patios, lawns, drains, and neighboring areas
  • preserving tree root flares and existing root-zone grades
  • removing local low spots and lightly firming soil only as required to reduce settlement
  • checking elevation and slope, conducting a controlled water observation where permitted, correcting failed areas, and documenting the final elevations and drainage path

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • grade an area that has not received an approved or conditional readiness decision under Chapter 1, or that has not completed the utility locating, vegetation removal, and soil work assigned to Chapters 2 through 4
  • direct runoff toward foundations, entrances, neighboring property, sidewalks, roads, public hazards, or a prohibited discharge point
  • bury siding, masonry weeps, foundation vents, door thresholds, window wells, wood framing, utility markers, shutoffs, cleanouts, or inspection zones under added soil
  • pile soil against trunks, alter grade over an established root flare, or compact soil over roots without qualified review
  • proceed past an unidentified utility, unstable slope, contaminated soil, buried tank, sewage, major erosion, foundation conflict, groundwater, hazardous material, or unknown drainage outlet
  • install edging, fabric, mulch, plants, turf, or irrigation over a grade that has not passed the inspection required by this chapter

Any area that fails this chapter's inspection must be corrected, or referred to a qualified drainage contractor, landscape architect, or surveyor where the conditions require it, and reinspected before Chapter 6 begins.

Trade Note

A finished grade that looks smooth by eye can still hold water. Elevation and slope are confirmed with a level, laser, stringline, or approved method at the checkpoints in this chapter, not by visual impression alone. A grade accepted on appearance rather than measurement is the most common source of the drainage failures diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not add or move soil until utilities, property limits, and lawful discharge in Steps 1 through 2 are confirmed, and no-fill zones in Step 4 are marked. Do not release the bed to Chapter 6 until the elevation and slope check in Steps 16 through 20 is complete and any failed area has been corrected and rechecked.

Shop Tip

Set the high and low reference points before any soil is moved, and mark them with stakes that remain visible for the entire grading operation. Reshooting elevations from memory partway through the work is the most common cause of an uneven finished plane.

Mark no-fill zones at siding, weeps, vents, thresholds, window wells, and wood framing, and preserve tree root flares and existing root-zone grades throughout the operation.

Measurement Cue — Set References Before Moving Soil

Establish the high and low reference points and stake them before adding or shaping any soil. Check the approved fall between them using a level, laser, or stringline, and record the elevation difference before beginning Section F.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Bury Building Components or Utility Access

Do not add soil that buries siding, masonry weeps, foundation vents, door thresholds, window wells, wood framing, utility markers, shutoffs, cleanouts, or inspection zones at any point in the grading operation, even temporarily. Do not direct runoff toward foundations, entrances, neighboring property, sidewalks, roads, public hazards, or a prohibited discharge point.

Field Safety Cue — Lift and Move Soil Safely

Use proper lifting technique, a wheelbarrow, or a cart when moving soil, and take a wider stance on sloped ground. Watch footing on uneven or wet surfaces, and stop work during lightning, extreme heat, or other hazardous weather.

Code Alert — Lawful Discharge and Stormwater Requirements

Confirm the finished grade directs water only to a lawful discharge point under adopted stormwater and drainage requirements for the jurisdiction. These requirements vary by location and must be verified rather than assumed before shaping the final fall.

E. Preparation — Plan Review, References, and No-Fill Zones

  1. Review the grading and drainage plan, foundation clearances, hardscape elevations, drains, plant depths, mulch, and soil settlement allowance. Compare each figure against the Chapter 1 findings for this project area.
  2. Confirm utilities, property limits, and lawful discharge. Verify utility markings remain visible and within their valid period, and confirm the plan's discharge point is lawful for the jurisdiction.
  3. Establish high and low reference points. Stake each point and record its elevation and the approved fall between them on the Grading and Drainage Record.
  4. Mark no-fill zones at siding, weeps, vents, thresholds, window wells, and wood framing. Flag each zone before any soil is added.

F. Soil Placement and Grade Shaping

  1. Remove debris, soft soil, and organic pockets. Clear the area down to stable soil before adding any approved material.
  2. Add approved topsoil or planting soil in controlled layers. Place and spread each layer before adding the next; do not dump the full quantity in one pile.
  3. Shape broad smooth grades rather than abrupt humps and depressions. Work from the high reference point toward the low reference point in overlapping passes.
  4. Maintain the approved fall away from structures. Check elevation at intermediate points between the two reference stakes as the grade develops.
  5. Blend the bed into walks, patios, lawns, drains, and neighboring areas. Taper the transition so no step, ridge, or reverse slope forms at the boundary.
  6. Preserve tree root flares and existing root-zone grades. Hand-grade around the root flare rather than running equipment or heavy tool passes across it.
  7. Do not pile soil against trunks or over established roots. Maintain the existing grade line at the trunk regardless of the surrounding bed elevation.
  8. Remove local low spots that would hold irrigation or rainwater. Identify and refill any point lower than its immediate surroundings.
  9. Lightly firm soil only as required to reduce settlement. Use the hand tamper in the specific areas the grading plan or settlement allowance identifies.
  10. Do not overcompact planting soil. Stop firming once the settlement allowance is addressed; do not compact the full bed surface.
  11. Rake the surface to a uniform plane. Finish with the landscape rake across the entire graded area.

G. Verification, Water Observation, and Correction

  1. Check elevation and slope using a level, laser, stringline, or approved method. Record the measured fall at the high and low reference points and at intermediate check points.
  2. Conduct a controlled water observation where permitted. Apply water at the high reference point and observe its path toward the low reference point and lawful discharge.
  3. Identify ponding, erosion paths, reverse slopes, and blocked outlets. Mark each failed location for correction.
  4. Correct failed areas. Reshape, add soil, or remove soil at each marked location until the approved fall is restored.
  5. Recheck after settlement or irrigation where specified. Repeat the elevation and slope check and the water observation for any corrected area.
  6. Document final elevations and drainage path. Record the confirmed high and low elevations, the measured fall, and the observed drainage path on the Grading and Drainage Record.

Common Inspection Review

Review every graded bed using the same checks regardless of the project area's size or drainage complexity.

  • Reference check: confirm the high and low reference points and the approved fall between them are recorded and staked.
  • No-fill zone check: confirm no added soil contacts siding, weeps, vents, thresholds, window wells, or wood framing.
  • Root-flare check: confirm no existing root flare has been buried, exposed beyond original grade, or compacted.
  • Low-spot check: confirm no point in the graded plane holds standing water during the controlled water observation.
  • Compaction check: confirm firming was limited to the areas requiring settlement reduction.
  • Documentation check: confirm final elevations and the drainage path are recorded on the Grading and Drainage Record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Water Ponds at a Point Within the Graded Bed

Probable cause: a local low spot was left after raking, or soil settled unevenly after placement.
Confirmation: conduct a controlled water observation and mark every location where water collects rather than continuing to flow toward the low reference point.
Correction: add approved soil to the marked low spot in a controlled layer, reshape the surrounding grade, and lightly firm only that area.
Reinspection: repeat the elevation and slope check and the water observation for the corrected location.

The Finished Grade Slopes Toward the Foundation

Probable cause: the high reference point was set incorrectly, or soil was added unevenly without checking intermediate elevations as the grade developed.
Confirmation: recheck the elevation at the foundation edge and at the high and low reference points using a level or laser.
Correction: remove or redistribute soil near the foundation until the approved fall away from the structure is restored, without creating a no-fill zone violation.
Reinspection: repeat the elevation and slope check at the foundation edge and the water observation.

Soil Was Placed Against a No-Fill Zone

Probable cause: the no-fill zone was not clearly marked before soil placement began, or the grading crew was not shown the marked flags before starting.
Confirmation: inspect siding, weeps, vents, thresholds, window wells, and wood framing for direct soil contact.
Correction: remove soil from the no-fill zone immediately and reestablish the required clearance.
Reinspection: repeat the no-fill zone check across the full bed perimeter.

An Established Tree's Root Flare Has Been Buried or Altered

Probable cause: soil was graded across the root-zone area at the same rate as the surrounding bed without preserving the existing root-flare elevation.
Confirmation: compare the current soil elevation at the trunk against the Chapter 1 or Chapter 4 photographic record of the root flare.
Correction: hand-remove soil from the root-flare area back to its original elevation; do not use powered equipment near the trunk. Refer to a qualified arborist if root damage is suspected.
Reinspection: repeat the root-flare check before proceeding to further grading.

The Graded Surface Feels Overcompacted

Probable cause: the hand tamper was used across the full bed surface rather than only at the areas identified for settlement reduction.
Confirmation: probe the soil at several points with the soil probe or trowel and compare resistance and infiltration against the Chapter 3 soil-test tilth record.
Correction: loosen the overcompacted area to the specified depth without disturbing the established fall, then rake to a uniform plane.
Reinspection: repeat the elevation and slope check and confirm tilth at the corrected location.

The Bed Does Not Blend Smoothly Into an Adjacent Walk or Lawn

Probable cause: the transition elevation at the boundary was not checked against the adjacent hardscape or turf elevation before finishing the grade.
Confirmation: measure the elevation difference at the boundary and compare it to the adjacent surface.
Correction: taper the bed grade over a longer distance at the boundary, adding or removing soil as needed to remove the step or reverse slope.
Reinspection: repeat the blending check and the elevation and slope check at the corrected boundary.

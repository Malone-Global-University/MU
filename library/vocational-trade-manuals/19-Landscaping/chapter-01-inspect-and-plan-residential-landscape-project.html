Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect a residential landscape area and produce one documented readiness plan covering climate exposure, drainage, soil, existing plants, utilities, irrigation, access, hazards, and prerequisites. The finished work product is a completed Landscape Readiness Worksheet, a deficiency and prerequisite list, and an issued project decision: approved, conditional, or rejected.

You will gather the plans and records that control the project, define the project area and goals, verify boundaries and restricted zones, request utility locating, inspect buildings and existing conditions, record sun and shade patterns, assess microclimate exposure, inspect grade and drainage, measure the landscape area, inspect soil, evaluate existing plants, assess irrigation and access, and issue a documented readiness decision.

This is the first chapter in the book. Every later chapter — soil amendment, vegetation removal, grading, bed edging, planting, turf work, irrigation, drainage, pruning, fertilizing, restoration, and the Chapter 20 capstone — begins from the readiness plan produced here. No later chapter authorizes work in an area that has not passed this inspection or had its deficiencies corrected and reinspected.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-001 — Inspect site conditions, including buildings, hardscape, utilities, irrigation, drains, and existing plants.
  • LS-002 — Identify sun and shade patterns by time of day and season, and record microclimate exposure.
  • LS-003 — Identify drainage issues, including runoff sources, ponding, erosion, and foundation-water risk.
  • LS-004 — Evaluate soil condition using field indicators of texture, compaction, color, odor, drainage, organic matter, and disturbance.
  • LS-005 — Measure landscape areas, including beds, lawn, slopes, paths, access routes, and grade changes.
  • LS-006 — Document project readiness and issue an approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-readiness decision.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter is the entry point for the entire book. The Landscape Readiness Worksheet you complete here becomes the controlling reference for soil testing and amendment in Chapter 3, vegetation removal in Chapter 4, grading in Chapter 5, bed layout in Chapter 6, every planting chapter from Chapter 8 through Chapter 10, turf work in Chapters 11 through 13, irrigation and drainage in Chapters 14 through 16, ongoing maintenance in Chapters 17 and 18, restoration in Chapter 19, and the complete capstone project in Chapter 20.

The sun-and-shade record, drainage findings, soil observations, and measured areas produced in this chapter are not preliminary notes to be discarded. They are the reference data every later chapter is required to use rather than re-derive or assume.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • gathering the site plan, property, drainage, utility, and planting information that controls the project
  • verifying property lines, easements, setbacks, and prohibited work zones
  • requesting utility locating before any later digging, trenching, or planting work
  • inspecting buildings, hardscape, utilities, irrigation, drains, and existing plants
  • recording sun and shade patterns and microclimate exposure
  • inspecting grade, drainage, runoff sources, and foundation-water risk
  • measuring planting beds, lawn areas, slopes, paths, and access routes
  • inspecting soil texture, compaction, drainage, and organic matter using field indicators
  • assessing existing plants, irrigation source, and equipment access
  • issuing a documented, approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-readiness decision

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • dig, trench, auger, drive stakes, install edging, or plant trees or shrubs before utility locating is complete and confirmed
  • begin soil amendment, grading, planting, turf, or irrigation work in an area that has not received an approved or conditional readiness decision under this chapter
  • proceed with an area showing unidentified utilities, unstable slopes, contaminated soil, buried tanks, sewage, major erosion, foundation conflicts, groundwater, hazardous materials, or an unknown drainage outlet
  • make final amendment, planting, or irrigation design decisions from this inspection alone where soil test results, an arborist, a landscape architect, an irrigation contractor, a backflow tester, or a surveyor is required by the conditions found

Any area that fails this inspection must be corrected, or referred to the required qualified professional, and reinspected under this chapter before any later chapter's work begins.

Trade Note

Every finding in this chapter is recorded on the Landscape Readiness Worksheet at the time it is observed, not reconstructed from memory afterward. A drainage pattern, a shade pattern, or a soil condition noted from memory hours later is the single most common source of the plan errors diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not request utility locating until the project area, intended use, and preliminary boundaries in Steps 1 through 3 are confirmed. Do not begin any digging, trenching, staking, or planting authorized by a later chapter until utility locating in Step 4 is complete and confirmed valid.

Shop Tip

Record sun and shade observations at more than one time of day whenever the inspection schedule allows it. A single midday observation misses morning and afternoon shade from buildings, fences, and mature trees, and is the most common cause of the sun-exposure errors diagnosed later in this chapter.

Boundaries, Utilities, and Stop-Work Conditions

Verify property lines, easements, setbacks, right-of-way, septic areas, wells, and prohibited work zones before defining the final project area. Request utility locating before any digging, trenching, auguring, driving stakes, installing edging, or planting trees and shrubs authorized by a later chapter. Stop work for unidentified utilities, unstable slopes, contaminated soil, buried tanks, sewage, major erosion, foundation conflicts, groundwater, hazardous materials, or unknown drainage outlets.

Measurement Cue — Record Sun and Shade by Time and Season

Record which parts of the project area receive direct sun, partial shade, and full shade, and note the time of day and, where the inspection schedule allows, the season. A single observation does not capture how a building, fence, or mature tree changes the pattern across the day.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Dig Before Utility Locating Is Confirmed

Do not drive stakes, probe soil below the surface at more than a shallow depth, or authorize any later digging, trenching, or planting until the utility-locating service has marked the project area and those marks have been confirmed valid. Contact with an unmarked utility line can cause serious injury, fire, explosion, or service disruption.

Field Safety Cue — Walk the Site Deliberately

Watch footing on slopes, uneven turf, hidden holes, tripping hazards, and wet or icy surfaces while walking the project area. Wear sun, insect, and weather protection appropriate to the season, and stop the inspection during lightning, extreme heat, or other hazardous weather.

Code Alert — Zoning, Stormwater, and Water-Use Restrictions

Confirm adopted zoning, stormwater, irrigation, backflow, environmental, and water-use requirements for the jurisdiction before finalizing the project scope. These requirements vary by location and must be verified rather than assumed.

E. Preparation — Records, Project Definition, and Boundaries

  1. Obtain the site plan, property information, grading plan, utility records, drainage information, planting goals, irrigation information, and local restrictions. Confirm each document is current before relying on it.
  2. Define the project area, intended use, desired maintenance level, budget, and establishment period. Record each on the Landscape Readiness Worksheet.
  3. Verify property lines, easements, setbacks, right-of-way, septic areas, wells, and prohibited work zones. Compare the site plan or survey against observed conditions.
  4. Request required utility locating. Do not proceed to any later digging, trenching, or planting authorized by another chapter until markings are placed and confirmed.

F. Site and Condition Inspection

  1. Inspect buildings, foundations, siding, windows, doors, hardscape, fences, utilities, irrigation, drains, and existing plants. Record condition and location of each.
  2. Record sun and shade conditions by time of day and season where possible. Note which project-area zones receive direct sun, partial shade, and full shade.
  3. Identify heat-reflective walls, wind exposure, frost pockets, salt exposure, and sheltered microclimates. Mark each condition's location on the plan.
  4. Inspect existing grade and drainage. Walk the full project area and note slope direction and low points.
  5. Identify runoff sources, ponding, erosion, roof discharge, low points, and foundation-water risks. Photograph each observed condition.
  6. Measure planting beds, lawn areas, slopes, paths, access routes, and grade changes. Record each dimension on the Landscape Readiness Worksheet.
  7. Inspect soil texture, compaction, color, odor, drainage, organic matter, roots, debris, and prior disturbance. Use the soil probe or trowel at multiple points across the project area.
  8. Identify existing plants as desirable, invasive, diseased, hazardous, overgrown, or unresolved. Record species where known and location for each plant assessed.
  9. Assess mature plant conflicts with buildings, utilities, hardscape, sight lines, and drainage. Note any existing plant whose mature size already conflicts, or is approaching conflict, with a fixed feature.
  10. Identify irrigation source, pressure, backflow protection, controller, hose access, and watering restrictions. Record what is present and what is missing.
  11. Inspect equipment and material access. Note gate widths, slopes, overhead clearance, and any obstruction that would restrict later material delivery or equipment routes.

G. Deficiency Review and Readiness Decision

  1. Stop work for unidentified utilities, unstable slopes, contaminated soil, major tree hazards, disputed boundaries, or unlawful drainage. Refer any such condition to the required qualified professional before continuing.
  2. Prepare a preliminary plant, soil, irrigation, drainage, and maintenance strategy. Base the strategy only on the conditions recorded in Sections E and F.
  3. Prepare a deficiency and prerequisite list. List every condition that must be corrected, or referred, before later chapters may proceed.
  4. Reinspect corrected conditions. Confirm each deficiency has been corrected or referred before issuing a final decision.
  5. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected landscape-readiness decision. Record the decision, the date, and the inspector's name on the Landscape Readiness Worksheet.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed Landscape Readiness Worksheet using the same checks regardless of the project area's size or intended use.

  • Completeness check: confirm all twelve sections of the Readiness Plan Section Schedule are completed with the required content.
  • Boundary and utility check: confirm property lines, easements, setbacks, and utility locating are verified and recorded.
  • Sun, shade, and drainage check: confirm sun and shade patterns and every drainage finding are recorded with location.
  • Measurement check: confirm every measured area, slope, path, and access route is recorded with its dimension.
  • Deficiency check: confirm every listed deficiency is corrected, referred, or documented as a condition of a conditional decision.
  • Decision check: confirm one of approved, conditional, or rejected is issued, signed, and dated.

Complete the Inspection Checklist in the section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Sun and Shade Record Does Not Match Actual Plant Performance Later

Probable cause: the sun-and-shade observation was recorded at only one time of day, missing shade cast later by a building, fence, or mature tree.
Confirmation: revisit the project area at a different time of day and compare the new observation to the recorded pattern.
Correction: update the Landscape Readiness Worksheet with observations from at least two additional times of day before finalizing the strategy.
Reinspection: repeat the sun, shade, and drainage check for the corrected sections.

A Drainage Condition Was Missed Until After Later Work Began

Probable cause: the grade and drainage inspection was performed during dry conditions without a controlled water observation, or a low point was not walked directly.
Confirmation: reinspect the area during or immediately after rainfall, or conduct a controlled water observation where permitted.
Correction: record the newly identified low point, runoff source, or foundation-water risk, add it to the deficiency list, and reissue the readiness decision as conditional until corrected.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary and utility check and the drainage check after correction.

Utility Locating Was Not Confirmed Before Later Digging

Probable cause: the deficiency list did not clearly flag utility locating as a prerequisite, or markings were assumed valid without visual confirmation.
Confirmation: inspect the project area for current utility-locating marks and confirm the request date against the marking's stated valid period.
Correction: stop any later digging, trenching, or planting immediately, request new utility locating, and confirm markings before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary and utility check before reissuing the readiness decision.

Measured Areas Do Not Match Later Material Takeoffs

Probable cause: a bed, lawn area, slope, or access route was measured informally by eye rather than with a tape measure or measuring wheel, or a grade change was not recorded.
Confirmation: remeasure the affected area using a tape measure or measuring wheel and compare the new figure to the recorded dimension.
Correction: update the measured-areas section of the Landscape Readiness Worksheet with the corrected dimension.
Reinspection: repeat the measurement check on the corrected section.

A Readiness Decision Was Issued With Unresolved Deficiencies

Probable cause: a deficiency was identified but not corrected, referred, or documented as a condition before the decision was signed.
Confirmation: compare the deficiency list against the signed decision and identify any item without a documented resolution.
Correction: reopen the decision, resolve or document each outstanding deficiency, and reissue the decision only after reinspection.
Reinspection: repeat the deficiency check and the decision check before proceeding to Chapter 2.

An Existing Plant's Mature-Size Conflict Was Not Identified

Probable cause: the existing-plant assessment recorded current size and condition but did not consider mature size against nearby buildings, utilities, hardscape, sight lines, or drainage.
Confirmation: research the mature size of the identified species and compare it against the measured distance to the nearest fixed feature.
Correction: add the conflict to the deficiency list and note whether the plant requires removal, relocation, or ongoing pruning management in a later chapter.
Reinspection: repeat the existing-plants section review before finalizing the strategy.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed worksheet retained for use by every later chapter, not filed away unused.

  • Remove all temporary stakes and flags placed only for this inspection unless they mark a condition later chapters must still reference
  • Return borrowed plans, surveys, and utility records to their source or designated storage
  • Organize inspection photographs by section to match the Readiness Plan Section Schedule
  • Store the completed Landscape Readiness Worksheet and deficiency list where every later chapter's work can reference them
  • Confirm the utility-locating markings remain visible and within their valid period before Chapter 2 begins

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to clear one planting-bed or lawn-conversion area of identified weeds, sod, and unwanted vegetation while protecting desirable plants, protected roots, soil, utilities, and adjacent surfaces. The finished work product is a cleared, protected planting area with a documented record of removed species, disposal method, and any unresolved condition, ready for the grading work in Chapter 5.

You will identify all vegetation present before removal begins, mark desirable and protected plants, confirm whether herbicide use is permitted for this project, remove weeds and sod using manual and mechanical methods matched to the vegetation present, protect adjacent plants and surfaces, remove and dispose of plant waste correctly, and inspect the cleared area for regrowth points, remaining roots, soft spots, erosion, and disturbed utilities before planning follow-up weed control.

This chapter depends on the Chapter 1 Landscape Readiness Worksheet and the Chapter 2 work-area setup. It uses the existing-plant assessment, boundary and utility findings, and protected-zone markings already recorded rather than re-deriving them. The cleared area produced here becomes the starting condition for the Chapter 5 grading work, the Chapter 6 bed layout and edging, and every planting and turf chapter that follows.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • LS-019 — Identify unwanted vegetation, including weeds, invasive plants, and volunteer growth, before removal begins.
  • LS-020 — Remove weeds manually, using hand-pulling and root-removal methods matched to species.
  • LS-021 — Cut and remove sod using a manual cutter, spade, or powered sod cutter at the correct depth.
  • LS-022 — Protect desirable plants, protected roots, and adjacent surfaces during removal work.
  • LS-023 — Dispose of plant waste correctly, separating compostable, recyclable, invasive, and diseased material.
  • LS-024 — Inspect the cleared planting area for regrowth points, remaining roots, soft spots, erosion, and disturbed utilities.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter follows directly from Chapter 1's existing-plant assessment and Chapter 2's protected work area, and it must be completed before Chapter 5 grading, Chapter 6 bed edging, and any planting or turf chapter begins. A planting bed or lawn-conversion area that still contains live weeds, unremoved sod, or unresolved roots cannot receive an accurate grade, an accurate soil test, or a durable planting.

The vegetation identification, removal record, and follow-up weed-control plan produced in this chapter are not disposable field notes. Chapter 5 relies on the cleared condition confirmed here rather than assuming the area is clear, and Chapter 18 maintenance relies on the follow-up weed-control plan established at the end of this chapter.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying every plant present in the work area before any removal begins
  • marking desirable plants, protected roots, invasive plants, toxic plants, and plants requiring specialist handling
  • confirming whether herbicide use is permitted for this specific project or whether manual and mechanical removal will be used
  • hand-pulling and root-removing weeds by species-appropriate method
  • cutting and removing sod using a manual cutter, spade, or powered sod cutter
  • protecting adjacent plants, water, hardscape, windows, and occupied areas during removal
  • separating and disposing of plant waste, including invasive or diseased material
  • inspecting the cleared area for regrowth points, remaining roots, soft spots, erosion, and disturbed utilities
  • documenting removed species, disposal method, and unresolved conditions

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • apply any herbicide, pesticide, or other chemical outside the product label, local restrictions, and applicator requirements confirmed in Step 3 and Step 4
  • cut protected tree roots, excavate within a critical root zone, or remove woody volunteer growth beyond the approved root and stump scope
  • begin removal in any area where utility locating from Chapter 1 has not been confirmed valid
  • proceed to Chapter 5 grading before regrowth points, remaining roots, soft spots, erosion, and disturbed utilities identified in this chapter's inspection have been corrected or documented

Any condition beyond this scope — a large stump requiring specialist removal, a diseased tree, a disputed invasive-species designation, or unresolved root damage to a protected tree — must be referred to the required qualified professional identified in the Chapter 1 prerequisites.

Trade Note

Identify every plant in the work area before removing anything. Removing an unidentified plant first and asking questions afterward is the single most common cause of accidental removal of a desirable perennial, a protected native plant, or a plant a neighboring property owner considers their own.

Setup Standard

Do not begin removal of any plant until Step 1 and Step 2 have identified and marked it. Do not use any herbicide until Step 3 and Step 4 confirm the product label, permitted use, weather, drift, runoff, and applicator requirements for this specific project.

Shop Tip

Hand-pull annual weeds before they set seed whenever the removal schedule allows it. Pulling a weed after seed has set still removes the plant, but it does nothing to prevent the seed already dropped in the bed from germinating during the following season.

Identification, Protection, and Chemical-Use Confirmation

Identify all vegetation in the work area before removal, and mark desirable plants, protected roots, invasive plants, toxic plants, and plants requiring specialist handling. Confirm whether herbicide use is permitted or whether manual and mechanical removal will be used, and review the product label, weather, drift, runoff, and applicator requirements before any chemical use.

Measurement Cue — Set and Verify Sod Cutting Depth

Set the sod cutter or spade to remove turf and its dense root mat without removing excessive topsoil beneath it. Check the cut depth on a test strip before cutting the full area, and adjust the setting if the cut leaves live root mat behind or removes soil beyond the turf's root depth.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Utility Locating Remains Valid Before Any Cutting

Do not operate a powered sod cutter, drive a spade below a shallow depth, or fork soil around suspected utility locations until the Chapter 1 utility-locating markings are confirmed still visible and within their valid period. If markings are faded, missing, or expired, stop work and request new utility locating before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Protect Hands and Eyes During Manual and Mechanical Removal

Wear gloves during all hand-pulling and root-removal work, and wear eye protection when operating a powered sod cutter or cutting woody volunteer growth. Watch footing on uneven or freshly disturbed ground, and keep other workers clear of the sod cutter's cutting path while it is running.

Code Alert — Herbicide and Waste-Disposal Restrictions

Confirm local restrictions on herbicide use, applicator licensing, and disposal or composting of invasive or diseased plant material before proceeding. These requirements vary by jurisdiction and must be verified rather than assumed.

E. Identification, Protection, and Method Confirmation

  1. Identify all vegetation in the work area before removal. Walk the full project area and record every plant species present, using the Chapter 1 existing-plant assessment as the starting reference and confirming it against current conditions.
  2. Mark desirable plants, protected roots, invasive plants, toxic plants, and plants requiring specialist handling. Use a distinct flag color for each category and record the color key on the Vegetation Removal Log.
  3. Confirm whether herbicide use is permitted or whether manual and mechanical removal will be used. Record the confirmed method on the Vegetation Removal Log before proceeding.
  4. Review product labels, weather, drift, runoff, and applicator requirements before any chemical use. Do not apply any herbicide if wind, forecast rain, or proximity to protected water, gardens, or occupied areas conflicts with the label.

F. Weed and Sod Removal

  1. Protect adjacent plants, water, hardscape, windows, and occupied areas. Cover or shield surfaces at risk from tool contact, soil disturbance, or chemical drift before removal begins.
  2. Hand-pull annual weeds before seed set where practical. Grip each plant at the base and pull steadily to remove the root rather than breaking the stem.
  3. Remove perennial weeds with roots, rhizomes, bulbs, or crowns according to species. Use the garden fork to loosen soil around the root system before pulling.
  4. Loosen soil carefully around desirable roots. Work outward from the desirable plant's marked protection zone rather than toward it.
  5. Cut sod using a manual cutter, spade, or powered sod cutter. Confirm utility-locating markings remain valid in the area to be cut before starting the cutter.
  6. Set cutting depth to remove turf and dense roots without excessive topsoil loss. Verify the setting on a test strip per the Measurement Cue above before cutting the full area.
  7. Roll or stack sod safely. Keep rolled or stacked sod off protected root zones and away from foot-traffic paths.
  8. Avoid burying live sod beneath new beds unless the approved sheet-mulching method requires it. Record on the Vegetation Removal Log if the sheet-mulching exception is used for this project.

G. Woody Growth, Waste Handling, and Clearance Inspection

  1. Remove woody volunteer plants only within the approved root and stump scope. Do not remove any woody plant marked for specialist handling in Step 2.
  2. Do not cut protected tree roots or large roots without review. Stop and mark the location if a protected root is encountered during weed or sod removal.
  3. Separate invasive or diseased plant waste where local disposal rules require it. Bag or contain this material separately from other removed vegetation.
  4. Compost only suitable weed-free material. Confirm removed vegetation is free of seed heads, rhizomes, bulbs, and invasive or diseased material before adding it to compost.
  5. Remove plastic, wire, rocks, construction debris, and roots from the cleared area. Sort debris into the waste zones established in Chapter 2.
  6. Rake the surface. Clear loose roots, debris, and remaining plant fragments to leave a uniform surface.
  7. Inspect for regrowth points, remaining roots, soft spots, erosion, and disturbed utilities. Walk the full cleared area and mark any condition found.
  8. Plan follow-up weed control before planting. Record the timing and method of any follow-up weed check required before Chapter 5 grading begins.
  9. Document removed species, disposal, and unresolved conditions. Complete the Vegetation Removal Log with species identified, removal method, disposal destination, and any condition referred to a qualified professional.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed Vegetation Removal Log using the same checks regardless of the project area's size or vegetation density.

  • Identification check: confirm every plant present was identified and classified before removal.
  • Protection check: confirm every desirable plant and protected root remains undamaged.
  • Chemical-use check: confirm any herbicide use stayed within the confirmed label, weather, drift, runoff, and applicator requirements.
  • Sod depth check: confirm cutting depth removed the turf root mat without excessive topsoil loss.
  • Clearance check: confirm no regrowth point, remaining root, soft spot, erosion, or disturbed utility mark remains undocumented.
  • Waste check: confirm invasive or diseased material was separated according to local disposal rules.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Desirable Plant Was Removed by Mistake

Probable cause: the plant identification in Step 1 was incomplete, or removal began in an area before Step 2 marking was finished.
Confirmation: compare the Vegetation Removal Log against the Chapter 1 existing-plant assessment to confirm which plant was removed and whether it was marked.
Correction: document the loss on the Vegetation Removal Log, notify the party responsible for the planting plan, and confirm whether replacement is required before Chapter 8 or Chapter 10 planting begins.
Reinspection: repeat the identification and protection check on the remaining unremoved area before continuing.

Weeds Regrow Within Days of Removal

Probable cause: a perennial weed was hand-pulled without removing its rhizome, bulb, or crown, leaving a regrowth point in the soil.
Confirmation: inspect the affected spot with the garden fork or hand tool to locate remaining root fragments.
Correction: remove the remaining root fragment completely, and update the follow-up weed-control plan to include a recheck of this area.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check on the affected area before proceeding to Chapter 5.

Sod Cutter Leaves Live Turf Mat in Some Areas

Probable cause: the cutting depth was set too shallow for the turf's actual root depth, or the depth was not verified with a test strip before cutting the full area.
Confirmation: lift a sample section of the cut turf and inspect the root mat for live, uncut root material.
Correction: increase the cutting depth, re-run the cutter over the affected section, and remove the remaining root mat.
Reinspection: repeat the sod depth check on the corrected section.

Invasive Plant Waste Was Composted With Weed-Free Material

Probable cause: waste was not separated at the point of removal, or the invasive classification from Step 2 was not carried through to disposal.
Confirmation: inspect the compost pile or container for invasive plant material and compare against the Vegetation Removal Log's classification.
Correction: remove the invasive material from the compostable waste and dispose of it according to local disposal rules.
Reinspection: repeat the waste check before closing out this chapter.

A Utility Mark Was Disturbed During Removal

Probable cause: sod cutting, forking, or raking occurred in an area where utility-locating markings had faded or were not re-verified before work began.
Confirmation: compare the current condition of the marked area against the Chapter 1 utility-locating record.
Correction: stop work in the affected area, request new utility locating, and confirm markings before resuming removal or proceeding to Chapter 5.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check, including the disturbed-utilities item, before issuing this chapter's closeout.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a cleared area and a completed Vegetation Removal Log retained for use by Chapter 5 and later chapters, not a pile of waste left in place.

  • Remove all marking flags used to identify removed vegetation unless a flag still marks a protected root or an unresolved condition
  • Transport separated waste to the correct disposal or composting destination confirmed in Step 15 and Step 16
  • Return hand tools, the sod cutter, and rakes to clean, inspected storage
  • Store the completed Vegetation Removal Log where Chapter 5 grading work can reference it
  • Confirm the follow-up weed-control plan and its timing are recorded before Chapter 5 begins

Field Terms

Unwanted Vegetation
Any weed, invasive plant, or volunteer growth identified as not part of the approved planting plan.
Desirable Plant
Any plant confirmed as part of the approved planting plan and marked for protection during removal.
Protected Root
A root system marked before removal that must not be cut, disturbed, or compacted.
Sod Cutting Depth
The depth set on a sod cutter or spade to remove turf and its root mat without excessive topsoil loss.
Regrowth Point
A remaining root, rhizome, bulb, crown, or stem fragment capable of resprouting after removal.
Weed-Free Compostable Material
Removed vegetation confirmed free of seed heads, rhizomes, bulbs, and invasive or diseased material.
Vegetation Removal Log
The document recording every identified species, its classification, removal method, and disposal.
Sheet-Mulching Exception
The approved method by which live sod may be buried beneath a new bed rather than removed, used only when the project design specifies it.

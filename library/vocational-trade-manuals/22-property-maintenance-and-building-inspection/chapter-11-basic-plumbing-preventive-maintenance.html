Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete one controlled basic plumbing preventive-maintenance cycle on fixtures already identified in Chapter 10 as within the approved maintenance scope. The finished work product is a documented maintenance cycle covering gently exercised shutoff valves, cleaned aerators, mechanically cleared minor accessible drain buildup, checked toilet operation, inspected supply hoses and connectors, and a verified leak-free result after testing.

You will select only fixtures Chapter 10 cleared for basic maintenance, protect the work area, exercise valves gently and stop at the first sign of failure, service aerators and minor drain buildup using non-destructive methods, inspect toilet tank components without adding unapproved chemicals, inspect supply connectors without assuming age alone requires replacement, and test every serviced fixture immediately and after a defined observation period.

This project depends directly on Chapter 10's fixture-by-fixture inspection record and feeds forward into Chapter 12, where the inspection scope shifts from plumbing to electrical devices. It also supplies verified maintenance history that Chapter 19 will use when building the property's prioritized repair plan.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-061 — Exercise shutoff valves gently within the approved scope and stop before forcing a valve that binds, leaks, or lacks support.
  • MI-062 — Clean approved faucet aerators using a product-compatible, non-damaging method.
  • MI-063 — Clear minor accessible sink-drain buildup using approved mechanical methods only.
  • MI-064 — Inspect toilet tank, fill, flush, and connection components for leakage and correct operation.
  • MI-065 — Inspect supply hoses and connectors for kinks, corrosion, bulges, and strain without assuming a replacement is required from age alone.
  • MI-066 — Verify leak-free operation immediately and after a defined observation period for every serviced fixture.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 10 built a fixture-by-fixture inspection record and identified which fixtures qualify for basic preventive maintenance. This project acts directly on that record: it does not reinspect every fixture from scratch, and it does not expand the scope beyond what Chapter 10 already cleared.

The valve-exercise discipline, aerator-cleaning method, minor-drain-clearing limit, toilet-check procedure, and connector-inspection standard used here recur throughout the rest of the book whenever plumbing maintenance intersects with another system, including Chapter 16's appliance and laundry connections and Chapter 18's seasonal interior maintenance cycle.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • selecting only Chapter 10 items within the approved basic-maintenance scope
  • gently exercising fixture shutoff valves and confirming operating position afterward
  • cleaning approved faucet aerators and reinstalling them without cross-threading or overtightening
  • clearing minor accessible sink-drain buildup using hand-operated, non-chemical mechanical methods
  • inspecting toilet tank, fill valve, flapper or seal, handle, linkage, supply connector, shutoff, base, and seat
  • inspecting supply hoses and connectors at approved fixtures and equipment
  • testing every serviced fixture immediately and after a defined observation period for leaks and abnormal operation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • service, force, or repair a valve, fixture, connector, or drain that shows severe corrosion, active leakage, structural damage, sewage exposure, or any other referral condition
  • use chemical drain cleaners, whether alone or combined with mechanical cleaning
  • remove flow-control components, enlarge drain openings, or push blockage further into concealed piping
  • replace a connector or valve merely because it appears old without verifying material, compatibility, and installation requirements

Any fixture, valve, or connector that fails during this maintenance cycle must be handled per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before the cycle continues.

Trade Note

Basic preventive maintenance is a controlled, reversible action on a fixture already accepted as serviceable. It is not an opportunity to diagnose or correct a condition Chapter 10 flagged as a referral. If a selected item shows a condition beyond gentle exercise, cleaning, or minor mechanical clearing once the work begins, stop that item and route it back to the referral list rather than pushing the maintenance further.

Setup Standard

Do not begin servicing any fixture until the Chapter 10 record confirms it is within the approved basic-maintenance scope, and until a leak-response plan, towels, pans, and communication method are staged (Step 2). A fixture not confirmed in the Chapter 10 record must be re-inspected before it is added to this maintenance cycle.

Shop Tip

Photograph each fixture's condition before beginning work and again immediately after testing. A consistent before-and-after photograph pair makes it far easier to confirm that a later drip or stain is new rather than something present before the maintenance cycle began.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every plumbing maintenance action in this chapter follows the same pattern: confirm the item is within scope, protect the work area, act gently and reversibly, test immediately, and test again after an observation period. Learning that pattern here, at the level of ordinary fixtures, prepares you for every later chapter that touches water-using equipment, including appliances, laundry connections, and seasonal interior maintenance.

Gentle Force Versus Forced Operation

A valve, aerator, or connector that resists normal hand force is signaling a condition beyond this chapter's scope. Stop at the first sign of binding, leakage, corrosion progression, or unusual resistance. Do not increase force, use pliers on a valve handle designed for hand operation, or continue past the point where the component's movement feels uncertain.

Documentation Cue — Record Before, During, and After for Every Fixture

Record the fixture identification, the pre-work condition, the action taken, the immediate test result, and the observation-period test result for every serviced item. Do not close out a fixture on the worksheet until both test results are recorded.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Immediately on Leakage, Binding, or Failure

Stop work on any valve, fixture, connector, or drain the moment it leaks, binds, shows a broken handle, moves piping, or fails to control flow as expected. Return the valve to its required operating position if possible, contain any water with towels and the catch pan, and record the item as a referral condition rather than continuing the maintenance action.

Field Safety Cue — Occupant Coordination and Cabinet Protection

Coordinate any water interruption with occupants before beginning. Protect cabinets, floors, finishes, and stored items around every fixture before starting work, and keep towels and the catch pan within reach throughout the cycle.

Code Alert — Basic Maintenance Has a Defined Ceiling

This project authorizes gentle valve exercise, aerator cleaning, minor mechanical drain clearing, toilet component checks, and connector inspection only. Chemical drain cleaning, connector replacement without verified compatibility, and any work on a fixture showing a referral condition require a separate, appropriately scoped procedure or a licensed plumber.

E. Preparation — Selection and Setup

  1. Select only Chapter 10 items within the approved basic-maintenance scope. Confirm each selected fixture, valve, aerator, drain, and connector against the Chapter 10 fixture-by-fixture inspection record.
  2. Confirm the main water shutoff and leak-response readiness. Verify the main water shutoff is accessible and that a leak-response plan, towels, pans, and communication method are ready.
  3. Coordinate water interruption and protect the work area. Coordinate any planned interruption with occupants and protect cabinets, floors, finishes, and stored items around each fixture.
  4. Reverify no referral condition is present. Confirm that no selected valve, fixture, connector, or drain shows severe corrosion, active leakage, structural damage, sewage, or another referral condition before beginning.
  5. Photograph the initial condition. Record fixture identification and photograph the pre-work condition of each selected item.

F. Valve Exercise

  1. Inspect and support each selected fixture shutoff. Support the valve and connected piping as required to avoid twisting during movement.
  2. Move the valve gently through its approved range. Use controlled hand force only, without forcing the valve past normal resistance.
  3. Stop immediately at the first sign of failure. Stop for leakage, binding, a broken handle, pipe movement, or failure to control flow, and record the item as a referral condition.
  4. Return the valve to its required operating position. Inspect the stem, packing, body, connection, and downstream fixture for leakage after returning the valve.

G. Aerator Service

  1. Remove the approved faucet aerator. Use protected tools that do not damage the finish.
  2. Record component orientation. Note the washer, screen, flow device, and orientation before cleaning.
  3. Clean mineral or debris buildup. Use a product-compatible method only; do not enlarge openings, remove required flow-control components, or mix incompatible chemicals.
  4. Rinse, reassemble, and reinstall. Avoid cross-threading or overtightening the aerator housing.
  5. Test flow and inspect the connection. Confirm normal flow and no leakage at the aerator connection.

H. Minor Drain Clearing

  1. Remove the sink stopper or strainer. Use the approved service procedure only.
  2. Remove accessible debris mechanically. Clear hair, soap, food, or debris without pushing material deeper into the drain.
  3. Use a hand-operated approved drain tool within the defined limit. Do not combine mechanical cleaning with chemical drain cleaners at any point.
  4. Stop for conditions beyond minor clearing. Stop for repeated backup, multiple slow fixtures, sewage odor, damaged trap, inaccessible blockage, or evidence of a main-drain problem.
  5. Reassemble and verify. Reassemble the stopper or strainer, verify adjustment, and fill and drain the fixture repeatedly while observing all accessible joints.

I. Toilet Checks and Connector Inspection

  1. Inspect toilet tank components. Check the fill valve, flapper or seal, handle, chain or linkage, supply connector, shutoff, base, and seat.
  2. Perform approved leak and running-water checks. Do not add unapproved chemicals or objects to the tank; adjust only manufacturer-approved service components within scope.
  3. Inspect supply hoses and connectors. Check sinks, toilets, washers, dishwashers, refrigerators, and other approved equipment for kinks, abrasion, corrosion, bulges, age concerns, loose support, leakage, and strain.
  4. Do not assume age alone requires replacement. Confirm material, compatibility, shutoff reliability, and installation requirements before recommending a connector be tightened or replaced.

J. Verification and Closeout

  1. Operate each serviced fixture through normal use. Cycle valves, faucets, drains, and toilets as appropriate to the service performed.
  2. Inspect immediately and again after the defined observation period. Check for drips, moisture, cabinet swelling, floor wetting, and abnormal operation at both intervals.
  3. Clean and dry all work areas. Leave surfaces clean and dry so any new leakage would be immediately visible.
  4. Record the completed maintenance cycle. Document valves exercised, aerators cleaned, drains serviced, toilet checks, connector findings, test results, referrals, and the next service condition.

Common Maintenance Cycle Inspection

Review every serviced item using the same checks regardless of which action was performed.

  • Scope check: confirm every serviced item was confirmed in the Chapter 10 record before work began.
  • Gentle-action check: confirm no valve, aerator, or connector was forced past normal resistance.
  • Chemical-free check: confirm no chemical drain cleaner was used at any point.
  • Referral check: confirm any item that failed during service was recorded as a referral condition rather than pushed further.
  • Verification check: confirm every serviced fixture has both an immediate and an observation-period test result recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Valve Leaks After Being Returned to Its Operating Position

Probable cause: the valve stem, packing, or body was disturbed during exercise, or the valve was already marginal before the cycle began.
Confirmation: inspect the stem, packing nut, and body connection with the valve in its operating position and dry surrounding surfaces to isolate the source.
Correction: stop further exercise of that valve, contain any active leakage with towels and the catch pan, and record the valve as a referral condition rather than continuing routine maintenance.
Reinspection: recheck the valve at the immediate test and again after the observation period before closing out the item.

An Aerator Will Not Reseat Without Leaking

Probable cause: cross-threading during reinstallation, a damaged washer, or debris trapped at the sealing surface.
Confirmation: remove the aerator again, inspect the threads and washer, and clean the sealing surface before reattempting.
Correction: reinstall using the correct thread engagement and orientation, replacing only the washer if it is visibly damaged and a matching replacement is available.
Reinspection: test flow and inspect the connection again before recording the aerator as serviced.

A Sink Drain Continues to Run Slowly After Mechanical Clearing

Probable cause: the blockage extends beyond the accessible reach of the approved hand-operated tool, or a vent or main-drain condition is contributing.
Confirmation: observe drain speed again after clearing and check whether other fixtures on the same line are also slow.
Correction: stop mechanical clearing at the approved limit, do not introduce a chemical drain cleaner, and record the fixture as a referral condition for further plumbing evaluation.
Reinspection: confirm the referral is recorded before the fixture is considered closed for this maintenance cycle.

A Toilet Continues Running After the Approved Check

Probable cause: a misaligned or worn flapper, an improperly set fill-valve level, or a linkage that is binding.
Confirmation: observe the fill valve and flapper through a complete fill-and-flush cycle and check the chain or linkage for proper length and alignment.
Correction: adjust only manufacturer-approved service components within scope; if adjustment does not resolve the running condition, record the toilet as a referral condition.
Reinspection: retest the fill-and-flush cycle and confirm the tank stops filling at the correct level before closing out the item.

A Connector Is Recommended for Replacement Without Verified Compatibility

Probable cause: the inspector assumed a connector needed replacement based on its age or appearance alone.
Confirmation: review the recorded finding for evidence of actual kinks, abrasion, corrosion, bulges, or strain rather than a general age observation.
Correction: revise the finding to state the specific observed condition, and verify material, compatibility, and installation requirements before recommending replacement.
Reinspection: confirm every connector recommendation in the record is supported by an observed condition rather than age alone.

Field Terms

Approved Basic-Maintenance Scope
The set of fixtures, valves, and connectors that Chapter 10 confirmed have no severe corrosion, active leakage, structural damage, or sewage exposure.
Gentle Exercise
Controlled hand-force movement of a valve through its approved range, stopped immediately at binding, leakage, or unusual resistance.
Minor Accessible Buildup
Debris reachable and removable with an approved hand-operated tool without disassembling concealed piping.
Referral Condition
A failure or defect encountered during this cycle, such as leakage, binding, corrosion, or sewage odor, that stops maintenance on that item and routes it for further evaluation.
Observation Period
The defined interval after servicing a fixture during which the inspector rechecks for drips, moisture, cabinet swelling, or floor wetting.
Plumbing Maintenance Cycle Record
The single dated document recording valves exercised, aerators cleaned, drains serviced, toilet checks, connector findings, test results, and referrals from this project.
Cross-Threading
Misaligned thread engagement during reassembly that can cause a fitting to leak or fail to seat correctly.
Flow-Control Component
An aerator screen, washer, or restrictor that regulates water flow and must not be removed during cleaning.

# Book-Level Authoring Rule

This file controls the actual project sequence for Book 22.

For every chapter, Claude must preserve the approved project title, filename, competency range, main-project outcome, and numbered sequence; expand every numbered step into complete field-manual prose; add point-of-use safety cues, access controls, inspection limits, weather controls, electrical and combustion-safety alerts, moisture and contamination alerts, manufacturer-instruction cues, referral thresholds, maintenance intervals, documentation standards, competency assessment, visual briefs, troubleshooting, and project closeout; not redesign the project while drafting; not omit or merge required steps; and not invent code requirements, service intervals, alarm replacement dates, roofing life estimates, structural diagnoses, crack tolerances, moisture limits, filter sizes, appliance clearances, electrical ratings, plumbing test procedures, combustion values, or repair specifications that are not supplied by adopted requirements, manufacturer instructions, approved property records, qualified professional direction, or the chapter project specification.

Property inspection and preventive maintenance are evidence-driven activities. The inspector must observe, operate where safe, compare current conditions with known requirements and prior records, document change, correct only work within the approved maintenance scope, and refer conditions that require destructive investigation, testing, design, permitting, specialized tools, or licensed trade work. The chapter author must treat the property as an interconnected system of site drainage, structure, enclosure, interior finishes, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, appliances, safety devices, occupant use, records, and recurring maintenance.

The following controls apply throughout the book:

- Use the approved inspection scope, property information, construction documents, prior reports, maintenance history, repair records, warranties, manuals, product labels, service records, permits, inspection records, and adopted building, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, fire, accessibility, environmental, and occupational-safety requirements.
- Confirm ownership authorization, occupancy, access, security, pets, children, vulnerable occupants, tenant rights, work hours, weather, and areas excluded from inspection.
- Establish whether the work is an owner maintenance inspection, contractor condition assessment, pre-repair inspection, seasonal inspection, or other approved activity. Do not represent the project as a regulated real-estate inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code-compliance certification unless the person and scope are qualified and authorized for that service.
- Inspect only areas that are safely and reasonably accessible. Do not force access, dismantle finishes, move unsafe loads, enter prohibited spaces, or damage property merely to improve visibility.
- Use a consistent route, component inventory, observation terminology, photograph system, severity classification, and closeout method so findings can be compared over time.
- Record the inspection date, time, weather, recent precipitation, occupancy condition, utilities available, equipment operating status, areas not inspected, and limitations affecting the findings.
- Separate observed conditions from interpretations, suspected causes, owner reports, and recommendations.
- Do not state that a concealed condition is satisfactory when it was not visible or tested.
- Do not diagnose structural adequacy, electrical safety, combustion performance, environmental contamination, or code compliance from appearance alone.
- Prioritize active life-safety hazards, fire and carbon-monoxide risks, electrical hazards, fuel-gas concerns, uncontrolled water entry, sewage, structural instability, unsafe stairs or guards, and blocked egress before routine maintenance or cosmetic work.
- Stop the inspection and secure or isolate the area when an immediate hazard is encountered.
- Contact emergency services, the utility, building management, or the appropriate qualified professional when the approved emergency procedure requires it.
- Treat damaged, missing, overheated, wet, arcing, sparking, buzzing, loose, or exposed electrical components as referral conditions.
- Do not remove service-panel dead fronts, junction-box covers, device covers, lighting canopies, equipment panels, or appliance covers unless the project expressly authorizes qualified de-energized service work.
- Do not touch live conductors, insert tools into electrical equipment, defeat interlocks, or use improvised electrical tests.
- Do not operate a shutoff valve, breaker, disconnect, appliance, pump, fireplace, or mechanical system when doing so may cause leakage, flooding, electrical damage, fuel release, equipment failure, freezing, overheating, or loss of essential service.
- Coordinate any operation that could interrupt water, power, heat, cooling, ventilation, refrigeration, medical equipment, sump pumping, alarms, security, or fire protection.
- Treat fuel-gas odor, suspected combustion spillage, soot, flame abnormalities, damaged venting, overheating, or carbon-monoxide alarm activation as immediate referral conditions.
- Do not perform combustion analysis, refrigerant service, electrical diagnosis, gas work, internal furnace or boiler service, or safety-control bypassing within this book’s basic maintenance scope.
- Use only safe, manufacturer-approved observation points for roofs, chimneys, gutters, attics, crawlspaces, equipment, and elevated exterior components.
- Prefer binocular, ground, window, balcony, camera-pole, drone, or other approved remote observation when roof walking is not specifically authorized and safely controlled.
- Do not walk roofs that are steep, wet, icy, snow-covered, brittle, damaged, excessively hot, fragile, contaminated, or outside the worker’s approved fall-protection plan.
- Use ladders only on firm, level support with required angle, extension, securing, access control, and three-point-contact practices.
- Do not work from the top steps, overreach, bridge ladders, place ladders on boxes or unstable surfaces, or use metal ladders near electrical hazards.
- Do not enter attics, crawlspaces, or confined areas with unsafe access, exposed wiring, suspected asbestos, vermiculite, mold, sewage, pests, aggressive animals, standing water, low oxygen, fuel-gas odor, extreme heat, damaged structure, or other uncontrolled hazards.
- Do not step between attic framing members or compress, disturb, or contaminate insulation unnecessarily.
- Do not disturb suspect lead-containing coatings, asbestos-containing materials, vermiculite insulation, mold, pest waste, silica-producing material, or other environmental hazards without the required assessment and controls.
- Treat staining, odor, condensation, swelling, corrosion, efflorescence, decay, fungal growth, insect evidence, and elevated moisture as evidence requiring source investigation rather than a cosmetic defect alone.
- Correct the source of water or moisture before repairing paint, drywall, flooring, trim, insulation, or finishes.
- Inspect water management from the roof and site down: roof covering, flashing, gutters, downspouts, discharge, grading, foundation, crawlspace or basement, plumbing, condensate, and interior evidence.
- Do not seal drainage openings, siding weeps, masonry weeps, window drainage paths, roof ventilation, appliance combustion air, dryer exhaust, plumbing vents, attic ventilation, or other intentional openings.
- Do not use caulk as a universal substitute for flashing, drainage, mechanical fastening, joint design, or failed materials.
- Do not excavate beside foundations, alter grading, extend downspouts, or redirect runoff in a way that harms neighboring property, septic systems, wells, slopes, retaining walls, public drainage, or the building foundation.
- Classify foundation cracks, displacement, rotation, settlement indicators, water entry, and floor or wall movement by observable characteristics without assigning structural significance beyond the inspector’s qualifications.
- Refer active movement, significant displacement, bowing, leaning, failed retaining walls, damaged beams or posts, widespread floor slope, or other structural concerns to the appropriate qualified professional.
- Operate doors, windows, plumbing fixtures, switches, lights, fans, alarms, appliances, and equipment only when normal operation is safe, authorized, and unlikely to cause damage.
- Use non-destructive maintenance methods first. Do not dismantle assemblies or enlarge defects merely to inspect them.
- Use product-specific tools, cleaners, lubricants, filters, batteries, hoses, sealants, and replacement parts.
- Verify compatibility before applying oil, grease, graphite, silicone, detergent, bleach, drain cleaner, biocide, caulk, patching material, or other maintenance product.
- Do not mix cleaning chemicals or use chemical drain cleaners as a routine substitute for mechanical cleaning and source correction.
- Exercise shutoff valves gently and stop if the valve leaks, binds, corrodes, lacks support, or appears likely to fail.
- Do not rely on a valve that has not been proven to stop flow; document inaccessible, inoperable, or missing shutoffs.
- Maintain access to electrical panels, water shutoffs, fuel shutoffs, cleanouts, traps, filters, condensate drains, appliance connections, fire extinguishers, alarms, and emergency exits.
- Verify that appliance anti-tip devices, drain pans, hoses, ducts, restraints, clearances, and service access remain in place where required.
- Treat dryer lint accumulation, damaged transition ducts, crushed ducts, disconnected exhaust, long or inaccessible vent paths, and indoor discharge as fire, moisture, and air-quality concerns.
- Replace HVAC filters only with the approved size, airflow direction, type, and performance range for the system.
- Do not install a filter that creates unapproved airflow restriction or bypass.
- Test smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms using their built-in test controls and manufacturer procedures. Do not use flame, smoke, vehicle exhaust, or improvised gas sources.
- Determine alarm service life, battery type, interconnected status, location, and replacement requirements from product labels, records, manufacturer instructions, and adopted requirements rather than from a generic interval.
- Do not paint, cover, relocate, silence, disconnect, or remove required alarms without approved replacement and authorization.
- Inspect fire extinguishers visually within the chapter scope, but refer discharged, damaged, corroded, recalled, improperly rated, inaccessible, unsealed, or out-of-service units to the appropriate service or replacement process.
- Keep findings factual and actionable. Each reported item should identify location, component, observed condition, consequence or priority, recommended next action, and supporting photograph where practical.
- Use a consistent priority system that distinguishes immediate hazards, urgent water or system failures, short-term repairs, recurring maintenance, monitoring items, planned capital replacement, and cosmetic preferences.
- Do not hide an urgent defect inside a general maintenance list or treat a cosmetic issue as equivalent to a life-safety concern.
- Record every maintenance action with date, person, product or part, model or size, observed condition, work performed, test result, next due condition, and photograph where useful.
- Keep manuals, warranties, permits, service contracts, invoices, inspection reports, floor plans, shutoff maps, equipment schedules, paint information, roofing information, and replacement histories organized for future use.
- Protect personal information, access codes, security-system details, keys, financial records, tenant records, and photographs that reveal private conditions.
- Reinspect after maintenance and document whether the condition was corrected, improved, unchanged, or referred.
- Do not mark a condition closed merely because it is temporarily dry, quiet, reset, lubricated, cleaned, or concealed.
- Complete each project by restoring access, guards, covers, filters, panels, alarms, doors, locks, fixtures, equipment settings, and occupant functions affected by the work.

The numbered sequences below are the minimum required maintenance-and-inspection workflow. Claude may divide a step into smaller substeps but may not change the technical order without documenting the controlling reason.

## System-Sequence Control

The chapter order is instructional. A real property-maintenance program must follow the approved baseline-to-closeout sequence even when that sequence crosses chapter boundaries.

In particular:

- Property identification, authorization, inspection scope, limitations, weather, utilities, occupancy, and route are established before inspection begins.
- A baseline walkthrough is completed before recurring records, seasonal comparisons, or repair priorities are created.
- Inspection findings are documented before maintenance changes the evidence.
- Exterior water management is inspected from roof discharge through grading and foundation before interior stains are treated as isolated finish defects.
- Roof and gutter work proceeds only from approved access and weather conditions.
- Siding, trim, flashing, and sealants are evaluated as a drainage and drying system rather than as appearance alone.
- Foundation cracks and movement indicators are measured and documented consistently before monitoring or referral.
- Window and door service follows inspection and cause identification; adjustment does not substitute for correcting structural movement, water entry, failed frames, or unsafe egress.
- Plumbing maintenance begins with verified shutoffs, controlled water use, and leak response.
- Electrical inspection remains visual and operational within approved limits; no live repair is included.
- Alarm testing follows manufacturer procedures and is completed before the property is represented as life-safety ready.
- HVAC filter and condensate maintenance preserves airflow, drainage, service access, and manufacturer requirements.
- Attic inspection proceeds only after access, temperature, electrical, structural, contamination, and respiratory hazards are accepted.
- Appliance and laundry inspection preserves fuel, electrical, water, drainage, exhaust, restraint, and service-clearance controls.
- Seasonal maintenance records are updated immediately after work so the next cycle begins from verified information.
- Chapter 19 converts findings into accountable work by priority, owner, timing, dependency, cost class, and verification method.
- Chapter 20 must pass scope, exterior, interior, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, life-safety, appliance, documentation, prioritization, maintenance-calendar, and owner-handoff reviews.

## Capstone Inspection Control

Chapter 20 uses one approved occupied or unoccupied residential property that can be inspected without destructive investigation.

The preferred capstone is a **complete single-family residence inspection and maintenance-planning exercise** covering the accessible site, exterior, roof drainage, foundation, interior rooms, plumbing fixtures, visible electrical devices, life-safety equipment, HVAC maintenance points, attic or alternate upper-building area, appliances, laundry or utility area, and property records.

A townhouse, condominium unit, duplex unit, small accessory dwelling unit, or similar residence may be used when ownership boundaries, common elements, access rights, association responsibilities, shared systems, and excluded areas are documented.

A commercial building, multifamily common-area inspection, regulated home inspection for a property transaction, code-compliance certification, structural evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, chimney inspection, sewer-scope inspection, invasive moisture investigation, or internal mechanical and electrical service is not a default capstone. It requires the relevant authorization, qualifications, equipment, standards, and professional controls.

## Capstone Continuity Rule

The capstone may use property records, repairs, and finish information developed in Books 1 through 21, but every condition included in the final plan must be supported by a current observation, valid service record, applicable manufacturer instruction, or clearly labeled limitation.

The capstone is handed off as a complete baseline property condition file, prioritized repair plan, recurring maintenance calendar, safety-readiness record, and documentation package ready for the blueprint-reading and construction-document work introduced in Book 23.

# Chapter 1

## Project Title
Perform a Complete Residential Property Walkthrough

## Filename
`chapter-01-complete-residential-property-walkthrough.html`

## Competencies
**MI-001 through MI-006:** Establish an inspection route, inspect exterior areas, inspect interior rooms, identify visible defects, photograph findings, and create a baseline property condition list.

## Main Project
Perform one systematic, non-destructive walkthrough of an accessible residence and produce a dated baseline condition list with photographs, limitations, immediate hazards, and follow-up needs.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Obtain owner authorization, property address, occupancy status, building type, approximate age, known additions, and areas excluded from inspection.
2. Collect available plans, prior inspection reports, repair records, warranties, permits, service records, and owner-reported concerns.
3. Define the inspection purpose, scope, limitations, reporting terminology, priority system, and conditions that require immediate notification.
4. Record date, time, weather, recent precipitation, exterior temperature, utilities available, equipment operating status, and occupant constraints.
5. Prepare the inspection tools, camera, flashlight, binoculars, measuring tools, moisture-screening tools where authorized, labels, forms, personal protective equipment, and communication device.
6. Confirm safe access, adequate lighting, pets controlled, children excluded from work areas, and emergency exits available.
7. Establish a consistent route beginning with the site and exterior, continuing through service and utility areas, then moving room by room through the interior.
8. Photograph all building elevations and major site relationships before close inspection.
9. Walk the property boundary areas that are accessible and note slopes, drainage paths, retaining walls, trees, hardscape, utilities, wells, septic features, and adjacent conditions.
10. Inspect the exterior from grade to roofline without climbing or entering restricted areas.
11. Record visible conditions at roofing, gutters, downspouts, siding, trim, flashing, windows, doors, decks, stairs, guards, porches, foundation, and penetrations.
12. Identify active water discharge, ponding, erosion, staining, decay, corrosion, loose components, unsafe walking surfaces, and blocked access.
13. Enter the building and identify the main water shutoff, fuel shutoff where safely visible, electrical service location, HVAC equipment, alarm locations, and emergency exits.
14. Inspect each interior room from ceiling to walls, openings, fixtures, floors, and built-in components using the same sequence.
15. Operate a representative or approved set of doors, windows, lights, fans, faucets, drains, toilets, locks, and appliances only when safe.
16. Inspect kitchens, bathrooms, laundry areas, utility rooms, basements, crawlspace entries, and attic access points for moisture, ventilation, clearance, and service concerns.
17. Identify visible cracks, stains, swelling, odors, surface movement, damaged finishes, loose hardware, trip hazards, and signs of previous repair.
18. Separate immediate hazards from active failures, maintenance needs, monitoring items, capital replacements, and cosmetic conditions.
19. Assign each finding a unique identifier tied to location and component.
20. Take an overview photograph and a close photograph for significant findings, including a scale reference where useful.
21. Record areas that were inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, not operating, or outside the approved scope.
22. Do not state that uninspected or concealed components are satisfactory.
23. Review immediate hazards and active water or utility concerns with the responsible party before leaving the property.
24. Prepare the baseline condition list with location, observation, priority, recommended next action, photo reference, and status.
25. Compare the list with owner-reported concerns and identify items requiring specialized inspection.
26. File the completed walkthrough, photographs, limitations, and urgent notifications in the property record.

## Required Outcome
A complete baseline property walkthrough record with consistent coverage, factual observations, photographs, access limitations, immediate hazard notifications, and an actionable condition list.

22. Create reminders or calendar entries for near-term service dates and seasonal tasks.
23. Back up digital records and protect originals from moisture, fire, loss, or unauthorized access.
24. Test the system by retrieving one equipment manual, one photograph, one repair record, and one upcoming maintenance task.
25. Correct duplicate, missing, inconsistent, or inaccessible records.
26. Document the record-maintenance procedure so future users can update the system consistently.
27. Review the binder or digital system with the owner or responsible party and confirm who maintains it.

## Required Outcome
A usable and secure property documentation system with indexed records, equipment inventory, service history, photographs, recurring schedules, open-item tracking, and defined record ownership.

# Chapter 3

## Project Title
Inspect Roof, Gutters, Downspouts, and Water Discharge

## Filename
`chapter-03-inspect-roof-gutters-and-downspouts.html`

## Competencies
**MI-013 through MI-018:** Inspect the roof surface from safe locations, inspect gutters, inspect downspouts, identify drainage defects, verify discharge away from the structure, and document roof-water issues.

## Main Project
Inspect one residential roof-drainage system from approved safe observation points and document conditions affecting roof covering, flashing, gutters, downspouts, discharge, and foundation water exposure.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Review roof age if known, covering type, slope, number of roof areas, prior leaks, repairs, warranties, drainage plan, and recent weather.
2. Confirm inspection scope and whether roof walking is excluded or separately authorized.
3. Check weather, wind, precipitation, temperature, lightning, roof-surface condition, and daylight before exterior observation.
4. Select safe ground, window, balcony, ladder, camera-pole, binocular, or approved remote observation points.
5. Establish public and ladder exclusion zones where elevated access is used.
6. Do not access a roof that is wet, icy, snow-covered, brittle, damaged, steep, contaminated, or outside the approved fall-protection plan.
7. Photograph each roof plane, eave, rake, valley, penetration, chimney, wall intersection, gutter run, downspout, and discharge point visible from safe locations.
8. Identify the roof covering material and visible installation pattern without assigning remaining service life solely from appearance.
9. Inspect for missing, displaced, cracked, curled, blistered, punctured, worn, or debris-covered roofing.
10. Inspect visible ridges, hips, valleys, eaves, rakes, roof-wall intersections, chimneys, skylights, vents, plumbing stacks, and other penetrations.
11. Identify exposed fasteners, failed sealant, loose flashing, damaged boots, open joints, corrosion, staining, biological growth, and temporary patches.
12. Inspect gutters for debris, vegetation, standing water, corrosion, holes, loose joints, sag, poor slope, detached hangers, and overflow evidence.
13. Inspect gutter attachment and fascia condition from a safe location.
14. Inspect downspouts for missing sections, open seams, crushed areas, loose straps, blockage evidence, reverse slope, and disconnected underground drains.
15. Trace each downspout to its discharge point where accessible.
16. Verify that discharge is directed through an approved path away from the foundation and does not create erosion, icing, ponding, neighbor impacts, or walkway hazards.
17. Inspect splash blocks, extensions, leaders, underground connections, catch basins, and outlets for position and visible function.
18. Observe roof runoff during rain or perform an approved controlled flow observation only when it can be done safely and without causing damage.
19. Inspect exterior walls, soffits, fascia, ceilings, attic access areas, and upper-room finishes for related staining or moisture evidence.
20. Correlate interior evidence with roof areas but label the suspected source as unconfirmed until verified.
21. Identify immediate conditions such as active leakage, loose overhead material, blocked primary drainage, unsafe chimney components, or major storm damage.
22. Refer roofing, flashing, structural, chimney, electrical, or high-access concerns to the appropriate qualified professional.
23. Record each finding by roof plane or elevation, component, observed condition, priority, photo, and next action.
24. Record inaccessible or concealed roof areas and observation limitations.
25. Update the property maintenance log with inspection date, weather, findings, referrals, and recommended reinspection condition.

## Required Outcome
A documented roof-water-management inspection identifying visible covering, flashing, gutter, downspout, and discharge defects, with safe-access limitations and prioritized follow-up.

# Chapter 4

## Project Title
Clean and Maintain Gutters and Downspouts

## Filename
`chapter-04-clean-and-maintain-gutters-and-downspouts.html`

## Competencies
**MI-019 through MI-024:** Set up ladder access safely, remove gutter debris, flush downspouts, repair minor loose components, verify water flow, and inspect completed gutter maintenance.

## Main Project
Clean and complete approved minor maintenance on one accessible gutter-and-downspout system while preserving roof, fascia, drainage, landscaping, and worker safety.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Review the Chapter 3 findings, gutter material, height, access points, debris type, downspout routing, guards, electrical hazards, and maintenance limits.
2. Confirm weather and surface conditions are dry and stable enough for the approved work.
3. Exclude roofs, gutters, or elevations requiring unapproved roof walking, lifts, scaffolds, fall protection, or specialty access.
4. Inspect the ground for slope, soft soil, landscaping, traffic, doors, windows, wells, septic features, and underground drainage hazards.
5. Select the correct ladder type and length and inspect rails, feet, rungs, locks, rope, labels, and accessories.
6. Maintain required clearance from overhead and service electrical conductors.
7. Set the ladder on firm, level support at the approved angle and extension.
8. Secure or stabilize the ladder and establish a ground exclusion zone.
9. Protect siding, gutters, roof edges, and landscaping from ladder and debris damage.
10. Stage hand tools, debris containers, hose, approved flushing equipment, replacement fasteners, and personal protective equipment without carrying loose items while climbing.
11. Remove gutter guards or screens only through the manufacturer-approved procedure and label sections for reinstallation.
12. Remove debris by hand or approved tool in manageable quantities.
13. Do not push compacted debris into downspouts.
14. Inspect the exposed gutter for holes, corrosion, separated seams, failed end caps, loose hangers, standing water, and damaged fascia.
15. Bag debris at the work location and lower it without dropping material on occupants, glazing, landscaping, or walkways.
16. Clear accessible downspout inlets and strainers.
17. Test each downspout with a controlled amount of water from the top or approved access point.
18. Stop flushing when water backs up toward the building, enters the wall, leaks at concealed joints, or cannot be safely discharged.
19. Clear minor accessible blockage using approved non-damaging methods.
20. Do not use unapproved pressure, sharp tools, chemical cleaners, or methods that may separate concealed piping.
21. Tighten or replace only minor accessible loose straps, hangers, elbows, or extensions within the approved maintenance scope.
22. Use compatible fasteners and do not create new roof, fascia, or siding leaks.
23. Do not attempt to repair rotted fascia, failed roof edge, major sag, damaged concealed drainage, or unsafe high components as routine maintenance.
24. Reinstall gutter guards, screens, and strainers according to the product instructions.
25. Flush the system from the highest practical point and observe flow through gutters, outlets, downspouts, extensions, and final discharge.
26. Verify that water does not overflow, leak behind the gutter, pond at the foundation, erode soil, cross walkways, or affect neighboring property.
27. Clean siding, windows, roof edges, landscaping, and walkways affected by the work.
28. Remove the ladder and barriers only after tools, debris, and loose components are secured.
29. Inspect final gutter attachment, downspout support, flow, discharge, and work-area condition.
30. Record debris quantity, repairs, flow results, unresolved defects, photographs, and next maintenance trigger.

## Required Outcome
A clean and flowing accessible gutter system with minor loose components secured, downspouts tested, discharge verified, hazards referred, and maintenance recorded.

# Chapter 5

## Project Title
Inspect Siding, Trim, Flashing, and Exterior Sealants

## Filename
`chapter-05-inspect-siding-trim-flashing-and-sealants.html`

## Competencies
**MI-025 through MI-030:** Inspect siding condition, inspect exterior trim, identify failed caulk, inspect flashing details, identify rot or moisture staining, and document envelope repair needs.

## Main Project
Inspect one building exterior elevation-by-elevation and document visible defects in siding, trim, flashing, penetrations, drainage openings, coatings, and sealant joints.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Review exterior material types, known repairs, paint history, prior leaks, manufacturer information, and recent storm or irrigation exposure.
2. Record weather, sun exposure, and whether surfaces are wet, shaded, frozen, or obscured.
3. Establish a safe clockwise inspection route around the building.
4. Photograph each elevation and major wall transition before close observations.
5. Identify siding type, orientation, panel or board pattern, and intentional drainage or ventilation features.
6. Inspect siding for cracks, holes, buckling, warping, loose pieces, impact damage, open joints, corrosion, delamination, swelling, decay, and biological growth.
7. Inspect clearances above grade, roofs, decks, slabs, hardscape, and vegetation where visible.
8. Identify soil, mulch, snow, irrigation, or debris contacting materials not intended for that exposure.
9. Inspect corner boards, fascia, soffits, frieze, band boards, water tables, door trim, window trim, and other exterior wood or composite components.
10. Inspect paint or coating for peeling, blistering, chalking, bare substrate, rust staining, and failed end-grain protection.
11. Inspect roof-to-wall, deck-to-wall, window, door, horizontal trim, masonry, penetration, and material-transition flashing where visible.
12. Do not infer proper concealed flashing merely because sealant is present.
13. Inspect window and door head details, sills, thresholds, pans, weeps, and drainage paths that are visible.
14. Inspect penetrations for pipes, ducts, cables, lights, receptacles, hose bibbs, meters, vents, and fasteners.
15. Identify missing escutcheons, open holes, unsupported penetrations, failed boots, and sealant separated from one or both surfaces.
16. Distinguish joints designed for sealant from drainage openings, siding laps, weeps, vents, and movement joints that must remain open.
17. Inspect sealant for cracking, hardening, softening, tearing, adhesion loss, staining, incompatible paint, excessive width, and three-sided adhesion evidence.
18. Inspect for moisture staining, soft material, fungal growth, insect damage, rusted fasteners, and repeated patching.
19. Probe material only through an approved non-destructive or limited method and owner authorization.
20. Correlate exterior defects with interior stains, window or door problems, and drainage conditions.
21. Identify loose overhead material, open wall cavities, active leakage, major decay, unsafe cladding, or electrical exposure requiring prompt action.
22. Refer structural cladding, masonry veneer, stucco, EIFS, high-access, window installation, or concealed-envelope concerns to qualified professionals.
23. Record every finding by elevation, location, material, condition, priority, photograph, and recommended next action.
24. Identify areas concealed by vegetation, decks, additions, stored materials, snow, or inaccessible height.
25. Create an envelope repair list that separates cleaning, vegetation control, coating maintenance, sealant replacement, flashing repair, material replacement, and invasive investigation.
26. Update the maintenance log and schedule reinspection after repair or the next wet-weather condition.

## Required Outcome
An elevation-based exterior-envelope record identifying siding, trim, flashing, coating, penetration, moisture, and sealant concerns without blocking intentional drainage or overstating concealed conditions.

# Chapter 6

## Project Title
Inspect Foundation, Grading, Drainage, and Crawlspace Conditions

## Filename
`chapter-06-inspect-foundation-grading-drainage-and-crawlspace.html`

## Competencies

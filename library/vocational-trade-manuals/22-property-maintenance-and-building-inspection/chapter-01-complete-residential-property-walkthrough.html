Job Overview

This project qualifies you to perform one complete, non-destructive walkthrough of an accessible residence and produce a dated baseline property condition list. The finished work product is a single written record: a location-by-location inventory of observed conditions, supported by photographs, that identifies immediate hazards, active defects, and areas that could not be inspected.

You will confirm authorization and scope, gather available property records, establish a consistent inspection route, walk the exterior from grade to roofline, walk the interior room by room, operate a representative set of fixtures and controls only where it is safe to do so, and record every finding with a location, a description, a priority, and a photograph reference.

This is the first project in this book. It establishes the route, terminology, priority system, and documentation method that every later chapter in this book depends on. Chapter 2 organizes this baseline record into a permanent property binder. Chapters 3 through 18 return to the areas identified here in greater depth. Chapter 19 converts the accumulated findings into a prioritized repair plan, and Chapter 20 repeats this walkthrough discipline at full scale as the book's capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-001 — Establish an inspection route covering the accessible exterior and interior of a residence in a consistent sequence.
  • MI-002 — Inspect exterior areas from grade to roofline without climbing or entering restricted areas.
  • MI-003 — Inspect interior rooms using a consistent ceiling-to-floor, opening-to-fixture sequence.
  • MI-004 — Identify visible defects and separate observed conditions from suspected causes.
  • MI-005 — Photograph findings with an overview image and a close image, using a scale reference where useful.
  • MI-006 — Create a baseline property condition list with location, observation, priority, recommended next action, and photo reference for every finding.

Where This Skill Fits

This is the first project in Book 22, and it has no prior chapter to build on. Instead, it establishes the foundation every later chapter uses: the route you walk here is the same route you will use every time you reinspect this property, the priority categories you assign here are the same categories used through Chapter 20, and the observation-versus-interpretation discipline you practice here controls how every finding in this book is worded.

Chapter 2 takes the condition list you produce here and files it into a permanent, organized property record. Chapters 3 through 18 revisit each system identified in this walkthrough — roof drainage, siding, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, attic, and appliances — in far greater technical depth. Nothing in this project authorizes destructive investigation, testing, or repair; it only establishes what is visible today.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining owner authorization and defining the areas included in and excluded from the walkthrough
  • establishing a single consistent exterior-then-interior inspection route
  • inspecting the exterior from grade to roofline using only safe, ground-level or approved observation points
  • inspecting interior rooms in a consistent top-to-bottom, opening-to-fixture sequence
  • operating a representative set of doors, windows, lights, faucets, drains, toilets, and appliances only when it is safe to do so
  • photographing every significant finding with an overview image and a close image
  • producing one baseline property condition list with location, observation, priority, recommended next action, and photo reference for every finding

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • represent this walkthrough as a regulated real-estate inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code-compliance certification
  • climb onto the roof, enter an attic or crawlspace, remove an electrical panel dead front or device cover, or perform any destructive or invasive investigation
  • operate a shutoff valve, breaker, disconnect, appliance, pump, or mechanical system when doing so may cause leakage, flooding, electrical damage, fuel release, or loss of essential service
  • diagnose structural adequacy, electrical safety, combustion performance, environmental contamination, or code compliance from appearance alone

Any condition that appears to be an immediate hazard must stop the routine walkthrough and be handled per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before the walkthrough continues.

Trade Note

Every finding in this chapter is recorded as an observed condition, not as a conclusion. Writing "efflorescence visible at the northeast foundation wall" is correct. Writing "the foundation is failing" is not, unless a qualified professional has made that determination. Confusing observation with interpretation is the single most common documentation error corrected in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the walkthrough until owner authorization, the inspection scope, and the excluded areas are confirmed and recorded in writing (Step 1). A finding recorded before authorization and scope are confirmed must be re-verified before it is entered on the baseline condition list.

Shop Tip

Photograph every building elevation and a wide interior overview of every room before making any close observations. Working from a wide-to-close photograph sequence keeps the record consistent even if the detailed notes are reorganized later.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every inspection in this book, from this baseline walkthrough to the Chapter 20 capstone, follows the same five-part sequence: confirm scope, observe, document, separate hazard from routine, and record limitations. Learning that sequence here, at the whole-property level, means every later chapter starts from a route and a vocabulary you already trust.

Site and exteriorBoundary, grading, hardscape, and building elevations from grade to roofline
Service and utility areasMain water shutoff, fuel shutoff where visible, electrical service, HVAC equipment, alarms, exits
Interior roomsCeiling, walls, openings, fixtures, floors, and built-ins, room by room
Wet and access areasKitchens, bathrooms, laundry, utility rooms, basement, crawlspace and attic access points
DocumentationFinding identifiers, priority category, photo reference, and limitations for every location
CloseoutImmediate-hazard review with the responsible party before leaving the property

Observation Versus Interpretation

Record what is visible, audible, or measurable. Do not record a diagnosis, a cause, or a code-compliance conclusion unless it comes from an identified prior report or a qualified professional. "Stain visible on the ceiling below the upstairs bathroom" is an observation. "The upstairs bathroom is leaking" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact unless confirmed.

Documentation Cue — One Finding, One Identifier, One Photo Pair

Assign a unique finding identifier tied to location and component before photographing. Take one overview photograph and one close photograph for every significant finding, using a scale reference where useful. Do not batch multiple unrelated findings under a single identifier or photograph.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Hazards Stop the Routine Walkthrough

If you encounter active fire or carbon-monoxide alarm activation, fuel-gas odor, active flooding or sewage, exposed energized wiring, unsafe stairs or guards, or blocked egress, stop the routine walkthrough immediately. Secure or isolate the area if it is safe to do so, and notify the owner, building management, the utility, or emergency services as the situation requires before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Access, Pets, and Occupants

Confirm pets are controlled and children are excluded from work areas before beginning. Maintain awareness of emergency exits throughout the walkthrough. Do not force access, dismantle finishes, move unsafe loads, or enter a prohibited or hazardous space merely to improve visibility.

Code Alert — This Walkthrough Is Not a Regulated Inspection

Do not represent this walkthrough as a regulated real-estate transaction inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code-compliance certification unless the person performing it and the defined scope are qualified and authorized for that specific service.

E. Preparation — Authorization, Records, and Route Planning

  1. Obtain owner authorization and confirm property information. Record the property address, occupancy status, building type, approximate age, known additions, and areas excluded from inspection.
  2. Collect available property records. Gather plans, prior inspection reports, repair records, warranties, permits, service records, and owner-reported concerns before entering the property.
  3. Define the inspection purpose and terminology. Confirm the scope, limitations, reporting terminology, priority system, and conditions requiring immediate notification, and record them on the worksheet.
  4. Record conditions at the start of the walkthrough. Record date, time, weather, recent precipitation, exterior temperature, utilities available, equipment operating status, and occupant constraints.
  5. Prepare inspection tools and PPE. Stage the camera, flashlight, binoculars, measuring tools, labels, forms, personal protective equipment, and communication device.
  6. Confirm safe access before entry. Confirm adequate lighting, pets controlled, children excluded from work areas, and emergency exits available.

F. Site and Exterior Walkthrough (Grade to Roofline)

  1. Establish the route. Begin with the site and exterior, continue through service and utility areas, then move room by room through the interior, and do not change this sequence mid-walkthrough.
  2. Photograph each building elevation. Photograph all elevations and major site relationships before beginning close inspection.
  3. Walk the accessible boundary areas. Note slopes, drainage paths, retaining walls, trees, hardscape, utilities, wells, septic features, and adjacent conditions.
  4. Inspect the exterior from grade to roofline without climbing or entering restricted areas. Use ground-level, window, or binocular observation only.
  5. Record visible conditions at each exterior component. Cover roofing, gutters, downspouts, siding, trim, flashing, windows, doors, decks, stairs, guards, porches, foundation, and penetrations.
  6. Identify active water and access concerns. Note active water discharge, ponding, erosion, staining, decay, corrosion, loose components, unsafe walking surfaces, and blocked access.

G. Service Areas and Interior Walkthrough

  1. Enter the building and locate service points. Identify the main water shutoff, fuel shutoff where safely visible, electrical service location, HVAC equipment, alarm locations, and emergency exits.
  2. Inspect each interior room in the same sequence. Work from ceiling to walls, openings, fixtures, floors, and built-in components in every room.
  3. Operate only a safe, representative set of fixtures and controls. Operate doors, windows, lights, fans, faucets, drains, toilets, locks, and appliances only when it is safe to do so.
  4. Inspect wet and access areas. Cover kitchens, bathrooms, laundry areas, utility rooms, basements, crawlspace entries, and attic access points for moisture, ventilation, clearance, and service concerns.
  5. Identify visible interior defects. Note cracks, stains, swelling, odors, surface movement, damaged finishes, loose hardware, trip hazards, and signs of previous repair.

H. Finding Classification and Photography

  1. Separate findings by priority. Classify each finding as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  2. Assign a unique finding identifier. Tie each identifier to a specific location and component.
  3. Photograph each significant finding. Take an overview photograph and a close photograph, including a scale reference where useful.
  4. Record inaccessible or excluded areas. Note areas that were inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, not operating, or outside the approved scope, and do not state that these areas are satisfactory.

I. Closeout and Recordkeeping

  1. Review immediate hazards and active water or utility concerns with the responsible party before leaving the property.
  2. Prepare the baseline condition list. Include location, observation, priority, recommended next action, photo reference, and status for every finding.
  3. Compare the list with owner-reported concerns. Identify items requiring specialized inspection beyond this chapter's scope.
  4. File the completed walkthrough. Store the baseline condition list, photographs, limitations, and urgent notifications in the property record.

Common Walkthrough Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which segment of the route it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding has a unique identifier tied to a specific location and component.
  • Observation check: confirm the recorded text states what was seen, heard, or measured, without an unsupported cause or diagnosis.
  • Priority check: confirm every finding is classified as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  • Photograph check: confirm every significant finding has an overview photograph and a close photograph.
  • Limitation check: confirm every inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, or excluded area is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Finding States a Cause Instead of an Observation

Probable cause: the inspector recorded a suspected source or diagnosis instead of the visible condition.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any word implying a cause, source, or compliance conclusion not supported by a prior report or qualified professional.
Correction: rewrite the finding to state only what was seen, heard, or measured, and move any suspected cause to a clearly labeled "suspected cause, unconfirmed" note.
Reinspection: review the full condition list for the same wording pattern before filing it.

A Room or Area Was Skipped

Probable cause: the fixed route was not followed, or an area was assumed to be identical to a similar room.
Confirmation: compare the recorded route segments against the property's actual room count and exterior elevations.
Correction: return to the skipped area and complete the same ceiling-to-floor or grade-to-roofline sequence used elsewhere.
Reinspection: confirm the finished condition list includes every segment in Section B of the work order.

A Finding Has No Photograph or an Incomplete Photograph Pair

Probable cause: the close photograph was taken without the matching overview photograph, or the photograph was not labeled with the finding identifier.
Confirmation: check the photograph index against the condition list for every significant finding.
Correction: return to the location, if still accessible, and capture the missing overview or close photograph with the correct identifier.
Reinspection: verify every significant finding has a complete, correctly labeled photograph pair before filing the record.

An Inaccessible Area Was Recorded as Satisfactory

Probable cause: the inspector assumed a concealed or unreachable area was in acceptable condition rather than recording it as a limitation.
Confirmation: review the condition list for areas marked "OK" or omitted entirely that were not actually observed.
Correction: change the entry to a recorded limitation stating the area was inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, not operating, or outside the approved scope.
Reinspection: confirm no entry states or implies that an unobserved area is satisfactory.

An Immediate Hazard Was Buried in the General List

Probable cause: the finding was recorded in sequence with routine items instead of being flagged and escalated at the time it was found.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving fire, carbon monoxide, electrical exposure, active flooding, sewage, or blocked egress.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard, notify the responsible party per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate hazard is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the walkthrough is closed out.

Cleanup

This project ends with a filed record, not a set of loose notes.

  • Confirm every door, panel, cover, and access point disturbed during the walkthrough is restored to its original position
  • Confirm pets, children, and occupant areas are undisturbed at the conclusion of the walkthrough
  • Transfer handwritten notes to the Baseline Condition List worksheet if a digital form was not used in the field
  • Back up or print all photographs and match them to their finding identifiers
  • Store the completed baseline condition list, photographs, and any immediate-hazard notifications in the property record established in Chapter 2

Field Terms

Limitation
An area or component that was inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, not operating, or outside the approved scope.
Baseline Condition List
The single dated document recording every finding, limitation, and immediate-hazard notification from this walkthrough.
Overview Photograph
A wide photograph establishing the location and context of a finding before a close photograph is taken.
Close Photograph
A detailed photograph of a specific finding, including a scale reference where useful.
Immediate Hazard
A condition such as active fire, carbon-monoxide alarm activation, fuel-gas odor, active flooding, sewage, exposed energized wiring, or blocked egress that stops the routine walkthrough for review and notification.
Baseline Walkthrough
The first systematic property inspection performed on a property, against which every later inspection is compared.

Skill Check

  1. Why must the inspection route follow the same fixed sequence every time this property is reinspected?
  2. What is the difference between an observed condition and an interpretation, and why must a finding avoid stating an unsupported cause?
  3. What must the inspector do immediately upon encountering an active fire, carbon-monoxide alarm activation, fuel-gas odor, active flooding, sewage, or blocked-egress condition?
  4. What six pieces of information must every entry on the baseline condition list include?
  5. Why must an inaccessible or concealed area be recorded as a limitation rather than marked as satisfactory?
  6. Under what conditions is roof climbing, attic entry, crawlspace entry, or panel-cover removal authorized during this project?
  7. Why must an overview photograph and a close photograph both be taken for a significant finding?
  8. Why must this walkthrough not be represented as a regulated real-estate inspection, engineering evaluation, or code-compliance certification?
  9. What must the inspector do before leaving the property if an immediate hazard or active water or utility concern was found?

Project Closeout

You have completed the first project in the property maintenance and building inspection sequence: performing a complete residential property walkthrough. This project required you to establish a consistent inspection route (MI-001), inspect exterior areas from grade to roofline (MI-002), inspect interior rooms in a consistent sequence (MI-003), identify visible defects while separating observation from interpretation (MI-004), photograph findings with overview and close images (MI-005), and create a baseline property condition list (MI-006).

You now have a dated baseline record of this property's condition, a repeatable inspection route, and a documentation method that carries directly into the next project: organizing this record, along with equipment information and service history, into a permanent property maintenance log and inspection binder.

File your completed baseline condition list, photographs, and any immediate-hazard notifications where they can be retrieved for Chapter 2. You will reuse this exact route and documentation method throughout the rest of this book.

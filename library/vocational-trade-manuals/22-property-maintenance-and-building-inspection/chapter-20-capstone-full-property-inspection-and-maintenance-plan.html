Job Overview

This capstone project integrates every competency taught in Chapters 1 through 19 into a single, complete property engagement. The finished work product is a full property condition and maintenance package: a systematic inspection covering site, exterior, roof drainage, foundation, interior rooms, plumbing, electrical, life-safety devices, HVAC, attic, appliances, and laundry or utility areas; a set of approved minor preventive-maintenance tasks actually performed and reinspected; a complete finding register; a 30-60-90 day and long-term repair plan; a twelve-month seasonal maintenance calendar; a list of required professional referrals; and a documented owner or responsible-party handoff.

You will use the same route, terminology, priority system, and documentation method established in Chapter 1, the property binder built in Chapter 2, and the system-by-system inspection and maintenance procedures taught in Chapters 3 through 18. You will not redesign that method here. This project asks you to run the entire system at once, on one real accessible property, and prove that you can hold every safety, scope, and documentation control in place across a full engagement rather than a single system.

This is the final project in Book 22. On completion, the property has a dated baseline condition file, verified life-safety readiness, a prioritized and accountable repair plan, and a maintenance calendar ready to guide the property forward. The completed package also becomes the reference record carried into Book 23, where the reader begins working from blueprints and construction documents.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-115 — Integrate exterior inspection findings across site, roof drainage, siding, foundation, and openings into one coordinated capstone record.
  • MI-116 — Integrate interior inspection findings across finish surfaces, plumbing, electrical devices, and life-safety equipment into the same record.
  • MI-117 — Perform and reinspect approved preventive maintenance across plumbing, HVAC, alarms, and exterior systems within the established scope limits.
  • MI-118 — Test and document life-safety devices and emergency egress across the full property using manufacturer procedures.
  • MI-119 — Build a prioritized 30-60-90 day repair plan and a twelve-month seasonal maintenance calendar from the completed finding register.
  • MI-120 — Assemble and deliver a complete, quality-reviewed property documentation package with professional referrals and a documented owner handoff.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior chapter in this book fed a piece of this capstone. Chapter 1 supplied the route and the observation-versus-interpretation discipline. Chapter 2 supplied the binder and equipment inventory that organize everything found here. Chapters 3 through 8 supplied the exterior, roof-drainage, and envelope inspection and minor maintenance methods. Chapters 9 through 16 supplied interior finish, plumbing, electrical, life-safety, HVAC, attic, and appliance methods. Chapters 17 and 18 supplied the seasonal maintenance cycle. Chapter 19 supplied the method for turning findings into a prioritized, accountable plan.

This project does not introduce a new inspection or maintenance technique. It requires you to sequence, coordinate, and document all of them on one property, in the order the Universal Master Instructions and this book's System-Sequence Control require, and to hand the finished package to the owner or responsible party. The documentation method, priority categories, and scope limits used here are identical to those used in every earlier chapter.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • selecting one approved occupied or unoccupied residential property that can be inspected without destructive investigation
  • running the full site, exterior, roof-drainage, foundation, interior, plumbing, electrical, life-safety, HVAC, attic, and appliance inspection sequence established in Chapters 1 through 16
  • performing only the approved minor preventive-maintenance tasks selected from Chapters 4, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 18, after documenting pre-work conditions
  • testing smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms and visually inspecting emergency equipment using Chapter 13 procedures
  • reinspecting every maintained component and recording its corrected, improved, unchanged, or referred status
  • building the Chapter 19 30-60-90 day plan, a twelve-month seasonal calendar, and a multiyear monitoring and capital list
  • assembling the complete documentation package and conducting a documented owner or responsible-party handoff

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • represent the completed work as a regulated real-estate transaction inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code-compliance certification unless the person and scope are separately qualified and authorized for that service
  • walk an unapproved roof, enter an attic or crawlspace outside the accepted access and hazard conditions, remove an electrical panel dead front or device cover, or perform combustion, refrigerant, gas, or internal-equipment service
  • operate a shutoff valve, breaker, disconnect, appliance, pump, or mechanical system when doing so may cause leakage, flooding, electrical damage, fuel release, or loss of essential service
  • assign structural, code-compliance, electrical-safety, or environmental conclusions from appearance alone, or state unsupported life-expectancy or capital-replacement dates

Any immediate hazard identified at any point in the capstone must stop routine work in that area and be handled through the notification procedure established in Chapter 1 and repeated throughout this book before the engagement continues.

Trade Note

The capstone is not a new skill. It is proof that the nineteen skills already taught in this book can be run together, in the correct order, on a real property, without dropping a safety control, blurring an observation into an interpretation, or leaving an urgent finding buried in a routine list. Treat every step below as a checkpoint against work you have already practiced, not as new material to learn for the first time.

Common Field Fundamentals

Coordinating Nineteen Chapters Into One Engagement

Work the capstone in the sequence given in the work order below. Do not begin approved maintenance in any system until that system's inspection is fully documented, and do not begin the Chapter 19 planning phase until every system's inspection and maintenance segment is complete and reinspected. This order preserves the evidence-before-maintenance rule stated in this book's Universal controls.

Documentation Cue — One Register, Traceable to Every Chapter

Carry finding identifiers forward from every prior chapter's forms rather than renumbering them. The capstone's finding register must let a reviewer trace any entry back to the system inspection that produced it, and it must record the reinspection status of every item on which maintenance was performed.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Hazards Stop Routine Work in That Area

If fire or carbon-monoxide alarm activation, fuel-gas odor, active flooding or sewage, exposed energized conductors, unsafe stairs or guards, blocked egress, or comparable hazards appear in any segment, stop routine work in that area, secure or isolate it if safe to do so, and notify the owner, building management, the utility, or emergency services before continuing the capstone.

Field Safety Cue — Multi-Visit Access and Occupant Control

Reconfirm pets controlled, children excluded from work areas, weather conditions, and emergency-exit availability at the start of each segment or scheduled visit, not only at the beginning of the first visit.

Code Alert — The Capstone Is Not a Regulated Certification

Do not represent this completed package as a regulated real-estate transaction inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code-compliance certification unless the inspector and the defined scope are separately qualified and authorized for that specific service.

E. Preparation — Authorization, Records, and Route Confirmation

  1. Select the capstone property and obtain written authorization. Confirm the property qualifies under this book's Capstone Inspection Control.
  2. Define property type and boundaries. Record ownership boundaries, common elements, occupied areas, excluded spaces, and the inspection purpose.
  3. Confirm the engagement is not a regulated service. Do not represent it as a transaction inspection, engineering evaluation, environmental assessment, pest inspection, energy audit, or code certification unless separately authorized and qualified.
  4. Collect available records. Gather plans, permits, prior reports, repair records, warranties, manuals, service history, equipment information, and owner concerns.
  5. Establish the inspection standard. Confirm the route, terminology, priority categories, photograph system, limitations, and emergency-notification process match Chapter 1.
  6. Record starting conditions. Log date, time, weather, recent precipitation, utilities available, equipment status, occupancy, and access constraints.
  7. Prepare tools and supplies. Stage inspection tools, maintenance supplies, replacement filters or batteries, PPE, approved ladder equipment, and documentation forms.
  8. Confirm safe access. Verify pet and occupant controls, emergency exits, lighting, and communication.
  9. Update the property binder. Refresh the Chapter 2 binder and equipment inventory before beginning field inspection.

F. Exterior, Roof Drainage, and Structure

  1. Photograph all elevations. Capture every elevation and major site relationship before close inspection.
  2. Inspect site drainage and hardscape. Cover accessible grading, retaining walls, vegetation, utilities, exterior structures, and walking surfaces.
  3. Inspect the roof-drainage system from safe observation points. Cover roof covering, flashing, chimneys, penetrations, gutters, downspouts, and discharge.
  4. Document roof-access limitations. Do not walk any roof surface not separately approved for this engagement.
  5. Inspect the exterior envelope. Cover siding, trim, coatings, sealant joints, flashing, penetrations, windows, doors, porches, decks, stairs, guards, and exterior electrical components.
  6. Inspect foundation, grading, and crawlspace or basement access. Cover foundation walls, grading, drainage, moisture indicators, and movement evidence.
  7. Record cracks and displacement consistently. Do not assign unsupported structural conclusions.

G. Interior Rooms, Openings, and Systems

  1. Inspect every interior room. Work ceiling to walls, openings, fixtures, floors, and built-ins in each room.
  2. Identify interior finish and moisture evidence. Note stains, cracks, finish damage, trip hazards, movement indicators, and condensation.
  3. Inspect windows and doors. Cover locks, latches, weatherstripping, and visible air-leak priorities.
  4. Inspect kitchens, bathrooms, laundry, and utility rooms. Cover water-using appliances for leakage, drainage, support, and access.
  5. Inspect plumbing fixtures and visible connections. Cover supply connectors, traps, shutoffs, drains, water-heater exterior, and related finish damage.
  6. Operate approved plumbing fixtures. Complete controlled fill-and-drain or flush checks within the approved scope.
  7. Inspect electrical devices and panel exteriors. Cover accessible receptacles, switches, lights, covers, working clearances, and the panel directory without removing covers.
  8. Record electrical referral conditions. Flag damaged, wet, overheated, exposed, or improperly covered components for qualified service.
  9. Test smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms. Use manufacturer procedures for every accessible device.
  10. Inspect emergency equipment and exit routes. Visually check fire extinguishers, emergency equipment, and exit paths.
  11. Inspect HVAC equipment areas externally. Cover access, storage, leakage, damage, venting concerns, and service records.
  12. Replace approved HVAC filters. Install the correct size, type, and airflow direction, and inspect registers, returns, and visible condensate drainage.
  13. Inspect the attic or approved alternate upper-building area. Proceed only after access, heat, structural, electrical, respiratory, and contamination conditions are accepted.
  14. Document attic findings. Cover insulation distribution, ventilation paths, roof evidence, ducts, moisture, electrical concerns, and pest evidence from safe locations.
  15. Inspect appliances, laundry connections, and utility-room service access. Cover hoses, dryer exhaust, anti-tip provisions, clearances, and service access.
  16. Operate only approved appliances. Use safe, normal controls only.

H. Approved Preventive Maintenance and Reinspection

  1. Identify and notify immediate hazards. Review any immediate hazard with the responsible party before routine maintenance continues.
  2. Document pre-work conditions. Record the condition of each item selected for maintenance before beginning work.
  3. Perform selected exterior maintenance. Clean accessible gutter or drainage components only where access and weather controls are approved (Chapter 4 scope).
  4. Service one approved door. Correct minor hinge, latch, lock, or weatherstripping conditions without concealing broader defects (Chapter 8 scope).
  5. Perform selected plumbing maintenance. Gently exercise serviceable shutoffs and clean approved aerators or minor drain buildup (Chapter 11 scope).
  6. Replace specified alarm batteries. Use the correct battery type and retest each affected alarm (Chapter 13 and 18 scope).
  7. Complete selected seasonal exterior and interior tasks. Clear approved exterior debris and interior access obstructions (Chapters 17–18 scope).
  8. Reinspect every maintained component. Record corrected, improved, unchanged, or referred status for each.

I. Correlation, Planning, and Documentation

  1. Correlate moisture and drainage evidence. Connect roof, envelope, foundation, plumbing, HVAC, attic, and interior findings without assigning unsupported cause.
  2. Correlate movement indicators. Connect door, window, wall, floor, and foundation observations without assigning unsupported structural cause.
  3. Create the complete finding register. Include identifiers, locations, observations, priorities, photographs, recommendations, limitations, and referral needs.
  4. Separate findings by category. Distinguish immediate hazards, urgent water or system issues, short-term repairs, recurring maintenance, monitoring items, capital replacement, and cosmetic preferences.
  5. Build the 30-60-90 day plan. Assign dependencies, responsibilities, qualifications, permits, target dates, and closure evidence using the Chapter 19 method.
  6. Build the twelve-month seasonal calendar. Base it on climate, property systems, manufacturer instructions, and observed conditions.
  7. Build the multiyear monitoring and capital list. Avoid unsupported life-expectancy claims.
  8. Update the property binder. Enter equipment, warranty, service, repair, photograph, permit, and inspection records.
  9. Prepare the shutoff and emergency-location summary. Protect sensitive access information appropriately.
  10. Prepare the professional referral list. Identify licensed contractor, engineer, environmental, pest, chimney, roofing, drainage, or other required referrals.

J. Quality Review and Owner Handoff

  1. Verify no urgent finding is buried in the general report. Confirm immediate hazards and urgent items are visibly distinguished.
  2. Perform a documentation quality review. Check for missing areas, inconsistent terms, unsupported conclusions, unclear photographs, duplicate findings, and unassigned actions.
  3. Conduct the owner or responsible-party walkthrough. Demonstrate maintenance locations, shutoffs, filters, alarms, drains, and recurring tasks within the approved scope.
  4. Explain limitations and deferrals. Cover access limitations, concealed conditions, monitoring items, deferred work, and the distinction between maintenance and professional repair.
  5. Obtain acknowledgment. Document the responsible party's acknowledgment of immediate notifications, approved priorities, assigned responsibilities, and any deferrals.
  6. Deliver the complete package. Hand off the baseline report, photograph index, maintenance log, equipment inventory, safety record, 30-60-90 day plan, seasonal calendar, referrals, and long-term plan.
  7. Archive the final package. Store the completed package and schedule the next review condition.
  8. Document the transition. Record the formal handoff of this property record into the Book 23 blueprint-reading and construction-document work.

Common Walkthrough Inspection

Review the completed finding register using the same checks used in every prior chapter, applied now across the whole property.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding has a unique identifier tied to a specific location and component, traceable to the chapter system that produced it.
  • Observation check: confirm every entry states what was seen, heard, or measured, without an unsupported cause or diagnosis.
  • Priority check: confirm every finding is classified as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  • Maintenance check: confirm every performed maintenance task stayed within its chapter-defined scope and includes a documented reinspection status.
  • Plan check: confirm the 30-60-90 day plan, seasonal calendar, and referral list account for every open finding.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Maintenance Began Before the System's Inspection Was Documented

Probable cause: the inspector serviced a fixture, filter, or door before recording its pre-work condition and finding identifier.
Confirmation: compare the maintenance log timestamps against the finding register entries for the same component.
Correction: stop maintenance in that system, record the best available description of the pre-work condition with a clear notation that it was reconstructed, and complete the remaining inspection before resuming maintenance elsewhere.
Reinspection: verify every remaining maintenance item in the capstone follows document-before-maintenance order.

An Immediate Hazard Found Mid-Engagement Was Not Escalated

Probable cause: the inspector continued the routine sequence after finding a hazard instead of stopping and notifying the responsible party.
Confirmation: review the finding register for any hazard-category item without a matching notification record and timestamp.
Correction: notify the responsible party immediately, document the notification separately from the routine register, and confirm the hazardous area was secured or isolated if safe to do so.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate hazard in the finished package has a documented notification distinct from the routine list.

A Finding From an Earlier Chapter Was Duplicated or Lost

Probable cause: the capstone register was built from scratch instead of consolidating and deduplicating prior chapter forms.
Confirmation: cross-check the capstone register against the Chapter 1 baseline list and each system-chapter's forms for missing or duplicated identifiers.
Correction: merge duplicate entries while preserving the original identifier and date, and add any confirmed finding that was dropped.
Reinspection: reconcile the finished register against every prior chapter's forms before building the 30-60-90 day plan.

The 30-60-90 Day Plan Omits an Open Finding

Probable cause: a finding was recorded in the register but not carried into the planning phase.
Confirmation: compare every entry in the finding register against the 30-60-90 day plan, seasonal calendar, and referral list.
Correction: add the missing item to the correct time window or recurring schedule with a responsible party and closure evidence.
Reinspection: confirm every register entry appears in exactly one planning document before the package is delivered.

The Owner Handoff Was Not Documented

Probable cause: the package was delivered without a recorded walkthrough or acknowledgment of priorities and deferrals.
Confirmation: check the property binder for a signed or dated acknowledgment record covering immediate notifications, priorities, and deferrals.
Correction: schedule and complete the handoff walkthrough, and obtain and file the responsible party's acknowledgment before archiving the package.
Reinspection: confirm the archived package includes the dated acknowledgment record.

Cleanup

This capstone ends with an archived package and a completed handoff, not a stack of separate chapter forms.

  • Confirm every door, panel, cover, filter access, and equipment setting disturbed during the engagement is restored
  • Confirm all tools, debris, packaging, and moved stored items are removed from every work area
  • Confirm alarm monitoring status and occupant notifications are restored where applicable
  • Consolidate every chapter's forms, photographs, and this capstone's finding register, plan, and calendar into the property binder
  • Confirm the responsible party has received the complete package and has acknowledged priorities and deferrals in writing

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect one residential roof-drainage system — roof covering, flashing, gutters, downspouts, and discharge — entirely from safe observation points, and to produce a dated record identifying visible defects and water-management deficiencies. The finished work product is a written, photograph-supported roof-water-management inspection record tied to the property's ongoing maintenance log.

You will confirm whether roof walking is authorized for this property, select safe ground, window, balcony, ladder, or remote observation points, photograph each roof plane and drainage component, trace water from the roof surface through the gutters and downspouts to its final discharge point, and correlate what you find with interior evidence and foundation exposure. You will not walk an unsafe roof, remove roofing material, or diagnose remaining roof life from appearance alone.

This project builds directly on the baseline route and documentation method established in Chapter 1 and the property record organized in Chapter 2. It supplies the roof-drainage evidence that Chapter 5 (siding, trim, and flashing), Chapter 6 (foundation and grading), and Chapter 9 (interior finishes) depend on when correlating water-management conditions across the whole building. Chapter 4 returns to this same system to perform approved cleaning and minor maintenance.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-013 — Inspect the roof surface from safe locations without climbing an unauthorized or unsafe roof.
  • MI-014 — Inspect gutters for debris, damage, slope, and attachment condition.
  • MI-015 — Inspect downspouts for damage, blockage, and connection to an approved discharge path.
  • MI-016 — Identify drainage defects at the roof, gutter, downspout, and discharge point.
  • MI-017 — Verify discharge is directed away from the structure and does not create erosion, ponding, or hazard.
  • MI-018 — Document roof-water issues by location, condition, priority, photograph, and recommended action.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 established the fixed inspection route, the finding-identifier system, and the observation-versus-interpretation discipline this project uses throughout. Chapter 2 gave you the property binder where this inspection's findings and photographs are filed. This project is the first system-specific inspection in the book, and it deliberately starts at the top of the water-management system — the roof — because the universal master instructions require water management to be inspected from the roof and site down before interior stains are treated as isolated finish defects.

The roof-drainage record you produce here directly controls the scope of Chapter 4's approved gutter and downspout maintenance. It also supplies the roof-source evidence that Chapter 5's siding and flashing inspection, Chapter 6's foundation and grading inspection, and Chapter 9's interior finish inspection must correlate against before assigning a suspected water source.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing roof age, covering type, prior leaks, repairs, warranties, and recent weather before inspection
  • confirming whether roof walking is excluded or separately authorized for this property
  • selecting safe ground, window, balcony, ladder, camera-pole, or binocular observation points
  • photographing every roof plane, eave, rake, valley, penetration, chimney, gutter run, downspout, and discharge point visible from safe locations
  • identifying visible roof-covering, flashing, gutter, and downspout defects without assigning remaining service life from appearance
  • tracing each downspout to its discharge point and verifying discharge is directed away from the foundation
  • correlating exterior roof findings with interior ceiling and upper-wall evidence, labeled as unconfirmed until verified
  • producing one roof-water-management inspection record with location, observation, priority, photograph, and recommended action for every finding

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • walk a roof that is wet, icy, snow-covered, brittle, damaged, steep, contaminated, or outside an approved fall-protection plan
  • remove roofing material, flashing, or gutter guards beyond the manufacturer-approved procedure covered in Chapter 4
  • assign a remaining roof-service-life estimate, a structural diagnosis, or a code-compliance conclusion from visual appearance alone
  • perform an invasive moisture investigation, cut into a roof assembly, or alter roof ventilation, drainage, or flashing design

Any active leakage, loose overhead material, blocked primary drainage, unsafe chimney component, or major storm damage must be treated as an immediate condition and referred per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before the routine inspection continues.

Trade Note

Every finding in this chapter is recorded as a visible condition, not as a remaining-life estimate or a structural conclusion. Writing "curled shingle tabs visible at the southwest roof plane" is correct. Writing "the roof needs to be replaced within two years" is not, unless that estimate comes from an identified manufacturer instruction or a qualified roofing professional. Confusing an observed condition with an unsupported service-life claim is the most common documentation error corrected in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the roof-drainage inspection until weather, wind, precipitation, temperature, and daylight have been checked and roof-walking authorization has been confirmed (Step 1). A finding recorded before these conditions are confirmed must be re-verified before it is entered on the inspection worksheet.

Shop Tip

Photograph each roof plane, eave, and gutter run before making any close observations. Working from a wide-to-close photograph sequence keeps the record consistent even if a downspout blockage is only confirmed later during the Chapter 4 flushing test.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every roof-drainage inspection in this book follows the same sequence: confirm access authorization and weather, observe from safe points only, trace water from roof to final discharge, correlate with interior evidence without assigning unconfirmed cause, and record limitations. Learning that sequence here, at the whole-roof-system level, means Chapter 4's cleaning and maintenance work and Chapter 6's foundation-drainage inspection both start from the same vocabulary and route.

Observation Versus Interpretation

Record what is visible from a safe observation point. Do not assign a remaining roof-service-life estimate, a structural conclusion, or a confirmed leak source unless it comes from an identified prior report or a qualified professional. "Curled shingle tabs visible at the southwest roof plane" is an observation. "This roof has five years of life left" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact.

Documentation Cue — One Finding, One Identifier, One Photo Pair

Assign a unique finding identifier tied to a roof plane, elevation, or component before photographing. Take one overview photograph and one close photograph for every significant finding. Do not batch multiple unrelated roof, gutter, or downspout findings under a single identifier or photograph.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Roof Conditions Stop the Routine Inspection

If you observe active leakage, loose overhead material, a blocked primary drainage path, an unsafe chimney component, or major storm damage, stop the routine inspection immediately. Do not approach beneath the affected area. Notify the owner and refer roofing, flashing, structural, chimney, or high-access concerns to the appropriate qualified professional before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Access, Weather, and Ladder Exclusion Zones

Do not access a roof that is wet, icy, snow-covered, brittle, damaged, steep, contaminated, or outside an approved fall-protection plan. Where elevated ladder observation is used, establish a ground exclusion zone and maintain required clearance from overhead electrical conductors. Prefer binocular, ground, window, balcony, or camera-pole observation whenever roof walking is not specifically authorized and safely controlled.

Code Alert — Roof-Walking Authorization Varies by Property

Do not walk a roof surface unless this project's scope specifically authorizes it and the required fall-protection controls are in place. Confirm authorization before beginning; this chapter does not by itself authorize roof access.

E. Preparation — Records, Weather, and Access Planning

  1. Review available roof and drainage records. Record roof age if known, covering type, slope, number of roof areas, prior leaks, repairs, warranties, drainage plan, and recent weather.
  2. Confirm the inspection scope and roof-walking authorization. Record whether roof walking is excluded or separately authorized for this property before proceeding.
  3. Check weather and site conditions. Record wind, precipitation, temperature, lightning, roof-surface condition, and daylight before beginning exterior observation.
  4. Select safe observation points. Choose ground, window, balcony, ladder, camera-pole, binocular, or other approved remote observation points for the property's roof configuration.
  5. Establish exclusion zones. Set up public and ladder exclusion zones wherever elevated access is used for observation.
  6. Confirm the roof is not accessed under unsafe conditions. Do not access a roof that is wet, icy, snow-covered, brittle, damaged, steep, contaminated, or outside the approved fall-protection plan.

F. Roof Plane and Penetration Inspection

  1. Photograph each roof plane and drainage component. Capture every roof plane, eave, rake, valley, penetration, chimney, wall intersection, gutter run, downspout, and discharge point visible from safe locations.
  2. Identify the roof covering material and pattern. Record the visible installation pattern without assigning remaining service life solely from appearance.
  3. Inspect the roof surface for visible defects. Note missing, displaced, cracked, curled, blistered, punctured, worn, or debris-covered roofing.
  4. Inspect ridges, hips, valleys, and penetrations. Cover visible ridges, hips, valleys, eaves, rakes, roof-wall intersections, chimneys, skylights, vents, plumbing stacks, and other penetrations.
  5. Identify flashing and fastener defects. Note exposed fasteners, failed sealant, loose flashing, damaged boots, open joints, corrosion, staining, biological growth, and temporary patches.

G. Gutter, Downspout, and Discharge Inspection

  1. Inspect gutters from a safe location. Note debris, vegetation, standing water, corrosion, holes, loose joints, sag, poor slope, detached hangers, and overflow evidence, along with gutter attachment and fascia condition.
  2. Inspect downspouts. Note missing sections, open seams, crushed areas, loose straps, blockage evidence, reverse slope, and disconnected underground drains.
  3. Trace each downspout to its discharge point where accessible. Follow the visible path from downspout outlet to final discharge.
  4. Verify discharge direction. Confirm discharge is directed through an approved path away from the foundation and does not create erosion, icing, ponding, neighbor impacts, or walkway hazards.
  5. Inspect discharge components. Check splash blocks, extensions, leaders, underground connections, catch basins, and outlets for position and visible function.

H. Correlation, Referral, and Documentation

  1. Correlate exterior evidence with interior finishes. Inspect exterior walls, soffits, fascia, ceilings, attic access areas, and upper-room finishes for related staining or moisture evidence, labeling any suspected roof source as unconfirmed until verified.
  2. Identify immediate conditions. Note active leakage, loose overhead material, blocked primary drainage, unsafe chimney components, or major storm damage as immediate referral conditions.
  3. Refer conditions beyond this chapter's scope. Direct roofing, flashing, structural, chimney, electrical, or high-access concerns to the appropriate qualified professional.
  4. Record every finding. Note roof plane or elevation, component, observed condition, priority, photograph, and next action for each finding.

I. Closeout and Recordkeeping

  1. Record inaccessible or concealed roof areas and observation limitations.
  2. Update the property maintenance log. Include inspection date, weather, findings, referrals, and recommended reinspection condition.
  3. File the completed inspection. Store the roof-drainage inspection record, photographs, and any immediate-hazard notifications in the property record established in Chapter 2.

Common Roof-Drainage Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which segment of the route it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding has a unique identifier tied to a specific roof plane, elevation, or component.
  • Observation check: confirm the recorded text states what was seen, without an unsupported service-life or structural conclusion.
  • Priority check: confirm every finding is classified as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  • Discharge check: confirm every traced downspout's discharge point is recorded as directed away from the foundation or flagged as a defect.
  • Limitation check: confirm every inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, or excluded roof area is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Finding States a Remaining Service Life Instead of an Observation

Probable cause: the inspector assigned a remaining-life estimate or replacement date from visual appearance alone.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any word implying a service-life estimate, structural conclusion, or code-compliance conclusion not supported by a prior report or qualified professional.
Correction: rewrite the finding to state only the visible condition, and move any suspected remaining-life concern to a clearly labeled referral for qualified roofing evaluation.
Reinspection: review the full inspection record for the same wording pattern before filing it.

A Downspout Was Not Traced to Its Discharge Point

Probable cause: the inspector recorded the downspout condition but did not follow it to a splash block, extension, or underground connection.
Confirmation: compare the finding list against the Section B route schedule and confirm every accessible downspout has a recorded discharge observation.
Correction: return to the downspout, trace it to its visible discharge point, and record whether discharge is directed away from the foundation.
Reinspection: confirm the finished inspection record includes a discharge observation for every accessible downspout.

A Finding Has No Photograph or an Incomplete Photograph Pair

Probable cause: the close photograph was taken without the matching overview photograph, or the photograph was not labeled with the finding identifier.
Confirmation: check the photograph index against the inspection record for every significant finding.
Correction: return to the location, if still accessible from a safe point, and capture the missing overview or close photograph with the correct identifier.
Reinspection: verify every significant finding has a complete, correctly labeled photograph pair before filing the record.

An Unauthorized Roof Area Was Walked to Improve the View

Probable cause: the inspector climbed onto a roof surface that was not specifically authorized in order to get a better photograph or closer look.
Confirmation: review the observation-point log for any entry indicating direct roof contact without documented authorization and fall-protection controls.
Correction: stop roof access immediately, return to an approved safe observation point, and record the affected roof plane as a limitation requiring separately authorized access.
Reinspection: confirm no finding in the record required unauthorized roof walking to obtain.

An Immediate Roof Hazard Was Buried in the General List

Probable cause: active leakage, loose overhead material, or storm damage was recorded in sequence with routine gutter findings instead of being flagged and escalated at the time it was found.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving active leakage, loose overhead material, blocked primary drainage, or chimney damage.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard, notify the owner and refer it per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate roof hazard is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the inspection is closed out.

Field Terms

Roof Plane
One continuous sloped or flat roof surface bounded by a ridge, hip, valley, eave, rake, or wall intersection.
Safe Observation Point
A ground, window, balcony, ladder, camera-pole, binocular, or other approved remote position from which the roof is viewed without unauthorized roof walking.
Flashing
Material installed at roof penetrations, valleys, and wall intersections to direct water away from a joint.
Discharge Point
The location where a downspout, splash block, extension, or underground drain releases water away from the building.
Suspected Source, Unconfirmed
A label applied to interior staining or moisture evidence correlated with a roof or gutter finding until the connection is verified.
Priority Category
The classification of a finding as immediate hazard, active failure, short-term repair, recurring maintenance, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic preference.
Limitation
A roof area, gutter run, or downspout segment that was inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, or outside the approved scope.
Overview Photograph
A wide photograph establishing the location and context of a finding before a close photograph is taken.
Close Photograph
A detailed photograph of a specific finding, including a scale reference where useful.
Roof-Drainage Inspection Record
The single dated document recording every roof, gutter, downspout, and discharge finding, limitation, and referral from this inspection.

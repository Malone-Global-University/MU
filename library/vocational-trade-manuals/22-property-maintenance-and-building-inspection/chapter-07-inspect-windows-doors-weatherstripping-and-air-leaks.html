Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect every accessible exterior window and door in a residence and produce a room- and elevation-based record of operation, water-management condition, weatherstripping, locks, latches, and visible air leakage. The finished work product is a single written record: an opening-by-opening inventory that separates safe, verified operation from conditions requiring service, and separates serviceable weatherstripping repair from conditions requiring frame, flashing, or replacement work.

You will review known drafts, leaks, condensation, and prior repairs; identify openings with egress, fire, security, accessibility, or emergency-rescue functions; inspect exterior and interior water-management details at each opening; operate each approved window and door through its normal functions; inspect weatherstripping and hardware; screen for visible air leakage using only approved non-destructive methods; and record every opening with a priority and a recommended next action.

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough and the Chapter 5 envelope inspection, both of which identified openings as part of a wider exterior review. It supplies the service list that Chapter 8 uses to complete minor door and hardware maintenance, and it feeds the Chapter 19 repair-priority plan and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-037 — Inspect window operation, including opening, closing, locking, and support functions, for every approved accessible window.
  • MI-038 — Inspect door operation, including drag, sag, latch resistance, threshold contact, and closer action, for every approved accessible exterior door.
  • MI-039 — Identify failed weatherstripping by compression, continuity, brittleness, tearing, displacement, and missing sections.
  • MI-040 — Identify drafts and visible air leakage using approved non-destructive screening methods at common leakage paths.
  • MI-041 — Inspect locks and latches for secure, non-forced engagement at every approved opening.
  • MI-042 — Document air-sealing priorities that separate minor weatherstripping service from conditions requiring frame, flashing, or enclosure work.

Where This Skill Fits

This project follows the Chapter 5 siding, trim, flashing, and sealant inspection, which already identified window and door head, sill, and trim conditions as part of the exterior envelope. Chapter 7 returns to those same openings from the inside and outside together, adding operation, security, weatherstripping, and air-leak findings that Chapter 5's exterior-only review did not cover.

The service list you create here is used directly in Chapter 8, which completes minor hinge, latch, lock, weatherstripping, sweep, and sealant service on one selected door. Findings that exceed minor maintenance — insulated-glass failure, structural frame movement, exterior flashing correction, or rated-assembly work — are carried forward as referrals into the Chapter 19 repair-priority plan and, where the property is used as the capstone, into Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing known drafts, leaks, condensation, security concerns, and prior adjustment or replacement history for each opening
  • identifying openings with egress, fire, security, accessibility, storm-protection, or emergency-rescue functions
  • inspecting exterior and interior water-management details at heads, jambs, sills, thresholds, trim, and visible drainage paths
  • operating each approved window and door through normal opening, closing, locking, and support functions
  • inspecting weatherstripping, sweeps, thresholds, hinges, and lock hardware for condition and secure engagement
  • screening for visible air leakage using visual, hand-feel, tissue-strip, or other approved non-destructive methods
  • producing one room- and elevation-based opening inspection record with operation results, water evidence, weatherstripping condition, air-leak priority, and recommended next action for every opening

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • operate a window or door that is broken, painted shut, severely distorted, or otherwise unsafe until it receives qualified evaluation
  • seal window weeps, sill drainage, door threshold drainage, or any other intentional ventilation or drainage opening
  • use open flame or uncontrolled smoke sources to detect air leakage
  • remove trim, dismantle frames, or perform any repair beyond visual and operational inspection within this chapter's scope
  • diagnose insulated-glass seal failure, structural frame movement, or concealed flashing condition from appearance alone

Any opening presenting an immediate egress, fall, or security hazard must stop routine inspection of that opening and be handled per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue before the inspection continues.

Trade Note

An opening that is difficult to operate is not automatically a repair item, and an opening that operates smoothly is not automatically free of water or air-leakage concerns. Record operation, water evidence, weatherstripping condition, and air-leak priority as four separate observations for every opening, even when they appear related, so Chapter 8 can select the correct service scope for each one.

Setup Standard

Do not operate a window or door before excluding it from operation if it is broken, painted shut, severely distorted, or otherwise unsafe (Step 3). An opening excluded from operation must still receive a full visual inspection and be recorded with the reason for exclusion.

Shop Tip

Photograph the exterior head, jamb, and sill of every opening before operating it from the interior. This keeps the water-management record independent of whatever the operation test reveals, which prevents a smooth-operating window from being assumed dry.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every opening in this chapter receives the same four-part review: water-management inspection at the frame, operation of locks and moving parts, weatherstripping and hardware condition, and an air-leak priority screening. Recording these four parts separately, for every opening, is what allows Chapter 8 to select a correct and limited service scope later.

Operation Versus Repair Boundary

Record whether an opening operates within normal effort and record any drag, sag, binding, or resistance as an observation. Do not force a binding window or door, and do not conclude that resistance alone requires frame repair; many resistance findings are corrected by the minor hinge, latch, or weatherstripping service in Chapter 8. Reserve the "requires broader repair" classification for frame decay, structural distortion, insulated-glass failure, or damage visible at the time of this inspection.

Documentation Cue — Four Observations Per Opening

Record operation result, weatherstripping and hardware condition, water evidence, and air-leak priority as four separate entries for every opening, tied to a single opening identifier. Do not combine them into one general comment such as "window is fine," which cannot be reused by Chapter 8 or Chapter 19.

Critical Safety Cue — Egress, Fall, and Security Hazards Stop Routine Operation

If an identified egress opening does not open freely, a sash fails to remain supported, a security device prevents required emergency operation, or glass is broken in a manner that creates a fall or cut hazard, stop routine operation of that opening immediately. Secure the area if it is safe to do so, and notify the owner or responsible party before continuing to the next opening.

Field Safety Cue — Sash Support and Child-Safety Devices

Confirm each sash remains supported by its balance, prop, or stop before releasing it, and do not allow a sash to fall or disengage during testing. Do not remove, defeat, or bypass a window guard, window opening control device, or other child-safety device to test full window travel.

Code Alert — Egress, Rated, and Security Openings Vary by Property

Egress dimension requirements, fire- or smoke-rated assembly requirements, and security or accessibility hardware requirements vary by property type, occupancy, and jurisdiction. Identify openings that appear to serve these functions, but do not represent this inspection as a code-compliance certification for egress, fire rating, or accessibility unless the inspector and scope are separately qualified and authorized for that service.

E. Preparation — Records, Egress Identification, and Route Planning

  1. Review known symptoms and history. Record known drafts, leaks, condensation, security concerns, difficult operation, prior adjustments, and window or door replacement history for the property.
  2. Identify functional openings. Identify every opening with an egress, fire, security, accessibility, storm-protection, or emergency-rescue function before beginning operation.
  3. Exclude unsafe openings from operation. Exclude unsafe, broken, painted-shut, severely distorted, or specialty openings from operation until qualified evaluation, and record the reason for exclusion.
  4. Photograph exterior and interior conditions. Photograph representative and defective openings at both the exterior frame and the interior side before close inspection.

F. Exterior Frame and Glass Inspection

  1. Inspect exterior frame details. Inspect head, jamb, sill, threshold, trim, flashing, sealant, weeps, drainage paths, and cladding transitions at each opening.
  2. Do not seal intentional drainage. Do not seal window weeps, sill drainage, door threshold drainage, or intentional ventilation openings during this inspection.
  3. Inspect glass condition. Inspect glass for cracks, impact damage, failed glazing, fogging, seal-failure indicators, and loose stops.
  4. Inspect frame and sash or slab condition. Inspect frames and sashes or slabs for decay, corrosion, swelling, delamination, distortion, loose components, and coating failure.

G. Operation, Hardware, and Lock Inspection

  1. Operate each approved window. Operate through normal opening, closing, locking, and support functions only when safe.
  2. Verify sash support. Confirm sashes remain supported and do not fall, bind dangerously, or disengage during operation.
  3. Inspect window hardware. Inspect balances, cranks, hinges, rollers, tracks, locks, limiters, screens, and child-safety devices without dismantling them.
  4. Operate each approved exterior door. Record drag, sag, latch resistance, threshold contact, closer action, and required force qualitatively.
  5. Inspect door hardware. Inspect hinges, frame, jamb, slab, threshold, sweep, lockset, deadbolt, strike, closer, and security hardware.
  6. Verify secure lock and latch engagement. Confirm locks and latches engage without lifting, slamming, or excessive force.

H. Weatherstripping, Air-Leak Screening, and Interior Evidence

  1. Inspect weatherstripping condition. Inspect for compression, continuity, brittleness, tearing, displacement, paint adhesion, and missing sections.
  2. Inspect sweeps and thresholds. Inspect door sweeps and thresholds for contact, drainage, wear, and floor clearance.
  3. Screen for air leakage using approved methods only. Use visual, hand-feel, tissue-strip, or other approved non-destructive method under safe and suitable conditions, and never use open flame or uncontrolled smoke.
  4. Check common leakage paths. Inspect operable joints, meeting rails, pulley pockets, trim gaps, recessed hardware, penetrations, and rough-opening transitions.
  5. Classify the leakage source. Distinguish leakage through serviceable weatherstripping from leakage requiring trim removal, frame repair, flashing correction, or broader enclosure work.
  6. Inspect interior evidence. Inspect interior stains, condensation, peeling paint, swelling, mold-like growth, and floor damage related to each opening.

I. Classification and Recordkeeping

  1. Identify priority conditions. Classify unsafe egress, broken glass, unsecured doors, falling sash, failed lock, active water entry, and severe frame decay as priority items.
  2. Refer conditions beyond this chapter's scope. Refer insulated-glass failure, structural frame movement, exterior flashing, replacement windows, rated doors, and high-security work as required.
  3. Record every opening. Record location, type, operation, lock status, weatherstripping condition, water evidence, air-leak priority, photograph, and recommendation.
  4. Create the service list. Separate cleaning, lubrication, minor adjustment, weatherstripping, sealant, hardware, frame repair, flashing repair, and replacement into distinct categories.
  5. Update the property log. Identify openings to be serviced in Chapter 8 and file the completed opening inspection record.

Common Opening Inspection

Review every opening using the same checks regardless of which segment of the route it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every opening has a unique identifier tied to a specific room, elevation, and opening.
  • Egress check: confirm every opening with an egress, fire, security, accessibility, or emergency-rescue function is identified before operation.
  • Four-observation check: confirm operation result, water evidence, weatherstripping and hardware condition, and air-leak priority are recorded separately for every opening.
  • Method check: confirm no air-leak screening entry references open flame or uncontrolled smoke.
  • Recommendation check: confirm every recommendation is limited to cleaning, lubrication, minor adjustment, weatherstripping, sealant, hardware, frame repair, flashing repair, or replacement.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Egress Opening Was Operated Before Its Function Was Identified

Probable cause: the inspector began operating openings before completing the egress and security identification step.
Confirmation: compare the operation sequence recorded against the egress and security identification list for the same room.
Correction: stop, complete the identification step for all remaining openings, and reinspect any egress opening operated out of sequence for support and lock function.
Reinspection: confirm every egress opening was identified before its operation entry was recorded.

Resistance Was Recorded as a Repair Conclusion Instead of an Observation

Probable cause: the inspector wrote a conclusion such as "frame needs repair" instead of recording drag, sag, or resistance as observed.
Confirmation: reread the operation entry and identify any word implying a required repair scope not supported by visible frame decay or distortion.
Correction: rewrite the entry to state the observed resistance and reclassify the recommendation using the defined service categories in Section H.
Reinspection: confirm the recommendation matches an observed condition rather than an assumed cause.

Air-Leak Screening Used an Unapproved Method

Probable cause: the inspector used an open flame, candle, or uncontrolled smoke source to detect drafts.
Confirmation: review the air-leak entries for any reference to flame, smoke, or combustion-based detection.
Correction: remove the finding, and if the opening still requires screening, repeat it using a tissue strip, hand-feel, or other approved non-destructive method.
Reinspection: confirm every air-leak entry references only an approved method.

Water Evidence Was Not Recorded Because the Opening Operated Smoothly

Probable cause: the inspector treated smooth operation as proof that the opening was also free of water intrusion.
Confirmation: review whether every opening has a separate water-evidence entry, independent of the operation result.
Correction: return to the opening and complete the exterior frame and interior evidence inspection from Section F and Section H.
Reinspection: confirm the worksheet shows four distinct entries for the opening.

A Referral-Level Condition Was Classified as Minor Weatherstripping Service

Probable cause: insulated-glass fogging, frame distortion, or active water entry was recorded as a weatherstripping recommendation.
Confirmation: review each weatherstripping recommendation against the glass, frame, and water-evidence entries for the same opening.
Correction: reclassify the finding under the correct referral category and remove it from the Chapter 8 minor-service list.
Reinspection: confirm the service list contains only conditions within the approved minor-maintenance scope before Chapter 8 selects a door.

Cleanup

This project ends with a filed opening inspection record, not a set of loose notes.

  • Confirm every window, door, screen, and hardware component disturbed during operation testing is returned to its normal closed and locked position
  • Confirm child-safety devices, guards, and window opening control devices are restored where removed for inspection
  • Transfer handwritten notes to the Opening Inspection Worksheet if a digital form was not used in the field
  • Back up or print all photographs and match them to their opening identifiers
  • Store the completed opening inspection record, service list, and photographs in the property record established in Chapter 2

Field Terms

Opening Identifier
A unique code tied to a specific room, elevation, and opening, assigned to every window and door inspected.
Egress Opening
A window or door identified as serving a required emergency-escape or rescue function.
Operation Result
The recorded observation of whether an opening moves, locks, and latches through its normal functions without excessive force or unsafe behavior.
Water Evidence
Visible staining, swelling, decay, or moisture at an opening's head, jamb, sill, threshold, or interior finish.
Air-Leak Priority
A classification of visible or felt air movement at an opening as serviceable through weatherstripping or requiring broader trim, frame, or flashing work.
Common Leakage Path
An operable joint, meeting rail, pulley pocket, trim gap, recessed hardware location, penetration, or rough-opening transition where air movement is commonly detected.
Opening Inspection Worksheet
The single dated document recording every opening's operation, lock and weatherstripping condition, water evidence, air-leak priority, and recommendation from this inspection.
Service List
The categorized list separating cleaning, lubrication, minor adjustment, weatherstripping, sealant, hardware, frame repair, flashing repair, and replacement needs identified across all openings.
Referral Condition
A finding, such as insulated-glass failure or structural frame movement, that exceeds this chapter's minor-maintenance scope and requires a qualified professional.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect an existing residential space, gather the facts a repair or remodeling project depends on, and produce the four documents every later chapter in this book assumes are already in hand: a written existing-condition report, an approved scope of work, a risk register identifying concealed hazards, and a signed repair-readiness decision.

You will obtain owner authorization, collect existing records, define the inspection boundary, interview the occupant, photograph and measure the space, inspect visible finishes and structure for damage, check for moisture and identify likely hidden utility routes, flag possible hazardous materials and structural or life-safety conditions, separate confirmed facts from suspected concealed conditions, define the approved and excluded work, list required permits and qualified trades, and issue a formal readiness classification.

This is the first chapter in the book. Every later chapter — from work-area setup in Chapter 2 through the Chapter 20 capstone remodel — begins from the existing-condition report, scope, risk register, and readiness decision produced here. No demolition, material ordering, or scheduling may occur until this chapter's readiness decision has been issued.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-001 — Inspect existing conditions of a residential space and record observed damage, wear, movement, staining, corrosion, and finish conditions.
  • RR-002 — Identify and record the owner's stated repair goal, desired completion condition, budget, and occupancy requirements.
  • RR-003 — Distinguish restoration of a failed or damaged component from an elective change in layout, material, fixture, or appearance.
  • RR-004 — Identify hidden-risk areas, including concealed utilities, structural elements, and suspect hazardous materials that require assessment before disturbance.
  • RR-005 — Document an approved project scope, including boundaries, repair limits, exclusions, and required permits or referrals.
  • RR-006 — Prepare a repair-readiness checklist and issue a formal readiness decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter in this book performs physical work — demolition, repair, or installation — inside a space whose finishes may conceal structural damage, wiring, plumbing, ducts, moisture, mold, pests, or previous unpermitted alterations. This chapter is where that concealed-condition risk is first identified and controlled, before a single fastener is removed.

The existing-condition report, risk register, and readiness decision produced in this chapter are the documents Chapter 2 reviews before setting up containment and dust control, and the documents Chapter 3 reviews before any demolition begins. The capstone project in Chapter 20 reuses this exact readiness process for its selected room.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining owner authorization, objectives, budget, occupancy requirements, and completion condition
  • collecting existing plans, permits, inspection reports, repair records, and prior contractor documentation
  • defining an inspection boundary and interviewing the occupant about symptoms and history
  • photographing and measuring the space before any content or finish is disturbed
  • inspecting visible finishes and structure for damage, movement, wear, staining, and corrosion
  • identifying moisture evidence, likely concealed utility routes, and possible hazardous materials
  • identifying structural, fire-rated, wet-area, egress, and life-safety conditions
  • separating confirmed facts from suspected concealed conditions and owner-reported symptoms
  • defining an approved scope, repair limits, exclusions, required permits, and required referrals
  • issuing a formal repair-readiness decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, drill, pry, fasten, or demolish any part of the inspected space; this chapter is investigation and documentation only
  • de-energize, cap, isolate, or disconnect any utility; treat every unidentified wire, pipe, duct, cable, valve, or line as active
  • disturb, sand, scrape, or sample any suspect lead-containing, asbestos-containing, mold, or other hazardous material; a suspect condition is flagged for assessment, not investigated by disturbance
  • approve a scope that includes structural alteration, plumbing or electrical relocation, or waterproofing work without the design, code, and qualified-trade review the later chapters require
  • classify a project as approved when hidden damage, contamination, or unresolved authorization exceeds this chapter's defined limits; such a project must be classified as conditionally approved or rejected

Any condition this chapter identifies as requiring a permit, inspection, engineer, environmental consultant, or licensed trade must remain unresolved in the readiness decision until that referral is complete.

Trade Note

Every fact recorded in this chapter must be labeled as an observed fact, an owner-reported symptom, or a suspected concealed condition. Recording a suspicion as a confirmed fact, or a confirmed fact as a suspicion, is the single most common cause of an inaccurate scope and an incorrect readiness decision.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the physical inspection (Step 5 forward) until owner authorization and objectives (Step 1) and the inspection boundary (Step 3) are both recorded in writing. An inspection performed before authorization is confirmed must be repeated once authorization is obtained.

Shop Tip

Photograph every proposed work area before you touch it, including areas you do not expect to disturb. A "before" photograph taken after contents have already been moved cannot be recreated, and disputes over pre-existing damage are far easier to resolve with a dated photograph than with memory.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every project in this book depends on the same three-part discipline taught here: separate fact from suspicion, distinguish a failed-component repair from an elective remodel, and stop at the edge of what has actually been verified. Learning that discipline on a documentation-only project, before any tool touches the building, means every later demolition and repair chapter starts from an accurate picture of the space.

Repair vs. Elective Remodel

A repair goal restores a component that has failed, is damaged, or no longer performs its intended function to a serviceable condition. An elective remodel changes the layout, material, fixture, or appearance of a component that is currently serviceable. The same project may contain both: replacing a failed toilet flange is a repair; changing the vanity style at the same time is an elective remodel. Record each stated goal separately and identify which category it falls into before the scope is finalized.

Measurement Cue — Record From the Same Reference Points Every Time

Measure and photograph from a consistent set of reference points: a fixed corner, a doorway, or a marked floor location. Do not estimate a dimension from memory after leaving the room. A measurement or photograph taken from an inconsistent reference point cannot be reliably compared to the punch-list inspection performed at project closeout in Chapter 19.

Critical Safety Cue — Treat Every Utility as Active

Do not operate, test, or bypass any shutoff, breaker, valve, or lock unless it is already in normal safe operating condition and doing so is within the inspection scope. Do not touch, move, or probe any exposed wiring, and do not enter an area with a suspected gas odor, standing water near an electrical source, or visible structural collapse. Exit the area and contact the appropriate utility, emergency service, or qualified professional immediately.

Field Safety Cue — Lighting and Footing in Concealed Spaces

Use a flashlight before entering any attic, crawlspace, or basement area with limited lighting, and confirm stable footing before placing weight on any surface. Do not step off of joists or rated walking surfaces in an attic or crawlspace, and do not enter a crawlspace or attic alone if the owner has not confirmed safe access.

Code Alert — Verify Permit and Referral Requirements Locally

Permit, inspection, and licensing requirements for structural, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, environmental, and specialty work vary by jurisdiction. This chapter identifies where a permit or qualified professional is likely required; the local building department, licensing authority, or qualified professional identified in the risk register controls the exact requirement.

E. Preparation — Authorization and Records Collection (Steps 1–4)

  1. Obtain owner authorization, objectives, budget, occupancy requirements, desired completion condition, and known building history before entering the space. Record the authorized party's name, the stated budget range, any occupancy restriction on work hours or access, and the condition the owner wants at completion, directly on the existing-condition report.
  2. Collect available plans, permits, inspection reports, repair records, product information, warranties, photographs, and prior contractor documentation. List each document collected and note any document the owner reports exists but cannot locate.
  3. Define the inspection boundary and identify adjacent rooms, floors, ceilings, exterior surfaces, attics, crawlspaces, basements, garages, and utility areas that may affect the work. Record the boundary on the Reference and Boundary Record before proceeding to Step 5.
  4. Record building age, construction type, finish materials, prior alterations, and visible evidence of unpermitted or incomplete work without assuming concealed compliance. Note any visible alteration that appears inconsistent with the building's age or the collected permit records as a suspected concealed condition on the risk register, not as a confirmed fact.

F. Interview, Photography, and Measurement (Steps 5–7)

  1. Interview the owner or occupant about leaks, odors, noise, movement, intermittent failures, previous repairs, seasonal conditions, and when the problem occurs. Record each statement as an owner-reported symptom, including the approximate date, frequency, and conditions under which it occurs.
  2. Photograph the room and each proposed work area before moving contents or disturbing finishes. Photograph from the same reference points established in the Reference Conventions, including wide views of each wall and close views of any visible damage.
  3. Measure room dimensions, openings, fixture locations, floor elevations, wall planes, ceiling condition, and access constraints. Record every measurement on the existing-condition report using the reference points established before the walk-through began.

G. Condition and Concealed-Risk Investigation (Steps 8–15)

  1. Identify the stated repair goal and distinguish restoration of a failed component from an elective change in layout, material, fixture, or appearance. Record each stated goal separately using the Repair vs. Elective Remodel definitions above.
  2. Inspect walls, floors, ceilings, trim, doors, windows, cabinets, fixtures, finishes, and visible structure for damage, movement, wear, staining, corrosion, and poor attachment. Record each finding as an observed fact with its location and photograph reference.
  3. Inspect for active or historic moisture using visual evidence, odor, approved moisture testing, and access to adjacent assemblies. Record the moisture-meter reading, location, and whether the condition appears active or historic.
  4. Identify likely routes of plumbing, wiring, gas, ducts, vents, drains, and structural framing before any cutting or drilling is planned. Record each likely route as a suspected concealed condition on the risk register; do not confirm a route by cutting, drilling, or probing during this chapter.
  5. Identify possible lead-containing coatings, asbestos-containing materials, mold, silica-producing materials, or other environmental hazards that require assessment before disturbance. Record each suspect material on the risk register with the required assessment method and the qualified professional who must perform it; do not sand, scrape, or sample the material.
  6. Identify load-bearing, braced, shear, fire-rated, garage-separation, wet-area, egress, accessibility, and life-safety conditions. Record each condition on the risk register with the qualified professional or code reference required to resolve it.
  7. Operate doors, drawers, latches, shutoffs, faucets, drains, toilets, lights, fans, and appliances only when safe and within the inspection scope. Record the observed operating condition of each item operated; do not operate any item that appears unsafe or that requires a qualified professional to access safely.
  8. Separate observed facts from suspected concealed conditions and from owner-reported symptoms. Review every entry recorded in Steps 8 through 14 and confirm each is labeled correctly before proceeding to Section H.

H. Scope Definition and Readiness Preparation (Steps 16–21)

  1. Define the approved repair limits, finish limits, access limits, and excluded work. State the exact boundary of approved work and list every condition, room, or component specifically excluded from this scope.
  2. Identify permits, inspections, utility shutdowns, specialty testing, licensed trades, design professionals, and manufacturer consultation required before work. List each requirement identified from the risk register and the Section G findings.
  3. Prepare a concealed-condition allowance and a stop-work list for conditions that cannot be resolved during the initial inspection. State the specific response required if each listed concealed condition is confirmed once demolition exposes it.
  4. Prepare an ordered scope of demolition, investigation, repair, installation, finishing, testing, cleanup, and handoff. List the work in the order it must occur, without specifying construction methods that belong to a later chapter.
  5. Prepare a materials and product-compatibility research list without selecting products whose suitability has not been verified. List the compatibility questions that must be answered before a product is selected in a later chapter.
  6. Prepare a repair-readiness checklist covering authorization, design, hazards, utilities, access, protection, materials, trades, permits, and inspections. Complete every line of the checklist using the information gathered in Sections E through H.

I. Owner Review, Classification, and Filing (Steps 22–24)

  1. Review the scope, exclusions, assumptions, change-control process, and expected disruption with the owner or responsible party. Confirm the owner's understanding and obtain a signature or documented verbal acceptance on the approved scope.
  2. Classify the project as approved, conditionally approved, or rejected pending additional investigation. State the specific reason for any classification other than approved, and identify the exact condition that must be resolved before reclassification.
  3. File the signed scope, photographs, measurements, risk register, prerequisites, and readiness decision. Store the complete package where it will be available to the learner performing Chapter 2 and every later chapter for this project.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed document using the same checks regardless of which room or area was inspected.

  • Authorization check: confirm owner authorization, objectives, budget, and occupancy requirements were recorded before the physical inspection began.
  • Boundary check: confirm the inspection boundary and adjacent areas are stated and match the areas actually photographed and measured.
  • Fact-labeling check: confirm every entry is labeled as an observed fact, an owner-reported symptom, or a suspected concealed condition.
  • Risk-register check: confirm every suspected hazardous material, structural condition, and life-safety condition identified during the inspection appears on the risk register with a required referral.
  • Scope check: confirm the approved scope states repair limits, finish limits, access limits, exclusions, required permits, and required trades.
  • Classification check: confirm the project is classified as approved, conditionally approved, or rejected, with a stated reason for any classification other than approved.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Suspected Condition Was Recorded as a Confirmed Fact

Probable cause: the inspector recorded a likely utility route, hazardous material, or structural condition without labeling it as suspected, or relied on assumption rather than direct observation.
Confirmation: review each entry against the Reference Conventions definitions and check whether the entry was directly observed, measured, or tested.
Correction: relabel the entry as a suspected concealed condition or an owner-reported symptom, and add it to the risk register with its required referral if it was omitted.
Reinspection: repeat the fact-labeling check across the full existing-condition report and risk register.

The Owner's Goal Was Recorded as a Single Undifferentiated Request

Probable cause: the interview in Step 5 or the goal identification in Step 8 did not separate a failed-component repair from an elective layout or material change.
Confirmation: review the stated goal against the Repair vs. Elective Remodel definitions and identify which category each part of the request falls into.
Correction: split the recorded goal into its repair and elective components and record each separately on the existing-condition report.
Reinspection: repeat the scope check to confirm the approved scope reflects both categories accurately.

A Photograph or Measurement Was Taken After Contents Were Already Moved

Probable cause: Step 6 was performed after Step 4's records collection disturbed the room, or contents were moved before the photography step was reached.
Confirmation: compare the photograph timestamps or the inspector's notes against the sequence in which the room was accessed.
Correction: document that the pre-disturbance condition could not be fully captured, and note this limitation on the existing-condition report rather than presenting a post-disturbance photograph as a pre-existing condition record.
Reinspection: confirm the limitation is disclosed to the owner during the Section I review.

The Risk Register Omits a Condition Identified During the Interview or Walk-Through

Probable cause: a hazard, structural flag, or concealed-utility route identified verbally or visually in Section G was not transferred to the risk register.
Confirmation: cross-check every entry in the existing-condition report and interview notes against the risk register for a matching entry.
Correction: add the missing condition to the risk register with its required referral before the readiness classification is issued.
Reinspection: repeat the risk-register completeness check.

The Project Was Classified as Approved Despite an Open Referral

Probable cause: the readiness classification in Step 23 was completed before every required permit, inspection, or qualified-professional referral identified in Section H was resolved or scheduled.
Confirmation: compare the risk register and Section H referral list against the readiness checklist to confirm every item is resolved, scheduled, or explicitly deferred with a stated condition.
Correction: reclassify the project as conditionally approved or rejected and state the specific unresolved condition.
Reinspection: repeat the classification check after the referral is resolved or scheduled.

Cleanup

This project ends with a complete, signed documentation package, not with any physical disturbance of the space.

  • Restore any contents moved during the inspection to their original position unless the owner has agreed otherwise
  • Confirm every door, shutoff, faucet, drain, light, fan, or appliance operated during the inspection is returned to its normal condition
  • Store photographs, measurements, and completed forms together as a single dated package for the inspected room or area
  • Provide the owner with a copy of the approved scope and readiness decision
  • File the complete package where it will be available to the learner performing Chapter 2

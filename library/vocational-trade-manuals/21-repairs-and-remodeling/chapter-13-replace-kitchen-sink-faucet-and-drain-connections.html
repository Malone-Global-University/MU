Job Overview

This project qualifies you to remove an existing kitchen sink, faucet, and drain assembly and install a compatible replacement in the same countertop opening, with the supply and drain connections tested, accessible, and leak-free before the cabinet is closed up and returned to service.

You will confirm the replacement sink and faucet are compatible with the existing countertop opening and plumbing before any demolition occurs, shut off and verify water and electrical isolation, disconnect and remove the existing sink and its attached equipment, inspect the exposed countertop opening and cabinet condition, dry-fit and set the new sink and faucet, connect supply lines and drain components including any disposal or dishwasher connection present, and run full-bowl and operating tests before closing out the project.

This chapter follows Chapter 11's bathroom vanity replacement and Chapter 12's toilet replacement in the plumbing-fixture sequence of this book. It reuses the same shutoff-verification, leak-testing, and documentation discipline established in those chapters, applied to a kitchen sink installed in a countertop rather than a floor-mounted or freestanding fixture.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-073 — Remove an existing kitchen sink and faucet using a controlled, protected procedure that preserves the countertop, cabinet, and concealed plumbing.
  • RR-074 — Prepare and inspect the countertop opening, verifying condition, edge support, and compatibility before installing a replacement sink.
  • RR-075 — Install a replacement kitchen sink using the specified mounting, sealant, and support method for the sink and countertop system.
  • RR-076 — Install a replacement faucet and its accessories according to the manufacturer's mounting and connection instructions.
  • RR-077 — Connect the drain assembly, including trap, branch, disposal, and dishwasher connections where present, maintaining required support and accessible joints.
  • RR-078 — Inspect and test water supply, drainage, and operating performance, and document the completed installation.

Where This Skill Fits

A kitchen sink combines a countertop-supported fixture, a faucet, a drain and trap assembly, and frequently a disposal and dishwasher drain connection in one confined cabinet space. Replacing it correctly depends on skills built earlier in this book: the readiness and scoping discipline of Chapter 1, the containment and protection habits of Chapter 2, and the fixture-replacement sequence — shut off, verify, disconnect, inspect, repair, reconnect, test — first practiced on the vanity in Chapter 11 and the toilet in Chapter 12.

The countertop opening and cabinet condition documented in this chapter carry forward into Chapter 14, which replaces the backsplash section above this same sink, and into the Chapter 19 punch-list inspection, which verifies this fixture's supply, drain, and operating condition as part of final project closeout.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying countertop material, sink type, bowl configuration, mounting method, opening dimensions, faucet holes, and existing disposal or dishwasher connections
  • confirming the replacement sink, faucet, and drain components are compatible with the existing opening, cabinet, and plumbing before any removal begins
  • shutting off water and, where applicable, isolating electrical power to a disposal, and verifying that both are controlled
  • disconnecting and removing the existing sink, faucet, drain, disposal, and dishwasher connections in a controlled sequence
  • inspecting the exposed countertop opening, cabinet rails, plumbing, shutoffs, and wiring for damage or unsafe conditions
  • setting and securing the replacement sink in the existing opening using the specified mounting system
  • installing the replacement faucet, drain assembly, disposal, and dishwasher connections
  • testing supply, drain, and operating performance and documenting the completed installation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, enlarge, or reshape a stone, solid-surface, glass, integral, or other specialty countertop opening; this work is referred to the qualified fabricator
  • relocate the drain, vent, water supply, disposal circuit, or dishwasher connection outside its existing approved location
  • perform electrical disconnection or reconnection of a hardwired disposal circuit without a qualified person where required
  • proceed past a cracked or structurally weakened countertop, extensive water damage, unsafe wiring, leaking supply or drain piping, or an incompatible rough-in; these conditions stop the project and require referral
  • rely on sealant alone where the sink or countertop system specifies mechanical clips, brackets, or rail support

Any condition identified during inspection that exceeds this chapter's approved repair limits must be documented as a change order and referred to the qualified trade or design professional identified in the Chapter 1 risk register before work continues.

Trade Note

Verify sink, faucet, and drain compatibility with the existing countertop opening, cabinet, and plumbing before demolition begins, not after the old sink is already out. A replacement sink that does not match the existing opening dimensions, faucet-hole spacing, or drain location turns a fixture replacement into an unplanned countertop or cabinet modification outside this chapter's scope.

Setup Standard

Do not disconnect any supply, drain, disposal, or electrical connection (Step 7 forward) until compatibility of the replacement sink, faucet, and drain components has been confirmed (Step 2) and the cabinet has been cleared and protected (Step 6). A disconnection performed before compatibility is confirmed risks leaving the kitchen without a working sink if the replacement does not fit.

Shop Tip

Photograph the underside of the existing sink, the trap arrangement, and the disposal and dishwasher connections before disconnecting anything. A photograph of the original connection layout is the fastest way to confirm the new installation restores the same accessible, supported arrangement.

Common Field Fundamentals

A kitchen sink installation succeeds or fails on three points: the sink must be mechanically supported by the specified method rather than sealant alone, every drain and supply joint must remain accessible and leak-free under a full-bowl test, and no accessory — disposal, dishwasher drain, sprayer — may be reconnected until the base plumbing has already passed its own leak test.

Sealant Is Not Structural Support

Sealant or gasket material controls water intrusion at the sink-to-countertop joint. It does not carry the weight of a full sink bowl or resist the leverage of a person pressing down on the sink rim. Where the sink or countertop manufacturer specifies clips, brackets, rails, or another mechanical support system, install that system as specified. Do not substitute additional sealant for a missing or damaged clip.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Opening and Faucet-Hole Match Before Cutting the Old Seal

Measure the existing countertop opening length, width, and corner radius, and count and measure the spacing of the existing faucet holes, before loosening any old sealant. Compare these measurements against the replacement sink and faucet specification sheet. Do not proceed to demolition if the replacement does not match; stop and resolve the mismatch first.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify Water and Electrical Isolation Before Disconnection

Shut off both hot and cold supply valves and test that flow has stopped at the faucet before disconnecting any supply line. If the existing disposal is hardwired, do not disconnect its electrical connection unless you are qualified to do so or a qualified electrician has isolated the circuit. Treat any wiring at the disposal or under-sink area as energized until it has been verified de-energized by a qualified person.

Field Safety Cue — Confined Cabinet Work

Work under a kitchen sink involves a confined, poorly lit space with sharp cut edges, standing water, and awkward reach angles. Use a work light, clear the cabinet of stored items before starting, and use a basin wrench rather than forcing a standard wrench at an unsafe angle. Stop and reposition rather than working with your face directly beneath a connection you are loosening.

Code Alert — Disposal Electrical and Drain Requirements Vary

Requirements for disposal electrical connections, air-gap or high-loop dishwasher drain configurations, and trap arrangements vary by jurisdiction. Verify the required configuration with the local plumbing and electrical code and the disposal manufacturer's instructions before finalizing the connection method.

E. Preparation — Identification and Compatibility Confirmation (Steps 1–5)

  1. Identify countertop material, sink type, bowl configuration, mounting method, opening dimensions, faucet holes, disposal or dishwasher connections, drain arrangement, shutoffs, and support requirements. Record every value on the Fixture and Opening Record before ordering or confirming the replacement components.
  2. Confirm the replacement sink and faucet are approved for the existing countertop, cabinet, opening, plumbing, accessories, and required clearances. Compare the replacement specification sheet against the measured opening, faucet-hole spacing, and cabinet clearance recorded in Step 1; do not proceed if any dimension does not match.
  3. Refer stone, solid-surface, glass, integral, or other specialty countertop cutting and support work to the qualified fabricator where required. Stop and issue a referral if the replacement sink requires any enlargement or reshaping of a specialty countertop opening.
  4. Confirm the project does not require unapproved drain, vent, water, electrical, disposal, dishwasher, or structural cabinet modification. Compare the existing drain, vent, and electrical locations against the replacement components' required connections and confirm no relocation is needed.
  5. Photograph the cabinet, countertop, sink rim, clips, faucet, supply, drain, disposal, dishwasher, and any leakage evidence. Photograph from a fixed position inside the cabinet doorway and close up at each connection point before any disturbance begins.

F. Isolation and Disconnection (Steps 6–14)

  1. Clear the base cabinet and protect the floor, cabinet, doors, countertop, backsplash, and access route. Remove stored items from the cabinet and install floor and cabinet-interior protection before any tool is used.
  2. Shut off water and disconnect electrical equipment only through the approved procedure and qualified person where required. Turn both hot and cold shutoff valves fully closed; contact a qualified electrician to isolate a hardwired disposal circuit before proceeding.
  3. Test that water and electrical energy are controlled. Open the faucet and confirm flow stops completely; confirm with the electrician or a non-contact tester that the disposal circuit is de-energized before touching any wiring.
  4. Set up water capture, lighting, ventilation, and safe under-sink access. Place a bucket and towels beneath each connection point and position a work light inside the cabinet.
  5. Disconnect supply lines, drain components, dishwasher drain, disposal, and accessories in the approved order. Disconnect supply connectors first, then the dishwasher drain connection, then the disposal branch connection, then the trap and tailpiece.
  6. Support the sink and attached equipment before releasing clips, brackets, adhesive, or sealant. Have a second person support the sink from below or install temporary blocking before the last mounting point is released.
  7. Remove disposal or heavy accessories separately where required. Detach and lower the disposal unit independently rather than removing it while still attached to the sink.
  8. Cut the existing seal without damaging the countertop. Score the sealant bead at the rim joint using a utility knife held flat against the countertop surface.
  9. Release mounting hardware and lift the sink using adequate workers or lifting devices. Confirm every clip or bracket is released before attempting to lift; use a second person for a cast-iron or heavy composite sink.

G. Opening Inspection and Preparation (Steps 15–20)

  1. Clean the opening and remove sealant without gouging, cracking, swelling, or delaminating the countertop. Use a plastic scraper or manufacturer-approved solvent rather than an aggressive metal blade on laminate or solid-surface countertops.
  2. Inspect the opening, edge support, countertop condition, cabinet rails, moisture, mold, plumbing, shutoffs, wiring, and drain alignment. Record each finding as an observed fact with a photograph reference.
  3. Stop for cracked or weakened countertop, extensive water damage, unsafe wiring, leaking piping, or incompatible rough-in. Issue a change order and refer the condition to the qualified trade identified in the risk register before continuing.
  4. Repair minor accepted cabinet or countertop support conditions using the approved detail. Complete only the repair explicitly approved in the change-control process; do not improvise a structural fix.
  5. Dry-fit the new sink and verify rim bearing, clip access, bowl clearance, faucet clearance, disposal or drain position, and cabinet operation. Set the sink into the opening without sealant and confirm full, even rim contact and that cabinet doors and drawers still operate.
  6. Make only manufacturer-approved minor adjustments to non-specialty openings; do not improvise structural countertop cuts. Stop and refer the project if any adjustment beyond the manufacturer's stated tolerance is needed.

H. Assembly and Setting (Steps 21–25)

  1. Assemble the faucet, accessories, drain bodies, strainers, and sink-mounted components before setting the sink where practical. Install the faucet, sprayer, strainer, and disposal mounting ring on the sink while it is accessible on a padded work surface.
  2. Apply the specified sealant or gasket to clean compatible surfaces. Apply the manufacturer-specified product to the rim or gasket channel only, following the stated bead size and placement.
  3. Set the sink square in the opening and install clips, brackets, rails, or support systems progressively. Center the sink in the opening before tightening any single clip fully, then tighten all clips in a progressive, even sequence.
  4. Do not use sealant alone where mechanical support is required. Confirm every specified clip or bracket is installed and tightened before considering the sink secured.
  5. Remove excess sealant and allow required cure before loading. Wipe excess sealant from the rim joint immediately and observe the manufacturer's stated cure time before applying weight or water to the sink.

I. Connection and Restoration (Steps 26–29)

  1. Connect supply lines without kinks, twists, cross-threading, or strain. Start each connector fitting by hand before using a wrench, and confirm the connector routes without a sharp bend.
  2. Install the approved drain, trap, branch, disposal, and dishwasher connections with proper support and accessible joints. Reconnect components in the reverse of the disconnection order, confirming each slip joint seats fully before tightening.
  3. Restore electrical equipment only after plumbing connections and clearances are accepted. Have the qualified electrician reconnect or re-energize the disposal circuit only after all plumbing connections are complete and inspected.
  4. Restore water slowly and inspect pressurized connections. Open shutoff valves gradually and observe each supply connector and faucet base for drips before opening valves fully.

J. Testing, Inspection, and Documentation (Steps 30–34)

  1. Fill each bowl, operate the faucet and sprayer, release full-bowl water, run the disposal where included, and operate the dishwasher drain connection through approved tests. Perform each test in sequence and observe the corresponding connection point during and immediately after.
  2. Inspect strainers, drain bodies, trap joints, disposal flange, dishwasher hose, supply lines, shutoffs, sink rim, cabinet base, and room below for leakage. Use a dry paper towel wiped along each joint to detect a slow drip that is not otherwise visible.
  3. Verify drainage, faucet flow, temperature control, aerator, sprayer, stopper, disposal, and cabinet clearances. Confirm hot and cold flow and temperature response, stopper operation, and full range of faucet and sprayer motion.
  4. Complete required plumbing and electrical inspections. Schedule and pass any inspection identified as required in the Chapter 1 risk register or local code before considering the installation final.
  5. Document sink support, products, connections, tests, cure conditions, and owner maintenance instructions. Record the sink, faucet, and drain-kit model numbers, sealant product and cure time, test results, and any manufacturer maintenance instructions on the project closeout record.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed installation using the same checks regardless of sink material or configuration.

  • Compatibility check: confirm the replacement sink, faucet, and drain components were verified compatible with the existing opening, cabinet, and plumbing before demolition began.
  • Isolation check: confirm water and, where applicable, electrical power were verified isolated before any connection was disconnected.
  • Support check: confirm the sink is supported by its specified mechanical method and that sealant was not used as a substitute for required clips or brackets.
  • Leak check: confirm every supply, drain, disposal, and dishwasher connection passed a full-bowl or operating test without leakage.
  • Accessibility check: confirm every connection remains reachable for future service without removing the sink.
  • Documentation check: confirm products, connections, tests, and cure conditions are recorded on the project closeout record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Sink Rocks Under Hand Pressure After Installation

Probable cause: one or more mounting clips were not fully engaged, the sink was not centered before tightening, or the rim is not making full contact with the countertop opening edge.
Confirmation: inspect each clip for engagement and check rim contact around the full perimeter of the opening with a flashlight.
Correction: loosen all clips, recenter the sink, and retighten progressively in a balanced sequence around the perimeter.
Reinspection: repeat the hand-pressure check at all four corners and the center of each bowl.

A Slow Drip Appears at a Supply Connector After Restoring Water

Probable cause: the connector was cross-threaded, undertightened, or the washer or seal inside the connector was damaged or omitted.
Confirmation: dry the area completely, wipe a paper towel along the connection, and observe for a new wet mark after several minutes.
Correction: shut off the valve, disconnect the connector, inspect the threads and seal, and reconnect with a new connector if the seal is damaged.
Reinspection: restore water slowly and repeat the five-minute observation period.

Water Appears at the Trap or Tailpiece During the Full-Bowl Test

Probable cause: a slip-joint nut was undertightened, the washer inside the joint is missing or damaged, or the tailpiece and trap are not properly aligned.
Confirmation: dry the joint, repeat the full-bowl release, and observe the exact joint where water first appears.
Correction: loosen the joint, realign the tailpiece and trap, confirm the washer is present and undamaged, and hand-tighten before finishing with pliers.
Reinspection: repeat the full-bowl test and confirm no further leakage at the corrected joint or any adjacent joint.

The Disposal Vibrates Excessively or Leaks at the Mounting Ring

Probable cause: the mounting ring was not seated evenly, the sink flange gasket was omitted or displaced, or the disposal is not fully engaged in the mounting assembly.
Confirmation: inspect the mounting ring and gasket from beneath the sink and confirm the disposal has locked fully into the mounting assembly per the manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: disconnect the disposal, reseat or replace the gasket, and reinstall following the manufacturer's mounting sequence.
Reinspection: run the disposal again and inspect the mounting ring and branch connection for leakage and unusual vibration.

Water Backs Up Into the Dishwasher or Appears at the Dishwasher Drain Connection

Probable cause: the required air-gap or high-loop configuration was not installed, or the dishwasher drain hose was connected to the wrong disposal or branch fitting inlet.
Confirmation: compare the installed configuration against the local code requirement and the dishwasher manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: reconfigure the connection to the required air-gap or high-loop arrangement and confirm the hose is connected to the correct inlet.
Reinspection: repeat a complete dishwasher drain cycle and observe the connection for leakage or backflow.

  • Return all tools, buckets, and protection materials from the work area

Field Terms

Rim Bearing
The contact surface where the sink rim rests on or against the countertop opening edge, distributing the sink's weight and load.
Tailpiece
The vertical drain fitting extending down from each sink bowl or disposal to the trap.
Branch Drain
The horizontal or angled drain fitting connecting two bowls, or a bowl and a disposal, before the shared trap.
Air Gap
A code-recognized fitting or configuration that prevents drain water from siphoning back into the dishwasher.
High Loop
An alternative dishwasher drain-hose configuration routed above the sink's flood level where an air gap is not used and local code permits it.
Full-Bowl Test
Filling a sink bowl completely and releasing the water at once to verify the drain, trap, and connections under a realistic flow load.
Accessible Joint
A supply, trap, disposal, or dishwasher connection that remains reachable for future inspection or repair without removing the sink.
Supply Strain
Any twist, misalignment, or force applied to a shutoff valve or supply connector during connection that is not relieved before tightening.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to remove an existing bathroom vanity and install a compatible replacement vanity, top, sink, faucet, supply connections, and drain assembly in the same approved location. The finished work is a level, securely anchored cabinet with a supported top, a properly connected faucet and drain, and every pressurized and drain connection tested for leaks before the room is returned to service.

You will review the existing plumbing arrangement, confirm compatibility of every new component before demolition, shut off and verify water, disconnect and remove the old vanity, inspect the exposed wall and floor, install backing where required, set and anchor the new cabinet, install the top, connect the faucet and drain, restore water slowly, test every connection, and document the finished installation.

This chapter assumes the Chapter 1 existing-condition report and readiness decision are already in hand for the room. It builds directly on the drywall repair skills of Chapter 5 and the wall-opening controls of later chapters where backing must be added, and it feeds directly into Chapter 19's punch-list inspection and the Chapter 20 capstone, which may reuse this exact vanity-replacement procedure for the alternate powder-room design.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-061 — Remove an existing bathroom vanity using a controlled utility-shutdown and disassembly sequence.
  • RR-062 — Prepare the wall and floor area, including inspection of concealed conditions and installation of approved backing.
  • RR-063 — Set a new vanity level, plumb, and securely anchored to verified structural backing.
  • RR-064 — Install a vanity top using the specified support, adhesive, clip, or mechanical method.
  • RR-065 — Connect the faucet and drain assembly using manufacturer-specified components and joints.
  • RR-066 — Inspect fit, leveling, anchorage, and leakage through a defined water and drain test.

Where This Skill Fits

This project uses the concealed-condition inspection habits from Chapter 1 and the drywall and backing skills from Chapter 5 to solve one of the most common bathroom repair requests: a damaged, outdated, or leaking vanity, top, or faucet. It introduces water-supply shutoff verification, drain-trap connections, and leak testing, all of which reappear in Chapter 12 (toilet replacement), Chapter 13 (kitchen sink and faucet), and Chapter 20 (capstone).

The wall and floor condition exposed when the old vanity is removed is treated the same way any concealed condition is treated throughout this book: inspected, documented, and either accepted or referred before the new component is installed.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing vanity, top, faucet, drain, and supply compatibility before demolition
  • shutting off and verifying water at the existing serviceable valves
  • disconnecting and removing an existing vanity, top, sink, and faucet
  • inspecting the exposed wall, floor, and plumbing for damage or deterioration
  • installing approved backing for the new cabinet where required
  • setting, shimming, and anchoring the replacement vanity to verified backing
  • installing the vanity top, faucet, drain body, and sink
  • connecting supply lines and the drain trap without strain, kinks, or cross-threading
  • restoring water slowly and testing every connection for leaks
  • documenting products, connections, tests, and owner care instructions

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • relocate the drain, vent, water supply, or electrical rough-in; the replacement must use the existing approved plumbing arrangement
  • continue past a leaking or damaged shutoff valve, corroded or leaking supply piping, unsound floor, extensive moisture damage, unsafe wiring, or an incompatible rough-in; these conditions stop the project and require referral
  • rely on the countertop, plumbing connections, sealant, or floor friction to secure the cabinet in place of verified structural fastening
  • install sealant at the base or backsplash before fit and leak testing are complete

Any stone, solid-surface, glass, or other specialty top that requires fabricator cutting or support is outside this chapter's default scope and must be referred to the qualified fabricator identified in the material takeoff.

Trade Note

Verify every new component's compatibility with the existing rough-in before a single fastener or connection is disturbed. A vanity, top, sink, faucet, or drain that does not match the existing supply spacing, drain location, or countertop opening turns a planned same-day replacement into an open bathroom with no working fixture.

Setup Standard

Do not disconnect any supply line or drain connection (Step 8 forward) until water has been shut off at a verified serviceable valve and tested to confirm flow has stopped (Step 6). A supply line disconnected against unverified water flow will flood the cabinet, the floor, and any room below.

Shop Tip

Photograph the existing supply and drain configuration before disconnecting anything. A photograph of the original trap arrangement, supply-valve spacing, and shutoff type resolves more reassembly questions than any written note taken from memory after the parts are already apart.

Common Field Fundamentals

This project depends on three disciplines carried forward from earlier chapters: verify a utility is safely controlled before disconnecting it, treat every concealed condition exposed by demolition as a fact to be inspected rather than assumed, and never substitute sealant or countertop weight for mechanical anchorage. Applying those three disciplines here prevents the two most common vanity-replacement failures: a flooded cabinet from an unverified shutoff, and a rocking or falling cabinet from inadequate anchorage.

Cabinet Anchorage Is Not Optional

A bathroom vanity carries the weight of the top, the sink, standing water, and anyone who leans, sits, or pulls on it. The cabinet must be shimmed level and fastened to verified structural backing with the specified fastener type, length, and spacing. Sealant, adhesive, or countertop weight alone is never an acceptable substitute for that mechanical fastening.

Measurement Cue — Set a Level Reference Independent of the Floor

Establish a level reference line on the wall before setting the cabinet. Bathroom floors are frequently out of level near drains and thresholds; shimming the cabinet to a floor-based reference instead of a true level line will telegraph the floor's slope into the top and every visible reveal.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify the Shutoff Before Disconnecting Any Line

Do not disconnect a supply line until the shutoff valve has been closed and tested by opening the faucet and confirming no flow. If a fixture shutoff does not fully stop flow, close the upstream main or branch shutoff and refer a corroded or non-functional fixture valve before proceeding. Do not force a stuck valve; a stripped or cracked valve stem can cause an uncontrolled leak.

Field Safety Cue — Lifting and Floor Protection

Use team lifting or a mechanical aid for the old and new tops, and keep the floor protected with sheeting from the doorway to the vanity location throughout the project. A dropped or dragged top can crack the finish floor or the top itself.

Code Alert — Verify Local Plumbing Permit and Inspection Requirements

This project reuses the existing supply, drain, and vent locations without relocation. Some jurisdictions still require a permit or inspection for fixture replacement. Verify the local requirement before beginning work and record the result on the project specification sheet.

G. Preparation and Compatibility Review (Steps 1–4)

  1. Review vanity dimensions, top configuration, faucet holes, sink, drain location, supply locations, wall backing, floor condition, door swing, clearances, ventilation, electrical proximity, and permit requirements. Record every dimension and location on the Rough-In and Compatibility Record before ordering or unpacking the new components.
  2. Confirm the existing plumbing arrangement is serviceable and the project does not require unapproved drain, vent, supply, electrical, or structural relocation. Stop and refer if the rough-in is damaged, corroded, or does not match the approved scope.
  3. Verify the new vanity, top, sink, faucet, drain, shutoffs, supply connectors, and hardware are compatible before demolition. Dry-check the new top's faucet-hole spacing and the new cabinet's width against the existing opening before removing the old vanity.
  4. Photograph plumbing, attachment, wall, floor, backsplash, and finish conditions. Photograph the existing supply-valve type, drain trap arrangement, and cabinet attachment points before any disconnection.

H. Removal (Steps 5–13) — Competency RR-061

  1. Clear the cabinet and protect the room, doorway, floor, toilet, tub, mirror, and adjacent finishes. Remove stored contents and install protective sheeting along the access route.
  2. Shut off water using verified serviceable valves and test that flow has stopped. Close both shutoffs, then open the faucet and confirm no flow before proceeding.
  3. Provide a controlled water-capture and spill-response setup. Position a bucket and towels beneath the connections to be disconnected.
  4. Disconnect supply and drain components using the approved procedure. Disconnect the supply connectors at the shutoff and faucet, then disconnect the trap and tailpiece, capturing residual water in the bucket.
  5. Cap or protect open piping as required and prevent sewer gas or debris entry. Plug or cover the open drain stub-out with an approved temporary cap.
  6. Remove doors, drawers, shelves, top, sink, and cabinet in a controlled sequence. Remove doors and drawers first, then release the top, then the cabinet.
  7. Support heavy or brittle tops and use team lifting or mechanical assistance. Do not set the old top down on an unprotected edge or corner.
  8. Remove fasteners without damaging concealed plumbing, wiring, wall backing, or floor. Back out cabinet-anchoring screws fully before pulling the cabinet away from the wall.
  9. Inspect wall, floor, plumbing, shutoffs, drain, moisture, mold, rot, and previous repairs. Photograph and record every exposed condition before continuing.

I. Concealed-Condition Acceptance and Backing (Steps 14–16) — Competency RR-062

  1. Stop for leaking or damaged piping, unsound floor, extensive moisture damage, unsafe wiring, or incompatible rough-in. Do not proceed to installation until every stop-work condition is resolved or referred.
  2. Repair accepted wall and floor surfaces that will remain accessible or visible. Use the Chapter 5 drywall-repair procedure for any accepted wall damage within the cabinet footprint.
  3. Locate framing or install approved backing through the authorized method. Mark verified stud locations on the reference level line, or install the specified backing member where the cabinet's fastener pattern does not align with framing.

J. Setting and Anchoring the Cabinet (Steps 17–20) — Competency RR-063

  1. Dry-fit the vanity and verify level, plumb, wall contact, door and drawer operation, plumbing clearance, and finished position. Set the cabinet in place without fasteners and confirm it clears the drain and supply locations.
  2. Make only approved cabinet cutouts with protected edges and adequate clearance from plumbing. Cut any required access opening in the cabinet back only at the manufacturer-marked location.
  3. Shim the vanity with durable compatible shims and fasten it to verified structural backing. Insert shims at load-bearing points until the cabinet reads level against the reference line, then drive the fasteners specified in Section D.
  4. Do not rely on the countertop, plumbing, caulk, or floor friction to secure the cabinet. Confirm the cabinet does not shift when firmly pushed at any corner before proceeding to the top.

K. Top, Faucet, and Drain Installation (Steps 21–25) — Competencies RR-064 and RR-065

  1. Assemble and install the faucet, drain body, sink, and accessories according to manufacturer instructions where not factory installed. Complete this assembly on the top before setting it on the cabinet where practical.
  2. Set and secure the vanity top using the specified support, adhesive, clips, or mechanical method. Confirm full contact with the cabinet rail with no rock before final fastening.
  3. Maintain required wall, backsplash, and movement joints. Do not force the top tight against an out-of-square wall; shim or scribe per the manufacturer's fit instructions.
  4. Connect supply lines without twisting, kinking, cross-threading, overtightening, or placing strain on valves. Hand-thread each connector before final wrench tightening.
  5. Connect the drain using the approved trap and fitting arrangement while maintaining access and alignment. Confirm the trap arm slopes toward the drain and the cleanout, if present, remains accessible.

L. Testing and Finish (Steps 26–32) — Competency RR-066

  1. Restore water slowly and inspect every pressurized connection. Open each shutoff a quarter turn at a time and check both connectors before opening fully.
  2. Fill and drain the sink repeatedly while inspecting the drain body, overflow where present, trap, joints, shutoffs, and cabinet base. Perform the fill-and-drain test at least three times, checking for leakage after each cycle.
  3. Operate faucet handles, stopper, doors, and drawers through their full range. Confirm full temperature range, full flow, and complete stopper seal and release.
  4. Install approved sealant only after fit and leak testing are accepted. Apply the specified sealant bead at the top-to-wall and top-to-backsplash joints only after Steps 27–29 are complete.
  5. Complete required plumbing inspection. Contact the local building department or qualified inspector if the project scope requires one, and record the result.
  6. Inspect cabinet anchorage, level, top support, clearances, finish, faucet operation, drainage, leakage, and access. Complete the Acceptance Standards table in Section F for every item.
  7. Document products, connections, tests, inspections, and owner care instructions. Record the finished installation on the project specification sheet and provide the owner with product data and a copy of the completed inspection record.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed installation using the same checks regardless of vanity style or product line.

  • Shutoff verification check: confirm water was verified off before any supply line was disconnected.
  • Concealed-condition check: confirm the exposed wall, floor, and plumbing were inspected and either accepted or referred before installation continued.
  • Anchorage check: confirm the cabinet is fastened to verified backing and does not shift when pushed at any corner.
  • Level and plumb check: confirm the cabinet and top read level against the reference line and plumb at both front corners.
  • Leak check: confirm every supply and drain connection passed the fill-and-drain and pressurized-connection tests without leakage.
  • Documentation check: confirm products, connections, tests, and inspections are recorded and available to the owner.

Complete the Inspection Checklist below before returning the room to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Cabinet Rocks on a Verified Level Floor

Probable cause: an unequal shim height, a twisted cabinet frame, or a fastener driven before shimming was complete.
Confirmation: hold the level against the reference line at each corner and identify which corner sits low.
Correction: loosen the anchoring fasteners, adjust shims at the low corner, and re-verify level before re-tightening.
Reinspection: repeat the level and anchorage check at all four corners.

Supply Connector Drips Under Pressure

Probable cause: a cross-threaded connection, a missing or damaged washer, or an under-tightened nut.
Confirmation: shut off water, dry the fitting, and reopen water briefly while observing the connection under direct light.
Correction: disconnect, inspect the washer and threads, replace a damaged washer or connector, and reconnect hand-tight plus the manufacturer-specified turn.
Reinspection: repeat the pressurized-connection test for the full observation period.

Drain Trap Leaks During the Fill-and-Drain Test

Probable cause: a missing or misaligned washer, an under-tightened slip nut, or a trap arm that does not slope toward the drain.
Confirmation: dry the joint, refill the sink, and observe the joint during drainage.
Correction: disconnect, reseat the washer in the correct orientation, and hand-tighten before final wrench-snug tightening; adjust the trap arm slope if required.
Reinspection: repeat the fill-and-drain test at least twice more with no leakage observed.

Door or Drawer Contacts the Supply Lines or Drain Trap

Probable cause: the drain or supply connections were routed without verifying clearance to the door or drawer swing during the dry-fit step.
Confirmation: operate the door or drawer through its full range while observing the plumbing.
Correction: reroute the flexible supply connectors or adjust the trap position within the approved rough-in location to restore clearance.
Reinspection: repeat the door and drawer operation check through the full range of motion.

Top Does Not Sit Flush on the Cabinet Rail

Probable cause: debris on the rail, an out-of-square wall forcing the top out of position, or a shim installed at the top instead of the cabinet base.
Confirmation: remove the top, inspect the rail for debris or damage, and check the wall for square at the backsplash line.
Correction: clean the rail, scribe the backsplash to the wall per the manufacturer's fit instructions, and reset the top without forcing it into contact.
Reinspection: repeat the top support and level check.

Cleanup

This project ends with a tested, sealed, fully operational vanity installation and a clean bathroom ready for use.

  • Remove all packaging, old fasteners, and disconnected old components from the room
  • Wipe down the cabinet, top, faucet, and drain of dust, sealant residue, and fingerprints
  • Remove protective sheeting from the floor and access route without damaging the new finish
  • Confirm the shutoff valves, faucet, and stopper are left in their normal operating position
  • Store or dispose of the old vanity, top, and hardware according to the owner's instructions
  • File the completed project specification sheet, photographs, and inspection record with the Chapter 1 documentation package

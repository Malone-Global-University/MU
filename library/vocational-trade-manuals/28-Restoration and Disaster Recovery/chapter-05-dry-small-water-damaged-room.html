Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to develop, operate, monitor, and close one limited structural-drying project for an approved clean-water-damaged room. The finished work product is a labeled moisture map, a documented drying goal for every affected material, a safe and correctly placed set of dehumidification and air-movement equipment, a repeatable monitoring record covering every visit, and a drying completion record that formally releases the area for repair.

You will confirm that the source has been corrected and non-salvageable material removed, identify every material and assembly that requires drying, select unaffected reference locations, take and record initial readings, define measurable drying goals, select and place equipment safely, monitor conditions and readings on a defined schedule, investigate any reading that does not behave as expected, and close the project only when every material meets its accepted drying goal under stable operating conditions.

This chapter follows directly from Chapter 4's selective material removal and precedes Chapter 6's drywall, trim, and flooring repair. The moisture map and drying completion record you build here become the controlling release document Chapter 6 requires before any closure begins, and this same monitoring discipline governs the Chapter 20 capstone's drying phase.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-025 — Set drying goals from reference materials, product requirements, and qualified direction.
  • RD-026 — Improve airflow across wet surfaces using correctly positioned air movers.
  • RD-027 — Use fans and dehumidifiers safely, matched to the affected air volume and drainage plan.
  • RD-028 — Monitor moisture indicators at labeled, repeatable points using a defined schedule.
  • RD-029 — Inspect hidden damp areas behind base, cabinets, and adjacent rooms.
  • RD-030 — Document drying progress with readings, equipment records, and a completion record.

Where This Skill Fits

Every clean-water restoration project in this book depends on a defensible drying record before any material is closed in. This chapter takes the exposed, cleaned cavities left by Chapter 4 and turns them into a documented, dry, repair-ready condition. The moisture map, drying goals, and completion record produced here are the release document Chapter 6 requires before installing drywall, trim, or flooring, and the same monitoring method scales directly into the Chapter 20 capstone's drying phase.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming source correction, removal completion, contamination classification, and structural and electrical access before drying begins
  • identifying every material and assembly intended for drying and selecting unaffected reference locations
  • recording initial readings and creating a labeled, repeatable moisture map
  • defining measurable drying goals for each material type from accepted references and manufacturer or restoration-standard direction
  • selecting, placing, and safely operating dehumidification, air-movement, and filtration equipment
  • monitoring conditions and readings on a defined schedule and investigating anomalies
  • closing the drying project with a documented completion record and release for repair

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • dry contaminated water losses, sewage, floodwater, or material outside the approved clean-water scope established in Chapters 3 and 4
  • use the building HVAC system as the drying system unless the plan expressly permits it and qualified personnel have approved the configuration
  • power drying equipment through wet, damaged, overloaded, ungrounded, or unprotected circuits, or supply temporary power without qualified support where required
  • cover, paint, seal, or rebuild any material before it meets its documented drying goal and the completion record is signed
  • convert a single moisture reading into a universal dry standard for a dissimilar material

Any material or condition that cannot be dried within this chapter's approved scope must be referred to qualified restoration or engineering review, not assumed dry.

Trade Note

A moisture reading is a comparison, not a certificate. Every drying goal in this chapter must trace back to an unaffected reference material, a manufacturer requirement, or a qualified restoration standard — never to a single remembered percentage. Closing a room on a number that has no defined reference is the most common way a drying project fails after the walls are already closed.

Setup Standard

Do not install any drying equipment until source correction, removal completion, contamination classification, and electrical safety are confirmed. Do not power equipment through a wet, damaged, overloaded, ungrounded, or unprotected circuit, and do not use the building HVAC system as the drying system unless the plan expressly permits it.

Shop Tip

Photograph and label each moisture-map point with a placard or marked tape flag before taking the first reading, and take every later reading at the exact same point using the exact same instrument setting. A drying record built from inconsistent points cannot show whether a material is actually getting drier.

Comparative Readings, Not Universal Numbers

A moisture-meter reading only means something next to its own reference material, instrument, and location. A pinless reading on drywall and a pin reading on framing are not comparable to each other, and neither is comparable to a remembered "dry" percentage from a different project. Every drying goal in this chapter must be tied to a labeled reference location, a manufacturer requirement, or qualified restoration-standard direction — never to an isolated number.

Documentation Cue — Label the Moisture Map Before the First Reading

Assign and mark every moisture-map point before taking the first reading, and use the same point, the same instrument, and the same setting for every later reading at that location. Do not relocate or renumber points once monitoring has begun.

Critical Safety Cue — Electrical Isolation Before Equipment Installation

Do not install or power any drying equipment on a circuit that has not been inspected and confirmed safe. Do not power equipment through wet, damaged, overloaded, ungrounded, or unprotected circuits, and obtain qualified temporary-power support wherever the inspection requires it.

Field Safety Cue — Cord, Hose, and Trip Control

Route power cords and condensate hoses to avoid trip hazards, standing water, and doorways used for normal traffic. Secure hoses to prevent disconnection, leakage, backflow, or freezing throughout the drying period.

Code Alert — HVAC Use Requires Express Approval

Do not use the building's permanent HVAC system to distribute drying air unless the restoration plan expressly permits it and the system has been inspected. Confirm this approval in writing before considering the HVAC system part of the drying setup.

F. Preparation — Confirm Readiness and Identify Materials

  1. Confirm source correction, removal completion, contamination classification, utility safety, and structural access. Verify each against the Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 records before proceeding.
  2. Identify every material and assembly intended for drying. List each by room location, material type, and current exposed condition.
  3. Identify materials already removed and materials excluded from salvage. Mark these as excluded from the drying scope on the moisture map.
  4. Select unaffected reference locations of similar material where available. Label each reference location and record its material type.

G. Initial Readings and Drying-Goal Definition

  1. Record initial moisture readings, temperature, relative humidity, material condition, and instrument information. Take the first reading at every labeled point before any equipment is installed.
  2. Create a labeled moisture map with repeatable measurement points. Mark each point on the sketch and tag it physically in the room.
  3. Define drying goals from accepted reference, material requirements, restoration standards, and qualified direction. Record the source of each goal on the goal schedule.
  4. Do not use a single universal moisture percentage for dissimilar materials. Set a separate goal for each material type present.

H. Equipment Selection and Installation

  1. Calculate or select equipment only through the approved restoration method. Match dehumidifier and air-mover capacity to the affected air volume using that method.
  2. Inspect available electrical circuits and obtain qualified temporary-power support where required. Confirm capacity before connecting any equipment.
  3. Select approved dehumidifiers, air movers, air filtration, heat, extraction, or specialty equipment. Match each unit to the material and contamination conditions present.
  4. Do not use building HVAC as the drying system unless expressly approved. Confirm this in writing before considering it part of the setup.
  5. Position dehumidifiers to support the affected air volume and drainage plan. Place units according to the equipment-calculation output.
  6. Route condensate to an approved drain or contained collection point. Confirm the discharge point before startup.
  7. Secure hoses to prevent disconnection, leakage, backflow, freezing, or trip hazards. Inspect all connections before leaving the equipment unattended.
  8. Position air movers to circulate air across wet surfaces without spreading contamination or damaging finishes. Angle units according to the affected surface geometry.
  9. Do not direct high-velocity air at suspect mold, sewage, soot, asbestos, lead dust, or loose contaminated material. Redirect or exclude any such area from active airflow.
  10. Maintain required clearance from curtains, paper, insulation, combustible materials, and occupant routes. Verify clearance distances before startup.
  11. Install air filtration or containment controls where required by the plan. Confirm placement matches the approved setup.
  12. Record each equipment item, serial number, location, setting, power source, and start time. Complete the equipment inventory before leaving the site.

I. Monitoring, Investigation, and Closeout

  1. Measure and record environmental conditions after startup. Take the first post-startup reading set before leaving the site.
  2. Inspect for condensation, overheating, vibration, blocked intakes, noise, leaks, and circuit loading. Correct any condition found before continuing.
  3. Establish a monitoring schedule appropriate to the loss and equipment. Record the schedule on the drying log cover sheet.
  4. At each visit, inspect equipment operation before taking readings. Confirm every unit is running at its recorded setting.
  5. Record temperature and relative humidity at defined indoor, affected, and reference locations. Use the same locations at every visit.
  6. Record moisture readings at the same labeled material points using the same method. Do not substitute a different point or instrument setting mid-project.
  7. Inspect behind base areas, cabinets, wall plates, floor transitions, insulation, and adjacent rooms for hidden dampness. Record findings even where no equipment is currently placed.
  8. Use infrared or non-invasive screening only as a guide for confirmation, not proof of dryness. Follow any infrared indication with a moisture-meter reading at that location.
  9. Investigate readings that increase, remain unchanged, or differ unexpectedly. Record the anomaly, the investigation performed, and the finding.
  10. Adjust equipment placement, airflow, containment, or removal plan only through documented qualified review. Record who approved the adjustment and why.
  11. Record outages, open windows, occupant changes, weather, leaks, and other events affecting drying. Note the date, time, and expected effect on the drying record.
  12. Do not cover, paint, seal, or rebuild while drying is incomplete. Confirm this restriction remains posted for the duration of the project.
  13. Confirm that materials meet the approved drying goal and remain stable under final operating conditions. Take a confirming reading set with equipment still running before scheduling shutdown.
  14. Perform a final source, odor, cleanliness, moisture, structure, and utility inspection. Record the result of each item on the completion record.
  15. Remove equipment in a controlled sequence and inspect the area for condensate, cord damage, and residual moisture. Document the shutdown time and condition of each unit.
  16. Create the drying completion record with readings, dates, equipment, photographs, limitations, and approval for rebuild. Obtain the release signature before Chapter 6 work begins.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed drying documentation set using the same checks regardless of room size or material mix.

  • Map check: confirm every affected material and reference location has a unique labeled point with instrument type and initial reading.
  • Goal check: confirm every drying goal traces to a reference location, manufacturer requirement, or restoration standard.
  • Equipment check: confirm every unit has a complete inventory entry and confirmed electrical safety before startup.
  • Consistency check: confirm every monitoring visit used the same labeled points, instruments, and method.
  • Anomaly check: confirm every unexpected reading has a recorded investigation and corrective action.
  • Completion check: confirm every material met its documented goal under stable conditions before the completion record was signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Reading Stalls or Increases Partway Through Drying

Probable cause: a hidden moisture source, blocked airflow, condensation on equipment, or a labeled point measured with an inconsistent instrument setting.
Confirmation: re-measure the point with the original instrument and setting, inspect surrounding hidden cavities, and check equipment operation at that location.
Correction: adjust equipment placement or airflow only through documented qualified review, or identify and correct a previously undetected moisture source.
Reinspection: repeat the reading at the next scheduled visit and confirm the trend has resumed toward the drying goal.

The Drying Goal Has No Identified Source

Probable cause: a goal was written as a remembered percentage instead of being tied to a reference location, manufacturer requirement, or restoration standard.
Confirmation: review the goal schedule and confirm each goal cites its reference source.
Correction: re-establish the goal from an identified unaffected reference location or qualified restoration-standard direction before continuing to monitor against it.
Reinspection: repeat the goal-traceability check across the full goal schedule.

Equipment Is Running But Not Recorded in the Inventory

Probable cause: a unit was installed or repositioned during a monitoring visit without updating the equipment inventory.
Confirmation: walk the room and compare every operating unit against the equipment inventory list.
Correction: add the missing entry with identification, location, setting, power source, and start time.
Reinspection: repeat the equipment check at the next scheduled visit.

A Hidden Damp Area Was Found Behind Base or Cabinets Late in the Project

Probable cause: the original moisture map did not include a point behind base, cabinets, or an adjacent wall plate, or that area was not inspected at earlier visits.
Confirmation: inspect behind all accessible base, cabinet, and floor-transition areas in the affected and adjacent rooms.
Correction: add the location to the moisture map as a new labeled point, set its drying goal, and record it as an anomaly with the date discovered.
Reinspection: monitor the new point on the same schedule as the rest of the room until it meets its goal.

The Room Was Closed Before Every Material Met Its Goal

Probable cause: the completion record was signed based on a partial reading set, or one material's goal was overlooked.
Confirmation: cross-check the final reading set against every point on the moisture map and every goal on the goal schedule.
Correction: reopen monitoring for any material that did not meet its goal, reinstall equipment if needed, and do not proceed with Chapter 6 repair on that material until it is confirmed dry.
Reinspection: repeat the completion check across the full moisture map before re-signing the completion record.

Equipment Was Installed Before Electrical Safety Was Confirmed

Probable cause: the setup proceeded to equipment placement without inspecting the circuit or obtaining qualified temporary-power support where required.
Confirmation: check the drying log cover sheet for a completed electrical-safety confirmation with name and timestamp.
Correction: shut down and unplug all equipment, obtain the required electrical inspection or qualified support, and only then resume equipment installation.
Reinspection: confirm the cover sheet is complete before any further monitoring is accepted into the drying file.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with equipment removed and a complete filed drying record, not with any remaining active work area.

  • Confirm every dehumidifier, air mover, and filtration unit has been shut down, unplugged, and inspected for condensate, cord damage, and residual moisture before removal from the site
  • Coil and inspect cords and hoses for damage before returning equipment to storage
  • Back up all readings, photographs, and digital records before leaving the property or immediately upon return
  • Store the moisture map, goal schedule, equipment inventory, monitoring log, anomaly record, and completion record together as one dated drying file
  • Deliver the completion record and next authorized action to the responsible party as required by the final step of Section I

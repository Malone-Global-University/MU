Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to perform one authorized, non-destructive mold-risk and moisture assessment of a residence, and to produce a moisture-control plan that a responsible party can act on. The finished work product is a documented visible-condition survey, an affected-area map, a moisture-screening record, a list of likely and secondary moisture sources, a set of ventilation and humidity-control recommendations within the qualified scope, a list of required professional referrals, and a written moisture-control plan with location, source, action, responsible party, timing, verification, and referral for every identified item.

You will confirm authorization and clarify the limits of the assessment before beginning, inspect the exterior envelope and interior plumbing and ventilation systems for moisture contributors, walk high-risk rooms for visible fungal-like growth and moisture indicators, screen suspect and reference locations with a moisture meter, identify materials that may need professional sampling before disturbance, stop and refer any condition that exceeds this chapter's basic-work threshold, and hand off a measurable, monitored moisture-control plan to the responsible party.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1's damage-log discipline of separating observed fact from suspected cause, and it sets the boundary for Chapter 8's limited mold-safe work practices. Any condition this chapter identifies as exceeding the basic-work threshold becomes a referral, not a task for Chapter 8 or any later chapter in this book.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-037 — Identify mold-risk conditions from visible indicators without diagnosing species or toxicity.
  • RD-038 — Identify moisture sources contributing to an affected area, including primary and secondary sources.
  • RD-039 — Inspect ventilation issues affecting humidity, airflow, and condensation.
  • RD-040 — Document affected materials using consistent mapping and screening methods.
  • RD-041 — Create a moisture-control plan with measurable actions, responsibilities, and verification.
  • RD-042 — Flag professional-remediation triggers and route them to the correct referral.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 3 through 6 stopped and controlled active water losses. This chapter looks for the slower, recurring moisture conditions that create mold risk even when no single incident triggered the inspection — condensation, inadequate exhaust, grading defects, and small unresolved leaks. The affected-area map and moisture-control plan built here become the controlling documents for Chapter 8's limited mold-safe work practices, and the referral list built here protects the boundary between a homeowner-level moisture assessment and licensed environmental or remediation work.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming owner authorization and clarifying that the project is a moisture and visible-condition assessment, not a medical diagnosis or comprehensive environmental clearance
  • a non-destructive exterior and interior visual assessment for moisture contributors and visible fungal-like growth
  • moisture screening at suspect and reference locations using an approved instrument
  • identifying — without diagnosing species, toxicity, or health effects — visible mold-risk conditions
  • identifying ventilation and humidity contributors and preparing corrective actions within the qualified scope
  • preparing a written moisture-control plan and a list of required professional referrals

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • identify mold species, determine toxicity, or make any health or medical claim from appearance, color, or odor
  • sample, disturb, remove, clean, or treat suspect material; those actions belong to Chapter 8 within its own approved threshold, or to a licensed environmental or remediation professional
  • proceed with basic work where sewage, floodwater, extensive visible growth, widespread odor, HVAC contamination, or immune-compromised occupant concerns are present; these conditions are referred without exception
  • represent this assessment as a comprehensive environmental clearance or as proof that indoor air quality is safe

Any condition that exceeds this chapter's approved threshold must be recorded and referred, not minimized, guessed at, or worked around.

Trade Note

This chapter's plan is a moisture-control plan, not a mold-clearance certificate. It records what was visible and measurable on the day of the assessment and defines the actions, responsible parties, and verification needed to reduce moisture risk. Presenting it as anything more than that — a health clearance, a species identification, or a guarantee against future growth — is outside this chapter's scope and outside a non-specialist's qualification.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every moisture-risk assessment in this book follows the same sequence: confirm authorization and clarify scope, record baseline conditions, inspect exterior and building-system moisture contributors, walk affected and reference locations for visible indicators and screening readings, identify sources and referral triggers, and prepare a measurable moisture-control plan. Learning that sequence here means every later mold-safe or moisture-related task in this book starts from a record you already trust.

Visible Indicator, Not Diagnosis

This chapter records what is visible: fungal-like growth, staining, musty odor, condensation, swelling, delamination, decay, and repeated coating failure. It does not identify species, toxicity, or health effects from appearance, color, or odor. Every visible-condition entry is a description of what was seen, not a conclusion about what it is or what it means for occupant health.

Documentation Cue — Take the Reference Reading First

Record moisture and humidity readings at an unaffected reference location of similar material and construction before screening any suspect area. Label every reading with instrument type, location, material, and any limitation affecting the result.

Critical Safety Cue — Professional-Remediation Triggers Stop Basic Work

Stop and refer immediately for sewage, floodwater, extensive visible growth, widespread odor, HVAC contamination, immune-compromised occupant concerns, or any other condition outside this chapter's approved threshold. Do not sample, disturb, clean, or treat the material to "confirm" the trigger before referring it.

Field Safety Cue — Low-Airflow and Confined Spaces

Confirm lighting, footing, and communication before entering attics, crawlspaces, closets, and other low-airflow spaces. Do not enter a confined space alone when the assessment plan calls for supervised or two-person entry.

Code Alert — Health and Environmental Questions Require Referral

Refer health questions to qualified medical professionals and environmental classification or clearance questions to qualified environmental consultants. Do not answer either type of question from this chapter's visible-condition assessment.

F. Preparation — Authorization and Baseline Conditions

  1. Obtain owner authorization, moisture history, prior leaks, prior remediation, occupant reports, and known building conditions. Record each on the authorization and assessment scope cover sheet before proceeding.
  2. Clarify that the project is a moisture and visible-condition assessment, not a medical diagnosis or comprehensive environmental clearance. Review this exclusion with the owner and record their acknowledgment.
  3. Record weather, indoor temperature, relative humidity, recent water events, HVAC operation, and occupancy patterns. Enter these on the assessment cover sheet before the exterior walk begins.

G. Exterior and Building-System Inspection

  1. Inspect the exterior for roof, gutter, siding, flashing, grading, drainage, foundation, irrigation, and opening defects. Record each observed condition with its location and photograph.
  2. Inspect plumbing fixtures, supply connections, drains, appliances, condensate, and utility areas for leakage. Do not disturb or test any fixture beyond a visual check.
  3. Inspect bathrooms, kitchens, laundry rooms, closets, basements, crawlspaces, attics, exterior corners, and low-airflow areas. Confirm lighting, footing, and communication before entering confined or low-airflow spaces.

H. Room-by-Room Visible-Condition and Moisture Screening (Repeat for Every Affected or Suspect Area)

  1. Identify visible fungal-like growth, staining, musty odor, condensation, swelling, delamination, decay, and repeated coating failure. Record each as a described visible indicator with room, surface, and approximate extent.
  2. Do not identify species or toxicity from appearance, color, or odor. Describe only what is visible; do not name a mold type or estimate a health risk.
  3. Map visible and suspected affected areas by room, surface, and approximate extent. Use the affected-area map form and a consistent room identifier.
  4. Photograph overview and close conditions without disturbing material. Index each photograph to the mapped location.
  5. Use approved moisture-screening methods at suspect and reference areas. Take the reference-location reading first wherever practical.
  6. Record instrument type, location, material, reading, and limitations. Enter each on the moisture-screening record form.
  7. Inspect ventilation paths, exhaust fans, duct terminations, supply and return airflow, and blocked openings. Record whether each exhaust or supply path is functional, blocked, or absent.
  8. Identify high indoor humidity, cold surfaces, thermal bridges, occupant moisture loads, and inadequate exhaust as possible contributors. Record each as a possible contributor, not a confirmed cause.
  9. Inspect contents and stored materials that restrict airflow or contact damp surfaces. Record location and condition without moving items beyond what is needed to observe them safely.
  10. Identify porous, semi-porous, and nonporous materials in the affected area. Record the material category for each affected surface.
  11. Identify materials that may require professional sampling or hazardous-material testing before disturbance. Mark these materials as do-not-disturb pending professional assessment.
  12. Stop for sewage, floodwater, extensive visible growth, widespread odor, HVAC contamination, immune-compromised occupant concerns, or other professional-remediation triggers. Record the trigger, the affected area, and the immediate referral.
  13. Refer health questions to qualified medical professionals. Record the question and the referral; do not answer it directly.
  14. Refer environmental classification and clearance questions to qualified consultants. Record the question and the referral; do not answer it directly.

I. Source Analysis, Referral, and Moisture-Control Plan

  1. Identify the primary moisture source, secondary moisture sources, and unresolved source questions. Enter each on the source-analysis form.
  2. Prioritize active leak control before cleaning or coating. Sequence the plan so any active source is addressed before cosmetic recommendations.
  3. Prepare ventilation and humidity-control actions appropriate to the building and climate. State the specific action for each identified ventilation or humidity deficiency.
  4. Prepare drying, dehumidification, drainage, insulation, air-sealing, or maintenance recommendations within the qualified scope. Limit recommendations to actions within this chapter's non-destructive, non-remediation scope.
  5. Identify materials requiring removal, cleaning, monitoring, or further investigation without promising salvage. State the condition triggering each recommendation, not a guaranteed outcome.
  6. Define containment, PPE, respiratory, cleaning, disposal, and occupancy requirements for any limited work. Cross-reference Chapter 8 for any work within its approved threshold.
  7. Identify permits, licensed trades, environmental professionals, and certified remediation contractors required. List each against the specific condition that triggered the referral.
  8. Create measurable source-control and monitoring checkpoints. State the specific reading, condition, or observation that will confirm each action succeeded.
  9. Establish reinspection conditions after rain, plumbing use, seasonal change, or repair. State the specific trigger event and the reinspection timing for each affected area.
  10. Prepare a moisture-control plan with location, source, action, responsible party, timing, verification, and referral. Complete one line per identified item on the moisture-control plan form.
  11. Review the plan with the responsible party and document deferred actions. Record who reviewed the plan, the date, and any action the owner has deferred.
  12. Store photographs, readings, observations, limitations, and recommendations in the restoration file. File the complete assessment set together as one dated record.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed assessment set using the same checks regardless of property size or incident history.

  • Reference check: confirm every suspect-area moisture reading has a corresponding reference-location reading of similar material.
  • Diagnosis check: confirm no visible-condition entry states a species, toxicity level, or health effect.
  • Trigger check: confirm every professional-remediation trigger is listed in the referral list with basic work stopped for that area.
  • Source check: confirm primary and secondary sources are separately identified, with unresolved questions listed rather than assumed.
  • Plan check: confirm every identified item in the moisture-control plan has a location, source, action, responsible party, timing, verification method, and referral status.
  • Reinspection check: confirm reinspection conditions are stated for every affected area.

Complete the Moisture-Control Plan Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 8.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Visible Indicator Was Recorded as a Species Identification

Probable cause: the assessor named a mold type or estimated a toxicity level based on color or appearance instead of describing the visible condition.
Confirmation: review every visible-condition entry for species names, toxicity claims, or health-effect statements.
Correction: rewrite the entry as a description of what was seen — color, texture, extent, and location — without naming a species or toxicity level.
Reinspection: repeat the diagnosis check across the full visible-condition survey.

A Suspect-Area Reading Has No Reference-Location Comparison

Probable cause: the assessor screened the suspect area without first taking a reading at an unaffected reference location of similar material.
Confirmation: review the moisture-screening record for suspect-area entries without a paired reference-location entry.
Correction: return to an appropriate reference location and record a comparative reading, or note in the record why no comparable reference location was accessible.
Reinspection: repeat the reference check across the full moisture-screening record.

A Professional-Remediation Trigger Was Treated as Basic Work

Probable cause: the assessor continued documenting or attempted cleaning at a condition that met the sewage, floodwater, extensive-growth, widespread-odor, HVAC-contamination, or immune-compromised-occupant threshold.
Confirmation: review each affected area against the professional-remediation trigger definitions and confirm whether any trigger condition is present.
Correction: stop any basic-work activity in that area immediately, add the condition to the referral list, and notify the responsible party.
Reinspection: repeat the trigger check across the full property before resuming any further basic-scope work.

The Moisture-Control Plan Is Missing a Responsible Party or Timing

Probable cause: an identified moisture item was carried into the plan with an action but no assigned responsible party, timing, or verification method.
Confirmation: cross-check every source-analysis and referral-list entry against the moisture-control plan to confirm each has a complete line item.
Correction: add the missing responsible party, timing, and verification method to the plan before it is delivered.
Reinspection: repeat the plan completeness check across the full moisture-control plan.

A Health or Environmental Question Was Answered Directly

Probable cause: the owner or occupant asked whether the condition was making them sick or whether the air was safe, and the assessor answered instead of referring the question.
Confirmation: review the assessment notes and referral list for any health or environmental question that was answered rather than referred.
Correction: correct the record to show the question as referred, and provide the owner with the appropriate medical or environmental professional contact.
Reinspection: repeat the referral list review to confirm the correction was applied.

Reinspection Conditions Were Omitted from the Plan

Probable cause: the plan listed corrective actions but did not state when the area should be reinspected after rain, plumbing use, seasonal change, or repair.
Confirmation: review the moisture-control plan for any affected area without a stated reinspection trigger or timing.
Correction: add the specific reinspection trigger and timing to that line of the plan.
Reinspection: repeat the reinspection check across the full moisture-control plan.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a complete, filed assessment record, not with any physical work area to clean.

  • Back up all photographs and digital records before leaving the property or immediately upon return
  • Return original notes, sketches, and forms to the restoration file without editing the originals
  • Store the visible-condition survey, affected-area map, moisture-screening record, source analysis, referral list, and moisture-control plan together as one dated record
  • Confirm any do-not-disturb markers placed on suspect material remain in place until Chapter 8 or a qualified professional addresses them
  • Deliver the moisture-control plan and referral list to the responsible party as required by Step 31

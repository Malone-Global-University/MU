Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to perform one authorized, non-destructive initial inspection of a residence after a water, fire, storm, or other qualifying incident, and to produce a complete, traceable, and honest record of what you found. The finished work product is a dated damage log, a full photograph set, a safety register, a room-by-room affected-area map, a written limitations statement identifying what could not be inspected, and a preliminary restoration scope that separates emergency work from later mitigation, cleaning, drying, repair, and rebuild.

You will confirm authorization and release before entry, assess the exterior for uncontrolled hazards, enter only through an approved route, photograph and sketch every affected room, identify water, fire, structural, electrical, gas, and contamination concerns without diagnosing or repairing them, separate confirmed incident damage from suspected pre-existing conditions, and hand off a preliminary scope and referral list to the responsible party.

This is the first chapter in the book. It establishes the incident record that every later chapter depends on. Chapter 2 uses this record to set up a safe restoration work area. Chapter 3 uses it to locate and control the water source. Chapter 16 rebuilds the full restoration action plan from this same damage register, and the Chapter 20 capstone begins with this exact inspection sequence.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-001 — Identify visible damage across an incident-affected residence without disturbing evidence.
  • RD-002 — Photograph affected areas using a repeatable, traceable method.
  • RD-003 — Document room conditions using a consistent sketch-and-log method.
  • RD-004 — Identify safety hazards and classify them by required response.
  • RD-005 — Create a damage log with unique identifiers for every room, area, and hazard.
  • RD-006 — Prepare an initial restoration scope that separates emergency work from later restoration phases.

Where This Skill Fits

Every restoration project in this book begins with this inspection sequence, not with tools or repair work. The damage log, photograph index, and affected-area map you build here become the controlling record for Chapter 2's work-area setup, Chapter 3's source control, Chapter 4's material removal, and every insurance-documentation requirement in Chapter 17. The habit built in this chapter — separate observed fact from suspected cause, and separate emergency action from documentation — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone applies across an entire restoration project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming emergency-service release, owner authorization, and entry conditions before approaching the residence
  • a non-destructive exterior and interior visual assessment
  • photographing and sketching every affected room using a repeatable method
  • identifying — without diagnosing, repairing, or remediating — water, fire, structural, electrical, gas, and contamination concerns
  • creating a damage log, photograph index, safety register, and affected-area map
  • preparing a preliminary restoration scope and a list of required professional referrals

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • enter a property with active fire, smoke, fuel-gas odor, electrical arcing, unstable structure, standing water contacting energized equipment, chemical release, sewage, collapse risk, downed power lines, or an evacuation order
  • move debris, shut off utilities, extract water, remove materials, clean, dry, or begin any repair; those actions belong to later chapters and require their own authorization
  • diagnose cause of loss, assign insurance coverage, determine structural safety, or clear electrical, gas, or environmental hazards; those determinations belong to qualified professionals identified in this chapter's referral list
  • disturb any material, sample, or debris before the required overview photographs are complete, except where immediate life safety requires action

Any condition that cannot be safely and non-destructively inspected under this chapter's scope must be marked inaccessible and referred, not guessed at or assumed safe.

Trade Note

This chapter's damage log is a factual record, not a conclusion. Every entry must be traceable to something observed, photographed, or reported by a named person. Confusing an owner's account of what happened with a verified fact is the most common source of disputed restoration scopes later in the project.

Setup Standard

Do not approach or enter the residence until emergency-service release, owner authorization, and the exterior hazard assessment (Steps 1–6) are complete. Do not move debris or disturb any material before overview photographs are taken, except where immediate life safety requires it.

Shop Tip

Assign each room a unique identifier before you photograph it, and say or write that identifier at the start of each room's photo set. Sorting an unlabeled photograph set after the visit is how damage areas get mismatched to the wrong room.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every inspection in this book, from this chapter's first walkthrough to the Chapter 20 capstone, follows the same sequence: confirm release and authority, assess exterior hazards, enter safely, document before disturbing, identify and classify hazards, then prepare the next authorized action. Learning that sequence here, on a non-destructive assessment, means every later restoration chapter starts from a record you already trust.

Observed Fact, Owner Statement, and Suspected Cause

Every damage log entry must be labeled as one of these three categories. An owner's account of when a leak started is an owner statement, not an observed fact, until it is independently confirmed. A guess about why a wall is stained is a suspected cause, not a finding. Mixing these categories in the record is the single most common source of disputed scope later in the project.

Documentation Cue — Assign the Room Identifier First

Assign each room a unique identifier before photographing or sketching it, and use that same identifier on the log, the photograph index, and the map. Do not renumber rooms mid-inspection or reuse an identifier for a different space.

Critical Safety Cue — Uncontrolled Hazards Stop the Inspection

Do not enter or continue inspecting any area with active fire, smoke, fuel-gas odor, electrical arcing, unstable structure, standing water contacting energized equipment, chemical release, sewage, collapse risk, downed power lines, or an evacuation order. Establish an exclusion zone, record the condition, and notify the responsible party or emergency services immediately.

Field Safety Cue — Two-Person or Supervised Entry

Use a two-person or supervised entry protocol whenever the incident response plan requires it, and confirm communication methods before entry. Do not enter an incident-affected residence alone when the plan calls for supervised entry.

Code Alert — Authorization Varies by Property and Incident

Confirm who has authority to direct work — owner, insurer, adjuster, emergency responder, permit holder, building official, engineer, or other responsible party — before beginning the assessment. Do not assume that a verbal request from any single person satisfies every authorization this chapter requires.

C. Material and Supply Takeoff

  • Documentation supplies: damage log forms, photograph index sheets, safety register forms, room-sketch forms, and preliminary scope forms, sufficient for one full property with a 20 percent reserve for additional rooms or hazards discovered during the walk.
  • Equipment: one camera or smartphone camera, one scale reference card, one flashlight, one moisture meter for screening use, and one notepad, tablet, or dictation device per inspector.

D. Identifier and Classification Schedule

For every room, area, and hazard, assign a unique identifier and record the following: identifier code, location description, observed condition, category (observed fact, owner statement, or suspected cause), and classification (immediate hazard, emergency stabilization, mitigation, investigation, repair, replacement, monitoring, or unrelated). Do not assign the same identifier to two different rooms or hazards, and do not leave any observed condition unclassified.

F. Preparation — Authorization, Records, and Exterior Assessment

  1. Confirm emergency-service release, owner authorization, site contact, incident type, discovery time, occupancy status, and areas prohibited from entry. Record each on the incident and authorization cover sheet before proceeding.
  2. Obtain available fire, police, utility, plumbing, roofing, weather, security, or emergency-contractor records. File copies or references to these records in the restoration file.
  3. Record whether electrical, water, gas, HVAC, fire-alarm, security, and other utilities are on, off, damaged, or unknown. Note the source of this information for each utility.
  4. Conduct an exterior approach assessment before entering the building. Walk the accessible exterior perimeter from a safe distance before approaching any opening.
  5. Look for downed conductors, gas odor, unstable trees, roof damage, leaning walls, broken glass, standing water, smoke, fire debris, and blocked exits. Record each condition found, with its location, on the safety register.
  6. Establish an exclusion zone and stop entry for any uncontrolled immediate hazard. Notify the responsible party or emergency services immediately and record the notification.

G. Entry Setup and Exterior Documentation

  1. Confirm required personal protective equipment and communication methods before entry. Verify PPE against the hazard conditions found in Section F.
  2. Use a two-person or supervised entry protocol where the incident plan requires it. Confirm the second person or supervisor is in place before entry.
  3. Photograph the building address, all elevations, access route, utilities, weather conditions, and visible incident effects. Index each photograph to the property record before entering.
  4. Record the date, time, weather, temperature, recent precipitation, and active intrusion conditions. Enter these on the incident cover sheet.
  5. Enter only through the approved route and do not move debris before overview photographs unless life safety requires it. Confirm the approved route with the site contact before entry.

H. Room-by-Room Interior Assessment (Repeat for Every Affected Room)

  1. Assign a unique identifier to the room before photographing or sketching it. Write the identifier on the damage log, photograph index, and sketch form.
  2. Photograph the room from the doorway and from opposing corners. Capture the full room in this initial overview set before any closer inspection.
  3. Create a room-by-room sketch identifying walls, openings, fixtures, equipment, contents, and visible damage. Use the room-sketch form and the assigned identifier.
  4. Identify water lines, soot boundaries, staining, swelling, delamination, charring, corrosion, debris, odor, and displaced materials. Record each as an observed fact with its location on the sketch.
  5. Identify visible structural concerns including sagging, cracking, leaning, burning, impact, or failed connections. Record each on the safety register and mark it on the sketch.
  6. Identify electrical concerns including wet devices, damaged cords, open boxes, arcing evidence, damaged panels, and submerged equipment. Do not touch or test any electrical device; record its condition and location only.
  7. Identify gas and combustion concerns including odor, displaced appliances, damaged vents, soot, and disconnected piping. Do not relight, adjust, or test any gas appliance.
  8. Identify possible sewage, floodwater, mold, chemical, asbestos, lead, pest, or biological hazards. Record the observed condition only; do not sample, disturb, or classify the material.
  9. Identify broken glazing, sharp metal, exposed fasteners, unstable ceilings, damaged stairs, holes, and trip hazards. Mark each on the sketch and record on the safety register.
  10. Identify affected contents separately from building materials. Use a separate contents log line for each affected item or group of items.
  11. Mark areas that were inaccessible, concealed, unsafe, unlit, or not inspected. Add each to the limitations statement with the reason.
  12. Take close photographs with a scale reference where useful without disturbing evidence. Index each close photograph to the room identifier and the specific damage-log entry it supports.
  13. Create an initial moisture or residue screening map only within the approved non-destructive scope. Record instrument type, location, material, and reading for each screening point.
  14. Separate confirmed incident damage from suspected pre-existing wear or prior repairs. Label each damage-log entry accordingly.
  15. Record owner statements and witness information as attributed reports rather than verified facts. Name the person and the statement exactly as given.
  16. Classify each condition as immediate hazard, emergency stabilization, mitigation, investigation, repair, replacement, monitoring, or unrelated. Enter the classification on the damage log for every logged item.

I. Notification, Referral, and Scope Preparation

  1. Notify the responsible party immediately of life-safety, structural, utility, active-water, or contamination concerns. Record the notification method, time, and recipient.
  2. Identify professional referrals for structure, electrical, gas, plumbing, roofing, environmental, mold, fire, or hazardous material. List each referral against the specific condition that triggered it.
  3. Prepare a preliminary restoration scope that distinguishes emergency work, mitigation, contents, demolition, drying, cleaning, temporary repair, rebuild, and closeout. Sort every damage-log entry into one of these categories.
  4. State assumptions, exclusions, access limitations, and pending tests or approvals. Add these directly to the preliminary scope form.
  5. Preserve original photographs, notes, sketches, and communications in the restoration file. Do not edit, crop, or delete any original photograph or note.
  6. Obtain review or acknowledgment of the initial findings and next authorized action. Record who reviewed the findings, the date, and what action was authorized.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed documentation set using the same checks regardless of incident type or property size.

  • Coverage check: confirm every affected room has a unique identifier, doorway and opposing-corner photographs, a sketch, and at least one damage-log entry.
  • Hazard check: confirm every identified hazard appears in the safety register with a classification and required response.
  • Category check: confirm every damage-log entry is labeled observed fact, owner statement, or suspected cause.
  • Traceability check: confirm every photograph is indexed to a room or hazard identifier.
  • Limitations check: confirm every area not inspected is listed with a stated reason.
  • Scope check: confirm every logged condition is sorted into a preliminary scope category and every required referral is listed.

Complete the Damage Log and Preliminary Scope Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Photographs Cannot Be Matched to a Room Later

Probable cause: the room identifier was not assigned before photography began, or the same identifier was reused for two different rooms.
Confirmation: compare the photograph index against the damage log and sketch set to find any photograph without a matching identifier.
Correction: return to the property if still authorized, or clearly annotate the existing photographs using timestamps, fixed landmarks, and cross-references to the sketch; assign identifiers before photography on every future inspection.
Reinspection: repeat the traceability check across the full photograph set.

An Owner's Account Was Logged as a Verified Fact

Probable cause: the inspector recorded what the owner said without labeling it as an owner statement.
Confirmation: review each damage-log entry and confirm whether it was directly observed, reported by the owner, or a suspected explanation not yet confirmed.
Correction: relabel the entry as an owner statement, and note whether it is independently supported by any observed fact or record.
Reinspection: repeat the category check across the full damage log.

A Hazard Was Noted in Prose but Not Added to the Safety Register

Probable cause: the hazard was described in a room note or sketch caption but never transferred to the safety register with a classification.
Confirmation: cross-check every damage-log entry describing a structural, electrical, gas, or contamination concern against the safety register.
Correction: add the missing entry to the safety register with its classification, required response, and notification record.
Reinspection: repeat the hazard check across the full damage log and safety register.

An Inaccessible Area Was Assumed Safe Instead of Recorded

Probable cause: the inspector could not safely reach an area — such as behind unstable debris, inside a locked space, or in poor lighting — and left it off the record instead of logging it as inaccessible.
Confirmation: walk the property plan or floor sketch and confirm every room and area is either documented or listed in the limitations statement.
Correction: add the area to the limitations statement with the specific reason it was not inspected, and identify what would be required to inspect it safely.
Reinspection: repeat the limitations check against the full property.

The Preliminary Scope Omits a Logged Condition

Probable cause: a damage-log entry was classified but never carried forward into the preliminary scope's emergency, mitigation, investigation, repair, replacement, monitoring, or unrelated categories.
Confirmation: cross-check every damage-log entry against the preliminary scope form to confirm each has a corresponding scope category.
Correction: add the missing item to the correct scope category and confirm any required referral is listed.
Reinspection: repeat the scope completeness check across the full damage log and preliminary scope.

Entry Occurred Before Authorization or Release Was Confirmed

Probable cause: the inspector proceeded to the exterior or interior assessment without confirming emergency-service release or owner authorization first.
Confirmation: check the incident and authorization cover sheet for a completed release and authorization record with names and timestamps.
Correction: stop the inspection, obtain and record the required release and authorization, and only then resume or repeat the assessment.
Reinspection: confirm the cover sheet is complete before any further documentation is accepted into the restoration file.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a complete, filed documentation set, not with any physical work area to clean.

  • Back up all photographs and digital records before leaving the property or immediately upon return
  • Return original notes, sketches, and forms to the restoration file without editing the originals
  • Store the damage log, photograph index, safety register, sketches, limitations statement, and preliminary scope together as one dated record
  • Confirm the exclusion zones and any temporary hazard markers established during the exterior assessment remain in place if the property will not be immediately re-entered
  • Deliver the initial findings and next authorized action to the responsible party as required by Step 33

Performance Check

With the exterior and interior assessment complete, review the finished documentation set against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every affected room has a unique identifier, doorway and opposing-corner photographs, a sketch, and a log entry; every hazard is classified in the safety register; every damage-log entry is labeled observed fact, owner statement, or suspected cause; every photograph is indexed; every uninspected area appears in the limitations statement; and every logged condition is sorted into the preliminary scope with required referrals. Chapter Closeout

You have completed the first chapter in the residential restoration and disaster recovery sequence: inspecting and documenting damage after an incident. This chapter required you to identify visible damage without disturbing evidence (RD-001), photograph affected areas using a repeatable method (RD-002), document room conditions with a consistent sketch-and-log method (RD-003), identify and classify safety hazards (RD-004), create a damage log with unique identifiers (RD-005), and prepare an initial restoration scope separating emergency work from later phases (RD-006).

You now have a complete, traceable initial damage record and a repeatable inspection method. That record and method carry directly into the next chapter: setting up a safe, contained restoration work area using the hazards, boundaries, and authorizations you have just documented.

File your damage log, photograph index, safety register, sketches, limitations statement, and preliminary scope together as one dated restoration record. Chapter 2 begins from this exact file.

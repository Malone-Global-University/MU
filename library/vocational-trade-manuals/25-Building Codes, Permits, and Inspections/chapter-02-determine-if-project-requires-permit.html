Job Overview

This project qualifies you to evaluate a defined residential project scope and produce a documented, source-supported permit decision before any work begins. The finished work product is one permit decision file containing a classified scope, a permit matrix, identified exemptions and their limitations, licensing and professional-boundary findings, official clarification correspondence, and a final documented decision of permit required, permit not required based on a cited exemption, or unresolved pending official determination.

You will define the property and proposed scope, classify each scope item using the jurisdiction's own definitions, identify structural, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, site, and zoning triggers, identify licensing and professional requirements, locate and test any claimed exemption, prepare focused official questions, contact the permit authority, and issue a documented decision supported entirely by the Project 01 binder and direct agency confirmation.

This is the second project in the book. It depends entirely on the authority register, adopted-code sources, and exemption resources built in Project 01. The permit decision produced here becomes the controlling input for Project 03's application packet and for every later inspection-readiness project that assumes the correct permits are already in place.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-007 — Identify permit triggers across structural, MEP, site, and zoning scope items.
  • CP-008 — Distinguish repair from alteration, addition, demolition, relocation, and change of use using local definitions.
  • CP-009 — Identify structural work requiring a permit or design-professional review.
  • CP-010 — Identify plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and other MEP work requiring a permit or licensed trade.
  • CP-011 — Identify exterior, site, and zoning triggers, including explicit permit exemptions and their limitations.
  • CP-012 — Prepare a documented permit-decision checklist supported by official sources and correspondence.

Where This Skill Fits

Every regulated residential project starts with the same question this chapter answers: does this specific scope require a permit, and under which classification? Project 03 cannot assemble an application packet until this decision identifies which permits apply and which licensed trades or design professionals are required. Every later inspection-readiness project in this book assumes the underlying permit was correctly identified and issued before work began.

The classification discipline in this chapter — using the jurisdiction's own definitions rather than assumption, and treating an exemption as narrow and conditional rather than automatic — is the same discipline the Chapter 19 remodel review and the Chapter 20 capstone depend on.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the property, scope, and existing conditions for one proposed residential project
  • classifying each scope item as maintenance, repair, alteration, addition, demolition, relocation, or change of use using local definitions
  • identifying structural, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, site, and zoning permit triggers
  • identifying licensing, owner-builder, and design-professional requirements
  • locating and testing any claimed permit exemption against its official conditions and limitations
  • preparing and submitting official clarification questions for ambiguous scope
  • issuing and filing a documented permit decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin demolition, excavation, structural work, utility disconnection, or equipment replacement before the permit decision and any required permit are complete
  • treat a claimed exemption as confirmed without verifying its exact conditions and limitations at the official source
  • size, approve, or field-modify any structural, MEP, or energy component
  • represent this decision checklist as a permit approval, inspection result, or legal opinion

Any scope item that cannot be classified from an official source must be logged as unresolved and referred to the responsible authority rather than decided by assumption.

Trade Note

The most common failure in this project is treating "replacement in kind" or a general exemption phrase as automatically permit-exempt. Almost every jurisdiction attaches conditions to these exemptions — for example, unchanged location, unchanged capacity, or no structural or electrical involvement. Test the exact wording of the exemption against the actual scope before relying on it.

Setup Standard

Do not classify a scope item from memory, a prior project's decision, or a general internet summary. Classify it using the jurisdiction's own definitions of maintenance, repair, alteration, addition, demolition, relocation, and change of use, sourced from the Project 01 binder or a direct agency confirmation.

Shop Tip

Build the stop-work list before finalizing the permit matrix. Deciding which activities cannot begin before permit issuance is what actually prevents the project from concealing or starting regulated work before the decision is complete.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every permit decision in this book, from a simple repair to the Chapter 20 capstone's full scope classification, follows the same sequence: define the scope, classify each item against the jurisdiction's own definitions, identify every trigger and exemption, identify licensing and professional boundaries, resolve ambiguity through the authority rather than assumption, and issue a documented decision. Learning that sequence here, on one defined project, means every later chapter's permit work starts from a method you already trust.

Classification, Exemptions, and Stop-Work Boundaries

Every scope item receives one classification using the jurisdiction's own definitions, and every claimed exemption is tested against its exact published conditions before it is relied upon. Record the source, section, and date verified for every classification and exemption entered in the permit matrix.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Begin Stop-Work Items

Do not begin demolition, excavation, structural work, utility disconnection, or equipment replacement listed on the stop-work list until the required permit is issued or a cited exemption is confirmed at the official source. Beginning regulated work before the decision is complete is a stop-work condition in itself.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Whether a wall is load-bearing, whether an owner qualifies as an owner-builder, and whether a scope item is legally exempt are determinations made by the design professional, permit authority, or plans examiner, not by this checklist or its preparer. Log any such question as unresolved and route it to the responsible party.

Field Safety Cue — Contacting the Permit Authority

Ask a specific, scope-tied question rather than describing the project vaguely. Record the agency, contact name or identifier where offered, date, exact question, and exact response. Preserve any written guidance and treat a verbal comment as field information requiring written confirmation where the agency requires it.

Code Alert — Exemptions Are Narrow and Conditional

An exemption from the permit process is never an exemption from code, zoning, licensing, safety, utility, or HOA obligations. Confirm the exact conditions and limitations of any claimed exemption at the official source before applying it to the scope.

E. Preparation — Define and Document the Scope

  1. Define the property, owner, proposed work, location, dimensions, systems affected, cost or valuation if required, and intended use. Record this information on the Property and Scope Record above before classifying any item.
  2. Collect photographs, sketches, plans, equipment information, and existing permit history relevant to the scope. File these with the scope description in the decision file.
  3. Identify the authority having jurisdiction and confirm the current permit rules from the Chapter 1 binder. Do not rely on a rule that has not been verified in the Project 01 binder or confirmed directly with the authority.

F. Classify the Scope

  1. Classify each scope item using local definitions for maintenance, repair, alteration, addition, demolition, relocation, change of use, and new construction. Record the classification and its official source for each item in the classification record.
  2. Do not treat replacement in kind as exempt until the local definition and limitations are verified. Confirm the exact conditions of any "like-for-like" or "replacement in kind" language at the official source.
  3. Identify structural work affecting foundations, bearing walls, beams, headers, posts, joists, rafters, trusses, shear walls, bracing, stairs, guards, decks, or retaining walls. Record each affected structural component in the trigger register.
  4. Identify work affecting exterior openings, roof structure, siding, fire separation, egress, occupancy, accessibility, or weather protection. Record each affected item in the trigger register.

G. Identify MEP, Site, and Zoning Triggers

  1. Identify plumbing work involving fixtures, water, drainage, venting, gas, water heaters, backflow, sewer, septic, well, or irrigation. Record each affected plumbing item in the trigger register.
  2. Identify electrical work involving service, panels, feeders, circuits, receptacles, lighting, alarms, generators, EV charging, solar, low voltage, or utility connections. Record each affected electrical item in the trigger register.
  3. Identify HVAC and mechanical work involving equipment, ducts, ventilation, exhaust, refrigerant, fuel, combustion air, chimneys, flues, or condensate. Record each affected mechanical item in the trigger register.
  4. Identify site work involving excavation, grading, drainage, driveways, right-of-way, utilities, trees, floodplain, erosion control, wells, septic, pools, fences, or accessory structures. Record each affected site item in the trigger register.
  5. Identify zoning or planning triggers for setbacks, use, height, lot coverage, parking, historic review, design review, or accessory dwelling units. Record each affected zoning item in the trigger register.
  6. Identify fire-department, health-department, utility, public-works, environmental, or other separate approvals. Record each separate agency approval required by the scope.

H. Identify Professionals, Licensing, and Exemptions

  1. Identify project conditions requiring architect, engineer, surveyor, geotechnical, energy, testing, or special-inspection services. Record each condition in the licensing findings.
  2. Identify contractor and trade licensing requirements for the proposed work. Record the applicable license categories from the Project 01 licensing section.
  3. Identify owner-builder options and required disclosures without assuming the owner qualifies. Record the disclosure requirement and route qualification questions to the owner and authority, not to assumption.
  4. Locate explicit permit exemptions and record their source, conditions, and limitations. File each exemption citation with its exact published wording.
  5. Confirm that an exemption does not remove code, zoning, licensing, safety, utility, or HOA obligations. Record this confirmation next to each exemption citation.
  6. Separate exempt work from permit-required work when the project contains both. Mark each scope item in the permit matrix as exempt, permit-required, or unresolved individually rather than applying one status to the whole project.
  7. Identify whether work may proceed under an over-the-counter, express, standard, deferred, or phased permit process. Record the applicable process for each permit-required item.
  8. Identify whether demolition or investigative opening requires authorization before the main permit. Add any required pre-authorization to the stop-work list.

I. Resolve Ambiguity and Issue the Decision

  1. Prepare specific official questions for ambiguous scope rather than describing the project vaguely. Draft one focused question per unresolved item, tied to the exact scope condition.
  2. Contact the permit authority through the approved channel and record written guidance where available. Use the Field Safety Cue above when making contact.
  3. Record the official contact, date, question, response, and any stated limitations. Complete one clarification-log entry per contact.
  4. Prepare a permit matrix listing each scope item, authority, permit type, exemption basis if any, license requirement, document need, and decision status. Complete one row per scope item.
  5. Prepare a stop-work list for activities that cannot begin before permit issuance or separate approval. Cross-check every stop-work item against the permit matrix.
  6. Identify permit, design, fee, review, inspection, and schedule impacts for the Book 24 estimate or project budget. Record these impacts for use in cost and schedule planning.
  7. Review the decision with the owner, contractor, and design professional as applicable. Record the reviewers and the date of review.
  8. Issue a documented decision of permit required, permit not required based on cited exemption, or unresolved pending official determination. Record the decision, its date, and its supporting sources in the final decision record.
  9. File the decision checklist, sources, correspondence, and project-scope description in the compliance record. Store the completed decision file where it is available for Project 03.

Common Decision-File Inspection

Inspect the finished decision file using the same checks regardless of which scope item is being reviewed.

  • Classification check: confirm every scope item's classification is sourced to the jurisdiction's own definition and dated.
  • Trigger check: confirm every structural, MEP, site, zoning, fire, health, and utility trigger present in the scope is recorded in the trigger register.
  • Exemption check: confirm every claimed exemption cites its official source, conditions, and limitations, and has been tested against the actual scope item.
  • Licensing check: confirm contractor, trade, owner-builder, and design-professional requirements are recorded for every applicable item.
  • Stop-work check: confirm every activity that cannot begin before permit issuance is listed and cross-checked against the permit matrix.
  • Unresolved-item check: confirm every unresolved item is contacted, answered, and recorded with agency, contact, date, question, and response.
  • Decision check: confirm the final decision is documented, dated, reviewed with the applicable parties, and filed with its supporting sources.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Scope Item Is Classified as Exempt Without Confirming the Exemption's Conditions

Probable cause: the learner assumed a general phrase such as "repair" or "replacement in kind" applied without reading the jurisdiction's exact conditions and limitations.
Confirmation: compare the exemption's exact published wording against the actual scope item, including location, capacity, and system changes.
Correction: reclassify the item as permit-required or unresolved if the exemption's conditions are not fully met, and record the corrected classification with its source.
Reinspection: repeat the exemption check on the corrected entry.

A Structural or MEP Trigger Was Missed in the Scope Description

Probable cause: the scope description did not identify every affected structural, plumbing, electrical, or mechanical component, so the trigger register is incomplete.
Confirmation: compare the scope description, photographs, and sketches against the trigger categories in Sections F and G.
Correction: add the missing trigger to the register and reclassify any affected scope item.
Reinspection: repeat the trigger check on the completed register.

An Owner-Builder Disclosure Is Missing or the Owner's Qualification Was Assumed

Probable cause: the learner treated owner-builder status as available by default rather than confirming the official disclosure and qualification requirements.
Confirmation: review the Project 01 licensing section and the licensing findings entry for the disclosed qualification basis.
Correction: obtain the official disclosure and route any qualification question to the owner and the permit authority.
Reinspection: repeat the licensing check on the corrected entry.

A Stop-Work Item Was Left Off the List

Probable cause: an activity that cannot begin before permit issuance was not cross-checked against the permit matrix during assembly.
Confirmation: compare every permit-required scope item in the matrix against the stop-work list.
Correction: add the missing activity to the stop-work list and confirm it has not already begun.
Reinspection: repeat the stop-work check on the completed list.

An Unresolved Item Was Decided Without an Agency Response

Probable cause: a scope item that could not be classified from published sources was decided by the learner's best guess instead of being logged and referred to the authority.
Confirmation: review the clarification log for entries missing a recorded contact, date, question, or response.
Correction: contact the responsible authority through the approved channel and record the full response before finalizing the decision.
Reinspection: repeat the unresolved-item check on the completed log.

The Final Decision Was Not Reviewed or Dated

Probable cause: the permit matrix was completed but the final decision record was never formally issued, dated, or reviewed with the owner, contractor, or design professional.
Confirmation: check the final decision record for a recorded decision, date, and list of reviewers.
Correction: complete the review, record the decision and date, and file the supporting sources.
Reinspection: repeat the decision check on the finalized record.

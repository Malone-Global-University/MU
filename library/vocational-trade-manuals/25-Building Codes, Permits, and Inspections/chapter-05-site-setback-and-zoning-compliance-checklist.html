Job Overview

This project qualifies you to identify setbacks, verify lot-coverage limits, identify easements, check height restrictions, review accessory-structure limits, and prepare a zoning compliance checklist for one defined residential parcel. The finished work product is one parcel-specific zoning and site compliance package: a marked-up site plan showing verified controlling dimensions, a completed checklist linking each requirement to its source and status, and a record of every unresolved item referred to the responsible professional or authority.

You will obtain the current survey, plat, and zoning map for the parcel; identify the zoning district and every applicable overlay; locate setback, easement, coverage, height, and accessory-structure requirements; measure the proposed project against the approved site plan; and resolve unclear or conflicting site conditions through the surveyor, zoning official, utility, or design professional rather than through field assumption.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 authority register and binder. It confirms the site-level constraints that must be resolved before Chapter 6's foundation-inspection preparation and before the Chapter 3 permit application packet can show an accurate site plan.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-025 — Identify setbacks required for the proposed project from the adopted zoning code and property record.
  • CP-026 — Verify lot-coverage limits using the jurisdiction's own definitions and the parcel's recorded dimensions.
  • CP-027 — Identify easements, rights-of-way, and no-build areas affecting the buildable area.
  • CP-028 — Check height restrictions using the jurisdiction's defined grade and measurement method.
  • CP-029 — Review accessory-structure limits applicable to the proposed project.
  • CP-030 — Prepare a zoning compliance checklist linking requirement, source, plan value, status, and responsible party.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 02 determined whether the proposed work needs a permit and flagged zoning triggers as a category. This project resolves those zoning triggers to specific, sourced, measured requirements before the Chapter 3 application packet is assembled. An inaccurate site plan discovered during plan review forces a resubmittal cycle; an inaccurate site plan discovered after foundation layout forces a stop-work order, a survey correction, and possible removal of completed work.

The same verification discipline used here — confirming property lines from the recorded survey rather than from fences or aerial imagery, and referring unresolved conditions to the surveyor or zoning official rather than guessing — carries forward into the Chapter 6 foundation-inspection preparation and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the parcel, zoning district, overlays, and recorded property information
  • identifying setback, lot-coverage, easement, height, and accessory-structure requirements from official sources
  • measuring the proposed project against the legally controlling reference on the approved or draft site plan
  • flagging missing survey information, uncertain lot lines, conflicting dimensions, and unverified easements
  • converting unresolved site issues into surveyor, zoning, utility, fire, or engineering referrals
  • preparing a sourced, dated zoning compliance checklist and a marked-up site plan

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • establish a legal property line, easement boundary, or elevation benchmark; that determination belongs to a licensed surveyor
  • grant, imply, or predict the outcome of a variance, conditional-use permit, or design-review approval
  • interpret an ambiguous zoning definition without confirming it with the zoning official
  • treat this checklist as a permit approval, survey, or engineering evaluation

Any dimension that cannot be traced to the recorded survey, an official zoning determination, or the approved site plan must be labeled unresolved rather than estimated.

Trade Note

Every controlling dimension on this checklist is measured from the recorded survey or plat, never from a fence line, a landscaping edge, a tax map, or an aerial image. A dimension estimated from a satellite photo is the single most common cause of a rejected site plan diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Required handling for this project: do not establish or represent a property line, easement boundary, or elevation benchmark from any source other than the recorded survey, plat, or a licensed surveyor's field work.

Setup Standard

Do not measure a proposed setback, height, or coverage value until the recorded survey, plat, or legally controlling site plan is in hand. A dimension measured from an unverified reference must be labeled unresolved and referred to the surveyor before it controls the checklist.

Shop Tip

Photograph the recorded survey's monument or marker locations before walking the site. Comparing the survey's marker descriptions with what is actually visible on the ground is faster and more reliable than trying to pace off a setback from a fence that may not sit on the actual property line.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every site-compliance task in this project follows the same sequence: confirm the legally controlling reference, identify the applicable zoning requirement from its official source, measure the proposed condition against that requirement, record the result with its source and date, and refer anything unresolved to the responsible professional or authority before it controls a design decision.

Official Sources and Legally Controlling References

Every setback, coverage, easement, height, or accessory-structure value entered on the checklist must be traced to the adopted zoning code, the recorded survey or plat, or an official written determination. A value estimated from a fence, a landscape edge, a tax map, or an aerial image cannot be entered as a controlling dimension.

Documentation Cue — Record Source and Reference for Every Dimension

Record the requirement, its official source and section, the plan value measured against it, the status, and the responsible party for every checklist entry. An entry without a recorded source cannot be treated as verified.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Establish Property Lines in the Field

Do not establish, assume, or adjust a property line, easement boundary, or elevation benchmark from a fence, landscaping, tax map, aerial image, or occupant statement. Use only the recorded survey, plat, legal description, or a licensed surveyor's field work. Stop and obtain a survey or surveyor confirmation whenever the legally controlling reference is unclear, missing, or contradicted by field conditions.

Field Safety Cue — Walking the Site

When walking the parcel to compare recorded conditions with field conditions, watch for uneven grade, overgrown vegetation, buried utility markers, animals, and traffic near the property edge. Do not enter a neighboring property to verify a shared line without permission.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Variances, conditional-use approvals, formal zoning interpretations, and property-line establishment are made by the zoning official, planning department, or a licensed surveyor, not by this checklist or its preparer. Where a requirement or boundary is ambiguous, label it unresolved and record the official source that must answer it.

E. Preparation — Confirm Parcel, Survey, and Zoning District

  1. Identify property address, parcel number, legal lot, ownership, and jurisdiction. Record this information on the Parcel and Project Record above.
  2. Obtain the current survey, recorded plat, legal description, site plan, zoning map, and property record. Confirm the survey or plat date and that it reflects the current recorded condition of the parcel.
  3. Do not establish property lines from fences, landscaping, tax maps, aerial imagery, or occupant statements. Use only the recorded survey, plat, or legal description as the controlling reference.
  4. Identify the zoning district and all overlays, planned-development conditions, historic controls, flood zones, and special districts. Record each applicable overlay in the Overlays and Special Districts section.
  5. Locate the permitted-use, conditional-use, accessory-use, and prohibited-use provisions. File the current published provisions in the Zoning District section.
  6. Confirm whether the proposed project changes use, occupancy, dwelling count, parking, or home-occupation status. Record the classification and its official source.

F. Identify Setbacks and Easements

  1. Identify front, side, rear, street-side, waterfront, slope, well, septic, and other setback requirements. Record each applicable setback value and its official source in the Setbacks section.
  2. Identify how the jurisdiction defines lot line, yard, building line, projection, eave, deck, porch, bay, stair, and accessory structure. Record each definition used to measure a setback.
  3. Measure proposed setbacks from the legally controlling reference on the approved site plan. Record the plan value against each adopted setback requirement.
  4. Identify recorded easements, rights-of-way, utility corridors, drainage easements, access easements, and no-build areas. Mark each easement location on the site plan from the recorded survey or plat.
  5. Confirm whether encroachment agreements, easement releases, or utility approvals are required. Record the requirement and status in the Easements and No-Build Areas section.

G. Calculate Coverage and Verify Height and Accessory Limits

  1. Calculate existing and proposed building footprint using the jurisdiction's lot-coverage definition. Record the calculation method and result in the Lot Coverage section.
  2. Calculate impervious coverage, landscape area, open space, or floor-area ratio where required. Record each applicable calculation with its official definition source.
  3. Identify height limits and the local method for measuring grade, average grade, ridge, roof, parapet, or structure height. Record the grade-measurement method before checking the proposed height.
  4. Identify story, basement, attic, mezzanine, and accessory-structure definitions affecting height or area. Record each definition applicable to the proposed scope.
  5. Identify accessory-building size, location, separation, foundation, utility, and use limits. Record each applicable limit in the Accessory Structures section.
  6. Identify deck, fence, pool, retaining wall, shed, driveway, parking, and mechanical-equipment restrictions. Record each restriction applicable to the proposed scope.

H. Identify Access, Environmental, and Private Restrictions

  1. Identify required parking spaces, driveway dimensions, sight triangles, curb cuts, and right-of-way permits. Record each requirement in the Parking, Access, and Environmental section.
  2. Identify landscape, tree, stormwater, erosion, wildfire, flood, coastal, or environmental conditions. Record each applicable condition and its official source.
  3. Identify utility, well, septic, fire-access, hydrant, and emergency-access restrictions. Record each restriction applicable to the proposed scope.
  4. Identify HOA, covenant, deed, lease, or architectural-review requirements separately. File these in the Private Restrictions section, clearly separated from government zoning requirements.

I. Compare, Flag, and Resolve

  1. Compare the site plan with zoning requirements line by line. Work through every section of the Component Schedule against the current site plan.
  2. Flag missing survey information, uncertain lot lines, conflicting dimensions, and unverified easements. Record each flagged item in the Unresolved Items and Referrals section.
  3. Convert unresolved site issues into surveyor, zoning, utility, fire, engineering, or legal questions as appropriate. Write each question as a specific, answerable request tied to the flagged item.
  4. Obtain official zoning determinations, variances, administrative approvals, or design-review approvals where required. Record the responsible agency, contact, date, question, and response for each item submitted.
  5. Do not represent a variance as likely or guaranteed before formal approval. Record only the status of the submitted request, not an anticipated outcome.
  6. Prepare the checklist with requirement, source, plan value, status, evidence, responsible party, and action. Complete the checklist entry for every section identified in Steps E through H.
  7. Attach a marked-up site plan showing verified controlling dimensions and unresolved areas. Distinguish verified dimensions from unresolved dimensions using a consistent marking method.

J. Review and Archive

  1. Review the checklist before foundation layout or excavation. Confirm every setback, coverage, height, and easement entry is sourced and dated before field layout begins.
  2. Update the checklist after plan-review comments or approved site revisions. Record the revision date and the reason for each update.
  3. Archive zoning approvals, survey, marked-up plan, correspondence, and final checklist. Store the archived package where it remains available to the Chapter 3 application packet and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Common Checklist Inspection

Inspect the finished checklist using the same checks regardless of which section is being reviewed.

  • Sourcing check: confirm every setback, coverage, height, and easement entry has a recorded official source and section.
  • Reference check: confirm every measured dimension traces to the recorded survey, plat, or legal description, not a fence, tax map, or aerial image.
  • Completeness check: confirm all eleven checklist sections from the Component Schedule are present and populated.
  • Marked-up plan check: confirm the site plan clearly distinguishes verified dimensions from unresolved areas.
  • Unresolved-item check: confirm every flagged item is logged, referred, and tracked to a recorded response before the checklist is used for layout.
  • Private-restriction check: confirm HOA, covenant, deed, or lease restrictions are recorded separately from government zoning requirements.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Setback Was Measured From a Fence Instead of the Recorded Property Line

Probable cause: the preparer measured from a visible fence, hedge, or landscape edge instead of the recorded survey or plat.
Confirmation: compare the fence location with the recorded survey's property-line dimensions and monument locations.
Correction: remeasure the setback from the legally controlling reference and correct the checklist entry and marked-up site plan.
Reinspection: repeat the reference check on the corrected entry.

An Easement Shown on the Recorded Plat Is Missing From the Site Plan

Probable cause: the site plan was prepared without cross-checking the recorded plat's easement notations.
Confirmation: compare the recorded plat's easement locations against the current site plan.
Correction: add the missing easement to the marked-up site plan and confirm whether it affects the buildable area or requires an encroachment agreement.
Reinspection: repeat the easement-identification check on the corrected site plan.

Lot Coverage Was Calculated Using an Assumed Formula Instead of the Local Definition

Probable cause: the preparer used a general or out-of-jurisdiction coverage formula instead of the adopted zoning code's own definition.
Confirmation: compare the calculation method used against the zoning code's published coverage definition.
Correction: recalculate lot coverage using the jurisdiction's own definition and correct the checklist entry.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage-calculation check with the corrected method.

A Height Was Measured From the Wrong Grade Reference

Probable cause: the preparer measured height from finished grade, natural grade, or an assumed point instead of the jurisdiction's defined grade-measurement method.
Confirmation: compare the measurement method used against the zoning code's grade-definition and height-measurement rules.
Correction: remeasure the height using the correct grade reference and correct the checklist entry.
Reinspection: repeat the height-verification check with the corrected measurement.

An Unresolved Item Was Carried Into Layout Without a Recorded Response

Probable cause: a flagged item was logged but never actually submitted to the surveyor or zoning official, or a verbal answer was recorded without a name, date, or agency.
Confirmation: review the Unresolved Items and Referrals section for entries missing a recorded response, contact, or date.
Correction: submit or resubmit the question through the approved channel and record the full response before layout proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the unresolved-item check on the completed log before authorizing foundation layout.

A Private Restriction Was Mixed Into the Government Zoning Section

Probable cause: an HOA, covenant, or deed restriction was filed in the same section as government setback or coverage requirements.
Confirmation: review each entry in the zoning sections and confirm whether it originates from a government authority or a private review body.
Correction: relocate the private restriction to the Private Restrictions section and label its source accordingly.
Reinspection: repeat the sourcing check on both the zoning and private-restriction sections.

Field Terms

Legally Controlling Reference
The recorded survey, plat, or legal description used to establish property lines and other controlling dimensions.
Setback
The minimum required distance between a lot line and a structure or feature, measured using the jurisdiction's defined method.
Lot Coverage
The portion of the lot occupied by building footprint, impervious surface, or floor area, calculated using the jurisdiction's own definition.
Easement
A recorded right held by another party to use a defined portion of the property for a stated purpose.
Grade-Measurement Method
The jurisdiction's defined method and reference point for measuring building height.
Accessory Structure
A detached building or structure subordinate to the primary residence, subject to its own size, location, and use limits.
Overlay District
An additional set of zoning requirements layered over the base zoning district, such as flood, wildfire, or historic controls.
Unresolved Site Issue
Any dimension, boundary, or requirement that cannot yet be confirmed from an official source and must be tracked until answered.
Marked-Up Site Plan
The site plan showing verified controlling dimensions distinguished from unresolved areas.
Private Restriction
An HOA, covenant, deed, or lease requirement confirmed and recorded separately from government zoning requirements.

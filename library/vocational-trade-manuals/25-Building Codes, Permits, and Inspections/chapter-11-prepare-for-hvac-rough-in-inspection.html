Job Overview

This project qualifies you to prepare one residential mechanical and HVAC rough-in for the required inspection by verifying that installed equipment, ducts, ventilation, exhaust, combustion-air, refrigerant, condensate, and control systems match the approved plans, load calculations, and manufacturer instructions before the work is concealed. The finished work product is one complete, photographed, and documented HVAC rough-in inspection-readiness package that a mechanical contractor, project manager, or building official can review and use.

You will confirm the mechanical permit and responsible licensed contractor, verify equipment type and clearances, verify duct routing and support, verify combustion-air and venting, verify refrigerant and condensate systems, confirm required tests, prepare safe inspector access, correct a deficiency list, and schedule and attend the inspection with the responsible trade representative.

This project depends on the Chapter 1 authority register, the Chapter 2 permit decision, and the Chapter 3 approved application packet. Its outcome — an approved HVAC rough-in — is one of the prerequisite trade approvals required before the Chapter 15 interior-concealment inspection and the Chapter 16 final building inspection can proceed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-062 — Inspect duct support, protection, and sealing basis.
  • CP-063 — Identify combustion-air requirements for fuel-burning equipment.
  • CP-064 — Verify equipment clearances for service, access, and combustion.
  • CP-065 — Review ventilation requirements for exhaust and whole-house systems.
  • CP-066 — Complete an HVAC rough-in inspection checklist and readiness record.

Where This Skill Fits

The HVAC rough-in inspection confirms that equipment, ducts, ventilation, exhaust, combustion, refrigerant, and condensate systems are installed as approved before insulation, drywall, and finishes conceal them. This project follows the same authority-scope-permit-document-inspect-correct-approve-archive workflow used in the Chapter 6 foundation, Chapter 7 framing, Chapter 9 plumbing rough-in, and Chapter 10 electrical rough-in projects, applied here to the mechanical trade.

The energy-code duct-sealing, insulation, and testing requirements identified here feed directly into the Chapter 12 energy-code compliance matrix. Any correction generated during this inspection is tracked using the Chapter 17 correction-log method. The approved HVAC rough-in becomes part of the Chapter 20 capstone's coordinated trade-inspection record.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the mechanical permit, responsible licensed contractor, approved plans, and revisions
  • verifying equipment type, location, support, access, and clearances
  • verifying supply, return, exhaust, and outside-air duct routing, support, and protection
  • verifying combustion-air openings, flues, chimneys, and vent terminations
  • verifying refrigerant lines, condensate systems, and required protections
  • confirming required duct-leakage, gas-pressure, or other tests and qualified testing parties
  • preparing safe access, correcting deficiencies, and scheduling and attending the inspection

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select, size, or approve HVAC equipment, ducts, or ventilation systems beyond the authorized design documents
  • perform or supervise gas-pressure, refrigerant, or combustion testing reserved for the licensed mechanical contractor
  • conceal ducts, piping, dampers, firestopping, or equipment connections before required approval is documented
  • treat a verbal inspector comment as authorization to proceed without written confirmation where the jurisdiction requires it

Any equipment, duct, or ventilation condition that cannot be verified against the approved documents must be logged as unresolved and referred to the responsible design professional, licensed mechanical contractor, or authority having jurisdiction.

Trade Note

An HVAC rough-in inspection covers ducts, combustion, ventilation, refrigerant, and condensate systems that will become inaccessible once insulation and drywall are installed. Verifying these systems against the approved documents before scheduling — rather than during the inspector's visit — is what prevents a failed inspection from becoming a schedule delay.

Setup Standard

Do not verify equipment, ducts, or combustion systems from memory of a similar project. Confirm every clearance, duct route, and vent termination against the actual approved plans, load calculations, and manufacturer instructions for this specific installation before marking it ready for inspection.

Shop Tip

Photograph every duct, damper, combustion, and condensate detail room by room as you verify it, rather than trying to reconstruct the record afterward. A dated photograph taken during the readiness review is often the only evidence available once insulation and drywall conceal the work.

Approved Documents, Manufacturer Instructions, and Verification Records

Every equipment, duct, or ventilation item verified during this project is checked against the approved mechanical plans, load calculations, energy documents, and the manufacturer's own installation instructions. Record the plan reference, manufacturer document, or listed value used for every checked item. An item verified without a recorded reference cannot be treated as confirmed.

Documentation Cue — Record the Governing Reference for Every Item

Do not verify a clearance, duct route, or vent termination from memory of a similar installation. Confirm it directly against the approved plans or manufacturer instructions for this equipment and record the reference before marking the item ready.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Conceal Before Approval

Do not insulate, drywall, enclose, or otherwise conceal ducts, piping, dampers, firestopping, or equipment connections before the required HVAC rough-in approval is documented. Do not connect fuel, energize equipment, or place a system in service beyond authorized temporary testing conditions before required approval.

Field Safety Cue — Gas, Refrigerant, and Combustion Conditions

Do not open, connect, pressurize, or test gas piping, refrigerant lines, or combustion equipment beyond the learner's qualification. Coordinate these items with the responsible licensed mechanical contractor and record the test method, result, and responsible party exactly as performed.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Equipment sizing, duct design, gas-pressure testing, and combustion-air calculations are performed by the design professional, licensed mechanical contractor, or authority having jurisdiction, not by this checklist or its preparer. Where a verified condition cannot be confirmed against the approved documents, log it as unresolved and identify the responsible party who must answer it.

E. Preparation — Confirm Permit, Documents, and Prerequisites

  1. Confirm mechanical permits, responsible licensed contractor, approved plans, load calculations, equipment schedules, energy documents, and revisions. Record the permit number and the current approved revision on the readiness worksheet.
  2. Identify separate mechanical, gas, duct, ventilation, fireplace, equipment, or final inspections. Confirm which inspection types apply to this project's scope from the Chapter 1 authority register.
  3. Verify equipment type, capacity, efficiency, fuel, location, support, and access, and manufacturer instructions. Compare the installed unit against the approved equipment schedule item by item.
  4. Do not approve equipment selection or sizing beyond the authorized design documents. Refer any mismatch between installed equipment and the approved schedule to the design professional or licensed contractor.
  5. Verify furnace, air handler, heat pump, boiler, water heater, mini-split, ventilation unit, and related equipment clearances. Measure each clearance directly and record it against the approved value.
  6. Verify service access, filter access, replacement path, electrical working space, drain access, and attic or platform access. Confirm each access path remains clear and usable after remaining rough-in trades finish their work.

F. Verify Ducts, Registers, and Exhaust Systems

  1. Verify supply, return, exhaust, outside-air, transfer, and other duct routing against approved plans. Trace each run from equipment to termination and compare it with the approved layout.
  2. Verify duct size, material, fittings, connections, support, protection, and sealing basis as documented. Record the sealing method used for later comparison with the Chapter 12 energy matrix.
  3. Verify flex-duct length, bend, compression, support, and damage without redesigning the system. Flag any flex-duct condition that does not match the approved installation basis rather than correcting it independently.
  4. Verify register, grille, diffuser, return, thermostat, control, and sensor locations coordinate with framing, cabinets, doors, and finishes. Confirm each location against the approved plan and the current finish layout.
  5. Verify exhaust fans, kitchen exhaust, bath exhaust, dryer exhaust, and other required systems terminate correctly according to approved documents. Trace each exhaust run to its exterior termination.
  6. Verify ducts do not discharge into attics, crawlspaces, garages, or other unapproved locations. Flag any duct terminating in an unapproved space as a deficiency item.

G. Verify Combustion, Venting, Refrigerant, and Condensate Systems

  1. Verify combustion-air openings, direct-vent systems, equipment rooms, closets, louvers, and communicating spaces as documented. Confirm opening size and location against the equipment listing and approved plans.
  2. Verify flues, chimneys, vent connectors, terminations, clearances, supports, firestops, and roof flashing. Confirm each vent termination clearance against the manufacturer instructions.
  3. Verify fuel-gas piping, valves, regulators, sediment traps, supports, bonding coordination, and pressure testing through licensed trade scope. Confirm this work is performed and documented by the responsible licensed party.
  4. Verify refrigerant lines, insulation, protection, support, penetrations, line-hide systems, and equipment connections. Confirm insulation continuity and protection at every penetration.
  5. Verify condensate pans, traps, drains, pumps, overflow protection, termination, support, and freeze considerations. Confirm the condensate path terminates as approved and includes required overflow protection.
  6. Verify mechanical ventilation, whole-house ventilation, local exhaust, controls, balancing, and commissioning documents where required. Confirm the ventilation system's control location and documentation match the approved energy path.

H. Coordinate, Test, and Photograph Before Concealment

  1. Verify energy-code duct sealing, insulation, air-handler location, controls, testing, and documentation requirements. Cross-check this item against the Chapter 12 energy-code compliance matrix.
  2. Verify fire, smoke, radiation, or other dampers and rated penetrations remain visible at the required stage. Confirm each damper location matches the approved fire-separation detail.
  3. Verify structural openings, framing alterations, equipment curbs, pads, hangers, and vibration isolation are approved. Confirm no unauthorized structural modification was made to accommodate equipment.
  4. Verify exterior penetrations coordinate with WRB, flashing, roofing, siding, and pest control. Confirm every exterior penetration is sealed and flashed according to the approved envelope details.
  5. Confirm required duct leakage, gas pressure, condensate, control, ventilation, or other tests and qualified testing parties. Schedule these tests with the responsible licensed party before requesting the inspection.
  6. Collect equipment data, listings, load calculations, ventilation calculations, test forms, and manufacturer instructions. Assemble these documents in the readiness folder alongside the permit.
  7. Photograph concealed ducts, supports, dampers, penetrations, condensate, combustion, and equipment access. Label each photograph with location, date, and system for the compliance record.

I. Correct, Schedule, and Attend the Inspection

  1. Provide safe access, lighting, attic platforms, ladders, guards, and service clearances. Confirm the inspector can reach every verification zone without an unsafe condition.
  2. Complete a system-by-system preinspection with the responsible mechanical contractor. Walk through each verification zone together before scheduling.
  3. Create and correct the HVAC rough-in deficiency list. Assign each identified item to a responsible party and verify the correction before proceeding.
  4. Confirm required tests are ready and approved documents are onsite. Verify test results are recorded and the approved plan set is staged at the jobsite permit station.
  5. Schedule the correct inspection and record confirmation. Use the request method, cutoff time, and confirmation procedure recorded in the Chapter 1 binder.
  6. Attend with the permit, approved plans, calculations, equipment documents, and trade representative. Bring a focused question list for any item that could not be fully verified in advance.
  7. Record approval, partial approval, corrections, testing status, and work that must remain open. Use the official inspection result as the controlling record rather than a verbal comment alone.
  8. Do not conceal ducts, piping, dampers, firestopping, or equipment connections before required approval. Hold all concealment work until the recorded approval authorizes it.
  9. File checklist, photographs, tests, inspection result, and correction status. Archive the completed readiness package alongside the permit record for use in the Chapter 20 capstone.

Common HVAC Rough-In Inspection

Inspect the finished readiness package using the same checks regardless of which verification zone is being reviewed.

  • Document check: confirm every verified item references an approved plan detail, load calculation, or manufacturer instruction.
  • Clearance check: confirm every measured clearance matches the approved value and manufacturer requirement.
  • Concealment check: confirm no verification zone was insulated, enclosed, or connected before the recorded approval.
  • Testing check: confirm every required test was performed by the qualified party and documented with method, result, and date.
  • Deficiency check: confirm every logged deficiency has a recorded correction and reverification.
  • Result check: confirm the official inspection result, not a verbal comment, is the controlling record filed in the compliance archive.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Installed Equipment Does Not Match the Approved Equipment Schedule

Probable cause: a substitute unit was installed without a processed revision, or the schedule reference was not rechecked before installation.
Confirmation: compare the nameplate data on the installed unit against the approved equipment schedule and load calculations.
Correction: process the required revision through the design professional and permit authority before requesting inspection, or replace the unit with the approved equipment.
Reinspection: repeat the equipment-verification check against the revised or corrected schedule.

Ducts Discharge Into an Unapproved Space

Probable cause: a duct run was terminated into an attic, crawlspace, or garage for convenience instead of the approved exterior or interior termination.
Confirmation: trace the affected duct from equipment to termination and compare it with the approved plan routing.
Correction: reroute the duct to the approved termination or obtain an approved revision before scheduling inspection.
Reinspection: repeat the duct-verification check on the corrected run.

Combustion-Air Opening Does Not Match the Equipment Listing

Probable cause: the opening was sized or located from a general assumption rather than the specific equipment's listed requirement.
Confirmation: compare the installed opening's size and location with the manufacturer instructions and approved plan detail.
Correction: resize or relocate the opening to the listed requirement, or obtain an approved alternative from the design professional.
Reinspection: repeat the combustion-air verification check on the corrected opening.

A Required Duct-Leakage or Gas-Pressure Test Was Never Performed

Probable cause: the test was overlooked during scheduling, or the qualified testing party was not coordinated before the inspection request.
Confirmation: review the readiness folder for a completed test form with method, result, date, and responsible party.
Correction: schedule and complete the test through the qualified party before requesting or reattending the inspection.
Reinspection: repeat the testing-completeness check on the completed test record.

Insulation or Drywall Concealed Ducts Before Approval

Probable cause: another trade proceeded with concealment work before the HVAC rough-in approval was recorded.
Confirmation: compare the concealment date against the recorded inspection-approval date in the compliance record.
Correction: notify the authority having jurisdiction, expose the concealed work if required, and obtain the required approval before reconcealing.
Reinspection: repeat the concealment-control check after the required approval is documented.

A Deficiency Item Was Corrected but Never Reverified

Probable cause: the correction was completed by the responsible trade, but the readiness team did not close the item on the deficiency log before scheduling.
Confirmation: review the deficiency log for items marked corrected without a reverification date or method.
Correction: reverify the corrected condition against the approved documents and record the verification date and method.
Reinspection: repeat the deficiency-check on the closed log before scheduling.

Field Terms

Rough-In Stage
The point at which HVAC equipment, ducts, and connections are installed but not yet concealed by insulation, drywall, or finishes.
Combustion Air
The air supply required by a fuel-burning appliance for safe combustion, sized and located per the equipment listing and approved documents.
Service Access
The clearance required to reach, remove, and service equipment components, defined by manufacturer instructions and approved plans.
Duct-Leakage Test
A required test verifying the duct system's air-tightness against the energy-code or approved target value, performed by a qualified testing party.
Condensate System
The pans, traps, drains, and overflow protection that manage moisture removed from conditioned air by cooling equipment.
Deficiency Item
A verified condition that does not match the approved documents or governing requirement and must be corrected before inspection.
Qualified Testing Party
The licensed mechanical contractor, technician, or testing agency authorized to perform required duct, gas, or condensate tests.
Concealment Hold Point
The stage at which regulated work must remain visible until the required inspection approval is documented.
Energy Coordination
The alignment of duct sealing, insulation, and equipment location with the project's approved energy-compliance path.
Readiness Package
The documented, photographed record confirming a rough-in stage is prepared for its required inspection.

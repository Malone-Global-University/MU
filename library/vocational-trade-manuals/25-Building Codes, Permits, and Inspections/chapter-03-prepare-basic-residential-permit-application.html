Job Overview

This project qualifies you to gather project information, identify property data, prepare a scope description, attach required drawings, list contractor or owner-builder information, and assemble a complete basic residential permit application packet. The finished work product is one internally coordinated packet — application form, project-data sheet, scope description, drawings and attachments, contractor or owner-builder information, and required signatures — that has passed a line-by-line check against the jurisdiction's official submittal checklist and is ready for submission.

You will confirm the permit decision and current submittal requirements, complete the property and applicant data, write a complete scope description, prepare or obtain the required drawings and supporting documents, cross-check every document against every other document, complete required signatures and acknowledgments, verify fees, and submit the packet through the authorized channel with a recorded confirmation.

This project depends directly on Project 01's authority register and Project 02's permit decision. It supplies the coordinated application, drawings, and data that Project 04's code-note review, every later inspection-readiness project, and the Chapter 20 capstone will reference as the controlling submitted record.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-013 — Gather project information required to complete a residential permit application.
  • CP-014 — Identify and verify property data against official records before completing the application.
  • CP-015 — Prepare a complete, non-minimizing scope description covering all regulated work.
  • CP-016 — Attach required drawings, calculations, and supporting documents identified by the submittal checklist.
  • CP-017 — List contractor, trade-license, or owner-builder information accurately and without fabricating credentials.
  • CP-018 — Assemble, cross-check, and submit a complete permit application packet with recorded confirmation.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 01 built the reference binder that identifies the authority and the current forms, checklists, and fee schedules. Project 02 classified the scope and produced a documented permit decision. This project turns that decision into one coordinated, submittable packet. Every plan-review comment tracked later, every inspection scheduled in Chapters 6 through 16, and the Chapter 20 capstone's application-and-revision history all trace back to the property data, scope description, and drawings assembled and cross-checked here.

The cross-checking discipline taught in this chapter — confirming that address, scope, dimensions, valuation, and revision dates agree across every document in the packet — is the same discipline the capstone uses to keep the permit scope, approved documents, and Book 24 estimate aligned throughout construction.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining the permit decision, current application form, submittal checklist, drawing standards, fee information, and portal requirements
  • confirming property, owner, contractor, and owner-builder data
  • writing a complete scope description covering demolition, structural, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, energy, fire, site, and exterior work
  • preparing or obtaining a site plan, floor plans, elevations, sections, details, schedules, structural drawings, and demolition plans appropriate to the scope
  • identifying deferred submittals and confirming they are permitted
  • cross-checking every document in the packet for consistency
  • completing required acknowledgments, signatures, and fee steps
  • performing a line-by-line check against the official submittal checklist
  • submitting the packet and recording confirmation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • design, size, or engineer any structural, MEP, or energy component; those documents come from the identified design professional, engineer, or manufacturer
  • make the owner-builder legal declaration on the owner's behalf
  • fabricate a contractor license number, insurance certificate, signature, or seal
  • calculate permit fees using anything other than the current official method, or represent an estimated fee as the amount due
  • submit a packet that has not passed the jurisdiction's official submittal checklist

Any document that cannot be traced to an approved source, the identified design professional, or the applicant's own verified information must be corrected or obtained before the packet is submitted.

Trade Note

A permit application is rejected far more often for an internal inconsistency — an address that does not match the site plan, a valuation that does not match the scope description, a missing signature line — than for a technical design flaw. The cross-check steps in this chapter exist because catching those inconsistencies before submission is faster and cheaper than a rejected packet.

Setup Standard

Do not begin filling out the application form until the Chapter 2 permit decision, current submittal checklist, and drawing standards for this specific project are confirmed from the Chapter 1 binder. An application built against an outdated checklist is rework, not progress.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every permit application follows the same underlying pattern: confirm the controlling requirements, gather accurate property and applicant data, describe the actual scope without minimizing it, attach the documents the checklist requires, verify every document against every other document, obtain the required signatures, and submit through the authorized channel with a recorded confirmation. Learning that pattern here, on one basic application, is what makes every later chapter's application, revision, or amended-permit work reliable.

Confirming Inputs Before Drafting

Confirm the Chapter 2 permit decision, the current application form, the submittal checklist, the drawing standards, and the fee information directly from the Chapter 1 binder before drafting any part of the application. A form or checklist that has changed since the binder was last verified must be rechecked at the official source before use.

Documentation Cue — Cross-Check Every Document Before Filing

Compare project address, owner, scope, dimensions, valuation, sheet dates, and revision across every document in the packet before submission. Do not rely on any single document being correct in isolation. An inconsistency found after submission becomes a plan-review correction instead of a five-minute fix.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Fabricate Credentials or Signatures

Do not enter a contractor license number, insurance certificate, engineering seal, or signature that has not been directly confirmed with the responsible party. Do not complete the owner-builder legal declaration on the owner's behalf. A fabricated or unverified credential is a stop-work condition until the actual, confirmed information is obtained.

Field Safety Cue — Confirming Deferred Submittals and Portal Requirements

Confirm with the authority, through the approved channel, whether a specific drawing or calculation may be submitted as a deferred submittal before treating it as deferred in the packet. Record the agency's confirmation, the date, and any conditions attached to the deferral.

Professional Boundary Cue — Design Content Stays With the Design Professional

Structural calculations, truss documents, engineering, and energy compliance reports are obtained from the identified design professional, engineer, or energy professional, not drafted or estimated by the preparer of this packet. Attach these documents as provided; do not summarize, simplify, or complete a missing calculation.

E. Preparation — Confirm Inputs and Property Data

  1. Obtain the permit decision, current application form, submittal checklist, drawing standards, fee information, and portal requirements. Confirm each item directly from the Chapter 1 binder and recheck any item flagged as due for review.
  2. Confirm project address, parcel number, legal description if required, zoning, occupancy, construction type if applicable, and flood or overlay status. Record these on the project-data sheet and verify them against an official property record.
  3. Confirm owner name, contact, authorization, mailing address, and applicant relationship. Obtain written authorization from the owner if the applicant is not the owner.
  4. Confirm contractor business name, license number, registration, insurance, bond, trade licenses, and responsible contact where required. Verify these directly with the licensing authority or contractor rather than accepting an unverified business card or invoice.
  5. If owner-builder is proposed, obtain the official disclosure and verify that the owner — not the preparer — makes the legal declaration. Do not complete or imply this declaration on the owner's behalf under any circumstance.

F. Write the Scope Description and Project-Data Sheet

  1. Define the complete project scope in plain language without minimizing regulated work. State every trade and system affected in ordinary, specific terms.
  2. Identify demolition, structural, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, energy, fire, site, and exterior work in the scope description. List each applicable category explicitly rather than using a single general phrase.
  3. State work location, dimensions, floor area, number of stories, valuation, and intended use using the jurisdiction's required basis. Use the same figures that will appear on the drawings and application form.
  4. Do not omit owner-furnished, deferred, subcontracted, or separately permitted work that affects review. Disclose this work in the scope description even when it will be tracked or permitted separately.
  5. Prepare the project-data sheet matching the plans and application. Confirm every field on the project-data sheet matches the corresponding field on the drawings and the application form before continuing.

G. Obtain Drawings and Supporting Documents

  1. Prepare or obtain the site plan showing property lines, setbacks, easements, structures, access, utilities, drainage, and proposed work as required. Confirm the site plan matches the zoning and property-mapping information in the Chapter 1 binder.
  2. Prepare or obtain floor plans, elevations, sections, details, schedules, structural drawings, and demolition plans appropriate to the scope. Include only the drawing types the classified scope actually requires.
  3. Obtain calculations, engineering, truss information, geotechnical documents, energy compliance, product approvals, and specifications where required. Request these directly from the identified design professional or manufacturer; do not draft or estimate them.
  4. Identify deferred submittals and confirm the jurisdiction permits them. Record the agency's confirmation and any conditions before listing an item as deferred.
  5. Confirm drawing sheet size, scale, title block, signatures, seals, revision dates, file names, bookmarks, and electronic formatting. Match every drawing to the current jurisdictional formatting requirements before filing it in the packet.

H. Cross-Check, Complete Signatures, and Confirm Fees

  1. Create a document index listing every submitted form, drawing, calculation, report, and attachment. Update the index as each document is added or replaced.
  2. Cross-check project address, owner, scope, dimensions, valuation, sheet dates, and revision across all documents. Record and resolve any discrepancy before continuing.
  3. Cross-check the site plan with zoning and property information. Confirm setbacks, lot lines, and easements shown on the site plan agree with the Chapter 1 zoning and property-mapping records.
  4. Cross-check architectural, structural, MEP, energy, and specification documents for obvious conflicts. Flag any conflict for the responsible design professional rather than resolving it independently.
  5. Confirm plan sheets identify applicable code editions and project data only as authorized by the design professional or jurisdiction. Do not add or alter a code-edition reference on a drawing without that authorization.
  6. Complete contractor, design-professional, and trade sections without fabricating credentials or signatures. Leave a field blank and flagged rather than filling it with unverified information.
  7. Complete utility, septic, well, flood, historic, HOA, or other prerequisite approvals before submission where required. Attach evidence of each completed prerequisite approval.
  8. Calculate fees only using the current official method or leave them for agency calculation when required. Do not present an estimated fee as the confirmed amount due.
  9. Identify required deposits, plan-review fees, technology fees, impact fees, and separate-agency charges. Record each fee category and its current source.
  10. Complete required acknowledgments, affidavits, owner authorizations, and disclosures. Match each acknowledgment to the party legally required to sign it.
  11. Obtain authorized signatures, notarization, and seals in the required form. Confirm each signature was executed by the actual authorized party.

I. Final Check and Submission

  1. Perform a line-by-line check against the official submittal checklist. Mark each checklist item as satisfied, not applicable, or outstanding.
  2. Correct blank required fields, conflicting data, missing attachments, unreadable files, and obsolete forms. Resolve every outstanding checklist item before proceeding.
  3. Create the electronic or physical packet in the required submission order. Match the packet order to the document index and the jurisdiction's required sequence.
  4. Save a pre-submission archive with checksum or controlled version identification where practical. Preserve this archive as the exact version submitted.
  5. Submit through the authorized portal, counter, mail, or other required channel. Use only the channel confirmed in the Chapter 1 binder.
  6. Record submission date, confirmation number, fees paid, reviewer assignment if available, and expected next step. File this record with the compliance record.
  7. Create a plan-review response folder and tracking log. Prepare this folder to receive the agency's review comments before they arrive.
  8. File the submitted packet and proof of submission in the compliance record. Store the packet where it remains available for Chapter 4's code-note review.

Common Packet Inspection

Inspect the finished packet using the same checks regardless of which document is being reviewed.

  • Consistency check: confirm address, owner, scope, dimensions, valuation, and dates agree across every document.
  • Completeness check: confirm every item on the official submittal checklist is satisfied, marked not applicable, or identified as an approved deferred submittal.
  • Credential check: confirm every license, insurance reference, signature, and seal was verified directly with the responsible party.
  • Scope check: confirm the scope description matches the Chapter 2 permit decision with no minimized or omitted regulated work.
  • Fee check: confirm fees were calculated using the current official method or left for agency calculation.
  • Submission-record check: confirm the submission date, confirmation number, fees paid, and next step are recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Scope Description Does Not Match the Chapter 2 Permit Decision

Probable cause: the scope description was written from the owner's informal summary instead of the documented permit decision.
Confirmation: compare the scope description line by line against the Chapter 2 permit-decision matrix.
Correction: rewrite the scope description to include every regulated item identified in the permit decision.
Reinspection: repeat the scope check on the corrected description.

Dimensions or Valuation Disagree Across Documents

Probable cause: the site plan, floor plan, and application form were completed at different times without a final cross-check.
Confirmation: run the cross-check worksheet against all documents and identify the exact figures that disagree.
Correction: confirm the correct figure with the design professional or owner and update every affected document.
Reinspection: repeat the consistency check across the corrected documents.

A Contractor License or Insurance Reference Cannot Be Verified

Probable cause: the number was copied from a business card, invoice, or prior project without direct confirmation.
Confirmation: check the license and insurance status directly with the licensing authority or insurer.
Correction: obtain current, verified documentation from the contractor before completing this field.
Reinspection: repeat the credential check on the corrected entry.

A Required Checklist Item Is Missing at Final Review

Probable cause: a document was assumed to be attached but was never actually collected, or the checklist itself was not rechecked before final review.
Confirmation: repeat the line-by-line check against the current official submittal checklist.
Correction: obtain and file the missing document, or confirm and record its approved deferred-submittal status.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check on the full packet.

An Owner-Builder Disclosure Was Completed by the Preparer

Probable cause: the preparer filled in the owner-builder declaration to save time instead of having the owner make the legal declaration.
Confirmation: review the disclosure for the owner's own signature and date, not the preparer's.
Correction: void the improperly completed disclosure and have the owner personally complete and sign it.
Reinspection: repeat the credential check on the corrected disclosure.

The Submission Confirmation Was Never Recorded

Probable cause: the packet was submitted but the confirmation number, date, or fees paid were not logged before moving on to other work.
Confirmation: check the compliance record for a recorded submission date, confirmation number, and fee amount.
Correction: contact the authority through the approved channel to retrieve the confirmation record and file it.
Reinspection: repeat the submission-record check on the completed file.

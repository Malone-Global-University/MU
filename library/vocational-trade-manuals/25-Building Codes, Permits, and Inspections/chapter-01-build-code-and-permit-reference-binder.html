Job Overview

This project qualifies you to identify governing authorities, locate the currently adopted codes and local amendments, and organize a verified, jurisdiction-specific reference binder before any permit decision is made. The finished work product is one complete, indexed binder covering authorities, adopted codes, zoning, licensing, permits, submittals, inspections, energy, life safety, fees, utilities, revisions, corrections, and closeout for one defined residential property.

You will define the property and its intended users, build an authority register, locate and record the adopted codes and local amendments, locate zoning and parcel resources, locate licensing and permit resources, locate fee and inspection procedures, organize the binder into labeled sections, verify one unresolved item directly with the responsible authority, and test the binder against a sample project before archiving the first controlled version.

This is the first project in the book. Every later project in this book — the permit decision in Project 02, the application packet in Project 03, every inspection-readiness project, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on the authority register and verified sources built here. Nothing in this book replaces the building official, plans examiner, inspector, or design professional as the final authority.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-001 — Identify governing authorities having jurisdiction over a defined residential property.
  • CP-002 — Locate applicable residential codes and record the adopted edition, local amendments, and official source.
  • CP-003 — Organize permit resources, forms, fee schedules, and submittal requirements by section.
  • CP-004 — Collect inspection contacts, request procedures, and result-access procedures.
  • CP-005 — Document local requirements with source, edition, date verified, and contact of record.
  • CP-006 — Build a code-reference binder that is tested, corrected, and archived under revision control.

Where This Skill Fits

Every project in this book begins with the same question: which authority controls this requirement, and where is the current official version of it? Project 02 cannot classify a scope item as permit-required or exempt without this binder's authority register and permit-exemption sources. Project 03 cannot assemble an application packet without this binder's forms, checklists, and fee information. Every rough-in and final-inspection project depends on the inspection-request procedures recorded here.

The verification discipline in this chapter — recording source, edition, amendment date, effective date, section, and date verified for every requirement — is the same discipline used throughout the rest of the book, including the Chapter 20 capstone's authority-to-closeout compliance archive.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the property, parcel, and intended binder users
  • identifying the primary building department and every separate authority that may apply
  • locating the currently adopted codes, local amendments, and administrative rules from official sources
  • locating zoning, parcel, licensing, permit, submittal, fee, and inspection resources
  • organizing the binder into labeled, indexed sections with source dating and secondary-guidance labeling
  • verifying one unresolved item directly with the responsible authority
  • testing the binder against a sample project and archiving the first controlled version

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • determine whether a specific project requires a permit; that decision is Project 02's scope
  • interpret, apply, or waive a code requirement, or treat this binder as a legal opinion, permit approval, or inspection result
  • rely on memory, an internet summary, an old permit packet, another jurisdiction's rule, or a model code edition that has not been confirmed as locally adopted
  • represent any secondary summary, training material, or industry reference in this binder as an official source

Any resource in the binder that cannot be traced to an authoritative source must be labeled unofficial or removed before the binder is archived.

Trade Note

Every entry in this binder is dated at the point it is verified — never assumed current from a prior project. A code edition, fee, or form carried forward from an old binder without rechecking the official source is the single most common source of the outdated-resource failures diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not record a code edition, fee, or permit rule in the binder until its official source, section, and date verified are confirmed. An entry copied from an old binder, a contractor's notes, or a general internet summary must be rechecked at an official source before it is placed in the binder.

Shop Tip

Create the source-verification page before you fill the binder's technical sections. Deciding how you will recheck each resource after the binder is already full of unlabeled entries is how outdated code editions end up guiding a real permit decision.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every compliance task in this book, from this chapter's authority register to the Chapter 20 capstone's authority-to-closeout archive, follows the same five-part sequence: identify the authority, locate the official source, record it with a verified date, organize it for use, and recheck it before it controls a real decision. Learning that sequence here, on one property's reference resources, means every later project starts from a method you already trust.

Official Sources, Secondary Guidance, and Verification Dates

Every entry placed in the binder is either an official source or clearly labeled secondary guidance. Record the source, title, edition, amendment date, effective date, section, webpage or office, contact, and date verified for every governing requirement placed in the binder. An entry without a recorded date verified cannot be treated as current.

Documentation Cue — Record Source and Date Verified Every Time

Do not carry a code edition, fee, or form forward from memory or a prior project's binder. Confirm it directly at the official source and record the date verified before filing it. Carrying an unverified entry forward allows outdated requirements to compound across future projects in this book.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Treat the Binder as an Approval

This binder is an educational reference, not a permit approval, inspection report, code-compliance certification, or legal opinion. Do not represent any checklist, summary, or organized resource in this binder as authorization to build, permit, or occupy. Stop and direct the learner to the authority having jurisdiction whenever a requirement cannot be confirmed from an official source.

Field Safety Cue — Contacting Agencies

When contacting an agency counter or staff member, ask a specific, focused question tied to the property and requirement. Record the agency, contact name or identifier where offered, date, question, and response exactly as given. Do not argue with, mislead, or pressure agency staff, and do not represent a verbal comment as a final determination without written confirmation where the agency requires it.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Zoning variances, code interpretations, structural sizing, and formal permit determinations are made by the zoning official, building official, plans examiner, or design professional, not by this binder or its preparer. Where the binder identifies an unresolved or ambiguous requirement, label it unresolved and record the official source that must answer it rather than selecting the least costly interpretation.

E. Preparation — Define Property, Authorities, and Users

  1. Define the property address, parcel, municipality, county, state, project types, and intended users of the binder. Record this information on the Property and Binder Record above before locating any resource.
  2. Identify the primary building department and confirm its jurisdiction over the property. Confirm the department's name and jurisdiction directly from an official source rather than assumption.
  3. Identify separate planning, zoning, fire, public-works, health, environmental, floodplain, utility, well, septic, historic, and transportation authorities that may apply. Record each authority that has any bearing on the defined property, even if its role will not be used immediately.
  4. Identify homeowners-association or other private review bodies separately from government authorities. Keep private review requirements in a clearly labeled section distinct from government permits.
  5. Create an authority register with agency name, responsibility, address, website, telephone, email, counter hours, submission portal, and date verified. Complete one register row for every authority identified in Steps 2 through 4.

F. Locate Adopted Codes, Amendments, and Zoning Resources

  1. Locate the authoritative list of adopted building, residential, existing-building, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, fuel-gas, fire, energy, accessibility, and other applicable codes. Use the official agency's published adoption list, not a model-code publisher's default edition.
  2. Record the adopted edition, local amendments, effective date, and official source for each code. File each entry in the Adopted Codes section with its supporting source link or saved document.
  3. Locate administrative ordinances, permit rules, enforcement policies, bulletins, interpretations, and standard details. File these in the Local Amendments section, separated from the adopted code text itself.
  4. Locate zoning maps, zoning code, use tables, setback tables, height limits, lot-coverage rules, accessory-structure rules, and overlay requirements. Confirm these resources are matched to the defined parcel's zoning district.
  5. Locate parcel, addressing, property-record, flood, wildfire, seismic, snow, wind, coastal, historic, and environmental mapping resources where applicable. File these in the Property Mapping section with the date each map layer was checked.

G. Locate Licensing, Permit, Submittal, Fee, and Inspection Resources

  1. Locate contractor-license, trade-license, registration, owner-builder, insurance, and bond requirements. File the current requirement text in the Licensing section.
  2. Locate permit applications for building, zoning, demolition, grading, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, gas, fire, driveway, right-of-way, utility, well, septic, and specialty work. Save the current published form for each applicable permit type.
  3. Locate plan-submittal checklists, drawing standards, calculation requirements, energy forms, product-approval requirements, and electronic-file rules. File these in the Submittals section.
  4. Locate current permit fee schedules, valuation methods, deposits, reinspection fees, extension fees, and payment methods. File these in the Fees section with the effective date of the schedule.
  5. Locate plan-review turnaround information, expedited-review policies, correction-response procedures, and revision-submittal rules. File these in the Review Procedures section.
  6. Locate inspection types, sequence, request methods, cutoff times, cancellation policies, contact requirements, and result-access procedures. File these in the Inspections section.
  7. Locate final approval, certificate of occupancy, certificate of completion, utility-release, and closeout requirements. File these in the Closeout section.
  8. Collect contact information for plans examiners, inspectors, zoning staff, utilities, and other official resources without relying on personal informal contacts. Use published agency directories rather than a single individual's private contact information.

H. Assemble, Label, and Verify the Binder

  1. Create binder sections for authorities, adopted codes, local amendments, zoning, licensing, permits, submittals, inspections, energy, life safety, standard details, fees, utilities, revisions, corrections, and closeout. Match the section order to the Component Schedule above.
  2. Save authoritative source documents or stable official links with access dates. Record the date accessed alongside every saved document or link.
  3. Label unofficial summaries, training materials, and industry references as secondary guidance. Do not place any unlabeled secondary resource next to an official source where it could be mistaken for one.
  4. Remove outdated forms and mark superseded code editions as archive-only. Do not leave a superseded form or edition in active use without a clear archive label.
  5. Create a source-verification page requiring recheck before each new project. State the recheck interval and the person responsible for it.
  6. Create a local-questions log for requirements that remain unclear. Record each unresolved requirement identified while assembling the binder.
  7. Contact the responsible authority for one unresolved item and record the response and verification date. Use the Field Safety Cue above when making this contact.

I. Test, Correct, and Archive

  1. Test the binder by locating the permit application, adopted code edition, inspection request procedure, and final-approval requirement for one sample project. Time the search and note any section that could not be located within a reasonable effort.
  2. Correct missing, duplicate, conflicting, inaccessible, or outdated resources. Replace or relabel every item identified as a failure during the test in Step 1.
  3. Assign responsibility for periodic binder review and update. Record the responsible reviewer and the review interval on the source-verification page.
  4. Archive the first controlled binder version with a revision date and change log. Record the archive date, version number, and a summary of what was verified at that date.

Common Binder Inspection

Inspect the finished binder using the same checks regardless of which section is being reviewed.

  • Sourcing check: confirm every technical entry has a recorded source, edition or effective date, section, and date verified.
  • Labeling check: confirm every secondary or unofficial resource is clearly labeled and cannot be mistaken for an official source.
  • Completeness check: confirm all fifteen binder sections from the Component Schedule are present and populated.
  • Verification-test check: confirm the sample-project test in Section I locates every required resource within a reasonable search.
  • Unresolved-item check: confirm at least one unresolved item was contacted, answered, and recorded with agency, contact, date, question, and response.
  • Revision-control check: confirm the archived version carries a revision date, change log, and assigned reviewer.

Complete the Source Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 02.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Recorded Code Edition Does Not Match the Agency's Current Adoption

Probable cause: the edition was copied from a model-code publisher's default page, a prior project's binder, or an informal summary instead of the agency's own adoption list.
Confirmation: recheck the agency's official adoption page or contact the building department directly and compare the stated edition and amendment date to the binder entry.
Correction: replace the entry with the confirmed edition, amendment date, and official source, and mark the superseded entry as archive-only.
Reinspection: repeat the sourcing check on the corrected entry.

An Unofficial Summary Is Being Treated as a Controlling Requirement

Probable cause: a training reference, industry article, or informal checklist was filed next to official sources without a secondary-guidance label.
Confirmation: review each entry in the section and confirm whether it links to the agency's own publication or to a third-party summary.
Correction: label the resource as secondary guidance, relocate it away from official entries if needed, and locate the corresponding official source if one exists.
Reinspection: repeat the labeling check for the affected section.

A Binder Section Is Missing or Incomplete

Probable cause: a section from the Component Schedule was skipped, combined with another section, or left partially populated during assembly.
Confirmation: compare the finished binder's section list against the fifteen sections required in the Component Schedule.
Correction: create or complete the missing section using the locating steps in Sections F and G.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check on the full binder.

The Sample-Project Test Fails to Locate a Required Resource

Probable cause: a resource is filed in the wrong section, is missing a stable link, or was never actually collected during Sections F and G.
Confirmation: repeat the sample-project search in Section I, Step 1, and note the exact resource and section that could not be found.
Correction: relocate, replace, or newly collect the missing resource and refile it in the correct section.
Reinspection: repeat the verification-test check with the same sample project.

An Unresolved Item Was Never Actually Answered

Probable cause: a question was logged on the local-questions log but the contact was never completed, or a verbal answer was recorded without a name, date, or agency.
Confirmation: review the local-questions log for entries missing a recorded response, contact, or date.
Correction: complete the contact through the agency's approved channel and record the full response with contact, date, and any stated limitations.
Reinspection: repeat the unresolved-item check on the completed log.

No Revision Date or Change Log Was Recorded at Archiving

Probable cause: the binder was assembled and used without formally closing out the first controlled version.
Confirmation: check the archive record for a version number, date, and change-log summary.
Correction: assign a version number, record the archive date, and summarize what was verified as of that date.
Reinspection: repeat the revision-control check on the archived binder.

Cleanup

This project ends with the first controlled binder version archived and ready for reuse, not left as a working draft.

  • Remove duplicate, superseded, or unlabeled resources from active sections
  • Confirm confidential owner, financial, or access information is stored securely and separately where required

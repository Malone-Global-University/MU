Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and apply one field-usable life-safety checklist for a single residential project. The finished work product is one checklist, keyed to approved documents and the adopted requirements recorded in the Project 01 binder, covering smoke alarms, carbon-monoxide alarms, sleeping-room emergency escape and rescue openings, egress doors and paths, fire separation between the dwelling and garage or other protected areas, penetration protection, fire blocking and draft stopping, address numbers, and final operational verification.

You will build a floor-by-floor life-safety plan from the approved documents, identify every required alarm and opening from those documents and the governing requirements, record each item's controlling dimensions and conditions, photograph concealed fire-separation and penetration details before they are covered, convert unclear or conflicting notes into formal clarification questions, and complete a final room-by-room test and operation check before the items are closed on the checklist.

This project depends on the Project 01 authority register and adopted-code sources, the Project 04 code-note summary and its life-safety notes, and the Project 07 framing inspection's fire-blocking and structural-connector verification. It feeds directly into Project 14's stair, guard, and handrail checklist, Project 16's final building inspection, and the Chapter 20 capstone's life-safety checkpoints.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CP-073 — Identify smoke alarm locations from approved documents and governing requirements.
  • CP-074 — Identify carbon-monoxide alarm requirements and project triggers from approved requirements.
  • CP-075 — Verify bedroom egress against the controlling jurisdictional method and approved documents.
  • CP-076 — Inspect emergency escape and rescue openings for tag, dimension basis, sill, operation, and discharge conditions.
  • CP-077 — Review fire-separation locations between the dwelling, garage, units, and other protected areas.
  • CP-078 — Prepare a life-safety checklist covering alarms, egress, fire separation, and final verification.

Where This Skill Fits

Alarms, emergency escape openings, and fire separation are life-safety items that must be verified against approved documents and adopted requirements, not decided in the field from convenience or habit. This project sits after the framing and rough-in inspections in Projects 6 through 11, because alarm boxes, opening rough dimensions, and fire-separation framing are set during those stages and must be confirmed before insulation, drywall, and trim conceal them.

The checklist you build here becomes one of the required inputs to the Project 16 final building inspection and to the Chapter 20 capstone's life-safety checkpoints. A life-safety item that was never verified at rough-in is far harder and more expensive to correct once finishes are installed.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • building a floor-by-floor life-safety plan from approved documents
  • identifying smoke alarm and carbon-monoxide alarm locations and requirements
  • identifying sleeping-room emergency escape and rescue openings and their controlling dimensions
  • identifying egress doors, landings, halls, stairs, and the exterior path to a safe location
  • identifying fire separation between the dwelling and garage, units, and other protected areas
  • identifying required firestopping, fire blocking, draft stopping, and penetration protection
  • performing a room-by-room final test and operation check and recording deficiencies, corrections, and approvals

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • relocate, add, or omit an alarm, opening, or fire-separation assembly based on convenience without an approved clarification or revision
  • certify egress or emergency-opening dimensions using any method other than the controlling jurisdictional method and actual approved product data
  • select, design, or field-modify a firestop, penetration treatment, or rated assembly outside the design or listed system identified in the approved documents
  • represent this checklist as an inspection approval, code-compliance certification, or authorization to conceal work

Any life-safety item that cannot be confirmed from the approved documents or the adopted requirements must be recorded as an open clarification question, not resolved by assumption.

Trade Note

Alarm placement rules, emergency escape opening dimensions, and fire-separation requirements are among the items most likely to differ between new construction and existing-building alteration, and between jurisdictions applying different local amendments. Confirm the applicable existing-building exceptions, if any, directly with the authority before relying on them.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the checklist until the current approved plan revision, the Project 01 authority register, and the Project 04 code-note summary are confirmed to match. An alarm location, opening dimension, or fire-separation detail taken from a superseded plan sheet is not a valid checklist entry.

Shop Tip

Build the floor-by-floor life-safety plan before filling in individual checklist rows. Sketching every sleeping room, exit, alarm, and fire-separation boundary on one plan first makes it far easier to spot a missing alarm or an unaccounted-for opening than working row by row through a blank table.

Common Field Fundamentals

A life-safety checklist is only as reliable as the documents and requirements it is built from. Every item on this checklist must trace back to an approved plan sheet, an adopted code requirement recorded in the Project 01 binder, or a written clarification from the authority having jurisdiction — never to habit, a prior project's layout, or an assumption about what "usually" applies.

AlarmsSmoke alarm and carbon-monoxide alarm locations, power, and interconnection
Egress OpeningsSleeping-room emergency escape and rescue opening dimensions and operation
Egress PathsDoors, landings, halls, and stairs to a safe exterior location
Fire SeparationGarage separation, unit separation, penetrations, and rated assemblies

Approved Documents Control Every Entry

Record the plan sheet, detail, or approved-response reference for every alarm, opening, and fire-separation item placed on the checklist. Do not enter a requirement from memory or from a different project's checklist.

Documentation Cue — Photograph Before Concealment

Photograph fire blocking, rated assemblies, penetrations, and alarm rough-ins by location before insulation, drywall, or trim covers them. An unphotographed concealed condition cannot be re-verified after concealment without opening the assembly.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Certify Egress Dimensions From Assumption

Do not certify an emergency escape and rescue opening, egress door, or egress path dimension without using the controlling jurisdictional method and the actual approved product data for the installed unit. Stop and request clarification from the design professional or authority if the approved documents and field conditions do not clearly agree.

Field Safety Cue — Testing Alarms and Operating Openings

Test alarms only through the manufacturer's and authority's approved procedure. Verify that emergency escape openings, egress doors, and locks operate without prohibited keys, tools, or special knowledge, but do not force, pry, or damage an opening or hardware while checking its operation.

Professional Boundary Cue — Reserved Determinations

Alarm relocation, existing-building egress exceptions, and fire-separation assembly substitutions are determined by the design professional or the building and fire officials, not by this checklist or its preparer. Record any such request as a formal clarification question rather than deciding it in the field.

E. Preparation — Confirm Requirements and Build the Life-Safety Plan

  1. Identify the adopted residential, building, fire, existing-building, and alarm requirements and local amendments applicable to the project. Confirm these directly against the Project 01 authority register rather than from memory.
  2. Obtain approved floor plans, life-safety notes, electrical plans, fire details, door schedules, and revisions. Confirm the plan set matches the currently controlling permit revision.
  3. Identify occupancy, sleeping rooms, stories, basements, attached garages, fuel-burning equipment, and alteration areas affecting life safety. Record these on the Project and Plan Record above.
  4. Create a floor-by-floor life-safety plan with room names, exits, sleeping rooms, alarms, stairs, and fire separations. Sketch every room and boundary before filling in individual checklist rows.
  5. Identify smoke alarm locations from approved plans and governing requirements. Mark each required location on the life-safety plan and record its checklist row.

F. Identify Alarm Requirements

  1. Identify carbon-monoxide alarm locations and project triggers from approved requirements. Record the specific project condition that triggers each required location.
  2. Identify power, battery backup, interconnection, monitoring, accessibility, and replacement-product requirements. Record these against the manufacturer instructions for the installed product.
  3. Do not relocate or omit alarms based on convenience without approved clarification. Record any requested relocation as a formal clarification question before proceeding.
  4. Identify existing alarms that must be upgraded because of the project scope. Compare existing alarm locations and types against the current project's triggered requirements.

G. Identify Egress Openings and Paths

  1. Identify sleeping-room emergency escape and rescue openings and applicable existing-building exceptions if formally approved. Confirm any claimed exception in writing before relying on it.
  2. Record opening tag, clear opening basis, sill, operation, security device, well, ladder, and exterior discharge conditions from approved documents. Use the actual installed product's rated clear-opening dimensions, not a nominal size.
  3. Do not certify egress dimensions without using the controlling jurisdictional method and actual approved product data. Stop and request clarification where the method or product data is unclear.
  4. Identify required egress door, landings, halls, stairs, and exterior path to a safe location. Trace each path continuously on the life-safety plan.
  5. Identify door swing, locks, latches, security bars, access control, and emergency operation concerns. Record any hardware that could obstruct emergency operation.

H. Identify Fire Separation and Penetration Protection

  1. Identify fire separation between dwelling and garage, units, property lines, utility rooms, shafts, chases, soffits, and other protected areas. Mark each boundary on the life-safety plan.
  2. Identify required wall, ceiling, floor, membrane, penetration, joint, door, duct, damper, and opening protection. Record the approved assembly reference for each.
  3. Use listed or approved assemblies and systems rather than field-created layer assumptions. Do not substitute an unlisted material or method.
  4. Identify fire blocking, draft stopping, concealed-space protection, and inspection stages. Record the required inspection stage for each location.
  5. Identify penetrations by plumbing, electrical, HVAC, gas, communications, structure, cabinets, and accessories. Record each penetration's location and trade.
  6. Assign approved firestop or penetration treatment only from the design or listed system. Do not select a generic sealant or filler without a matching listed system reference.
  7. Identify garage doors, self-closing provisions where required, thresholds, glazing, ducts, and penetrations. Record each condition against the approved detail.

I. Coordinate, Photograph, and Test

  1. Identify guards, handrails, lighting, address numbers, firefighter access, hydrant, or fire-department conditions applicable to the project. Record each against the adopted addressing and access requirement.
  2. Identify sprinkler, alarm, fire-flow, water-supply, or fire-department review when required. Confirm whether a separate fire-department submittal or review applies.
  3. Coordinate alarm circuit, mechanical fuel equipment, fire assemblies, insulation, drywall, doors, and finishes. Confirm no scheduled trade will conceal a life-safety item before its required inspection.
  4. Create inspection checkpoints before framing concealment, insulation, drywall, device trim, and final. Assign each checkpoint to the corresponding stage on the project schedule.
  5. Photograph fire blocking, rated assemblies, penetrations, and alarm rough-ins before concealment. Label each photograph with location, date, and checklist row reference.
  6. Create a room-by-room final test and operation checklist. List every room and life-safety item requiring a final check.
  7. Test alarms only through manufacturer and authority-approved procedures. Record the test method and result for each alarm.
  8. Verify exits and emergency openings operate without prohibited keys, tools, special knowledge, or obstruction as required. Record the operation check result for each door and opening.
  9. Verify required labels, instructions, certificates, and test records. Confirm these are present and legible for each installed product.
  10. Record deficiencies, responsible trade, correction, reinspection, and approval. Assign every failed item to a responsible party with a target date.
  11. Archive the completed checklist, product data, listed systems, photographs, tests, and inspection records. Store the archived checklist where it remains available to Project 14, Project 16, and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Common Checklist Inspection

Inspect the finished checklist using the same checks regardless of which section is being reviewed.

  • Sourcing check: confirm every alarm, opening, and fire-separation entry references a specific approved plan sheet, detail, or written authority response.
  • Alarm completeness check: confirm every triggered smoke and carbon-monoxide alarm location is recorded with power, backup, and interconnection data.
  • Egress verification check: confirm every emergency escape opening and egress path entry uses the controlling jurisdictional method and actual product data.
  • Fire-separation check: confirm every rated assembly and penetration entry matches an approved or listed system.
  • Photographic check: confirm every concealed location has a dated, labeled photograph.
  • Final-test check: confirm every room and item has a recorded test result, with deficiencies corrected and reverified before closeout.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Emergency Escape Opening Fails the Field Measurement

Probable cause: the installed product's actual rated clear-opening dimensions differ from the approved plan's nominal size, or the sill height was measured from the wrong finished-floor reference.
Confirmation: compare the manufacturer's rated clear-opening data for the installed unit against the approved documents and the controlling jurisdictional measurement method.
Correction: obtain a design-professional or authority clarification, and replace or adjust the opening only through an approved revision.
Reinspection: repeat the egress-verification check on the corrected opening.

A Required Smoke or Carbon-Monoxide Alarm Location Is Missing

Probable cause: the alarm location shown on the approved electrical plan was omitted, relocated, or not carried through to the rough-in.
Confirmation: compare the floor-by-floor life-safety plan against the actual rough-in locations observed in the field.
Correction: install the missing alarm at the approved location or obtain an approved revision if relocation is warranted.
Reinspection: repeat the alarm-completeness check for the affected room or story.

A Fire-Separation Assembly Does Not Match an Approved or Listed System

Probable cause: a field substitution, generic material, or unlisted method was used instead of the design or listed system shown in the approved documents.
Confirmation: compare the installed assembly against the approved detail or listed-system documentation.
Correction: replace or complete the assembly using the approved or listed system, or obtain a formal revision before proceeding.
Reinspection: repeat the fire-separation check on the corrected assembly.

A Concealed Life-Safety Detail Was Never Photographed

Probable cause: insulation, drywall, or finishes were installed before the fire-blocking, rated-assembly, or alarm rough-in photograph was taken.
Confirmation: review the photographic record against the checklist rows requiring a concealed-location photograph.
Correction: where the assembly remains accessible, photograph it immediately; where it has been concealed, coordinate with the responsible trade and the authority on the required verification method.
Reinspection: repeat the photographic-completeness check once the record is corrected.

An Egress Path Contains an Unresolved Gap

Probable cause: a door, landing, hall, or stair segment shown on one plan sheet does not connect cleanly to the segment shown on an adjoining sheet, or a locked or obstructed condition was found in the field.
Confirmation: trace the complete path on the life-safety plan and in the field, noting the exact location where the path breaks.
Correction: correct the field condition, obtain a design clarification, or process a revision to resolve the plan discrepancy.
Reinspection: repeat the egress-path-continuity check on the corrected path.

A Final Test Item Was Marked Complete Without a Recorded Result

Probable cause: the room-by-room final test was performed informally without recording the test date, method, and result on the checklist.
Confirmation: review the final test and operation log for rows missing a recorded date, method, or result.
Correction: repeat the test using the approved procedure and record the complete result.
Reinspection: repeat the final-test-completion check on the corrected log.

Field Terms

Emergency Escape and Rescue Opening
A required opening sized and located to allow escape or rescue, verified by clear opening area, width, height, and sill height using the controlling jurisdictional method.
Clear Opening Basis
The manufacturer's rated clear-opening dimensions for the actual installed product.
Egress Path
The continuous, approved route from a room to a public way.
Fire Separation
A required wall, ceiling, floor, membrane, or opening-protection assembly between the dwelling and a protected area.
Fire Blocking / Draft Stopping
Material or assemblies required to limit fire or smoke spread through concealed spaces.
Listed System
A tested and approved firestop, penetration, or assembly system identified in the approved documents.
Existing-Building Exception
A formally approved reduction or alternative to a new-construction requirement, applicable only when confirmed in writing.
Life-Safety Plan
The floor-by-floor plan marking rooms, exits, sleeping rooms, alarms, stairs, and fire separations.
Final Test and Operation Log
The working document recording the date, method, result, deficiency, correction, and reinspection for each life-safety item.
Deficiency Log
The record assigning each failed checklist item to a responsible party with a target correction date.

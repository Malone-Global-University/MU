Job Overview

This project qualifies you to identify and document the visible components of one residential HVAC system without opening energized equipment, refrigerant circuits, or restricted covers. The finished work product is one completed HVAC System Map and Existing-Condition Record: a written and photographed inventory of the heating equipment, cooling equipment, air-distribution paths, ventilation components, condensate system, and controls serving the selected system or zone.

You will define the system or zone included in the project; identify the heating and cooling equipment by type, location, and visible labels; trace visible supply and return duct paths; identify ventilation, exhaust, and condensate components; identify thermostats, disconnects, and breakers without removing restricted covers; mark unresolved components rather than guessing at their function; and prepare a schematic and existing-condition record comparing field conditions with any available drawings.

This is the first project in the book. Every later chapter — filter service, grille cleaning, duct inspection and sealing, insulation, register and return work, flexible duct, balancing, thermostat replacement, cabinet inspection, condensate maintenance, condenser cleaning, ventilation installation, indoor air quality, diagnostics, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on the system map produced here to correctly identify equipment, ducts, and components before any later work begins. Chapters 3 through 19 of this book are performed only on systems and components identified using this exact map.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-001 — Identify heating equipment by type, location, fuel or energy source, manufacturer, model, and visible service labels.
  • HV-002 — Identify cooling equipment by indoor and outdoor components without opening the refrigerant circuit.
  • HV-003 — Identify supply ducts, including plenums, trunks, branches, register boots, registers, and dampers.
  • HV-004 — Identify return ducts, including return grilles, return boots, return trunks, and filter locations.
  • HV-005 — Identify ventilation components, including outdoor-air intakes, bathroom and kitchen exhausts, dryer exhaust, and whole-house ventilation equipment.
  • HV-006 — Create a basic HVAC system map showing equipment, airflow paths, controls, condensate, and unresolved conditions.

Where This Skill Fits

Every project in this book — from filter replacement in Chapter 3, to grille cleaning in Chapter 4, to duct inspection, sealing, and insulation in Chapters 5 through 7, to register, return, and flexible-duct work in Chapters 8 through 10, to balancing, thermostat, cabinet, condensate, and condenser work in Chapters 11 through 15, to ventilation, indoor air quality, and diagnostics in Chapters 16 through 19 — depends on correctly identifying the equipment, ducts, and components mapped here. A duct sealed, a filter replaced, or a breaker opened on a misidentified system is a hazard and a wasted repair.

The documentation habit established in this chapter — recording equipment, labels, photographs, and unresolved conditions — is the same discipline used for the shutdown and readiness record in Chapter 2 and the deficiency and diagnostic records required throughout the book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the exact system or zone included in the project
  • identifying heating equipment, cooling equipment, air handlers, furnaces, fan-coil units, heat pumps, packaged equipment, boilers, radiators, or baseboards present, by type, location, and visible labels
  • identifying visible supply and return duct paths, registers, grilles, filters, and dampers
  • identifying visible ventilation, exhaust, and condensate components
  • identifying thermostats, zone controls, disconnects, service switches, breakers, and equipment labels without removing restricted covers
  • marking components whose function cannot be confirmed as unresolved rather than guessing
  • preparing a schematic and existing-condition record and comparing it with any available drawings

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • open the refrigerant circuit or any sealed refrigerant component
  • remove a restricted electrical, combustion, or equipment cover
  • operate, adjust, or shut down equipment as part of this project
  • diagnose, repair, or correct any observed condition beyond recording it for a later chapter
  • proceed with any later chapter project when a condition identified in this chapter requires licensed inspection and has not been addressed

Any gas odor, scorch mark, damaged flue, suspected refrigerant leakage, electrical damage, or severe corrosion identified in this chapter must be recorded and escalated to a qualified professional; it is not corrected as part of this project.

Trade Note

Every component in this chapter is identified visually, from labels, accessible markings, and observable routing — never by opening a refrigerant circuit, removing a restricted cover, or guessing at a hidden condition. A component whose function cannot be confirmed is marked unresolved on the system map, not assumed and carried forward into later work. Guessing at an unidentified duct, control wire, or disconnect is the most common cause of misapplied work documented in this book's later chapters.

Setup Standard

Do not open a refrigerant circuit, remove a restricted electrical or combustion cover, or operate equipment as part of this project. Identify every component visually and from accessible labels. If a component's function cannot be confirmed without removing a restricted cover or operating equipment, mark it unresolved on the system map and move on.

Restricted Covers, Refrigerant Circuits, and Unresolved Components

Every component identified in this chapter is identified visually, from accessible labels, nameplates, and observable routing. Do not remove a restricted electrical, combustion, or refrigerant-component cover, and do not open, cut, loosen, braze, or otherwise alter a refrigerant circuit at any point in this project. If a component's function cannot be confirmed without doing so, mark it unresolved on the system map rather than guessing.

Measurement Cue — Identify by Label and Visible Routing, Not by Assumption

Confirm equipment type, fuel or energy source, and duct routing from visible labels, nameplates, and observable connections. Do not assume a duct's function from its size or location alone, and do not assume a control wire's function from its color alone. Mark any component that cannot be confirmed as unresolved.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Open Restricted Covers or the Refrigerant Circuit

Do not remove a restricted electrical, combustion, or refrigerant-component cover, and do not open, cut, loosen, braze, solder, charge, recover, evacuate, or otherwise alter a refrigerant circuit at any point in this project. Treat every electrical and combustion component as though it is energized or operating until a later chapter's shutdown procedure establishes otherwise. This project does not authorize operating or shutting down equipment.

Field Safety Cue — Attic, Crawlspace, and Confined-Area Access

Inspect attic, crawlspace, and mechanical-closet access for structural support, adequate lighting, insulation hazards, pests, and confined-space conditions before entering. Do not enter a contaminated cavity, mold-affected area, or suspected asbestos-containing space without the required controls. Treat suspected asbestos-containing duct wrap, transite, vermiculite, lead dust, animal waste, sewage contamination, heavy mold growth, or fire damage as a stop-work condition.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Nameplates and Labels Control Identification

Photograph and record every accessible equipment nameplate, model number, serial number, filter specification label, and electrical rating label. These labels, not assumptions from equipment size or appearance, control correct identification and are required by later chapters for filter selection, electrical work, and manufacturer-specified procedures.

E. Preparation and Prior-Record Review

  1. Obtain available mechanical plans, equipment schedules, service records, owner information, and manufacturer labels. Record what exists and what does not before beginning the walk-through.
  2. Define the system or zone included in the project. State the exact system or zone in writing before proceeding to Step 3.

F. Heating and Cooling Equipment Identification

  1. Identify the primary heating equipment by equipment type, location, fuel or energy source, manufacturer, model, and visible service labels. Photograph the nameplate and surrounding installation.
  2. Identify the cooling equipment by indoor and outdoor components without opening the refrigerant circuit. Do not remove a refrigerant-line cover or access panel that exposes refrigerant components.
  3. Identify air handlers, furnaces, fan-coil units, heat pumps, packaged equipment, boilers, radiators, baseboards, or other system types present. Record every equipment type found, even if it is not the primary system.

G. Supply and Return Duct Identification

  1. Identify visible supply plenums, trunk ducts, branches, register boots, registers, and dampers. Trace each accessible supply path from the equipment plenum toward its termination.
  2. Identify visible return grilles, return boots, return trunks, filter locations, and return paths. Trace each accessible return path from the grille toward the equipment.
  3. Trace accessible duct routes and note changes in material, size, insulation, support, and condition. Record approximate duct sizes using the tape measure.
  4. Distinguish supply air, return air, exhaust air, outdoor air, combustion air, and natural building ventilation. Mark each traced duct or opening with its identified air type.

H. Ventilation and Condensate Identification

  1. Identify outdoor-air intakes, bathroom exhausts, kitchen exhausts, dryer exhausts, whole-house ventilation equipment, attic ventilation, and other ventilation components. Record the visible termination point of each, where accessible.
  2. Identify visible condensate drains, traps, pumps, overflow devices, and discharge locations. Record the visible routing and discharge point of each.

I. Controls Identification and Unresolved Components

  1. Identify thermostats, zone controls, disconnects, service switches, breakers, and equipment labels without removing restricted covers. Record the location and visible label of each.
  2. Mark components whose function cannot be confirmed as unresolved rather than guessing. Attach a label or tag where practical and record the location on the worksheet.
  3. Record equipment clearances, filter size, airflow arrows, service access, visible damage, corrosion, water staining, disconnected ducts, and blocked openings. Complete this record for every accessible area inspected.

J. Schematic and Existing-Condition Documentation

  1. Photograph representative components and labels. Confirm every equipment nameplate and each major duct, ventilation, and condensate component has a corresponding photograph.
  2. Prepare a schematic showing equipment, supply paths, return paths, ventilation components, controls, condensate, and exterior terminations. Use the blank schematic sheet or grid paper and label every component to match the worksheet.
  3. Compare field conditions with available drawings and record discrepancies. Note any difference between the schematic you produced and any available mechanical plans or prior service records.
  4. Identify conditions requiring licensed inspection, including gas odor, scorch marks, damaged flues, suspected refrigerant leakage, electrical damage, or severe corrosion. Record each condition and the recommended escalation; do not correct it as part of this project.
  5. Complete the final HVAC system map and existing-condition record. Confirm every field on the worksheet is filled in before authorizing later chapter projects to begin.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed system map using the same checks regardless of which residence or training system the project involves.

  • Heating check: confirm the heating equipment is identified by type, location, fuel or energy source, manufacturer, model, and visible labels.
  • Cooling check: confirm the cooling equipment is identified by indoor and outdoor components without opening the refrigerant circuit.
  • Duct check: confirm accessible supply and return duct paths are traced and recorded from equipment to termination or to the limit of access.
  • Ventilation and condensate check: confirm every accessible ventilation and condensate component is identified and recorded.
  • Controls check: confirm every accessible thermostat, zone control, disconnect, and breaker is identified without removing a restricted cover.
  • Unresolved-component check: confirm every component whose function could not be confirmed is marked unresolved rather than guessed at.
  • Documentation check: confirm the HVAC System Map and Existing-Condition Worksheet and schematic are fully completed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Duct or Component Cannot Be Traced to Its Termination

Probable cause: the duct passes into an inaccessible cavity, concealed chase, or restricted area, or the termination is hidden behind finished surfaces.
Confirmation: check for the duct's reappearance at another accessible point, such as a register, grille, or exterior termination.
Correction: mark the duct as unresolved at the point access ends; do not guess at its destination or function.
Reinspection: confirm the unresolved marking appears on both the worksheet and the schematic at the correct location.

An Equipment Label or Nameplate Is Illegible or Missing

Probable cause: age, corrosion, paint overspray, or a replaced access panel without a matching label.
Confirmation: check alternate label locations on the equipment, such as an interior service panel or a secondary rating plate.
Correction: record the equipment as unresolved for manufacturer and model information; do not assume a model or rating from appearance alone.
Reinspection: confirm the unresolved entry is recorded on the worksheet with the equipment's location and observable characteristics.

Field Conditions Do Not Match Available Mechanical Plans

Probable cause: a prior modification, an undocumented repair, or an outdated drawing set.
Confirmation: re-check the field condition against the plan at the specific point of disagreement.
Correction: record the discrepancy on the worksheet; do not alter the schematic to match the plan when field conditions differ.
Reinspection: confirm every identified discrepancy appears in the discrepancy record with its location and description.

A Hazardous or Restricted Condition Is Observed During the Walk-Through

Probable cause: gas odor, scorch marks, damaged flue components, suspected refrigerant leakage, electrical damage, severe corrosion, suspected asbestos-containing material, or heavy mold growth.
Confirmation: do not approach, disturb, or further investigate the condition; confirm only what is visible from a safe distance.
Correction: stop work in the affected area, record the condition and its location, and escalate to a qualified professional; do not continue mapping that area until the condition is addressed.
Reinspection: resume mapping the affected area only after the condition has been corrected or a qualified professional has authorized continued access.

The System or Zone Was Not Clearly Defined Before the Walk-Through Began

Probable cause: the system or zone included in the project was not written down before equipment identification began.
Confirmation: compare the equipment and ducts actually mapped against the intended scope of the assigned chapter project.
Correction: re-define the system or zone in writing and repeat any identification step completed without a confirmed boundary.
Reinspection: repeat the equipment identification and duct-tracing steps for the confirmed system or zone.

Project Closeout

You have completed the first project in the residential HVAC sequence: identifying and documenting a residential HVAC system.

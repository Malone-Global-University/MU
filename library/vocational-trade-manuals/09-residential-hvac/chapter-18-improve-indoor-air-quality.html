Job Overview

This project qualifies you to assess the filtration, ventilation, humidity, dust-source, and moisture conditions of one residence or training space and to produce one written Indoor-Air-Quality Assessment and Maintenance Plan. The finished work product is a documented plan listing current conditions, low-risk maintenance actions the occupant or learner may perform, professional referrals for conditions outside this book's scope, a humidity-monitoring schedule, and a reinspection date.

You will confirm the assessment is not a medical evaluation, mold certification, hazardous-material survey, or guarantee of healthy air; review filter, ventilation, and humidity history; inspect filter fit and rack bypass; identify whether bathroom, kitchen, and laundry ventilation exhaust outdoors; record indoor relative humidity at defined locations and times; inspect for condensation and visible moisture damage; identify dust and particle sources; verify unobstructed combustion-appliance air and venting without altering those systems; and produce the written plan with maintenance actions, referrals, monitoring, and reinspection dates.

This project depends on the HVAC system map produced in Chapter 1, the filter and rack condition documented in Chapter 3, the duct-deficiency findings from Chapter 5 and the sealing and insulation work from Chapters 6 and 7, and the ventilation installations completed in Chapters 16 and 17. The IAQ plan you produce here feeds directly into the comfort-problem diagnostics of Chapter 19 and the closeout of the Chapter 20 capstone system.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-103 — Assess filtration level, including filter fit, rack bypass, loading condition, and compliance with the equipment's approved filtration range.
  • HV-104 — Identify ventilation needs, including whether bathroom, kitchen, laundry, and whole-house ventilation components exhaust outdoors.
  • HV-105 — Monitor humidity using a verified instrument at defined locations and times, and record indoor relative humidity.
  • HV-106 — Reduce dust sources by identifying open returns, unsealed ducts, construction dust, pets, stored chemicals, combustion products, and outdoor infiltration.
  • HV-107 — Identify moisture problems, including condensation, water staining, active leakage, musty odor, and standing water.
  • HV-108 — Create an indoor-air-quality maintenance plan listing current conditions, maintenance actions, professional referrals, monitoring, and reinspection dates.

Where This Skill Fits

The filter assessment in this chapter builds directly on the filter-rack service taught in Chapter 3. The ventilation-termination check builds on the bathroom-exhaust and dryer-exhaust installations in Chapters 16 and 17. The moisture and condensation inspection uses the same duct and condensate observations documented in Chapters 5, 6, 7, and 14. The comfort-diagnostic procedure in Chapter 19 and the capstone closeout in Chapter 20 both reference the IAQ plan produced here when a comfort complaint or handoff record involves filtration, ventilation, humidity, or moisture.

An IAQ plan produced without reviewing the equipment's approved filtration range, the ventilation system's actual termination points, and a verified humidity reading is not a maintenance plan — it is an unsupported opinion. This chapter requires verified instrument readings and documented observations at every step.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the occupied areas, HVAC systems, ventilation equipment, occupant concerns, and assessment limits
  • assessing filter fit, rack bypass, loading, and the equipment's approved filtration range
  • identifying whether bathroom, kitchen, laundry, and whole-house ventilation components exhaust outdoors
  • recording indoor relative humidity using a verified instrument at defined locations and times
  • inspecting for condensation, water staining, active leakage, musty odor, and standing water
  • identifying dust and particle sources and housekeeping conditions around returns, equipment, ducts, and exhaust grilles
  • verifying that combustion appliances have unobstructed air and venting without altering those systems
  • creating a written IAQ maintenance plan with monitoring schedule, maintenance actions, and professional referrals

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform a medical evaluation, diagnose a health condition, or certify a mold-remediation outcome
  • conduct a hazardous-material survey for asbestos, lead, or other regulated contaminants
  • alter combustion-appliance venting, draft, or combustion air as part of this project
  • perform ventilation-system redesign, dehumidification or humidification equipment installation, or major duct modification
  • publish an airflow, filtration, indoor-air-quality, mold-remediation, or energy-savings claim that is not supported by the identified test, instrument reading, or design

Any condition involving sewage contamination, widespread mold, suspected asbestos, active combustion concerns, severe water damage, or reported health symptoms must be recorded and referred to a qualified professional; it is not investigated further or corrected as part of this project.

Trade Note

This chapter produces a maintenance and referral plan, not a certification. Every entry on the plan must trace to a verified instrument reading, a documented observation, or a manufacturer or design specification. Stating that a space has "poor air quality" or "high humidity" without a recorded reading and a stated threshold is exactly the kind of unsupported claim this book does not permit.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the assessment without the Chapter 1 HVAC system map and the equipment's approved filtration range. Do not record a humidity reading without first verifying the instrument against a known reference or the manufacturer's calibration statement. Shop Tip

Record humidity readings at the same time of day across the monitoring schedule so later comparisons are not distorted by normal daily swings in temperature and occupant activity such as cooking, showering, or laundry.

Scope Limits and Referral Boundary

This project produces a maintenance and referral plan based on filtration, ventilation, humidity, dust sources, and visible moisture conditions. It is not a medical evaluation, a mold certification, a hazardous-material survey, or a guarantee of healthy air. State this limitation to the occupant before beginning the assessment and again in the written plan.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop and Refer Rather Than Investigate

Stop work and refer to a qualified professional any condition involving suspected sewage contamination, widespread mold, suspected asbestos-containing material, active combustion concerns, severe water damage, or reported health symptoms. Do not disturb, sample, or further investigate the condition as part of this project.

Measurement Cue — Verify the Instrument Before Recording Readings

Check the relative-humidity instrument against a known reference or the manufacturer's stated calibration method before recording any reading used in the IAQ plan. Record readings at the same defined locations and times across the monitoring schedule so later comparisons are valid.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Filtration Range Is Equipment-Specific

Compare the installed filter against the equipment manufacturer's approved filtration range, including maximum pressure drop. A higher-rated filter than the equipment's approved range can restrict airflow and is not automatically an improvement. Reference the equipment nameplate and filter-rack instructions identified in Chapter 3.

Field Safety Cue — Combustion-Air and Venting Observation Only

Confirm that combustion appliances have unobstructed combustion air and venting by visual inspection only. Do not alter, adjust, or test combustion-air openings, flues, vents, or draft components as part of this project. Refer any obstruction, damage, or suspected spillage to a qualified professional.

E. Scope Definition and Prior-Record Review

  1. Define the occupied areas, HVAC systems, ventilation equipment, occupant concerns, and assessment limits. Record the scope in writing before beginning the walk-through.
  2. Explain that the project is not a medical evaluation, mold certification, hazardous-material survey, or guarantee of healthy air. State this limitation to the occupant and record it on the worksheet.
  3. Review filter specifications, replacement history, ventilation equipment, exhaust fans, humidity records, and known moisture events. Reference the Chapter 1 system map and Chapter 3 filter records.

F. Filtration Assessment

  1. Inspect filter fit, rack bypass, loading, and manufacturer-approved filtration range. Compare the installed filter's rating and dimensions against the equipment manufacturer's instructions.

G. Ventilation Assessment

  1. Identify bathroom, kitchen, and laundry, and whole-house ventilation components and whether they exhaust outdoors. Visually trace each accessible ventilation duct to its exterior termination and record the result. Flag any termination that cannot be confirmed as unresolved.

H. Humidity Monitoring

  1. Record indoor relative humidity using a verified instrument at defined locations and times. Verify the instrument against a known reference before the first reading, then log each reading with location, date, and time on the humidity-log sheet.
  2. Inspect for condensation on windows, pipes, ducts, or cold surfaces. Record the location and extent of any condensation observed.

I. Moisture and Dust-Source Identification

  1. Inspect for visible water staining, active leakage, musty odor, damaged materials, and standing water. Record the location and description of each condition found.
  2. Stop work and refer conditions involving sewage, widespread mold, asbestos, combustion concerns, severe water damage, or health symptoms. Record the condition and the recommended referral; do not investigate further.
  3. Identify dust and particle sources such as open returns, construction dust, unsealed ducts, pets, stored chemicals, combustion products, and outdoor infiltration. Record each identified source with its location.
  4. Inspect housekeeping conditions around returns, equipment, ducts, and exhaust grilles. Record any condition contributing to dust or restricted airflow.
  5. Verify that combustion appliances have unobstructed air and venting without altering those systems. Observe from a safe distance and record the result; do not adjust or test combustion-air or venting components.

J. Plan Development and Documentation

  1. Compare actual filter and ventilation conditions with manufacturer and approved design requirements. Record any deviation from the equipment's approved filtration range or the ventilation design.
  2. Identify low-risk source-control actions such as filter replacement, grille cleaning, moisture cleanup, exhaust-fan use, and storage control. List each action the occupant or learner may complete without design or licensed work.
  3. Identify improvements requiring design or licensed work, including ventilation-system changes, dehumidification, humidification, combustion testing, or major duct modifications. List each referral with its supporting observation.
  4. Establish a humidity-monitoring schedule and acceptable project-specific review thresholds. State the monitoring frequency, duration, and the threshold at which a reading triggers a referral or corrective action.
  5. Establish filter, exhaust-fan, dryer-duct, condensate, and outdoor-unit maintenance intervals. Reference the manufacturer instructions and prior chapter records for each interval.
  6. Create a written IAQ plan listing current conditions, maintenance actions, professional referrals, monitoring, and reinspection dates. Complete every field on the worksheet before closeout.
  7. Document limitations and avoid unsupported health or performance claims. Restate the assessment's scope limits on the final written plan.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Ventilation Duct Cannot Be Traced to Its Exterior Termination

Probable cause: the duct passes into an inaccessible cavity, attic, or chase, or terminates behind finished surfaces.
Confirmation: check for the duct's reappearance at another accessible point, such as an exterior wall or roof cap, and compare with the Chapter 1 system map.
Correction: record the termination as unresolved and recommend a licensed inspection to confirm the duct does not terminate in an attic, soffit, or wall cavity.
Reinspection: confirm the unresolved marking and referral appear on the IAQ plan.

A Humidity Reading Appears Inconsistent with Occupant-Reported Conditions

Probable cause: the instrument was not verified before use, the reading was taken at an unrepresentative time or location, or recent occupant activity such as cooking or showering affected the reading.
Confirmation: re-verify the instrument against a known reference and repeat the reading at the same defined location and a comparable time.
Correction: record both readings with their conditions and note the discrepancy on the humidity log; do not discard either reading without explanation.
Reinspection: confirm the monitoring schedule includes enough repeated readings to distinguish a normal swing from a persistent condition.

The Installed Filter Exceeds the Equipment's Approved Filtration Range

Probable cause: a higher-rated filter was installed under the assumption that higher filtration is always better.
Confirmation: compare the installed filter's rating and pressure-drop specification against the equipment manufacturer's approved range from Chapter 3.
Correction: record the deviation and recommend replacement with a filter within the approved range as a maintenance action; do not select or install a specific replacement filter as part of this project unless Chapter 3 procedures are repeated.
Reinspection: confirm the recommendation and supporting comparison appear on the IAQ plan.

A Condition Requiring Referral Is Found Mid-Assessment

Probable cause: sewage contamination, widespread mold, suspected asbestos, active combustion concerns, severe water damage, or a reported health symptom is observed or disclosed.
Confirmation: do not approach, disturb, sample, or further investigate the condition; confirm only what is visible from a safe distance or reported directly by the occupant.
Correction: stop the assessment in the affected area, record the condition and its location, and recommend the appropriate qualified professional; do not continue assessing that area.
Reinspection: resume the assessment in the affected area only after the condition has been addressed or a qualified professional has authorized continued access.

The Finished Plan Contains an Unsupported Claim

Probable cause: a maintenance action, referral, or conclusion was written without a corresponding instrument reading, observation, or manufacturer specification.
Confirmation: review each entry on the IAQ plan against the worksheet's recorded readings and observations.
Correction: remove or revise any entry not supported by a specific reading, observation, or specification; do not publish an airflow, filtration, indoor-air-quality, mold-remediation, or energy-savings claim without that support.
Reinspection: confirm every entry on the final plan traces to a specific recorded reading, observation, or specification.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to insulate one accessible duct section, its fittings, and its register boot using the exact thermal and vapor-control system identified by the approved duct plan or manufacturer instructions. The finished work product is a continuously insulated duct run with a sealed, continuous vapor facing, covered fittings and boot, and every label, damper, access panel, and required inspection point still visible and reachable.

You will confirm the duct location, operating temperature, condensation risk, insulation type, required thermal resistance, facing, and closure materials; verify the duct has already been sealed under Chapter 6 and that sealant has cured; measure the duct and fitting geometry; cut, wrap, and secure insulation to the duct, fittings, boot, and transitions with the required overlap and vapor-facing continuity; and inspect the completed work for gaps, compression, exposed metal, and open seams before restoring equipment.

This project depends directly on Chapter 6. It follows the identical duct section that was sealed there and does not begin until that sealing work has passed inspection and cured. It in turn supports Chapter 8's register boot work, Chapter 10's flexible-duct branch installation, and the Chapter 20 capstone, each of which specifies insulation coverage using the exact procedure taught here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-037 — Select duct insulation matching the approved type, thermal resistance, facing, and closure system.
  • HV-038 — Wrap duct sections with the required overlap and vapor facing orientation.
  • HV-039 — Seal insulation seams continuously using compatible listed tape or adhesive.
  • HV-040 — Protect vapor facing from punctures, tears, and discontinuities.
  • HV-041 — Insulate boots and transitions while preserving service access.
  • HV-042 — Inspect completed thermal coverage for gaps, compression, and vapor-facing continuity.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 6 seals the leakage points in a duct run. This project keeps that sealed air at the correct temperature and prevents the surface condensation that develops on an uninsulated cold-air duct in a warm attic or on a heated duct run in an unconditioned crawlspace. An improperly insulated or gapped duct wastes conditioned air, sweats onto insulation and framing, and undermines the leakage work completed one chapter earlier.

The insulation habits established here — full coverage without compression, continuous vapor facing, and preserved access to labels and dampers — are the same habits required at the register boot in Chapter 8, the flexible-duct branch in Chapter 10, and the complete practice system built in the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing duct location, operating temperature, condensation risk, insulation type, required thermal resistance, facing, closure materials, and clearances from the approved plan or manufacturer instructions
  • confirming that duct leakage has been sealed and all sealants have cured before insulation begins
  • measuring duct perimeter and fitting geometry and cutting insulation to provide full coverage without excessive compression
  • wrapping the straight duct section, maintaining the manufacturer-required seam overlap, and orienting the vapor facing outward where required
  • sealing vapor-facing seams continuously with compatible listed tape or adhesive
  • forming and installing insulation around elbows, transitions, takeoffs, dampers, and boots while preserving service access
  • repairing punctures and damaged facing and inspecting the completed coverage for gaps, compression, and exposed metal

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • insulate over an unsealed joint, an uncured sealant, or a duct section not yet accepted under Chapter 6
  • cover labels, dampers, access panels, electrical boxes, combustion clearances, or required inspection points
  • select an insulation product, thickness, or facing not identified by the approved duct plan or manufacturer instructions
  • resize, reroute, or otherwise alter the duct while insulating it
  • conceal the completed insulation work until it has passed the required inspection

Any duct section found damaged, contaminated, or structurally unsuitable during this project must be recorded and referred back to the Chapter 5 deficiency report; it is not corrected as part of this project.

Trade Note

Insulation without a continuous, sealed vapor facing does not stop condensation — it only delays it. A gap, a puncture, or an unsealed seam gives moist air a path to the cold duct surface underneath, where it condenses out of sight. Every seam sealed and every puncture patched in this project protects the framing, insulation, and duct sealed in the previous chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not begin insulating until the duct section has been sealed under Chapter 6 and the sealant has fully cured to the manufacturer-controlled time. Do not cover a label, damper, access panel, electrical box, combustion clearance, or required inspection point with insulation at any point in this project.

Shop Tip

Cut insulation slightly oversize and compress it only enough to close the seam, never to force it around a fitting. Compressed insulation loses thermal resistance exactly where the duct needs it most — at elbows, transitions, and boots where turbulence and surface temperature differences are greatest.

Common Field Fundamentals

Insulating ductwork protects two things at once: the temperature of the air moving through the duct, and the surfaces around the duct that would otherwise collect condensation. Both depend on the same requirement — a continuous, unbroken vapor facing with no gaps, compression, or punctures.

Sealing Must Precede Insulation

Insulation installed over an unsealed joint or uncured sealant traps moisture against the leak point and hides it from view. Confirm the Chapter 6 sealing record for this exact duct section before cutting any insulation, and confirm the sealant has cured for the manufacturer-controlled time.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Insulation Type, R-Value, and Facing Orientation Are Not Optional

Install only the insulation type, thickness, and facing identified by the approved duct plan or manufacturer instructions. Do not substitute a different product, a lower R-value, or a different facing orientation because it is more convenient to install. Orient the vapor facing outward unless the manufacturer instructions specify a different orientation for the identified product.

Measurement Cue — Full Coverage Without Compression

Cut insulation to the measured duct perimeter and fitting geometry, providing full coverage without excessive compression. Maintain the manufacturer-required overlap at every longitudinal and circumferential seam. Compressed insulation loses thermal resistance at the exact points — elbows, transitions, and boots — where the loss matters most.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Cover Required Access or Clearance Points

Do not cover labels, dampers, access panels, electrical boxes, combustion clearances, or required inspection points with insulation at any point in this project. A covered damper cannot be adjusted without cutting into finished insulation, and a covered combustion clearance becomes a fire hazard that is invisible until it causes damage.

Field Safety Cue — Insulation Handling in Attics and Crawlspaces

Wear the dust mask or respirator specified for the insulation product's safety data sheet, and wear long sleeves and gloves when handling fiberglass or mineral-fiber insulation. Confirm adequate lighting, footing, and structural support before working overhead in an attic or in a confined crawlspace.

E. Preparation and Prerequisite Verification

  1. Review duct location, operating temperature, condensation risk, insulation type, required thermal resistance, facing, closure materials, and clearances. Confirm these values against the approved duct plan or manufacturer instructions and record them on the worksheet.
  2. Shut down equipment. Use the Chapter 2 procedure and verify the duct section is not moving air before beginning work.
  3. Verify that duct leakage has been sealed and all sealants have cured. Confirm the Chapter 6 record for this exact duct section and the manufacturer-controlled cure time before proceeding.
  4. Inspect the duct for moisture, corrosion, contamination, damaged supports, and prohibited heat-source proximity. Record any condition that must be corrected before insulation continues.

F. Measurement and Cutting

  1. Measure duct perimeter and fitting geometry. Record the measured dimensions for the straight run and each fitting, transition, takeoff, and boot.
  2. Cut insulation to provide full coverage without excessive compression. Cut each piece to the measured dimension plus the manufacturer-required overlap allowance.

G. Wrapping the Straight Duct Section

  1. Maintain the manufacturer-required overlap at longitudinal and circumferential seams. Confirm the overlap dimension at each seam before securing the insulation.
  2. Wrap the straight duct section with the vapor facing outward where required. Confirm the facing orientation matches the manufacturer instructions for the identified product.
  3. Secure insulation using approved fasteners, bands, or pins without crushing it. Space fasteners, bands, or pins according to the manufacturer instructions.
  4. Seal vapor-facing seams continuously with compatible listed tape or adhesive. Burnish every seam with the seam roller for full, continuous adhesion.

H. Fittings, Boots, and Transitions

  1. Form and install insulation around elbows, transitions, takeoffs, and dampers while preserving service access. Cut and fit insulation pieces individually at each fitting rather than forcing the straight-run piece to bend around it.
  2. Insulate boots to the approved boundary without blocking register attachment or fire-rated details. Confirm the register can still be installed and removed after boot insulation is complete.
  3. Seal insulation to adjoining vapor-control layers where required. Confirm continuity with any adjoining building vapor-control layer identified by the approved plan.

I. Facing Repair and Access Preservation

  1. Repair punctures, tears, and damaged facing with the approved patch method. Confirm the patch restores continuous vapor-facing coverage at the repaired location.
  2. Do not cover labels, dampers, access panels, electrical boxes, combustion clearances, or required inspection points. Confirm each preserved access point remains visible and reachable before continuing.

J. Inspection and Restoration

  1. Check for gaps, compressed areas, exposed metal, open seams, and unsupported insulation. Correct any deficiency found before restoring equipment.
  2. Restore equipment and inspect for condensation or movement under operation. Observe the insulated section during an authorized operating cycle.
  3. Correct failed coverage or vapor-facing continuity. Re-inspect any corrected area before closing out the project.
  4. Document insulation product, thickness, facing, closures, and completed locations. Complete every field on the Duct Insulation Worksheet before authorizing the next chapter project to begin.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed insulation run using the same checks regardless of which duct section or training system the project involves.

  • Prerequisite check: confirm Chapter 6 sealing is accepted and sealant is fully cured for this exact duct section.
  • Coverage check: confirm full insulation coverage of the straight run, fittings, transitions, takeoffs, dampers, and boot with no gaps or exposed metal.
  • Seam check: confirm the manufacturer-required overlap and continuous sealed vapor facing at every longitudinal and circumferential seam.
  • Compression check: confirm no area is compressed beyond the manufacturer-permitted limit.
  • Access check: confirm every label, damper, access panel, electrical box, combustion clearance, and inspection point remains visible and reachable.
  • Operating check: confirm no condensation or insulation movement is observed during an authorized operating cycle.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Duct Insulation Worksheet is fully completed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Vapor Facing Shows a Gap or Open Seam After Installation

Probable cause: insufficient overlap at a longitudinal or circumferential seam, or tape applied to a dusty or damp surface that did not bond.
Confirmation: inspect the seam for the manufacturer-required overlap dimension and check for tape lift or incomplete adhesion.
Correction: clean and dry the surface, add insulation or facing material to achieve the required overlap, and reseal with new listed tape or adhesive, burnishing fully.
Reinspection: confirm the seam shows continuous, burnished adhesion with no visible gap.

Insulation Is Compressed or Crushed at a Fitting or Support Point

Probable cause: the straight-run insulation piece was forced around a fitting instead of being individually cut and fitted, or a fastener, band, or pin was overtightened.
Confirmation: measure the insulation thickness at the compressed area against the specified thickness elsewhere on the run.
Correction: remove the compressed section, cut a properly fitted piece for the fitting, and reinstall fasteners, bands, or pins to the manufacturer-specified tension.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected area matches the specified thickness and is not compressed.

A Required Access Point Is Covered by Insulation

Probable cause: insulation was wrapped continuously across a damper handle, access panel, label, or electrical box without cutting an opening.
Confirmation: check the worksheet's list of preserved access points against the completed insulation run.
Correction: cut back the insulation to expose the access point and reseal the cut edge with the approved facing repair method.
Reinspection: confirm the access point is visible, reachable, and the surrounding facing is continuous.

Condensation Appears on the Duct or Surrounding Surfaces After Restoring Equipment

Probable cause: a gap, puncture, or discontinuous vapor facing is allowing moist air to reach the cold duct surface underneath.
Confirmation: inspect the insulation at and near the condensation location for a facing discontinuity.
Correction: shut down equipment, locate and repair the facing discontinuity using the approved patch or reseal method, and allow the repair to cure before restoring equipment.
Reinspection: observe the repaired location during a subsequent authorized operating cycle for continued condensation.

Insulation Was Installed Before Chapter 6 Sealant Fully Cured

Probable cause: the manufacturer-controlled cure time for the mastic or tape was not confirmed before insulation work began.
Confirmation: check the Chapter 6 record for the cure time specified and the date sealing was completed.
Correction: remove the insulation over the affected joint, verify the sealant has now cured or reseal if it failed to cure properly, and reinstall insulation only after cure is confirmed.
Reinspection: confirm the joint shows no leakage and the reinstalled insulation meets all coverage and seam standards.

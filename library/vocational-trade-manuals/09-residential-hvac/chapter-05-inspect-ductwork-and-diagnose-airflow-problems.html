Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect the accessible duct system serving selected rooms and produce a written, evidence-based airflow-deficiency report. The finished work product is one completed Duct and Airflow Deficiency Report: a documented inspection of accessible supply and return duct runs identifying disconnections, crushing, leakage, undersized-return symptoms, and other restrictions, together with recommended corrections and a clear separation between work this book authorizes and work requiring professional design or licensed service.

You will trace the supply path from equipment plenum to each selected register and the return path from selected return grilles to the equipment; identify disconnected joints, crushed flex duct, sagging, and damaged boots; inspect return paths for undersized-return symptoms; compare room airflow using approved observation or instrumentation; and prepare a deficiency report using location, symptom, confirmation evidence, recommended correction, and priority.

This project depends on the system map completed in Project 01 and uses the shutdown procedure established in Project 02 before any physical inspection begins. The deficiency report produced here directly controls the work performed in Projects 6 through 11: duct sealing, insulation, register-boot work, return-path construction, flexible-duct installation, and airflow balancing. This project does not authorize resizing ducts, redesigning the system, or performing any correction beyond the noninvasive observations described here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-025 — Trace duct runs from equipment plenum to selected registers and from selected return grilles to equipment.
  • HV-026 — Identify crushed ducts, including collapsed flex liners, sharp bends, and structurally damaged sections.
  • HV-027 — Identify disconnected ducts, including open seams, separated joints, and detached boots.
  • HV-028 — Identify undersized-return symptoms, including closed-door pressure effects, restricted transfer paths, and filter-related restriction.
  • HV-029 — Locate airflow restrictions using approved comparison or measurement methods without altering the system.
  • HV-030 — Document duct deficiencies in a structured, evidence-based report with recommended correction and priority.

Where This Skill Fits

This project sits between the basic-service work of Projects 3 and 4 and the duct-system correction work of Projects 6 through 11. The system map from Project 01 identifies what exists; the readiness procedure from Project 02 shuts equipment down safely; this project determines what is wrong with the accessible duct system and where; and Projects 6 through 11 correct only the deficiencies this report identifies as within their scope.

The evidence-based reporting habit established here — location, symptom, confirmation evidence, recommended correction, and priority — is the same structure used again in the comfort-diagnostic work of Project 19.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the system map, complaint history, room uses, filter condition, equipment status, and available duct drawings
  • confirming safe access to attics, crawlspaces, basements, closets, and chases
  • shutting down equipment before physical duct inspection
  • tracing accessible supply and return paths and recording duct material, approximate size, insulation, supports, fittings, dampers, and transitions
  • identifying disconnected joints, open seams, crushed flex duct, torn jackets, severe sags, collapsed liners, sharp bends, blocked registers, and damaged boots
  • inspecting return grilles and return paths for closed doors, blocked transfer paths, undersized openings, filter restriction, or unintended leakage from dirty spaces
  • inspecting accessible dampers for position and operability without changing them yet
  • checking for condensation, wet insulation, rust, staining, or biological growth
  • comparing room airflow qualitatively or with the approved instrument and test setup
  • preparing a deficiency report with location, symptom, confirmation evidence, recommended correction, and priority

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • add ducts, enlarge returns, or change equipment capacity or design
  • correct any deficiency identified during this inspection; correction occurs only in the chapter that the report assigns it to
  • change damper positions as part of this project; damper adjustment occurs under Project 11
  • enter contaminated cavities, mold-affected areas, or spaces with suspected asbestos-containing material
  • diagnose refrigerant charge, combustion performance, heat-exchanger condition, or equipment sizing
  • continue physical inspection of any area where suspected asbestos-containing duct wrap, transite, vermiculite, lead dust, animal waste, sewage contamination, heavy mold growth, or fire damage is observed

Any deficiency identified in this chapter that requires design calculations, equipment service, duct replacement, or licensed evaluation must be recorded and escalated; it is not corrected as part of this project.

Trade Note

This project is a diagnostic project, not a repair project. Every finding is recorded with its confirming evidence before any correction is proposed, and every recommended correction is checked against the scope limits of Projects 6 through 11 before it is assigned. A deficiency report that recommends work outside this book's scope — resizing a duct, adding a return without an approved design, or replacing equipment — must say so plainly rather than quietly expanding the project.

Shop Tip

Photograph every deficiency from a location and angle that will still make sense to you when you return to correct it in a later chapter. A crushed section noted only as "attic, near the middle" is much harder to find again than one photographed against a labeled joist or duct-run reference point.

Diagnosis Without Redesign

This project diagnoses accessible duct and airflow conditions only. It does not authorize adding ducts, enlarging returns, resizing branches, or changing equipment capacity. A finding that appears to require any of these actions must be recorded as requiring design calculations or licensed evaluation, not corrected or assumed resolved by a larger duct or return opening chosen in the field.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Every Finding Before Recording It

Record a deficiency only after you have traced the duct to the point of the observed condition and identified the specific evidence — a visible gap, a measured collapse, a door-closure pressure effect, or a comparable observation. Do not record a symptom based on a room's reputation for being "always cold" without confirming it during this inspection.

Critical Safety Cue — Shut Down Before Physical Duct Inspection

Shut down equipment using the Project 02 procedure before beginning physical inspection of duct runs, boots, and connections. Restore equipment and perform only noninvasive observations with all safety panels installed when comparing room airflow. Do not touch energized components, and do not remove a restricted electrical, combustion, or refrigerant-component cover at any point in this project.

Field Safety Cue — Access Hazards and Stop-Work Conditions

Inspect attic, crawlspace, and mechanical-closet access for structural support, adequate lighting, insulation hazards, pests, and confined-space conditions before entering. Stop work and do not continue inspecting an area where suspected asbestos-containing duct wrap, transite, vermiculite, lead dust, animal waste, sewage contamination, heavy mold growth, or fire damage is observed.

Code Alert — Return-Air Source Restrictions

Do not treat a garage, mechanical room, contaminated space, or other restricted location as an acceptable return-air source when recording undersized-return symptoms or proposing a correction. Any return-air change identified as necessary is recorded for the approved-design procedure in Project 09, not decided or implemented here.

E. Preparation and Prior-Record Review

  1. Review the system map, complaint history, room uses, filter condition, equipment status, and available duct drawings. Confirm the system map from Project 01 matches the system or zone to be inspected.
  2. Confirm safe access to attics, crawlspaces, basements, closets, and chases. Verify structural support, lighting, and confined-space conditions before entering any area.

F. Shutdown and Access Preparation

  1. Shut down equipment before physical duct inspection. Apply the complete Project 02 shutdown and readiness procedure and confirm the equipment cannot start unexpectedly.
  2. Inspect access areas for structural, electrical, pest, insulation, heat, confined-space, and contamination hazards. Stop work in any area where a stop-work condition is observed and record it before proceeding elsewhere.

G. Supply and Return Path Tracing

  1. Trace the supply path from equipment plenum to each selected register. Follow the path continuously and note where access ends if the full path cannot be observed.
  2. Trace the return path from selected return grilles to the equipment. Follow the path continuously and note where access ends if the full path cannot be observed.
  3. Record duct material, approximate size, insulation, supports, fittings, dampers, and transitions. Use the tape measure for approximate sizes and record each fitting and transition by location.

H. Duct Damage and Deficiency Identification

  1. Identify disconnected joints, open seams, crushed flex duct, torn jackets, severe sags, collapsed liners, sharp bends, blocked registers, and damaged boots. Record the exact location, the specific evidence observed, and a photograph for each.
  2. Inspect return grilles and return paths for closed doors, blocked transfer paths, undersized openings, filter restriction, or unintended leakage from dirty spaces. Record the confirming observation for each undersized-return symptom.
  3. Inspect accessible dampers for position and operability without changing them yet. Record the as-found position of every damper without adjusting it.
  4. Check for condensation, wet insulation, rust, staining, or biological growth. Record the location and extent of any moisture-related condition found.
  5. Stop work for suspected asbestos, severe mold, sewage contamination, fire damage, or unsafe access. Record the condition and its location, and escalate to a qualified professional rather than continuing inspection of that area.

I. Airflow Comparison and Restriction Location

  1. Restore equipment and perform only noninvasive observations with all safety panels installed. Confirm no panels or covers remain open before restoring power.
  2. Compare room airflow qualitatively or with the approved instrument and test setup. Use the same method and operating condition for every selected room to keep the comparison valid.
  3. Record room temperature differences under defined operating conditions where required. Note the thermostat setting, run time, and outdoor condition at the time of the comparison.
  4. Distinguish equipment-capacity symptoms from obvious distribution restrictions without making unsupported conclusions. Record only what the evidence supports; mark ambiguous findings as requiring further evaluation rather than assuming a cause.

J. Scope Classification and Deficiency Reporting

  1. Identify deficiencies that can be corrected under Projects 6–11. Match each finding to the specific later-chapter scope that authorizes its correction.
  2. Identify conditions requiring design calculations, equipment service, duct replacement, or licensed evaluation. Record these separately and do not assign them to a book-scope correction.
  3. Prepare a deficiency report with location, symptom, confirmation evidence, recommended correction, and priority. Complete one entry for every finding recorded during the inspection.
  4. Do not recommend adding ducts, enlarging returns, or changing equipment without approved design review. Confirm the report contains no such recommendation before completing it.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Room Shows Weak Airflow but No Duct Damage Is Found Along the Traced Path

Probable cause: a restriction beyond accessible inspection, a loaded filter, closed or partially closed dampers, or an equipment-capacity condition rather than a duct defect.
Confirmation: re-check filter condition, damper positions, and the complete traced path for any missed section.
Correction: record the finding as requiring further evaluation rather than assigning it to a specific duct correction; do not guess at an unconfirmed cause.
Reinspection: confirm the report reflects the unresolved status and recommends the correct next step.

A Duct Section Cannot Be Fully Traced Due to Limited Access

Probable cause: the duct passes into an inaccessible cavity, concealed chase, or restricted area beyond the point access ends.
Confirmation: check for the duct's reappearance at another accessible point, such as a register, grille, or exterior termination.
Correction: record the trace as ending at the specific point access was lost; do not assume the condition of the unseen section.
Reinspection: confirm the worksheet identifies the exact point where tracing stopped and why.

A Closed-Door Room Shows a Pressure or Airflow Symptom

Probable cause: an undersized or blocked return-air transfer path relative to the room's supply airflow.
Confirmation: observe the symptom with the door closed under the defined test condition and compare with the door open.
Correction: record the finding as an undersized-return symptom with the confirming observation; do not cut a new return opening as part of this project.
Reinspection: confirm the finding is assigned to the approved-design procedure in Project 09 rather than corrected here.

Field Conditions Do Not Match the Project 01 System Map

Probable cause: a prior modification, an undocumented repair, or an incomplete original trace.
Confirmation: re-check the field condition against the system map at the specific point of disagreement.
Correction: record the discrepancy on the deficiency report; do not alter the original system map to match the new observation without updating both records.
Reinspection: confirm every identified discrepancy appears in the report with its location and description.

A Hazardous or Restricted Condition Is Observed During the Inspection

Probable cause: suspected asbestos-containing material, heavy mold growth, sewage contamination, fire damage, or an unsafe access condition.
Confirmation: do not approach, disturb, or further investigate the condition; confirm only what is visible from a safe distance.
Correction: stop work in the affected area, record the condition and its location, and escalate to a qualified professional; do not continue inspecting that area until the condition is addressed.
Reinspection: resume inspecting the affected area only after the condition has been corrected or a qualified professional has authorized continued access.

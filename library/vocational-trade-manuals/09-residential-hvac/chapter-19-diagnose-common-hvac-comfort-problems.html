Job Overview

This project qualifies you to investigate one residential comfort complaint — weak airflow, uneven room temperatures, short-cycling symptoms, suspected duct leakage, or a suspected thermostat issue — using controlled, repeatable observations rather than assumptions. The finished work product is one completed HVAC Comfort-Diagnostic Report: a symptom, probable cause, confirmation evidence, recommended correction or referral, and reinspection result for the investigated complaint, built entirely from the system map, filter history, duct-deficiency report, and prior repair records already on file for this system.

You will record the complaint exactly as reported; review the HVAC map, filter history, duct-deficiency report, and thermostat configuration; inspect for emergency conditions before any diagnostic step begins; observe one complete authorized operating cycle with all equipment panels installed; compare airflow and temperatures using repeatable methods; classify each finding by who is authorized to correct it; correct only issues within this book's scope; and repeat the original observation after any correction to confirm the result.

This project depends on every earlier chapter in this book. The system map from Project 1, the shutdown procedure from Project 2, the filter record from Project 3, the grille and airflow-path condition from Project 4, the duct-deficiency report from Project 5, the sealing and insulation records from Projects 6 and 7, the register, return, and flexible-duct condition from Projects 8 through 10, the balancing record from Project 11, the thermostat configuration from Project 12, the cabinet inspection from Project 13, the condensate record from Project 14, the condenser condition from Project 15, and the ventilation and indoor-air-quality plan from Projects 16 through 18 are the evidence base this diagnosis is built from. This project does not repeat that evidence-gathering work; it uses the completed records.

  • HV-110 — Diagnose uneven room temperatures using duct distribution, solar gain, insulation, air leakage, return path, and control location.
  • HV-111 — Diagnose short-cycling symptoms without opening refrigerant, combustion, or electrical systems.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • recording a reported comfort complaint and the conditions surrounding it
  • reviewing the existing HVAC map, filter history, duct-deficiency report, and thermostat configuration on file
  • observing one complete authorized operating cycle with all equipment panels installed
  • comparing airflow and room temperatures using repeatable, approved methods
  • diagnosing weak airflow, uneven temperatures, and short-cycling symptoms within this book's authorized scope
  • checking thermostat wire labels, configuration, and mounting only within the Chapter 12 scope
  • classifying each finding as homeowner maintenance, duct repair within book scope, thermostat correction, building-envelope issue, or licensed HVAC service
  • correcting only the issues authorized by earlier chapters and reinspecting after correction
  • preparing a structured diagnostic report

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • diagnose refrigerant charge, heat-exchanger failure, combustion performance, compressor condition, or equipment sizing
  • open a refrigerant circuit, a combustion component, or a line-voltage compartment at any point in this project
  • remove an access panel during the observed operating cycle
  • recommend adding ducts, enlarging returns, or changing equipment capacity without an approved design review
  • proceed past an emergency condition — gas odor, overheating, electrical damage, water leakage, frozen coils, or repeated breaker trips — without stopping and escalating first

Any finding that requires refrigerant, combustion, heat-exchanger, compressor, or line-voltage evaluation must be recorded and referred to a qualified professional; it is not investigated further or corrected as part of this project.

Trade Note

A comfort diagnosis is only as reliable as the observation behind it. Comparing today's airflow at one register to a memory of how it felt last year is not confirmation. Comparing today's airflow at one register to today's airflow at another register, under the same operating cycle, with the same instrument or method, is confirmation. Every finding in this project's report must trace back to an observation made during this project or to a record from an earlier chapter — never to an assumption.

Common Field Fundamentals

A comfort diagnosis in this book always follows the same structure: record the complaint, review the existing record, rule out emergency conditions, observe one authorized operating cycle, compare results using a repeatable method, classify the finding, correct only what this book authorizes, and reinspect. Skipping the record review or the emergency check and moving straight to correction is the most common source of a wrong diagnosis and an unauthorized repair.

Emergency Conditions and Refrigerant, Combustion, and Electrical Boundaries

Do not proceed with any diagnostic step if an emergency condition is present. Do not diagnose refrigerant charge, heat-exchanger failure, combustion performance, compressor condition, or equipment sizing at any point in this project, and do not open a refrigerant circuit, combustion component, or line-voltage compartment. Thermostat checks are limited to the wire labels, configuration, and mounting condition established in Chapter 12.

Measurement Cue — Compare Under the Same Stable Condition Every Time

Record the thermostat setpoint, fan setting, door position, and operating duration before taking any comparison measurement, and hold those conditions constant for every measurement taken during this project. A room-to-room or before-and-after comparison taken under different stable conditions is not valid confirmation evidence.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop for Emergency Conditions Before Any Diagnostic Step

Inspect for gas odor, overheating, electrical damage, water leakage, frozen coils, or repeated breaker trips before beginning the record review or the observed operating cycle. If any of these conditions is present, stop work immediately, do not operate the equipment, and escalate to a qualified professional. Do not continue the diagnostic sequence until the condition has been addressed.

Field Safety Cue — Observed Cycle Runs With All Panels Installed

Observe the complete operating cycle with every access panel installed and secured. Do not remove a panel to "get a better look" during the observed cycle. If a panel-level inspection is required, it is performed separately under the Chapter 13 shutdown and inspection procedure, not during this project's operating observation.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Thermostat Checks Stay Within Chapter 12 Scope

Confirm thermostat wire labels, configuration, and mounting only against the terminal mapping and configuration record established in Chapter 12. Do not open a line-voltage thermostat, a proprietary communicating control, or a zoning panel as part of this project's diagnosis.

E. Complaint Intake and Prior-Record Review

  1. Record the complaint, affected rooms, time of day, weather, thermostat setting, occupancy, door position, and recent changes. Use the occupant's own description; do not translate it into a diagnostic term at this step.
  2. Review the HVAC map, filter history, duct-deficiency report, thermostat configuration, and prior repairs. Note any prior finding for the affected rooms before proceeding.
  3. Inspect for emergency conditions such as gas odor, overheating, electrical damage, water leakage, frozen coils, or repeated breaker trips. Stop work and escalate to a qualified professional if any condition is present; do not proceed to Step 4 until cleared.

F. Baseline Confirmation and Filter, Grille, and Thermostat Check

  1. Verify filter condition, grille position, return-path access, and obvious obstructions. Record the current filter installation date and condition against the Chapter 3 schedule.
  2. Confirm thermostat mode, setpoint, schedule, fan setting, displayed temperature, and mounting condition. Record each value exactly as displayed.

G. Observed Operating Cycle

  1. Observe one complete authorized operating cycle with all equipment panels installed. Do not remove a panel during this observation.
  2. Record equipment start, blower start, delivered airflow, cycle duration, shutdown, and any fault indications. Use the stopwatch or timer and record each event time.

H. Airflow and Temperature Comparison

  1. Compare airflow at selected registers using the approved instrument or repeatable observation. Use the same instrument or method at every register compared.
  2. Record room and return temperatures under a defined stable condition. Hold thermostat setpoint, fan setting, door position, and operating duration constant across all readings.
  3. Inspect accessible ducts for leakage, disconnection, crushing, severe sag, or closed dampers. Limit inspection to ducts already identified as accessible in the Chapter 5 report.
  4. Check whether closed doors create return-path pressure symptoms. Observe door movement, whistle, or airflow change with doors closed and open under the same cycle.

I. Findings Classification

  1. Identify weak airflow caused by obvious restriction, blocked grille, failed duct connection, filter loading, or unresolved equipment condition. Cite the specific confirming observation for each identified cause.
  2. Identify uneven-temperature patterns associated with duct distribution, solar gain, insulation, air leakage, return path, or control location. Cite the specific confirming observation for each identified pattern.
  3. Identify short-cycling symptoms without opening refrigerant, combustion, or electrical systems. Record cycle duration and frequency observed during Step 8; do not open any restricted component to investigate further.
  4. Check thermostat wire labels, configuration, and mounting only within Chapter 12 scope. Do not exceed the Chapter 12 wiring and configuration boundary.
  5. Do not diagnose refrigerant charge, heat-exchanger failure, combustion performance, compressor condition, or equipment sizing without qualified testing. Record these as items requiring licensed HVAC service if suspected.
  6. Classify each finding as homeowner maintenance, duct repair within book scope, thermostat correction, building-envelope issue, or licensed HVAC service. Complete this classification for every finding before correcting any of them.

J. Authorized Correction and Reinspection

  1. Correct only authorized issues covered by earlier chapters. Apply the exact procedure from the relevant earlier chapter; do not improvise a different method.
  2. Repeat the original observation after correction. Use the same stable condition, instrument, and method used in Steps 8 through 11.
  3. Prepare a report using symptom → probable cause → confirmation → correction or referral → reinspection. Complete one full entry for every finding identified in Section I.
  4. Document unresolved variables and avoid claiming certainty without supporting measurements. Record any finding that could not be fully confirmed as unresolved rather than stated as certain.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Room Reads Cold on One Visit and Warm on a Later Visit With No Apparent Cause

Probable cause: the comparisons were taken under different stable conditions, such as a different thermostat setpoint, fan setting, door position, or time since the last cycle.
Confirmation: compare the recorded stable-condition values from each visit.
Correction: repeat both readings under one identical stable condition before drawing a conclusion.
Reinspection: confirm the repeated readings were taken under matching recorded conditions.

Airflow Feels Weak at a Register but the Filter Was Replaced Recently

Probable cause: a closed or partially closed damper, a crushed or disconnected branch duct, or a blocked grille rather than filter loading.
Confirmation: inspect the accessible branch duct and damper position identified in the Chapter 5 report and compare airflow at the affected register against another register on the same trunk.
Correction: correct the damper position, blockage, or duct connection using the authorized Chapter 8, 9, 10, or 11 procedure; do not replace the filter again as the sole correction.
Reinspection: repeat the airflow comparison at the same registers under the same stable condition.

The Equipment Appears to Cycle On and Off More Frequently Than Expected

Probable cause: a restriction such as a loaded filter, closed supply registers, or a blocked return, or a control or equipment condition outside this book's scope.
Confirmation: record cycle duration and frequency during the observed operating cycle and compare filter, grille, and register conditions against the current record.
Correction: correct any restriction within book scope using the authorized chapter procedure; if no restriction is found, classify the finding as licensed HVAC service and do not open the refrigerant, combustion, or electrical system to investigate further.
Reinspection: repeat the observed operating cycle and record the resulting cycle duration and frequency.

A Finding Cannot Be Clearly Classified Between Book Scope and Licensed Service

Probable cause: the confirming evidence is incomplete, or the finding depends on a component this book does not authorize the learner to open or test.
Confirmation: review the finding against the scope boundaries in Section D and the exclusions in this chapter's Scope and Intended Use.
Correction: classify the finding as licensed HVAC service whenever the required confirmation would exceed this book's authorized scope; do not guess at a book-scope classification to avoid a referral.
Reinspection: confirm the classification and referral are recorded on the worksheet with the specific reason the finding exceeds book scope.

An Emergency Condition Is Discovered After the Observed Operating Cycle Has Already Begun

Probable cause: gas odor, overheating, electrical damage, water leakage, frozen coils, or repeated breaker trips appearing during operation rather than during the pre-cycle inspection.
Confirmation: do not approach, disturb, or further investigate the condition; confirm only what is observable from a safe distance.
Correction: stop the equipment using the Chapter 2 shutdown procedure, record the condition and its location, and escalate to a qualified professional; do not resume the observed cycle.
Reinspection: resume this project's observation only after the condition has been corrected or a qualified professional has authorized continued operation.

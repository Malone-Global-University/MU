Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect, clean, and correctly service one accessible HVAC filter rack, and to install a replacement filter that matches the equipment manufacturer's approved size, type, airflow direction, and maximum pressure-drop rating. The finished work product is one serviced filter rack with a correctly sized and oriented replacement filter seated, bypass gaps controlled, and a documented maintenance schedule.

You will identify the filter location and rack from the Project 01 system map; review the equipment and filter-rack instructions for size, type, airflow direction, and permitted filtration range; shut down the blower using the Project 02 shutdown procedure; remove and inspect the old filter; measure the rack opening; select and install a compatible replacement filter in the correct orientation; seal unintended rack or door bypass gaps using an approved closure; and restore equipment operation while confirming normal airflow and panel seating.

Filter selection and pressure drop directly affect airflow, equipment life, and indoor air quality throughout the rest of this book. A filter that restricts airflow beyond the equipment's approved range can starve the coil, reduce comfort, and cause premature equipment failure; a rack with uncontrolled bypass gaps allows unfiltered air to reach the coil and ducts regardless of the filter installed. Higher filtration is not automatically better — the manufacturer's approved filtration range controls this project, not the filter with the highest advertised rating.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-013 — Identify filter size, including nominal size, actual size, thickness, and the manufacturer's approved filtration range.
  • HV-014 — Identify airflow direction from the filter's airflow arrow and the rack's return-to-blower orientation.
  • HV-015 — Remove old filters while controlling dust and containing biological contamination.
  • HV-016 — Install replacement filters in the correct orientation, fully seated and supported.
  • HV-017 — Seal filter-rack gaps using approved gasket or closure materials without blocking required access, drainage, or service openings.
  • HV-018 — Document the replacement schedule, including installation date, filter specification, and inspection interval.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on the equipment identification completed in Project 01 and the shutdown procedure established in Project 02. The filter rack identified and photographed in Project 01, and the blower shutdown steps documented in Project 02, are the starting references for this project; do not begin filter service on equipment that has not been identified and shut down using those procedures.

Filter condition also affects later projects. Chapter 4's grille and return-path cleaning, Chapter 5's duct and airflow diagnosis, and Chapter 11's room-airflow balancing all begin from the assumption that the filter rack has been serviced and the installed filter is within the equipment's approved range. A loaded, oversized, undersized, or bypassing filter will produce airflow symptoms that later chapters could otherwise misattribute to duct or balancing problems.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the equipment and exact filter location from the Project 01 system map
  • reviewing the equipment and filter-rack instructions for size, type, airflow direction, maximum resistance, and permitted filtration range
  • shutting down the blower using the Project 02 procedure and verifying it cannot start
  • removing, inspecting, and containing one used filter
  • measuring the rack opening and selecting a compatible replacement filter
  • installing the replacement filter in the correct orientation, fully seated and supported
  • sealing unintended rack or door bypass gaps using an approved gasket or closure
  • restoring equipment operation and confirming normal airflow and panel seating
  • documenting the installation date, filter specification, rack condition, and inspection schedule

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a filter with a higher filtration rating than the equipment manufacturer's approved range
  • seal a required access, drainage, or service opening
  • disassemble, remove, or service the blower assembly, motor, or internal electrical compartment
  • resize the filter rack, cut a new rack opening, or modify the equipment cabinet
  • continue operating equipment that shows filter movement, whistle, panel vibration, or an abnormal airflow fault after restoration

Any filter rack that cannot accept a correctly sized filter, shows structural damage, or requires cabinet modification is outside this project's scope and must be referred for licensed evaluation.

Trade Note

Filter selection is controlled by the equipment manufacturer's approved size and maximum pressure-drop rating, not by the filter with the highest advertised filtration efficiency. A higher-efficiency filter that exceeds the equipment's approved resistance restricts airflow across the coil, which can reduce comfort, freeze a cooling coil, or shorten blower-motor life. When the manufacturer's instructions state a maximum resistance or a specific filtration range, that value controls the filter selected in this project.

Setup Standard

Confirm the blower is shut down and cannot start using the Project 02 procedure before opening the filter rack. Do not seal a required access, drainage, or service opening while closing bypass gaps. Do not select a filter outside the equipment manufacturer's approved size and pressure-drop range.

Shop Tip

Measure the rack opening itself, not just the old filter's printed label. A previous installer may have fit an incorrectly sized filter, and relying on that label carries the error forward into this project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Filter service depends on matching three things: the rack's actual opening, the equipment's approved filtration range, and the airflow arrow's orientation. Getting any one of these wrong creates a bypass, a restriction, or a filter that will not stay seated.

Filtration Range, Pressure Drop, and Bypass Control

The equipment manufacturer's instructions establish the maximum airflow resistance the blower is designed to overcome. A filter with a higher filtration rating than the approved range increases resistance beyond that design limit, even though the filter itself is a legitimate product. This project selects the replacement filter from the manufacturer's approved range, not from the highest available filtration rating, and controls unintended bypass gaps without blocking required access, drainage, or service openings.

Measurement Cue — Measure the Rack Opening, Not Only the Old Filter

Measure the rack opening directly with the tape measure before selecting a replacement filter. Do not rely solely on the nominal size printed on the old filter's frame, since a previously installed filter may already have been the wrong size.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Approved Filtration Range Controls Selection

Select the replacement filter only from the type, thickness, and maximum pressure-drop rating identified in the equipment manufacturer's instructions. Do not install a higher-efficiency filter than the manufacturer's approved range solely because it is available or marketed as an upgrade.

Safety Cue — Contain Dust and Biological Material During Removal

Remove the old filter slowly and bag it before moving it through the occupied space. Do not shake or tap a loaded or microbially affected filter to dislodge debris. Wear the dust mask or respirator specified for the observed filter condition.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm the Blower Cannot Start Before Opening the Rack

Confirm the blower is shut down and cannot start using the Project 02 procedure before opening the filter-rack access panel or door. Do not reach into an open filter rack while the blower is capable of starting.

E. Preparation and Reference Review

  1. Identify the equipment and exact filter location from the system map. Confirm this matches the equipment identified and shut down in Project 02.
  2. Review the equipment and filter-rack instructions for size, type, airflow direction, maximum resistance, and permitted filtration range. Record the manufacturer's stated values on the filter service record before proceeding.

F. Shutdown and Access

  1. Shut down the blower and verify it cannot start. Use the Project 02 shutdown procedure for this equipment.
  2. Inspect the filter access for electrical hazards, sharp edges, water, biological contamination, and damaged panels. Stop and escalate any condition identified as a stop-work condition under Project 02.

G. Existing Filter Documentation and Removal

  1. Record the installed filter dimensions, nominal size, actual size, thickness, type, rating, and airflow arrow. Write these values on the filter service record before removing the filter.
  2. Confirm airflow direction from return toward the blower or equipment. Verify the printed arrow direction against the known return and equipment sides identified in Project 01.
  3. Photograph the existing filter orientation and rack condition. Capture the airflow arrow, rack rails, and door condition before disturbing anything.
  4. Remove the filter slowly while controlling dust. Support the filter with both hands and avoid dragging it across rack edges.
  5. Bag or contain the used filter without shaking it in the occupied space. Seal the used-filter bag before carrying the filter through occupied areas.
  6. Inspect the filter for collapse, bypass marks, heavy loading, moisture, microbial growth, or incorrect fit. Record findings on the filter service record.

H. Rack Service and Measurement

  1. Vacuum or wipe the accessible rack surfaces without disturbing internal equipment or hazardous material. Use the brush attachment on rails, tracks, and the door interior only.
  2. Inspect rack rails, door, gasket, clips, latches, and edges. Record any damaged or missing component that requires correction before the replacement filter is installed.
  3. Measure the rack opening rather than relying solely on the old filter label. Record the measured width, height, and depth on the filter service record.

I. Replacement Filter Selection and Installation

  1. Select a replacement filter compatible with the equipment's approved dimensions and pressure-drop limitations. Confirm the selected filter's nominal size, type, thickness, and rating match the manufacturer's approved range recorded in Step E2.
  2. Install the filter with the airflow arrow in the correct direction. Confirm the arrow points from the return side toward the blower or equipment before sliding the filter fully into the rack.
  3. Confirm that the filter is fully seated, supported, and accessible for removal. Check that the filter is not bowed, gapped at the edges, or resting against an unsupported span.
  4. Seal only unintended rack or door bypass gaps using the approved gasket or listed closure. Apply gasket tape or the listed closure strip only at gaps identified in Step H2.
  5. Do not seal required access, drainage, or service openings. Confirm the closure material does not cover a required access, drainage, or service opening before continuing.

J. Restoration and Documentation

  1. Reinstall and secure the filter door or access panel. Confirm all latches, clips, or fasteners are fully engaged.
  2. Restore equipment and listen for filter movement, whistle, panel vibration, or abnormal airflow. Observe the equipment through one complete authorized operating cycle.
  3. Stop work if the filter is pulled out of position, the blower sounds strained, or the equipment faults. Re-shut down the equipment using Project 02 before making any correction.
  4. Record installation date, filter specification, rack condition, and the manufacturer- or condition-based inspection schedule. Complete the filter service record before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed filter service using the same checks regardless of which residence or training system the project involves.

  • Size check: confirm the replacement filter matches the measured rack opening, not only the old filter's printed label.
  • Type check: confirm the replacement filter is within the equipment manufacturer's approved filtration range and maximum pressure-drop rating.
  • Airflow check: confirm the airflow arrow points from the return side toward the blower or equipment.
  • Rack check: confirm rails, door, gasket, clips, latches, and edges are clean and functional.
  • Bypass check: confirm unintended gaps are sealed and required access, drainage, and service openings remain unobstructed.
  • Restoration check: confirm the equipment operates without filter movement, whistle, panel vibration, or fault after restoration.
  • Documentation check: confirm the filter service record is complete with installation date, filter specification, and inspection schedule.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Filter Is Pulled Against the Rack or Whistles After Restoration

Probable cause: the installed filter is undersized for the rack opening, or the filter's pressure drop exceeds the equipment's approved range.
Confirmation: re-measure the rack opening and compare it to the installed filter's actual size; compare the filter's rating to the manufacturer's approved range.
Correction: shut down the equipment using Project 02, remove the filter, and install a filter matching the measured opening and approved range.
Reinspection: restore equipment and confirm no whistle, movement, or strain during a complete operating cycle.

The Filter Was Installed With the Airflow Arrow Reversed

Probable cause: the filter was installed without confirming the arrow direction against the known return and equipment sides.
Confirmation: compare the installed arrow direction to the return and equipment sides identified in the Project 01 system map.
Correction: shut down the equipment using Project 02, remove the filter, and reinstall it with the arrow pointing from return toward the blower or equipment.
Reinspection: confirm arrow orientation visually before closing the access panel.

Bypass Gaps Remain at the Rack Door After Sealing

Probable cause: the gasket tape or closure strip was applied at the wrong location, is the wrong width, or has failed to adhere.
Confirmation: visually inspect the rack door perimeter with the equipment off and compare against the gaps identified before sealing.
Correction: remove failed closure material, clean the surface, and reapply the approved gasket tape or closure strip at the identified gap only.
Reinspection: confirm no visible gap remains and that no required access, drainage, or service opening has been covered.

The Correct Replacement Filter Is Not Available at the Time of Service

Probable cause: the measured rack opening or approved filtration range does not match any filter on hand.
Confirmation: re-confirm the measured rack opening and the manufacturer's approved range against the available filter options.
Correction: do not install an incorrectly sized or out-of-range filter as a substitute; reinstall the existing filter temporarily only if it is not the cause of a stop-work condition, and obtain the correct replacement before completing this project.
Reinspection: complete Steps I1 through J4 once the correct replacement filter is on hand.

Field Terms

Nominal Size
The rounded filter dimension printed on the filter frame or rack, such as 16 x 25 x 1 inch.
Actual Size
The measured dimension of the filter or rack opening, typically smaller than the nominal size.
Airflow Arrow
The printed arrow on the filter frame indicating the required direction of air travel through the filter.
Approved Filtration Range
The filter type, thickness, and maximum pressure-drop rating identified in the equipment manufacturer's instructions as acceptable for that equipment.
Bypass Gap
Any unintended opening at the rack, door, or filter edge that allows air to bypass the filter media.
Rack Opening
The measured interior dimension of the filter rack that the replacement filter must fit and be supported by.
Pressure Drop
The airflow resistance created by a filter, measured across the filter under a defined airflow condition.
Filter Rack
The frame, rails, and door assembly that holds the filter in the return airstream ahead of the blower or equipment.

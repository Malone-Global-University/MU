Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install one listed insulated flexible-duct branch between an approved takeoff collar and a supply-register boot. The finished work product is one fully connected, sealed, supported, and airflow-tested flexible-duct branch installed to the exact diameter, length, route, support spacing, and closure system supplied by the approved design.

You will confirm the branch size and route from the approved design; mark support points and the shortest practical route; extend the duct to its intended length before cutting; cut the outer jacket, insulation, and inner liner without damaging the liner; connect and seal the inner liner at both the takeoff collar and the register boot; pull insulation over each sealed connection and close the outer jacket; support the duct at the required spacing without compressing the insulation; form long-radius bends while avoiding kinks and crushing; and inspect the completed branch under airflow.

This project builds directly on the duct system map from Project 1, the shutdown procedure from Project 2, the duct-sealing skills from Project 6, and the duct-insulation skills from Project 7. It supplies the flexible-branch method referenced by the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HV-055 — Lay out the duct branch using the approved diameter, length, route, and support points.
  • HV-056 — Cut flexible duct, including the outer jacket, insulation, and inner liner, without damaging the liner.
  • HV-057 — Connect the inner liner to the takeoff collar and register boot with the required engagement, clamp, and seal.
  • HV-058 — Secure the outer jacket over the sealed connection using the specified closure.
  • HV-059 — Support the duct without sagging, using the specified strap type and spacing.
  • HV-060 — Inspect the airflow path for collapse, leakage, excessive sag, and register delivery.

Where This Skill Fits

Flexible-duct branch installation connects the duct-sealing skills of Project 6 and the duct-insulation skills of Project 7 to a single completed supply run, using the register-boot installation from Project 8 as its termination point. The same cut, connect, seal, jacket, and support sequence taught here is the sequence used for every flexible branch built in the Chapter 20 capstone practice system.

This project depends on the approved design supplying the branch diameter, length, route, bend limits, support spacing, and closure system. It does not authorize sizing or rerouting a branch based on comfort symptoms alone; that determination belongs to the approved design or a qualified professional.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the approved duct diameter, length, route, takeoff, boot, bend limits, support spacing, insulation, and closure system before beginning
  • marking support points and maintaining the shortest practical approved route
  • cutting the flexible duct's outer jacket, insulation, and inner liner without damaging the liner
  • connecting, sealing, and clamping the inner liner at the takeoff collar and register boot
  • pulling insulation over each sealed connection and securing and sealing the outer jacket
  • supporting the duct at the required spacing using wide approved straps
  • forming long-radius bends and avoiding kinks, crushing, and contact with damaging surfaces
  • inspecting the completed branch under airflow for collapse, leakage, movement, vibration, and excessive sag

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select or change the branch diameter, length, or route without the approved design
  • resize equipment, ductwork, or perform code-level system design calculations
  • cut return openings or connect prohibited spaces
  • use household duct tape or unlisted closures on any duct connection
  • exceed the manufacturer's maximum support spacing or bend limits

Any concealed utility, structural restriction, moisture damage, or unknown material found during layout must be recorded and escalated before cutting or fastening continues.

Trade Note

Flexible duct fails most often at its connections, not along its length. A liner that is not fully engaged on the collar, a clamp that is not tightened to the correct torque or position, or a seal applied to a dusty surface will leak long before the insulation or jacket shows any sign of a problem. Treat every connection as the point that determines whether the branch performs.

Setup Standard

Do not size or reroute the branch without the approved design. Shut down equipment before beginning. Confirm the diameter, length, route, takeoff, boot, bend limits, support spacing, insulation, and closure system before cutting any material.

Shop Tip

Extend the flexible duct fully to its working length before marking the final cut. A duct measured while still compressed will read short once extended, producing a branch that is too tight at the connections and prone to kinking at the first bend.

Common Field Fundamentals

A flexible-duct branch performs only as well as its weakest connection and its worst-supported span. This project treats the layout, the cut, the two liner connections, the jacket closure, and the support spacing as five separate checkpoints, each with its own pass condition.

Design Authority and Sizing

The branch diameter, length, route, bend limits, support spacing, and closure system must come from the approved design. Do not select or change any of these values based on comfort symptoms, convenience of routing, or leftover material. If the approved design is incomplete or does not match field conditions, stop and obtain a corrected design before cutting any duct.

Measurement Cue — Extend Before You Cut

Extend the flexible duct to its full intended length before marking or cutting. A duct cut while compressed will be too short once installed, forcing tight connections and increasing the risk of kinking at the first bend.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Clamp Engagement and Torque

Slide the inner liner fully onto the takeoff collar or boot collar to the manufacturer-specified engagement length before clamping. Tighten the listed clamp or draw band to the manufacturer-specified position or torque. An under-engaged or under-tightened connection is the most common point of branch failure.

Critical Safety Cue — Shutdown Before Cutting or Connecting

Shut down the equipment using the Project 2 procedure and verify the blower cannot start before cutting duct, connecting to a takeoff collar, or working inside a register boot. Do not perform this work with the blower operating or capable of unexpected startup.

Field Safety Cue — Route Inspection Before Cutting

Inspect the approved route for sharp edges, heat sources, moisture, structural restrictions, and other utilities before cutting or routing the duct. Stop work if concealed wiring, plumbing, or structural members are found in the marked route and were not identified in the approved design.

G. Preparation, Design Confirmation, and Access

  1. Review the approved duct diameter, length, route, takeoff, boot, bend limits, support spacing, insulation, and closure system. Record each value on the Branch Specification Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm that the branch size and route are provided by the approved design. Do not proceed if any required value is missing; obtain the corrected design first.
  3. Shut down equipment and verify safe access. Use the Project 2 procedure and confirm the blower cannot start.
  4. Inspect the route for sharp edges, heat sources, moisture, structural restrictions, and other utilities. Stop work if an unidentified condition is found and it was not accounted for in the approved design.

H. Layout and Support Marking

  1. Mark support points and maintain the shortest practical approved route. Use the marker to identify each support location along the route.
  2. Measure the installed path without adding unnecessary excess length. Record the measured path length against the approved maximum length.

I. Cutting the Flexible Duct

  1. Extend the flexible duct to its intended length before final cutting. Stretch the duct fully along the marked route or on a flat surface of equivalent length.
  2. Cut the outer jacket and insulation without damaging the inner liner. Use the duct-rated shears or knife and cut only through the outer layers at the marked point.
  3. Cut the inner liner square using the approved tool. Make a clean, square cut so the liner engages the collar or boot fully around its circumference.

J. Inner-Liner Connection at the Takeoff Collar

  1. Slide the inner liner fully over the takeoff collar to the required engagement. Confirm the liner reaches the manufacturer-specified engagement point before clamping.
  2. Secure the liner with the listed clamp, draw band, or fastening system. Position and tighten the clamp per the manufacturer instructions in the Fastener and Hardware Schedule.
  3. Seal the inner-liner connection using approved mastic or listed tape. Apply the mastic or tape completely around the connection with no gaps.

K. Jacket Closure at the Takeoff Collar

  1. Pull insulation over the sealed connection without gaps. Confirm the insulation fully covers the sealed liner joint.
  2. Secure and seal the outer jacket using the specified closure. Apply the manufacturer-specified closure method to maintain a continuous vapor facing over the connection.

L. Repeat Connection at the Register Boot

  1. Repeat the connection at the register boot. Perform the same liner engagement, clamping, sealing, insulation coverage, and jacket closure steps described in Sections J and K at the register-boot end of the branch.

M. Support, Bends, and Route Protection

  1. Support the duct using wide approved straps that do not compress the insulation. Install straps at each marked support point.
  2. Maintain the specified support spacing and maximum sag. Verify spacing against the approved design or manufacturer maximum before continuing.
  3. Form long-radius bends and avoid kinks, crushing, sharp offsets, and contact with damaging surfaces. Adjust the route at each direction change to preserve the duct's full internal diameter.
  4. Keep the duct off the ground and away from water or pest exposure. Confirm supports hold the duct clear of any wet or contaminated surface along the route.

N. Restoration, Airflow Test, and Correction

  1. Restore equipment. Reverse the Project 2 shutdown procedure and confirm normal startup.
  2. Inspect the full branch under airflow for collapse, movement, leakage, vibration, and excessive sag. Walk the full route while the system operates and note any deficiency.
  3. Verify register airflow using the approved comparison or measurement method. Confirm delivery at the register matches the expected result for the approved design.
  4. Correct support, bend, or connection failures. Re-isolate power or shut down equipment before making any correction, then repeat the affected steps.
  5. Document route, length, supports, closures, and inspection results. Complete the worksheet before considering the branch finished.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed flexible-duct branch using the same checks regardless of which register or route the project involves.

  • Design check: confirm the installed diameter, length, and route match the approved design exactly.
  • Liner-connection check: confirm the inner liner is fully engaged, clamped, and sealed at both the takeoff collar and the register boot.
  • Jacket-closure check: confirm the outer jacket is secured and sealed continuously over both sealed connections.
  • Support check: confirm supports are installed at the specified spacing without compressing insulation and without exceeding maximum sag.
  • Bend check: confirm all bends are long-radius with no kinks, crushing, or sharp offsets.
  • Airflow check: confirm the branch shows no collapse, leakage, excessive vibration, or register underperformance during the authorized test.
  • Documentation check: confirm the worksheet records route, length, supports, closures, and inspection results.

Complete the Flexible Duct Branch Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 11.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Branch Delivers Noticeably Less Airflow Than Expected

Probable cause: a kinked or crushed section, an undersized bend radius, or excess installed length beyond the approved design.
Confirmation: walk the full route under operation and inspect for visible kinks, crushing, or excess sag between supports.
Correction: shut down equipment, straighten or re-support the affected section, and remove excess length if it exceeds the approved design.
Reinspection: restore equipment and repeat the register airflow verification.

Air Noise or Whistle Is Heard Near a Connection

Probable cause: incomplete liner engagement on the collar or boot, an under-tightened clamp, or a gap in the mastic or tape seal.
Confirmation: shut down equipment and inspect the connection visually and by hand for looseness or a visible gap.
Correction: re-engage the liner to the full manufacturer-specified depth, re-tighten the clamp, and reapply mastic or tape to close the gap.
Reinspection: restore equipment and confirm the noise no longer occurs at the corrected connection.

The Duct Sags Beyond the Manufacturer-Specified Limit Between Supports

Probable cause: support spacing that exceeds the approved design or manufacturer maximum, or a strap that has slipped or loosened.
Confirmation: measure the sag against the manufacturer-specified maximum at the affected span.
Correction: add or reposition support straps to bring the span within the specified spacing.
Reinspection: re-measure sag at the corrected span and confirm it meets the standard.

The Duct Visibly Collapses or Narrows Under Airflow

Probable cause: a kink, a support strap installed too tightly and compressing the liner, or a bend radius smaller than the manufacturer minimum.
Confirmation: shut down equipment and inspect the affected section for compression marks, kinking, or an undersized bend.
Correction: loosen or replace the offending strap, reform the bend to the required radius, or replace the damaged section of duct.
Reinspection: restore equipment and observe the corrected section under operation for continued collapse.

Field Conditions Do Not Match the Approved Design

Probable cause: an obstruction, utility, or dimension in the field that was not accounted for in the approved design.
Confirmation: compare the actual route and dimensions against the approved design at the specific point of disagreement.
Correction: stop work and obtain a corrected design before continuing; do not improvise a route, diameter, or length not supplied by the design.
Reinspection: resume installation only after the corrected design has been obtained and reviewed.

Cleanup

This project ends with the branch installed, sealed, supported, tested, and the work area restored, not left with exposed cut ends, loose material, or unsecured access panels.

  • Confirm all mastic, tape, and closure materials have cured per manufacturer instructions before concealment
  • Confirm every access panel, attic hatch, or crawlspace cover opened for the installation is closed and secured
  • Remove cut-off duct scraps, packaging, and unused fasteners from the work area
  • Store unused flexible duct, clamps, mastic, and straps in a protected, undamaged condition
  • Store the Flexible Duct Branch Worksheet for reference by later projects and the Chapter 20 capstone

Field Terms

Takeoff Collar
The fitting on the supply plenum or trunk to which the flexible duct's inner liner is connected.
Inner Liner
The airtight interior duct surface of the flexible duct through which air travels.
Outer Jacket
The exterior vapor-facing layer covering the flexible duct's insulation.
Extended Length
The duct's length when fully stretched to its intended installed condition, measured before final cutting.
Support Spacing
The maximum distance between wide approved straps, as specified by the approved design or manufacturer instructions.
Long-Radius Bend
A gradual change in direction that does not reduce the duct's internal diameter or crease the inner liner.
Draw Band
A listed mechanical fastening strap used to secure the inner liner or outer jacket to a collar.
Sag
The downward deflection of an installed duct between two support points.

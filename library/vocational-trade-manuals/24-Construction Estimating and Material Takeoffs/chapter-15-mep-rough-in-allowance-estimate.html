Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build one transparent MEP allowance worksheet for the plumbing, electrical, lighting, and HVAC rough-in scope of a residential estimate. The finished work product is one worksheet organized by system, showing fixture and device counts, documented unit bases, qualified subcontractor budgets where available, explicit allowance amounts, stated inclusions and exclusions, a risk register, and a conversion checklist for replacing each allowance with a fixed price once complete trade design and pricing exist.

You will confirm the pricing purpose of the worksheet, count plumbing fixtures, electrical devices, lighting fixtures, and HVAC equipment directly from the plan set, request and level qualified subcontractor budgets, apply documented unit allowances only where design or pricing is incomplete, separate fixture and equipment allowances from installation and rough-in scope, and build a risk register and conversion checklist before the worksheet is entered into the master workbook.

This project depends on the workbook built in Chapter 1 and the scope review completed in Chapter 2. It supplies the MEP allowance section referenced in Chapter 17's labor estimate for self-performed work, Chapter 18's pricing and markup procedure, and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate, where this worksheet becomes the MEP section of the complete residential estimate.

  • ET-086 — Count electrical devices by type, location, tag, and dedicated-circuit need from the approved plan set.

  • ET-089 — Identify specialty trade scope requiring qualified subcontractor pricing rather than an estimator-built allowance.
  • ET-090 — Prepare an MEP allowance worksheet with counts, unit bases, subcontractor input, exclusions, risk, and a conversion checklist.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 3 through 14 measured and priced scope the estimator can size directly from plans and specifications. MEP rough-in is different: plumbing, electrical, and HVAC design is frequently incomplete at the time of a residential estimate, and pricing this scope from device counts alone risks presenting a rough number as a firm price. This chapter builds the discipline of counting what is shown, pricing what is quoted, and clearly labeling everything else as an allowance with a stated basis and an explicit trigger for conversion to a fixed price.

The worksheet produced here enters the "MEP" cost-code section of the Chapter 1 workbook. Chapter 17 excludes MEP labor unless the estimator is self-performing a specific task; most MEP labor rides inside the subcontractor or allowance figures built here. Chapter 18 applies waste, tax, escalation, and markup to this worksheet using the same rules as every other trade. Chapter 20 reconciles this worksheet against the final subcontractor proposals before the estimate is frozen.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • counting plumbing fixtures, electrical devices, lighting fixtures, and HVAC equipment directly from the approved plan set
  • separating fixture or equipment allowance from rough-in, trim, installation, testing, and permit scope
  • requesting and leveling qualified subcontractor budgets using common documents, scope, schedule, and assumptions
  • applying documented unit allowances only to items not sufficiently designed or quoted
  • building an MEP-specific risk register and a conversion checklist for replacing allowances with fixed prices
  • preparing the worksheet by system, count, basis, material, labor, subcontract, fee, allowance, and total

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • infer circuit design, wire size, panel capacity, pipe sizing, duct sizing, or code-required equipment capacity from a device or fixture count
  • design plumbing, electrical, or HVAC systems, or substitute an estimator judgment call for a licensed trade design
  • present an MEP allowance as a fixed installed price without the conversion steps in Section I
  • combine a fixture or equipment allowance with installation cost in a single unlabeled figure

Any MEP line that cannot be supported by a plan count, a qualified subcontractor budget, or a documented unit allowance must be logged as an open question and excluded from the total until resolved.

Trade Note

An allowance is not a guess. It is a documented placeholder with a stated unit basis, an included-scope statement, a source, and a date, used only where complete design or qualified pricing does not yet exist. A number without those four elements is not an allowance — it is an unverified figure and must be treated that way until corrected.

Common Field Fundamentals

An MEP allowance worksheet is built system by system — plumbing, electrical, lighting, low-voltage, HVAC, ventilation, controls, fire, and specialty — with each system separated so that a gap or duplication in one system cannot hide inside another. Every count traces to a plan tag or location. Every allowance carries a unit basis, an included-scope statement, a source, and a date. Every subcontractor budget is leveled against the same documents and scope before it is compared or accepted.

Counts Are Not Design

Counting a receptacle, a light fixture, or a supply register from a plan tells the estimator how many devices exist. It does not tell the estimator the circuit it belongs to, the wire size feeding it, the panel capacity available, or the duct size required to serve it. Do not infer circuit design, wire size, panel capacity, or code requirements from device counts. Where a plan does not show this information, treat the routing and sizing as unresolved and carry it as a labeled conceptual basis or a subcontractor allowance, never as an estimator-designed value.

Traceability Cue — Tag Every Count

Record the plan sheet, tag, and location for every plumbing fixture, electrical device, lighting fixture, and HVAC component counted. A count with no recorded plan reference cannot be reconciled against a revised plan set and must be treated as unverified until a reference is added.

Critical Control Cue — Label Every Allowance as an Allowance

Every allowance figure in this worksheet must be visibly labeled as an allowance, never blended into a fixed-scope total. A client or reviewer who cannot distinguish a fixed price from an allowance will treat the entire MEP total as guaranteed, which this chapter does not authorize.

Code / Design Alert — Do Not Design the System

This chapter does not authorize circuit design, panel sizing, pipe sizing, duct sizing, or code compliance determinations. Where the plans do not provide this information, request it from a licensed design professional or a qualified subcontractor, and carry the item as an allowance until resolved.

Trade Coordination Cue — Level Before Comparing

Confirm every subcontractor budget responds to the same plan set, scope narrative, and assumption list before comparing figures. A lower budget built on a smaller scope is not a lower price; it is an unresolved scope gap that must be closed before comparison.

E. Preparation — Documents, Purpose, and Trade Boundaries

  1. Review plumbing, electrical, lighting, architectural, structural, ceiling, cabinet, equipment, and appliance documents. Confirm the current revision of each against the Chapter 1 document register.
  2. Confirm whether pricing is an allowance, conceptual budget, unit-price model, subcontract estimate, or fixed proposal. Record the confirmation per Section C.
  3. State that allowances do not replace complete trade design, code review, calculations, permits, or qualified pricing. Record this statement on the worksheet header.
  4. Create separate plumbing, electrical, lighting, low-voltage, HVAC, ventilation, controls, fire, and specialty sections. Use the nine sections listed in Section D.

F. Fixture, Device, and Equipment Count Procedure

  1. Count plumbing fixtures by type, location, tag, and responsibility. Record each on the fixture and device count sheet.
  2. Count sinks, faucets, toilets, tubs, showers, valves, drains, hose bibbs, appliances, water heaters, pumps, and specialty fixtures. Tag each to its plan location.
  3. Distinguish fixture allowance from rough-in, trim, supply, setting, testing, and permit scope. Enter each as a separate line so the two costs are never combined.
  4. Measure visible routing only where documents provide a suitable basis. Do not extend a routing measurement beyond what the documents actually show.
  5. Identify trenching, drilling, firestopping, access panels, insulation, pads, and restoration boundaries. Record which party is responsible for each.
  6. Count receptacles, switches, dimmers, controls, detectors, panels, disconnects, appliances, and dedicated circuits. Tag each to its plan location.
  7. Count light fixtures by type and distinguish fixture allowance from installation. Enter each as a separate line.
  8. Identify service, feeder, generator, EV, solar, low-voltage, security, data, and specialty scope. Flag each for subcontractor pricing rather than an estimator-built allowance.
  9. Do not infer circuit design, wire size, panel capacity, or code requirements from device counts. Carry unresolved sizing as a subcontractor allowance per the Code / Design Alert above.
  10. Count HVAC equipment, thermostats, supplies, returns, exhaust fans, ventilation equipment, dampers, and controls. Tag each to its plan location or equipment schedule.
  11. Measure duct or line routes only as a labeled conceptual basis when design is incomplete. Mark every conceptual routing line clearly as conceptual, not measured.
  12. Identify condensate, refrigerant, gas, electrical, curb, venting, penetration, firestop, and startup scope. Record which trade or allowance carries each item.
  13. Identify structural, envelope, cabinet, roofing, ceiling, and finish coordination. Flag any conflict for the clarification log established in Chapter 2.

G. Subcontractor Coordination, Allowance Pricing, and Worksheet Assembly

  1. Request qualified subcontractor budgets using common documents, scope, schedule, assumptions, and return format. Send the identical package to every subcontractor solicited for a given system.
  2. Level proposals for permits, design, equipment, fixtures, labor, material, controls, testing, tax, and exclusions. Reconcile each proposal against the same checklist before comparison.
  3. Use documented unit allowances only for items not sufficiently designed or quoted. Do not substitute a unit allowance for a system that already has a qualified subcontractor budget.
  4. Record unit basis, included scope, price date, geographic source, escalation, and confidence. Complete this record for every allowance line before it is entered into the worksheet.
  5. Separate owner-selected fixture or equipment allowance from installation and rough-in. Confirm the separation matches Section F, Step 3.
  6. Identify code-driven or utility-driven costs that remain unresolved. Log each as an open clarification rather than estimating a value.
  7. Include mobilization, supervision, layout, supports, firestopping, testing, commissioning, cleanup, and punch where assigned. Confirm which party carries each cost.
  8. Include permit, inspection, utility, connection, meter, design, and engineering fees only when known or clearly allowed. Do not estimate a fee without a documented source.
  9. Identify long-lead equipment, procurement risk, and deposits. Record lead time and deposit terms from each subcontractor's or supplier's quotation.
  10. Do not combine fixed scope with uncertain allowance without separate lines. Confirm every worksheet row is labeled Fixed, Budget, or Allowance per Section C.
  11. Prepare a conversion checklist for replacing allowances with fixed prices. State the specific design deliverable, subcontractor proposal, or quotation required for each allowance line.
  12. Create an MEP risk register for capacity, routing, utility work, selections, availability, and coordination. Assign each risk a response: clarify, exclude, allow, alternate, unit price, contingency, transfer, or accept.
  13. Prepare the worksheet by system, count, basis, material, labor, subcontract, fee, allowance, and total. Use the nine system sections from Section D and the row structure from Section B.

H. Documentation and Independent Review

  1. Document exclusions and trade boundaries. State plainly what each system section does and does not include.
  2. Complete independent review of counts, proposal leveling, duplication, and allowance clarity. Have a second reviewer trace at least one line in each system back to its plan tag, subcontractor budget, or documented unit basis.

I. Closeout — Entry Into the Master Workbook

  1. Enter the completed worksheet into the MEP cost-code section of the master workbook. Confirm every row carries a source, a confidence label, and a value that matches the worksheet.
  2. File the fixture and device count sheet, subcontractor budget requests and responses, allowance documentation, risk register, and conversion checklist in the estimating record. Log the entry in the workbook's revision log with date, person, and reason.

Common Worksheet Inspection

Inspect the finished worksheet using the same checks regardless of which system section is being reviewed.

  • Count check: confirm every plumbing fixture, electrical device, lighting fixture, and HVAC component counted carries a plan sheet reference and tag.
  • Boundary check: confirm fixture or equipment allowance is never combined with rough-in, trim, or installation cost in a single line.
  • Confidence check: confirm every row is labeled Fixed, Budget, or Allowance and that the label matches its actual basis.
  • Leveling check: confirm every subcontractor budget compared on the worksheet responded to the same documents, scope, and assumptions.
  • Design-boundary check: confirm no circuit, wire size, pipe size, duct size, or code requirement was inferred from a device count.
  • Documentation check: confirm the risk register and conversion checklist are complete and every allowance shows a unit basis, source, and date.

Complete the MEP Worksheet Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 16.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Allowance Line Has No Recorded Unit Basis

Probable cause: the allowance was entered from memory or a rough figure before the unit basis, included scope, source, and date were documented.
Confirmation: review the allowance line against the Section G, Step 4 requirement and confirm each of the five required fields is present.
Correction: research and document the missing fields, or mark the line as unverified and exclude it from the worksheet total until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the Documentation Check from the Common Worksheet Inspection section.

Two Subcontractor Budgets Cannot Be Compared

Probable cause: the budgets were requested without a common scope package, or one subcontractor priced against an earlier plan revision.
Confirmation: compare each subcontractor's stated scope, exclusions, and plan revision against the request package sent in Section G, Step 1.
Correction: reissue the common scope package to both subcontractors and request a revised budget before any comparison or selection.
Reinspection: repeat the Leveling Check with both revised budgets.

A Fixture Allowance Was Combined With Its Rough-In Cost

Probable cause: the two costs were entered as a single line during initial data entry.
Confirmation: the Boundary Check in the Common Worksheet Inspection section identifies the combined line.
Correction: split the line into a fixture allowance row and a rough-in row, each with its own basis and source.
Reinspection: repeat the Boundary Check on the corrected rows.

A Device Count Was Used to Infer Circuit or Panel Capacity

Probable cause: the estimator extended a count into a design judgment without a licensed design source.
Confirmation: the Design-Boundary Check identifies any wire size, circuit assignment, or capacity figure without a documented design source.
Correction: remove the inferred value and replace it with a documented design source, a subcontractor budget, or a logged clarification.
Reinspection: repeat the Design-Boundary Check after correction.

A Count Cannot Be Traced to a Plan Reference

Probable cause: the count sheet was completed from memory or a walkthrough note without recording the plan sheet and tag.
Confirmation: the Count Check identifies rows without a plan sheet reference.
Correction: return to the plan set and confirm the correct sheet and tag for each affected row.
Reinspection: repeat the Count Check on the corrected rows.

A Fixed-Scope Row and an Allowance Row Were Blended Into One Total

Probable cause: a subcontractor budget and an estimator allowance for the same system were summed into a single unlabeled figure.
Confirmation: the Confidence Check identifies any row whose label does not match its actual basis.
Correction: separate the row into its Fixed and Allowance components with independent labels and sources.
Reinspection: repeat the Confidence Check on the corrected rows.

Field Terms

Fixed Scope

Fixture Allowance

Rough-In

Conceptual Routing

Leveling
Adjusting or comparing subcontractor proposals against a common scope, document set, and assumption list before evaluation.
Conversion Checklist
The list of design deliverables or proposals required to replace an allowance with a fixed price.

Dedicated Circuit
An electrical circuit serving a single device or appliance, identified by the plans or a qualified electrical design.
Risk Register
A record of unresolved capacity, routing, utility, selection, and coordination risks and their assigned responses.

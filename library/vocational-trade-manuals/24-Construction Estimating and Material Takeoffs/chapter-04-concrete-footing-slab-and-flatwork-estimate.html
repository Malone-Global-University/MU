Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a complete concrete cost worksheet for a residential foundation and flatwork package. The finished work product is one populated concrete cost-code section inside your Chapter 1 master workbook, containing separated footing, wall, pier, slab, step, and flatwork volumes, form materials, reinforcement, vapor barrier, joint and accessory quantities, placement and finishing labor, testing, and a reconciled, priced, and documented worksheet.

You will review foundation plans and structural notes, separate every concrete element, calculate volumes by an approved method and reconcile them by an independent method, apply placement waste, convert volume into supplier order units, take off forms, reinforcement, vapor barrier, and accessories, estimate placement and finishing labor, price every material and labor line from dated sources, and complete an independent review before the worksheet is considered ready for Chapter 18 pricing.

This is the fourth project in the book. It depends on the workbook built in Chapter 1 and the scope review completed in Chapter 2, and it feeds directly into the framing takeoff in Chapter 5, the labor estimate in Chapter 17, the pricing chapter in Chapter 18, and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-019 — Calculate concrete volume for footings, walls, piers, slabs, steps, and flatwork using approved geometry.
  • ET-020 — Estimate form materials for footings, walls, slab edges, curbs, steps, and blockouts.
  • ET-021 — Estimate reinforcement, including reinforcing bar, welded wire, fibers, and accessories.
  • ET-022 — Estimate vapor barrier by area, laps, turn-ups, penetrations, and waste.
  • ET-023 — Calculate waste factors specific to concrete placement, access, and pump priming.
  • ET-024 — Prepare a concrete cost worksheet organized by element, placement, quantity, unit cost, labor, equipment, and total.

Where This Skill Fits

This project uses the workbook structure, cost codes, and formula conventions built in Chapter 1 and the reviewed scope, exclusions, and clarifications produced in Chapter 2. It follows the sitework and excavation takeoff in Chapter 3, which establishes the subgrade, fill, and access conditions that concrete placement depends on.

The framing lumber takeoff in Chapter 5 depends on the foundation and slab geometry confirmed here. The labor estimate in Chapter 17 pulls its concrete task list directly from the placement, form, and finishing entries built in this chapter. The pricing chapter in Chapter 18 applies current unit costs to the quantities produced here without recalculating them. The Chapter 20 capstone estimate opens this exact worksheet structure and repeats the same reconciliation and review discipline for the full residential foundation package.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • separating footings, walls, piers, pads, slabs, thickened edges, steps, porches, walks, and patios into distinct takeoff elements
  • calculating volume for each element by an approved method and reconciling volume by an independent method
  • applying placement waste and converting volume into supplier order units, including short-load identification
  • taking off form materials, reinforcement, welded wire, fibers, accessories, vapor barrier, base, joints, and accessory hardware
  • estimating form, reinforcing, placement, finishing, setup, cleanup, and stripping labor
  • pricing ready-mix, reinforcement, forms, vapor barrier, base, insulation, accessories, delivery, and short-load costs from dated sources
  • preparing a worksheet by element, placement, quantity, unit cost, labor, equipment, and total, with documented assumptions and exclusions

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent concrete strength, exposure class, air content, admixture, color, fiber, or finish requirements not supplied by the structural notes or specifications
  • invent reinforcing bar size, spacing, lap length, cover, or hook dimensions not supplied by the structural schedule or approved detail
  • invent waste percentages, short-load charges, delivery fees, or unit prices not supplied by a dated quotation or company policy
  • design a missing footing, wall, slab, or joint detail; unresolved geometry must be converted into a clarification, assumption, or exclusion per Chapter 2
  • apply structural engineering judgment beyond confirming that the approved documents are read together correctly

Any element without a confirmed source for its geometry, strength, or reinforcement must be listed on the assumptions, exclusions, and clarifications register before pricing proceeds.

Trade Note

Concrete is priced by the cubic yard, but it is estimated element by element. A single combined volume for "foundation concrete" hides waste, short-load risk, and placement-labor differences between footings, walls, and slabs. Keep every element on its own line until the cost-code summary rolls them up.

Estimating Standard

Do not price a concrete element until its volume has been reconciled by an independent method (Step 5) and its reinforcement, forms, and accessories have been separately taken off. A volume that has not been reconciled must be marked unverified and excluded from the priced worksheet total until it is corrected.

Shop Tip

Calculate every element's volume twice, using two different approved methods, before entering it into the workbook. A footing volume calculated once and never checked is the most common source of a concrete estimate that is wrong by a full truckload.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every concrete element in a residential foundation and flatwork package is measured, reconciled, and priced using the same sequence: separate the element, confirm its geometry and reinforcement from the structural documents, calculate volume by one method, reconcile it by a second method, apply placement waste, convert to order volume, take off forms and reinforcement, and price from a dated quotation. Applying this same sequence to every element, from a small pier to the largest slab, is what keeps the worksheet defensible.

Reading Foundation Documents Together

Read the foundation plan, structural notes, sections, schedules, and details together before calculating any volume. A footing width shown on the plan may be overridden by a schedule note; a wall thickness shown in a section may not match a generic detail. Do not scale a drawing when a written dimension, schedule, or approved clarification controls, per the book-level authoring rule.

Critical Control Cue — Reconcile Every Volume by an Independent Method

Calculate each element's volume by the approved primary method, then recalculate it by a second method before entering it into the workbook. An unreconciled volume that is priced and carried into Chapter 18 will silently overstate or understate every downstream total, including the material cost, the labor hours tied to that quantity, and the final bid.

Traceability Cue — Record the Governing Document for Every Element

Record the specific sheet, section, schedule, or detail supporting each element's dimensions, strength, and reinforcement at the moment the element is entered. Do not calculate a volume first and locate its source afterward.

Estimating Caution — Do Not Combine Elements With Different Strength or Finish

Keep footings, walls, slabs, and flatwork on separate lines even when they will be placed on the same day. Combining elements with different strength, exposure, or finish requirements into one volume line hides the correct unit price for each and makes the waste calculation unreliable.

Company Policy Alert — Confirm the Waste Basis

Company waste percentages for footings, walls, and slabs are not universal. Confirm the company's approved waste percentage for each element type, based on size, access, pump priming, geometry, and history, before entering a waste value; do not reuse a percentage from a different project without confirming it still applies.

Supplier Cue — Confirm the Short-Load Threshold

Confirm the ready-mix supplier's full-load threshold and short-load charge before converting order volume into a delivery plan. An order volume below the threshold must show the short-load charge as a separate priced line, not absorbed into the unit cost.

C. Volume, Waste, and Order-Quantity Basis

  • Approved volume methods: calculate each element's net volume using length × width × depth for rectangular elements, or the approved plan, section, or schedule dimensions for irregular elements; do not estimate a volume without a documented method.
  • Independent reconciliation: recalculate every element's volume using a second approved method (a different dimension source, a unit-quantity check such as square-foot-per-cubic-yard coverage at the specified thickness, or an independent takeoff) before entering the volume into the workbook.
  • Placement waste: apply the company-approved waste percentage for each element type based on element size, access, pump priming, geometry, and placement history; do not apply one waste percentage to every element.
  • Order-volume conversion: convert net volume plus waste into the supplier's cubic-yard order unit, and identify any order below the supplier's full-load threshold as a short load.

E. Preparation — Documents, Elements, and Strength Basis

  1. Review foundation plans, structural notes, sections, schedules, details, site elevations, specifications, and geotechnical information. Confirm the controlling revision using the Chapter 2 document register.
  2. Separate footings, walls, piers, pads, slabs, thickened edges, steps, porches, walks, patios, and other elements. Give each its own row group in the concrete cost-code section.
  3. Record concrete strength, exposure, air, admixture, color, fiber, and finish requirements. Enter these on the Concrete Element and Strength Record in the Concrete Estimating Conventions section above, using the exact values stated in the structural notes or specifications.
  4. Calculate continuous, isolated, pier, wall, slab, step, curb, and irregular volumes separately. Use the approved volume method for each element type from Section C.
  5. Reconcile volume using an independent method. Enter the reconciled volume in the workbook and confirm it matches the net volume within the company-approved tolerance before proceeding.
  6. Apply approved placement waste based on element size, access, pump priming, geometry, and history. Record the waste percentage and its basis for each element.

F. Takeoff Procedure — Volume, Forms, Reinforcement, Vapor Barrier, Joints

  1. Convert volume into supplier order units and identify short-load charges. Compare each element's order volume against the supplier's full-load threshold.
  2. Identify separate placements, joints, mobilizations, and sequencing. Record each distinct pour as its own placement entry, even when elements share a cost-code row group.
  3. Measure footing, wall, slab-edge, curb, step, and blockout forms. Record form area or length by element.
  4. Differentiate reusable forms, stay-in-place forms, lumber, plywood, stakes, ties, braces, release agent, and stripping. Price each category separately using the dated quotation.
  5. Calculate reinforcing bar by size, spacing, length, laps, hooks, dowels, waste, and stock length. Use only the sizes, spacings, and lap lengths stated in the structural reinforcing schedule.
  6. Count welded wire, fibers, chairs, supports, tie wire, anchor bolts, embeds, sleeves, blockouts, and waterstops. Record each as a separate accessory line.
  7. Calculate vapor barrier by area, laps, turn-ups, penetrations, roll size, and waste. Apply only where the specifications call for a vapor barrier; do not add an unspecified vapor barrier.
  8. Calculate aggregate base, insulation, termite treatment, and underslab preparation where assigned. Confirm assignment against the Chapter 2 scope boundaries before including these lines.
  9. Measure expansion, isolation, control-joint, saw-cut, and sealant requirements. Record joint layout, spacing, and material per the specifications or approved detail.
  10. Identify pumping, conveyor, buggy, chute, crane, or direct-discharge needs for each placement. Record the placement method and any related equipment cost.
  11. Include access, washout, curing, blankets, weather protection, heat, and temporary enclosure. Record these as separate priced lines tied to the relevant placement.

G. Labor, Testing, and Pricing Procedure

  1. Estimate subgrade preparation, fine grading, and compaction where part of scope. Confirm this scope boundary against the Chapter 3 sitework takeoff before duplicating any quantity.
  2. Estimate form, reinforcing, placement, finishing, setup, cleanup, and stripping labor. Assign each task a quantity, crew, and production-rate source per the Chapter 17 labor-estimating conventions where available; otherwise leave the rate field blank pending Chapter 17.
  3. Adjust productivity for congestion, access, elevation, finish class, weather, and number of pours. Record the adjustment and its basis for each affected task.
  4. Include testing, cylinders, survey, inspection, and special inspection. Record these only where required by the specifications or adopted requirements.
  5. Price ready-mix, reinforcement, forms, vapor barrier, base, insulation, accessories, delivery, fuel, and short-load costs. Use only dated supplier quotations confirming product, strength, and delivery terms.
  6. Identify waiting time, cancellation, minimum-order, and price-validity terms. Record each term from the supplier quotation on the pricing log.
  7. Include curing, joint cutting, sealing, patching, rubbing, and cleanup. Price these as separate labor or material lines tied to the relevant placement.
  8. Include demolition and disposal for replacement work. Apply only where the scope includes removal of existing concrete.

H. Worksheet Assembly and Independent Review

  1. Prepare the worksheet by element, placement, quantity, unit cost, labor, equipment, and total. Confirm every column matches the Section D formula basis.
  2. Compare cubic-yard and square-foot metrics as secondary checks. Confirm slab and flatwork volumes are consistent with area × thickness at the specified depth.
  3. Document assumptions, exclusions, alternates, and unresolved site or structural conditions. Link each entry to the affected element row in the assumptions, exclusions, and clarifications register from Chapter 1.
  4. Complete independent review of volumes, reinforcement, forms, placements, and scope. Have a second reviewer, or an independent recalculation by the same estimator, confirm each high-value or high-risk element before the worksheet is marked complete.

Common Worksheet Inspection

Inspect the finished concrete cost-code section using the same checks regardless of which element or placement is being reviewed.

  • Separation check: confirm footings, walls, piers, pads, slabs, steps, and flatwork are on separate rows and are not combined into one volume.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm every element's net volume has a recorded independent reconciliation within the company-approved tolerance.
  • Waste check: confirm the applied waste percentage matches the company-approved basis for that element type and is not duplicated in the unit cost.
  • Reinforcement check: confirm bar size, spacing, and lap length match the structural reinforcing schedule exactly.
  • Pricing check: confirm every unit cost traces to a dated quotation and that short-load charges are shown separately where applicable.
  • Documentation check: confirm every element row shows a recorded source document, date, and revision.

Complete the Concrete Worksheet Verification Sheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 05.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Reconciled Volume Does Not Match Net Volume

Probable cause: a dimension was misread from the plan, a schedule value was not applied, or the independent method used a different thickness or depth than the governing document.
Confirmation: compare both calculations line by line against the governing plan, section, schedule, or detail dimension.
Correction: correct the calculation that used the wrong dimension and re-enter the corrected volume.
Reinspection: repeat the independent reconciliation and confirm both methods agree within the company-approved tolerance.

Order Volume Does Not Reflect a Short Load

Probable cause: the supplier's full-load threshold was not confirmed before the order volume was entered, or the short-load charge field was left blank.
Confirmation: compare the element's order volume to the supplier's stated full-load threshold in the dated quotation.
Correction: add the short-load charge as a separate priced line and confirm it is not absorbed into the unit cost.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing check for the affected element.

Reinforcing Bar Quantity Does Not Match the Structural Schedule

Probable cause: the takeoff used a generic spacing or lap assumption instead of the exact value stated in the structural reinforcing schedule.
Confirmation: compare the takeoff's bar size, spacing, and lap length line by line against the structural schedule.
Correction: recalculate the reinforcing quantity using the exact schedule values and stock-length conversion.
Reinspection: repeat the reinforcement check for the corrected element.

Two Elements With Different Strength Requirements Were Combined Into One Volume Line

Probable cause: the takeoff grouped elements by location or pour sequence instead of by strength, exposure, or finish requirement.
Confirmation: compare the combined row's strength entry against the structural notes for each individual element it contains.
Correction: split the combined row into separate element rows, each with its own strength, waste, and unit-cost entry.
Reinspection: repeat the separation check across the full concrete cost-code section.

An Element Has No Recorded Source Document

Probable cause: the element was measured directly from a scaled drawing without confirming a written dimension, schedule, or approved clarification.
Confirmation: the Chapter 1 error-check sheet's missing-source flag identifies the affected row.
Correction: locate the governing written dimension, schedule, or approved clarification and record it as the source; if none exists, convert the item to a clarification per Chapter 2 before pricing.
Reinspection: repeat the missing-source check on the corrected row.

Vapor Barrier Was Added Without a Specification Reference

Probable cause: the estimator assumed a vapor barrier was required based on common practice rather than the project specifications.
Confirmation: search the specifications and slab details for an explicit vapor-barrier requirement.
Correction: remove the unapproved vapor-barrier line, or convert it into a documented assumption and route it through the Chapter 2 clarification process before pricing.
Reinspection: repeat the documentation check for the affected slab element.

Field Terms

Net Volume
The measured concrete volume before placement waste is added.
Order Volume
Net volume plus placement waste, converted to the supplier's cubic-yard order unit.
Short Load
A ready-mix order below the supplier's full-load threshold, carrying an additional per-load charge.
Placement
One distinct concrete pour, separated by joint, sequencing, or scheduling requirement.
Reinforcing Schedule
The structural document stating bar size, spacing, length, laps, hooks, and dowel requirements.
Vapor Barrier
The membrane placed under a slab-on-grade to control moisture transmission.
Stay-in-Place Form
A form material left in the completed structure rather than stripped after curing.
Waterstop
An embedded strip installed at a construction joint to resist water passage.
Control Joint
A formed or saw-cut joint intended to control the location of shrinkage cracking.
Reconciliation
Comparing a volume or quantity by an independent method to confirm accuracy.

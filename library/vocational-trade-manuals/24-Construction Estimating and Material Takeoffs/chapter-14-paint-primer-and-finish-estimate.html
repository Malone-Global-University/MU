Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a complete residential paint, primer, stain, and surface-finish estimate. The finished work product is one coating schedule and cost worksheet organized by room and surface, covering substrate condition, preparation, primer, wall and ceiling paint, trim enamel, stain and clear-coat products, coat counts, sundries, protection, environmental controls, labor, equipment, and pricing.

You will build a coating schedule from the finish schedule and selections, measure wall, ceiling, and trim surfaces by the approved deduction rule, document substrate condition and required preparation, calculate primer and finish-coat quantities by product-specific coverage rate, calculate sundries and protection materials, estimate labor by task and coat, price every product from dated quotations, reconcile the coating area against the finish schedule, and prepare the worksheet for entry into the Chapter 1 workbook.

This project depends on the workbook built in Chapter 1, the scope review completed in Chapter 2, and the room dimensions established in Chapter 13. The output of this chapter is one required input to the Chapter 18 pricing worksheet and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-079 — Calculate wall and ceiling areas by room using the approved opening-deduction rule.
  • ET-080 — Estimate primer quantity by substrate, coverage, porosity, method, and coat count.
  • ET-081 — Estimate paint quantity by area, coverage, color change, sheen, texture, and coats.
  • ET-082 — Estimate trim coating quantity by developed surface or approved linear conversion.
  • ET-083 — Estimate stain or clear-coat quantity by coverage and coats.
  • ET-084 — Prepare a paint and finish material worksheet with sundries, protection, labor, equipment, and total cost.

Where This Skill Fits

The coating estimate is the final interior finish-material takeoff in the sequence that began with drywall in Chapter 10, continued through finish carpentry in Chapter 11, cabinets in Chapter 12, and flooring in Chapter 13. Coating quantities depend on the same room dimensions, wall and ceiling areas, and trim lengths established in those chapters and must be reconciled against them rather than remeasured from scratch.

The worksheet produced here feeds directly into the Chapter 18 pricing worksheet, where waste, freight, tax, overhead, and profit are applied, and into the Chapter 20 capstone estimate, where it is reconciled against the finish schedule one final time before the bid proposal is issued.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • building a coating schedule by room, surface, substrate, primer, finish, color, sheen, and coat count
  • measuring wall, ceiling, door, window, casing, base, crown, cabinet, shelving, stair, and rail surfaces for coating
  • documenting substrate condition and determining required preparation
  • calculating primer, paint, trim enamel, stain, sealer, and clear-coat quantities by product-specific coverage
  • calculating sundries, protection materials, and environmental controls
  • estimating coating labor and preparing a priced worksheet reconciled against the finish schedule

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a coating product, color, or system not shown in the approved finish schedule or owner selections
  • assign a single generic coverage rate to every product and substrate in the estimate
  • estimate hazardous-material abatement, containment design, or remediation scope; that work requires a qualified specialist and is identified as a separate allowance
  • issue this worksheet as a client-facing bid proposal; that document is assembled separately in Chapter 19

Any coated surface without a documented substrate condition or coverage source must be flagged and resolved before the worksheet is priced.

Trade Note

Coverage rate is the single most product-specific number in this estimate. A primer, a flat wall paint, a trim enamel, and a penetrating stain do not share a coverage rate even when applied to the same wall, and using one rate for all of them is the most common source of a coating estimate that runs short on the job.

Coverage Standard

Do not price any primer, paint, trim enamel, stain, sealer, or clear-coat quantity until the coverage rate for that specific product has been confirmed against its manufacturer data sheet. A quantity calculated from an assumed or borrowed coverage rate must be marked unverified and corrected before pricing.

Shop Tip

Build the substrate-condition log before the coverage calculations. Porosity, existing color, and surface condition change the coat count long before a coverage rate ever matters, and a coat count decided after the material quantity is already calculated forces a rework of the entire room.

Common Field Fundamentals

A coating estimate fails in one of three places: an area measured without a documented deduction rule, a coverage rate borrowed from the wrong product, or a coat count assumed without regard to substrate condition and color change. This chapter builds the schedule in an order that forces each of those decisions to be made and recorded before any quantity is calculated.

Coverage Rate Is Never Generic

Confirm the manufacturer-stated coverage rate for every individual product before calculating a quantity from it. Do not carry a coverage rate from one product to another product of a different type, sheen, or brand, even when both are used on the same wall in the same room.

Critical Control Cue — Do Not Use One Coverage Rate for All Products

A primer, a flat interior paint, a satin trim enamel, and a penetrating exterior stain each carry a different coverage rate on the same manufacturer's data sheet. Applying a single coverage rate across all products in the estimate will produce a material quantity that is wrong for every product except the one the rate was taken from.

Traceability Cue — Record the Coverage Source With Every Product

Record the manufacturer, product name, coverage rate, and data-sheet date for every product entered into the coating schedule. A coverage rate without a recorded source cannot be defended in a later review and must be treated as unverified.

Documentation Cue — Log Substrate Condition Before Coat Count

Record the documented substrate condition for every surface before assigning a coat count. New drywall, a color change, a glossy existing finish, and a stain-prone substrate each carry a different coat-count requirement under the same product system.

Company Policy Alert — Confirm the Approved Opening-Deduction Rule

Company policy on deducting door, window, and cabinet openings from gross wall area varies. Confirm the approved rule before measuring, and apply it consistently across every room; do not deduct openings in one room and skip the deduction in another.

C. Substrate and Coverage Reference

  • Substrate categories to document: new drywall, existing painted surface, bare wood, metal, masonry, stain-prone wood, repaired surface, glossy existing finish, contaminated surface, and weathered exterior surface.
  • Coverage-rate source: use only the coverage rate stated on the current manufacturer data sheet for the exact product and sheen specified; do not substitute a rate from a similar product.
  • Coat-count basis: assign coat count from the finish schedule first; where the finish schedule is silent, assign coat count from the documented substrate condition and color-change requirement, and record the basis.
  • Hazardous-coating screening: for buildings constructed before the applicable regulated period for lead-based paint, flag existing coated surfaces for hazardous-coating assessment before pricing any disturbance of the existing finish.

D. Measurement Procedure

  1. Review finish schedules, elevations, room data, product specifications, color selections, substrate requirements, and quality expectations. Confirm the current revision of each document before measuring.
  2. Create the coating schedule by room, surface, substrate, condition, primer, finish, color, sheen, and coat count. Use one row per surface type per room.
  3. Measure wall area by perimeter and height. Record the source dimension for each room, whether from the plan, the Chapter 13 room schedule, or a verified field measurement.
  4. Deduct openings according to the approved method while retaining cutting-in labor. Record the specific opening-deduction rule used and apply it consistently across every room; do not deduct openings for cutting-in labor purposes.
  5. Measure ceiling area by room, slope, tray, soffit, beam, and specialty condition. Record each specialty ceiling condition on a separate schedule row.
  6. Measure doors, frames, windows, casing, base, crown, cabinets, shelving, stairs, rails, and trim. Record count or length for each item by room and coordinate counts with the Chapter 11 and Chapter 12 takeoffs.
  7. Separate exterior and specialty surfaces from interior work. Place exterior coating scope on its own schedule section with its own substrate and product entries.

E. Substrate Condition and Preparation Procedure

  1. Identify new drywall, existing paint, bare wood, metal, masonry, stain-prone, repaired, glossy, contaminated, and weathered substrates. Record the specific condition for every surface row in the coating schedule.
  2. Determine preparation from documented condition and product system. Do not assign preparation scope from an assumed condition; base it on the recorded substrate condition and the manufacturer's specified system.
  3. Measure cleaning, scraping, sanding, deglossing, caulking, filling, patching, masking, and spot repair. Record quantity or area for each preparation task by room.
  4. Identify hazardous-coating assessment and containment for older buildings. Flag any surface requiring assessment and exclude disturbance-labor pricing for that surface until the assessment is complete.

F. Product Quantity Calculation Procedure

  1. Calculate primer by substrate, coverage, porosity, method, and coat count. Use the primer product's own data-sheet coverage rate.
  2. Calculate wall and ceiling paint by area, coverage, color change, sheen, texture, and coats. Apply an additional coat where the finish schedule or documented color change requires it.
  3. Calculate trim enamel by developed surface or the approved linear conversion. Record which method was used and the conversion factor applied.
  4. Calculate stain, sealer, clear coat, varnish, oil, or specialty finish by coverage and coats. Use each product's own data-sheet coverage rate.
  5. Account for spray, roller, brush, container, touch-up, and retention losses. Add the manufacturer or company-approved retention-loss percentage separately from area-based waste.

G. Sundries, Containers, and Protection Procedure

  1. Do not use one coverage rate for all products and substrates. Confirm each product row in the schedule references its own data-sheet coverage rate before totals are calculated.
  2. Convert quantity into actual container sizes and minimum order. Round up to the next available container size per product and color.
  3. Identify separate colors, bases, sheens, batches, and custom-match charges. Record each as a separate line so batch or custom-match pricing is not averaged across the estimate.
  4. Calculate caulk, filler, masking, abrasives, rags, strainers, liners, rollers, brushes, tips, and cleaners. Base quantities on room count, surface area, and company consumption history.
  5. Include floor, furniture, fixture, window, landscape, vehicle, and occupant protection. Record protection scope by room and note any items requiring the owner's or occupant's cooperation.
  6. Include ventilation, heat, cooling, dehumidification, filtration, and curing restrictions. Record any product-specific environmental requirement from the data sheet.

H. Labor and Equipment Procedure

  1. Estimate labor for setup, protection, preparation, primer, each coat, trim, spray work, cleanup, and punch. Use the Chapter 17 production-rate basis where available; otherwise record the source of the rate used.
  2. Adjust production for occupancy, height, color change, dark colors, fine finish, spray restrictions, and repairs. Document each adjustment and its basis.
  3. Include ladders, scaffolds, lifts, sprayers, dust extraction, and respiratory controls. Record equipment by task and duration.

I. Pricing, Reconciliation, and Worksheet Procedure

  1. Price coatings and sundries from dated supplier data. Record supplier, quote date, and expiration for each priced item.
  2. Include delivery, environmental fees, disposal, and leftover-material handling. Add each as a separate line rather than folding it into material unit cost.
  3. Reconcile coating area against room and finish schedules. Compare the total net coating area to the Chapter 13 room schedule and resolve any variance before pricing is finalized.
  4. Compare material quantity with coat count and coverage. Confirm the calculated quantity for each product matches its recorded coat count and coverage rate.
  5. Prepare the worksheet by room, surface, product, coats, material, labor, equipment, and total. Enter the completed worksheet into the finishes cost-code section of the Chapter 1 workbook.
  6. Document assumptions for condition, color count, occupancy, furniture, repair, and touch-up. Link each assumption to the affected schedule row.
  7. Complete independent review of areas, coats, trim, preparation, and protection. Have a second reviewer or an independent recalculation confirm the highest-value rooms and products.

Common Estimate Inspection

Inspect the finished coating worksheet using the same checks regardless of which room or product is being reviewed.

  • Schedule check: confirm every room and surface in the finish schedule has a corresponding coating-schedule row with substrate, condition, product, and coat count recorded.
  • Coverage check: confirm every product quantity references its own manufacturer data-sheet coverage rate and coat count, not a rate borrowed from another product.
  • Deduction check: confirm the same approved opening-deduction rule was applied consistently across every room.
  • Preparation check: confirm every surface with a documented condition other than new substrate has a corresponding preparation-labor line.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm the total net coating area matches the Chapter 13 room schedule within the documented tolerance.
  • Pricing check: confirm every priced item carries a supplier, quote date, and expiration.

Complete the Coating Estimate Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 15.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Material Quantity Runs Short on a Room With a Color Change

Probable cause: the coat count for the room was taken from a generic default rather than the documented color-change requirement in the finish schedule.
Confirmation: compare the coating schedule's recorded coat count for the affected room against the finish schedule's stated color and the substrate-condition log.
Correction: revise the coat count to match the documented color-change requirement and recalculate the product quantity for that room.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage check for the corrected room and confirm the quantity now matches the revised coat count.

Total Coating Area Does Not Match the Chapter 13 Room Schedule

Probable cause: a room was measured independently in this chapter using a different source dimension or opening-deduction rule than the one used in Chapter 13.
Confirmation: compare the source dimension and deduction rule recorded for the variant room against the Chapter 13 room schedule.
Correction: adopt the Chapter 13 room dimension and the approved deduction rule, and recalculate the room's net coating area.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check across the full coating schedule.

A Product Quantity Was Calculated Using Another Product's Coverage Rate

Probable cause: a coverage rate was carried forward from a similar product in the schedule instead of being confirmed against that specific product's own data sheet.
Confirmation: compare the coverage rate recorded for the affected product row against its own manufacturer data sheet.
Correction: replace the coverage rate with the correct product-specific value and recalculate the quantity.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage check for every product row sharing the same substrate or room.

Preparation Labor Is Missing for a Documented Substrate Condition

Probable cause: the substrate-condition log was completed after the labor estimate was built, so a documented condition such as glossy existing finish or contaminated surface was never converted into a preparation task.
Confirmation: compare the substrate-condition log against the Section H labor estimate for the affected room.
Correction: add the missing preparation task and labor hours, and log the correction with its source condition.
Reinspection: repeat the preparation check across every room with a documented condition other than new substrate.

A Priced Product Has No Recorded Quotation Date

Probable cause: a unit price was entered from memory, a prior project, or a supplier website without recording the quote date and expiration.
Confirmation: the pricing check flags the affected row for a missing date or expiration.
Correction: obtain a current dated quotation for the product and replace the unsourced price; if no current quotation is available, mark the price as unverified and exclude it from the priced total until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing check on the corrected row.

An Older-Building Surface Was Priced for Disturbance Without a Hazardous-Coating Assessment

Probable cause: the Section E hazardous-coating screening step was skipped or the building age was not confirmed before pricing scraping, sanding, or removal labor on an existing coated surface.
Confirmation: confirm the building's construction date and check whether the affected surface was flagged in the substrate-condition log.
Correction: remove disturbance-labor pricing for the affected surface, flag it for a qualified hazardous-coating assessment, and record the exclusion in the assumptions log.
Reinspection: repeat the hazardous-coating screening step across every existing coated surface in the schedule.

Field Terms

Wall area measured by perimeter times height before opening deduction.
Net Coating Area
Gross area after the approved opening deduction, with cutting-in labor retained.
Developed Surface
The total exposed face area of a profiled trim member used for enamel coverage.
Coverage Rate
The manufacturer-stated area one unit of product covers per coat.
Coat Count
The number of coats required by the finish schedule, substrate condition, and color change.
Retention Loss
Product lost to application method, container residue, and touch-up reserve.
Substrate Condition
The documented physical and chemical state of a surface that determines preparation and coat count.
Deglossing
Reducing the sheen of an existing glossy finish to improve adhesion of a new coating.
Cutting-In
Hand-applied coating along edges, corners, and openings not reached by roller or spray.
Hazardous-Coating Assessment
A qualified evaluation of an existing coated surface for hazardous material before disturbance.

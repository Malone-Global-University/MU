Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a complete structural-panel takeoff for one residential project, entered into the same estimating workbook you built in Chapter 1. The finished work product is one structural-panel cost-code section covering wall sheathing, shear panels, subfloor, underlayment, roof sheathing, soffit panels, and miscellaneous panels, each identified by panel type, thickness, span rating, exposure, edge condition, sheet size, location, fastening schedule, adhesive requirement, waste factor, and construction phase.

You will measure gross wall, floor, and roof-slope area from the approved structural and architectural documents; separate panel categories that must never share a waste factor; convert net area into realistic purchase quantities of full and partial sheets; calculate fasteners and adhesive from the approved shear, diaphragm, and field schedules; and reconcile the completed panel takeoff against the framing quantities produced in Chapter 5.

This project follows directly from Chapter 5's framing lumber takeoff and precedes Chapter 7's roofing materials estimate. The wall, floor, and roof geometry measured here is the same geometry the roofing takeoff in Chapter 7 and the envelope takeoff in Chapter 8 will reconcile against, so an error in this chapter's area measurement propagates into both of those chapters if it is not caught here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-031 — Calculate wall sheathing quantities from gross elevation or wall-line area with approved opening deductions.
  • ET-032 — Calculate roof sheathing quantities from actual slope area, including irregular planes.
  • ET-033 — Calculate subfloor panel quantities by level, platform, cantilever, and opening.
  • ET-034 — Account for panel waste separately by wall, roof, and floor application without cross-application of offcuts.
  • ET-035 — Estimate panel fasteners from field, edge, shear, and boundary nailing schedules.
  • ET-036 — Prepare a structural panel takeoff summary reconciled against floor, wall, and roof geometry.

Where This Skill Fits

The panel quantities produced in this project enter the framing and roofing cost-code sections of the Chapter 1 workbook, using the same source, quantity, waste, order-quantity, and unit-cost columns built in that project. The wall-area and roof-slope-area figures calculated here are reused as reconciliation checks in Chapter 7's roofing estimate and Chapter 8's envelope estimate, so both quantities must be independently defensible before this chapter closes.

Labor for panel installation is priced in Chapter 17 using the task list and productivity basis identified in this chapter's labor section. Panel, fastener, and adhesive pricing is finalized in Chapter 18 using the dated quotations recorded here.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • measuring gross wall, floor, and roof-slope area from approved structural and architectural documents
  • separating wall sheathing, shear panels, subfloor, underlayment, roof sheathing, soffit panels, and miscellaneous panels
  • calculating net sheets, purchase quantities, and panel-specific waste for each category
  • calculating fasteners and adhesive from field, edge, shear, diaphragm, and boundary schedules
  • identifying fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, radiant-barrier, or specialty panels
  • reconciling the completed panel takeoff against wall, floor, and roof geometry

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • redesign bracing zones, shear-panel locations, or nailing schedules shown on the approved structural documents
  • apply a single generic waste percentage to every panel type, thickness, or application
  • treat offcuts from one panel type or thickness as available stock for a different panel type or thickness
  • substitute an unapproved panel product for a specified fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, or radiant-barrier panel
  • price the takeoff; pricing is completed in Chapter 18 from the dated quotations recorded here

Any wall, floor, or roof area that cannot be measured from the approved documents must be converted into a clarification, approved assumption, or allowance before the takeoff proceeds.

Trade Note

A structural-panel takeoff is not a single square-footage number. Wall sheathing, roof sheathing, subfloor, and specialty panels each carry a different coverage rate, a different waste history, and a different fastening schedule. Collapsing them into one combined area figure is the most common cause of a panel order that runs short on one category while carrying excess stock in another.

Takeoff Standard

Do not enter a panel quantity into the workbook without a recorded source drawing, section, or detail. Do not calculate fasteners before the correct nailing schedule for that panel's location — field, edge, shear boundary, or diaphragm boundary — has been identified and cited.

Shop Tip

Measure and record wall, floor, and roof-slope area by zone before converting anything into sheet counts. A takeoff built directly in sheet counts hides the underlying area figure that Chapters 7 and 8 need for reconciliation.

Common Field Fundamentals

A structural-panel takeoff converts measured wall, floor, and roof geometry into purchase-ready sheet counts, fasteners, and adhesive. Each panel category — wall sheathing, shear panels, subfloor, underlayment, roof sheathing, soffit, and miscellaneous panels — has its own coverage rate, waste history, and fastening schedule, and each must be tracked separately through the workbook's cost-code section for framing and roofing panels.

Wall sheathingGross elevation area, openings, gables, bracing zones
SubfloorLevel, platform, cantilever, opening, layout
Roof sheathingActual slope area, valleys, hips, dormers, overhangs
Fasteners/adhesiveField, edge, shear, diaphragm, boundary schedules
WasteSeparate factors for wall cuts, roof geometry, floor layout, damage, bundles
Specialty panelsFire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, radiant-barrier
Labor basisLayout, cutting, carrying, fastening, adhesive, support, cleanup
ReconciliationPanel area compared to Chapter 5 wall, floor, and roof geometry

Panel Category Separation

Never combine wall sheathing, subfloor, roof sheathing, and specialty panels into one blended area or one blended waste factor. Each category has a different sheet-coverage rate, a different cut pattern, and a different history of damage and offcut loss. A blended figure conceals shortfalls in one category behind surplus in another.

Traceability Cue — Record the Source Sheet or Detail for Every Area

Cite the specific elevation, framing plan, section, or detail supporting every wall, floor, and roof-slope area entered into the workbook. Do not enter an area figure carried over from memory or from a prior chapter's rough total without re-verifying it against the current document set.

Critical Control Cue — Confirm Bracing and Shear-Zone Locations Before Estimating Fasteners

Locate every continuous-sheathing zone, bracing zone, narrow panel, and specialty fastening zone on the structural documents before calculating fastener quantities. A fastener count based on a generic field schedule where a boundary or shear schedule actually applies will produce a fastener shortage that is not visible until installation.

Documentation Cue — Log Panel-Type Substitution Restrictions

Record any fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, or radiant-barrier panel requirement in the assumptions field linked to that takeoff row. Do not allow a standard panel to be substituted during pricing without a logged clarification and approval.

Manufacturer Cue — Verify Adhesive Cartridge Coverage

Confirm the adhesive manufacturer's stated bead size and cartridge coverage before calculating adhesive quantity. Do not assume a generic coverage rate; use the coverage rate stated in the current manufacturer instructions for the specified bead size and joint condition.

C. Panel-Type and Waste Reference

  • Panel identification: record panel type, thickness, span rating, exposure, edge profile, treatment, and approved sheet size for every category before measuring area.
  • Waste separation: assign waste independently to wall cuts, roof geometry, floor layout, damage risk, and minimum-bundle rounding; do not apply one waste percentage to every category.
  • Offcut restriction: do not treat an offcut from one panel type or thickness as available stock for a different panel type or thickness.
  • Specialty panels: identify fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, and radiant-barrier panels separately, with their own quantity, source, and pricing lines.

E. Preparation — Documents, Zones, and Category Setup

  1. Review structural and architectural plans, bracing/shear information, floor framing, roof framing, sections, details, and specifications. Confirm the current revision against the Chapter 2 document register before measuring.
  2. Identify panel type, thickness, span rating, exposure, edge profile, treatment, and approved sheet size for every category. Record each on the panel-type reference in Section C.
  3. Separate wall sheathing, shear panels, subfloor, underlayment, roof sheathing, soffit, and miscellaneous panels. Create a distinct takeoff row group in the workbook for each category.
  4. Locate continuous sheathing zones, bracing zones, narrow panels, and specialty fastening zones on the structural documents. Mark each zone's boundary and cite the supporting sheet or detail.

F. Wall Sheathing and Shear Panel Takeoff

  1. Measure gross exterior wall area by elevation or wall line. Record the source elevation or wall-line reference for each measurement.
  2. Deduct openings only according to the approved deduction rule from Chapter 2's clarification log. Preserve the cut-waste implication of each deduction rather than treating deducted area as recovered material.
  3. Measure gables, rakes, parapets, knee walls, and irregular areas separately. Do not fold irregular areas into the gross wall-line figure without a labeled sub-line.
  4. Identify continuous sheathing zones, bracing zones, narrow panels, and specialty fastening zones. Confirm each zone's boundary against the structural bracing/shear documents.

G. Subfloor and Roof Sheathing Takeoff

  1. Measure subfloor by level, platform, cantilever, and opening. Record the source framing plan for each level.
  2. Account for tongue-and-groove orientation, staggered joints, edge support, and layout. Confirm layout direction against the framing plan or an approved assumption.
  3. Measure roof sheathing by actual slope area. Use the approved slope factor and cite the roof plan and slope note supporting each plane.
  4. Separate roof planes by thickness, exposure, clips, and structural requirement. Do not combine planes with different thickness or exposure requirements into one line.
  5. Measure valleys, hips, dormers, overhangs, and irregular roofs. Record each as its own sub-line with its own source citation.
  6. Identify backing, blocking, clips, splines, and edge supports. Add each as a separate accessory line linked to the affected roof or wall category.

H. Sheet Conversion, Waste, and Fastener Calculation

  1. Calculate net sheets using actual coverage and layout for each category. Apply the approved sheet size from Section C.
  2. Convert partial sheets into realistic purchase quantities. Round up to the next full sheet at the purchase-unit stage only, per the Chapter 1 workbook convention.
  3. Apply separate waste for wall cuts, roof geometry, floor layout, damage, and minimum bundles. Enter each waste percentage on its own labeled input cell.
  4. Confirm offcuts are not treated as interchangeable across panel types or thicknesses. Review each category's waste basis against the Section C offcut restriction.
  5. Calculate fasteners from field, edge, shear, diaphragm, and boundary schedules using Section D. Cite the manufacturer schedule supporting each fastener quantity.
  6. Calculate adhesive by bead size, joint length, cartridge coverage, and waste. Cite the manufacturer adhesive data supporting the quantity.
  7. Count clips, seam tape, sealant, and accessories. Enter each as its own line under Section D.
  8. Identify fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, radiant-barrier, or specialty panels. Confirm each carries its own quantity and pricing line per Section C.
  9. Include cut-edge treatment and weather protection. Add as a labeled line where the specification requires it.

I. Delivery, Labor Basis, and Pricing Preparation

  1. Plan delivery by floor, wall, and roof phase. Record the intended delivery sequence in the phase field for each category.
  2. Include unloading, staging, lifting, hoisting, and moisture protection. Add each as a labeled line where the project conditions require it.
  3. Identify the labor task list for layout, cutting, carrying, fastening, adhesive application, support, and cleanup. Record the task list for use in the Chapter 17 labor estimate; do not assign labor hours in this chapter.
  4. Note productivity factors for height, pitch, wind, panel weight, openings, and shear fastening. Record these as documented conditions for Chapter 17 rather than calculating hours here.
  5. Request or confirm dated quotations for panels, fasteners, adhesive, clips, and delivery. Record supplier, quote number, date, expiration, freight, lead time, minimum order, and qualifications in the Chapter 1 quotation fields; do not calculate extended cost in this chapter.

J. Reconciliation and Closeout

  1. Reconcile panel area against floor, wall, and roof geometry from the Chapter 5 framing takeoff. Compare wall-line length, floor area, and roof-plane area between the two chapters and record the result.
  2. Compare sheet count with fastener and adhesive quantities. Confirm the fastener and adhesive quantities are proportional to the reconciled sheet count for each category.
  3. Prepare the takeoff summary by type, thickness, rating, sheet size, quantity, accessories, phase, and cost placeholder. Use the Chapter 1 workbook summary sheets to present the completed takeoff.
  4. Complete independent review of slope area, specialty panels, shear zones, and packaging. Have a second reviewer verify at least the roof-slope area and the shear-zone fastener schedule independently.

Common Takeoff Inspection

Inspect the finished panel takeoff using the same checks regardless of which category is being reviewed.

  • Category-separation check: confirm wall sheathing, subfloor, roof sheathing, and specialty panels each carry their own area, waste, and fastener lines.
  • Source check: confirm every measured area cites a specific elevation, framing plan, section, or detail.
  • Waste check: confirm no single waste percentage was applied across more than one panel category.
  • Fastener-schedule check: confirm bracing, shear, and boundary zones use the correct fastening schedule rather than a generic field schedule.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm panel area matches the Chapter 5 wall length, floor area, and roof-plane area within the workbook's approved variance.
  • Specialty-panel check: confirm fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, and radiant-barrier panels are separately identified and not substituted.

Complete the Panel Takeoff Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 07.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Panel Area Does Not Match the Chapter 5 Framing Takeoff

Probable cause: a wall length, floor area, or roof-plane area was measured from a superseded document revision, or an irregular area was omitted from one of the two takeoffs.
Confirmation: compare the source citation for each area figure in both takeoffs and confirm both reference the same current document revision.
Correction: remeasure the affected area from the current revision and update whichever takeoff is incorrect, logging the correction in the revision log.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check and confirm both takeoffs agree within the approved variance.

Fastener Count Is Too Low for a Shear or Boundary Zone

Probable cause: the fastener formula used a generic field-nailing spacing instead of the tighter edge, shear, or diaphragm boundary spacing required in that zone.
Confirmation: compare the zone boundary marked in Section E, Step 4 against the fastener formula's cited schedule.
Correction: rebuild the fastener formula using the correct manufacturer schedule for that specific zone.
Reinspection: repeat the fastener-schedule check for every zone bordering the corrected area.

A Specialty Panel Was Priced as a Standard Panel

Probable cause: the fire-treated, preservative-treated, coated, or radiant-barrier requirement was not separated into its own takeoff line before quotations were requested.
Confirmation: compare the specification's panel requirement against the panel type recorded on the corresponding takeoff line and quotation.
Correction: separate the specialty panel into its own line, request a corrected quotation for the correct product, and log the correction in the assumptions field.
Reinspection: repeat the specialty-panel check across the full takeoff.

Waste From One Panel Category Was Applied to Another

Probable cause: the workbook waste input cell was copied across categories without adjusting for that category's cut pattern, damage risk, or bundle size.
Confirmation: compare each category's waste percentage against its documented basis in Section C.
Correction: enter an independently determined waste percentage for the affected category based on its own layout, cuts, and packaging.
Reinspection: repeat the waste check across all panel categories.

Roof-Slope Area Is Understated

Probable cause: the roof-plane area was measured using the horizontal footprint instead of the actual slope area, or an irregular plane such as a dormer or cricket was omitted.
Confirmation: recheck the slope factor applied to each plane and compare the total roof-plane list against the roof framing plan for omitted planes.
Correction: reapply the correct slope factor and add any omitted irregular plane as its own sub-line.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check against the Chapter 5 roof-plane geometry.

Field Terms

Shear Panel
A structural panel installed and fastened to provide lateral bracing capacity per the structural documents.
Field Fastener
A fastener located within the interior of a panel, away from panel edges and framing boundaries.
Boundary Fastener
A fastener located at a panel edge, shear-panel boundary, or diaphragm boundary, typically spaced tighter than field fasteners.
Net Sheets
The purchase-ready sheet count calculated from net measured area and approved sheet coverage.
Span Rating
The manufacturer-assigned rating identifying a panel's approved maximum support spacing for a given application.
Radiant-Barrier Panel
A structural panel with a reflective facing intended to reduce radiant heat transfer, specified separately from a standard panel.
Diaphragm Boundary
The perimeter of a roof or floor panel assembly acting as a structural diaphragm, subject to its own fastening schedule.
Cut-Edge Treatment
A specified sealer or coating applied to a field-cut panel edge to restore weather or treatment protection.

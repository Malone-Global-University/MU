Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert an approved residential plan set, reflected ceiling plan, finish schedule, and rated-assembly detail into a complete drywall and finishing-materials estimate. The finished work product is one room- and assembly-based drywall takeoff entered into the Chapter 1 workbook, covering board type, thickness, layers, and finish level; fastener and adhesive quantities; tape, joint compound, and corner-bead quantities; access, protection, and drying requirements; and a labor and cost summary reconciled against room and wall geometry.

You will review the governing documents, identify board type and finish level by room and assembly, measure wall and ceiling area, convert net area into sheet counts, apply material-specific waste, calculate fasteners, tape, and compound, measure corner bead and specialty accessories, account for access and protection, estimate labor, price the takeoff from dated sources, and complete an independent reconciliation before preparing the room-by-room summary.

This project depends directly on the workbook built in Chapter 1 and the scope review completed in Chapter 2, and it produces one of the interior cost-code sections that feeds the Chapter 17 labor estimate, the Chapter 18 pricing summary, and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-055 — Calculate drywall sheets from net wall and ceiling area, board size, and layout.
  • ET-056 — Estimate screws from framing, layer count, field spacing, edge spacing, and rated-assembly requirements.
  • ET-057 — Estimate joint tape from board layout, seams, corners, and repair allowance.
  • ET-058 — Estimate joint compound from finish level, area, bead, texture, and product coverage.
  • ET-059 — Estimate corner bead from openings, reveals, arches, and control-joint locations.
  • ET-060 — Prepare a drywall takeoff summary reconciled by room, assembly, material, finish level, labor, and total.

Where This Skill Fits

The framing takeoff from Chapter 5 and the structural-panel takeoff from Chapter 6 established the framed surfaces this project now covers with board. The insulation and air-sealing quantities from Chapter 9 establish which cavities and rated assemblies affect board layering and fastener schedule requirements here. This project's board, fastener, tape, compound, and bead quantities feed directly into Chapter 11's trim and door coordination, Chapter 14's paint and finish area calculations, Chapter 17's labor estimate, and Chapter 18's pricing summary.

The room-by-room, assembly-based organization built in this chapter is the same organization used again in Chapter 13's flooring estimate and Chapter 14's paint estimate, so the room schedule fields set up here should remain consistent for the rest of the book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing plans, reflected ceilings, wall types, sections, finish schedules, rated assemblies, and moisture areas
  • identifying board type, thickness, size, layers, edge, rating, moisture resistance, backing, and finish level by room and assembly
  • measuring wall and ceiling area, converting net area into sheet counts, and applying material-specific waste
  • calculating fasteners, adhesive where permitted, tape, joint compound, and corner bead
  • identifying access, protection, dust control, and drying-time requirements
  • estimating labor for stocking, hanging, fastening, taping, coating, sanding, texture, and cleanup
  • pricing the takeoff from dated sources and preparing the room-by-room summary

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a rated-assembly board type, layer count, or fastening schedule that is not shown on the documents or a manufacturer's listed assembly
  • apply a single generic waste percentage to every board type, room, and finish level without documenting the basis for each
  • issue this takeoff as a client-facing proposal; that document is prepared separately in Chapter 19
  • invent a joint-compound coverage rate, screw spacing, or texture product without an identified manufacturer or company source

Any room or assembly whose board type, rating, or finish level is not clearly shown or specified must be converted into a documented clarification or approved assumption before its quantity is finalized.

Trade Note

Drywall quantities look simple because a sheet count is easy to calculate, but the compound, tape, and finish-level quantities are where most drywall estimates lose accuracy. A Level 5 finish in a formal room and a Level 4 finish in a closet use the same board but very different compound and labor quantities, so finish level must be tracked by room from the start, not assumed uniform across the project.

Setup Standard

Do not calculate compound or labor quantities before the finish level for every room has been confirmed against the finish schedule. A compound quantity calculated against the wrong finish level will be wrong regardless of how accurately the sheet count and area were measured.

Shop Tip

Build the room schedule with a finish-level column before measuring a single wall. Assigning finish level room by room at the start prevents the common error of applying one compound coverage rate to an entire house that actually contains three different finish levels.

Common Field Fundamentals

A drywall takeoff converts framed wall and ceiling surfaces into purchase-ready board, fastener, tape, and compound quantities without losing the distinctions that control cost: which rooms carry which finish level, which walls or ceilings are rated assemblies requiring specific board and fastening, and which areas require specialty board for moisture, impact, or acoustic performance.

Board, Layer, and Finish-Level Identification

Identify board type, thickness, size, layers, edge condition, rating, moisture resistance, backing, and finish level for every room and assembly before measuring area. Do not assume a single board type applies to the entire project; garages, wet areas, and rated assemblies typically require different board than standard interior walls and ceilings.

Critical Control Cue — Confirm Rated-Assembly Requirements Before Counting Sheets

Confirm board type, layer count, and fastening schedule for every fire- or sound-rated wall and ceiling against the specified assembly detail before calculating sheet counts or fasteners for that area. A rated assembly counted using standard single-layer board and standard fastener spacing will produce a takeoff that cannot support the required rating and must be corrected before pricing.

Traceability Cue — Record Finish Level by Room

Record the finish level for every room from the finish schedule the moment the room is entered into the room schedule. A compound and labor quantity calculated without a documented finish level must be treated as unverified until the finish level is confirmed and recorded.

Documentation Cue — Separate Specialty Board Quantities

Keep moisture-resistant, fire-rated, impact-resistant, and other specialty board quantities in their own line items rather than combining them with standard board. Combining specialty and standard board quantities makes it impossible to verify that the correct product was priced and ordered for each area.

Manufacturer Cue — Use Documented Coverage Rates

Calculate compound and adhesive quantities using the coverage rate published by the identified manufacturer for the specific product and finish level, not a generic rate applied to every compound type. Setting, topping, skim, and specialty compounds have different coverage rates and cannot be estimated interchangeably.

C. Material Takeoff Basis

  • Board: stock size and thickness confirmed against the finish schedule and rated-assembly details; do not assume a stock size not confirmed available from the identified supplier.
  • Fasteners: screw type, length, and spacing confirmed against the framing schedule, layer count, and any rated-assembly fastening schedule.
  • Tape: paper or mesh tape confirmed against the specified or company-standard finishing system; do not mix tape types within one continuous joint system without documented reason.
  • Compound: setting, topping, all-purpose, and specialty compounds identified separately by manufacturer and coverage rate; do not substitute one compound type's coverage rate for another.
  • Corner bead and accessories: metal, vinyl, or paper-faced bead confirmed against the specification; reveals, arches, and control joints confirmed against the finish schedule and details.
  • Waste allowance: applied separately for board, tape, compound, and bead, based on room size, openings, angles, damage risk, board size, handling, and finish level; do not apply one waste percentage to all materials.

E. Preparation — Document Review and Room Schedule

  1. Review plans, reflected ceilings, wall types, sections, finish schedules, rated assemblies, and moisture areas. Confirm the document set matches the current revision recorded in the Chapter 1 document register.
  2. Identify board type, thickness, size, layers, edge, rating, moisture resistance, backing, and finish level for every room and assembly. Record each on the room schedule before measuring any area.
  3. Separate walls, ceilings, soffits, shafts, chases, garages, wet areas, rated assemblies, and specialty panels. Give each its own row or group on the room schedule.
  4. Confirm whether openings are deducted from wall and ceiling area under the workbook's approved method. Apply the same method to every room in this takeoff.
  5. Identify tall walls, long sheets, orientation, butt joints, and lift requirements for every room. Note any room requiring a lift, boom, or specialty handling on the room schedule.

F. Area, Sheet Count, Fastener, Tape, and Compound Procedure

  1. Measure wall area by room, type, height, side, and layer count. Record the source document and dimension for each measurement.
  2. Measure ceiling area by room, slope, tray, soffit, and specialty condition. Record the source document and dimension for each measurement.
  3. Deduct openings only according to the approved method confirmed in Section E, Step 4. Do not apply a different deduction method to different rooms without a documented reason.
  4. Convert net area into actual sheet counts by board size and layout. Optimize orientation and layout to minimize waste without assuming an unavailable stock size.
  5. Apply waste based on room size, openings, angles, damage, board size, handling, and finish level. Record the waste basis for each room or assembly separately.
  6. Separate specialty board quantities and offcuts from standard board quantities. Keep each in its own line item.
  7. Calculate fasteners from framing, layers, field spacing, edge spacing, and rated-assembly requirements. Use the schedule in Section D as the fastening basis for each condition.
  8. Calculate adhesive where permitted from bead length and manufacturer coverage. Confirm adhesive is permitted for the specific assembly before including it.
  9. Measure tape from board layout, seams, corners, and repair allowance. Record the repair-allowance percentage and its basis separately from the measured seam length.
  10. Calculate compound by finish level, area, bead, texture, and product coverage. Use the finish level recorded on the room schedule for each room.
  11. Separate setting, drying, topping, skim, texture, and specialty compounds. Do not combine compound types into a single generic quantity.
  12. Measure corner bead, reveals, arches, control joints, expansion joints, and terminations. Record each by room and assembly using the schedule in Section D.
  13. Count access panels, backing, clips, sound sealant, firestop, and acoustical accessories where assigned. Confirm assignment against the rated-assembly detail and MEP coordination documents before including a quantity.

G. Access, Labor, and Pricing Procedure

  1. Identify dust control, ventilation, heat, humidity, protection, and drying-time requirements. Record any condition affecting scheduling or sequencing.
  2. Include scaffolds, lifts, carts, panel hoists, and ceiling supports for tall walls, high ceilings, or specialty rooms identified in Section E, Step 5. Price each from a dated rental or ownership source.
  3. Estimate labor for stocking, hanging, fastening, taping, coating, sanding, texture, cleanup, and punch. Record production rate source, crew, and hours for each task using the Chapter 17 labor methodology where available.
  4. Adjust production for height, small rooms, angles, finish level, occupancy, rated layers, and drying time. Document the adjustment basis for each factor applied.
  5. Include disposal of cutoffs, damaged board, compound waste, and packaging. Price from a dated disposal source.
  6. Price board, compound, bead, tape, fasteners, sealant, delivery, stocking, and boom service from dated quotations. Record each quotation in the pricing log with supplier, date, and expiration.
  7. Confirm supplier scope for stocking, weather delays, and returns. Record any qualification affecting price or schedule.

H. Reconciliation and Summary (Perform Once, Before Pricing Is Finalized)

  1. Reconcile wall and ceiling areas with room geometry and wall types. Select one room and recalculate its area by an independent method; record the comparison result.
  2. Compare sheet count with fastener, tape, compound, and bead quantities. Confirm each quantity is proportional to the reconciled sheet count for that room.
  3. Prepare the summary by room, assembly, material, finish level, labor, equipment, and total. Link every summary value to its source row in the takeoff.
  4. Document assumptions for sheet orientation, texture, primer, paint, and damage by other trades. State each assumption as a clear, testable statement.
  5. Complete independent review of layers, specialty board, heights, and finish scope. Have a second reviewer confirm rated-assembly compliance and finish-level assignment before the takeoff is entered into pricing.

Common Drywall Takeoff Inspection

Inspect the finished takeoff using the same checks regardless of which room or assembly is being reviewed.

  • Board and layer check: confirm every rated assembly uses the specified board type and layer count.
  • Area and sheet-count check: confirm net area, waste, and sheet count reconcile with room geometry and the approved deduction method.
  • Finish-level check: confirm every room shows a finish level matching the finish schedule and that compound quantities were calculated against that finish level.
  • Fastener and accessory check: confirm fastener, tape, compound, and bead quantities are proportional to the reconciled sheet count.
  • Pricing check: confirm every priced quantity carries a dated source in the pricing log.
  • Summary check: confirm the room-by-room summary matches the underlying takeoff with no manually re-entered values.

Complete the Drywall Takeoff Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 11.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Compound Quantity Does Not Match the Room's Finish Level

Probable cause: the finish-level field on the room schedule was left blank or copied from a different room before the compound formula was built.
Confirmation: compare the room schedule's finish-level entry against the finish schedule for that room and recalculate the expected compound quantity from the documented coverage rate.
Correction: correct the finish-level entry on the room schedule and rebuild the compound formula for that room.
Reinspection: repeat the finish-level check for every room in the takeoff, not only the corrected room.

A Rated Assembly Is Counted With Standard Fastening

Probable cause: the room schedule did not flag the wall or ceiling as a rated assembly before fasteners were calculated.
Confirmation: compare the rated-assembly detail's fastening schedule against the fastener quantity calculated for that wall or ceiling.
Correction: flag the assembly as rated on the room schedule, recalculate fasteners using Section D's rated-assembly basis, and confirm board layer count matches the listed assembly.
Reinspection: repeat the rated-assembly check across all walls and ceilings flagged as rated in the document set.

Sheet Count Does Not Reconcile With Room Geometry

Probable cause: the opening-deduction method was applied inconsistently, or a wall segment was measured twice or omitted.
Confirmation: recalculate the room's net area by an independent method per Section H, Step 1 and compare to the takeoff value.
Correction: correct the area calculation using the approved deduction method and rebuild the sheet-count formula for that room.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation for the corrected room and spot-check two additional rooms of similar type.

Specialty Board Quantity Is Combined With Standard Board

Probable cause: moisture-resistant, fire-rated, or impact-resistant board was entered into the same line item as standard board.
Confirmation: compare the wet-area, garage, and rated-assembly room-schedule entries against the board line items in the takeoff.
Correction: separate the specialty board quantity into its own line item per Section F, Step 6 and re-price each board type from its own dated quotation.
Reinspection: confirm every specialty area in the room schedule has a corresponding separated line item.

A Priced Quantity Has No Dated Quotation

Probable cause: a unit cost was entered from memory or a prior project without logging a current quotation.
Confirmation: the workbook's missing-source error check identifies the affected row.
Correction: obtain and record a current dated quotation for the specific product, or mark the value as unverified and exclude it from pricing until a source is added.
Reinspection: repeat the missing-source check on the corrected row and confirm the pricing log is complete.

Tape or Bead Quantity Does Not Scale With the Sheet Count

Probable cause: tape or bead was measured from a generic room-size assumption instead of the actual board layout and opening perimeter.
Confirmation: compare the tape and bead quantities against the reconciled sheet count and opening count for the same room per Section H, Step 2.
Correction: remeasure tape from the actual seam and corner layout and bead from the actual opening and edge conditions for that room.
Reinspection: repeat the proportionality check for the corrected room and one additional room of similar layout.

Field Terms

Finish Level
The documented compound and sanding requirement, Level 0 through Level 5 or an equivalent standard, assigned to a room or surface.
Rated Assembly
A wall or ceiling assembly with a specified fire, sound, or moisture rating that controls board type, layer count, and fastening schedule.
Net Area
Wall or ceiling area measured before or after opening deduction according to the workbook's approved method.
Sheet Count
Net area converted into full and partial board sheets using the approved board size and layout.
Repair Allowance
An approved additional tape or compound quantity carried for anticipated punch-list and callback repair.
Setting Compound
A chemically hardening joint compound used for early coats or rapid-sequence finishing, distinct from air-drying topping compound.
Corner Bead
A metal, vinyl, or paper-faced edge protection product installed at outside corners, reveals, arches, and terminations.
Control Joint
An installed joint that allows a large drywall surface to move without cracking, placed per the specified spacing or detail.
Reconciliation
Comparing a major quantity by an independent method or reviewer to confirm accuracy.
Direct Cost
Material, labor, equipment, and subcontract cost before general conditions, overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit.

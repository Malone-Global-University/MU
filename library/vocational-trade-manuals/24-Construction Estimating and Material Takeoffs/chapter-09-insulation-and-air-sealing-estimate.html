Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a complete insulation and air-sealing estimate inside the Chapter 1 workbook. The finished work product is one insulation and air-sealing worksheet covering exterior walls, sound walls, attics, roof slopes, cathedral ceilings, floors, rims, foundations, and mechanical spaces, with net quantities, purchase-unit conversions, air-sealing and vapor-control materials, accessory counts, labor, equipment, testing, pricing, and an independent reconciliation.

You will review the energy documents and insulation notes, separate the building into assemblies, measure each assembly's area or volume, select only the specified insulation type and method, convert measured quantities into installed purchase units, calculate air-sealing and vapor-control materials from joint geometry and product yield, price every item from dated sources, estimate labor and equipment, and reconcile the finished worksheet before it moves into pricing.

This project follows the Chapter 8 envelope estimate and precedes the Chapter 10 drywall estimate. The wall, attic, and floor assemblies identified in Chapter 8 and the framing information from Chapter 5 both carry forward into this chapter's assembly definitions. The insulation and air-sealing totals produced here carry forward into Chapter 17 labor, Chapter 18 pricing, and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-049 — Calculate wall insulation quantities by cavity area, type, depth, and installation method.
  • ET-050 — Calculate attic insulation quantities by floor or roof-slope area and installed depth.
  • ET-051 — Calculate floor insulation quantities for floors, rims, crawlspace walls, and foundation assemblies.
  • ET-052 — Estimate air-sealing products from joint geometry, product yield, and compatible material selection.
  • ET-053 — Estimate vapor-control materials by area, laps, transitions, and repair allowance.
  • ET-054 — Prepare an insulation estimate worksheet with reconciled quantities, package conversions, labor, equipment, testing, and total cost.

Where This Skill Fits

The wall, roof, and floor assemblies measured for sheathing in Chapter 6 and the envelope quantities measured in Chapter 8 define the cavities and surfaces insulated in this chapter. The insulation cost-code section built in Chapter 1 receives every line item produced here. Chapter 10 references the same wall and ceiling area measurements when it calculates drywall quantities, so the room and assembly boundaries set in this chapter must remain consistent with those used later.

The distinction between measured scope, allowance, and specified-product requirement enforced throughout this book is especially important here: insulation type, density, thickness, and facing must come from the documents or an approved equivalent, never from an assumed or substituted product.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • separating exterior walls, sound walls, attics, roof slopes, cathedral ceilings, floors, rims, foundations, and mechanical spaces
  • measuring cavity area, attic and roof-slope area, and floor and foundation area from approved documents
  • selecting only specified or approved insulation type, density, thickness, facing, and installation method
  • converting area and volume into bags, batts, rolls, boards, kits, or installed units using manufacturer coverage
  • calculating air-sealing joints, sealant, foam, gasket, membrane, and tape quantities
  • calculating vapor-control material with laps, transitions, penetrations, and repair allowance
  • estimating labor, equipment, testing, and pricing for the complete insulation and air-sealing scope

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select an insulation type, density, or vapor barrier that is not specified or approved by the documents
  • add an unapproved vapor barrier to an assembly where one is not specified
  • invent blown-in installed density, spray-foam yield, thermal or fire-barrier ratings, or coverage rates not supplied by the manufacturer or the documents
  • substitute a single generic coverage rate for every insulation product and substrate in the project

Any assembly, product, or air-sealing detail not clearly defined by the documents must be converted into a clarification, allowance, or approved assumption before pricing.

Trade Note

Insulation and air-sealing quantities are frequently miscalculated because a single wall-area or ceiling-area figure is reused across products with different coverage, waste, and installed-thickness characteristics. Measure each assembly once, then apply the correct product-specific conversion for each material that assembly requires.

Estimating Standard

Do not select an insulation type, thickness, or vapor barrier that is not specified or approved. Where the documents leave a thermal, air, or vapor requirement unresolved, record the item as a clarification and do not proceed with an assumed product until it is answered or converted into an approved assumption.

Shop Tip

Build the assembly schedule before opening a single quantity column. Deciding which wall, attic, or floor condition a line item belongs to after the area is already measured is how insulation quantities end up double-counted or omitted.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every insulation and air-sealing quantity in this chapter traces to a defined assembly, an approved product, and a documented coverage or yield basis. The same discipline used to separate net quantity from waste and order quantity in earlier chapters applies here, with the added requirement that a single wall or ceiling area figure is never applied to more than one product without confirming that product's own coverage and waste characteristics.

Product Selection Without Redesign

Identify the thermal, air, vapor, ignition-barrier, fire-blocking, sound, and ventilation function of every assembly directly from the documents. Do not redesign an assembly by substituting a different insulation type, density, or method than what is specified, even where the substitution appears equivalent. Convert any unresolved product question into a clarification.

Energy-Code Alert — Verify the Climate Zone and Applicable Code Edition

Thermal and air-barrier requirements vary by jurisdiction, climate zone, and adopted code edition. Confirm the applicable climate zone and code edition on the project-information sheet before selecting any insulation product or vapor-control detail, and do not apply a requirement from a different jurisdiction or edition.

Critical Control Cue — Do Not Add an Unapproved Vapor Barrier

An unapproved vapor barrier added to an assembly can trap moisture and cause a building-science failure. Install a vapor-control layer only where the documents specify one, and convert any assembly with an ambiguous vapor-control requirement into a clarification before pricing.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Use Product-Specific Coverage and Yield

Confirm the coverage rate for batts, the installed density and settled thickness for blown-in products, the yield for rigid board layouts, and the yield, thickness, temperature, and substrate basis for spray foam directly from the manufacturer's current published data before calculating any purchase quantity.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Assumption Separately

Record substitution, availability, and installation-method assumptions on the assumptions and risk register linked to the affected assembly row, not as a note buried inside the quantity calculation.

C. Purchase-Unit and Waste Basis

  • Batt and roll products: convert net cavity area into full and partial batts or roll length using the manufacturer's stated batt size or roll coverage; do not assume a generic coverage rate across different products.
  • Blown-in products: convert installed area and target settled thickness into bag count using the manufacturer's installed-density coverage chart for the specific product and target R-value.
  • Rigid board products: convert net board area into full sheet count using the specified board size, layer count, and joint layout, with waste applied for cuts and edge conditions.
  • Spray foam: convert area and specified thickness into set or kit count using the manufacturer's approved yield at the documented temperature and substrate condition only.
  • Sealant, foam, gasket, and tape: convert linear or unit joint counts into cartridge, can, or roll count using the manufacturer's stated yield or coverage per unit.
  • Vapor-control membrane: convert net area plus laps, transitions, and approved repair allowance into roll count using the manufacturer's roll coverage.

E. Preparation — Document Review and Assembly Definition

  1. Review energy documents, sections, insulation notes, air-barrier details, specifications, and climate requirements. Confirm the applicable climate zone and code edition on the project-information sheet.
  2. Identify thermal, air, vapor, ignition-barrier, fire-blocking, sound, and ventilation functions without redesign. Record each function next to its assembly on the assembly schedule; do not substitute a different function or product than specified.
  3. Separate exterior walls, sound walls, attics, roof slopes, cathedral ceilings, floors, rims, foundations, and mechanical spaces. Create one assembly row for each on the assembly schedule.
  4. Measure wall cavity area by type, height, and opening rule. Apply the approved opening-deduction rule from the workbook conventions and record the source for each measurement.
  5. Measure attic floor or roof-slope area using actual geometry. Reconcile with the roof-slope area calculated in Chapter 7 where that chapter has already been completed for the same project.
  6. Measure floors, rims, crawlspace walls, basement walls, and foundation insulation. Record area separately for each condition.
  7. Identify cavity depth, framing spacing, narrow cavities, irregular spaces, and obstructions. Note each condition that will affect the installed method or require a specialty product.

F. Quantity, Purchase-Unit, and Air-Sealing Build Procedure

  1. Select only specified or approved insulation type, density, thickness, facing, and method. Record the specification or approval reference for each assembly row.
  2. Convert area or volume into bags, batts, rolls, boards, kits, or installed units using manufacturer coverage. Apply the Section C basis for the specific product.
  3. Adjust blown-in quantities for installed density and settled thickness. Use the manufacturer's installed-density chart for the specified R-value; do not use a generic bag-per-square-foot rate.
  4. Calculate rigid insulation by board area, layer, joint layout, edge, and waste. Record layer count separately where more than one layer is specified.
  5. Calculate spray foam only from approved yield, thickness, temperature, and substrate basis. Do not calculate a spray-foam quantity without a documented yield source.
  6. Identify fire or thermal barriers, coatings, mesh, netting, supports, baffles, dams, and markers. Count each by location and add to the accessory schedule.
  7. Measure air-sealing joints at plates, rims, penetrations, openings, shafts, ducts, and service entries. Record each joint location, length or count, and condition on the air-sealing joint schedule.
  8. Select sealant, foam, gasket, membrane, tape, mortar, or other compatible product. Confirm compatibility with adjacent materials before selection.
  9. Calculate sealant and foam from joint geometry and yield. Apply the Section C sealant and foam basis.
  10. Calculate tapes and membranes from length, width, overlap, corner, and penetration conditions. Record overlap and corner allowances separately from field area.
  11. Measure vapor-control material with laps, transitions, penetrations, and repair allowance. Apply the Section C vapor-control membrane basis.
  12. Do not add an unapproved vapor barrier. Confirm every vapor-control row traces to a specific specification reference before pricing.
  13. Count attic baffles, wind dams, access dams, rulers, hatch insulation, and weatherstripping. Add each to the accessory schedule with location and quantity.

G. Access, Labor, Equipment, and Testing

  1. Include PPE, ventilation, containment, masking, cleanup, and disposal. Record each as a separate line item where it carries its own cost.
  2. Include lifts, scaffolds, crawlspace access, confined-work restrictions, and temporary lighting. Identify any access condition that will affect production rate.
  3. Estimate labor by batt, blown, board, spray, membrane, air sealing, baffle, and inspection task. Separate labor rows by task type on the labor schedule.
  4. Adjust production for height, congestion, occupancy, irregular framing, temperature, and testing. Record the adjustment basis for each affected task.
  5. Include blower-door, depth, density, infrared, or other testing only when assigned. Do not add a testing line item unless the documents or scope require it.
  6. Price products from dated sources confirming coverage, package, lead time, storage, and shelf life. Record the quotation date, source, and expiration for each priced item.
  7. Include freight, minimum order, equipment rental, hose, machine, fuel, and crew requirements. Add each as a separate cost row linked to the affected assembly or task.

H. Reconciliation and Worksheet Assembly

  1. Reconcile insulation area against conditioned-envelope geometry. Compare the sum of wall, attic, roof-slope, and floor areas against the building's overall conditioned-envelope dimensions using an independent method.
  2. Compare package count with coverage and installed depth. Confirm each product's order quantity produces the specified installed depth or density when applied to the net area.
  3. Prepare the worksheet by assembly, product, quantity, labor, equipment, testing, and total. Confirm every row links correctly to the workbook's Insulation cost-code section and summary sheets.

I. Closeout — Independent Review and Documentation

  1. Complete independent review of area, coverage, depth, and air-sealing scope. Have a second reviewer or an independent recalculation confirm at least the highest-value assembly and the highest-risk air-sealing joint.
  2. Document assumptions, exclusions, and unresolved product or coverage questions on the assumptions and risk register. Link each entry to the affected assembly row before the worksheet is treated as complete.

Common Worksheet Inspection

Inspect the finished insulation and air-sealing worksheet using the same checks regardless of which assembly or product category is being reviewed.

  • Assembly check: confirm every wall, sound wall, attic, roof-slope, cathedral-ceiling, floor, rim, foundation, and mechanical-space assembly in the documents appears on the assembly schedule.
  • Product-selection check: confirm every insulation, air-sealing, and vapor-control product traces to a specification reference or approved clarification, with no substituted or assumed product.
  • Purchase-unit check: confirm every order quantity uses the correct manufacturer coverage or yield for its specific product, not a generic rate borrowed from a different product.
  • Vapor-barrier check: confirm no vapor-control line item was added to an assembly without a specification reference.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm the total insulated area reconciles with the conditioned-envelope geometry using an independent method.
  • Documentation check: confirm every assumption, exclusion, and unresolved question is recorded and linked to its assembly row.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Order Quantity Does Not Produce the Specified Installed Depth

Probable cause: the order-quantity formula used a generic coverage rate instead of the specific manufacturer chart for the target R-value and product.
Confirmation: compare the order quantity's implied coverage against the manufacturer's installed-density chart for the specified R-value.
Correction: rebuild the order-quantity formula using the correct manufacturer coverage for that exact product and target R-value.
Reinspection: repeat the Section H, Step 2 package-coverage comparison for the corrected row.

A Vapor-Control Line Item Has No Specification Reference

Probable cause: a vapor barrier was added by assumption to match a similar assembly elsewhere in the project instead of being confirmed against the documents.
Confirmation: check the vapor-control row's source field for a specification or approved-clarification reference.
Correction: remove the unapproved vapor-control item, or submit a clarification and hold the item as unresolved until answered.
Reinspection: repeat the vapor-barrier check across every assembly row.

Insulated Area Does Not Reconcile With Conditioned-Envelope Geometry

Probable cause: an assembly was measured twice, an assembly was omitted, or an opening-deduction rule was applied inconsistently between assemblies.
Confirmation: compare the assembly-by-assembly area sum against an independent overall conditioned-envelope calculation and isolate the assembly causing the variance.
Correction: remeasure the affected assembly, confirm the opening-deduction rule was applied consistently, and correct the area entry.
Reinspection: repeat the Section H, Step 1 reconciliation after correction.

A Spray-Foam Quantity Has No Documented Yield Source

Probable cause: a yield figure was estimated from memory or a different project instead of the current manufacturer's published yield for the specified thickness, temperature, and substrate.
Confirmation: check the spray-foam row's source field for a specific manufacturer yield reference.
Correction: obtain the current manufacturer yield data for the specified product, thickness, temperature, and substrate, and recalculate the quantity.
Reinspection: repeat the purchase-unit check for the corrected row.

An Air-Sealing Joint Is Missing From the Estimate

Probable cause: the air-sealing joint schedule was built from a single detail sheet instead of reviewing plates, rims, penetrations, openings, shafts, ducts, and service entries across the full set.
Confirmation: compare the air-sealing joint schedule against every penetration and transition shown in the air-barrier details and mechanical documents.
Correction: add the missing joint to the schedule with its product and yield-based quantity.
Reinspection: repeat the air-sealing joint schedule review after correction.

Field Terms

Assembly
A defined thermal or air-barrier boundary measured and insulated as one unit.
Installed Density
The density a blown-in product must achieve at the installed thickness to meet the specified R-value.
Settled Thickness
The thickness a blown-in product reaches after settling, used with installed density to confirm coverage.
Yield
The coverage a sealant, foam, or adhesive product achieves per unit of product.
Repair Allowance
An approved additional quantity of vapor-control material reserved for field damage and penetrations.
Ignition Barrier
A required covering over certain foam products to limit ignition, distinct from a thermal or vapor barrier.
Air Barrier
The continuous system of materials and joints that controls air movement through the envelope.
Conditioned-Envelope Geometry
The overall building dimensions used to independently check total insulated area.

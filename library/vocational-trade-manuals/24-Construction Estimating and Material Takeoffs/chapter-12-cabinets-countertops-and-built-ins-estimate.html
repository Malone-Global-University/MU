Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build a complete cabinet, countertop, and built-in cost sheet for the residential estimate started in Project 01. The finished work product is one cost sheet in the Chapter 1 workbook containing a room-by-room cabinet and built-in schedule, countertop area and edge quantities, hardware and accessory counts, cutout coordination, allowance and quotation records, self-performed and coordinated labor entries, and a reconciled total by room, tag, top, labor, delivery, and allowance.

You will count and record cabinet boxes by tag from the approved cabinet plans and schedules, measure fillers, panels, trim, and shelving, calculate countertop developed length and area with edge, backsplash, seam, and cutout quantities, count hardware, level supplier and fabricator quotations to the same scope, price the estimate from dated sources, and complete an independent review before the sheet is entered into the workbook.

This is the twelfth project in the book. It draws its takeoff discipline from Chapter 2's scope review and feeds directly into Chapter 17's labor estimate, Chapter 18's pricing, and the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-067 — Count cabinet boxes by tag, type, dimension, and accessory from approved plans and schedules.
  • ET-068 — Estimate fillers and panels, including scribes, end panels, skins, finished backs, and appliance panels.
  • ET-069 — Calculate countertop lengths, area, edge, backsplash, seams, and cutouts.
  • ET-070 — Estimate hardware, including pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, and specialty mechanisms.
  • ET-071 — Estimate shelving materials, including shelves, pins, brackets, cleats, and rods.
  • ET-072 — Prepare a cabinet and built-in cost sheet with reconciled quantities, labor, delivery, allowances, and total.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends on the workbook cost codes and formulas built in Project 01 and the scope boundaries confirmed in Project 02. The cabinet and countertop quantities entered here flow into the Chapter 17 labor estimate for stock, semi-custom, custom, and built-in installation, into the Chapter 18 pricing worksheet for material, fabricator, and delivery costs, and into the Chapter 19 proposal and Chapter 20 capstone as one of the interior finish cost-code sections.

The count-by-tag discipline used here — recording every cabinet, filler, panel, and cutout against its plan reference before pricing begins — is the same discipline the flooring takeoff in Chapter 13 and the paint and finish estimate in Chapter 14 rely on to keep interior quantities traceable to the approved documents.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • counting base, wall, tall, vanity, island, specialty, and utility cabinets by tag from approved plans, elevations, and schedules
  • measuring fillers, scribes, end panels, skins, finished backs, appliance panels, toe kicks, and supports
  • calculating countertop developed length, area, edge length, backsplash, waterfall panels, seams, cutouts, overhangs, and supports
  • counting hardware, shelving, and built-in components from approved selections and details
  • leveling supplier and fabricator proposals to the same scope and identifying allowances
  • preparing a reconciled cost sheet by room, tag, accessory, top, labor, delivery, and allowance

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select cabinet construction, style, finish, hardware, or countertop material on the owner's behalf; use only the selections shown in the approved documents or a recorded owner selection
  • price field cutting of specialty tops when the fabricator proposal states fabricator cutting is required
  • design or size backing, supports, or structural reinforcement; use only backing and reinforcement shown in the approved documents or assigned by scope
  • select the lowest fabricator or supplier proposal before scope gaps and qualifications are resolved

Any cabinet plan, appliance dimension, or countertop detail that cannot be confirmed from the approved documents must be converted to a clarification, allowance, or exclusion before the cost sheet is completed.

Trade Note

A cabinet count taken from a floor plan alone, without checking the elevations and schedule, is the most common source of a missed filler, missed appliance panel, or wrong-handed cabinet in a residential kitchen estimate. Always reconcile the plan, elevation, and schedule together before recording a count.

Setup Standard

Do not price any cabinet, countertop, or built-in line until its tag, dimension, and accessory count are confirmed against the plan, elevation, and schedule together. A count taken from only one document must be treated as unverified until the other documents confirm it.

Shop Tip

Build the countertop cutout list before pricing the countertop. A fabricator quotation priced against the wrong cutout count is a change order waiting to happen.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every cabinet and built-in estimate in this book starts from the same reconciliation discipline: count the box by tag, confirm its dimension and accessories, measure the panels and fillers around it, calculate the countertop that sits on it, count its hardware, and price the whole assembly from a leveled quotation before any total is entered into the workbook.

Counting Cabinets by Tag

Count every cabinet by the tag shown on the plan or schedule, not by visual estimation from the elevation alone. Record width, height, depth, handedness, door count, drawer count, and accessories for each tag. Where a tag appears in more than one room, count each occurrence separately and do not assume identical accessories without confirming against the schedule.

Traceability Cue — Record the Plan or Schedule Reference With Every Count

Enter the plan sheet or schedule reference for every cabinet count the moment it is recorded, not afterward. A cabinet count without a recorded source cannot be defended in a later review and must be treated as unverified until a source is added.

Critical Control Cue — Confirm Appliance Dimensions Before Pricing Panels and Fillers

Confirm actual appliance dimensions, swings, utilities, and clearances before pricing any filler, panel, or opening built around that appliance. Pricing a filler or panel against an assumed appliance dimension that does not match the confirmed dimension will produce an incorrect count that is not caught by any later error check.

Documentation Cue — Log Every Selection and Allowance

Record the room, item, selection status, and allowance amount for every cabinet, top, or hardware item not yet finalized. State plainly what each allowance includes and excludes before it is entered into the cost sheet.

Fabricator Cue — Confirm Template and Cutting Responsibility

Confirm whether the countertop fabricator requires a field template, in-house measurement, or plan-based fabrication, and confirm which party is responsible for field cutting of specialty tops. Do not price field cutting labor when the fabricator proposal states fabricator cutting is required.

  • Cabinet categories: base, wall, tall, vanity, island, specialty, and utility cabinets, each counted by tag from the approved schedule.
  • Specialty storage: sink bases, appliance cabinets, corners, pullouts, trash units, dividers, and other specialty storage identified separately from standard boxes.

E. Takeoff and Count Procedure

  1. Review cabinet plans, elevations, schedules, appliance information, countertop details, selections, and specifications. Confirm the current revision of each document before counting.
  2. Identify cabinet construction, style, material, finish, box dimensions, fronts, overlay, and hardware. Record these on the room-by-room schedule.
  3. Count base, wall, tall, vanity, island, specialty, and utility cabinets by tag. Record each tag occurrence on a separate row.
  4. Record width, height, depth, handedness, door count, drawer count, and accessories for each tag. Confirm against both the elevation and the schedule.
  5. Identify sink bases, appliance cabinets, corners, pullouts, trash units, dividers, and specialty storage. Record each as its own line.
  6. Measure fillers, scribes, end panels, skins, finished backs, appliance panels, toe kicks, and supports. Record the condition requiring each.
  7. Measure crown, light rail, valance, base trim, decorative molding, and furniture ends. Record by elevation.
  8. Count shelves, pins, brackets, cleats, rods, and adjustable hardware. Record by cabinet tag or built-in component.
  9. Count pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, catches, latches, and specialty mechanisms not included in the box price. Confirm which hardware is included in the cabinet quotation and which must be priced separately.
  10. Confirm soft-close, extension, load rating, finish, and hole spacing for all hardware. Record against the approved specification or selection.
  11. Measure countertop developed length and area by material and fabrication method. Use the Section D quantity basis.
  12. Calculate edge length, backsplash, waterfall panels, seams, cutouts, overhangs, and supports. Record each against its own line.
  13. Identify sink, cooktop, faucet, outlet, appliance, and accessory cutouts. Confirm each cutout dimension against the confirmed fixture or appliance dimension before recording.
  14. Do not price field cutting of specialty tops when fabricator work is required. Confirm cutting responsibility from the fabricator quotation.
  15. Count brackets, corbels, concealed supports, legs, anchors, and rails. Record against the approved detail requiring each.
  16. Measure built-in shelving, benches, desks, bookcases, mudroom units, and custom components. Record each as its own line with material, finish, and dimension.
  17. Prepare a room-by-room cabinet and built-in schedule. Consolidate every count from Steps 3 through 16 by room.
  18. Confirm appliance dimensions, swings, utilities, wall dimensions, floor level, and clearances. Record the confirmation source for each.
  19. Identify shop drawings, field verification, samples, templates, and approvals required before fabrication. Record each as a coordination item.

F. Pricing, Coordination, and Reconciliation Procedure

  1. Include delivery, inspection, protected storage, acclimation, unpacking, and disposal. Confirm which party is responsible for each.
  2. Include layout, shimming, fastening, scribing, filler cutting, panel installation, trim, and adjustment. Record as labor scope, not material scope.
  3. Include countertop template, fabrication, delivery, lifting, seams, setting, support, and polish where quoted. Confirm which of these the fabricator quotation includes.
  4. Coordinate sink, faucet, appliance, plumbing, electrical, and backsplash without duplication. Confirm each item is priced in only one trade's scope.
  5. Include backing and substrate preparation only where assigned. Do not add backing or substrate scope not shown in the approved documents.
  6. Estimate labor separately for stock, semi-custom, custom, built-in, hardware, countertop coordination, and punch. Record each labor category against its own line.
  7. Adjust productivity for access, tall units, custom scribing, fragile finishes, and heavy tops. Record the adjustment basis.
  8. Price cabinets and tops from dated quotations identifying selections, freight, tax, installation, lead time, deposit, and exclusions. Enter the quotation date and source on every priced row.
  9. Level supplier and fabricator proposals to the same scope. Adjust each proposal so quantity, included services, and exclusions match before comparing price.
  10. Identify allowances and state their included cost components. Record what each allowance includes and excludes.
  11. Reconcile cabinet count against elevations, plans, and appliances. Confirm every tag on the schedule appears once on the cost sheet and every appliance clearance is confirmed.
  12. Reconcile countertop area, edge, backsplash, cutouts, and support. Confirm the fabricator quotation's area and cutout count match the takeoff.
  13. Prepare the cost sheet by room, tag, accessory, top, labor, delivery, allowance, and total. Enter the reconciled quantities into the workbook cost-code section.
  14. Document exclusions and unresolved dimensions, selections, hardware, support, and responsibility. Convert each unresolved item into a clarification, allowance, or exclusion.
  15. Complete independent review of boxes, panels, fillers, tops, cutouts, and long-lead items. Have a second reviewer confirm the count and reconciliation before pricing is finalized.

Common Cost-Sheet Inspection

Inspect the finished cost sheet using the same checks regardless of which room or cabinet run is being reviewed.

  • Tag check: confirm every cabinet tag on the approved schedule appears exactly once on the cost sheet, with no omitted or duplicated tag.
  • Clearance check: confirm appliance dimensions, swings, and clearances are confirmed for every appliance-adjacent cabinet, panel, or filler.
  • Countertop check: confirm developed length, area, edge, backsplash, seams, cutouts, and supports are recorded for every top and match the fabricator quotation.
  • Cutout check: confirm every sink, cooktop, faucet, outlet, and appliance cutout is recorded against a confirmed fixture or appliance dimension.
  • Hardware check: confirm hardware included in the cabinet quotation is not duplicated in the separate hardware count.
  • Leveling check: confirm every compared supplier or fabricator proposal is leveled to the same scope before its price is used.
  • Allowance check: confirm every allowance states its included cost components.
  • Coordination check: confirm sink, faucet, appliance, plumbing, electrical, and backsplash items are priced in only one trade's scope.

Complete the Cost-Sheet Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 13.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Cabinet Tag Appears on the Schedule but Not on the Cost Sheet

Probable cause: the count was taken from the elevation only and the schedule was not cross-referenced, or a tag was skipped when transcribing rows.
Confirmation: compare the cost sheet row list against every tag on the approved schedule, one tag at a time.
Correction: add the missing tag with its dimension, handedness, and accessories, sourced from the schedule and elevation together.
Reinspection: repeat the tag check across the full schedule after correction.

A Countertop Cutout Does Not Match the Fixture or Appliance Dimension

Probable cause: the cutout was sized from an assumed standard dimension instead of the confirmed fixture or appliance dimension.
Confirmation: compare the recorded cutout dimension against the confirmed fixture or appliance cut sheet.
Correction: update the cutout dimension from the confirmed cut sheet and notify the fabricator before fabrication proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the cutout check against all confirmed fixture and appliance dimensions.

Hardware Cost Appears Twice

Probable cause: hardware included in the cabinet box quotation was also entered as a separate hardware line without checking the quotation's inclusions.
Confirmation: review the cabinet quotation's stated inclusions against the separate hardware count.
Correction: remove the duplicated hardware line or adjust the separate hardware count to include only items not covered by the box quotation.
Reinspection: repeat the hardware check against the corrected quotation inclusions.

Two Proposals Are Compared Without Being Leveled

Probable cause: one proposal excludes delivery, installation, or hardware that the other proposal includes, and the comparison did not adjust for the difference.
Confirmation: list every included and excluded cost component for both proposals side by side.
Correction: adjust each proposal to a matched scope before comparing price, and document the adjustment.
Reinspection: repeat the leveling check on the corrected comparison.

An Allowance Does Not State Its Included Components

Probable cause: the allowance amount was entered as a lump sum without recording what it covers.
Confirmation: review the allowance and exclusion log for the affected line.
Correction: add the included material, labor, tax, delivery, markup, and installation components to the allowance record.
Reinspection: repeat the allowance check on the corrected record.

A Filler or Panel Was Sized From an Assumed Appliance Dimension

Probable cause: the filler or panel was measured before the actual appliance dimension was confirmed.
Confirmation: compare the recorded filler or panel dimension against the confirmed appliance cut sheet or field measurement.
Correction: update the filler or panel dimension from the confirmed source and record the source on the row.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check across all appliance-adjacent lines.

Field Terms

Cabinet Tag
The identifier assigned to a cabinet on the plan or schedule.
Developed Length
The total linear length of a countertop measured along its actual run, including returns and corners.
Filler
A non-functioning strip of material used to close a gap between a cabinet and a wall, appliance, or another cabinet.
Scribe
A strip of material left oversized and cut on site to fit an irregular condition.
Cutout
An opening fabricated into a countertop for a sink, cooktop, faucet, outlet, or other accessory.
Waterfall Panel
A countertop panel that continues the top's material down a vertical face, such as an island end.
Leveled Proposal
A supplier or fabricator proposal adjusted so its scope, quantity, and exclusions match the proposals being compared.
Allowance
A stated dollar amount covering an item not yet finalized, with its included scope stated separately.
Overlay
The amount a cabinet door or drawer front covers the box frame or opening.
Finished End
A cabinet side panel finished to match exposed cabinetry where no adjoining cabinet or panel conceals it.

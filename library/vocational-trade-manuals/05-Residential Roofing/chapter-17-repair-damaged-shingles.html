Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to remove and replace one or more damaged asphalt shingles without damaging the surrounding roofing, underlayment, flashing, or deck. The finished work product is a repaired shingle field in which the replacement shingle or shingles match the surrounding course exposure, joint offset, fastening, and sealing, with the underlying deck and underlayment left intact or restored under an approved reference where minor repair was authorized.

You will identify the exact damaged shingles, determine whether the damage is isolated or evidence of a larger problem, inspect the attic or underside where accessible, select matching replacement shingles, release the adhesive bond and fasteners on the courses above the damage without tearing reusable shingles, remove the damaged shingle without cutting the underlayment or disturbing adjacent flashing, install and fasten the replacement shingle, refasten any disturbed upper shingles, hand-seal the repair, and document the repair location, cause, and inspection results.

This chapter depends on the shingle-installation and fastening knowledge built in Chapters 6 through 9 and on the flashing-integration knowledge built in Chapters 10 through 12. It also feeds directly into Chapter 18, where a damaged or improperly repaired shingle is one of several possible sources of a traced leak, and into Chapter 19, where damage that extends into the deck is handled under a larger structural repair scope.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-097 — Identify damaged shingles and distinguish isolated damage from evidence of a larger deck, flashing, wind, heat, impact, or installation problem.
  • RF-098 — Loosen surrounding tabs and release adhesive bonds and fasteners on the courses above the damaged shingle without tearing granule surfaces or creasing reusable shingles.
  • RF-099 — Remove damaged shingles without cutting the underlayment or disturbing adjacent flashing.
  • RF-100 — Install replacement shingles aligned with course exposure and joints, fastened through the approved fastening zone with the specified nail quantity and location.
  • RF-101 — Seal the repair area using compatible hand-seal adhesive at the manufacturer-required tab locations without smearing sealant onto exposed surfaces.
  • RF-102 — Inspect the completed repair for exposure, alignment, joint offset, fastener concealment, tab adhesion, granule condition, and color variation.

Where This Skill Fits

Every roof eventually shows isolated shingle damage from wind, impact, heat cycling, foot traffic, or an installation defect that was not caught during the original field-shingle work in Chapters 8 and 9. This chapter gives you the controlled procedure for correcting that isolated damage without disturbing the water-shedding order established in the earlier chapters — releasing shingles from the top down, replacing only what is damaged, and restoring the same lap and fastening pattern.

This chapter is also the first stop when isolated damage is suspected but a leak has not yet been traced. If the inspection in this chapter reveals evidence of a larger problem — widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active leakage, or structural movement — the work stops here and moves to the diagnostic procedure in Chapter 18 or the structural repair procedure in Chapter 19.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying the exact damaged shingles and confirming whether the damage is isolated
  • inspecting the attic or underside where accessible for leakage, deck damage, and missed fasteners
  • selecting matching replacement shingles of the same approved product, size, exposure, and compatible color where available
  • releasing the adhesive bond and fasteners on the courses above the damaged shingle without tearing or creasing reusable material
  • removing the damaged shingle without cutting the underlayment or disturbing adjacent flashing
  • inspecting the exposed underlayment and deck for concealed defects
  • installing and fastening the replacement shingle, refastening disturbed upper shingles, and hand-sealing the repair
  • documenting the repair location, cause, materials, concealed conditions, and inspection results

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with a shingle repair when the damage includes widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active leakage, structural movement, or hazardous materials; stop and expand the investigation instead
  • perform any underlayment repair beyond an approved minor repair; refer deck or extensive underlayment damage to Chapter 19
  • trace an undiagnosed leak to its source; that procedure is covered in Chapter 18
  • substitute a non-matching shingle product, size, or exposure, or apply sealant as a substitute for correct fastening and lap restoration

Any repair location where the investigation reveals damage beyond isolated shingle failure must be referred to the applicable chapter or responsible authority before the shingle repair proceeds.

Trade Note

A shingle repair is only as good as the fastening and lap pattern it restores. Do not treat this chapter as a sealant-and-nail patch. Every replacement shingle must be fastened in the original zone, at the original quantity, and lapped exactly as the surrounding field shingles are lapped, or the repair introduces a new water path instead of closing one.

Access Standard

Perform this repair only from the roof-access and fall-protection system already accepted for the site under Chapter 2. This chapter does not establish or re-evaluate roof access. If access conditions have changed since the original setup, stop and correct access before beginning the repair.

Shop Tip

Warm shingles only by an approved method when cold or brittle conditions would otherwise cause cracking during tab lifting. Never force a cold, brittle tab; a cracked tab on a shingle you intended to reuse turns a one-shingle repair into a multi-shingle repair.

Common Field Fundamentals

A shingle repair follows the same top-down release and bottom-up restoration logic used throughout the book: the layer that sheds water onto the damaged shingle must come off first and go back on last. Skipping that order — for example, prying out a damaged shingle without first releasing the course above it — tears reusable material and can create a reverse lap that the repair itself introduces.

Identifying Isolated Damage vs. a Larger Problem

Identify the exact damaged shingles and determine whether the damage is confined to those shingles or is evidence of a larger deck, flashing, wind, heat, or installation problem. Inspect the attic or underside where accessible for leakage, deck damage, and missed fasteners before proceeding with the physical repair.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop for Evidence of a Larger Problem

Stop work and expand the investigation if the damage includes widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active leakage, structural movement, or hazardous materials. Do not proceed with an isolated shingle repair over a condition that requires the Chapter 18 diagnostic procedure or the Chapter 19 structural repair procedure.

Field Safety Cue — Cold and Brittle Shingles

Warm or condition shingles only by an approved method when cold or brittle conditions would cause cracking during tab lifting. Do not force a tab that resists lifting in cold weather.

Manufacturer Cue — Matching Product and Fastening

Select replacement shingles of the same approved product, size, exposure, and compatible color where available. Fasten the replacement through the approved fastening zone using the specified nail quantity and location; do not substitute a fastening pattern that differs from the original manufacturer instructions.

Code Alert — Concealed Deck or Underlayment Damage

Punctures, moisture, rot, loose fasteners, or other concealed defects found in the exposed underlayment or deck are outside this chapter's authorized correction scope beyond minor underlayment repair. Refer deck damage to Chapter 19 before completing the shingle repair over that section.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review the shingle product, repair instructions, weather limits, sealant requirements, matching-material availability, and roof-access controls. Confirm the original product identification where available and record it on the repair record.
  2. Identify the exact damaged shingles and determine whether damage is isolated or evidence of a larger deck, flashing, wind, heat, impact, or installation problem. Mark the damaged shingles and the surrounding area, and record the observed damage type for each.
  3. Inspect the attic or underside where accessible for leakage, deck damage, and missed fasteners. Use the attic or another interior access point and a flashlight, and record any finding against the framing or grid reference established in Chapter 1.
  4. Stop work and expand the investigation if the damage includes widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active leakage, structural movement, or hazardous materials. Route the finding to the Chapter 18 diagnostic procedure, the Chapter 19 structural repair procedure, or another responsible authority before continuing this chapter's repair.
  5. Select replacement shingles of the same approved product, size, exposure, and compatible color where available. Record the replacement product identification on the repair record.
  6. Mark the replacement limits and identify the courses whose adhesive seals and fasteners must be released. Mark the release course directly above each damaged shingle using chalk visible from the working position.
  7. Warm or condition shingles only by an approved method when cold or brittle conditions would cause cracking. Do not proceed with tab lifting on a shingle that resists lifting without conditioning.
  8. Carefully lift the tabs in the courses above the damaged shingle using the specified flat tool. Work from a stable position and lift only the tabs identified in Step 6.
  9. Release the adhesive bonds without tearing granule surfaces or creasing reusable shingles. Work the flat tool along the adhesive line rather than prying upward against the tab.
  10. Remove or raise the fasteners that secure the damaged shingle and any fasteners from the course above that block removal. Record each fastener location removed so it can be restored in Step 16.
  11. Slide the damaged shingle out without cutting the underlayment or disturbing adjacent flashing. Work the damaged shingle free from its exposed edge and stop if it does not release cleanly; re-check for a missed fastener rather than forcing it.
  12. Inspect the exposed underlayment and deck for punctures, moisture, rot, loose fasteners, or concealed defects. Record every finding, including its exact location relative to the repair.
  13. Complete only approved minor underlayment repairs; refer deck damage to Chapter 19. Do not proceed with the shingle replacement over an unresolved deck or major underlayment defect.
  14. Slide the replacement shingle into the original position and align it with course exposure and joints. Confirm the replacement matches the horizontal exposure line and the joint offset of the surrounding course before fastening.
  15. Fasten the replacement through the approved fastening zone using the specified nail quantity and location. Seat each fastener flush without cutting the shingle surface or leaving a raised head.
  16. Refasten any upper shingles whose nails were removed. Use the fastener locations recorded in Step 10 and confirm each is seated to the same standard as Step 15.
  17. Apply compatible hand-seal adhesive at the manufacturer-required tab locations. Apply only the quantity and location specified by the manufacturer's repair instructions.
  18. Press tabs into position without smearing sealant onto exposed surfaces. Confirm each tab lies flat against the course below before releasing pressure.
  19. Inspect exposure, alignment, joint offset, fastener concealment, tab adhesion, granule condition, and color variation. Compare the repair to the surrounding field shingles from a safe position.
  20. Remove debris and document repair location, cause, materials, concealed conditions, and inspection results. Attach photographs to the repair record and file it with the project record.

Common Repair Checks

Apply the same checks to every damaged-shingle repair, regardless of the number of shingles involved.

  • Classification check: confirm the damage is isolated before proceeding, or route the finding to Chapter 18 or Chapter 19.
  • Product-match check: confirm the replacement shingle matches the original product, size, exposure, and compatible color where available.
  • Release check: confirm the release course was lifted and released without tearing granule surfaces or creasing reusable shingles.
  • Concealed-condition check: confirm the exposed underlayment and deck were inspected and any finding was corrected or referred to the correct authority.
  • Fastening check: confirm the replacement and every refastened upper shingle are seated flush in the approved fastening zone.
  • Sealing check: confirm hand-seal adhesive was applied only at required locations without smearing onto exposed surfaces.
  • Documentation check: confirm the repair record, photographs, and inspection results are complete before closing the repair.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Release-Course Tab Cracks During Lifting

Probable cause: the shingle was too cold or brittle when lifting began, or the flat tool was pried upward against the tab rather than worked along the adhesive line.
Confirmation: inspect the cracked tab and compare the ambient temperature and shingle condition to the manufacturer's approved conditioning method.
Correction: replace the cracked release-course shingle as an additional repair item rather than reusing it, and record the additional shingle on the repair record.
Reinspection: recheck the enlarged repair area for exposure, alignment, and fastening once the additional shingle is installed.

Replacement Shingle Does Not Match Surrounding Color

Probable cause: the original product is discontinued, weathered, or from a different lot than available replacement stock.
Confirmation: compare the replacement shingle to the surrounding field shingles in daylight from a safe position.
Correction: select the closest compatible color available and record the color variation on the repair record; do not substitute an incompatible shingle product to force a closer match.
Reinspection: recheck the visual result after installation and record the accepted variation.

Underlayment Beneath the Damaged Shingle Is Punctured or Wet

Probable cause: the same event that damaged the shingle also penetrated the underlayment, or a pre-existing leak caused both conditions.
Confirmation: inspect the exposed underlayment and deck directly and check the attic underside for corresponding staining.
Correction: complete only an approved minor underlayment repair; refer deck damage, active leakage, or extensive underlayment damage to Chapter 19 before installing the replacement shingle.
Reinspection: recheck the area once the underlayment or deck repair is complete and accepted.

Replacement Shingle Sits Proud or Does Not Lie Flat

Probable cause: a fastener from the course above was not fully removed, debris remains beneath the replacement, or the replacement was not slid fully into the original position.
Confirmation: lift the surrounding tabs again and inspect for an obstruction or a remaining fastener.
Correction: remove the obstruction or remaining fastener and reposition the replacement shingle before refastening.
Reinspection: recheck that the shingle lies flat and aligns with the surrounding course exposure.

Investigation Reveals Evidence of a Larger Problem After Repair Has Begun

Probable cause: isolated damage was assumed at Step 2, but removal or attic inspection reveals wider uplift, active leakage, or structural movement.
Confirmation: stop the repair and reinspect the surrounding roof area and attic underside for the full extent of the condition.
Correction: stop work at this chapter's scope, secure the open area against weather, and route the finding to the Chapter 18 diagnostic procedure or the Chapter 19 structural repair procedure.
Reinspection: resume this chapter's repair only after the referred condition is resolved and the affected area is cleared to proceed.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, matched, and sealed repair, not loose cutoffs and unresolved fasteners on the roof.

  • Remove the damaged shingle, cutoffs, and released fasteners from the roof surface and gutters
  • Dispose of removed material using an approved method that does not discharge material from the roof edge or the ground below
  • Store any unused matching replacement shingles for future repairs at the same roof
  • Compile the repair record, photographs, and inspection results into the project record
  • Note the repair location on the roof sketch or readiness record established in Chapter 1 for future reference

Performance Check

With the repair complete, review the repair record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the damage was correctly classified as isolated, the attic or underside was inspected, the replacement product matches the original in size, exposure, and compatible color, the release course was lifted without tearing or creasing, the exposed underlayment and deck were inspected and any finding corrected or referred, the replacement and refastened upper shingles are seated flush in the approved fastening zone, hand-seal adhesive was applied only at required locations, and the completed repair matches the surrounding field in exposure, alignment, and color. Chapter Closeout

You have completed a controlled shingle repair: identifying damaged shingles and distinguishing isolated damage from a larger problem (RF-097), releasing surrounding tabs without damage (RF-098), removing the damaged shingle without disturbing the underlayment or flashing (RF-099), installing and fastening a matching replacement (RF-100), sealing the repair at the required tab locations (RF-101), and inspecting and documenting the completed repair (RF-102).

You now have a documented repair record that fixes the repair location, cause, and inspection results for this section of roof. That record supports any future inspection of the same location and feeds directly into Chapter 18 if a leak is later traced near this repair, or into Chapter 19 if deck-level damage is discovered at a different location.

Store the completed repair record and photographs with the project record. Reference this record if a future leak investigation traces to this location.

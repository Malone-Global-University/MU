Shingle a Roof Valley

Install one approved open, closed-cut, or woven asphalt-shingle valley, coordinating both adjoining roof planes over the required valley membrane and metal so that water is carried downslope without exposed fasteners or interrupted shingle laps.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to install one complete asphalt-shingle valley, using the approved open, closed-cut, or woven method, on a dedicated valley assembly or on a roof where the adjoining shingle fields have been staged for integrated installation. The finished work product is a clean, continuous valley that carries water downslope without exposed fasteners, reverse laps, or interrupted shingle courses.

You will confirm the approved valley type and its compatibility with both adjoining roof slopes and the selected shingle product, verify that valley membrane and any approved valley metal are already in place, establish the valley centerline and fastener-exclusion zone, coordinate courses from both adjoining roof planes as they cross the valley, cut and finish the valley by the approved method, and inspect the completed valley for water-path integrity before higher courses or ridge work conceal it.

Valleys are the single highest-volume drainage feature on most sloped roofs. A valley built with a narrowed water channel, an exposed fastener, or a broken shingle lap will leak regardless of how well the rest of the roof is installed. This chapter treats the valley as its own controlled assembly, integrated with — not layered on top of — the surrounding shingle field.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-049 — Identify the approved valley type and confirm its compatibility with both adjoining roof slopes and the selected shingle product.
  • RF-050 — Verify and prepare valley underlayment, self-adhered membrane, and any approved valley metal before shingle installation begins.
  • RF-051 — Install valley shingles from both adjoining roof planes in coordinated, low-to-high sequence.
  • RF-052 — Cut valley lines using the approved method for the selected valley type without cutting underlying materials.
  • RF-053 — Maintain an unobstructed water path by keeping fasteners, debris, and sealant out of the valley channel.
  • RF-054 — Inspect the completed valley for water-control integrity, including laps, cutlines, fastening exclusion, and drainage continuity.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter depends directly on the roof-edge, membrane, and underlayment work completed in Chapters 3 through 5, and on the layout and starter-strip work completed in Chapters 6 and 7. The general shingle-field technique from Chapter 8 carries forward here, applied specifically to the valley intersection of two roof planes.

The valley must be built while the surrounding shingle courses are installed, one course at a time on each adjoining plane. It is not a detail that can be added after both fields are shingled. This coordination requirement carries forward into Chapter 10 (sidewall step flashing), Chapter 11 (headwall and apron flashing), and Chapter 12 (penetration flashing), which follow the same course-by-course integration principle, and into the Chapter 20 capstone, which installs any valley present on the approved shed design using this same procedure.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the approved valley type, membrane, metal, centerline, cutline, fastening-exclusion zone, and lap rules from the roofing-system specification and manufacturer instructions
  • confirming that the selected valley method is permitted for both adjoining roof slopes and the chosen shingle product
  • inspecting the valley deck, membrane, and metal for support, straightness, damage, debris, and adequate width before shingling begins
  • installing shingles from both adjoining roof planes in coordinated, low-to-high sequence for open, closed-cut, or woven valleys
  • cutting valley lines using the approved method for the selected valley type
  • maintaining exposure, joint offset, and an unobstructed water channel through the valley
  • inspecting and documenting the completed valley before it is covered by higher courses or ridge work

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • insert a new valley beneath two completed shingle fields; that work requires an approved repair detail and is outside this chapter's scope
  • install valley membrane or valley metal; those tasks were completed in Chapters 3 and 4 and are only verified here
  • select a valley method not permitted by the roofing-system specification for the roof slopes and shingle product involved
  • substitute sealant for required laps, cutline placement, or fastening-exclusion zones
  • represent the local water-path observation performed in this chapter as proof of whole-roof or whole-building watertightness

Trade Note

A valley concentrates the runoff of two full roof planes into one narrow channel. Every decision in this chapter — cutline placement, fastener location, sealant use — is governed by the need to keep that channel clear and continuous. When a step in this chapter conflicts with what would be faster or neater on a flat field, the valley's water path controls.

Valley-Method Standard

Do not select a valley method that is not permitted by the roofing-system specification for the shingle product and both adjoining roof slopes. If the specification does not clearly authorize open, closed-cut, or woven construction for this valley, stop and confirm the approved method before shingling across the valley in either direction.

Shop Tip

Work the valley from the lower end upward, and coordinate courses from both roof planes together rather than shingling one plane fully before starting the other. Working out of sequence is the most common cause of stacked joints and mismatched exposure at the valley.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before shingling begins, and use them consistently across both adjoining roof planes.

Common Field Fundamentals

A valley is not a repair to a finished roof plane; it is a coordinated intersection of two planes built together, course by course, from the lower end toward the ridge. The membrane and any metal installed in Chapters 3 and 4 already establish the water path beneath the shingles. This chapter's job is to keep that path clear as the shingle field crosses it — never to insert a valley after the fact and never to interrupt the shingle-lap order that carries water from upper courses onto lower ones.

Coordinating Both Roof Planes

Shingle both adjoining roof planes together as they approach the valley, matching course exposure and joint offset from each plane's own layout lines established in Chapter 6. Do not complete one entire roof plane and then start the second; the valley method you are using controls which plane crosses first and how the second plane laps over or weaves with it.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Retrofit a Valley Beneath Finished Fields

Do not attempt to insert a new valley beneath two completed shingle fields. This creates hidden reverse laps and unverifiable fastening conditions that cannot be corrected without removing the surrounding roofing. If a valley must be added to an already-shingled roof, that work requires an approved repair detail and is outside this chapter's scope.

Field Safety Cue — Footing and Cutting Direction

Maintain secure footing on the valley slope, which is steeper and more exposed than the surrounding field at the point where two planes meet. Direct all cutting strokes away from your body and away from installed membrane and metal.

Measurement Cue — No-Fastener Zone

Mark the no-fastener zone from the valley centerline or metal edge using the width specified in the roofing-system specification or manufacturer instructions before installing any valley-adjacent shingle. Do not estimate this width by eye.

Manufacturer Cue — Sealant Location

Apply sealant only at the specific concealed locations identified by the shingle or valley-metal manufacturer for the selected valley type. Sealant is not a substitute for the cutline, lap, or fastening-exclusion requirements set out in this chapter.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review the approved valley type, roof slopes, shingle limitations, membrane, metal, centerline, cutline, fastening exclusion zones, lap rules, and manufacturer details. Confirm every value against the roofing-system specification and record it on the Valley Work Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm that the selected valley method is permitted for both adjoining roof slopes and the chosen shingle product. If the specification does not clearly authorize the method for both planes, stop and obtain confirmation before continuing.
  3. Do not attempt to insert a new valley beneath two completed shingle fields unless an approved repair detail specifically permits it. Verify that both adjoining fields have been staged, not completed, for integrated installation before beginning this chapter's work.
  4. Inspect the valley deck for support, straightness, damage, debris, and adequate width. Confirm findings against the readiness record from Chapter 1 and correct only items authorized under that chapter's scope.
  5. Verify that required self-adhered valley membrane and any approved valley metal are installed in low-to-high shingle-lap order. Confirm this against the Chapter 4 membrane record before placing any shingle in the valley.
  6. Establish the valley centerline and approved cutline or shingle-control lines. Snap the centerline from the membrane or metal reference and mark the cutline or control lines at the specified offset.
  7. Identify and mark the no-fastener zone adjacent to the valley center or metal. Mark the zone at the specified width on both adjoining planes before installing any valley-adjacent shingle.
  8. Begin shingle installation at the lower valley end and coordinate courses from both adjoining roof planes. Work upslope, matching each plane's own exposure and joint-offset references from Chapter 6.
  9. For a closed-cut valley, install the first roof plane across the valley by the required distance and fasten outside the exclusion zone. Extend the specified plane's shingles across the valley centerline and secure only fasteners located outside the marked no-fastener zone.
  10. Install the second roof-plane shingles over the first and mark the approved cutline. Lap the second plane's shingles over the first plane's valley-crossing courses and mark the cutline at the specified offset before cutting.
  11. Cut the upper shingle layer without cutting the underlying shingles, membrane, or valley metal. Use the hooked or straight utility-knife blade specified for this cut and control blade depth to the shingle thickness only.
  12. Clip upper corners where required to direct water into the valley. Trim the upper corner of each valley-crossing shingle at the angle specified by the manufacturer instructions where the detail requires it.
  13. Apply manufacturer-approved sealant only at specified concealed locations. Do not apply sealant to the cutline, exposed shingle edges, or any location not explicitly identified for sealant use.
  14. For an open valley, trim shingles to the approved opening and keep cut edges straight and symmetrical. Cut both adjoining planes to the same offset from centerline and check the opening width at intervals along the valley length.
  15. For a woven valley, weave courses only when the product and roof geometry permit it and keep joints out of the valley center. Alternate courses from each plane across the valley according to the manufacturer's weave sequence and confirm no shingle joint lands within the marked centerline zone.
  16. Maintain course exposure and joint offsets as shingles cross or approach the valley. Check each course against the horizontal and vertical control lines established in Chapter 6 before fastening.
  17. Remove cutoffs, loose granules, nails, and sealant from the water channel. Clear the channel after each cutting or fastening operation, not only at the end of the chapter.
  18. Inspect the full valley for reverse laps, exposed fasteners, punctures, uneven cutlines, trapped debris, narrow water paths, and unsupported shingle edges. Walk the full valley length from an approved position and record each finding.
  19. Correct defects before installing higher courses or ridge work. Complete every correction identified in Step 18 and recheck the affected section before shingling continues above the valley.
  20. Conduct the approved visual or controlled water-path observation without overstating it as a whole-roof leak test. Perform only the observation method authorized by the roofing-system specification and record its limits in the project record.
  21. Photograph and document valley type, membrane, metal, fastening exclusion, cutline, and completed condition. Attach photographs to the project record before higher courses conceal the valley.

Common Valley Installation Checks

Apply the same checks to every valley, regardless of the roof's overall size or configuration.

  • Method-authorization check: confirm the selected valley method is permitted for both adjoining slopes and the shingle product before the first valley-crossing shingle is installed.
  • Membrane and metal check: confirm Chapter 4 membrane and any valley metal are complete and accepted before shingling begins.
  • No-fastener zone check: confirm the marked zone width matches the specification and remains free of fasteners on inspection.
  • Cutline or opening check: confirm the cutline or opening is straight, symmetrical, and at the specified offset along the full valley length.
  • Coordination check: confirm exposure and joint offset from both adjoining planes are maintained through the valley.
  • Housekeeping check: confirm the water channel is free of cutoffs, granules, nails, and sealant before the valley is covered by higher courses.

Complete the Valley Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before installing higher courses or beginning ridge work.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Cutline Is Uneven Along the Valley Length

Probable cause: the cutline was marked freehand or in short segments without a continuous straightedge or chalk-line reference, or the centerline reference drifted from the membrane or metal edge.
Confirmation: recheck the marked cutline against the valley centerline at several points along its length using a tape measure.
Correction: remark the cutline from a verified centerline reference before cutting any additional shingle, and recut any already-cut shingle that does not match the corrected line.
Reinspection: sight the full cutline from the lower end of the valley to confirm a straight, symmetrical result.

Fastener Found Inside the No-Fastener Zone

Probable cause: the zone was not marked before fastening began, or a fastener drifted from its intended location during installation.
Confirmation: measure the fastener's distance from the valley centerline or metal edge and compare it to the specified zone width.
Correction: remove the fastener, seal the resulting hole only if the manufacturer instructions specifically authorize sealing a fastener hole in this location, and refasten the shingle at an approved location outside the zone.
Reinspection: recheck the corrected area and the surrounding zone for additional misplaced fasteners.

Water Channel Narrower Than Specified

Probable cause: the cutline or opening was marked too close to the centerline, or a course was installed without checking the offset before fastening.
Confirmation: measure the channel width at several points along the valley and compare to the specified minimum.
Correction: remove and reinstall the affected valley-crossing shingles at the correct offset; do not attempt to widen the channel with sealant or trimming after fastening.
Reinspection: remeasure the corrected sections and confirm the full valley length meets the specified width.

Course Exposure Does Not Match Between the Two Adjoining Planes

Probable cause: the two planes were shingled independently without checking each course against the valley coordination steps in this chapter.
Confirmation: compare the exposure and course lines from each plane at the valley edge against the Chapter 6 layout record for that plane.
Correction: adjust the affected courses on the plane that has drifted from its layout lines, working from the nearest verified reference, before continuing upslope.
Reinspection: recheck both planes' exposure at the valley edge after correction and before installing the next course.

Debris or Sealant Found in the Water Channel Before Covering

Probable cause: cutoffs, granules, or excess sealant were not cleared after cutting or fastening operations.
Confirmation: visually inspect the full valley length from an approved position before higher courses are installed.
Correction: remove all debris and excess sealant from the channel; do not cover a contaminated channel with additional courses.
Reinspection: recheck the cleared channel immediately before the next course is installed over it.

Local Water-Path Observation Shows Continued Moisture

Probable cause: an undetected reverse lap, an exposed fastener, an insufficient cutline offset, or a defect in the underlying membrane or metal from Chapter 4.
Confirmation: repeat the approved observation while inspecting the valley section by section rather than as a whole, checking laps, fasteners, and cutline placement at each section.
Correction: remove and correct the specific defective section identified; do not add sealant across the valley as a substitute for the actual defect.
Reinspection: repeat the local observation on the corrected section only, under the same conditions used in Step 20.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a clean, inspected valley and a documented record, not exposed cutoffs or unmarked corrections.

  • Remove all shingle cutoffs, loose granules, nails, and packaging from the valley channel and surrounding roof surface
  • Dispose of removed material using a method that does not discharge it from the roof edge or the ground below
  • Wipe excess sealant from any exposed surface adjacent to approved concealed sealant locations
  • Store unused shingles, matched by product and lot, for use in the adjoining field courses
  • Compile the Valley Work Record, photographs, and inspection findings into the project record before installing higher courses or beginning ridge work

Field Terms

Open Valley
A valley in which approved valley metal remains exposed between trimmed shingle edges on both adjoining planes.
Closed-Cut Valley
A valley in which one plane's shingles run continuously across the valley and are covered by the second plane's shingles, cut along an approved cutline.
Woven Valley
A valley in which courses from both adjoining planes alternate and overlap across the valley without a cutline.
Valley Centerline
The reference line marking the low point of the valley, used to establish the cutline and no-fastener zone.
No-Fastener Zone
The marked area adjacent to the valley center or metal within which fasteners are prohibited.
Cutline
The approved line, offset from the centerline, along which the upper shingle layer of a closed-cut valley is trimmed.
Clipped Corner
The upper corner of a valley-crossing shingle, trimmed at an angle where required to direct water into the valley.
Water Channel
The clear path within the valley through which runoff drains downslope, kept free of fasteners, debris, and excess sealant.

Performance Check

With the valley complete, review the Valley Inspection Record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the valley method matches the approved specification for both adjoining slopes, membrane and metal were verified before shingling, the no-fastener zone is clear on inspection, the cutline or opening is straight and symmetrical at the specified offset, course exposure and joint offset are coordinated between both planes, the water channel is free of debris and excess sealant, and the approved local water-path observation is complete and documented with its limits stated. Correct every outstanding item and recheck the affected section before installing higher courses or beginning ridge work.

Chapter Closeout

You have completed the roof-valley chapter in the shingle-installation sequence: integrating two adjoining roof planes into one continuous, water-controlled valley. This chapter required you to identify the approved valley type and confirm its compatibility with both roof slopes and the shingle product (RF-049), verify valley membrane and metal preparation (RF-050), install valley shingles in coordinated sequence (RF-051), cut valley lines using the approved method (RF-052), maintain the water path (RF-053), and inspect the completed valley for water-control integrity (RF-054).

You now have a documented, inspected valley that the surrounding shingle field and every later chapter depend on. That record carries forward into the remaining flashing chapters, which use the same course-by-course integration principle at sidewalls, headwalls, and penetrations, and into the Chapter 20 capstone, which applies this exact valley procedure to the approved shed design where a valley condition is present.

Store the completed Valley Work Record, photographs, and inspection findings with the project record before installing higher courses or beginning ridge work.

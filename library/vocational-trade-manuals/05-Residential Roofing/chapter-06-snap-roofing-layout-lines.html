Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to establish a complete roofing-layout control grid on one roof plane before any starter strip or shingle course is installed. The finished work product is a verified set of horizontal and vertical chalk reference lines, a dry-layout check confirming symmetry and joint spacing, and a written layout record that Chapter 7 and Chapter 8 will build directly onto.

You will confirm the shingle manufacturer's exposure and offset requirements, measure the roof plane, calculate the number of full exposures to the ridge or upper termination, adjust the starter reference only within manufacturer limits to avoid a narrow final course, snap horizontal and vertical control lines, coordinate the layout through valleys, sidewalls, and penetrations, perform a dry-layout check with representative shingles, and correct any drift before installation begins.

This chapter builds on the completed edge metal, membrane, and underlayment from Chapters 3 through 5. No starter strip or field-shingle course may be installed over a roof plane that has not received a verified layout grid from this chapter.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-031 — Establish starter reference lines from the approved eave and rake relationship.
  • RF-032 — Measure and calculate shingle exposure and full-course count from the manufacturer's instructions.
  • RF-033 — Snap horizontal control lines at the required course interval.
  • RF-034 — Snap vertical control lines square to the eave for the selected installation method.
  • RF-035 — Correct layout drift, narrow final courses, and joint stacking before installation.
  • RF-036 — Verify roof symmetry and document the completed layout control grid.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 5 delivered a continuous, secured underlayment surface. This chapter turns that surface into a controlled grid that Chapter 7 uses to align starter strips and Chapter 8 uses to keep every field-shingle course straight, symmetrical, and manufacturer-compliant from the first course to the ridge.

The reference lines and layout record you produce here are checked again in Chapter 9 when valley shingles are coordinated between adjoining roof planes, and in the Chapter 20 capstone when the shed roof planes are laid out before shingling.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the shingle manufacturer's exposure, starter position, offset pattern, rack or stair-step method, and minimum cut-size requirements
  • confirming that edge metal, membranes, underlayment, penetrations, and roof-plane dimensions are complete and accepted
  • measuring the roof plane and calculating the number of full shingle exposures to the ridge or upper termination
  • adjusting the starter reference or permitted course exposure only within manufacturer limits to avoid a narrow final course
  • snapping horizontal and vertical control lines and coordinating them through valleys, sidewalls, penetrations, and adjoining roof planes
  • performing a dry-layout check with representative shingles and correcting drift before installation
  • documenting the controlling exposure, offset pattern, reference points, and approved adjustments

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • install starter strips or field shingles; those tasks begin in Chapter 7 and Chapter 8, only after this chapter's layout grid is verified and protected
  • change the manufacturer's exposure, minimum cut-size, or offset requirements to make the layout arithmetic work; any adjustment must remain within the manufacturer's stated limits
  • seal or permanently fasten any dry-layout shingle used to check the grid
  • proceed with layout over a roof plane whose edge metal, membrane, underlayment, or penetration locations have not been completed and accepted

Any roof plane where the final course would fall below the manufacturer's minimum width must have its starter reference or exposure adjusted within approved limits and re-verified before chalk lines are snapped.

Trade Note

Every layout decision in this chapter is recorded against a fixed reference: the shingle manufacturer's exposure and offset instructions and the measured roof-plane dimensions. Do not lay out a roof plane by eye or by matching a neighboring roof from the ground. If the manufacturer's limits and the measured plane produce a conflict, such as a final course narrower than the minimum width, resolve it with an approved adjustment and record the adjustment rather than installing courses that will require rework.

Access Standard

Perform all layout work only from the roof-access and fall-protection system already established and accepted under Chapter 2. Do not step onto the roof plane to snap lines or perform the dry-layout check unless that system is in place, inspected, and accepted for the current work session.

Shop Tip

Snap horizontal lines first and check every third or fourth line against the tape measure directly, rather than relying on accumulated marks from the line above it. Small errors compound quickly over many courses and are far easier to correct before any shingle is fastened.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every layout in this book follows the same pattern: confirm the manufacturer's exposure and offset requirements, measure the actual roof plane, calculate whether the final course will meet the minimum-width standard, adjust within approved limits only if needed, and verify the result with a dry-layout check before any material is fastened. Skipping the calculation step and snapping lines from an assumed exposure is the most common cause of a narrow, visually inconsistent final course at the ridge.

Exposure Calculation and Line Placement

Calculate the number of full shingle exposures from the starter reference to the ridge or upper termination before snapping any horizontal line. Determine whether the final course will meet the manufacturer's minimum width and appearance requirements, and adjust the starter reference or permitted course exposure only within manufacturer limits if it will not.

Critical Safety Cue — Maintain Roof-Access Discipline During Layout

Layout work requires repeated movement across the roof plane while carrying a chalk line, tape measure, and sample shingles. Do not step outside the approved work zone established under Chapter 2, and do not lean across an unprotected edge to snap a line or check a measurement. Reposition using the approved access system instead.

Field Safety Cue — Weather and Chalk-Line Accuracy

Stop layout work in rain, frost, snow, ice, or wind conditions strong enough to disturb a snapped chalk line or make footing unsafe. Wind can also blow a chalk line off its intended path before it snaps; use two workers to hold long lines taut where required.

Measurement Cue — Verify Lines Independently, Not Cumulatively

Mark additional horizontal control lines at the required interval without relying on accumulated tape errors from the line below. Check every third or fourth horizontal line directly against the eave or starter reference using the tape measure, not against the line immediately below it.

Manufacturer Cue — Exposure and Offset Are Product-Specific

Use only the exposure, offset pattern, minimum cut size, and joint-spacing values stated in the current shingle manufacturer's instructions for the specific product being installed. Do not carry over exposure or offset values from a different shingle product or a prior project.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review the shingle manufacturer's exposure, starter position, offset pattern, rack or stair-step method, minimum cut size, joint-spacing restrictions, and layout recommendations. Confirm the product name and revision on the manufacturer's instructions before using it as a reference, and record it on the governing-documents table above.
  2. Confirm that edge metal, membranes, underlayment, penetrations, and roof-plane dimensions are complete and accepted. Locate the Chapter 3, Chapter 4, and Chapter 5 acceptance records for the roof plane being laid out. If any is missing or shows an open item, stop this chapter and route the project back to the responsible chapter before continuing.
  3. Measure the roof plane from eave to ridge and rake to rake using consistent reference points. Record the eave-to-ridge and rake-to-rake dimensions on the layout record, using the same reference points established in the Chapter 1 roof sketch.
  4. Identify visible focal points, centered penetrations, dormers, valleys, hips, sidewalls, and ridge termination conditions that affect layout. Cross-reference each feature against the Chapter 1 roof sketch and note which features require centered or symmetrical treatment.
  5. Calculate the number of full shingle exposures from the starter reference to the ridge or upper termination. Divide the eave-to-ridge dimension by the manufacturer's stated exposure and record the resulting whole-course count and remainder on the layout record.
  6. Determine whether the final course will meet the manufacturer's minimum width and appearance requirements. Compare the calculated remainder from Step 5 to the manufacturer's stated minimum width for a final course.
  7. Adjust the starter reference or permitted course exposure only within manufacturer limits to avoid a narrow final course. If the remainder from Step 6 is below the minimum width, apply only an adjustment method identified in the manufacturer's instructions, and record the specific adjustment applied.
  8. Establish the first horizontal reference line for the starter strip or first shingle course. Position this line from the drip-edge and eave relationship stated in the roofing-system instructions, and verify it is parallel to the eave using the tape measure at both ends.
  9. Mark additional horizontal control lines at the required interval without relying on accumulated tape errors. Measure each new line's position directly from the starter reference line or from a fixed vertical reference, not from the line immediately below it.
  10. Snap lines using two workers where necessary and keep chalk from contaminating adhesive surfaces. Use two workers to hold long horizontal lines taut on wide roof planes, and avoid snapping chalk directly onto any exposed self-adhered membrane or starter-strip adhesive surface.
  11. Establish the first vertical reference line from a verified square or centered roof reference. Use the framing square to confirm the line is square to the eave, or use a centered reference where the roof plane requires symmetrical treatment around a focal point.
  12. Mark vertical control lines at intervals that support the selected shingle-installation method. Space vertical lines according to the rack, stair-step, or other method identified in the manufacturer's instructions and recorded in Step 1.
  13. Verify that vertical lines remain square to the eave and do not create unacceptable end cuts at both rakes. Check the vertical lines against the framing square at multiple points along their length and compare the resulting rake cuts to the manufacturer's minimum cut-size requirement.
  14. Coordinate layout through valleys, sidewalls, penetrations, and adjoining roof planes without forcing joints into prohibited zones. Reference the Chapter 1 roof sketch to confirm that no vertical control line places a required joint inside a valley no-fastener zone, sidewall flashing zone, or penetration flashing zone identified in later chapters.
  15. Compare the planned left and right rake cuts for symmetry where appearance requires it. Measure the planned cut width at both rakes and record the comparison on the layout record where the roofing-system specification requires visual symmetry.
  16. Mark starter-joint offsets and first-course joint positions. Mark the required offset distance between starter-strip joints and first-course shingle joints directly on the underlayment at the starter reference line.
  17. Perform a dry-layout check with representative shingles without sealing or final fastening. Place representative shingles along at least two full courses at a representative section of the roof plane, following the marked control lines, and verify exposure, offset, and joint spacing without engaging any adhesive strip or driving any fastener.
  18. Correct layout drift, narrow pieces, joint stacking, or misalignment before installation. Identify any deviation observed during the dry-layout check, re-snap the affected control lines, and repeat the dry-layout check at the corrected location.
  19. Protect reference lines from rain, traffic, and material staging. Schedule material staging and access paths to avoid walking directly on freshly snapped lines, and reschedule installation if rain is forecast before the lines can be used.
  20. Record the controlling exposure, offset pattern, reference points, and approved adjustments. Complete the layout record with the manufacturer's exposure value, the offset pattern used, all reference-point locations, and the specific text of any starter or exposure adjustment applied in Step 7.

Common Layout Checks

Apply the same checks to every roof plane, regardless of plane size or configuration.

  • Prerequisite completion check: confirm edge metal, membrane, underlayment, and penetrations are accepted before any line is snapped.
  • Exposure calculation check: confirm the full-course count and final-course width were calculated before lines were snapped, not estimated afterward.
  • Horizontal line accuracy check: confirm every third or fourth line was verified directly against the tape rather than the line below it.
  • Vertical line squareness check: confirm vertical lines remain square to the eave along their full length.
  • Coordination check: confirm layout does not force a joint into a valley, sidewall, or penetration exclusion zone.
  • Dry-layout verification check: confirm the dry-layout check was completed and any drift corrected before this chapter is closed out.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Final Course Calculates Narrower Than the Manufacturer's Minimum Width

Probable cause: the eave-to-ridge dimension does not divide evenly by the manufacturer's stated exposure, and no starter or exposure adjustment was applied before layout.
Confirmation: recheck the eave-to-ridge measurement and repeat the exposure calculation from Step 5.
Correction: apply an adjustment method identified in the manufacturer's instructions, such as a modified starter position or a small exposure adjustment distributed across multiple courses, and record the specific adjustment used.
Reinspection: repeat the final-course calculation after the adjustment and confirm it now meets the minimum width.

Horizontal Lines Drift Out of Parallel with the Eave

Probable cause: each new line was measured from the line below it instead of from a fixed reference, allowing small errors to accumulate.
Confirmation: measure the suspect lines directly against the starter reference line or a fixed vertical reference using the tape measure.
Correction: re-snap the affected lines using the fixed reference rather than the adjacent line.
Reinspection: verify every third or fourth line across the corrected section against the fixed reference before proceeding.

Vertical Control Lines Are Not Square to the Eave

Probable cause: the first vertical line was established without a verified square or centered reference, or the framing square was not checked against a known reference before use.
Confirmation: check the framing square itself against a verified reference, then recheck the vertical line at multiple points along its length.
Correction: re-establish the vertical reference line using a verified square reference and re-snap all dependent vertical lines.
Reinspection: recheck squareness at multiple points and confirm resulting rake cuts meet the minimum cut-size requirement.

Dry-Layout Check Reveals Joint Stacking

Probable cause: the vertical control-line spacing does not match the offset pattern required by the selected rack or stair-step method.
Confirmation: compare the vertical line spacing used during the dry-layout check to the manufacturer's offset pattern from Step 1.
Correction: re-snap vertical lines at the correct offset interval for the selected method.
Reinspection: repeat the dry-layout check at the corrected section and confirm no vertical joints align between adjacent courses.

Layout Places a Required Joint Inside a Valley or Sidewall Exclusion Zone

Probable cause: the layout grid was established without cross-referencing the Chapter 1 roof sketch for valley, sidewall, or penetration locations.
Confirmation: overlay the snapped control-line locations on the Chapter 1 roof sketch and identify each conflict with a marked exclusion zone.
Correction: adjust the vertical control-line spacing so no required joint falls inside an identified exclusion zone, re-snapping lines as needed.
Reinspection: recheck the corrected layout against the roof sketch before performing or repeating the dry-layout check.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a verified, documented layout grid ready for Chapter 7, not a set of unverified chalk marks.

  • Remove the dry-layout sample shingles from the roof plane without disturbing the snapped control lines
  • Confirm no adhesive strip on any dry-layout shingle was engaged and no fastener was driven during the check
  • Store representative sample shingles for reuse in later dry-layout checks on other roof planes
  • Compile the layout record, measurements, exposure calculation, and any approved adjustment into the project record
  • Transfer the completed layout record to the location Chapter 7 will reference before installing starter strips

Performance Check

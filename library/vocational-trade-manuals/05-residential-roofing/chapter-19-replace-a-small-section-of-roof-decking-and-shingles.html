Chapter 19

Replace a Small Section of Roof Decking and Shingles

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to remove a limited, approved section of damaged roof sheathing and restore a complete, integrated roofing system over the repair. The finished work product is a structurally supported replacement deck panel installed on sound framing, with self-adhered membrane, underlayment, flashing, and asphalt shingles restored in the correct shingle-lap order so the repaired section performs and looks like an original part of the roof.

You will confirm the approved repair scope, distinguish sheathing-only damage from framing or other system damage, establish fall protection and containment for an open roof section, remove shingles and underlayment beyond the repair boundary, cut out and remove the damaged panel without disturbing framing or concealed services, install and fasten replacement sheathing, and reinstall membrane, underlayment, flashing, and shingles using the original exposure, offset, fastening, and sealing requirements.

This chapter depends directly on the deck-inspection and readiness skills from Chapter 1, the roof-access and fall-protection skills from Chapter 2, the edge-metal, membrane, and underlayment skills from Chapters 3 through 5, the shingle and valley skills from Chapters 7 through 9, and the flashing and repair skills from Chapters 10 through 12 and 17. It feeds directly into the Chapter 20 capstone, which requires the same escalation discipline whenever a readiness inspection uncovers deck damage that must be corrected before the shed structure is roofed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-109 — Remove shingles beyond the repair boundary without damaging adjoining roofing or concealed services.
  • RF-110 — Cut out and remove damaged decking to the deck thickness without cutting framing or concealed services.
  • RF-111 — Install replacement sheathing on approved framing or blocking with full edge support and the specified fastening schedule.
  • RF-112 — Reinstall self-adhered membrane and underlayment over the repair with laps integrated beneath existing upper layers and over existing lower layers.
  • RF-113 — Reinstall asphalt shingles and disturbed flashing using the original exposure, offset, fastening, and sealing requirements.
  • RF-114 — Inspect the structural and water-control repair from the attic and roof and document the completed work.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter is the deck-level counterpart to Chapter 17's shingle-only repair and Chapter 18's leak diagnosis. Chapter 17 replaces individual shingles without disturbing the deck. Chapter 18 traces a leak to its source and may identify this chapter as the required correction when the source is damaged sheathing rather than a failed shingle, flashing, or fastener. This chapter is the procedure that follows that diagnosis, or that follows a Chapter 1 or Chapter 20 readiness inspection that found deck damage beyond minor surface correction.

Every water-control layer disturbed by this repair — self-adhered membrane, synthetic underlayment, drip edge, flashing, and shingles — must be restored using the same governing chapters that installed them the first time: Chapter 3 for edge metal, Chapter 4 for membrane, Chapter 5 for underlayment, Chapters 7 through 9 for starter strips and field shingles, and Chapters 10 through 12 for any disturbed flashing. This chapter does not introduce new water-control methods; it applies the existing ones to a repaired opening.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • obtaining the approved repair scope, deck specification, framing plan, sheathing fastening schedule, roofing-system instructions, weather plan, and inspection requirements
  • determining whether damage involves only sheathing or also framing, blocking, fascia, insulation, mold, wiring, chimneys, or other systems
  • establishing fall protection, interior protection, debris containment, temporary weather protection, and below-roof exclusion zones for an open repair
  • marking repair limits so replacement-panel edges land on approved framing or added blocking, and locating hidden framing, fasteners, wiring, ducts, pipes, and penetrations before cutting
  • removing shingles, underlayment, and flashing in controlled layers beyond the repair boundary while documenting the original failure
  • cutting out and removing the damaged sheathing panel to deck thickness without cutting framing or concealed services
  • inspecting exposed framing and installing approved edge blocking or repair framing only according to a controlling detail
  • fitting and fastening replacement sheathing with full edge support and no unsupported edges
  • reinstalling self-adhered membrane, underlayment, disturbed flashing, and asphalt shingles in the original shingle-lap order, exposure, offset, fastening, and sealing requirements
  • inspecting the completed repair from the attic and roof, conducting an approved local water-path observation, and documenting the work

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with a sheathing repair when damage also involves rafters, trusses, blocking, fascia, insulation, mold, wiring, chimneys, or other systems, without first obtaining structural or environmental direction for those systems
  • cut, notch, or remove any framing member, truss, or blocking beyond what an approved framing detail specifically authorizes
  • reuse shingles, membrane, or underlayment whose condition does not meet the repair instructions, or substitute an incompatible replacement product for the original roofing system
  • close in the repair with roofing material before the exposed framing and the fastened replacement panel have been inspected and accepted
  • represent an approved local water-path observation as proof of whole-building watertightness

Any condition discovered during this repair that falls outside sheathing replacement — structural damage, widespread decay, mold, fire damage, suspected asbestos-containing material, or an unstable roof — must stop this chapter's work on the affected area until the responsible structural or environmental authority provides direction.

Trade Note

A small deck repair fails for the same reason a large one does: the replacement panel edge lands on unsupported framing, or the water-control layers above and below the repair are not restored in the correct shingle-lap order. Treat this repair as a complete, miniature version of the original roofing sequence — deck, membrane, underlayment, flashing, shingles — not as a patch.

Escalation Standard

This chapter authorizes sheathing replacement only. If cutting or inspection reveals rafter, truss, or blocking damage, widespread decay, mold, fire damage, suspected asbestos-containing material, or an unstable roof, stop work on the affected area and obtain structural or environmental direction before continuing. Do not conceal a condition outside this chapter's scope with replacement sheathing or roofing material.

Shop Tip

Photograph the original failure, the exposed framing, and each restored layer before it is covered by the next one. A repair record with no photographs of the framing and the layer sequence is difficult to defend later if a leak recurs near the repair.

Common Field Fundamentals

This chapter follows the same pattern as every prior installation chapter in this book: confirm the governing documents, inspect from safe positions, compare the exposed condition to the approved specification, correct only what is authorized, and restore every disturbed layer in the original shingle-lap order before documenting the result. The difference here is that the repair opens a hole in a system that is otherwise complete, so containment, sequencing, and escalation discipline matter more than on new construction.

Scope Confirmation Before Any Cutting

Determine whether damage involves only sheathing or also rafters, trusses, blocking, fascia, insulation, mold, wiring, chimneys, or other systems before any material is removed. This determination controls whether this chapter's procedure applies or whether the work must stop for structural or environmental direction.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Damage Beyond Sheathing

Stop work and obtain structural or environmental direction before continuing if the exposed condition includes framing damage, widespread decay, mold, fire damage, suspected asbestos-containing material, or an unstable roof. Do not proceed with sheathing replacement over an unresolved structural or environmental finding.

Field Safety Cue — Open-Roof Containment

Establish fall protection, interior protection, debris containment, temporary weather protection, and below-roof exclusion zones before removing any shingles or decking. Keep the exposed opening covered or protected whenever it will be left unattended, and monitor weather conditions continuously while the section is open.

Measurement Cue — Set Saw Depth to Deck Thickness

Set the circular-saw cutting depth to the deck thickness identified in the approved sheathing schedule before cutting the damaged panel. Verify the setting against a scrap piece or an exposed edge; do not estimate the depth by eye.

Code Alert — Concealed Findings and Framing Repairs

Any framing repair, added blocking, or structural correction required by the exposed condition is controlled by the approved framing detail and applicable structural requirements, not by this chapter. Confirm the controlling detail before installing edge blocking or repair framing.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Obtain the approved repair scope, deck specification, framing plan, sheathing fastening schedule, roofing-system instructions, weather plan, and inspection requirements. Confirm the revision or date on each document and record the confirmation on the governing-documents table above.
  2. Determine whether damage involves only sheathing or also rafters, trusses, blocking, fascia, insulation, mold, wiring, chimneys, or other systems. Base this determination on the Chapter 1, Chapter 17, or Chapter 18 findings that identified this repair, and on a visual inspection of the area from an approved position before cutting.
  3. Stop work and obtain structural or environmental direction for framing damage, widespread decay, mold, fire damage, asbestos-containing material, or an unstable roof. Do not proceed with this chapter's sheathing-replacement procedure until that direction authorizes it.
  4. Establish fall protection, interior protection, debris containment, temporary weather protection, and below-roof exclusion zones. Extend the site's existing roof-access and fall-protection system to cover the open repair section for its full duration.
  5. Mark the repair limits so replacement-panel edges land on approved framing or added blocking. Identify the framing members bordering the damaged area and mark the cutline directly over them rather than at the visible edge of the damage alone.
  6. Locate hidden framing, fasteners, wiring, ducts, pipes, and penetrations before cutting. Use the attic or interior access to confirm framing spacing and any concealed services before marking the final cutline on the roof surface.
  7. Remove shingles beyond the repair boundary far enough to create proper underlayment and shingle laps. Work from the repair boundary outward using the approved shingle-removal method, keeping cutoffs and loose fasteners contained.
  8. Preserve reusable shingles only when product condition and repair instructions permit. Set aside shingles suitable for reinstallation and discard any that are cracked, brittle, torn, or otherwise unsuitable.
  9. Remove underlayment and flashing in controlled layers while documenting the original failure. Photograph the condition of each layer as it is exposed, before removing the next, to preserve a record of the original cause.
  10. Set saw depth to the deck thickness and cut the damaged sheathing without cutting framing or concealed services. Verify the depth setting against the sheathing schedule before making the first cut, and recheck it if the blade encounters unexpected resistance.
  11. Remove the damaged panel section and all unsafe fasteners and debris. Pry the panel free without levering against adjoining sound sheathing, and remove protruding fasteners from the exposed framing.
  12. Inspect exposed framing for bearing, decay, splitting, alignment, moisture, and connection condition. Compare the exposed condition to the framing plan and record any deviation before proceeding.
  13. Install approved edge blocking or repair framing only according to the controlling detail. Do not add blocking or framing beyond what the approved detail specifies, and confirm bearing and fastening for any added member.
  14. Cut replacement sheathing to the specified material, thickness, orientation, edge gap, and support condition. Match the panel to the deck specification confirmed in Step 1, including the required spacing gap at panel edges.
  15. Dry-fit the panel and verify full edge support and required spacing. Confirm that every edge of the replacement panel bears on framing or approved blocking before fastening.
  16. Fasten the replacement sheathing using the approved edge and field schedule. Use the fastener type, length, and spacing identified in the sheathing fastening schedule confirmed in Step 1.
  17. Verify panel plane, framing contact, fastener seating, and absence of unsupported edges. Check the panel surface against adjoining sheathing for a flush plane and confirm every fastener is seated flush without overdriving.
  18. Install self-adhered membrane or underlayment patches with laps extending beneath upper existing layers and over lower layers. Follow the Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 lap and adhesion requirements for the membrane and underlayment products in use.
  19. Reinstall required drip edge, valley, wall, or penetration flashing disturbed by the repair. Follow the governing chapter for each flashing type — Chapter 3 for edge metal, Chapter 9 for valley flashing, Chapter 10 or 11 for wall flashing, Chapter 12 for penetration flashing — to restore the original detail.
  20. Install replacement shingles using the original exposure, offset, fastening, and sealing requirements. Match the surrounding course lines established before the repair and blend replacement shingles for color and lot where available, following Chapter 7 and Chapter 8 methods.
  21. Hand-seal disturbed surrounding tabs where required. Apply compatible hand-seal adhesive at the manufacturer-required tab locations disturbed by the repair, following the Chapter 17 sealing method.
  22. Inspect the repair from the attic and roof for deck plane, support, fasteners, laps, shingle alignment, and drainage continuity. Confirm every item on the Acceptance Standards table before considering the repair complete.
  23. Conduct the approved local water-path observation when conditions permit. Perform the observation under safe weather conditions and record the result without representing it as a whole-building leak test.
  24. Document removed material, cause of damage, framing condition, repair components, concealed work, and inspection results. Compile the photographs from Steps 9, 12, 16, and 18 with the completed repair record before closing out the project.

Common Repair Checks

Apply the same checks to every small-section deck and shingle repair, regardless of the repair's size or location on the roof.

  • Scope check: confirm the damage is sheathing-only, or that structural or environmental direction was obtained before proceeding.
  • Cut-depth check: confirm the saw was set to deck thickness and no framing or concealed service was cut.
  • Framing check: confirm exposed framing bearing, alignment, and moisture condition were inspected and accepted before panel installation.
  • Panel-support check: confirm every replacement-panel edge lands on approved framing or blocking with full support.
  • Layer-sequence check: confirm membrane, underlayment, flashing, and shingles were restored in the original shingle-lap order.
  • Documentation check: confirm removed material, framing condition, repair components, concealed work, and inspection results are recorded before closeout.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Saw Contacts Framing While Cutting the Damaged Panel

Probable cause: the depth setting was not verified against the actual deck thickness, or the deck thickness varies from the specification at this location.
Confirmation: stop cutting immediately and measure the actual deck thickness at the cut location against the sheathing schedule.
Correction: readjust the saw depth to the confirmed thickness and inspect the framing contact point for damage before continuing; record any framing mark found and obtain structural direction if the contact affected bearing.
Reinspection: recheck the completed cutline and the framing condition once the correct depth is confirmed.

Replacement Panel Edge Does Not Land on Framing or Blocking

Probable cause: the repair boundary was marked from the visible damage rather than from the actual framing layout, or the panel was cut to an incorrect size.
Confirmation: locate the actual framing members from the attic or interior side and compare their position to the installed panel edge.
Correction: install approved edge blocking under the unsupported joint according to the controlling detail, or re-cut the panel to the correct size so its edge lands on existing framing.
Reinspection: verify full edge support at every panel joint before installing membrane or underlayment.

Exposed Framing Shows Decay or Moisture Beyond the Sheathing Damage

Probable cause: the original leak or damage source affected the framing before or in addition to the sheathing.
Confirmation: probe the affected framing member and compare its condition to the framing plan and structural requirements.
Correction: stop this chapter's sheathing-only procedure on the affected area and obtain structural direction before installing any replacement panel over the framing.
Reinspection: resume sheathing replacement only after the framing repair is complete and accepted.

Restored Membrane or Underlayment Creates a Reverse Lap

Probable cause: the patch was installed without confirming which existing layer is above and which is below the repair opening.
Confirmation: trace the water-shedding direction of the surrounding intact membrane and underlayment before evaluating the patch.
Correction: remove and reinstall the patch so it extends beneath the existing upper layer and over the existing lower layer, matching the original shingle-lap order.
Reinspection: recheck every lap at the repair boundary before installing shingles.

Replacement Shingles Do Not Match Surrounding Exposure or Course Lines

Probable cause: the repair course lines were established independently of the surrounding field rather than extended from it.
Confirmation: measure the exposure and alignment of the surrounding intact courses and compare them to the installed repair courses.
Correction: remove and reinstall the affected repair courses using the surrounding field's exposure and alignment as the controlling reference.
Reinspection: recheck alignment across the full repair width before hand-sealing tabs.

Local Water-Path Observation Shows Continued Moisture at the Repair

Probable cause: a disturbed flashing detail was not fully restored, a lap was missed, or the original failure source was not the only entry point.
Confirmation: repeat the observation while inspecting each restored layer and flashing detail individually rather than only the shingle surface.
Correction: return to Chapter 18 diagnostic procedures to trace the remaining entry point rather than adding sealant to the symptom.
Reinspection: repeat the local water-path observation after the additional correction is complete.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, closed-in repair and an organized record set, not an open section or a pile of removed material.

  • Remove all cutoffs, old fasteners, damaged sheathing, and packaging from the roof and the ground below
  • Dispose of removed material using an approved method, separating any material requiring special handling
  • Store reusable shingles and leftover matching material for future authorized repairs
  • Compile removed-material notes, framing-condition photographs, concealed-work photographs, and inspection results into the project record
  • Transfer the completed repair record to the project file alongside any Chapter 17 or Chapter 18 records that identified this repair

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to trace a documented roof leak to its actual water-entry point and to complete one authorized targeted repair. The finished work product is a completed leak-diagnosis record, one repair of the failed shingle, flashing, boot, underlayment, fastener, seal, or drainage component using the governing chapter's procedure, and a documented verification of the repair within the limits of the approved local test method.

You will interview the occupant or project representative, review roof and repair history, inspect interior evidence, distinguish roof-water entry from other moisture sources, trace the moisture path upslope across framing, inspect exterior roof features, identify the most probable leak mechanism, confirm the actual source, remove only the minimum roofing required to expose the defect, repair the failed layer using the correct governing chapter, restore every disturbed layer in shingle-lap order, and verify the repair under safe, limited test conditions.

This chapter depends on the shingle, flashing, penetration, valley, and underlayment procedures taught earlier in this book. It does not replace those chapters. It teaches how to find the correct failure point so the correct earlier-chapter procedure can be applied to the correct location, rather than applying sealant to a visible symptom.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-103 — Trace a documented leak path from interior evidence to its probable exterior source using fixed references.
  • RF-104 — Inspect flashing at sidewalls, headwalls, valleys, and penetrations for the failure mechanisms that produce leaks.
  • RF-105 — Inspect penetrations, including boots, curbs, and fasteners, for conditions that allow water entry.
  • RF-106 — Inspect valleys for reverse laps, exposed fasteners, and interrupted water paths that produce leaks.
  • RF-107 — Perform a targeted repair that corrects the confirmed failed layer using the governing chapter procedure.
  • RF-108 — Verify leak correction using an approved local test method, and document the result and its limitations.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter draws directly on the shingle-field procedure in Chapter 8, the valley procedure in Chapter 9, the sidewall step-flashing procedure in Chapter 10, the headwall and apron-flashing procedure in Chapter 11, the penetration-flashing procedure in Chapter 12, the shingle-repair procedure in Chapter 17, and the gutter procedure in Chapter 16. Diagnosis in this chapter identifies which of those governing procedures applies to the confirmed failure; it does not substitute a new method for any of them.

Where diagnosis reveals damage beyond the roofing layers themselves, such as deck damage, this chapter routes the project to Chapter 19 rather than attempting a structural repair here.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • interviewing the occupant or project representative and reviewing roof age, product type, repair history, recent work, storms, ice conditions, and attic ventilation
  • inspecting interior stains, wet materials, drip paths, framing, insulation, fasteners, penetrations, and condensation indicators
  • distinguishing likely roof-water entry from plumbing, HVAC condensate, attic condensation, ice dams, wall leakage, or window leakage
  • tracing the moisture path upslope and across framing rather than assuming the visible stain marks the entry point
  • inspecting exterior roof surfaces, shingle damage, fastener exposure, valleys, step flashing, headwalls, penetrations, ridge details, gutters, and drainage backups
  • confirming the suspected source by direct observation, moisture pattern, controlled sectional testing, or opening only the minimum area required
  • selecting and completing the smallest approved repair that corrects the actual failed layer
  • restoring disturbed roofing layers in correct shingle-lap order and verifying the repair under an approved local test

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • flood the roof, introduce water near electrical systems, or conduct a hose test without interior observation and runoff control already arranged
  • add sealant over a symptom instead of correcting the failed layer identified by diagnosis
  • proceed with a repair when the investigation finds widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active structural leakage, structural movement, or hazardous materials; those conditions require the applicable structural, environmental, or licensed authority
  • represent a local test result as proof of whole-building watertightness
  • perform deck replacement under this chapter; refer confirmed deck damage to Chapter 19

Any leak whose confirmed cause falls outside this chapter's authorized repair scope must be documented and routed to the responsible authority before any repair is attempted.

Trade Note

The visible interior stain is evidence of where water arrived at a ceiling or framing member, not evidence of where water entered the roof. Water travels along the underside of the deck, along rafters, and along underlayment before it drips. Trace the path upslope from the stain before marking an exterior repair location.

Testing Standard

An approved local water-path observation confirms or fails to confirm one suspected entry point under limited, controlled conditions. It is not a whole-building leak test. Do not flood the roof, do not test near electrical systems, and do not begin a local test without an observer stationed at the interior evidence point and a plan for stopping the water source immediately.

Shop Tip

Record every fixed reference point — ceiling joist number, wall stud bay, ridge distance, rake distance — before climbing to the exterior. A diagnosis that cannot be pointed to from both inside and outside using the same references is not yet confirmed.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every leak diagnosis in this chapter follows the same pattern: gather evidence, distinguish roof-water entry from other sources, trace the path upslope rather than assuming the stain marks the entry point, confirm the source with the least invasive method available, repair only the confirmed failed layer using its governing procedure, and verify the repair within the honest limits of a local test.

Evidence Gathering and Path Tracing

Interview the occupant or project representative and record when, where, and under what weather conditions the leak appears. Review roof age, product type, repair history, recent work, storms, ice conditions, and attic ventilation. Inspect the interior stain, wet material, drip path, framing, insulation, fasteners, penetrations, and air-leak or condensation indicators, and distinguish likely roof-water entry from plumbing leaks, HVAC condensate, attic condensation, ice dams, wall leakage, or window leakage before tracing the moisture path upslope and across framing.

Critical Safety Cue — Controlled Testing Only

Do not flood the roof, introduce water near electrical systems, or conduct a hose test without interior observation and runoff control already arranged. Station an observer at the interior evidence point before any water is introduced, and confirm a method to stop the water source immediately if unexpected entry appears.

Field Safety Cue — Protect Interior Finishes

Place interior-protection sheeting below the suspected leak path before beginning interior inspection or any local test. Stop the local test at the first sign of unexpected water entry beyond the confirmed zone.

Code Alert — Findings Beyond This Chapter's Scope

Widespread uplift, brittle roofing, active structural leakage, structural movement, or hazardous materials found during the investigation are outside this chapter's authorized repair scope. Record the finding, stop the repair, and route it to the applicable structural, environmental, or licensed authority.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Interview the occupant or project representative and record when, where, and under what weather conditions the leak appears. Record the description in the occupant's own words along with the date, time, and weather conditions reported for each occurrence.
  2. Review roof age, product type, repair history, recent work, storms, ice conditions, attic ventilation, and known penetrations. Confirm the revision or date on each record before using it, and note any recent work performed near the reported leak location.
  3. Protect interior finishes and establish safe attic and roof access. Place interior-protection sheeting below the suspected leak path before further interior inspection, and confirm the fall-protection provisions already accepted for the site before any roof-surface access.
  4. Inspect the interior stain, wet material, drip path, framing, insulation, fasteners, penetrations, and air-leak or condensation indicators. Record the exact location of each finding using a fixed interior reference such as a ceiling joist number or wall stud bay.
  5. Distinguish likely roof-water entry from plumbing leaks, HVAC condensate, attic condensation, ice dams, wall leakage, or window leakage. Rule out each alternative cause using its own distinguishing evidence, such as supply-line routing for plumbing or duct routing for HVAC condensate, before proceeding as a roof-water investigation.
  6. Trace the moisture path upslope and across framing rather than assuming the visible stain marks the entry point. Follow staining, discoloration, or dampness along the underside of the deck and along framing members toward the roof's higher elevations.
  7. Mark suspected exterior zones from fixed interior and exterior references. Convert the interior framing reference to an exterior location using consistent measurements from a fixed point such as a rake or ridge.
  8. Inspect roof surfaces, shingle damage, fastener exposure, valleys, step flashing, headwalls, penetrations, ridge details, gutters, and drainage backups. Examine every feature within the marked suspected zone from an approved position before examining unrelated areas of the roof.
  9. Identify the most probable leak mechanism and list alternative causes. Record the leading candidate and at least one alternative explanation still under consideration at this stage.
  10. Confirm the suspected source by direct observation, moisture pattern, controlled sectional testing, or opening only the minimum area required. Select the least invasive confirmation method that can produce a reliable result for the suspected mechanism.
  11. Do not flood the roof, introduce water near electrical systems, or conduct hose testing without interior observation and runoff control. Station an observer at the interior evidence point and confirm a method to stop the water source before any water is introduced.
  12. Select the smallest approved repair that corrects the actual failed layer rather than coating the symptom with sealant. Identify the governing chapter procedure — shingle field, valley, sidewall, headwall, apron, penetration, or gutter — that applies to the confirmed defect.
  13. Remove only the roofing components required to expose the defect. Limit removal to the minimum shingles, flashing, or fasteners needed to reach the confirmed failed layer.
  14. Repair or replace failed shingle, flashing, boot, underlayment, fastener, seal, or drainage component using the governing chapter procedure. Follow the numbered sequence in that governing chapter exactly; do not substitute a different method because the area is small.
  15. Restore all disturbed roofing layers in correct shingle-lap order. Reinstall underlayment, flashing, and shingles so upper layers shed onto lower layers with no reverse laps introduced by the repair.
  16. Inspect the repair visually from exterior and interior positions. Confirm the restored layers match the governing chapter's acceptance standards and that no new gap, reverse lap, or exposed fastener was introduced.
  17. Conduct the approved local verification under safe weather and test conditions. Apply water only to the confirmed zone, beginning at the lowest point of that zone and working upslope in the manner directed by the governing chapter's test guidance.
  18. Observe for leakage during the specified test period and after drainage stops. Maintain the interior observer at the evidence point for the full test period and for a defined period after water is shut off.
  19. If leakage remains, return to diagnosis rather than adding indiscriminate sealant. Reopen the investigation at Step 6, expand the suspected zone, and repeat confirmation before attempting a second repair.
  20. Document evidence, diagnosis, repair, test method, test limitations, unresolved moisture, and recommended monitoring. Record the complete diagnosis-to-verification record, including any unresolved item and the recommended monitoring period before the record is closed.

Common Diagnosis and Repair Checks

Apply the same checks to every leak investigation, regardless of the suspected feature.

  • Alternative-cause check: confirm plumbing, HVAC condensate, attic condensation, ice dams, wall leakage, and window leakage have each been considered and ruled out or identified as the cause.
  • Path-tracing check: confirm the moisture path was traced upslope and across framing rather than assumed from the visible stain.
  • Minimum-opening check: confirm only the minimum roofing components required to expose the defect were removed.
  • Governing-chapter check: confirm the repair followed the correct governing chapter procedure for the confirmed failed layer.
  • Restoration check: confirm every disturbed layer was restored in correct shingle-lap order.
  • Verification check: confirm the local test was conducted safely, with an interior observer, and that its limitations are documented.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Interior Stain Reappears Despite a Completed Repair

Probable cause: the confirmed entry point was incorrect, a second entry point exists nearby, or the moisture path was traced to the wrong exterior zone.
Confirmation: return to Step 6, retrace the moisture path with the repair now visible, and expand the suspected zone to adjoining features such as an overlooked valley lap or fastener.
Correction: do not add sealant to the original repair location; identify the actual entry point and apply the correct governing chapter procedure to that location.
Reinspection: repeat the local verification test on the newly confirmed zone.

Local Water Test Produces No Leak but the Reported Symptom Continues

Probable cause: the leak is intermittent, weather-dependent (such as wind-driven rain or ice damming), or caused by a non-roof source not yet ruled out.
Confirmation: compare the occupant's reported weather conditions to the conditions used during the local test, and re-review the alternative causes listed in Step 5.
Correction: repeat the local test under conditions matching the reported occurrence where safely possible, or reopen the interior inspection for a previously ruled-out alternative cause.
Reinspection: document the revised test conditions and result before closing the record.

Multiple Plausible Entry Points Are Found in the Suspected Zone

Probable cause: more than one deficiency exists in the same area, such as an exposed fastener near a step-flashing lap that is also short.
Confirmation: inspect and document each plausible entry point separately, using the governing chapter's acceptance standard for that specific feature.
Correction: repair every confirmed deficiency in the zone using each item's governing chapter procedure rather than treating the zone as a single repair.
Reinspection: verify each repaired feature individually before conducting the combined local test.

Investigation Reveals Deck Damage Beneath the Confirmed Entry Point

Probable cause: sustained water entry has damaged the sheathing beneath the failed roofing layer.
Confirmation: inspect the exposed deck for delamination, soft areas, rot, or fastener pull-through once the roofing layer is opened.
Correction: stop the repair at this chapter's scope, record the deck condition, and route the project to Chapter 19 for sheathing replacement before the roofing repair is completed.
Reinspection: resume this chapter's repair only after Chapter 19 restores an approved deck condition.

Occupant Report and Physical Evidence Do Not Agree

Probable cause: the occupant's description of location or timing does not match the observed staining or moisture pattern, often because of light travel or drip-path distance from the actual entry point.
Confirmation: re-walk the interior with the occupant, if available, and compare the reported location to the documented framing reference.
Correction: rely on the documented physical evidence and traced moisture path rather than the reported location alone, and record the discrepancy in the diagnosis record.
Reinspection: confirm the physical-evidence-based zone using the same confirmation methods described in Step 10.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a signed diagnosis-to-verification record and a restored roof surface, not an open investigation.

  • Remove interior-protection sheeting and dispose of any water used during the local test according to the approved runoff-control method
  • Remove cutoffs, loose fasteners, and packaging generated by the repair from the roof surface and the ground below
  • Store any unused governing-chapter materials for reuse in later authorized repairs
  • Compile the occupant interview, evidence record, diagnosis, repair details, test method, and result into the project record
  • Record any recommended monitoring period or unresolved item clearly in the closed record

Field Terms

Entry Point
The specific exterior location where water actually passes through the roof covering or flashing.
Evidence Point
The interior location where water becomes visible, which may be downslope or lateral from the actual entry point.
Moisture Path
The route water follows along framing, decking, or underlayment between the entry point and the evidence point.
Targeted Repair
The smallest approved repair that corrects the confirmed failed layer, using the governing chapter's procedure.
Local Verification
An approved, limited observation of one suspected entry point under controlled conditions, not a whole-building test.
Alternative Cause
A non-roof moisture source, such as plumbing, HVAC condensate, attic condensation, or window leakage, that must be ruled out before an exterior repair proceeds.
Sectional Testing
Applying water to one confirmed section of the roof under controlled conditions to isolate a suspected entry point.
Governing Chapter
The chapter in this book whose procedure controls the repair method for a specific confirmed defect type.

Performance Check

With the repair and local test complete, review the diagnosis record and the Leak-Diagnosis and Repair-Verification Record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the occupant interview and interior evidence are recorded with fixed references, alternative causes were considered and ruled out or identified, the moisture path was traced upslope rather than assumed, the confirmed entry point was identified using the least invasive method that produced a reliable result, the repair followed the correct governing chapter procedure at the smallest approved scope, all disturbed layers were restored in shingle-lap order, and the local verification test was conducted safely with its result and limitations documented. If leakage remains after the completed repair and test, reopen the diagnosis rather than closing the record with an unresolved item unaddressed.

Chapter Closeout

You have completed a documented roof-leak diagnosis and one targeted repair. This chapter required you to trace a leak path from interior evidence to a probable exterior source (RF-103), inspect flashing at sidewalls, headwalls, valleys, and penetrations (RF-104), inspect penetrations for water-entry conditions (RF-105), inspect valleys for interrupted water paths (RF-106), perform a targeted repair using the correct governing chapter procedure (RF-107), and verify the repair using an approved local test method with documented limitations (RF-108).

You now have a documented diagnosis-to-verification record that distinguishes a correctly traced and repaired roof leak from a symptom masked with sealant. That discipline carries directly into the next chapter, where damage extends beyond the roofing layers themselves into the deck and requires a structural repair before the roofing system can be restored.

Store the completed diagnosis record, repair documentation, and local-verification result with the project record. Chapter 19 requires this record when diagnosis reveals deck damage beyond this chapter's scope.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to install one compatible plumbing-vent boot or approved roof-penetration flashing and integrate it, course-by-course, with the surrounding underlayment and asphalt-shingle field. The finished work product is a penetration flashing assembly that sheds water around and below the opening, with fasteners sealed only at manufacturer-approved locations, shingles cut and fitted without exposing the boot collar, and a documented local water-path observation completed within its stated limits.

You will confirm the penetration is authorized and properly secured, select the correct flashing product for the penetration diameter, roof slope, roofing type, and climate, install lower shingle courses up to the penetration, place the flashing so its lower flange laps over the lower shingles and its upper flange laps beneath the upper courses, fasten and seal only where the manufacturer permits, and finish the surrounding shingle courses while preserving full drainage over the flange.

This chapter builds directly on the shingle-field skills from Chapter 8 and the valley-integration skills from Chapter 9. It applies the same shingle-lap water-flow principle to a point penetration instead of a linear intersection, and it prepares you for the sidewall and headwall flashing integration taught in Chapters 10 and 11.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RF-067 — Locate the roof penetration and confirm it is authorized, secured, and extended to the specified height.
  • RF-068 — Select a pipe boot or flashing product matched to penetration diameter, roof slope, roofing type, climate, and service temperature.
  • RF-069 — Cut shingles around the penetration to support the flashing without binding against the pipe or blocking the boot collar.
  • RF-070 — Install the flashing boot in the correct shingle-lap relationship with the surrounding roof covering.
  • RF-071 — Seal fasteners correctly using compatible sealant only at manufacturer-designated locations.
  • RF-072 — Inspect the completed penetration for reverse laps, exposed fasteners, blocked drainage, and other defects, and document the completed assembly.

Where This Skill Fits

Penetration flashing is completed while the surrounding shingle field is being installed, not after the field is finished. This chapter assumes the roof deck (Chapter 1), roof access (Chapter 2), edge metal (Chapter 3), self-adhered membrane (Chapter 4), underlayment (Chapter 5), layout lines (Chapter 6), starter strips (Chapter 7), and the shingle field up to the penetration (Chapter 8) are already complete for the roof plane containing the penetration.

The technique in this chapter also governs the training or capstone penetration installed in Chapter 20, and it supports the diagnostic work in Chapter 18, where a failed penetration flashing is one of the most common leak sources traced back to its entry point.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the penetration location, clearance, size, and authorization against the approved plan and framing
  • selecting a compatible flashing boot or curb for the penetration diameter, roof slope, roofing type, climate, and service temperature
  • integrating the flashing with the self-adhered membrane, underlayment, and shingle courses in the correct shingle-lap order
  • fastening and sealing the flashing only at manufacturer-approved locations
  • completing the surrounding shingle field while preserving full drainage over the flange
  • conducting an approved local water-path observation limited to the penetration area

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, relocate, or resize a roof penetration, or cut rafters, trusses, or structural decking to accommodate one, without an approved framing detail
  • install a penetration that was not confirmed authorized in Chapter 1 or does not agree with the framing and ventilation plans
  • substitute roofing cement or sealant for the required flange laps, fastening, or drainage geometry
  • represent a local water-path observation or hose test as proof of whole-building watertightness

Any penetration whose diameter, height, plumbness, or securement does not match the approved specification must be corrected under the authority that governs that system before flashing begins.

Trade Note

A pipe boot fails from the same causes every time: the wrong size for the pipe, fasteners driven into the exposed lower flange, shingles cut too tight against the collar, or the upper flange left exposed instead of lapped beneath the next course. Confirm size and lap direction before you drive a single fastener.

Access Standard

Do not begin flashing work at a penetration until the roof-access and fall-protection system established in Chapter 2 is in place and accepted for the work area surrounding the penetration. Treat the penetration opening itself as a controlled hazard while it is uncovered.

Shop Tip

Dry-fit the flashing before cutting a single shingle. Confirm the collar clears the pipe with room to slide into place, and confirm the flange dimensions against the shingle courses already installed, before you commit to a cutline.

Common Field Fundamentals

A penetration flashing follows the same shingle-lap water-flow principle as every other flashing in this book: the lower flange sits under the upslope shingles' drainage path by lying over the lower course, and the upper flange sits under the next course so water sheds over it rather than behind it. The flashing is integrated one course at a time while the field is being built, never inserted afterward.

Lower flangeLaps over the lower, already-installed shingle course
Boot collarFits the pipe diameter without binding or tearing
Upper flangeLaps beneath the next upslope shingle course
Exclusion zoneNo fasteners in the exposed lower flange or primary water path
Fastener sealingSeal only exposed or designated fasteners with compatible sealant
Storm collarInstall where specified, above the boot collar
CutlineShingles cut closely enough to support the flashing without binding the collar
VerificationLocal water-path observation only; not a whole-roof leak test

Selecting the Flashing Product

Select the flashing boot or curb using the pipe or penetration diameter, the roof-slope range printed in the manufacturer instructions, the roofing type being installed, the climate classification for the project location, and the service temperature of the penetration itself. Reject a boot that does not match the measured pipe diameter within the manufacturer's stated fit range.

Manufacturer Cue — Product Match Governs Fit and Fastening

The flashing manufacturer's instructions control fastener type, fastener location, sealant compatibility, and the boot's roof-slope range. Do not substitute a flashing product rated for a different roofing type, slope range, or service temperature.

Critical Safety Cue — Uncovered Penetrations Are a Fall and Water-Entry Hazard

Treat every uncovered roof penetration as a controlled hazard. Do not leave a penetration opening exposed to weather beyond the work period, and maintain fall-protection provisions for the duration of work at the opening.

Code Alert — Authorized Penetrations Only

Confirm the penetration matches the framing and ventilation plans confirmed in Chapter 1 before flashing it. Do not flash a penetration that was not authorized, relocated, or resized without an approved change document.

D. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review the approved penetration location, roof-framing clearance, pipe or curb size, flashing product, roof-slope range, temperature rating, material compatibility, and manufacturer instructions. Confirm each document's revision or date before using it as a reference and record it on the Governing Documents table.
  2. Confirm that the penetration is authorized and does not require unapproved cutting of rafters, trusses, or structural decking. Compare the penetration location and framing clearance to the Chapter 1 readiness record and the current framing plan. Stop work and route to the responsible authority if the penetration does not match.
  3. Verify the pipe, vent, or penetration is secured, plumb where required, and extended to the specified height. Measure the extension height against the approved specification and correct or refer any deficiency before flashing.
  4. Inspect the surrounding deck, underlayment, membrane, and shingle courses for damage or contamination. Record any deficiency and correct only what is authorized before proceeding.
  5. Select the correct flashing boot or curb for penetration diameter, roof slope, roofing type, climate, and service temperature. Reject a boot outside the manufacturer's stated fit range for the measured pipe diameter.
  6. Dry-fit the flashing and mark the shingle and underlayment integration limits. Use a chalk line to mark the cutline on the surrounding shingles before removing the flashing.
  7. Extend or patch self-adhered membrane around the penetration where required by the approved detail. Lap new membrane over the existing membrane in shingle-lap order, low to high.
  8. Install lower shingle courses up to the penetration. Maintain the established exposure and offset pattern from Chapter 6 layout lines.
  9. Cut the lower shingles closely enough to support the flashing without binding against the pipe. Use the utility knife and the marked cutline; do not leave a gap that would leave the flashing unsupported.
  10. Slide or place the flashing over the penetration without tearing the boot or deforming the flange. Seat the boot collar fully against the pipe before positioning the flanges.
  11. Position the lower flange over the lower shingles and the upper flange beneath the upper shingle courses. Confirm the shingle-lap relationship before fastening: lower flange on top of lower shingles, upper flange underneath upper shingles.
  12. Fasten the flashing only at manufacturer-approved locations using compatible roofing fasteners. Keep fasteners out of the exposed lower flange and any zone identified as an exclusion zone.
  13. Seal exposed or designated fasteners using the approved compatible sealant. Apply sealant only where the manufacturer specifies; do not use sealant as a substitute for the flange lap itself.
  14. Install side and upper shingles around the flashing while preserving full drainage over the flange. Maintain the established exposure and offset pattern and confirm each course laps correctly over the upper flange.
  15. Keep nails out of the exposed lower flange and prohibited water-path zones. Recheck fastener locations against the exclusion zone before covering them with the next course.
  16. Cut shingles neatly without leaving unsupported narrow strips or blocking the boot collar. Verify the collar remains clear and functional after all surrounding shingles are trimmed.
  17. Install storm collars, counterflashing, clamps, or accessory seals where specified. Follow the manufacturer's installation sequence for each accessory.
  18. Inspect for cracked boots, improper fit, reverse laps, exposed fasteners, blocked drainage, open upper corners, and excessive sealant. Correct any defect found before proceeding to the verification step.
  19. Perform the approved local water-path observation or hose test only when permitted and safe. Limit the observation to the penetration area and record the test duration and conditions.
  20. Correct defects and repeat the local inspection. Reinspect the corrected area using the same standard as the original check.
  21. Photograph and record the penetration type, flashing product, integration sequence, fasteners, sealant, and test limits. Attach photographs to the flashing record before the assembly is considered complete.

Common Penetration Flashing Checks

Apply the same checks to every penetration flashed, regardless of penetration type or roof plane.

  • Authorization check: confirm the penetration matches the approved framing and ventilation plans before flashing begins.
  • Product-match check: confirm the selected flashing product matches pipe diameter, roof slope, roofing type, climate, and service temperature.
  • Lap-direction check: confirm the lower flange sits over the lower shingles and the upper flange sits beneath the upper shingles.
  • Fastener-zone check: confirm no fasteners were placed in the exposed lower flange or exclusion zone.
  • Sealant check: confirm sealant was applied only at designated fastener locations, not as a substitute for laps.
  • Documentation check: confirm the flashing record, photographs, and test limits are complete before the area is covered by additional work.

Complete the Inspection Checklist section below before considering the penetration flashing complete.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Boot Collar Binds Against the Pipe or Splits Under Tension

Probable cause: the flashing product was undersized for the measured pipe diameter, or the collar was forced over the pipe without adequate clearance.
Confirmation: remeasure the pipe diameter and compare it to the manufacturer's stated fit range for the selected product.
Correction: replace the flashing with the correctly sized product; do not force an undersized boot into place or enlarge the collar opening beyond the manufacturer's limit.
Reinspection: recheck collar seating and clearance once the correct product is installed.

Fastener Found in the Exposed Lower Flange

Probable cause: the fastening zone was not identified before fastening, or a fastener was driven through the flange while securing an adjacent shingle.
Confirmation: inspect the lower flange for penetrations and compare their location to the manufacturer's exclusion zone.
Correction: remove the misplaced fastener, seal the resulting hole with the approved patch method, and confirm no water path remains open at that location.
Reinspection: recheck the flange for additional misplaced fasteners before covering it with the next course.

Upper Flange Left Exposed Instead of Lapped Beneath the Next Course

Probable cause: the upper shingle course was installed without confirming it laps over the upper flange, or the course was installed before the flashing was placed.
Confirmation: inspect the transition between the upper flange and the next shingle course for exposed metal or membrane.
Correction: lift the affected shingle course, reposition it to lap over the upper flange, and refasten using the approved fastening zone.
Reinspection: confirm full flange coverage and correct drainage direction after correction.

Shingle Cut Too Tight Blocks or Deforms the Boot Collar

Probable cause: the cutline was marked without dry-fitting the flashing, or the shingle was cut before the collar dimension was confirmed.
Confirmation: inspect the collar for pinching, deformation, or restricted clearance around the pipe.
Correction: trim the shingle back from the collar to the dimension confirmed during the dry-fit step; replace a shingle cut too far to remain supported.
Reinspection: recheck collar clearance and shingle support after the correction.

Excess Sealant Applied Across Flange Laps

Probable cause: sealant was used to compensate for a short lap, misaligned flange, or uncertainty about the correct shingle-lap relationship.
Confirmation: inspect the flange laps for the correct manufacturer-specified overlap without relying on sealant to bridge a gap.
Correction: remove excess sealant, correct the underlying lap or alignment defect, and reapply sealant only at designated fastener locations.
Reinspection: recheck lap dimensions and fastener sealing after correction.

Local Water-Path Observation Shows Continued Leakage

Probable cause: an undetected defect remains in the flange lap, fastener sealing, collar fit, or shingle integration.
Confirmation: repeat the observation while inspecting each integration point identified in this chapter's sequence rather than adding sealant to the visible symptom.
Correction: disassemble only the affected layer, correct the identified defect using the governing step in this chapter, and restore the surrounding shingles.
Reinspection: repeat the local water-path observation after the correction and document the result.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, tested flashing assembly and an organized record, not loose cutoffs and unsealed fasteners.

  • Remove shingle cutoffs, release film, and packaging from the work area and keep them out of drainage paths
  • Store leftover flashing accessories, fasteners, and sealant for reuse in later authorized penetration work
  • Compile the flashing record, photographs, fastener and sealant locations, and test results into the project record
  • Confirm the penetration area is protected from unnecessary traffic before adjoining shingle work continues
  • Transfer the completed flashing record to the project file referenced by Chapter 18 diagnostic work, if this penetration is later investigated for a leak

Performance Check

With the flashing installed, review the flashing record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the penetration was authorized before flashing began, the selected product matches the pipe diameter, roof slope, roofing type, climate, and service temperature, the lower and upper flanges are correctly lapped with the surrounding shingles, no fasteners were placed in the exclusion zone, sealant was applied only at designated locations, the boot collar remains clear and functional, and the local water-path observation and documentation are complete. Issue and sign the penetration flashing decision before continuing the shingle field over the remainder of the roof plane. If any item cannot be verified, record it as an open item and issue a conditional-complete decision rather than a full completion.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to conduct and document a complete exterior water-control inspection of a structure that has passed through every prior project in this book, and to issue a documented pass, conditional pass, or failed-envelope decision. The finished work product is a complete, evidence-based exterior water-control inspection record covering every elevation, opening, penetration, and sealant joint, together with a prioritized deficiency list, completed corrections, and a final readiness decision for the Project 20 capstone closeout.

You will assemble the plans, product instructions, installation records, concealed-work photographs, and prior inspection reports from every earlier project; define the inspection boundaries and access methods; inspect the WRB, windows, doors, rainscreen, siding, trim, penetrations, and sealant joints against their controlling specifications; verify that every drainage, weep, and ventilation path remains open; check for signs of hidden moisture; conduct only approved non-destructive checks and localized observations; record and prioritize every deficiency; correct or assign each one; reinspect every correction; and issue a documented decision.

This project is the second-to-last project in Book 6 and depends on the concealed-work photographs and installation records produced in every prior project. It sits immediately before Project 20, the capstone dry-in and weatherproofing of the small practice structure, which begins with this project's inspection method and readiness decision applied to the capstone structure itself.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BE-109 — Inspect water-resistive barrier laps, corners, terminations, and transitions, using concealed-work photographs and records to verify covered conditions.
  • BE-110 — Inspect window flashing, including head flashing, jamb integration, sill drainage, weep openings, frame condition, sealant, and operation.
  • BE-111 — Inspect exterior door flashing, including sill-pan drainage, head flashing, jamb flashing, threshold condition, sealant, and operation.
  • BE-112 — Inspect siding clearances, fastening, expansion gaps, joint distribution, and rainscreen drainage and ventilation continuity.
  • BE-113 — Inspect exterior sealant joints for adhesion, continuity, profile, movement capacity, contamination, and prohibited sealing of designed drainage or ventilation openings.
  • BE-114 — Document deficiencies by location, condition, controlling requirement, required correction, and responsible party, and issue a supported pass, conditional pass, or failed-envelope decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior project in this book installed one layer of the water-control system and issued its own concealed-work photographs and inspection record. This project is the first point where all of those layers are reviewed together as a single connected system rather than as isolated installations. A lap that appeared correct in Project 2, a window that operated correctly in Project 6, and a siding clearance that measured correctly in Project 12 must now be checked against each other at every transition, corner, and penetration where they meet.

The document-review, inspection, deficiency-prioritization, and readiness-decision method used here is the same method applied at the Project 1 envelope-readiness checkpoint, and it is the method the Project 20 capstone reuses in full to close out the small practice structure. A deficiency missed here is the deficiency most likely to reach an owner, a code official, or a future repair project without a documented trail back to its cause.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • assembling the approved plans, product instructions, envelope details, installation records, concealed-work photographs, prior inspection reports, and change documents from every prior project
  • defining the inspection boundaries, elevations, access methods, and conditions that remain concealed
  • inspecting visible WRB laps, repairs, corners, terminations, and roof-to-wall and foundation transitions
  • using concealed-work photographs and records to verify covered WRB and flashing details that cannot be reopened
  • inspecting every window and exterior door for flashing, drainage, sealant, and operation
  • inspecting rainscreen openings, furring continuity, insect screens, and drainage outlets where visible
  • inspecting siding, trim, penetrations, vents, and utility blocks for fit, fastening, clearance, and drainage continuity
  • inspecting sealant joints for adhesion, continuity, and prohibited sealing of designed openings
  • checking for signs of hidden moisture and performing only approved non-destructive checks or localized observations
  • recording, prioritizing, correcting, reinspecting, and documenting every deficiency, and issuing a documented readiness decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • open concealed WRB, flashing, or structural assemblies to inspect them directly when concealed-work photographs and records already exist and are adequate; unresolved gaps in the photographic or documentary record are themselves a deficiency to record, not a reason to demolish finished work without authorization
  • conduct a full-building water test, hose test, or destructive probe without an approved protocol, interior monitoring, and defined stop conditions
  • represent this inspection, or any single test performed during it, as proof that the entire building is watertight or airtight
  • correct a deficiency outside the authorized scope of this project without the responsible design professional, manufacturer, or authority having jurisdiction assigning that correction

Any active water intrusion, mold or contamination requiring specialized handling, structural damage, or uncontrolled electrical hazard discovered during inspection must stop work in that area until qualified direction is obtained.

Trade Note

This project cannot recreate a missing concealed-work photograph or installation record. Where a required record from an earlier project is incomplete, treat the affected condition as unverified rather than as passing by default, and record it as a documentation-gap deficiency with its own assigned correction, even if the visible surface condition appears acceptable.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the exterior water-control inspection until the plans, product instructions, envelope details, installation records, concealed-work photographs, prior inspection reports, and change documents from Projects 1 through 18 are assembled and confirmed complete (Step 1). Do not treat a missing or illegible concealed-work photograph as passing; record it as a documentation-gap deficiency (Step 4).

Shop Tip

Work the inspection elevation by elevation, and within each elevation work bottom to top, following the same shingle-lap water path the assembly is designed to follow. Checking a head flashing before its supporting jamb flashing has been confirmed makes it easy to miss a reverse lap that only becomes obvious when the drainage path is traced in order.

Common Field Fundamentals

This inspection follows the same method used at the Project 1 envelope-readiness checkpoint: assemble the controlling documents, establish access, inspect every visible and concealed-work-verified condition against its specification, identify conditions outside authorized correction, correct what is authorized, and issue a documented decision. The difference in this project is scope — every water-control layer installed across seventeen intervening projects is now reviewed as one connected system, and this same method is reused in full at the Project 20 capstone.

Verifying What Cannot Be Reopened

Many of the details this project must confirm — WRB laps beneath siding, sill-pan corners beneath a set window, jamb flashing beneath exterior trim — are no longer visible. Confirm these conditions using the concealed-work photographs and installation records produced in the project where each detail was installed. Where the photographic or documentary record does not clearly show a required detail, record a documentation-gap deficiency rather than assuming the condition passed.

Measurement Cue — Recheck Clearances at Every Elevation

Verify siding, trim, and flashing clearances above grade, roofs, decks, and paving at each elevation against the values approved in Projects 11 through 14. Do not assume a clearance confirmed at one elevation applies uniformly at another; grade, paving, and deck elevations often vary around the same structure.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Conduct an Unauthorized Water Test

Do not spray, flood, or otherwise apply water to a wall assembly, window, door, or penetration without an approved protocol, defined stop conditions, and interior monitoring in place. An uncontrolled water application can drive water past a marginal detail and into the wall cavity, creating the exact damage this inspection exists to prevent.

Field Safety Cue — Elevated and Roof-Adjacent Access

Use only approved ladders, scaffolds, lifts, or work platforms to inspect upper-wall, roof-to-wall, and high-transition details. Do not stand on siding, trim, or window and door units to gain height, and confirm footing and tie-off points before inspecting a roof-to-wall transition at height.

Manufacturer Cue — Product-Specific Inspection Criteria

Confirm WRB, flashing, sealant, and cladding conditions against each product's current manufacturer instructions rather than a generic visual standard. A lap width, cure condition, or joint geometry that appears adequate by eye may not meet the specific manufacturer's requirement for that product.

Code Alert — Inspection and Test Protocols Vary by Jurisdiction

Required exterior water-control inspection sign-offs, permitted localized test protocols, and reporting formats vary by adopted code and jurisdiction. Verify the current requirement with the authority having jurisdiction before treating this project's readiness decision as satisfying any jurisdictional inspection requirement.

E. Document Review and Inspection Planning (Steps 1–2)

  1. Assemble the approved plans, product instructions, envelope details, installation records, concealed-work photographs, inspection reports, and change documents. Confirm the record set covers every project from 1 through 18 and cross-reference each item against the project's document-control log.
  2. Define the inspection boundaries, elevations, access methods, and conditions that remain concealed. List each elevation, identify the access method required for each, and note which details will be verified through direct inspection versus concealed-work photographs and records.

F. WRB and Envelope Continuity Review (Steps 3–4)

  1. Inspect visible WRB laps, repairs, corners, terminations, roof-to-wall transitions, and foundation transitions. Confirm every visible lap follows the required shingle-lap direction and every termination remains sealed or protected as detailed.
  2. Use concealed-work photographs and records to verify covered WRB and flashing details. Record a documentation-gap deficiency for any location where the photographic or documentary record is missing, illegible, or does not clearly show the required detail.

G. Window, Door, and Rainscreen Review (Steps 5–7)

  1. Inspect each window for head flashing, jamb integration, sill drainage, weep openings, frame condition, sealant, and operation. Compare each unit against its Project 4 through 6 flashing and installation records.
  2. Inspect each exterior door for sill-pan drainage, head flashing, jamb flashing, threshold condition, sealant, and operation. Compare each unit against its Project 7 through 9 flashing and installation records.
  3. Inspect rainscreen base and head openings, furring continuity, insect screens, and drainage outlets where visible. Confirm the rainscreen cavity remains unobstructed at every accessible opening and transition.

H. Siding, Trim, and Penetration Review (Steps 8–11)

  1. Inspect horizontal and vertical siding for level or plumb, fastening, expansion gaps, joints, edge treatment, and damage. Check both cladding systems installed under Projects 12 and 13 against their respective acceptance standards.
  2. Verify clearances above grade, roofs, decks, paving, flashings, and horizontal surfaces. Recheck each clearance individually at every elevation rather than assuming uniformity.
  3. Inspect window, door, corner, roofline, and transition trim for fit, flashing clearance, and open drainage paths. Confirm no trim installation has closed a designed weep, sill-pan outlet, or ventilation opening.
  4. Inspect every vent, utility block, hose bibb, electrical box, conduit, cable, and service penetration. Confirm each penetration flashing from Projects 16 and 17 remains intact and undamaged by subsequent trades.

I. Sealant, Drainage, and Moisture Review (Steps 12–16)

  1. Inspect exterior sealant joints for adhesion, continuity, profile, movement, contamination, and prohibited sealing. Confirm every joint matches the Project 15 sealant schedule and that no designed opening has been sealed shut.
  2. Check that weeps, sill-pan outlets, cladding drainage, rainscreen ventilation, and vent openings remain unobstructed. Confirm each outlet identified in the approved details is open and free of debris, sealant, or trim obstruction.
  3. Inspect wall surfaces for staining, swelling, buckling, cracks, loose components, corrosion, and signs of hidden moisture. Give particular attention to areas below windows, doors, and penetrations and at wall-to-roof and wall-to-foundation transitions.
  4. Perform approved non-destructive moisture checks where indicated by staining or risk. Use the moisture meter at flagged locations and compare readings against the applicable material's acceptable range.
  5. Conduct localized water observations or tests only under an approved protocol with interior monitoring and defined stop conditions. Do not proceed with an observation unless the protocol, monitoring method, and stop conditions are confirmed and documented in advance.

J. Deficiency Management and Readiness Decision (Steps 17–22)

  1. Record each deficiency by elevation, location, condition, controlling requirement, required correction, and responsible party. Use the same format for every deficiency so the record can be prioritized consistently.
  2. Prioritize deficiencies as immediate stop-work, water-path failure, durability concern, appearance issue, or documentation gap. Assign the priority level based on the controlling requirement violated and the potential consequence of leaving it uncorrected.
  3. Correct or assign each deficiency. Complete every correction within this project's authorized scope directly, and assign every other deficiency to its responsible design professional, manufacturer representative, or authority having jurisdiction.
  4. Reinspect every completed correction. Confirm each corrected condition against the Acceptance Standards table before marking the deficiency closed.
  5. Photograph final conditions and complete the exterior water-control inspection record. Label each photograph with its elevation, location, and corresponding deficiency reference.
  6. Issue a pass, conditional pass, or failed-envelope decision for the capstone closeout. Base the decision on the completed deficiency list, the Acceptance Standards table, and the status of every assigned item.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the completed exterior water-control system using the same checks regardless of which elevation it covers.

  • Documentation check: confirm every required plan, product instruction, installation record, and concealed-work photograph from Projects 1 through 18 is assembled, indexed, and referenced on the exterior water-control inspection record.
  • WRB and flashing check: confirm every visible and concealed-work-verified lap, corner, termination, and transition matches the approved detail.
  • Opening check: confirm every window and exterior door has intact head flashing, jamb integration, sill drainage, sealant, and operation.
  • Cladding check: confirm rainscreen, siding, and trim clearances, fastening, joints, and drainage continuity match the approved specifications.
  • Sealant and drainage check: confirm every sealant joint is correctly placed and every weep, outlet, and ventilation opening remains unobstructed.
  • Decision check: confirm a documented pass, conditional pass, or failed-envelope decision has been issued with every deficiency prioritized, corrected, or assigned to a responsible party.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Required Concealed-Work Photograph Is Missing or Illegible

Probable cause: the photograph was not taken, was taken before the detail was complete, or was lost or corrupted in the project record set.
Confirmation: search the full record set from the project where the detail was installed and confirm with the responsible installer whether documentation exists elsewhere.
Correction: record a documentation-gap deficiency and assign it to the responsible party rather than assuming the concealed condition passed.
Reinspection: close the deficiency only when acceptable documentation is produced or the responsible authority accepts an alternative verification method.

A Sealant Joint Has Been Applied Across a Designed Weep or Drainage Opening

Probable cause: the sealant application in Project 15 did not correctly separate designated joints from designed drainage outlets, or a later trade sealed an opening during trim or siding work.
Confirmation: compare the sealed location against the approved sealant schedule and the drainage-outlet locations shown in the window, door, or siding details.
Correction: cut out the sealant obstructing the opening and confirm the outlet drains freely; do not leave a partial obstruction.
Reinspection: confirm the outlet remains open under a controlled water check where the approved protocol permits it.

Staining Is Found Below a Window or Door That Passed Its Individual Project Inspection

Probable cause: a lap, sealant joint, or drainage path that appeared correct in isolation is failing at a transition point only visible once surrounding work is complete.
Confirmation: check moisture content at the affected location and trace the water path upward through the head flashing, jamb flashing, and sill drainage using available concealed-work photographs.
Correction: assign qualified review if the failure point requires reopening completed work; do not conceal or paint over the staining.
Reinspection: repeat the moisture and visual check at the location once the documented correction is complete.

A Rainscreen or Ventilation Opening Has Been Blocked by Later Trim or Siding Work

Probable cause: trim or siding installed after the rainscreen was inspected in Project 10 covered or crushed a required base or head opening.
Confirmation: visually inspect the affected opening and compare its current condition against the approved rainscreen detail and Project 10 inspection record.
Correction: reopen the obstructed opening using the approved method without damaging surrounding cladding.
Reinspection: confirm airflow and drainage continuity at the corrected opening.

A Deficiency Cannot Be Assigned Because No Responsible Party Is Identified

Probable cause: the deficiency falls outside every prior project's documented scope, or responsibility was never assigned in the original contract or specification.
Confirmation: review the approved plans, specification, and change documentation to determine which trade or authority controls the affected detail.
Correction: escalate the unassigned deficiency to the general contractor or design professional for a responsibility determination before closing the item.
Reinspection: repeat the applicable inspection once the assigned party completes the correction.

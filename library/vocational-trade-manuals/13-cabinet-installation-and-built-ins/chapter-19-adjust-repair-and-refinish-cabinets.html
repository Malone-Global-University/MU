Job Overview

This project qualifies you to diagnose and correct a controlled set of cabinet alignment, hardware, joint, surface, and finish defects found on installed cabinets. The finished work product is a set of repaired cabinet components — hinges and slides returned to correct adjustment, loose joints reglued and clamped, damaged fastener holes repaired, surface chips and finish damage matched and blended — verified by repeated operation and documented on a cabinet-repair record.

You will record the complaint and affected component, inspect the cabinet box for the conditions that must be corrected before any hardware adjustment, identify which category of defect is present, match the repair method to the affected material, adjust hinges and slides in the manufacturer-specified sequence, repair damaged fastener holes and loose joints, patch surface damage, blend finish, replace a component when repair cannot restore safe support or acceptable appearance, and verify the repair by operating every affected door, drawer, pullout, and shelf.

This project assumes the affected cabinets were installed under Projects 4 through 18 and that the original fastener records, cabinet designations, and hardware settings from those projects are available for reference. It sits immediately before Project 20, the capstone, which relies on this project's diagnostic and repair method during its own punch-list correction phase.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CB-109 — Adjust cabinet door hinges for height, side, and depth in the manufacturer-specified sequence to restore correct reveal and operation.
  • CB-110 — Adjust drawer slides for height, tilt, side clearance, and closing action to restore correct drawer-front alignment and operation.
  • CB-111 — Repair loose cabinet joints and damaged fastener holes using a method matched to the cabinet material and structural requirement.
  • CB-112 — Patch damaged cabinet surfaces using a repair material compatible with the affected substrate.
  • CB-113 — Touch up cabinet finishes by blending color and sheen within the existing finish system.
  • CB-114 — Inspect repaired cabinet operation and document the verified cause, repair method, and final result.

Where This Skill Fits

Every repair in this project depends on records created earlier in the book. The cabinet designations, fastener locations, and hardware settings documented in Projects 4 through 6 and 12 identify what the affected component should look like when correctly installed, and the finish schedule referenced throughout the book controls which repair material is compatible with the affected surface. A repair completed here that does not match the original structural fastening or finish system creates a second defect in place of the first.

The diagnostic method used here — record the complaint, inspect before adjusting, correct structural or moisture conditions before cosmetic conditions, match the repair to the material, and verify by operation — is the same method the Chapter 20 capstone applies during its own punch-list correction and reinspection phase.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • recording the complaint, affected component, finish type, damage cause, and any prior adjustment history
  • inspecting the cabinet box for level, plumb, square, fastening, moisture, and structural damage before any hardware adjustment
  • identifying whether the defect is a hinge adjustment, slide adjustment, loose joint, damaged fastener hole, surface chip, scratch, delamination, or finish-damage condition
  • matching the repair method to solid wood, veneer, plywood, MDF, particleboard, laminate, thermofoil, or painted material
  • adjusting hinge height, side, and depth, and drawer-slide height, tilt, side clearance, and closing action, in the manufacturer-specified sequence
  • tightening loose hardware without stripping the substrate, and repairing damaged fastener holes using an approved insert, larger listed fastener, or reinforced repair
  • clamping and gluing loose cabinet joints after checking alignment, and allowing adhesive to cure before loading or operation
  • patching small chips, dents, and edge damage with compatible repair material, and cleaning, scuffing, priming, staining, painting, or topcoating only within the existing finish system
  • replacing a component when repair cannot restore safe support or acceptable appearance
  • operating every repaired door, drawer, pullout, and shelf repeatedly and documenting the verified cause, repair, finish material, and final operation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • adjust hinges, slides, or hardware, or apply any cosmetic patch or finish, before correcting a loose cabinet mounting, active moisture condition, or structural defect found during inspection
  • use a filler, adhesive, or finish material incompatible with the affected substrate, including filler intended for solid wood used on flexible laminate or thermofoil
  • enlarge a fastener hole, drill a new joint location, or substitute a different structural fastener without matching the original fastener record and cabinet-construction requirement
  • load, operate, or place weight on a repaired joint, patched surface, or refinished component before the specified adhesive or finish cure time has elapsed
  • conceal a defect that cannot be corrected by an approved repair method rather than replacing the affected component

Any condition suggesting active moisture, structural damage, hazardous coatings, or a defect outside this project's authorized repair methods must stop cosmetic work in that area until direction is obtained.

Trade Note

A repair completed without first verifying the cabinet's structural mounting, level, and moisture condition treats a symptom instead of the defect. Restoring a hinge reveal on a cabinet that is no longer level, or patching a finish over an active moisture source, produces a repair that fails again and obscures the original cause from the next inspection.

Setup Standard

Do not begin hinge or slide adjustment, joint repair, patching, or refinishing until the complaint, affected component, finish type, damage cause, and prior adjustment history are recorded on the cabinet-repair record (Step 1), and the cabinet box has been inspected for level, plumb, square, fastening, moisture, and structural damage (Step 2).

Shop Tip

Photograph the defect before making any adjustment. A hinge reveal, a joint gap, or a finish blemish photographed before correction gives the reinspection step a clear before-and-after record and helps confirm the verified cause if the defect recurs.

Separating Structural Conditions From Cosmetic Conditions

Every defect found in this project falls into one of two categories: a structural condition affecting the cabinet's mounting, level, plumb, square, or moisture exposure, or a cosmetic condition affecting hardware adjustment, surface appearance, or finish. A structural condition must be corrected first. Adjusting a hinge or patching a finish on a cabinet that is not level, plumb, or securely fastened restores appearance temporarily and allows the underlying condition to reopen the same defect.

Critical Safety Cue — Correct Structural or Moisture Conditions Before Cosmetic Repair

Do not adjust hinges or slides, patch a surface, or apply finish over a cabinet with loose structural mounting, active moisture, or unresolved framing or backing defects. Correct the structural or moisture condition first, using the applicable installation project's fastening and moisture-control requirements, and confirm the correction before proceeding with any cosmetic repair.

Manufacturer Cue — Hinge and Slide Adjustment Sequence

Adjust hinge height, side, and depth, and drawer-slide height, tilt, side clearance, and closing action, only in the sequence specified by the hinge or slide manufacturer's instructions. Adjusting these points out of sequence can cause one adjustment to undo another and produce a reveal that will not hold.

Measurement Cue — Match the Repaired Reveal to the Original Installation

Compare the corrected door or drawer-front reveal against the adjacent doors and drawers and against the original reveal recorded in the Project 12 hardware settings. A repair that corrects one door or drawer in isolation without matching the surrounding reveal is not complete.

Code Alert — Hazardous Coatings and Materials Vary by Building Age

Stop sanding, scraping, or otherwise disturbing an existing finish if lead-based coatings, asbestos-containing materials, or other hazardous substances are suspected based on the building's age or condition. Verify the current testing and abatement requirement with the authority having jurisdiction before continuing any repair that disturbs the existing finish.

C. Repair Materials and Consumables

  • Adhesive: a wood or panel adhesive compatible with the cabinet material identified in the finish schedule, sufficient for every loose joint identified.
  • Filler: a wood filler, laminate-compatible filler, or other repair material matched to the affected substrate for every chip, dent, or damaged fastener hole identified.
  • Finish materials: a stain, paint, or topcoat matched to the existing finish schedule, sufficient to blend every touch-up area identified.
  • Documentation: one cabinet-repair record per reported complaint or per cabinet run as required by the project specification.

E. Complaint Recording and Structural Inspection (Steps 1–3)

  1. Record the complaint, affected component, finish type, damage cause, and prior adjustments. Enter this information directly on the cabinet-repair record before touching the affected component.
  2. Inspect the cabinet box for level, plumb, square, fastening, moisture, and structural damage. Use the 4-foot level and moisture meter, and compare current fastening against the original fastener record.
  3. Correct loose cabinet mounting or active moisture before hardware adjustment. Reference the applicable installation project's fastening and moisture-control requirements, and confirm the correction before continuing.

F. Defect Classification and Material Matching (Steps 4–5)

  1. Identify whether the defect is hinge adjustment, slide adjustment, loose joint, damaged fastener hole, surface chip, scratch, delamination, or finish damage. Record the classification on the cabinet-repair record using the categories in the Defect-Category and Repair-Method Schedule.
  2. Match the repair method to solid wood, veneer, plywood, MDF, particleboard, laminate, thermofoil, or painted material. Confirm the selected adhesive, filler, or finish material is compatible with the identified substrate before proceeding.

G. Hardware Adjustment and Structural Repair (Steps 6–11)

  1. Adjust hinge height, side, and depth in the manufacturer-specified sequence. Compare the resulting reveal against the adjacent doors and the original Project 12 settings.
  2. Adjust drawer-slide height, tilt, side clearance, and closing action. Operate the drawer through its full range after each adjustment.
  3. Tighten loose hardware without stripping substrate. Stop turning a fastener the moment it seats; do not continue driving a fastener that spins without tightening.
  4. Repair damaged fastener holes using an approved insert, larger listed fastener, or reinforced repair. Match the repair to the original fastener record's load requirement; do not substitute an undersized or incompatible fastener.
  5. Clamp and glue loose cabinet joints only after checking alignment. Confirm the joint returns to its original square and flush condition before applying adhesive.
  6. Allow adhesive to cure before loading or operation. Reference the adhesive manufacturer's specified cure time and record it on the cabinet-repair record.

H. Surface Repair and Finish Blending (Steps 12–16)

  1. Patch small chips, dents, and edge damage using compatible repair material. Apply the filler with the putty knife and level it slightly proud of the surrounding surface for sanding.
  2. Do not use incompatible filler on flexible laminate or thermofoil. Use a reglued or manufacturer-approved repair method for delamination or edge lifting instead of rigid wood filler.
  3. Clean, scuff, prime, stain, paint, or topcoat only within the finish system. Confirm the finish schedule's specified product and sequence before applying any coat.
  4. Blend color and sheen using a sample area. Test the stain, paint, or topcoat on an inconspicuous or purpose-prepared sample area before applying it to the visible repair.
  5. Replace components when repair cannot restore safe support or acceptable appearance. Identify the affected component on the cabinet-repair record and coordinate its replacement rather than concealing a repair that does not meet the Acceptance Standards table.

I. Verification and Documentation (Steps 17–19)

  1. Operate doors, drawers, pullouts, and shelves repeatedly. Confirm smooth operation through the full range of travel with no binding, sagging, or unintended contact.
  2. Inspect reveals, hardware security, joint movement, finish blend, and cabinet mounting. Compare the repaired condition against the Acceptance Standards table before closing the record.
  3. Document the verified cause, repair, finish material, and final operation. Complete the cabinet-repair record with the classification, repair method, materials used, cure time observed, and the result of the final operational check.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the repaired component using the same checks regardless of which defect category was corrected.

  • Structural check: confirm the cabinet box remains level, plumb, square, and securely fastened, and shows no active moisture, before the repair is considered complete.
  • Hardware check: confirm hinge and slide adjustments restore the original reveal and produce smooth operation without binding or sagging.
  • Joint and fastener check: confirm repaired joints and fastener holes show no movement under the applied test and match the original fastener record.
  • Surface and finish check: confirm patched surfaces are level with the surrounding material and blended finish matches color and sheen under normal lighting.
  • Cure-time check: confirm no component was loaded or operated before the specified adhesive or finish cure time elapsed.
  • Documentation check: confirm the verified cause, repair method, materials, cure time, and final operation are recorded on the cabinet-repair record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Door Reveal Will Not Hold After Hinge Adjustment

Probable cause: the cabinet box has shifted out of level or plumb since original installation, or the hinge adjustment sequence was not followed in the manufacturer-specified order.
Confirmation: check the cabinet box level and plumb against the Project 4 through 6 acceptance standards, and repeat the hinge adjustment sequence in the specified order.
Correction: correct the underlying cabinet mounting condition before readjusting the hinge; do not readjust hinges repeatedly on a cabinet that is not level or plumb.
Reinspection: confirm level and plumb, then reconfirm the reveal against adjacent doors after the hinge adjustment.

A Drawer Binds or Closes Unevenly After Slide Adjustment

Probable cause: a slide adjustment point was turned out of the manufacturer-specified sequence, or debris is obstructing the slide track.
Confirmation: inspect the slide track for debris and repeat the adjustment sequence in the specified order while operating the drawer after each adjustment.
Correction: clean the slide track and readjust height, tilt, and side clearance in sequence until the drawer closes evenly without binding.
Reinspection: operate the drawer repeatedly through its full range and confirm even closing action before documenting the repair.

A Glued Joint Separates Again After Cure

Probable cause: the joint was loaded or operated before the adhesive's specified cure time elapsed, or the joint was not clamped in correct alignment during cure.
Confirmation: inspect the joint surfaces for a clean break versus a re-separated glue line, and confirm the recorded cure time against the adhesive manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: clean the joint surfaces, reapply compatible adhesive, reclamp in correct alignment, and allow the full specified cure time before any loading.
Reinspection: test the joint for movement only after the full cure time has elapsed.

A Repaired Fastener Hole Does Not Hold the Original Fastener Load

Probable cause: the repair used an undersized insert or a fastener smaller than the original fastener record specifies.
Confirmation: compare the repaired fastener size and type against the original fastener record from the applicable installation project.
Correction: remove the inadequate repair and install the approved insert, larger listed fastener, or reinforced repair matched to the original load requirement.
Reinspection: confirm fastener engagement and retest the component's structural condition before returning it to service.

A Blended Finish Does Not Match Under Normal Lighting

Probable cause: the stain, paint, or topcoat was applied without first testing it on a sample area, or the existing finish has aged or faded since original application.
Confirmation: compare the blended area against the surrounding finish under the room's normal lighting condition, and test an additional sample area if a mismatch is visible.
Correction: adjust the finish material or application method based on the sample-area test before reapplying to the visible repair area.
Reinspection: reinspect the blend under normal lighting after the adjusted finish has cured.

A Repair Cannot Restore Safe Support or Acceptable Appearance

Probable cause: the damage exceeds what the approved repair method can correct, such as a fastener hole enlarged beyond the reinforced-repair limit or a panel damaged beyond compatible patching.
Confirmation: compare the extent of damage against the approved repair method's limits identified in the Defect-Category and Repair-Method Schedule.
Correction: identify the affected component on the cabinet-repair record and coordinate its replacement rather than applying a repair that will not meet the Acceptance Standards table.
Reinspection: reinspect the replaced component under the applicable installation project's acceptance standards.

Cleanup

This project ends with a documented cabinet-repair record and a verified operating component, not with the affected cabinet left in a partially adjusted or unfinished state.

  • Remove clamps, masking, and sample-area materials once the specified cure time has elapsed
  • Store leftover adhesive, filler, and finish materials according to the manufacturer's storage instructions
  • Store the cabinet-repair record, photographs, and updated fastener or hardware settings with the project's permanent documentation
  • Confirm the repair area remains accessible and undamaged before leaving the site
  • Communicate any component identified for replacement to the responsible designer, cabinet installer, or manufacturer before closing the record

Field Terms

Verified Cause
The confirmed condition responsible for the reported defect, established by inspection rather than assumed from the symptom.
Repair Method
The specific adjustment, adhesive, filler, or finish process matched to the affected material and defect category.
Reveal
The consistent gap between adjacent door or drawer-front edges, or between a door or drawer front and the cabinet face.
Sample Area
A small, inconspicuous, or purpose-prepared surface used to test filler, stain, paint, or topcoat blend before the visible repair.
Structural Repair
A repair that restores a cabinet joint, fastener hole, or mounting to its original load-bearing condition.
Cosmetic Repair
A repair that restores surface or finish appearance without affecting structural mounting or load-bearing condition.
Cure Time
The manufacturer-specified period an adhesive or finish must set before the repaired component may be loaded or operated.
Cabinet-Repair Record
The document recording the complaint, verified cause, repair method, finish material, cure time, and final verified operation.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to evaluate an existing crack, spall, or minor surface defect in cured exterior concrete flatwork and complete a compatible, bonded repair within documented cause-based limits. The finished work product is one repaired defect: cleaned, prepared, filled or patched with the approved repair system, finished to match the surrounding plane and texture, cured, and fully documented with its diagnosed cause and any referral limitations.

You will record the damage condition, inspect for signs of active structural movement, stop work on any condition outside the scope of nonstructural repair, prepare the defect, select and mix the correct repair product, apply and finish the repair, cure and protect it, and inspect the bonded result.

This chapter depends directly on the earlier chapters in this book. The crack-routing and joint-preservation work here uses the same jointing discipline taught in Chapter 17. The repair itself must never conceal an unresolved drainage, subgrade, or reinforcement problem of the kind Chapters 4 through 8 were built to prevent. Chapter 19 depends on this chapter: sealing exterior flatwork requires that any repairable crack or joint already be repaired and cured before a sealer is applied.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-103 — Evaluate concrete damage, including movement, moisture, load, age, and freeze exposure.
  • FW-104 — Clean repair areas of loose material, laitance, dust, oil, and contaminants.
  • FW-105 — Route cracks using wet cutting or HEPA extraction to the approved geometry.
  • FW-106 — Patch spalled areas to sound concrete using the specified repair geometry.
  • FW-107 — Apply repair mortar or filler accurately, in specified lifts, without trapped air.
  • FW-108 — Inspect repair bond, finish, and documentation, and identify referral limitations.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter sits after the applied flatwork projects in Chapters 13 through 16 and the jointing work in Chapter 17, and immediately before the sealing and maintenance work in Chapter 19. A repair completed here must be finished to match the surrounding plane and texture and must preserve every movement joint identified in Chapter 17, because Chapter 19 will seal across both the original surface and this repair as one continuous system.

The stop-work discipline carried through every chapter in this book applies with particular force here: this chapter repairs only a verified nonstructural defect. Any condition involving active structural movement, foundation settlement, or failed load-bearing slabs must be referred rather than patched.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • evaluating a single crack, spall, or minor surface defect in existing, cured exterior concrete flatwork
  • diagnosing whether a crack is dormant, moving, shrinkage-related, or joint-related from documented observation
  • routing and sealing dormant, nonstructural cracks using wet cutting or HEPA extraction
  • preparing and patching a spall to sound concrete using a manufacturer-identified repair product
  • applying repair mortar, epoxy, or polymer filler in the specified lifts, curing it, and finishing it to match the surrounding surface
  • preserving existing movement joints throughout the repair
  • inspecting the finished repair's bond, plane, texture, and documentation

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • repair an active, moving structural crack, vertical displacement, widespread scaling, severe reinforcement corrosion, foundation movement, or a failed load-bearing slab
  • invent a repair-product mix ratio, cure time, bonding-agent requirement, or lift thickness not supplied by the manufacturer's identified instructions
  • expose or treat reinforcement except under an approved repair detail
  • eliminate, relocate, or seal over a movement joint that the approved joint plan requires to remain functional
  • proceed with a cosmetic repair when the underlying cause is unresolved, unidentified, or outside the scope of nonstructural repair

Any condition that requires structural, geotechnical, or other qualified engineering review must be documented and referred, not patched over.

Trade Note

A repair that looks correct the day it is finished but was never diagnosed for cause is not a completed repair — it is a delayed failure. Recording the diagnosed cause in Step 4 of the sequence below is not paperwork; it is the decision that determines whether the rest of this chapter's procedure is even permitted to proceed.

Setup Standard

Do not begin surface preparation, routing, or patching until the damage has been recorded and evaluated for movement, settlement, corrosion, or structural cause. A repair applied before this evaluation is complete must be treated as unauthorized and stopped.

Shop Tip

Photograph the defect before any preparation begins, including a scale reference such as a tape measure in the frame. The pre-repair photograph is often the only remaining record of the original width, depth, and extent once the area has been routed, chiseled, and patched.

Common Field Fundamentals

A repair is not a cosmetic patch applied to whatever concrete happens to be broken. It is a diagnosed, cause-based decision about whether this defect may be repaired at all, followed by a preparation and application sequence controlled by the identified repair product's own instructions.

Diagnosis Before Preparation

Every other step in this chapter depends on the diagnosis completed in Steps 1 through 5 of the sequence below. Routing a crack, chiseling a spall, or mixing a repair product before the cause has been evaluated risks concealing a condition that required referral. Do not allow the visible convenience of an available repair product to substitute for the diagnostic step.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work on Active Structural Movement

Stop work for active structural cracks, vertical displacement, widespread scaling, severe reinforcement corrosion, foundation movement, or failed load-bearing slabs. Document the specific condition and refer it for structural or geotechnical review rather than proceeding with a cosmetic repair.

Measurement Cue — Record Before You Alter

Record damage location, width, depth, length, movement, moisture, load, age, freeze exposure, and prior repair before any routing, chiseling, or cleaning begins. Preparation work permanently alters the original defect geometry, so the pre-repair measurement is the only accurate record of the untouched condition.

Field Safety Cue — Dust and Slurry Control

Control cement, aggregate, and silica dust during routing, chiseling, and cleanup. Do not dry cut, grind, or sweep concrete dust without the required wet-cutting, HEPA collection, or respiratory protection called for by the preparation method.

Manufacturer Cue — Substrate Condition Controls Bond

Condition the substrate exactly as dry, damp, primed, or saturated-surface-dry according to the identified repair product's instructions. Applying the product to a substrate condition the manufacturer does not specify is a common cause of the debonding failures diagnosed later in this chapter.

Repair / Diagnostic Qualification

Crack, Spall, and Surface-Defect Repair

Difficulty: Intermediate

Estimated Time: 3–6 hours, plus cure time

Estimated Cost: Varies with repair product and measured defect area

Finished Work: One bonded, cured, documented repair to a verified nonstructural defect

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one crack, spall, or minor surface defect in existing, cured exterior concrete flatwork, diagnosed for cause, prepared, repaired with a manufacturer-identified compatible product, cured, finished to match the surrounding plane and texture, and documented with its diagnosed cause and any referral limitations.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Record damage location, width, depth, length, movement, moisture, load, age, freeze exposure, and prior repair. Complete this record before any preparation begins.
  2. Inspect for settlement, heave, structural movement, corrosion, failed base, drainage, chemical attack, or ongoing impact. Evaluate the surrounding area, not only the defect itself.
  3. Stop work for active structural cracks, vertical displacement, widespread scaling, severe reinforcement corrosion, foundation movement, or failed load-bearing slabs. Document the condition and refer it rather than proceeding.
  4. Determine whether the crack is dormant, moving, shrinkage-related, joint-related, or unresolved. Base the determination only on the documented evaluation from Steps 1 and 2.
  5. Select routing and sealing only for the approved crack condition. Do not route and seal a crack whose cause has not been determined or that requires referral.
  6. Barricade and protect the work area. Establish clear boundaries before cutting, chiseling, or mixing begins.
  7. Route the crack using wet cutting or HEPA extraction. Control dust and slurry throughout routing.
  8. Remove loose concrete, laitance, dust, oil, sealers, and contaminants. Clean the full prepared area, not only the visible defect.
  9. Prepare spall edges to sound concrete and the repair product's required geometry. Confirm soundness by the method specified for this repair before proceeding.
  10. Expose and treat reinforcement only under an approved repair detail. Do not expose reinforcement without a specific, approved instruction to do so.
  11. Condition the substrate as dry, damp, primed, or saturated-surface-dry according to the product. Verify the condition using the specified method before mixing.
  12. Mix repair mortar, epoxy, polymer filler, or sealant accurately. Follow the manufacturer's identified ratio, mixing time, and working time.
  13. Apply bonding agent where required. Use only the bonding agent identified as compatible with the selected repair product.
  14. Fill the crack or spall in the specified lifts. Do not exceed the lift thickness identified by the manufacturer.
  15. Consolidate repair material without trapping air. Work the material into full contact with the prepared substrate.
  16. Finish the repair to match surrounding plane and texture. Compare the finished repair directly against the adjacent original surface.
  17. Preserve movement joints. Confirm no joint required by the approved joint plan has been filled, bridged, or eliminated by the repair.
  18. Cure and protect the repair. Follow the manufacturer's identified cure method, duration, and protection requirements.
  19. Inspect bond, edge feathering, shrinkage, cracking, color, plane, and texture. Confirm the repair meets the acceptance standards below.
  20. Repeat movement or moisture observations where applicable. Reconfirm dormant-crack status or substrate moisture condition if the repair schedule requires a delayed check.
  21. Document cause, product, preparation, cure, and repair limitations. Complete the repair log before closeout.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Repair log: the completed record of every finding and action in Steps 1–20, including the diagnosed cause and final acceptance.
  • Pre-repair photographs: damage location, width, depth, and extent, with a scale reference, taken before preparation began.
  • Post-repair photographs: finished repair showing bond, plane, texture, and joint preservation.
  • Product record: identified repair product, bonding agent, mix ratio, lift count, and cure method used.
  • Repair limitations disclosure: any condition this repair does not resolve, carried forward to Chapter 19 before sealing and to future maintenance records.

Common Repair Inspection

Inspect every repair using the same checks regardless of defect type.

  • Cause check: confirm the crack or defect was classified as dormant, moving, shrinkage-related, joint-related, or unresolved before any preparation began.
  • Referral check: confirm any structural, settlement, or corrosion condition was documented and referred rather than patched.
  • Preparation check: confirm loose material, laitance, dust, and contaminants were removed and spall edges reached sound concrete.
  • Substrate check: confirm the substrate condition matched the identified repair product's requirement before mixing.
  • Application check: confirm the product was mixed accurately and applied in the specified lifts without trapped air.
  • Joint check: confirm every required movement joint remains functional and unfilled by the repair.
  • Finish and cure check: confirm the repair matches the surrounding plane and texture and was cured per the manufacturer's instructions.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Repair Applied Before Cause Was Determined

Probable cause: the crack or defect was patched using an available repair product before Steps 1 through 4 confirmed whether the condition was dormant, moving, or required referral.
Confirmation: review the repair log for a documented cause determination dated before any preparation or application step.
Correction: if the cause was never determined, evaluate the finished repair area and surrounding slab now for movement, settlement, or corrosion, and refer any condition outside the scope of this chapter.
Reinspection: repeat the cause-determination check before accepting the repair as complete.

Debonding at the Repair Edge

Probable cause: the substrate was not conditioned to the moisture or preparation state the manufacturer's instructions require, or bonding agent was omitted where specified.
Confirmation: inspect the repair edge for hollow sound, visible gap, or separation, and compare the preparation record against the manufacturer's substrate-condition requirement.
Correction: remove the debonded repair material, re-prepare the substrate to the specified condition, reapply bonding agent where required, and reapply the repair product.
Reinspection: repeat the bond check after the new repair has cured.

Movement Joint Filled or Bridged by the Repair

Probable cause: repair material was placed across a required movement joint without protecting or preserving the joint opening.
Confirmation: compare the repaired area against the approved joint plan and inspect for continuous repair material spanning a joint line.
Correction: cut back the repair material to reestablish the full joint width and depth required by the joint plan.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-preservation check before finishing the repair.

Repair Mortar Cracked or Shrunk After Cure

Probable cause: the product was mixed outside the manufacturer's ratio, placed in a single lift exceeding the specified thickness, or cured without the specified protection.
Confirmation: review the mixing and application record against the manufacturer's instructions and inspect the repair for shrinkage cracking or surface crazing.
Correction: remove the affected repair material and reapply following the manufacturer's specified ratio, lift thickness, and cure method.
Reinspection: repeat the finish and cure check on the corrected repair.

Structural Movement Discovered After a Cosmetic Repair Was Started

Probable cause: preparation began before the full evaluation in Steps 1 and 2 was complete, and signs of settlement, heave, or ongoing movement were discovered mid-repair.
Confirmation: stop work immediately and reinspect the surrounding slab, base, and drainage for the specific condition observed.
Correction: document the condition, halt further cosmetic repair, and refer the area for structural or geotechnical review before any further work proceeds.
Reinspection: do not resume repair work until the referral is resolved and a new readiness decision is documented.

Finished Repair Does Not Match Surrounding Texture or Color

Probable cause: the finishing technique or product selection did not account for the surrounding surface's texture, or the repair was finished at the wrong stage of set.
Confirmation: compare the finished repair directly against the adjacent original surface under the same lighting conditions.
Correction: where the manufacturer's instructions permit, refinish the surface within the product's working time; otherwise document the color or texture variance as a known limitation.
Reinspection: repeat the finish comparison after any correction and before closeout.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, filed repair log, not a verbal impression of the finished patch.

  • Clean mixing containers, trowels, and tools only in the contained washout area
  • Do not discharge repair-product washout, slurry, or excess material to soil, drains, gutters, waterways, or streets
  • Remove barricades and protection materials only after the repair has cured to the manufacturer's specified condition
  • Store leftover repair product, bonding agent, and manufacturer instructions together and labeled for future reference
  • File the completed repair log, pre- and post-repair photographs, and product record together

Field Terms

Dormant Crack
A crack showing no measured or observed change in width, alignment, or vertical offset across the documented observation period.
Moving Crack
A crack showing measured or observed change in width, alignment, or vertical offset, requiring referral rather than routine repair.
Spall
An area where surface concrete has fractured, delaminated, or broken away, exposing underlying material.
Sound Concrete
Concrete free of loose material, delamination, or unsound bond, confirmed by the method specified for the repair.
Saturated-Surface-Dry (SSD)
A substrate condition, specific to the identified repair product, that is neither dry nor holding visible surface water.
Bonding Agent
A product identified as compatible with the selected repair material, applied to improve adhesion between new and existing concrete.
Repair Limitation
Any documented condition, cause, or defect scope that a completed repair does not resolve, disclosed at closeout.
Movement Joint
An isolation, expansion, contraction, or control joint that must remain functional and unfilled through a repair.
Lift
A single layer of repair material placed and consolidated before the next layer is applied, per the manufacturer's specified thickness.
Repair Log
The document recording every finding, diagnosis, preparation step, product used, and final acceptance for a single repair.

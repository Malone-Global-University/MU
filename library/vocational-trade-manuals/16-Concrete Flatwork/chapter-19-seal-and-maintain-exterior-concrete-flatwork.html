Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect, clean, prepare, seal, protect, and document one cured exterior concrete-flatwork surface. The finished work product is a clean, repaired, uniformly sealed exterior concrete surface with preserved drainage, movement, and slip characteristics, and a completed maintenance record.

You will confirm the concrete has reached the required cure and drying condition, test for existing sealer, contamination, and moisture, select a sealer compatible with the surface's use and exposure, clean the surface, repair approved cracks and joints, mask and protect surrounding surfaces, apply the sealer at the specified coverage, protect the sealed surface through cure, and inspect and document the completed work.

This is the last applied-technique chapter in Book 16 before the Chapter 20 capstone. It depends directly on the cured, finished surfaces produced in Chapters 10 through 16, the joint work completed in Chapter 17, and any repairs completed in Chapter 18. Nothing in this chapter alters the drainage slope established in Chapter 7, the joint layout established in Chapter 17, or the finish texture established in Chapters 11 and 14 through 16 — sealing preserves those decisions rather than replacing them.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-109 — Clean the concrete surface using approved methods that do not erode the surface or force water into structures.
  • FW-110 — Select a sealer compatible with the concrete's use, exposure, slip requirement, and existing condition.
  • FW-111 — Prepare joints and cracks for sealing while preserving movement and drainage.
  • FW-112 — Apply concrete sealer at the specified coverage using an approved method.
  • FW-113 — Protect the sealed surface from rain, dust, traffic, and water through the required cure period.
  • FW-114 — Inspect the maintenance condition and document cleaning, repairs, sealer, and maintenance interval.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter depends on a concrete surface that has already been placed, finished, jointed, cured, and — where needed — repaired. Chapter 12 established the original curing period; this chapter cannot begin sealer selection or cleaning until that period, or the longer drying period a sealer manufacturer specifies, has passed. Chapter 17's joint layout and Chapter 18's repair work must be complete and cured before this chapter's crack and joint preparation begins, because sealing over an unrepaired or improperly cured crack locks in a defect rather than correcting it.

The compatibility discipline in this chapter — refusing to apply a sealer that conflicts with existing sealer, moisture condition, or slip requirement — carries directly into the Chapter 20 capstone, where sealing is the final applied step before project closeout.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the concrete has reached the required cure and drying condition before any cleaning or sealer work begins
  • testing for existing sealer, contamination, and moisture
  • selecting a sealer compatible with the surface's use, exposure, slip requirement, and existing condition
  • cleaning the surface without erosion or forced water intrusion
  • repairing approved cracks and joints before sealing
  • masking, applying, and recoating sealer at the specified coverage
  • protecting the sealed surface through the required cure and documenting the completed maintenance work

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • apply sealer before the concrete has reached the cure and drying condition the sealer manufacturer requires
  • apply a film-forming sealer where slip, moisture, or compatibility conditions prohibit it
  • invent a coverage rate, recoat window, cure period, or slip-additive rate not supplied by the manufacturer's instructions or the chapter project specification
  • seal over an unresolved crack, active movement joint, or unrepaired spall without completing Chapter 18 repair work first
  • alter drainage slope, joint layout, or finish texture while performing sealing or maintenance work

Any condition requiring re-evaluation of the underlying repair, drainage, or structural condition must be documented and referred, not resolved by proceeding with sealer application.

Trade Note

A sealer is a maintenance layer applied over a completed, cured, and — where necessary — repaired slab. It does not correct drainage errors, joint failures, or unresolved cracking recorded in earlier chapters. Applying sealer over an unresolved defect to make the surface look finished, rather than referring the defect back through Chapter 17 or Chapter 18, is the single most common cause of the sealer failures diagnosed later in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not apply any sealer until the concrete has reached the cure and drying condition specified by the sealer manufacturer, and until any approved crack or joint repair has itself completed its required cure. A maintenance decision issued before these conditions are confirmed must be treated as conditional only.

Shop Tip

Test a small, inconspicuous area with the selected sealer and application method before committing to the full surface. This confirms compatibility with the existing finish and any previous sealer before the coverage, appearance, and slip characteristics are locked in across the entire project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every sealed surface in this book, from a small utility pad to the Chapter 20 capstone walkway, depends on treating sealing as a maintenance system rather than a cosmetic finish. A sealer applied over the wrong moisture condition, an unrepaired crack, or an incompatible existing coating fails early and can trap moisture against the slab it was meant to protect.

Cleaning, Repair, and Sealing as One System

A sealed surface's performance depends on cleaning, repair, and application working together, not on any one factor alone. A properly selected sealer applied over surface contamination, or over a crack that was never repaired, will not perform as the product data sheet describes. Every finding recorded in this chapter must be evaluated against how it will affect the sealer's bond and long-term performance, not treated as an isolated observation.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Moisture Before Any Sealer Contacts the Surface

Test surface and subsurface moisture with the moisture meter before selecting or applying any sealer. Applying a film-forming sealer over a surface that has not reached the manufacturer's specified dryness traps moisture beneath the coating and is a common cause of the whitening and bubbling failures diagnosed later in this chapter.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Discharge Wash Water or Sealer to Prohibited Locations

Do not discharge concrete washout, cleaning wash water, sealer, or repair-product waste to soil, storm drains, gutters, waterways, or streets. Provide a contained collection area and dispose of waste according to the product's approved method and local requirements.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilation and Footing During Application

Provide ventilation appropriate to the selected sealer's vapor characteristics, and follow the manufacturer's respiratory protection requirements during mixing and application. Freshly applied sealer can be slippery; keep unauthorized foot traffic off the surface until the manufacturer's no-traffic period has passed.

Code Alert — Slip Resistance and Accessibility

Verify that the selected sealer does not reduce slip resistance below what is required for the surface's use, particularly on accessible routes, ramps, and public walking surfaces. A slip additive may be required by the approved finish schedule; do not omit it or substitute an unspecified alternative.

Sealing / Maintenance Qualification

Exterior Concrete Flatwork Sealing and Maintenance Project

Difficulty: Intermediate

Estimated Time: Half day preparation plus manufacturer cure and recoat window

Estimated Cost: Varies with sealer type and surface area

Finished Work: One cleaned, repaired, sealed, and documented exterior concrete surface

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one cured exterior concrete-flatwork surface, inspected, cleaned, repaired where approved, sealed with a compatible product at the specified coverage, protected through cure, and documented with a completed maintenance log.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review concrete age, cure, moisture, previous sealer, finish, exposure, slip requirement, weather, and selected sealer. Confirm every reference document and prior chapter record applies to the exact surface being sealed.
  2. Confirm the concrete has reached the required cure and drying condition. Compare the current age and moisture reading against the sealer manufacturer's minimum requirement.
  3. Test for existing sealer, contamination, and moisture. Use the manufacturer's recommended test method, such as a water-droplet test or moisture meter reading, at multiple locations.
  4. Select penetrating, film-forming, joint, stain-resistant, or other approved sealer based on use and compatibility. Match the product to the surface's use, exposure, existing sealer condition, and slip requirement.
  5. Do not apply a film-forming sealer where slip, moisture, or compatibility conditions prohibit it. Select an alternative product or method where these conditions exist.
  6. Protect buildings, vegetation, drains, vehicles, and adjacent surfaces. Cover or barricade every surface that must not receive cleaner, repair material, or sealer.
  7. Sweep with HEPA collection or vacuum loose debris. Remove all loose material before washing.
  8. Wash using the approved cleaner and pressure. Follow the cleaner manufacturer's dilution, dwell time, and pressure-washer settings.
  9. Avoid eroding the surface or forcing water into structures. Direct wash water away from foundations, doors, and drains.
  10. Remove oil, efflorescence, mildew residue, curing compound, or failed sealer using approved methods. Use only the cleaner or stripper identified for the specific contaminant.
  11. Allow the surface to dry. Confirm dryness with the moisture meter before proceeding.
  12. Repair approved cracks and joints before sealing. Complete any required Chapter 18 repair work and allow it to reach its own specified cure before continuing.
  13. Preserve movement and drainage. Confirm repairs and cleaning have not filled or blocked movement joints or altered the drainage slope.
  14. Mask edges and no-coat areas. Apply masking tape and film to every surface identified in Step 6 immediately before application.
  15. Mix or stir sealer as required. Follow the manufacturer's mixing method and time exactly.
  16. Apply at the specified coverage using sprayer, roller, squeegee, or brush. Use the application method and coverage rate stated on the product data sheet.
  17. Avoid puddles, lap marks, overspray, and excess film. Work in a consistent pattern and remove excess material promptly.
  18. Apply additional coats only within the recoat window. Confirm the elapsed time against the manufacturer's stated window before applying a second coat.
  19. Broadcast slip additive only where specified. Apply the additive at the rate and timing stated in the finish schedule or manufacturer instructions.
  20. Protect from rain, dust, traffic, and water through cure. Barricade the surface and monitor weather through the full cure period.
  21. Inspect uniformity, adhesion, whitening, bubbles, puddles, slip, and drainage. Walk the full surface and record findings on the maintenance log.
  22. Document cleaning, repairs, sealer, weather, coverage, cure, and maintenance interval. Complete the maintenance log and file it with the project's other closeout records.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Maintenance log: the completed record of every finding in Steps 1–22, including cleaning, repairs, sealer, weather, coverage, cure, and the next required maintenance interval.
  • Moisture and contamination test record: readings and observations taken before sealer selection.
  • Product data sheets: the manufacturer instructions for the cleaner, repair materials, and sealer used, retained for the next maintenance cycle.
  • Photographs: before-cleaning, after-cleaning, repaired areas, and the completed sealed surface, referenced in the maintenance log.

Common Sealing and Maintenance Inspection

Inspect every sealing project using the same checks regardless of sealer type or surface use.

  • Cure check: confirm the concrete and any repair material has reached the required cure and drying condition before cleaning or sealing begins.
  • Testing check: confirm existing sealer, contamination, and moisture were tested and recorded before product selection.
  • Selection check: confirm the selected sealer is compatible with the surface's use, exposure, slip requirement, and existing condition.
  • Cleaning check: confirm the surface was cleaned using approved methods without erosion or forced water intrusion.
  • Repair check: confirm approved cracks and joints were repaired and cured, and that movement and drainage were preserved.
  • Application check: confirm coverage rate, recoat window, and slip-additive requirements were followed exactly.
  • Protection and documentation check: confirm the surface was protected through cure and the maintenance log is complete.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Whitening or Bubbling Beneath the Sealer

Probable cause: sealer was applied before the concrete reached the manufacturer's specified dryness, trapping moisture beneath the film.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area for a milky or bubbled film and check the moisture-test record against the sealer manufacturer's minimum dryness requirement.
Correction: remove the affected sealer using the manufacturer's approved stripping method, allow the surface to dry to the required condition, and reapply.
Reinspection: retest moisture and reinspect the reapplied area before closing out the maintenance log.

Sealer Applied Over an Unrepaired Crack

Probable cause: a crack or spall identified during inspection was sealed over rather than referred through the Chapter 18 repair sequence.
Confirmation: compare the sealed surface against the pre-cleaning photographs and inspection notes for any documented crack or spall.
Correction: remove sealer from the affected area, complete the required Chapter 18 repair, allow the repair to cure, and reseal only after the repair cure is confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the crack and joint preparation check before finalizing the maintenance log.

Puddles, Lap Marks, or Excess Film

Probable cause: sealer was applied faster than it could be worked out, or a second coat was applied before the first had leveled.
Confirmation: inspect the surface under raking light for uneven sheen, ridges, or pooled areas.
Correction: remove excess material according to the manufacturer's instructions, allow the surface to level or dry, and reapply using a slower, more controlled pass.
Reinspection: repeat the application check across the full surface before proceeding to cure protection.

Reduced Slip Resistance After Sealing

Probable cause: a film-forming sealer was applied to a surface with a slip requirement without the specified slip additive, or an incompatible product was substituted.
Confirmation: compare the applied product and additive against the approved finish schedule and slip requirement.
Correction: broadcast the specified slip additive according to the manufacturer's rate and timing, or remove and replace the sealer with an approved product where the additive cannot correct the condition.
Reinspection: retest slip resistance using the method specified for the surface's use before final acceptance.

Movement Joint Filled or Blocked During Sealing

Probable cause: cleaning residue, repair material, or sealer was allowed to fill or bridge an isolation, expansion, or control joint.
Confirmation: inspect every joint identified in the Chapter 17 joint record for continuity and clear movement capacity.
Correction: remove the material bridging the joint and restore the specified joint width and depth before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-preservation check before documenting the maintenance log as complete.

Sealer Traffic Restriction Ignored

Probable cause: the surface was opened to pedestrian, pet, or vehicle traffic before the manufacturer's specified no-traffic period had elapsed.
Confirmation: compare the recorded application time against the elapsed time before traffic was observed on the surface.
Correction: re-barricade the surface for any remaining portion of the required period and inspect for footprints, tire marks, or disturbed film.
Reinspection: repeat the cure-protection check and repair any disturbed area before final documentation.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, filed maintenance log, not a verbal impression of the sealed surface.

  • Remove masking tape and film without pulling fresh sealer from edges
  • Dispose of cleaner, repair-material, and sealer waste according to the manufacturer's approved method and the contained washout requirements established in Chapter 2
  • Return unused sealer, cleaner, and repair materials to sealed, labeled, and properly stored containers
  • Remove barricades and signage only after the required no-traffic period has fully elapsed
  • File the completed maintenance log, moisture and contamination test record, product data sheets, and photographs together

Field Terms

Manufacturer Cure and Drying Condition
The age, moisture, and surface condition the sealer manufacturer requires before application.
Recoat Window
The manufacturer-specified time range within which an additional coat may be applied.
No-Coat Area
Any surface, joint, or edge the sealer must not contact, identified before masking begins.
Maintenance Log
The document recording cleaning, repairs, sealer selection, application, and the next required maintenance interval.
Slip Requirement
The approved slip-resistance condition for the surface's use, which may prohibit certain film-forming sealers.
Compatibility Test
A small test-area application confirming the selected sealer bonds properly with the existing surface and any prior sealer.
Film-Forming Sealer
A sealer that cures as a surface coating rather than penetrating into the concrete.
Penetrating Sealer
A sealer that absorbs into the concrete without forming a surface film.
Slip Additive
A material broadcast into or onto a sealer coat to restore or maintain slip resistance.
Efflorescence
A white, powdery mineral deposit on the concrete surface that must be removed using an approved method before sealing.

Skill Check

  2. Why must existing sealer, contamination, and moisture be tested before a new sealer is selected?
  3. Under what conditions must a film-forming sealer not be applied, and what alternative should be selected instead?
  4. Why must approved crack and joint repairs be completed and cured before sealing rather than sealed over directly?
  5. What is the difference between a penetrating sealer and a film-forming sealer, and why does that difference affect surface selection?
  6. Why must a compatibility test be performed on a small area before the full surface is sealed?
  7. What must the learner do if the recoat window has already passed before a second coat is applied?
  8. Why must a slip additive be broadcast only where specified, and what risk does omitting it create on an accessible route?
  9. What must the learner document if drainage or a movement joint is found to be blocked after cleaning or sealing?
  10. Why does the maintenance log record a next maintenance interval rather than treating the sealed surface as permanently complete?

Performance Check

With the maintenance log fully recorded, review it against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm the concrete reached the required cure and drying condition, existing sealer and contamination were tested before selection, the selected sealer is compatible with the surface's use and slip requirement, the surface was cleaned without erosion or forced water intrusion, required repairs were completed and cured before sealing, the sealer was applied at the specified coverage without defects, and the surface was protected through the full cure period. Complete the maintenance log and correct any open item before issuing a final maintenance decision. If any condition requires re-evaluation of drainage, joints, or an underlying repair, refer it rather than resolving it by proceeding with sealer application, and record the referral before beginning Chapter 20.

Project Closeout

You have completed the nineteenth chapter in the concrete flatwork sequence: sealing and maintaining exterior concrete flatwork. This chapter required you to clean the concrete surface using approved methods (FW-109), select a sealer compatible with the surface's use and condition (FW-110), prepare joints and cracks while preserving movement and drainage (FW-111), apply sealer at the specified coverage (FW-112), protect the sealed surface through the required cure period (FW-113), and inspect and document the completed maintenance work (FW-114).

You now have a cleaned, repaired, sealed, and documented exterior concrete surface with preserved drainage, movement, and slip characteristics. That completed maintenance record carries directly into the next chapter: the Chapter 20 capstone project, which integrates layout, excavation, base, forms, reinforcement, placement, finishing, curing, jointing, sealing, and final inspection into one complete exterior walkway or patio section.

File your completed maintenance log, moisture and contamination test record, and product data sheets together. You will reference these exact findings as the sealing step of the Chapter 20 capstone project.

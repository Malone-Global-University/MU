Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare a controlled work area for one concrete-flatwork project using exactly the conditions documented in the Chapter 1 site-readiness plan. The finished work product is a protected, organized site: established zones, verified delivery and worker routes, staged tools and materials, a contained washout area, weather protection, and a completed work-area readiness checklist.

You will confirm utility-locate markings remain valid, establish work zones, verify delivery routes, protect adjacent surfaces, stage tools and materials, set up emergency and washout provisions, and complete a readiness inspection before any excavation or layout work begins.

This is the second chapter in Book 16. It depends entirely on Chapter 1: the utility-locate confirmation, boundary limits, drainage findings, soil evaluation, and hazard list recorded there control every decision made here. Chapter 3 then lays out the slab within the zones this chapter establishes, and Chapter 4 excavates using the access and protection this chapter sets up. Nothing in this chapter invents a route, clearance, or protection measure that the Chapter 1 plan or approved site documents do not already support.

  • FW-008 — Stage tools and materials for the planned sequence of work.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 produced a documented readiness decision; this chapter turns that decision into a physically controlled site. Chapter 3 cannot safely lay out a squared slab until this chapter has established the excavation zone and confirmed utility markings remain visible. Chapter 4 cannot excavate until this chapter has protected adjacent surfaces and established stable equipment routes. Chapter 9 through Chapter 12 depend on the washout area, staging plan, and weather protection this chapter sets up remaining in place and undisturbed.

The stop-work discipline carried from Chapter 1 continues here: this chapter does not proceed past inadequate access, missing washout containment, unresolved weather protection, or insufficient crew size for the planned work.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the Chapter 1 readiness plan, pour sequence, delivery method, equipment, crew assignments, and weather plan
  • confirming utility-locate markings remain visible and valid
  • establishing excavation, form, mixing, placement, finishing, curing, and restricted zones
  • establishing and verifying concrete-delivery and worker-movement routes
  • protecting adjacent buildings, paving, landscaping, and property
  • staging tools, materials, and PPE
  • establishing a contained washout area and weather protection
  • completing a documented work-area readiness inspection

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • disturb, remove, or rely on expired utility-locate markings without reconfirming them
  • begin excavation, layout, or material staging in an area that has not been confirmed against the Chapter 1 readiness plan
  • discharge concrete washout, slurry, or wash water to soil, drains, gutters, waterways, or streets
  • invent a route width, clearance, staging dimension, or protection method that is not supplied by the approved site plan, manufacturer instructions, or documented site conditions
  • proceed with inadequate access, crew size, washout containment, or weather protection for the planned work

Any condition that requires additional review, permitting, or professional coordination must be documented and referred, not resolved by the learner.

Trade Note

Every zone, route, and protection measure established in this chapter carries forward, undisturbed, into every later chapter. Moving a barricade, filling in a washout pit, or reopening a protected route without documenting the change is the single most common source of the delivery, protection, and washout failures diagnosed later in this book.

Setup Standard

Do not begin staging materials, marking zones, or establishing routes until the Chapter 1 utility-locate confirmation, boundary verification, and hazard list have been reviewed and found current. A work area established over an unconfirmed or expired utility locate must be treated as not ready.

Shop Tip

Walk the planned delivery route with the same vehicle or equipment type that will actually be used, not just on paper. A route that looks wide enough on a site plan can still fail on turning radius, overhead clearance, or soil support once a loaded truck or pump actually attempts it.

Zones, Routes, and Protection as One System

A work area with a clear delivery route but no washout containment, or well-staged materials but no protection for adjacent siding, is not yet a ready site. Every zone and route established in this chapter must be evaluated against how it will support every later chapter, not treated as an isolated setup task.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Utility Markings Before Any Ground Contact

Confirm utility-locate markings remain visible and within their valid period before driving any stake, barricade post, or marking pin. If any marking is disturbed, faded, or expired, request reconfirmation before proceeding with zone layout.

Measurement Cue — Verify Routes With Actual Equipment Dimensions

Confirm route width, overhead clearance, turning radius, and soil support using the actual dimensions of the equipment planned for delivery and placement, not general assumptions. Record the verified measurements on the work-area checklist.

Field Safety Cue — Moving Equipment and Worker Routes

Keep worker movement routes separated from equipment and delivery routes wherever practical. Establish spotter positions and communication signals before trucks, pumps, or compactors begin moving through the site.

Code Alert — Washout, Discharge, and Traffic-Control Requirements

Verify local requirements for washout containment, wash-water discharge, and public traffic or pedestrian control before setup. Work adjacent to a public street or right-of-way may require permitted signage, cones, or a traffic-control provider not required for interior-lot projects.

Work-Area Setup Qualification

Concrete Flatwork Work-Area Setup

Difficulty: Beginner

Estimated Time: 2–3 hours

Estimated Cost: Low

Finished Work: One protected, organized work area with verified access, staged materials, contained washout, and completed readiness checklist

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one controlled concrete-flatwork work area, established from the Chapter 1 readiness plan and documented as complete zones, routes, staging, protection, washout, weather controls, and emergency provisions, with a signed readiness checklist.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review the approved site plan, pour sequence, delivery method, equipment, crew assignments, and weather plan. Confirm every item matches the Chapter 1 readiness plan.
  2. Confirm utility-locate markings remain visible and valid. Request reconfirmation if any marking is disturbed, faded, or expired.
  3. Establish excavation, form, mixing, placement, finishing, curing, and restricted zones. Mark each zone clearly and separately.
  4. Establish concrete truck, pump, chute, buggy, and material-delivery routes. Base routes on the access identified in Chapter 1.
  5. Verify route width, overhead clearance, soil support, slope, turning, and backup control. Use the actual dimensions of the planned delivery equipment.
  6. Protect buildings, siding, doors, paving, landscaping, vehicles, and neighboring property. Install protection before any staging or equipment movement begins.
  7. Install barricades, cones, signage, and pedestrian or traffic controls. Place these before opening any zone to work.
  8. Stage form lumber, stakes, reinforcement, aggregate, concrete materials, curing supplies, joint materials, and protection. Position staged materials clear of delivery and worker routes.
  9. Inspect excavators, compactors, mixers, saws, vibrators, screeds, floats, edgers, jointers, brooms, hoses, cords, and hand tools. Confirm each tool is in safe working condition before staging.
  10. Establish potable water and emergency eye- and skin-washing access. Confirm access is functional and reachable from every active work zone.
  11. Stage waterproof gloves, boots, long sleeves, eye protection, hearing protection, respiratory protection, and high-visibility gear. Stage PPE for every task planned in later chapters.
  12. Establish a contained concrete washout area. Locate it away from drains, soil, and waterways.
  13. Protect storm drains, gutters, soil, and waterways from wash water and slurry. Confirm containment before any mixing or cleanup activity is scheduled.
  14. Establish dust-control and wet-cutting procedures. Prepare water sources and HEPA extraction access needed for later cutting or grinding tasks.
  15. Establish shade, windbreak, evaporation-control, cold-weather, rain, or hot-weather protection as required. Base the plan on the weather exposure recorded in Chapter 1.
  16. Establish lighting and backup equipment where the work could extend. Confirm availability before work that may run past daylight hours.
  17. Establish communication signals for delivery drivers, pump operators, and crew. Confirm every crew member understands the signals before delivery begins.
  18. Mark no-entry areas around moving equipment and placement operations. Keep these areas visible and enforced throughout active work.
  19. Verify first-aid, burn-response, spill-response, and emergency procedures. Confirm supplies and procedures are staged and known to the crew.
  20. Complete the work-area readiness checklist. Record every item confirmed in Steps 1–19.
  21. Stop work if access, washout, weather protection, crew size, delivery control, or emergency response is inadequate. Document the specific deficiency and the required correction.

Common Work-Area Setup Inspection

Inspect every work-area setup using the same checks regardless of project size.

  • Chapter 1 check: confirm the readiness plan and utility-locate markings remain current before any setup activity.
  • Zone check: confirm every required zone is established, marked, and separated.
  • Route check: confirm delivery and worker routes are verified against actual equipment dimensions.
  • Protection check: confirm adjacent buildings, paving, landscaping, and property are protected before staging begins.
  • Washout check: confirm the washout area is contained and located away from drains, soil, and waterways.
  • Weather and emergency check: confirm weather protection and emergency provisions match the recorded site conditions.
  • Readiness check: confirm the checklist is fully documented before excavation or layout work begins.

Complete the Work-Area Readiness Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 3.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Delivery Route Verified on Paper Only

Probable cause: route width and clearance were confirmed against the site plan but never measured against the actual truck, pump, or equipment dimensions planned for delivery.
Confirmation: re-measure the route using the actual equipment's width, height, and turning radius.
Correction: adjust the route, remove obstructions, or select alternate equipment; document the change on the work-area checklist.
Reinspection: repeat the route check before delivery day.

Utility Markings Disturbed Before Zone Layout

Probable cause: barricades, stakes, or staged materials were placed without first confirming utility-locate markings were still visible and valid.
Confirmation: compare current marking visibility and dates against the Chapter 1 utility-locate record.
Correction: request reconfirmation of the locate before continuing zone layout; treat the work area as not ready until confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the utility check once the locate is reconfirmed.

Washout Area Located Near a Drain or Waterway

Probable cause: the washout area was positioned for worker convenience without checking proximity to storm drains, gutters, or natural drainage paths.
Confirmation: trace the surrounding grade and drainage path from the proposed washout location.
Correction: relocate the washout area to a contained location away from any discharge point, or add containment sufficient to prevent runoff.
Reinspection: repeat the washout check before any mixing or cleanup activity is scheduled.

Zones Established Without Clear Separation

Probable cause: excavation, mixing, and placement areas overlapped or were not distinctly marked, creating confusion about equipment paths and restricted areas.
Confirmation: walk the site and compare marked boundaries against the required zone list.
Correction: re-mark zone boundaries with clear, separated barricades or flagging.
Reinspection: repeat the zone check before staging materials or beginning equipment movement.

Protection Installed After Staging or Equipment Movement Began

Probable cause: materials were staged or equipment began moving before protective sheeting or padding was installed against adjacent surfaces.
Confirmation: inspect adjacent siding, paving, and landscaping for marks, scuffs, or damage.
Correction: halt staging and equipment movement, install the required protection, and document any damage that occurred.
Reinspection: repeat the protection check before resuming staging or movement.

Readiness Checklist Completed With Open Deficiencies

Probable cause: the work-area readiness checklist was signed off while access, washout, weather protection, or emergency provisions remained incomplete.
Confirmation: review the checklist against the actual site condition, item by item.
Correction: correct every open item before proceeding, and re-sign the checklist only once all items are confirmed complete.
Reinspection: repeat the full readiness check before beginning Chapter 3.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, filed work-area readiness checklist, not a verbal confirmation that the site "looks ready."

  • Confirm all barricades, cones, and signage remain in place and undisturbed for Chapter 3 and Chapter 4
  • Confirm staged materials remain organized and clear of delivery and worker routes
  • Confirm washout containment remains intact and unblocked
  • Confirm protection installed on adjacent surfaces remains in place
  • File the completed work-area readiness checklist, route verification record, and washout and protection record together with the Chapter 1 documents
  • Store photographs with clear labels referencing the checklist entries they support

Field Terms

Work Zone
A defined area assigned to a single function — excavation, forms, mixing, placement, finishing, curing, or restricted access.
Delivery Route
The verified path a concrete truck, pump, chute, buggy, or wheelbarrow will follow from the street or staging area to the placement zone.
Washout Area
A contained location where concrete equipment is cleaned and wash water and slurry are captured rather than discharged.
Restricted Zone
An area marked no-entry during equipment operation, placement, or other active work.
Readiness Checklist
The documented confirmation that zones, routes, staging, protection, washout, and emergency provisions are complete.
Staging
The organized placement of tools, materials, and PPE in a location clear of delivery and worker routes, ready for the planned sequence of work.
Backup Control
The verified method for safely reversing delivery vehicles or equipment, including spotters and communication signals.
No-Entry Area
A marked area kept clear of workers and bystanders during active equipment operation or concrete placement.
Spill Response
The staged procedure and materials for responding to a fuel, oil, or chemical spill during equipment operation.
Weather Protection Plan
The staged shade, windbreak, insulation, or covering matched to the weather exposure recorded during site inspection.

Skill Check

  1. Why must utility-locate markings be reconfirmed before any zone marking or staking, even though they were already confirmed in Chapter 1?
  2. Why must delivery routes be verified using the actual dimensions of the planned equipment rather than the site plan alone?
  3. Why must protection be installed on adjacent surfaces before staging or equipment movement begins, rather than after?
  4. What conditions make a proposed washout location unacceptable, even if it is convenient for the crew?
  5. Why must excavation, mixing, and placement zones be clearly separated rather than left as one general work area?
  6. What is the relationship between the weather exposure recorded in Chapter 1 and the weather protection plan established in this chapter?
  7. Under what conditions must the learner stop work rather than proceed with an incomplete work-area readiness checklist?
  8. Why must communication signals for delivery drivers, pump operators, and crew be confirmed before delivery begins, not during it?
  9. What must the learner do if a route, zone, or protection measure fails inspection after it has already been marked or installed?
  10. Why does the work-area readiness checklist need to remain filed alongside the Chapter 1 documents rather than replacing them?

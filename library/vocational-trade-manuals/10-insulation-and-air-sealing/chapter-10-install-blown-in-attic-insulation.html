Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install loose-fill attic insulation using calibrated blower equipment, to the depth, density, coverage, and product-quantity requirements stated in the approved insulation schedule. The finished work product is an attic floor evenly covered with loose-fill insulation at the target settled depth, with ventilation channels, heat clearances, and service access preserved and documented.

You will confirm that the attic is dry and that prerequisite air-sealing, bypass-blocking, baffle, and clearance work from earlier chapters is complete; establish depth markers and dams; calculate and track the required product quantity; operate the blower machine as a coordinated two-person crew; distribute material evenly while protecting ventilation and access; and verify final depth, coverage, and clearances before closing the attic.

This project depends directly on Chapters 1 through 6. Attic bypasses must already be sealed (Chapter 5) and ventilation baffles must already be installed (Chapter 6) before loose-fill material is blown, because loose-fill insulation cannot be un-poured from a sealed chase or a blocked soffit channel without disturbing the finished floor.

Where This Skill Fits

This is the point in the book where the attic thermal boundary is completed. Chapters 1 through 6 established the inspection, air-sealing, bypass-blocking, and ventilation-protection prerequisites this project depends on. Chapter 11 builds on this project when additional insulation is added over an existing layer, and Chapter 19 verifies this installation as part of the final thermal-envelope quality inspection.

The depth-marker and product-quantity-tracking method taught here is the same method used throughout the remainder of the book whenever loose-fill material is installed or added, including the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming that prerequisite air-sealing, bypass, baffle, and clearance work is complete and inspected
  • establishing attic walk paths, depth markers, and dams before blowing insulation
  • calculating minimum product quantity from the approved coverage chart
  • operating blower equipment as a two-person crew with established communication
  • distributing loose-fill insulation evenly across the attic floor to the target depth
  • protecting soffit vents, baffles, recessed lights, chimneys, flues, and equipment during installation
  • verifying final depth, product quantity, and clearances, and documenting the completed installation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin blowing insulation before the Chapter 1 inspection, Chapter 5 bypass sealing, and Chapter 6 baffle installation are complete and verified for the same attic area
  • bury junction boxes, dampers, valves, or other components that must remain accessible
  • block soffit-to-ridge ventilation paths or crush installed baffles
  • install insulation over vermiculite, suspected asbestos, active roof leaks, or other stop-work conditions
  • claim a specific R-value beyond what the identified product and installed depth support

Any condition meeting a stop-work criterion in Step 6 must halt blowing in that area and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before work continues.

Trade Note

Loose-fill insulation is measured two ways that must both be satisfied: settled depth and installed bag count per the coverage chart. A deep-looking pile that used too few bags is under-density and will settle thinner than the target depth. Track both numbers throughout the job, not just one.

Setup Standard

Do not begin blowing insulation until depth markers, dams, and ventilation baffle protection are installed and confirmed by both crew members. Do not operate the blower machine without an established communication method between the machine operator and the hose installer.

Shop Tip

Work from the farthest attic area back toward the access opening. This keeps your only exit path clear of freshly blown material and prevents you from having to walk back across finished, uncompacted insulation.

Common Field Fundamentals

Loose-fill installation is a distribution problem, not a filling problem. The goal is a uniform settled depth across the entire attic floor, not simply enough material somewhere in the attic to meet the total bag count. Piles and bare spots both fail the acceptance standard even if the average depth is correct.

Depth, Density, and Product Quantity Are Linked

The coverage chart states the minimum number of bags required to achieve the rated density at the target settled depth over a given attic area. Installing the target depth using fewer bags than the chart requires produces under-density insulation that will perform below its rated value and may settle below the target depth over time. Track installed bag count against the calculated minimum throughout the job, not only at the end.

Measurement Cue — Grid Spacing for Depth Markers

Install depth markers at the grid spacing stated in the approved insulation schedule. Mark each card or rod clearly at the target settled depth so it remains visible once the surrounding area is filled to grade.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Roof Leaks, Damaged Framing, or Hazardous Materials

Stop the installation in any area with active roof leakage, damaged or unsafe framing, exposed or damaged wiring, or suspected hazardous materials such as vermiculite or asbestos-containing insulation. Do not blow loose-fill material over an unresolved moisture source or a hazard that has not been cleared by a qualified professional.

Field Safety Cue — Attic Footing and Fall-Through Protection

Maintain walk boards or joist-supported footing throughout the operation. Once loose-fill material is blown, ceiling joists and the drywall between them are no longer visible; walk only on established walk paths that were confirmed and marked before installation began.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Blower Machine Settings

Set machine feed rate, agitation, and air pressure according to the manufacturer's instructions for the specific product being installed. Do not increase feed rate beyond the manufacturer's setting to finish faster; doing so produces inconsistent density and inaccurate coverage.

D. Preparation — Schedule Review and Attic Readiness

  1. Review the insulation schedule, product coverage chart, bag count, target settled depth, density, attic area, access, and manufacturer instructions. Confirm the product identified in the schedule matches the material staged for installation before opening any bags.
  2. Confirm that the attic is dry and that roof leaks, air leaks, bypasses, ventilation baffles, electrical hazards, and heat clearances have been resolved. Walk the attic and visually confirm the Chapter 5 bypass seals and Chapter 6 baffles are intact for the area to be insulated.
  3. Confirm that required rough and air-sealing inspections are complete. Review inspection records for this attic area before proceeding; do not blow insulation over an area awaiting a required inspection.

E. Access, Markers, and Dams

  1. Establish attic walk paths, lighting, communication, and fall-through protection. Confirm walk boards are positioned across joists along the planned travel route before any material is staged.
  2. Install depth markers at the required grid spacing and mark the target depth. Use a tape measure to place markers at the schedule's specified spacing and mark each one clearly at the target settled depth.
  3. Build durable dams around attic access, storage paths, mechanical platforms, recessed-light clearances, chimneys, flues, and equipment. Build each dam to the full target depth before blowing begins so it will not be overtopped once material is installed.
  4. Protect soffit vents and baffles from blockage. Visually confirm each Chapter 6 baffle is in place and undamaged before the area behind it is filled.
  5. Mark junction boxes, valves, dampers, and service points that must remain accessible. Flag each with a visible marker taller than the target insulation depth.

F. Quantity Calculation and Equipment Setup

  1. Calculate the minimum product quantity from the approved area and coverage chart. Multiply the attic square footage by the chart's bags-per-square-foot value for the target depth and record the result on the worksheet.
  2. Record starting bag count and product lot information. Log the lot number before staging bags so all installed material can be traced.
  3. Inspect blower machine, hose, power supply, guards, and controls. Confirm the hopper guard is in place and the hose couplings are secure before starting the machine.
  4. Assign one trained operator at the machine and one installer at the hose. Confirm both crew members understand the planned distribution route before starting.
  5. Establish communication and emergency-stop signals. Test the radio or intercom connection and agree on a stop signal before the machine is started.

G. Distribution

  1. Feed material into the machine at the controlled rate. Follow the manufacturer's stated feed rate for the product; do not overfeed the hopper.
  2. Begin at the farthest attic area and work back toward the access. This keeps the installer's exit path clear of freshly blown, uncompacted material.
  3. Hold the hose at the specified angle and distance to distribute material without creating piles or bare areas. Sweep the hose in overlapping passes rather than holding it stationary over one spot.
  4. Maintain consistent coverage around framing, braces, ducts, and irregular spaces. Direct material into corners and around obstructions deliberately rather than relying on overspray to reach them.
  5. Do not bury required access points or damage duct insulation. Confirm marked access points remain visible above the fill line as work proceeds.
  6. Check depth against markers during installation. Stop periodically and read several markers before continuing to the next section.
  7. Track actual bags installed. Log each bag as it is fed into the machine so the running total can be compared to the calculated minimum.
  8. Correct low areas without excessively overfilling adjacent areas. Return to any area reading below target depth and add material specifically to that area.

H. Verification and Closeout

  1. Inspect ventilation channels, heat clearances, access dams, equipment platforms, and walk paths. Confirm baffles are still open, dams are intact, and all flagged access points remain visible.
  2. Verify final depth and product quantity. Confirm the installed bag count meets or exceeds the calculated minimum and that representative depth readings meet the target across the attic.
  3. Record representative depth readings and coverage photographs. Photograph markers at several locations along with overall coverage views.
  4. Clean equipment and occupied areas using controlled dust procedures. Vacuum tracked material from walk paths and the attic access before leaving the site.
  5. Complete the attic-insulation inspection before closing access. Finalize the installation worksheet with depth readings, bag count, product lot, and photographs before securing the attic hatch.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed installation using the same checks regardless of which section of the attic produced the reading.

  • Depth check: confirm representative readings across the attic meet the target settled depth.
  • Quantity check: confirm installed bag count meets or exceeds the calculated minimum from the coverage chart.
  • Uniformity check: confirm no bare spots or excessive piling.
  • Ventilation check: confirm soffit vents and baffle channels remain open.
  • Clearance check: confirm heat sources, electrical equipment, and access points are not buried.
  • Documentation check: confirm the worksheet records depth readings, bag count, product lot, and photographs.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Depth Readings Are Below Target in Several Areas

Probable cause: the hose was moved too quickly across those sections, or the machine feed rate was too low to deliver adequate material before the installer moved on.
Confirmation: compare depth readings at the low areas against the target depth markers and check the running bag count against the calculated minimum.
Correction: return to the low areas and add material specifically to those sections until markers read at or above target depth.
Reinspection: repeat the depth check across the corrected areas and confirm the running bag count is proceeding toward the calculated minimum.

Installed Bag Count Is Below the Calculated Minimum at Apparent Target Depth

Probable cause: the material was distributed too loosely, producing the correct visual depth at below-rated density; this will settle thinner over time.
Confirmation: compare the running bag count to the calculated minimum from the coverage chart at the point where depth markers first read target depth.
Correction: continue installing material across the full attic area until the calculated minimum bag count is reached, even though markers may already read at target depth.
Reinspection: repeat the quantity check and confirm final bag count meets or exceeds the coverage chart minimum.

A Soffit Vent or Baffle Channel Is Found Blocked After Installation

Probable cause: the dam at that eave was not built to full target depth, or the hose was directed into the baffle channel instead of the field of the attic floor.
Confirmation: inspect the baffle from the soffit side, if accessible, and from the attic side to confirm the blockage location and extent.
Correction: carefully remove the loose-fill material blocking the channel without damaging the baffle, rebuild the dam to full depth, and do not re-blow material into that section until the dam is corrected.
Reinspection: repeat the ventilation check on the corrected bay and confirm continuous airflow from the soffit intake through the baffle.

A Flagged Junction Box or Access Point Is Buried

Probable cause: the marker used to flag the component was shorter than the target insulation depth and was covered during distribution.
Confirmation: locate the component using the pre-installation photographs and worksheet location notes, then carefully probe and clear material from above it.
Correction: clear insulation from the component to restore the required access, and install a taller marker before any further material is added nearby.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check to confirm the component remains visible and accessible.

Communication Between the Machine Operator and Hose Installer Fails During the Job

Probable cause: the radio or intercom lost power, connection, or range between the machine location and the attic.
Confirmation: attempt to reestablish contact using the established stop signal or a backup method.
Correction: stop the machine immediately if contact cannot be reestablished, and do not resume blowing until communication is restored.
Reinspection: test the communication method before resuming installation.

Cleanup

This project ends with a documented, evenly insulated attic floor and a clean work area, not loose material tracked through the structure.

  • Vacuum or sweep tracked insulation material from walk paths, stairs, and the attic access opening
  • Clean and store the blower machine and hose according to the manufacturer's instructions
  • Confirm no tools, depth markers meant for removal, or debris remain in the attic that could interfere with future service access
  • Match depth readings, bag count, and product lot information to the completed installation worksheet
  • Close and secure attic access after the installation inspection is complete

Field Terms

Target Settled Depth
The installed loose-fill depth specified in the approved insulation schedule, stated as a settled measurement.
Coverage Chart
The manufacturer table relating bag count, attic area, and settled depth to rated density.
Depth Marker
A visible card or rod installed at the schedule's grid spacing and marked at the target settled depth.
Insulation Dam
A durable barrier keeping loose-fill material out of the soffit, access openings, and restricted clearance zones.
Machine Operator
The crew member who feeds material into the blower machine and controls machine settings.
Hose Installer
The crew member who directs the hose in the attic and distributes material.
Under-Density Installation
Material installed at the correct visual depth using fewer than the required bag count, which performs below rated value and settles below target depth over time.
Wind Washing
Displacement or degradation of loose-fill insulation performance caused by outside air moving through or across it, controlled by baffles and dams.

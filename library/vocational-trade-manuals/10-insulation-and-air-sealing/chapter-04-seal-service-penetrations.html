Job Overview

This project qualifies you to air-seal the plumbing, electrical, and mechanical penetrations that cross a building's air-control boundary. Every pipe, cable, conduit, and duct that passes through a top plate, rim joist, wall, floor, or ceiling creates a potential air-leakage path, and each one requires a sealing method matched to its material, movement, temperature, and fire-protection requirement. The finished work product is a set of continuously sealed penetrations, each documented with its location, service type, and sealing method.

You will work from the Chapter 1 inspection findings and the project's penetration schedule to identify every included penetration, confirm that rough plumbing, electrical, and HVAC inspections are complete, select the correct sealant, foam, gasket, collar, or listed firestop system for each service, prepare the surrounding surfaces, install the seal without bonding serviceable components or damaging cable insulation, and inspect the finished work for continuity, adhesion, movement allowance, and fire designation.

This project builds directly on the framing-joint air sealing completed in Chapter 3 and prepares the enclosure for the attic bypass and chase work in Chapter 5. A building can have every framing joint sealed and still leak significant air through a single unsealed pipe or wire penetration, so this project treats each penetration as its own small air-sealing problem with its own required material and method.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-019 — Identify plumbing, electrical, and mechanical penetrations through the air-control boundary.
  • IN-020 — Select the sealant, foam, gasket, collar, or firestop system appropriate to each penetration.
  • IN-021 — Seal pipe penetrations without bonding valves, unions, or serviceable joints.
  • IN-022 — Seal wire and conduit penetrations without damaging cable insulation or burying junction boxes.
  • IN-023 — Seal duct and boot penetrations to the air barrier without crushing insulation or blocking dampers.
  • IN-024 — Inspect completed penetration seals for continuity, adhesion, movement allowance, and fire designation.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 3 sealed the framing joints of the air-control boundary. This chapter completes the same boundary at every point where a plumbing, electrical, or mechanical service passes through it. Chapter 5 then closes the larger attic bypasses and chases, several of which contain the same penetrations sealed here at a smaller scale. Chapter 13 revisits rim-joist penetrations specifically, and Chapter 16 revisits duct-adjacent air sealing in unconditioned spaces in more depth. This chapter is the general method that those later, more specialized chapters extend.

The penetration-sealing method taught here — identify, select compatible material, prepare, seal, allow movement, cure, and inspect — is the method referenced throughout the book any time a service crosses an air or thermal boundary, including the Chapter 19 final inspection and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying plumbing, electrical, and mechanical penetrations included in the project's penetration schedule
  • selecting caulk, low-expansion foam, gaskets, escutcheons, collars, or listed firestop material compatible with each service and assembly
  • preparing penetration surfaces and installing backing material where required
  • sealing plumbing, electrical, conduit, and duct or boot penetrations while preserving movement, service access, and required clearances
  • inspecting completed seals for continuity, adhesion, movement allowance, and fire designation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • seal any penetration before the corresponding rough plumbing, electrical, or HVAC inspection has passed
  • work on damaged wiring, leaking plumbing, hot flues, unknown pipes, unprotected gas piping, or any penetration not on the approved schedule
  • substitute ordinary sealant or foam where a listed firestop system is specified
  • bond, coat, or restrict the movement of valves, unions, cleanouts, dampers, or other serviceable components

Any penetration meeting a stop-work criterion in Step 5 must be excluded from this project and referred to the appropriate qualified professional.

Trade Note

Not every penetration takes the same sealant. A copper supply line that expands and contracts with temperature needs a flexible sealant and a movement allowance; a nonmetallic cable needs a product that will not degrade its jacket; a duct boot needs a seal at the boot-to-framing joint, not a seal that touches the duct's interior airflow path. Match the method to the service before you pick up a tool.

Setup Standard

Do not seal any penetration until the plumbing, electrical, or HVAC work at that location has passed its required rough inspection. Do not seal a penetration involving damaged wiring, active leakage, an unidentified pipe, or unprotected gas piping; stop and refer these conditions before proceeding.

Shop Tip

Work through the penetration schedule in the same order it was written rather than jumping between rooms. Sealing every penetration of one service type in sequence — all pipe penetrations, then all wire penetrations, then all duct boots — keeps you using one product at a time and reduces the chance of applying the wrong sealant to the wrong service.

Common Field Fundamentals

A penetration seal fails for one of three reasons: the wrong material was used for the service, the surface was not properly prepared, or the seal restricted something that needed to keep moving or stay accessible. This project treats every penetration as a small decision tree: identify the service, confirm it is ready to seal, select the compatible material, prepare the surface, install the seal, and inspect it before moving to the next penetration.

PlumbingSupply lines, drain-waste-vent pipes, and their movement and temperature requirements
ElectricalCables, conduit, and junction boxes that must remain undamaged and accessible
MechanicalDucts, boots, and exhaust penetrations that must preserve airflow and clearance
Fire-ratedPenetrations through rated assemblies requiring a listed firestop system

Selecting the Correct Sealing Method

Do not default to one-component foam as a universal penetration seal. Foam is appropriate for many irregular gaps around cable and small conduit, but it is not a substitute for a listed firestop system at a fire-resistance-rated assembly, and it is not appropriate at joints requiring significant movement allowance, such as some plumbing penetrations subject to vibration or thermal cycling. Select the material specified by the penetration schedule for each service; where the schedule specifies a listed firestop system, only that system and its labeled components may be used.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Damaged or Unsafe Services

Stop work at any penetration involving damaged wiring, leaking plumbing, a hot or active flue, an unidentified pipe, unprotected gas piping, or a penetration not included in the approved schedule. Do not seal around an energized component that cannot be verified safe, and do not attempt to make a safety determination about gas piping or flue conditions yourself. Record the condition and refer it to the appropriate qualified professional before any sealing occurs at that location.

Code Alert — Listed Firestop Systems Are Not Optional

Where the assembly is fire-resistance-rated, verify which listed firestop system applies before sealing. Ordinary caulk or foam does not restore fire resistance at a penetration through a rated wall, floor, or ceiling. Confirm the required system with the approved plans, adopted code, or the authority having jurisdiction, and label the completed installation as required.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Movement and Cure Requirements

Follow the manufacturer's stated joint-width limits, cure time, temperature range, and movement-capability rating for every sealant, foam, and firestop product used. Do not seal a joint wider or narrower than the product's approved range, and do not disturb a seal before the manufacturer's stated cure time has passed.

Field Safety Cue — Confined and Overhead Work

Confirm safe footing, lighting, and ventilation before sealing penetrations in an attic, crawlspace, or confined space. Keep a second person aware of your location during confined-space penetration work, and avoid prolonged overhead sealant application without eye protection.

C. Material Selection Takeoff

  • Caulk: selected for narrow, accessible gaps where the penetration schedule specifies caulk and where movement is minimal.
  • Low-expansion foam: selected for irregular gaps around cable and small conduit within the product's permitted width and depth.
  • Gaskets, collars, and escutcheons: selected where the schedule specifies a mechanical seal at a pipe, duct, or fixture penetration.
  • Listed firestop material: selected only where the assembly is fire-resistance-rated, matched to the specific tested system identified for that assembly, penetrating item, and annular gap.
  • Rigid backing: selected for openings larger than the sealant manufacturer's maximum permitted gap.

D. Acceptance Standards

CheckStandard
ContinuitySeal is unbroken around the full perimeter of the penetration with no gaps, pinholes, or skipped sections
AdhesionSealant or foam is fully bonded to both the penetrating item and the surrounding surface where the product design requires bonding
Movement allowancePipes and ducts subject to expansion, contraction, or vibration retain the manufacturer-specified clearance and flexibility
Service accessValves, unions, cleanouts, dampers, and junction boxes remain fully operable and accessible after sealing
Fire designationEvery penetration through a fire-resistance-rated assembly uses the specified listed firestop system and is labeled as required
Cable and jacket conditionNo cable insulation is cut, melted, or degraded by the sealing product or application method

E. Preparation — Schedule Review and Inspection Confirmation

  1. Obtain the penetration schedule and identify which wall, ceiling, floor, or rim penetrations are included. Confirm the schedule lists service type, material, and required sealing method for every penetration assigned to this project.
  2. Confirm that rough plumbing, electrical, and HVAC inspections are complete. Obtain written or documented confirmation before sealing any penetration; do not proceed on verbal assurance alone.
  3. Verify that pipes, cables, ducts, boxes, sleeves, and supports are permanently located. Confirm with the responsible trade that no further relocation is planned before sealing.

F. Penetration Identification and Material Selection

  1. Identify each penetration by service, material, temperature, movement, annular-gap size, and fire or moisture requirement. Record these characteristics on the penetration worksheet before selecting a sealing method.
  2. Stop work for damaged wiring, leaking plumbing, hot flues, unknown pipes, unprotected gas piping, or unapproved penetrations. Record the condition, exclude the penetration from this project, and identify the qualified professional required before it can be sealed.
  3. Select caulk, foam, gasket, membrane, collar, escutcheon, firestop, or rigid backing compatible with the assembly and service. Match the product to the category schedule in Section B and the specific penetration schedule.
  4. Do not use ordinary foam where a listed firestop system is required. Confirm the fire-resistance rating of the assembly before selecting a product; where rated, use only the specified listed system.

G. Surface Preparation

  1. Clean and dry the substrate and service surface within the approved bonding area. Remove dust, oil, frost, and failed sealant using the wire brush and shop vacuum, and confirm dryness with the moisture indicator where moisture is suspected.
  2. Install backing material where required to control sealant depth. Cut and fit rigid backing at openings that exceed the sealant manufacturer's maximum permitted gap before applying sealant.

H. Sealing Plumbing Penetrations

  1. Seal plumbing penetrations without bonding valves, unions, cleanouts, or serviceable joints. Apply sealant or foam to the annular gap only, keeping serviceable joints free to be opened later.
  2. Provide movement allowance for pipes subject to expansion or vibration. Use the manufacturer-specified joint width and flexible sealant where the pipe is expected to move with temperature change.

I. Sealing Electrical Penetrations

  1. Seal electrical cable penetrations without damaging insulation or burying junction boxes. Apply sealant or foam around the cable jacket without direct contact that could degrade the jacket, and keep junction boxes fully exposed and accessible.
  2. Seal conduit penetrations using the approved fitting or seal while preserving access. Use a listed fitting or compatible sealant matched to the conduit material and the surrounding assembly.

J. Sealing Mechanical and Duct Penetrations

  1. Seal duct and boot penetrations to the air barrier without crushing insulation or blocking dampers. Apply sealant only at the boot-to-framing joint, keeping the duct's interior airflow path and any dampers fully clear.
  2. Seal around exhaust ducts while maintaining required heat and fire clearances. Confirm the manufacturer or code-required clearance before applying any sealant near an exhaust duct or termination.
  3. Integrate large sleeves and blocks with membranes in the approved lap sequence. Where a sleeve or block penetrates a water-control membrane, follow the specified shingle-lap sequence so water drains over, not into, the penetration.
  4. Avoid trapping water at exterior or drainage-plane penetrations. Confirm the sealing method does not create a dam or pocket where water could collect against the assembly.

K. Cure, Inspection, and Documentation

  1. Allow products to cure. Follow the manufacturer's stated cure time and temperature range before disturbing, testing, or loading any completed seal.
  2. Inspect continuity, adhesion, movement allowance, fire designation, and service access. Check each penetration individually against the Acceptance Standards table in Section D.
  3. Label or document listed firestop systems and concealed seals. Record the specific listed system used at each rated penetration and photograph every seal that will be concealed by insulation or finishes.
  4. Correct failed or incompatible seals before insulation. Remove and reseal any penetration that fails continuity, adhesion, movement, fire-designation, or access checks before any cavity insulation is installed.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review every completed penetration using the same checks regardless of service type.

  • Continuity check: confirm the seal is unbroken around the full perimeter of each penetration.
  • Adhesion check: confirm sealant or foam is fully bonded where the product requires bonding.
  • Movement check: confirm pipes and ducts subject to expansion or vibration retain their required clearance and flexibility.
  • Access check: confirm valves, unions, cleanouts, dampers, and junction boxes remain operable and accessible.
  • Fire-designation check: confirm every rated penetration uses the specified listed firestop system and is labeled.
  • Documentation check: confirm every concealed seal is photographed and recorded before it is covered by insulation.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Sealant Cracks or Separates at a Plumbing Penetration

Probable cause: the sealant selected does not have sufficient movement capability for a pipe subject to thermal expansion, or the joint was sealed too narrow for the product's rated movement range.
Confirmation: inspect the cracked seal, measure the joint width, and compare it against the product's manufacturer-stated movement rating.
Correction: remove the failed sealant, widen the joint if required, and reseal using a product rated for the required movement, following the manufacturer's joint-width and depth limits.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity and movement checks after the replacement seal has cured.

Foam Has Contacted and Degraded a Cable Jacket

Probable cause: foam was applied directly against the cable without confirming compatibility with the jacket material, or excess foam expanded against the cable during cure.
Confirmation: inspect the cable jacket at the penetration for softening, discoloration, or surface damage.
Correction: remove the foam from contact with the cable, replace any damaged section of cable per the responsible electrical trade, and reseal using a product confirmed compatible with the cable jacket, keeping the product off the jacket surface where the manufacturer requires clearance.
Reinspection: repeat the cable and jacket condition check after correction.

A Junction Box or Valve Was Partially Buried by Sealant or Foam

Probable cause: sealant or foam was applied without first confirming the exact location of the serviceable component, or excess material was allowed to expand over it during cure.
Confirmation: inspect the location and attempt to fully open or access the component.
Correction: remove the sealant or foam covering the component without damaging it, and reseal only the surrounding annular gap, leaving the component fully exposed and operable.
Reinspection: repeat the service access check at that location.

Ordinary Foam Was Used at a Fire-Resistance-Rated Penetration

Probable cause: the fire-resistance rating of the assembly was not confirmed before the penetration was sealed, or the installer treated all penetrations as identical.
Confirmation: review the approved plans or adopted code to confirm the assembly's fire-resistance rating and the required listed firestop system.
Correction: remove the incompatible foam and install the specified listed firestop system exactly as tested and labeled, with no substitution of components.
Reinspection: repeat the fire-designation check and confirm the installation is labeled as required.

A Sealed Duct Boot Shows Restricted Airflow After Sealing

Probable cause: sealant or foam entered the duct's interior airflow path or contacted a damper instead of remaining at the boot-to-framing joint.
Confirmation: inspect the interior of the duct and boot at the penetration and confirm damper movement.
Correction: remove any sealant or foam that has entered the airflow path or contacted the damper, and reseal only the exterior boot-to-framing joint.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity and access checks and confirm unrestricted airflow and damper movement.

Cleanup

This project ends with continuously sealed, documented penetrations, not a work area coated in overspray and scrap foam.

  • Trim excess cured foam flush without breaking the continuous seal at any penetration
  • Remove all sealant tubes, foam cans, backing scraps, and packaging from the work area
  • Wipe down tools, nozzles, and applicator guns according to the product's cleanup instructions
  • Confirm every serviceable component remains fully accessible and operable
  • File the completed penetration worksheet and photographs for use in Chapter 5 and the Chapter 19 final inspection

Field Terms

Penetration
Any point where a pipe, cable, conduit, duct, or other service passes through the air-control boundary.
Annular Gap
The clearance space between a penetrating item and the surrounding framing, sheathing, or opening.
Movement Allowance
The clearance and sealant flexibility required to accommodate thermal expansion, contraction, or vibration.
Listed Firestop System
A tested and labeled assembly of materials that restores fire resistance at a penetration through a rated assembly.
Serviceable Component
A valve, union, cleanout, damper, junction box, or similar item that must remain operable and accessible after sealing.
Continuity
An unbroken seal around the full perimeter of a penetration with no gaps, pinholes, or skipped sections.
Boot-to-Framing Joint
The exterior connection between a duct boot and the surrounding framing or air barrier, distinct from the duct's interior airflow path.
Backing Material
Rigid material installed at an opening to control sealant depth where the gap exceeds the sealant's maximum permitted width.

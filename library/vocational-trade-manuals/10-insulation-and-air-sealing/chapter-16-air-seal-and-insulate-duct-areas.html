Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete the building-envelope air sealing and thermal insulation around one accessible duct, boot, chase, or mechanical penetration located in an unconditioned space such as an attic, crawlspace, garage, or unconditioned basement, without performing any duct-sealing or duct-repair work covered under Book 9. The finished work product is a continuously air-sealed and insulated enclosure boundary around the assigned duct area, with the duct's own insulation preserved at full thickness, service access maintained, and condensate drainage undisturbed.

You will review the duct location and HVAC records, confirm the duct is de-energized where work could disturb it, inspect for existing duct-insulation damage or moisture, identify the enclosure boundary around the duct, seal framing and service gaps adjacent to the duct, seal boot transitions to the building air barrier, repair minor duct-insulation facing tears using HVAC-compatible methods only, install building insulation around the duct area without compressing the duct's own insulation, and inspect the finished area for gaps, thermal bypasses, and blocked access.

This project depends on prior air-sealing and cavity-inspection chapters and depends specifically on Book 9 duct-sealing and duct-insulation records, which this chapter does not repeat or redo. It feeds directly into the Chapter 19 final quality inspection and the Chapter 20 capstone, both of which verify duct-adjacent thermal control as part of the complete thermal enclosure.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-091 — Identify ducts located in unconditioned space and the enclosure boundary around them.
  • IN-092 — Seal duct-adjacent framing and service gaps using compatible air-sealing materials.
  • IN-093 — Protect existing duct insulation from damage and compression during building-insulation work.
  • IN-094 — Seal boot connections to the building air barrier without blocking registers or dampers.
  • IN-095 — Maintain service access to filters, dampers, shutoffs, cleanouts, and condensate drains.
  • IN-096 — Inspect the completed duct area for thermal-bypass gaps and confirm airflow-related conditions after work.

Where This Skill Fits

Framing-joint sealing from Chapter 3, service-penetration sealing from Chapter 4, and attic bypass work from Chapter 5 all apply around ducts the same way they apply anywhere else in the enclosure. What makes duct-adjacent work distinct is the requirement to never treat the duct itself, its insulation, or its connections as part of this chapter's scope. Any duct leakage, disconnection, or insulation damage beyond a minor facing tear must be referred to Book 9.

This chapter's completed record becomes one line item in the Chapter 19 final inspection and one required task in the Chapter 20 capstone sequence, both of which confirm that enclosure work around mechanical systems did not compromise duct performance, service access, or condensate management.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the building-enclosure boundary around one accessible duct, boot, chase, or mechanical penetration
  • air-sealing framing and service gaps adjacent to the duct using compatible materials
  • sealing boot-to-floor, boot-to-wall, or boot-to-ceiling transitions to the building air barrier
  • repairing minor tears in duct-insulation facing only, using the approved HVAC-compatible method
  • installing building insulation around the duct area while preserving full duct-insulation thickness
  • maintaining clearances from flues, exhaust ducts, motors, transformers, and other heat-producing equipment
  • preserving service platforms, filter access, shutoffs, cleanouts, and electrical clearances

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • seal, patch, reconnect, or otherwise repair the duct system itself; that work belongs to Book 9 and to a qualified HVAC contractor
  • compress, remove, or replace the duct's own insulation beyond a minor facing repair
  • block register openings, dampers, balancing devices, condensate drains, or filter access
  • work on energized equipment, disturbed refrigerant lines, or duct systems showing signs of significant leakage, disconnection, or contamination

Any condition that meets a stop-work criterion in Step 5 of the Work Order must halt work in that area and be referred to a qualified HVAC contractor or other appropriate professional before this chapter's work continues.

Trade Note

The building's air barrier and the duct system are two separate systems that happen to occupy the same unconditioned space. Sealing the building envelope around a duct does not fix a leaky duct, and sealing a leaky duct does not substitute for building envelope air sealing. Keep the two systems and their required professionals distinct in your documentation.

Setup Standard

Do not begin work on any duct area until HVAC equipment has been shut down where the work could disturb ducts, wiring, or controls, and until you have confirmed the duct insulation is dry. Do not proceed if refrigerant lines, energized equipment, or significant duct disconnection is present; stop and refer the condition instead.

Shop Tip

Photograph the duct area, its insulation condition, and the boot connection before you begin sealing or insulating. A clear "before" photograph makes it much easier to prove during the Chapter 19 inspection that duct insulation thickness and service access were preserved rather than disturbed.

Common Field Fundamentals

Duct-adjacent work always separates two questions: what belongs to the building enclosure, and what belongs to the duct system. Everything in this chapter answers only the first question. If a duct problem is discovered, document it and refer it; do not attempt to fix it as part of this project.

Building Envelope Versus Duct System

A duct passing through an unconditioned attic or crawlspace crosses the building's thermal and air-control boundary without being part of it. The building envelope must still be continuous around the penetration. Confusing the two systems leads to two common errors: sealing the building envelope so tightly that duct service access is buried, or attempting duct repairs that belong to a qualified HVAC contractor under Book 9.

Measurement Cue — Verify Duct-Insulation Thickness Before and After

Measure and record the existing duct-insulation thickness at the start of work. After installing building insulation around the duct area, re-measure at the same locations and confirm the duct's own insulation has not been compressed or reduced.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Duct and Equipment Hazards

Stop work in the duct area for wet duct insulation, damaged refrigerant lines, energized equipment that has not been shut down, severe contamination, or any duct disconnection or leakage beyond what this chapter authorizes. Do not attempt to reconnect, patch, or otherwise repair the duct system. Record the condition and refer it to a qualified HVAC contractor or to Book 9 duct-sealing procedures.

Field Safety Cue — Working Near Ducts in Attics and Crawlspaces

Maintain safe footing on joists or walk boards while working near ducts; do not use flexible duct or duct supports as a step or handhold. Keep foam and sealant away from moving dampers, electrical equipment, and any exposed conductors until the equipment has been confirmed de-energized or otherwise made safe.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Duct-Insulation Facing Repair

Repair minor tears in duct-insulation facing using only the HVAC-compatible tape and method identified for that product. Do not use general-purpose building tape, foam, or sealant on the duct's own insulation or facing, and do not attempt a repair beyond a minor tear; larger damage is a referral condition.

D. Preparation — Records and Shutdown

  1. Review the duct location, HVAC drawings, duct-insulation condition, air-barrier detail, equipment access, condensate, and fire-clearance requirements. Record the source document and date reviewed for each.
  2. Coordinate with Book 9 duct-sealing and insulation records. Confirm the duct itself has already been sealed and insulated under Book 9 procedures before beginning this chapter's building-envelope work.
  3. Shut down HVAC equipment when work could disturb ducts, wiring, or controls. Record the shutdown time and the person who authorized it, or record that shutdown was not required and state why.

E. Inspection and Stop-Work Decision

  1. Inspect for damaged duct insulation, condensation, disconnected ducts, leakage, pests, mold, and hazardous materials. Record the condition of the duct insulation and boot connection before any work begins.
  2. Stop work for wet insulation, refrigerant-line damage, energized equipment, severe contamination, or inaccessible mechanical defects. Record the condition as a referral, identify the qualified HVAC contractor or other professional required, and do not proceed with this chapter's work in that area until the referral is resolved.

F. Air Sealing Around the Duct Area

  1. Identify the enclosure boundary around the duct or chase. Mark where the building air barrier and thermal boundary pass the duct so the seal is complete rather than a partial patch.
  2. Seal framing and service gaps adjacent to the duct using compatible materials. Apply the Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 methods appropriate to each gap's size and service type.
  3. Seal boot-to-floor, boot-to-wall, or boot-to-ceiling transitions to the building air barrier. Apply a continuous bead or bond around the full perimeter of the transition.
  4. Do not block register openings, dampers, duct access, balancing devices, or condensate drains. Verify each remains fully operable after the seal cures.

G. Duct-Insulation Protection and Repair

  1. Repair minor tears in duct insulation facing only with the approved HVAC-compatible method. Confirm the tear is minor before proceeding; refer larger damage instead of attempting a repair.
  2. Do not compress duct insulation with new building insulation. Maintain a visible gap or supported separation between the building insulation and the duct's own insulation layer where the design requires it.

H. Building Insulation Installation

  1. Install insulation around the duct area while preserving the full duct-insulation thickness. Cut and fit building insulation to the surrounding framing, not against the duct's insulated surface.
  2. Use dams or supports to prevent loose fill from loading flexible ducts. Install dams before any loose-fill insulation is placed nearby.
  3. Maintain clearances around flues, exhaust ducts, motors, transformers, and heat-producing equipment. Confirm no insulation or sealant enters a prohibited clearance zone near the duct area.
  4. Preserve service platforms, filter access, shutoffs, cleanouts, and electrical clearances. Confirm each remains reachable without disturbing the completed insulation.

I. Inspection, Restoration, and Documentation

  1. Inspect for gaps, thermal bypasses, crushed duct insulation, buried access, and blocked ventilation. Correct any deficiency before concealing the area.
  2. Restore equipment and observe the area for condensation, movement, or airflow-related disturbance. Confirm the HVAC system operates normally after restoration.
  3. Document the air-sealing and insulation treatment and any HVAC repairs referred to Book 9. Record materials used, locations sealed, duct-insulation thickness before and after, and any referral made.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed duct-area work using the same checks regardless of which portion of the boundary was sealed.

  • Boundary check: confirm the building air barrier is continuous around the duct, boot, or chase with no unsealed gap remaining.
  • Duct-insulation check: confirm duct-insulation thickness at the recorded locations equals or exceeds the pre-work measurement.
  • Access check: confirm filters, dampers, shutoffs, cleanouts, and condensate drains remain reachable.
  • Clearance check: confirm no insulation or sealant entered a prohibited heat-source clearance zone.
  • Referral check: confirm any duct defect beyond a minor facing tear was documented and referred rather than repaired in this chapter.
  • Restoration check: confirm HVAC equipment was restored and observed for normal operation after the work.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Duct Insulation Is Compressed by New Building Insulation

Probable cause: building insulation was pressed directly against the duct's insulated surface instead of being supported by the surrounding framing or a dam.
Confirmation: re-measure duct-insulation thickness at the recorded locations and compare to the pre-work measurement.
Correction: remove the building insulation from contact with the duct, install a dam or support to maintain separation, and reinstall the building insulation against the framing.
Reinspection: repeat the duct-insulation thickness check at the same locations.

A Register, Damper, or Condensate Drain Is Blocked

Probable cause: sealant, foam, or insulation was applied without first confirming the exact location and swing or clearance of the device.
Confirmation: operate the damper or check the condensate drain path and confirm free movement or flow.
Correction: remove the obstructing material and reapply the seal or insulation clear of the device's required clearance.
Reinspection: repeat the access check for that device.

A Duct Leak or Disconnection Was Discovered and Sealed as Part of This Chapter

Probable cause: the boundary between building-envelope work and duct-system work was not maintained, and a duct-system defect was treated as a building air-sealing task.
Confirmation: review the work performed and identify whether any material was applied directly to a duct seam, connection, or damaged section rather than to the surrounding building assembly.
Correction: remove any material applied to the duct system itself, document the discovered defect, and refer it to Book 9 duct-sealing procedures or a qualified HVAC contractor.
Reinspection: confirm the referral is documented and that no further duct-system work occurs under this chapter.

Loose-Fill Insulation Has Loaded and Deformed a Flexible Duct

Probable cause: no insulation dam or support was installed before loose-fill insulation was placed near the duct.
Confirmation: inspect the duct shape and support condition beneath the insulation.
Correction: remove the loose-fill insulation from contact with the duct, install a dam or support, and reinstall insulation so it does not rest on the duct.
Reinspection: repeat the ventilation and duct-support check for the area.

Condensation Appears on or Near the Duct After the Area Is Closed

Probable cause: a gap in the building air barrier is allowing warm humid air to reach the cold duct surface, or the duct's own insulation was compressed or gapped during the work.
Confirmation: inspect the completed seal for missed gaps and re-measure duct-insulation thickness and continuity.
Correction: correct the missed air-seal gap or restore the duct-insulation thickness and continuity; refer to Chapter 18 if the moisture source is not resolved by these corrections.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check and observe the area again after correction.

Field Terms

Duct-Adjacent Boundary
The building air-barrier and thermal boundary near a duct chase, boot, or mechanical penetration, distinct from the duct system itself.
Boot Connection
The transition where a duct or register boot passes through the floor, wall, or ceiling into the building envelope.
Duct-Insulation Facing
The outer vapor-retarder or reflective layer on existing duct insulation.
Insulation Dam
A supported barrier preventing loose-fill or batt insulation from loading a flexible duct.
Service Access Point
A filter, damper, balancing device, shutoff, cleanout, or condensate drain that must remain reachable.
Referral Condition
Any duct-system defect beyond a minor facing tear that must be referred to Book 9 or a qualified HVAC contractor.
Thermal Bypass
A path allowing air, and the heat it carries, to move around or through insulation without crossing a continuous air barrier.
Condensate Drain
The pipe or path carrying condensation away from HVAC equipment; must remain clear and reachable.

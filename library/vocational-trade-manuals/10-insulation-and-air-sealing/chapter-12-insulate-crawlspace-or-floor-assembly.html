Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install and support insulation in a floor assembly or crawlspace ceiling after the approved crawlspace moisture and ventilation strategy has already determined where insulation belongs. The finished work product is a dry, fully fitted, continuously supported floor-insulation installation covering every assigned joist bay, with wiring, pipes, bracing, and ducts accommodated without compression, and with any ground vapor retarder installed and sealed according to the approved scope.

You will confirm the approved insulation location, inspect the crawlspace or floor assembly for moisture and safety hazards, air-seal accessible floor penetrations and rim transitions included in the approved design, measure and cut batts for full subfloor contact, fit insulation around obstructions, install supports that hold the insulation in place without damaging it, and inspect the completed floor plane before closeout.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 inspection, the moisture-source corrections identified along the way, and the rim-joist detail addressed separately in Chapter 13. It also depends on the System-Sequence Control principle that crawlspace and floor-insulation work must follow the approved crawlspace moisture and ventilation strategy rather than a general assumption about where insulation belongs.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-067 — Inspect floor framing for condition, moisture, and readiness for insulation.
  • IN-068 — Install batt supports that hold insulation against the subfloor without excessive compression.
  • IN-069 — Insulate rim areas according to the approved assembly detail.
  • IN-070 — Secure insulation with the specified facing orientation and support method.
  • IN-071 — Protect insulation from moisture, wind washing, and pest disturbance.
  • IN-072 — Inspect floor-insulation coverage for gaps, voids, and unsupported edges.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 identified whether floor or crawlspace insulation was missing, compressed, wet, or unsupported. Chapter 2 established a safe, ventilated work zone. This project performs the actual floor-insulation installation identified by that inspection, using the same moisture-first sequencing principle applied throughout the book: bulk-water and moisture problems must be corrected before insulation is installed.

The rim-joist bays adjacent to this floor assembly are treated separately in Chapter 13, using rigid-foam inserts and edge seals rather than batt insulation. This chapter installs field-of-floor insulation only; do not substitute this chapter's methods for the rim-joist detail.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming whether the approved crawlspace design places insulation at the floor plane or at the foundation walls
  • inspecting crawlspace or floor-framing conditions for moisture, pests, structural condition, and hazards
  • air-sealing accessible floor penetrations and rim transitions included in the approved design
  • measuring, cutting, and fitting batt insulation for full subfloor contact around wiring, pipes, bracing, and ducts
  • installing insulation supports that hold batts in place without excessive compression
  • installing or verifying a ground vapor retarder only when included in the approved crawlspace scope
  • inspecting the completed floor plane for gaps, voids, unsupported edges, and moisture contact

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with floor insulation when the approved design calls for a sealed and conditioned crawlspace wall system instead
  • enter a crawlspace with sewage contamination, standing water, structural damage, heavy mold, hazardous insulation, unsafe wiring, or unresolved confined-space hazards
  • insulate rim-joist bays using this chapter's methods; rim joists are addressed in Chapter 13
  • install a ground vapor retarder or insulation over active plumbing leaks or uncorrected exterior water entry

Any condition meeting a stop-work criterion in Step 4 must halt work in that area and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before insulation continues.

Trade Note

A floor-insulation project is only as good as the moisture strategy behind it. Installing dry, well-fitted batts over a crawlspace with an unresolved ground-moisture or plumbing-leak problem produces damp insulation and hidden decay within one season. Confirm the moisture strategy before cutting the first batt.

Setup Standard

Do not enter a crawlspace that has not been checked for standing water, sewage contamination, unsafe wiring, structural damage, or confined-space hazards. Confirm a second person is aware of your location and expected duration before extended crawlspace work.

Shop Tip

Stage cut batts near the crawlspace access opening in the order you will install them, working from the far end of the space back toward the access. This avoids repeated crawling trips and reduces the chance of crushing already-installed insulation.

Common Field Fundamentals

Floor and crawlspace insulation work follows the same top-down logic as wall insulation: confirm the design intent, inspect for hazards and moisture, air-seal what is accessible, then insulate. The one added complication is gravity — batt insulation in a floor assembly has nothing holding it up except its support system, so a support failure shows up as sagging, gapping insulation within weeks rather than immediately.

Floor Plane vs. Foundation-Wall Insulation

Before cutting any material, confirm which assembly the approved crawlspace design actually calls for. A vented crawlspace typically insulates at the floor plane, leaving the crawlspace itself unconditioned. A sealed and conditioned crawlspace typically insulates at the foundation walls instead, leaving the floor above uninsulated. Installing floor insulation in a crawlspace designed to be sealed and conditioned works against the approved design and must not proceed under this chapter.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Crawlspace Hazards

Stop work and do not enter further for sewage contamination, standing water, structural damage, heavy mold, hazardous insulation such as suspected asbestos or vermiculite, unsafe wiring, or confined-space conditions that cannot be made safe. Record the condition and refer it to the appropriate qualified professional — a mold remediator, structural professional, hazardous-material professional, or electrician as applicable — before any further entry.

Field Safety Cue — Confined-Space Awareness

Confirm headroom, footing, and air quality before fully entering a crawlspace. Keep a second person aware of your location, expected duration, and check-in method. Do not work alone in a crawlspace with limited egress for extended periods.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Support Method Limits

Follow the manufacturer's spacing and installation instructions for wire rods, netting, or lacing. A support system installed at wider-than-specified spacing will sag under the insulation's own weight over time, even if it looks adequate on installation day.

D. Design Confirmation and Inspection

  1. Review the approved crawlspace design and determine whether insulation belongs at the floor plane or foundation walls. Record the confirmed location on the project worksheet before proceeding.
  2. Do not proceed with floor insulation if the approved design calls for a sealed and conditioned crawlspace wall system. If the design specifies wall insulation instead, stop this project and refer to the correct approved assembly detail.
  3. Inspect access, soil moisture, vapor cover, drainage, pests, standing water, mold, wiring, plumbing, ducts, and structural condition. Walk or crawl the full assigned area before installing any material.
  4. Stop work for sewage, standing water, structural damage, heavy mold, hazardous insulation, unsafe wiring, or confined-space conditions. Record the condition, exclude the affected area, and refer it to the qualified professional required before work resumes.
  5. Verify that plumbing leaks and exterior water entry have been corrected. Confirm this against the Chapter 1 or Chapter 18 moisture-correction record before installing insulation.

E. Preparation and Air Sealing

  1. Confirm floor-cavity dimensions, insulation type, support method, facing, and required clearances. Record these against the approved schedule on the project worksheet.
  2. Air-seal accessible floor penetrations and rim transitions included in the approved design. Use the Chapter 4 penetration-sealing methods for accessible plumbing, electrical, and mechanical penetrations within this chapter's scope.

F. Cutting and Fitting

  1. Measure and cut batts for full contact with the subfloor. Measure each bay individually rather than assuming uniform spacing throughout the assembly.
  2. Fit insulation around wiring, pipes, bracing, and ducts without compression. Split or notch batts as needed so insulation maintains full thickness on both sides of an obstruction.
  3. Install insulation with the specified facing orientation. Confirm the facing direction against the approved vapor-control design before installing the first batt, and keep the orientation consistent across the entire assembly.

G. Support Installation

  1. Hold batts against the subfloor using approved supports, netting, rods, or lacing. Install supports at the manufacturer-specified spacing across every bay.
  2. Do not use supports that excessively compress or cut the insulation. Verify each support holds the batt firmly without crushing it below its rated thickness.
  3. Maintain access to plumbing valves, electrical boxes, traps, dampers, and cleanouts. Route insulation and supports around these components rather than covering them.

H. Rim Areas and Moisture Protection

  1. Insulate rim areas only according to the Chapter 13 assembly detail. Do not extend this chapter's batt-and-support method into rim-joist bays.
  2. Protect insulation from wind washing, pests, and moisture as specified. Verify baffles, blocking, or barrier details called for by the approved design are in place where they intersect this assembly.
  3. Install or verify the ground vapor retarder only when included in the crawlspace scope. Confirm scope on the project worksheet before installing membrane material.
  4. Seal ground-membrane seams and edges using the approved method. Lap seams by the specified amount and seal with the approved tape or adhesive.

I. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect the entire floor plane for fallen batts, voids, unsupported edges, moisture contact, and blocked access. Check every bay, not only a sample, before closing out the project.
  2. Correct defects and document inaccessible bays. Record any bay that could not be fully inspected or insulated, along with the reason.
  3. Complete the crawlspace or floor-insulation inspection. Sign off on the project worksheet and photograph the completed assembly before it is concealed by later work.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed floor-insulation installation using the same checks regardless of which bay produced the finding.

  • Location check: confirm insulation is installed at the floor plane only when the approved design specifies that location.
  • Coverage check: confirm every assigned bay is filled at full depth with no gaps, voids, or slumping.
  • Obstruction check: confirm insulation is fitted around wiring, pipes, bracing, and ducts without compression.
  • Support check: confirm supports are installed at the specified spacing and hold insulation without sagging or crushing.
  • Access check: confirm plumbing valves, electrical boxes, traps, dampers, and cleanouts remain accessible.
  • Membrane check: confirm any required ground vapor retarder is continuous with sealed seams and edges.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Batts Sag or Fall Within Weeks of Installation

Probable cause: insulation supports were installed at wider-than-specified spacing, or the wrong support type was used for the batt weight and cavity span.
Confirmation: inspect the support spacing against the manufacturer instructions and compare it to the sagging bays.
Correction: remove the affected batt, install supports at the correct spacing, and reinstall the batt at full depth.
Reinspection: repeat the support check across the entire assembly, not just the failed bay.

Insulation Is Installed at the Wrong Location for the Approved Design

Probable cause: the approved crawlspace design called for a sealed and conditioned crawlspace with wall insulation, but floor insulation was installed by default without checking the design first.
Confirmation: review the approved crawlspace moisture and ventilation strategy against the installed location.
Correction: remove the incorrectly located insulation and install the assembly specified by the approved design, following the correct chapter or detail.
Reinspection: repeat the design-location check before continuing any further insulation work.

Insulation Is Wet or Shows Mold at Reinspection

Probable cause: a plumbing leak or exterior water-entry source was not actually corrected before insulation was installed, or a new leak developed after installation.
Confirmation: inspect the wet or moldy area, trace the moisture source, and use a moisture indicator to confirm the extent of wetting.
Correction: remove and discard wet or moldy insulation, correct the moisture source, allow the assembly to dry and be confirmed dry, and reinstall new insulation.
Reinspection: repeat the moisture check and coverage check on the corrected area before closeout.

Access to a Valve, Box, or Cleanout Is Buried

Probable cause: insulation or support material was installed without first marking service points that must remain accessible.
Confirmation: compare the installed insulation against the mapped service points from Step 6.
Correction: remove and reroute insulation and supports around the component, restoring clear access without leaving an uninsulated gap larger than necessary.
Reinspection: repeat the access check across the entire assembly.

Ground-Membrane Seams Separate or Show Exposed Soil

Probable cause: seams were not lapped by the specified amount, or tape was applied to a dusty or uneven surface without proper adhesion.
Confirmation: inspect all seams and edges for separation, gaps, or exposed soil.
Correction: clean the surface, re-lap the seam by the specified amount, and reseal with the approved tape or adhesive.
Reinspection: repeat the membrane check across the full crawlspace floor.

Cleanup

This project ends with a documented, fully supported floor-insulation assembly, not loose batt scraps and unrecorded gaps.

  • Remove all scrap insulation, cut membrane pieces, and packaging from the crawlspace or floor cavity
  • Confirm no tools, fasteners, or support hardware were left in the cavity or crawlspace
  • Vacuum or sweep accessible surfaces disturbed during installation
  • Match every photograph to its corresponding bay or area on the project worksheet
  • File the completed inspection record with the project records for reference at the Chapter 19 final quality inspection

Field Terms

Floor Plane
The insulation location at the underside of the subfloor, used in a vented crawlspace design.
Foundation-Wall Plane
The alternative insulation location at the crawlspace perimeter wall, used in a sealed and conditioned crawlspace design.
Full-Depth Fit
Insulation occupying the complete cavity depth without gaps, compression, or slumping.
Support Method
The wire rod, netting, or lacing system that holds insulation against the subfloor.
Ground Vapor Retarder
The membrane covering exposed crawlspace soil, installed only when included in the approved scope.
Facing Orientation
The direction a batt's vapor-retarder facing must face according to the approved vapor-control design.
Wind Washing
Displacement or degradation of insulation performance caused by outside air moving through or across the insulation.
Confined Space
A crawlspace or similar area with restricted entry, exit, or ventilation requiring specific safety precautions.

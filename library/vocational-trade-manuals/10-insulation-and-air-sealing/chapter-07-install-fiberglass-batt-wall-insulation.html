Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install fiberglass batt insulation in framed wall cavities so that every cavity is filled to full depth, with complete contact on all sides, and without gaps, voids, compression, or improperly fitted material around wiring, boxes, pipes, and blocking. The finished work product is a controlled set of wall cavities insulated to the approved schedule, inspected, photographed, and documented before the cavities are closed.

You will confirm that air sealing and required rough inspections are already complete, measure each cavity and its obstructions, select and cut batts to the correct size, fit the batts around wiring and boxes without compressing them, fill irregular spaces with accurately cut pieces, maintain the approved facing orientation, preserve heat and electrical clearances, and inspect every completed cavity before it is concealed.

This project builds directly on the air-sealing work completed in Chapters 3 through 5 and the attic ventilation protection installed in Chapter 6. It applies the System-Sequence Control principle that air sealing generally occurs before cavity insulation, and that electrical, plumbing, and required rough inspections must be complete before cavities are closed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-037 — Select batt size matching the approved width, thickness, thermal rating, and facing for each cavity.
  • IN-038 — Cut batts accurately using an insulation knife and straightedge on a clean cutting surface.
  • IN-039 — Fit insulation around wiring so the batt is split and lies both behind and in front of the cable.
  • IN-040 — Fit insulation around electrical boxes without stuffing large compressed masses behind them.
  • IN-041 — Avoid compression by cutting batts to size instead of forcing oversized or undersized material into a cavity.
  • IN-042 — Inspect cavity-fill quality for full top, bottom, edge, and back contact before concealment.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 3 through 5 sealed the framing joints, service penetrations, and attic bypasses that make up the air barrier. This project fills the cavities behind that air barrier with insulation, which is a materially different function: the air barrier stops air movement, while the batt slows heat transfer. Both must be correct and continuous for the wall to perform as designed.

Chapter 8 teaches the same cavity-fill objective using mineral wool batts, which require a different cutting and friction-fit technique. Chapter 15 installs the vapor retarder, if the approved assembly requires one, at the location and facing orientation established in this chapter. Chapter 19 re-inspects every completed cavity using the same full-depth, edge-contact, and compression standard taught here.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming that air sealing, rough systems, fireblocking, and required inspections are complete before any cavity is filled
  • measuring stud spacing, cavity depth, blocking, wiring, pipes, boxes, and irregular geometry for each assigned cavity
  • selecting fiberglass batts matching the approved width, thickness, thermal rating, and facing
  • cutting batts accurately and fitting them from top to bottom without folding or forcing
  • splitting batts around horizontal wiring and fitting them around electrical boxes, pipes, bracing, and blocking
  • filling narrow and irregular spaces with accurately cut pieces
  • maintaining the approved facing orientation and required heat-source clearances
  • inspecting, correcting, photographing, and documenting every completed cavity

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • insulate a cavity before required air-sealing, fireblocking, electrical, plumbing, or HVAC inspections are confirmed complete
  • insulate a wet, contaminated, or hazardous-material-affected cavity
  • cover junction boxes, serviceable equipment, or heat-producing components with insulation
  • select or install a vapor-retarder facing orientation that differs from the approved assembly design

Any cavity found wet, contaminated, or containing an unresolved hazard must be excluded from this project and referred back to the applicable earlier chapter or a qualified professional before insulation proceeds.

Trade Note

A cavity that looks "filled" from the room side can still fail this project's acceptance standard if the batt does not reach full depth at the back of the cavity, behind wiring, or into narrow corners. Full contact on all four sides plus the back of the cavity is the actual standard, not visual fullness at the front face.

Setup Standard

Do not begin insulating any cavity until the air sealing, rough electrical, rough plumbing, rough HVAC, fireblocking, and any required inspections for that cavity are confirmed complete. Do not insulate over a wet, contaminated, or hazardous-material-affected cavity.

Shop Tip

Cut several batts of the same standard cavity width before starting installation, then handle the irregular cavities — those with blocking, multiple services, or non-standard framing — one at a time as you reach them. This keeps the standard cavities moving quickly while giving the irregular cavities the individual attention they need.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every cavity in this project is handled the same way: measure the cavity and its obstructions, select or cut the correct batt, place it from top to bottom without folding, fit it around any obstruction without compressing the insulation, and inspect for full contact before moving to the next cavity.

Standard cavitiesFull-width bays with no obstructions, filled with a single uncut or length-cut batt
Wired cavitiesBays with horizontal cable requiring the batt to be split front and back
Boxed cavitiesBays with an electrical box requiring a notched fit without compressed backing
Irregular cavitiesBays with blocking, bracing, pipes, or non-standard width requiring individually cut pieces

Facing Orientation and the Vapor-Control Design

The facing on a batt, when present, may function as part of the assembly's vapor-control strategy. Install facing in the orientation specified by the approved wall-insulation schedule, and do not change that orientation based on general assumptions about climate. Chapter 15 covers vapor-retarder installation in more detail; this chapter only requires that facing already present on a batt be installed exactly as the schedule specifies.

Measurement Cue — Measure Every Cavity Independently

Do not assume all cavities in a wall section share the same width or depth. Measure stud spacing, cavity depth, and the presence of blocking or bracing for each cavity before cutting its batt, since minor framing variations are common even in walls built to a consistent layout.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Rough Inspections Before Concealing Any Cavity

Do not insulate a cavity that has not passed its required electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or fireblocking inspection. Insulating a cavity before the required inspection conceals the work that inspector needs to see. Stop and confirm inspection status with the responsible party before proceeding on any cavity in doubt.

Field Safety Cue — Fiberglass Handling

Wear gloves, eye protection, and an N95 or better respirator at all times while cutting, handling, or fitting fiberglass batt insulation. Ventilate the work area and avoid unnecessary contact between fiberglass fibers and bare skin. Wash exposed skin after handling and before eating or drinking.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Batt Product Specification

Install only the batt product identified in the approved wall-insulation schedule, matching width, thickness, thermal rating, and facing. Do not substitute a different thickness or facing type based on what is available on-site. If the specified product is unavailable, stop and obtain an approved substitution before proceeding.

  • Batt product: width, thickness, thermal rating, and facing exactly as identified in the approved wall-insulation schedule.
  • Quantity: sufficient batts to cover every assigned cavity at full width and height, plus a reasonable allowance for cutting waste around obstructions.
  • Fasteners: staples only if the approved facing-attachment method specifies stapled facing tabs; otherwise none required.

E. Preparation — Review and Verification

  1. Review the wall-insulation schedule, cavity dimensions, batt product, facing requirements, fire restrictions, and inspection stage. Confirm the schedule matches the wall section you are assigned before cutting any material.
  2. Confirm that air sealing, rough systems, fireblocking, and required inspections are complete. Obtain sign-off or written confirmation from the responsible party for any inspection you did not personally witness.
  3. Verify that cavities are dry, clean, and free of debris, pests, and hazardous materials. Exclude and refer any cavity that fails this check before proceeding to the next step.

F. Measurement and Batt Selection

  1. Measure stud spacing, cavity depth, blocking, wiring, pipes, boxes, and irregular geometry. Record measurements for each cavity independently rather than assuming uniform framing across the wall.
  2. Select batts matching the approved width, thickness, thermal rating, and facing. Confirm the product label against the schedule before cutting.

G. Cutting

  1. Stage cutting on a clean surface using the approved insulation knife and straightedge. Keep the cutting surface free of debris that could contaminate or damage the batt facing.
  2. Cut batts slightly oversize only as directed to obtain full edge contact. Do not cut batts undersize, which leaves gaps at the plates or stud edges.

H. Placement and Fitting

  1. Place the batt from top to bottom without folding or forcing it. Support the batt as you position it so it does not sag or twist before reaching the bottom plate.
  2. Split the batt around horizontal wiring so insulation lies behind and in front of the cable. Do not compress the batt flat against the cable; separate it into two layers with the wire between them.
  3. Fit insulation around electrical boxes without stuffing large compressed masses behind them. Notch the batt to the box outline rather than packing loose material behind it.
  4. Cut around pipes, bracing, and blocking to preserve full depth on both sides. Make relief cuts sized to the obstruction rather than tearing an opening larger than needed.
  5. Fill narrow and irregular spaces with accurately cut pieces rather than scraps loosely packed into voids. Measure and cut a single piece for each irregular space where practical.
  6. Keep facing orientation and attachment consistent with the approved vapor-control design. Verify facing direction against the schedule before placing each batt.
  7. Do not staple facing in a manner that creates gaps or violates the specified installation. If stapling is required, place staples on the flange only, without pulling the facing away from the framing.

I. Clearances and Restricted Components

  1. Maintain clearances around non-IC lights, flues, chimneys, heat-producing devices, and other restricted components. Verify each component's listing and required clearance before insulating near it, and stop if the listing cannot be confirmed.
  2. Do not cover junction boxes or serviceable equipment. Leave every box, panel, and access point fully visible and reachable.

J. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect every cavity for top, bottom, edge, and back contact. Use a flashlight to check the back of each cavity, not just the visible front face.
  2. Correct gaps, compression, folded corners, slumping, or wrong-facing orientation. Remove and refit any batt that fails the inspection rather than patching over a deficiency.
  3. Photograph the completed wall before drywall. Capture each cavity clearly enough to verify fill quality from the photograph alone.
  4. Document batt product, cavity count, deficiencies, and corrections. Record this information on the project worksheet for use in the Chapter 19 final inspection.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review every completed cavity using the same checks regardless of which cavity type produced it.

  • Contact check: confirm full contact at the top plate, bottom plate, both stud edges, and the back of the cavity.
  • Compression check: confirm no batt is compressed below its manufactured thickness anywhere in the cavity.
  • Obstruction check: confirm wiring is split front and back, boxes are notched and visible, and pipes and bracing are cleanly fitted around.
  • Facing check: confirm facing orientation and attachment match the approved schedule in every cavity.
  • Clearance check: confirm no insulation contacts a restricted heat-producing component.
  • Documentation check: confirm every completed cavity is photographed and recorded on the worksheet.

Complete the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 8 or proceeding to concealment.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Batts Are Compressed Behind Electrical Boxes

Probable cause: loose fiberglass was packed behind the box to fill the space instead of notching the batt to the box outline.
Confirmation: pull back the facing and check whether the material behind the box is at full manufactured thickness or visibly compressed.
Correction: remove the compressed material, cut a properly notched piece that fits around the box at full thickness, and reinstall.
Reinspection: repeat the compression check on the corrected cavity and on any other box in the same wall section.

A Gap Appears at the Top or Bottom Plate

Probable cause: the batt was cut short of the measured cavity height, or it slumped after placement without being resupported.
Confirmation: measure the gap and compare it to the batt's cut length recorded on the worksheet.
Correction: remove the batt, cut a correctly sized replacement or add a fitted filler piece, and reinstall with full contact at both plates.
Reinspection: repeat the full-contact check at both plates for the corrected cavity.

A Batt Is Compressed Flat Against a Horizontal Wire Instead of Split

Probable cause: the installer pushed the batt past the wire instead of separating it into front and back layers.
Confirmation: check whether insulation exists on both sides of the wire at full thickness or only on one side with the wire pressed into the facing.
Correction: remove the batt, split it at the wire location, and reinstall so insulation lies both behind and in front of the cable.
Reinspection: repeat the wiring-fit check at the corrected location.

Facing Orientation Does Not Match the Approved Schedule

Probable cause: the installer assumed a facing direction based on general practice instead of checking the wall-insulation schedule for this specific assembly.
Confirmation: compare the installed facing direction against the schedule for every cavity in the affected section.
Correction: remove and reinstall any batt with incorrect facing orientation, matching the schedule exactly.
Reinspection: repeat the facing check across the full wall section before concealment.

Insulation Was Installed Before a Required Rough Inspection

Probable cause: the installer proceeded without confirming inspection status, assuming the rough work was already approved.
Confirmation: check inspection records or contact the responsible trade or inspector to confirm whether the required inspection occurred.
Correction: if the inspection was not completed, remove the insulation from the affected cavities, restore access for inspection, and reinstall only after the required inspection is confirmed and documented.
Reinspection: repeat the prerequisite check for the corrected cavities before reinstalling insulation.

Cleanup

This project ends with fully insulated, inspected, and documented cavities, not loose scraps and unlabeled photographs.

  • Collect and bag fiberglass scraps and packaging for disposal according to local waste requirements
  • Vacuum or sweep loose fibers from the work area, particularly the floor beneath cut cavities
  • Confirm no tools, staples, or cut-off pieces remain inside any completed cavity
  • Match every photograph to its corresponding cavity location on the worksheet
  • File the completed material and inspection records for use in Chapter 19

Field Terms

Cavity
The enclosed space between two adjacent studs that receives one batt or a set of accurately fitted pieces.
Full-Depth Contact
Condition in which the batt touches the top plate, bottom plate, both stud edges, and the back of the cavity without gaps.
Compression
Reduction of a batt's manufactured thickness caused by forcing it into an undersized space or packing material behind an obstruction.
Splitting
Separating a batt into front and back layers around a horizontal wire or pipe so insulation remains at full thickness on both sides.
Facing
The kraft paper, foil, or other membrane on one face of a batt, which may serve as the assembly's vapor retarder depending on the approved design.
Irregular Cavity
Any cavity containing blocking, bracing, multiple services, or a non-standard shape that a standard-width batt cannot fill without cutting.
Notching
Cutting a batt to fit around an obstruction such as an electrical box without compressing surrounding material.
Slumping
Downward settling or sagging of an improperly supported batt after installation, leaving a gap at the top of the cavity.

Project Closeout

You have completed the seventh project in the insulation and air-sealing sequence: installing fiberglass batt insulation in wall cavities.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to investigate a reported moisture complaint connected to insulation and produce a documented, evidence-based cause-and-correction report without performing hazardous remediation, without disturbing active damage, and without adding insulation before the moisture source is understood. The finished work product is a completed diagnostic record that identifies the probable moisture source, the confirmation method used, the required correction, the drying and reinspection plan, and any conditions requiring professional referral.

You will record the reported symptom and its conditions, review prior records, inspect for immediate hazards, distinguish liquid leakage from condensation and other moisture sources, inspect exterior water-control conditions, record temperature and humidity, inspect insulation and adjacent air-barrier and vapor-control layers, identify cold surfaces and air-leakage paths, check exhaust terminations, use diagnostic instruments only under approved conditions, and prepare recommendations in a fixed reporting format.

This project is diagnostic and does not authorize repair, insulation installation, or remediation. Chapter 18 stands between the earlier insulation and air-sealing chapters and the Chapter 19 final quality inspection: it exists specifically because insulation problems and moisture problems are frequently confused with one another, and because installing more insulation over an unresolved moisture source makes the underlying problem worse, not better.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-103 — Identify condensation signs and distinguish them from other moisture sources.
  • IN-104 — Identify roof-leak indicators and other liquid-water entry patterns.
  • IN-105 — Identify vapor-control problems, including discontinuity, wrong location, and double-layer entrapment.
  • IN-106 — Identify mold-risk conditions without performing remediation.
  • IN-107 — Document moisture sources with confirmation methods and supporting evidence.
  • IN-108 — Prepare corrective recommendations in the required symptom-to-correction format.

Where This Skill Fits

Every earlier chapter in this book depends on the System-Sequence Control principle that moisture sources must be corrected before insulation is installed. This chapter is where that principle becomes an active diagnostic skill rather than a background rule. The classification system used here — water-management repair, air sealing, insulation correction, ventilation change, humidity control, vapor-control redesign, drying, or professional remediation — feeds directly into the Chapter 19 final inspection, which checks whether every moisture problem was corrected and dried before concealment, and into the Chapter 20 capstone, which requires a completed moisture-readiness review before any insulation step begins.

This chapter also protects the learner from a common and costly error: treating a condensation problem as if it were a roof leak, or treating a vapor-control failure as if it were simply "not enough insulation." Each of those misdiagnoses leads to a different, and sometimes damaging, correction.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • recording a reported moisture symptom with its location, season, weather, occupancy, humidity, recent work, and duration
  • reviewing roof, plumbing, HVAC, ventilation, envelope, and insulation records relevant to the complaint
  • inspecting for immediate hazards requiring a stop-work referral
  • distinguishing liquid leakage, condensation, capillary rise, ground vapor, air-transported moisture, and construction moisture from one another
  • inspecting exterior water-control conditions, roof, flashing, gutters, grading, foundation, plumbing, ducts, and exhaust terminations
  • recording indoor and outdoor temperature and relative humidity under defined conditions
  • inspecting insulation, air-barrier, and vapor-control layers for wetting, discontinuity, and double-layer entrapment
  • using moisture meters, infrared imaging, probes, or data loggers only under approved methods
  • classifying corrective actions and preparing a symptom-to-correction recommendation report

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform mold remediation, sewage cleanup, structural repair, or removal of suspected hazardous material
  • add insulation, close cavities, or conceal any assembly before the moisture source and drying plan are resolved
  • make unsupported mold, health, or building-science claims not confirmed by direct evidence
  • work around electrical hazards, active roof leakage during a storm, unsafe structural conditions, or sewage contamination

Any condition that meets a stop-work criterion in Step 4 of the Work Order must halt further inspection of that area and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before this chapter's diagnostic work continues in that location.

Trade Note

A moisture diagnosis is only as good as its confirmation method. "I think it's condensation" is not a finding; "surface temperature at the stain was 4 degrees below the dew point at the time of inspection, and no roofing or flashing defect was found above the location" is a finding. Every recommendation in this chapter's report must trace back to a specific confirmation step you performed.

Setup Standard

Do not enter or extensively inspect an area with widespread visible mold, sewage contamination, standing water, active roof leakage during a storm, or unsafe electrical or structural conditions. Shop Tip

Ask about the timing of the symptom before you inspect anything. A stain that appears only during or immediately after rain points toward liquid leakage; a stain that appears during cold weather regardless of rain points toward condensation. That single question often narrows your inspection before you ever pick up an instrument.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every moisture diagnosis in this project moves from symptom to source to confirmation to correction. Recording a stain is not a diagnosis. A diagnosis requires ruling competing sources in or out using direct evidence, not assumption, before recommending a correction.

SymptomReported location, timing, season, and duration of the complaint
Source TypeLiquid leakage, condensation, capillary rise, ground vapor, air-transported, or construction moisture
ConfirmationDirect evidence: meter readings, temperature/humidity data, visual inspection, or instrument readings under approved conditions
CorrectionWater-management repair, air sealing, insulation correction, ventilation change, humidity control, vapor-control redesign, drying, or referral

Distinguishing Roof Leaks From Condensation

Roof-leak patterns typically follow gravity from a point of entry, correlate with rain events, and often show a defined path down framing or sheathing. Condensation patterns typically appear on the coldest available surface, correlate with temperature difference rather than rain, and often appear symmetrically at multiple similar locations, such as several roof nails or several corners of the same type. Neither pattern alone is conclusive; use both the reported timing and the physical pattern together before confirming a source.

Measurement Cue — Record Temperature and Humidity Under Defined Conditions

Record indoor and outdoor temperature and relative humidity at the time of inspection, and note the general weather conditions of the preceding several days. A single reading taken on a mild, dry day will not confirm or rule out a condensation-driven complaint that occurs only during cold weather; note this limitation on the worksheet when conditions at the time of inspection do not match the reported complaint conditions.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Emergency and Hazardous Conditions

Stop the inspection of any area with electrical hazards, structural damage, sewage contamination, active roof leakage during a storm, widespread visible mold, or other suspected hazardous materials. Do not touch, disturb, sample, or attempt to remediate any suspected hazardous condition. Record the area as a referral, state the specific hazard, and identify the qualified professional required before further inspection or any corrective work occurs.

Field Safety Cue — Attic, Crawlspace, and Ladder Safety During Diagnosis

Apply the same attic walk-board, crawlspace entry, and ladder safety practices used in Chapter 1 whenever this diagnosis requires entering those spaces. A moisture investigation does not reduce the fall, confined-space, or footing hazards present in the same locations during a standard inspection.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Diagnostic Instrument Limits

Operate moisture meters, infrared cameras, and data loggers only under the conditions specified by the manufacturer, including any required minimum temperature difference for infrared use. Document the instrument, the test conditions present, and a note that instrument readings are indicative and must be confirmed by direct inspection wherever practical.

D. Symptom Record and Records Review

  1. Record the reported symptom, location, season, weather, occupancy, humidity, recent work, and duration. Interview the owner or occupant directly and record their description in their own terms, then add your own observed description.
  2. Review roof, plumbing, HVAC, ventilation, envelope, and insulation records. Record the source and date of each document reviewed, and note if no records exist for a relevant system.

E. Hazard Screening and Stop-Work Decision

  1. Inspect for electrical hazards, structural damage, sewage, active roof leakage, widespread mold, or hazardous materials. Perform this screening before close-range inspection of the affected area.
  2. Stop work and refer emergency or remediation conditions. Record the area as a referral, state the specific hazard, and identify the required qualified professional before any further inspection of that location.

F. Source-Type Identification

  1. Identify whether moisture appears as liquid leakage, condensation, capillary rise, ground vapor, air-transported moisture, construction moisture, or unresolved. Use the Moisture-Source Classification Schedule to guide the initial hypothesis before confirming it with direct evidence.
  2. Inspect exterior water-control conditions above and outside the affected area. Walk the relevant exterior area and record any condition that could allow bulk water to reach the affected assembly.
  3. Inspect roof, flashing, gutters, grading, foundation, plumbing, ducts, and exhaust terminations where relevant. Record each condition inspected and whether it shows evidence of a defect.

G. Environmental and Assembly Inspection

  1. Record indoor and outdoor temperature and relative humidity under defined conditions. Note the date, time, and general weather pattern of the preceding several days alongside the reading.
  2. Inspect insulation for wetting, matting, compression, staining, displaced material, and odor. Record the specific location and extent of any affected insulation.
  3. Inspect adjacent air-barrier and vapor-control layers for discontinuity, wrong location, tears, or double-layer entrapment. Record any location where two low-permeance layers may be trapping moisture between them.
  4. Identify cold surfaces created by missing insulation, thermal bridges, wind washing, or compressed batts. Record the location of each cold surface found near the affected area.
  5. Identify air leakage paths carrying warm humid air to cold assemblies. Trace the path from its likely source, such as a bathroom, kitchen, or occupied space, to the cold surface where condensation appears.
  6. Check whether bathroom, kitchen, dryer, and HVAC exhausts terminate outdoors. Record the termination point of each exhaust system serving or near the affected area.

H. Instrumented Confirmation and Classification

  1. Distinguish roof-leak patterns from condensation patterns using location, weather history, and material condition. Compare the reported timing and the physical evidence pattern before assigning a source classification.
  2. Use moisture meters, infrared imaging, probes, or data loggers only under approved methods and document their limitations. Record the instrument, the test conditions present, and a note that readings are indicative rather than conclusive.
  3. Do not add insulation until the moisture source and drying plan are resolved. State this explicitly on the worksheet as a controlling condition for any dependent insulation chapter.

I. Reporting and Recommendations

  1. Classify corrective actions as water-management repair, air sealing, insulation correction, ventilation change, humidity control, vapor-control redesign, drying, or professional remediation. Assign one primary corrective classification per finding.
  2. Prepare recommendations in the format: symptom → probable source → confirmation → required correction → drying and reinspection. Write one complete line of this format for every distinct finding.
  3. Identify materials that require removal or professional evaluation. State the specific material, its location, and the professional required.
  4. Document uncertainty and avoid unsupported mold, health, or building-science claims. Where evidence is inconclusive, state that the source is unresolved and identify what additional monitoring or professional evaluation is required.
  5. Establish the reinspection condition before the assembly can be closed. State the specific condition, such as a dry moisture-meter reading at the same location after a stated interval, that must be met before any dependent insulation or air-sealing chapter proceeds.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed diagnostic report using the same checks regardless of which source type was found.

  • Symptom check: confirm the reported symptom includes location, season, weather, occupancy, humidity, recent work, and duration.
  • Classification check: confirm every finding has one primary moisture-source classification or is stated as unresolved with a reason.
  • Confirmation check: confirm every classified source is supported by a stated confirmation method and evidence.
  • Hazard check: confirm every stop-work condition was recorded as a referral rather than inspected further or disturbed.
  • Sequencing check: confirm the report does not recommend adding insulation before the moisture source and drying plan are resolved.
  • Format check: confirm every recommendation follows the required symptom-to-correction reporting format.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Condensation Problem Is Diagnosed and Corrected as a Roof Leak

Probable cause: the stain pattern was assumed to be a roof leak without comparing its timing to rain events or checking surface temperature against the dew point.
Confirmation: review the reported timing against local weather history and take a surface-temperature reading at the stain compared to the current dew point.
Correction: reclassify the finding based on the confirmed pattern, and revise the recommended correction to address air sealing, ventilation, or humidity control rather than roofing repair.
Reinspection: repeat the source-classification check on the corrected finding.

A Recommendation Calls for Adding Insulation Before the Moisture Source Is Resolved

Probable cause: the report was written area by area without checking each insulation-related recommendation against the unresolved-moisture control in Step 16.
Confirmation: compare every recommendation involving insulation against the list of unresolved moisture findings for the same assembly.
Correction: revise the recommendation to state the required correction and drying condition that must be met before insulation proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the sequencing check on the corrected report.

A Finding Has No Stated Confirmation Method

Probable cause: the finding was recorded based on visual impression alone, without a moisture-meter reading, temperature/humidity record, or other direct evidence.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for any classified finding without a confirmation method listed.
Correction: return to the location and take the appropriate measurement, or revise the finding to state that it is unresolved pending confirmation.
Reinspection: repeat the confirmation check across the full worksheet.

A Suspected Mold-Affected Area Was Inspected Too Closely or Disturbed

Probable cause: the hazard-screening step was not completed before close-range inspection, so the extent of visible mold was not recognized before approaching the material.
Confirmation: ask the inspector to describe the exact distance maintained and any contact that occurred with the material.
Correction: stop all further inspection of that area, record it as a referral with the specific condition described, and refer it to a qualified mold-remediation professional before any further entry.
Reinspection: confirm the referral entry is complete and that no further inspection of that area occurs until the referral is resolved.

The Report Makes an Unsupported Mold or Health Claim

Probable cause: the report extended beyond the direct evidence gathered, drawing a health or building-science conclusion that the inspection did not confirm.
Confirmation: review each claim in the report against the specific evidence recorded to support it.
Correction: remove or revise the unsupported claim, and state clearly what evidence exists and what remains unresolved or requires professional evaluation.
Reinspection: repeat the report-format check on the corrected document.

Field Terms

Liquid Leakage
Bulk water entering an assembly from roofing, flashing, plumbing, or grading defects.
Condensation
Moisture forming when air reaches its dew point at a cold surface.
Capillary Rise
Moisture drawn upward through a porous material from a wet source below.
Ground Vapor
Water vapor moving upward from exposed or poorly covered soil into a crawlspace or floor assembly.
Air-Transported Moisture
Water vapor carried by air leakage from a humid space to a cold surface, where it condenses.
Construction Moisture
Residual moisture from concrete, framing, or other materials installed wet, still drying from recent work.
Double-Layer Entrapment
Two low-permeance layers positioned so moisture is trapped between them without an approved hygrothermal design.
Confirmation Method
The specific measurement, instrument reading, or direct observation used to support a moisture-source classification.

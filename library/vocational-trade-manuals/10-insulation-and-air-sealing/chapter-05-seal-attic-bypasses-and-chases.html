Job Overview

This project qualifies you to block and air-seal accessible attic bypasses, open chases, dropped soffits, partition tops, and heat-source transitions identified during the Chapter 1 inspection. The finished work product is a continuous attic-plane air barrier at every assigned bypass, with rigid backing installed and sealed at large openings, partition tops and penetrations sealed with compatible product, chimney and flue transitions protected with approved noncombustible blocking and clearance, and every closure photographed and mapped before loose-fill insulation covers the work.

You will review the attic air-barrier plan and ventilation layout, establish safe attic access, identify open chases and heat-source penetrations, measure and install rigid backing over large bypasses, seal partition tops and penetrations, install noncombustible blocking and clearance at chimneys and flues, build insulation dams around required clearance zones, and inspect the completed attic plane from both sides where accessible.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 inspection record and, where applicable, on the framing-joint and service-penetration seals completed in Chapters 3 and 4. The bypass closures completed here must be finished and inspected before Chapter 6 baffles and Chapter 10 loose-fill insulation can proceed, because loose-fill material poured over an open bypass will fall through it and fail to air-seal the assembly.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-025 — Identify attic bypasses at chases, soffits, partition tops, and penetrations.
  • IN-026 — Block open chases using approved rigid air-barrier backing.
  • IN-027 — Seal around chimneys and flues using approved noncombustible materials and clearances.
  • IN-028 — Seal dropped soffits without trapping recessed fixtures or ducts.
  • IN-029 — Seal partition tops and associated penetrations with compatible sealant.
  • IN-030 — Inspect the completed attic air barrier for continuity, ventilation clearance, and heat-source protection.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 3 and 4 sealed framing joints and service penetrations at the wall and floor levels. This chapter completes the air barrier at the attic plane, where the largest and most consequential bypasses typically occur: open wall cavities that run straight from a wall stud bay into the attic, plumbing and duct chases, dropped soffits over cabinets, and the partition-top gap that runs the length of every interior wall.

Every bypass closure completed here must be finished, inspected, and photographed before Chapter 6 installs ventilation baffles and before Chapter 10 installs loose-fill attic insulation. Loose-fill material cannot correct an open bypass; it will simply fall through into the wall cavity below, wasting material and leaving the air leak in place. The clearances and noncombustible blocking installed here at chimneys and flues also establish the exclusion zones that Chapter 10's insulation dams must respect.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the attic air-barrier plan, ventilation layout, fireblocking requirements, and heat-source clearances identified in the chapter project specification
  • identifying open wall cavities, plumbing chases, duct shafts, dropped soffits, stair openings, partition tops, tub surrounds, and cabinet chases from safe attic access
  • identifying chimney, flue, fireplace, recessed-light, fan, and other heat-producing penetrations requiring clearance
  • measuring bypass openings and installing approved rigid air-barrier backing with mechanically supported edges
  • sealing partition-top joints and associated wire or pipe penetrations with the approved sealant
  • closing dropped-soffit openings at the attic plane without trapping recessed fixtures or ducts
  • installing approved noncombustible blocking and high-temperature sealant at chimneys and flues, and building insulation dams around required clearance zones
  • inspecting the completed attic air barrier from both sides where accessible and documenting all concealed closures

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • use combustible foam, caulk, wood blocking, or standard insulation within a prohibited fire-clearance zone at any chimney, flue, or heat-producing component
  • seal, block, or reduce a soffit-to-ridge ventilation channel, attic intake or exhaust vent, or any required ventilation path
  • disturb, sample, or work near vermiculite, suspected asbestos, active knob-and-tube wiring, or other stop-work hazardous conditions
  • install loose-fill or batt attic insulation; that work belongs to Chapters 10 and 11 and may not begin until this chapter's closures are inspected

Any condition meeting a stop-work criterion in Step 5 must halt work in that area and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before the bypass closure continues.

E. Reference Conventions

Attic plane: the horizontal air-barrier surface at the ceiling level of the attic, where bypasses are closed before loose-fill insulation is added. Bypass: any opening that allows air to move from a lower conditioned space into the attic without crossing a continuous air barrier. Clearance zone: the area around a heat-producing component within which insulation, foam, and combustible material are prohibited by the specified clearance distance. Insulation dam: a rigid barrier built to keep loose-fill insulation out of a clearance zone, ventilation path, or attic access area.

Trade Note

An attic bypass is often invisible from below and easy to underestimate from above, since warm, humid house air rising through a single unsealed chase can carry more heat loss than a dozen unsealed electrical outlets combined. Treat every chase, dropped soffit, and partition top on the Chapter 1 list as a required closure, not an optional one.

Shop Tip

Work the attic in the same order the Chapter 1 inspection recorded bypasses, starting at the area farthest from the attic access. This keeps your walk path clear of installed backing and dams and prevents you from having to step over completed work later in the project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every bypass closure in this project follows the same underlying logic: identify the opening, choose backing sized to bear on framing rather than float over the gap, seal the backing's perimeter continuously, and preserve any ventilation or clearance the opening was never supposed to interrupt. Heat-source transitions add a second requirement on top of the air-sealing requirement — every material used near a chimney, flue, or other heat-producing component must be noncombustible and installed at the specified clearance, with no exception for convenience.

Backing Versus Sealant Alone

A joint narrow enough for sealant to bridge on its own does not need rigid backing; an open chase or chute wide enough that sealant would simply sag or blow out does. Use the width and shape of each identified bypass, not convenience, to decide whether backing is required before sealant is applied. Do not use sealant or one-component foam as a substitute for structural backing at any opening the chapter project specification designates as requiring backing.

Critical Safety Cue — Chimney and Flue Clearance

Use only approved noncombustible blocking material and high-temperature sealant within the specified clearance distance of any chimney or flue. Never place combustible foam, caulk, membrane, wood blocking, or standard insulation inside a prohibited fire-clearance zone. Confirm the flue is not in active use and has cooled before working near it. If the clearance distance or flue condition cannot be confirmed, stop work at that location and refer it to a qualified professional.

Field Safety Cue — Attic Footing Near Open Chases

An open chase or chute is itself a fall hazard before it is closed. Identify and mark every open chase before beginning work in that area, and do not straddle or step near an open chase edge without confirming solid footing on a joist or walk board.

Code Alert — Ventilation Path Continuity

Do not seal, cover, or reduce a soffit-to-ridge ventilation path, attic intake vent, or exhaust vent while closing an unrelated bypass nearby. Confirm the location of every required ventilation opening before installing backing, and route backing edges and sealant away from ventilation paths accordingly. Verify local code requirements for attic ventilation, since minimum vent area and configuration vary by jurisdiction.

Measurement Cue — Bypass Opening Dimensions

Measure each bypass opening at its widest point in two directions before selecting backing size, and record the measurement alongside the location reference from the Chapter 1 inspection. Irregular openings should be measured at several points along their perimeter so the backing panel can be cut to the actual shape rather than a single averaged dimension.

IN-025 – IN-030

Air Sealing / Fire Protection / Inspection

Attic Bypass and Chase Sealing

Difficulty: Intermediate

Estimated Time: 4–7 hours

Estimated Cost: $80–$250

Finished Work: A continuous, documented attic air barrier at every assigned bypass, chase, soffit, and heat-source transition

A. Preparation — Plan Review and Safe Access

  1. Review the attic air-barrier plan, ventilation layout, fireblocking requirements, and heat-source clearances. Cross-reference this plan against the Chapter 1 inspection record for the specific attic being sealed, and confirm the chapter project specification identifies the approved backing, sealant, and blocking products.
  2. Establish safe attic access, walk boards, lighting, and fall-through protection. Confirm the Chapter 2 work-area setup remains in place, or re-establish it before beginning bypass work.

B. Identify Bypasses and Heat-Source Components

  1. Identify open wall cavities, plumbing chases, duct shafts, dropped soffits, stair openings, partition tops, tub surrounds, and cabinet chases. Confirm each item against its Chapter 1 location reference, and mark any bypass found during this walkthrough that was not previously recorded.
  2. Identify chimney, flue, fireplace, recessed-light, fan, and other heat-producing penetrations. Record the required clearance distance for each from the manufacturer instructions or adopted code, and mark the clearance zone boundary on the attic deck or framing.
  3. Stop work for vermiculite, damaged wiring, severe mold, active roof leakage, unsafe framing, or unknown flue conditions. Exclude the affected area, state the specific reason, and identify the qualified professional required before that area can be closed.

C. Mark Ventilation and No-Block Zones

  1. Mark required ventilation paths and no-block zones. Identify every soffit vent, ridge vent, and required ventilation opening in the work area, and mark a clear no-block boundary around each before installing any backing nearby.

D. Install Rigid Backing at Chases and Large Bypasses

  1. Measure each bypass opening and select approved rigid air-barrier backing. Use the Measurement Cue procedure above; select backing material and thickness as specified by the chapter project specification.
  2. Cut backing to fit without leaving unsupported edges. Add the overlap specified by the project specification so every edge bears on framing, blocking, or an adjacent supported surface.
  3. Install backing over open chases and large bypasses using mechanical support. Fasten according to the Fastener and Hardware Schedule, confirming full bearing on all sides before final fastening.
  4. Seal backing edges continuously to surrounding framing. Apply the specified sealant or foam around the full perimeter of each backing panel and tool it into contact with both surfaces.

E. Seal Partition Tops and Close Dropped Soffits

  1. Seal partition-top joints and wire or pipe penetrations using the approved product. Clean the joint of dust and debris, then apply a continuous bead along the entire assigned partition-top run, sealing each wire or pipe penetration individually where it interrupts the joint.
  2. Close dropped-soffit openings at the attic plane without trapping recessed fixtures or ducts. Confirm the location of any recessed light, duct, or fixture inside the soffit before cutting backing, and route the closure around it rather than covering it.

F. Protect Heat-Source Transitions

  1. At chimneys and flues, install only approved noncombustible blocking and high-temperature sealant. Confirm product listing and temperature rating against the manufacturer instructions before installation.
  2. Maintain the specified clearance from combustible material and foam. Verify the completed blocking does not encroach on the marked clearance zone at any point.
  3. Build insulation dams around required clearance zones. Construct dams tall enough and positioned to keep future loose-fill insulation out of every marked clearance zone, ventilation path, and access area.
  4. Preserve access to junction boxes, dampers, equipment, and attic paths. Confirm every access point identified in Chapter 1 remains reachable after dams and backing are installed.

G. Inspect and Document

  1. Inspect the attic plane from both sides where accessible. Where a bypass closure can be viewed from both the attic and an accessible interior space, confirm continuity from both vantage points.
  2. Use approved airflow or smoke observation only under safe test conditions. Confirm required test conditions are met before using a smoke pencil, and document the conditions alongside the result.
  3. Correct missed gaps, unsupported backing, blocked ventilation, or improper heat clearances. Revisit and correct any deficiency found during inspection before proceeding to documentation.
  4. Photograph and map all concealed bypass closures before loose-fill insulation. Label each photograph with the same location reference used in the Chapter 1 inspection record and this chapter's work order.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed attic before closing access or scheduling Chapter 6 or Chapter 10 work.

  • Coverage check: confirm every bypass, chase, soffit, and partition top on the Chapter 1 list has a corresponding closure.
  • Support check: confirm no backing panel has an unsupported edge.
  • Perimeter check: confirm every backing panel and partition-top run is sealed continuously.
  • Clearance check: confirm every chimney, flue, and heat-producing component maintains its specified clearance and uses only approved noncombustible materials.
  • Ventilation check: confirm no soffit vent, ridge vent, or required ventilation path was blocked by this project's work.
  • Access check: confirm junction boxes, dampers, equipment, and attic walk paths remain accessible.
  • Documentation check: confirm every closure has a labeled photograph and location reference.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Backing Panel Sags or Pulls Away From an Edge

Probable cause: the panel was cut without the specified overlap, leaving one or more edges unsupported by framing.
Confirmation: inspect every edge of the panel and confirm each bears on framing, blocking, or a supported adjacent surface.
Correction: remove the panel, recut it to the specified overlap dimension, and reinstall with full edge support before resealing.
Reinspection: repeat the support check on the corrected panel.

Sealant Bead Along a Partition Top Has Gaps or Skips

Probable cause: the substrate was not cleaned of dust before application, or the bead was applied too quickly to bridge minor irregularities in the joint.
Confirmation: visually inspect the full length of the joint under adequate lighting and press-test suspect areas for adhesion.
Correction: clean the gap, reapply sealant to the affected section, and tool it into contact with both sides of the joint.
Reinspection: repeat the perimeter check on the corrected section.

Insulation Dam or Backing Encroaches on a Chimney Clearance Zone

Probable cause: the clearance boundary was not marked before installation, or the installer estimated the clearance distance instead of measuring from the manufacturer instructions or adopted code.
Confirmation: measure the installed clearance distance directly against the specified requirement.
Correction: remove and reposition the encroaching material outside the marked clearance zone, using only approved noncombustible material for anything remaining inside the zone.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check and document the corrected measurement.

A Soffit Vent Is Found Blocked After Backing Installation

Probable cause: the ventilation path was not marked before backing was installed nearby, or the backing panel extended farther than measured.
Confirmation: inspect the vent from both the exterior and the attic side to confirm the blockage and its extent.
Correction: trim or reposition the backing to clear the ventilation path completely, resealing any new edge created by the correction.
Reinspection: repeat the ventilation check from both the exterior and attic side.

A Recessed Light or Duct Is Found Buried Inside a Closed Soffit

Probable cause: the soffit interior was not inspected for fixtures or ducts before the closure was cut and installed.
Confirmation: compare the Chapter 1 inspection record and any available plans against the installed closure to identify the fixture's expected location.
Correction: remove the closure, verify the fixture's listing and required clearance, and reinstall the closure routed around the fixture rather than over it.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage and clearance checks for the corrected soffit.

Field Terms

Bypass
An opening that allows air to move from a lower conditioned space into the attic without crossing a continuous air barrier.
Chase
A vertical shaft, such as a plumbing or duct chase, that runs from a lower floor into the attic.
Dropped Soffit
A built-down ceiling section, often over cabinets or a tub surround, that creates a hidden attic-connected cavity.
Partition Top
The continuous gap at the top of an interior partition wall where it meets the attic plane.
Clearance Zone
The area around a heat-producing component within which insulation, foam, and combustible material are prohibited by the specified clearance distance.
Insulation Dam
A rigid barrier built to keep loose-fill insulation out of a clearance zone, ventilation path, or attic access area.
Noncombustible Blocking
Approved fire-rated material used to close a bypass within a required fire-clearance zone.
Attic Plane
The horizontal air-barrier surface at ceiling level within the attic, where bypasses are closed before loose-fill insulation is added.

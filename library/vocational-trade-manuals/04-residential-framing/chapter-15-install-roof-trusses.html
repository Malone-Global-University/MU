Job Overview

This project qualifies you to receive, stage, set, brace, connect, and inspect a prefabricated roof-truss package on a designated structure. The finished work product is a complete truss roof system installed at the plan-specified spacing, seated fully on bearing, restrained by the required temporary bracing during erection, and connected and permanently braced according to the sealed truss documents.

You will obtain the sealed truss design drawings and placement diagram, confirm the structure was designed for a truss roof, inspect delivered trusses for damage and correct identification, stage and arrange the trusses in installation order, mark truss centers on the bearing walls, set and plumb the first truss with temporary bracing, erect the remaining trusses in sequence with continuous lateral restraint, install the required connectors and permanent bracing, and verify the completed system against the truss documents before it is closed in with roof sheathing.

This project follows Chapter 13, Frame Ceiling Joists, and depends on the wall system raised, plumbed, braced, and sheathed in Chapters 11 and 12. It is an alternate roof-framing method to the site-built rafter system in Chapter 14 and must not be combined with that system on the same roof. The completed truss roof becomes the structure that receives the roof sheathing installed in Chapter 16.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FR-085 — Receive, inspect, and stage a delivered truss package according to the supplier's handling instructions and the sealed truss documents.
  • FR-086 — Lay out truss spacing on the bearing walls from a common reference point and mark the location of every truss, including special trusses.
  • FR-087 — Set each truss on its marked location, seat it fully on bearing, and restrain it against twisting or rolling during erection.
  • FR-088 — Install and maintain the temporary bracing system required to hold trusses plumb, spaced, and stable until permanent bracing is complete.
  • FR-089 — Fasten truss connectors, clips, ties, hangers, bolts, and straps according to the approved connector schedule using listed fasteners.
  • FR-090 — Inspect the completed truss system for identification, placement, spacing, plumb, bearing, connectors, and bracing, and document the results.

Where This Skill Fits

The wall system raised, plumbed, connected, and sheathed in Chapters 11 and 12 exists to carry the loads this truss roof will impose. The truss centers marked on the top plates in this project must align with the wall framing and any concentrated-load points identified in the wall and truss documents. The temporary-bracing discipline introduced for individual walls in Chapter 11 is extended here into a continuous system across an entire roof plane, where an unbraced or improperly spaced truss can fail without warning during erection.

The completed and permanently braced truss system becomes the platform for the roof sheathing installed in Chapter 16, and the connector and load-path verification performed here is the same verification repeated at the capstone shed roof in Chapter 20 if a truss roof is selected for that structure.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining and reviewing the sealed truss design drawings, placement diagram, permanent-bracing information, and connector schedule
  • inspecting delivered trusses for identification, orientation, damage, broken plates, field alterations, and moisture exposure
  • staging trusses upright or flat only as permitted by the supplier
  • marking truss centers and setting, plumbing, and temporarily bracing each truss in sequence
  • installing the listed connectors and completing the permanent bracing shown on the truss documents
  • inspecting and documenting the completed truss system

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut, drill, notch, splice, or otherwise alter any truss member or metal connector plate
  • substitute a different truss, spacing, or connector for the one specified on the sealed truss documents
  • remove temporary bracing before permanent bracing required by the truss documents is complete
  • set trusses on a structure that was not designed for a truss roof, or combine truss framing with the site-built rafter system from Chapter 14 on the same roof without an engineered transition detail
  • direct a crane or mechanical lift without a qualified operator, an approved lift plan, and required signaling and exclusion-zone controls

Any damaged, unidentified, or field-altered truss must be quarantined and referred to the truss designer or manufacturer before it is used.

Trade Note

A truss carries its designed load only when every member, connector plate, and permanent brace shown on the sealed truss documents is present and unaltered. Unlike a site-built rafter, a truss cannot be field-modified to correct a spacing or fit problem. Every correction in this project comes from the truss designer, the manufacturer, or the approved documents — never from field judgment alone.

Setup Standard

Do not set any truss until the bearing walls, top plates, and wall bracing have passed the inspection required before truss loading (Step 3), and do not remove any temporary truss bracing until the permanent bracing shown on the truss documents is complete (Step 19). Trusses erected without the specified temporary bracing must be treated as unsafe until the bracing is installed.

Shop Tip

Mark truss centers on both bearing walls before the first truss is lifted, and mark every special truss location — girder, gable-end, valley, or attic — directly from the placement diagram rather than from memory. A single mislabeled special truss set in the wrong bay can require re-lifting an entire section of the roof.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every truss-erection sequence in this book, whether a common truss, girder truss, or gable-end truss, follows the same pattern: review the sealed documents, verify the supporting structure, inspect the delivered trusses, lay out and mark placement, set and temporarily brace each truss in sequence, install permanent bracing and connectors, and inspect and document the completed system. Learning that sequence here protects the roof framing that carries every later roofing and enclosure project in this book series.

Temporary Bracing and the Load Path

A single unbraced truss has no lateral stability and can roll or fall under its own weight or a light wind load. The temporary-bracing system specified by the truss documents ties trusses together as they are erected, so that the erected section resists lateral movement as a unit rather than relying on any single truss. Permanent bracing performs a different function: it holds individual truss members — webs and chords — in the position the design requires so the truss develops its rated capacity. Removing temporary bracing before permanent bracing is complete removes the only lateral restraint the roof has at that stage.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Remove Temporary Bracing Early

Do not remove or modify temporary truss bracing until the permanent bracing shown on the truss documents is installed and complete for that section of roof. A roof section that appears stable can collapse laterally if temporary bracing is removed before the permanent system is in place. Stop work and restore temporary bracing immediately if any is found loose, damaged, or missing.

Safety Cue — Lift Plan and Crew Communication

Follow the approved lift plan for every truss lift. Assign one person to direct the lift and to stop the operation immediately for loss of control, changing wind conditions, or any worker inside the exclusion zone. Use tag lines to control truss position and rotation; do not steady a suspended truss by hand.

Field Safety Cue — Working at Wall-Top and Truss Height

Use fall protection whenever working on wall tops, ladders, or truss top chords during erection and bracing. Keep the erection area clear of stored material, offcuts, and tripping hazards, and maintain a clear path for temporary-bracing installation at every truss.

Manufacturer Cue — Truss Members Are Not Field-Modified

Do not cut, drill, notch, splice, or otherwise alter any truss member or metal connector plate. If a truss is damaged, does not fit its marked location, or conflicts with another building component, stop work at that truss and obtain a repair or modification detail from the truss designer or manufacturer before proceeding.

E. Document Review and Delivery Inspection (Steps 1–5)

  1. Obtain the sealed truss design drawings, truss-placement diagram, permanent-bracing information, connector schedule, handling instructions, and approved building plans. Confirm the revision date of each document against the project's document-control log before using it.
  2. Confirm that the selected structure is designed for trusses and is separate from the site-built rafter project in Chapter 14. Verify against the approved building plans before any truss is staged.
  3. Verify bearing walls, top plates, wall bracing, dimensions, truss supports, and lifting access. Confirm the wall system has passed the inspection required before truss loading and record the confirmation on the truss-erection log.
  4. Inspect delivered trusses for identification, orientation, damage, broken plates, cuts, field alterations, and moisture exposure. Check each truss against its identification mark on the placement diagram as it is inspected.
  5. Quarantine damaged or unidentified trusses and obtain manufacturer or engineer direction. Tag each quarantined truss, record its identification and defect, and do not stage it for erection until direction is received.

F. Staging and Layout (Steps 6–9)

  1. Stage trusses upright or flat only as permitted by the supplier, with adequate support and restraint. Confirm the staging method matches the supplier's handling instructions before trusses are unloaded from the delivery vehicle.
  2. Arrange trusses in installation order according to the placement diagram. Sequence the staged trusses so each is accessible in the order it will be lifted.
  3. Mark truss centers on both bearing walls from a common reference point. Measure every truss-center mark from the same wall-top reference point identified in Section G below; do not measure sequentially from the previous mark.
  4. Mark girder trusses, gable-end frames, attic trusses, valley sets, and special trusses by location. Label each special-truss location directly from the placement diagram and confirm it does not conflict with the common-truss layout.

G. Lift Planning and First-Truss Erection (Steps 10–12)

  1. Review the lift plan, crane or mechanical equipment, tag lines, crew roles, exclusion zones, wind limits, and fall protection. Confirm every crew member's role and the location of the exclusion zone before the first lift.
  2. Set the first truss at the designated location. Align the truss with its marked centerline and seat it fully on bearing before releasing rigging tension.
  3. Plumb and brace the first truss using the approved temporary-bracing system. Install temporary bracing per the truss documents or supplier's standard detail before releasing the lifting equipment.

H. Sequential Erection and Restraint (Steps 13–16)

  1. Set the second and subsequent trusses in the prescribed sequence. Follow the order established in Step 7 and confirm each truss's identification against the placement diagram before it is lifted.
  2. Install continuous lateral restraint and diagonal bracing as trusses are erected. Do not allow any erected truss to stand without temporary lateral restraint, even briefly.
  3. Maintain specified spacing using approved gauges or temporary spacers. Check spacing at both the top chord and bottom chord of each truss as it is set.
  4. Seat each truss fully on bearing without twisting or rolling it. Verify full bearing contact and plumb before moving to the next truss.

I. Connectors and Permanent Bracing (Steps 17–20)

  1. Install clips, ties, hangers, bolts, straps, or other connectors using listed fasteners. Match connector type, quantity, and orientation to the approved connector schedule for each truss-to-wall and truss-to-truss connection.
  2. Install gable-end bracing and restraint according to the truss and building design. Complete gable-end vertical and lateral bracing before removing temporary bracing at that location.
  3. Complete permanent web and chord bracing shown on the truss documents. Install every permanent brace shown on the truss drawings before releasing any corresponding temporary brace.
  4. Do not cut, drill, notch, splice, or alter any truss member or metal plate. Refer any fit, damage, or conflict issue to the truss designer for a documented repair or modification detail.

J. Inspection and Documentation (Steps 21–24)

  1. String and inspect truss peaks, heels, tails, and bearing lines. Sight along the string line and record any truss that deviates beyond the tolerance specified by the truss documents.
  2. Verify truss identification, placement, spacing, plumb, bearing, connectors, temporary bracing, and permanent bracing. Complete the full acceptance-standards table in Section D for every truss before concealment.
  3. Obtain repair details from the truss designer for any damage or installation conflict. Log the conflict, the date reported, and the party contacted; do not conceal the affected truss until direction is received.
  4. Record truss documents, delivery condition, placement, connectors, repairs, and inspection results. Complete every required entry on the truss-erection log before roof sheathing begins.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the completed truss system using the same checks regardless of which section of the roof it covers.

  • Documentation check: confirm the sealed truss drawings, placement diagram, permanent-bracing information, and connector schedule are current and referenced on the truss-erection log.
  • Placement check: confirm every truss matches its identification mark and location on the placement diagram, including all special trusses.
  • Spacing and plumb check: confirm truss centers match the specified spacing and every truss is plumb within the stated tolerance.
  • Bearing check: confirm every truss is fully seated on its bearing wall or beam without twist or roll.
  • Connector and bracing check: confirm every connector matches the approved schedule and every permanent brace shown on the truss documents is installed before temporary bracing is removed.

Complete the Truss-Erection Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 16.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Truss Spacing Drifts Out of Tolerance Along a Run

Probable cause: spacing was measured sequentially from the previous truss rather than from the common reference point, allowing small errors to accumulate.
Confirmation: re-measure every truss center in the affected run from the common reference point identified in Step 8, and compare each mark against the placement diagram.
Correction: loosen temporary bracing only as needed to reposition the affected trusses to their correct marked centers, then re-install temporary bracing before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the spacing check across the full run after correction.

A Truss Will Not Seat Fully on Bearing

Probable cause: the bearing wall top plate is out of level, the truss heel is obstructed, or the wrong truss was set at that location.
Confirmation: verify the truss identification against the placement diagram, check the top plate for level at the bearing point, and inspect the heel area for obstructions.
Correction: if the wrong truss was set, remove and replace it with the correct identified truss; if the top plate is out of level, obtain direction before shimming, since an unauthorized shim can alter the bearing condition assumed by the truss design.
Reinspection: repeat the bearing check once the correction is confirmed.

A Truss Shows Damage Discovered After Erection

Probable cause: damage was not visible during the delivery inspection, or occurred during lifting or setting.
Confirmation: photograph and document the damage, its exact location on the truss, and the truss identification mark.
Correction: do not conceal, brace, or sheath over the damaged truss; contact the truss designer or manufacturer and obtain a documented repair detail before proceeding at that location.
Reinspection: re-inspect the repaired truss against the approved repair detail before permanent bracing and sheathing continue at that location.

Temporary Bracing Loosens or Fails During Erection

Probable cause: insufficient fastening at the bracing connection, undersized bracing lumber, or bracing spacing wider than the truss documents specify.
Confirmation: inspect every temporary-bracing connection in the affected run for fastener condition, bracing size, and spacing against the truss documents or supplier's standard detail.
Correction: stop erection in the affected area, re-fasten or replace the failed bracing, and add bracing where spacing exceeds the specified interval before resuming work.
Reinspection: re-verify plumb and stability of every truss in the affected run after bracing is corrected.

A Connector Does Not Match the Approved Schedule

Probable cause: an incorrect connector was installed, or a generic fastener was substituted for the listed fastener required by the connector.
Confirmation: compare the installed connector type, size, and fastener against the approved connector schedule for that specific connection.
Correction: remove the incorrect connector or fastener and install the specified listed connector and fastener; do not leave a mismatched connector in place.
Reinspection: repeat the connector check at that location and at any other location where the same substitution may have occurred.

Permanent Bracing Was Omitted Before Temporary Bracing Was Removed

Probable cause: the crew treated a plumb, connected truss run as complete without verifying every permanent brace shown on the truss documents was installed.
Confirmation: compare the installed bracing against the permanent-bracing information from the truss documents, member by member, for the affected section.
Correction: re-install temporary bracing at the affected section if it was removed, then install every missing permanent brace before removing temporary bracing again.
Reinspection: repeat the full bracing check for the affected section before proceeding to roof sheathing.

Cleanup

This project ends with a complete, braced, connected, and inspected truss roof system, not with roof sheathing installed.

  • Remove rigging, tag lines, and lifting equipment from the site or return them to staging as directed by the lift plan
  • Clear offcuts, packaging, and loose fasteners from the wall tops and ground-level staging area
  • Confirm every temporary brace remaining in place is intact, fastened, and marked so it is not mistaken for scrap
  • Store the signed truss-erection log, inspection checklist, and supporting photographs with the project's permanent documentation
  • Communicate any open damage report or repair detail to the truss designer, manufacturer, or general contractor before Project 16 begins

Project Closeout

This project required you to receive, inspect, and stage the delivered truss package (FR-085), lay out truss spacing and mark every truss location (FR-086), set and seat each truss on bearing (FR-087), install and maintain the required temporary bracing system (FR-088), fasten the specified connectors using listed fasteners (FR-089), and inspect and document the completed truss system (FR-090).

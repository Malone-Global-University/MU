Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build the wall-corner and intersecting-wall assemblies used throughout this book's exterior and interior framing, and to install the specific configuration required by the current structure. The finished work product is a set of inspected practice corner and intersection assemblies — when the approved plan calls for a comparison exercise — plus the actual corner and intersection framing installed in the designated exterior walls and interior partitions of the current project.

You will review the approved corner, intersection, insulation, backing, load-path, and sheathing details; prepare labeled stock for a three-stud corner, a California corner, and an intersecting-wall backing assembly when the plan calls for all three as a comparison exercise; dry-fit and fasten each assembly; inspect each for fastening, alignment, backing, cavity access, and member damage; then identify and install the plan-required corner and intersection types in the actual walls of the current structure, bringing adjoining walls into full contact, fastening the connections, and verifying plumb, straightness, square, and stable bracing.

This project follows Project 07, Frame an Exterior Wall Section, and depends on the plates, studs, and squared wall sections built there. It also depends on the framing baseline and wall lines established in Project 01. The corner and intersection work completed here must be finished and inspected before rough openings are framed in Project 09, because king studs, jack studs, and header bearing in that project are measured from the corner and intersection framing installed here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FR-043 — Build a three-stud corner assembly with full bearing, flush faces, and the approved fastening schedule.
  • FR-044 — Build a California corner assembly that preserves the insulation cavity and interior drywall-backing surface.
  • FR-045 — Frame an intersecting wall using the approved backing configuration, such as ladder blocking or an added backing stud.
  • FR-046 — Provide drywall backing at every corner and intersection so that interior finish materials have continuous fastening surface.
  • FR-047 — Align adjoining walls at corners and intersections so that wall faces, top plates, bottom plates, and sheathing planes are flush and plumb.
  • FR-048 — Inspect corner and intersection assemblies for fastening, alignment, backing, cavity access, and member damage, and correct authorized deficiencies.

Where This Skill Fits

The exterior wall section built in Project 07 is not a complete building until its corners tie into the adjoining wall and its intersections connect to interior partitions. A corner assembly that is undersized, unbacked, or out of alignment creates a weak point in the wall system, a missing surface for drywall fastening, and a visible defect in the finished wall plane. This project isolates corner and intersection framing as its own skill so that every later wall, from Chapter 9's rough openings through the Chapter 20 capstone shed, ties into a corner system that has already been inspected and verified.

The three-stud corner, California corner, and intersecting-wall backing methods taught here are the same methods used at every subsequent corner and intersection in this book, including the capstone shed corners in Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the approved corner, intersection, insulation, backing, load-path, and sheathing details for the current structure
  • identifying which corner and intersection assemblies are practice comparisons and which assembly is required in the actual structure
  • building, fastening, and inspecting a three-stud corner, a California corner, and an intersecting-wall backing assembly when the plan requires the comparison exercise
  • identifying the plan-required corner and intersection types for the current wall system
  • installing the required corner assemblies in the designated exterior walls
  • positioning, fastening, and aligning intersecting interior walls at their layout lines
  • verifying interior finish backing, insulation access, plumb, straightness, and square at every completed corner and intersection

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • substitute a corner or intersection type other than the one specified on the approved plan
  • force an adjoining wall out of plumb to bring a corner or intersection into contact
  • omit drywall backing at any corner or intersection, even when a practice assembly is not required
  • remove primary temporary bracing from either wall while bringing corners or intersections together

Any corner or intersection condition that does not match the approved plan, or that reveals a defect in the wall sections built in Project 07, must be logged and referred to the responsible design professional or general contractor before the corner or intersection is fastened as final.

Trade Note

A three-stud corner and a California corner accomplish the same structural task — a full-bearing exterior corner — using different amounts of framing lumber and different insulation-cavity access. Neither corner type is universally correct. The approved plan controls which type is used at each corner, and the two types are not interchangeable within the same designated corner.

Setup Standard

Do not fasten any corner or intersection as final in the actual structure until the adjoining wall sections have passed the square, plumb, and stability verification required at the end of Project 07. A corner built from an unverified wall section must be treated as unverified until that wall's inspection is confirmed.

Shop Tip

Label the interior face, exterior face, and drywall-backing surface on every corner and intersection component before assembly. Once studs are fastened together in a corner configuration, the wrong face is difficult to identify without disassembling the corner.

Corner Bearing and Backing Requirements

A structural corner must provide full bearing for both intersecting wall sections and a continuous nailing surface for interior finish material on the interior face of both walls. The three-stud corner achieves this with solid lumber on all three bearing faces. The California corner achieves the same bearing and backing requirement with horizontal blocking rather than a solid third stud, which leaves the corner cavity open for insulation. Both methods are acceptable only where the approved plan specifies that method for that location; neither method may be substituted for the other without an approved plan revision.

Measurement Cue — Match the Controlled Wall Height

Cut every corner and intersection component to the same controlled wall height or practice-module dimension used for the wall section it belongs to. A corner component cut to a different length than its adjoining wall studs will throw the top plate out of level or leave a bearing gap at the bottom plate.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Force Plumb Walls Out of Alignment

Do not force an adjoining wall out of plumb to close a gap at a corner or intersection. Stop work, re-verify the plumb and layout of both wall sections, and correct the corner or intersection framing rather than the wall position. Forcing a plumb wall out of alignment to close a corner gap creates a hidden defect that later trades will not detect.

Field Safety Cue — Working at Braced Wall Corners

Keep clear of the area directly behind a temporarily braced wall while working at its corner or intersection. Do not remove or loosen a primary temporary brace to gain access; reposition secondary bracing only, and restore any brace removed for access before leaving the area.

Code Alert — Backing Requirements Vary by Finish Material

The backing spacing and fastening surface required at corners and intersections can vary with the interior finish material and the adopted code. Verify the approved backing detail before assuming a single backing method applies to every wall in the structure.

E. Document Review and Assembly Identification (Steps 1–3)

  1. Review the approved corner, intersection, insulation, backing, load-path, and sheathing details. Confirm the revision date of each document against the project's document-control log before using it.
  2. Identify which assemblies are practice comparisons and which assembly is required in the actual structure. Record this determination on the wall-framing record before preparing any stock.
  3. Prepare labeled stock for a three-stud corner, California corner, and intersecting-wall backing assembly when all are required. Cut extra stock only if the plan requires all three comparison assemblies; otherwise prepare stock only for the designated type.

F. Comparison Assembly Construction (Steps 4–12)

  1. Mark reference faces, inside faces, exterior faces, and drywall-backing surfaces. Label each face on every component before assembly begins.
  2. Cut components to the controlled wall height or practice-module dimension. Use the same length established for the adjoining wall studs in Project 07.
  3. Dry-fit the three-stud corner and verify full bearing and flush faces. Confirm no gap is detectable with a straightedge at either bearing face.
  4. Fasten the three-stud corner using the approved schedule. Follow the fastener type, spacing, and quantity specified in Section C.
  5. Dry-fit the California corner and verify the insulation cavity and interior backing surface. Confirm the cavity remains open per the approved detail and the backing blocks provide a continuous interior nailing surface.
  6. Fasten the California corner using the approved schedule. Follow the fastener type, spacing, and quantity specified in Section C.
  7. Prepare the approved intersecting-wall configuration, such as ladder blocking or an added backing stud. Confirm the configuration matches the plan-specified backing detail for interior-wall intersections.
  8. Install blocking or backing at the specified spacing and orientation. Verify spacing against the approved backing detail before fastening.
  9. Inspect each practice assembly for fastening, alignment, backing, cavity access, and member damage. Log the inspection result for each assembly on the wall-framing record.

G. Installation of the Designated Corner and Intersection (Steps 13–19)

  1. Identify the plan-required corner and intersection types for the current wall system. Confirm each designated location against the approved plan before installation.
  2. Install the required corner assemblies in the designated exterior walls. Position each corner assembly per Section F and confirm it matches the type identified in Step 13.
  3. Position intersecting walls on their layout lines. Use the wall lines established in Project 01 to confirm the intersecting wall's location before bringing it into contact with the receiving wall.
  4. Bring adjoining wall ends into full contact without forcing either wall out of plumb. Re-verify plumb on both walls before fastening; stop and correct framing rather than forcing alignment.
  5. Fasten wall intersections using the approved connection schedule. Follow the fastener type, spacing, and quantity specified in Section C.
  6. Align wall faces, top plates, bottom plates, and sheathing planes. Confirm alignment with a straightedge at each corner and intersection.
  7. Install required top-plate laps, straps, or tie details. Install only the laps, straps, or ties specified by the approved plan for each corner and intersection.

H. Verification and Documentation (Steps 20–23)

  1. Verify interior finish backing and insulation access remain available. Confirm every corner and intersection provides the backing and cavity access required by the approved detail.
  2. Recheck adjoining walls for plumb, straightness, square, and stable bracing. Use the same diagonal and plumb checks established in Project 07.
  3. Correct gaps, offset faces, missing backing, split members, or incomplete connections. Re-inspect any corrected joint before proceeding.
  4. Record the corner types, intersection details, fastening, and inspection results. Enter every finding on the wall-framing record before Project 09 begins.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect every completed corner and intersection using the same checks regardless of which wall section it belongs to.

  • Type check: confirm the installed corner or intersection matches the type designated on the approved plan for that location.
  • Bearing check: confirm full bearing contact at every corner face with no gap detectable by straightedge.
  • Backing check: confirm a continuous drywall-fastening surface is present at every corner and intersection.
  • Alignment check: confirm wall faces, top plates, bottom plates, and sheathing planes are flush and plumb within the approved tolerance.
  • Fastening check: confirm every joint matches the approved fastener type, quantity, and spacing with no split or damaged members.

Complete the Corner and Intersection Framing Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 09.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Gap at the Corner Bearing Face

Probable cause: a corner component was cut to an incorrect length, or the adjoining wall section was not plumb before the corner was fastened.
Confirmation: measure each corner component against the controlled wall height, and check both adjoining wall sections for plumb with a 4-foot level.
Correction: replace an incorrectly sized component before fastening; if a wall section is out of plumb, correct the wall rather than forcing the corner into contact.
Reinspection: repeat the bearing and alignment check with a straightedge after correction.

Missing Drywall Backing at a Corner or Intersection

Probable cause: a backing block, backing stud, or ladder blocking component was omitted or installed at the wrong spacing.
Confirmation: compare the installed backing against the approved backing detail for type, spacing, and orientation.
Correction: install the missing backing component per the approved detail and fastening schedule before the corner or intersection is treated as complete.
Reinspection: repeat the backing check for the corrected corner or intersection.

California Corner Cavity Is Blocked or Inaccessible

Probable cause: a backing block or stud was installed in the wrong location, closing off the insulation cavity the California corner is designed to preserve.
Confirmation: compare the installed backing block locations against the approved California corner detail.
Correction: remove and relocate the incorrectly placed backing block to the approved location without reducing required bearing or backing elsewhere.
Reinspection: repeat the cavity-access and backing check after correction.

Wall Faces Are Not Flush at a Corner or Intersection

Probable cause: one wall section's stud spacing or plate alignment shifted during raising, or a corner component was assembled with an incorrect reference face.
Confirmation: check the labeled reference faces on the corner components, and re-verify each wall section's plumb, straightness, and square from Project 07.
Correction: reposition the corner or intersection framing to the correct reference face; if a wall section itself is out of square or plumb, correct the wall section before re-fastening the corner.
Reinspection: repeat the wall-face alignment check with a straightedge after correction.

Installed Corner or Intersection Type Does Not Match the Plan

Probable cause: the designated type for the location was not confirmed before construction began, or a comparison assembly was mistakenly installed as the final corner.
Confirmation: re-check the approved plan for the designated corner or intersection type at that specific location.
Correction: remove the incorrect assembly and rebuild the corner or intersection using the plan-designated type and its approved fastening schedule; do not leave a comparison assembly installed as final framing.
Reinspection: repeat the type check, bearing check, and backing check for the rebuilt corner or intersection.

Field Terms

Three-Stud Corner
An exterior corner assembly using three full-length studs to provide full bearing and interior backing at both walls.
California Corner
An exterior corner assembly using backing blocks in place of a solid third stud to preserve an open insulation cavity.
Intersecting-Wall Backing
A backing stud, ladder blocking, or other approved member providing continuous drywall-fastening surface at an interior wall intersection.
Reference Face
The interior, exterior, or backing face of a component identified and marked before assembly.
Practice Comparison Assembly
A corner or intersection assembly built to demonstrate a method, distinct from the type actually installed in the structure.
Designated Corner or Intersection
The specific corner or intersection type identified on the approved plan for a given location.
Ladder Blocking
Horizontal blocking installed between a backing stud and the adjoining wall framing to provide drywall backing at an intersection.
Insulation Cavity
The open space within a wall corner or cavity intended to receive insulation material.

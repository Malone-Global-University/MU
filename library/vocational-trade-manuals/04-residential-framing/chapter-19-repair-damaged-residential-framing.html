Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete one approved residential framing repair from initial damage assessment through final inspection and closeout. The finished work product is a documented repair: damaged framing safely supported, exposed, and removed only to the extent required; a replacement or sistered member installed under an approved repair detail; all disturbed blocking, bridging, sheathing, and fire blocking reinstated; and a signed repair record with photographs and inspection results.

You will define the damage and its immediate safety concerns, restrict access and stop loading the affected area, document the condition, determine whether the member requires engineering direction, obtain an approved repair detail, install temporary support, remove the damaged material, install and fasten the replacement or sister member, transfer load back to the repaired framing under observation, and complete required inspection before concealment.

This project follows the stair-framing projects in Chapters 17 and 18 and precedes the Chapter 20 capstone. It draws on the temporary-support, connector-installation, and inspection skills built throughout this book, and it applies them to existing framing rather than new construction, where hidden conditions, occupied space, and prior damage add constraints that new-construction projects do not have.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FR-109 — Identify damaged framing, document the condition with photographs and field notes, and determine whether the member requires engineering direction before repair.
  • FR-110 — Plan and install temporary support that transfers load away from the damaged member without damaging finishes, utilities, or adjacent framing.
  • FR-111 — Remove damaged connectors, blocking, sheathing, or member sections in the sequence and extent specified by the approved repair detail.
  • FR-112 — Prepare and install a sistered or replacement framing member fitted to the required bearing, overlap, and contact surfaces specified by the approved detail.
  • FR-113 — Reinforce the repaired connection with the specified fasteners, bolts, plates, straps, or hangers and reinstate disturbed blocking, bridging, and fire blocking.
  • FR-114 — Inspect the completed repair for movement, cracking, connector distress, and restored load path, and document the repair through required inspection and closeout.

Where This Skill Fits

Every earlier chapter in this book installs new framing under controlling plans written before the first cut is made. This chapter is different: the learner arrives after the framing already exists, already carries load, and already has a defect. The documentation, temporary-support, and load-transfer sequence taught here is the sequence a framer uses whenever existing structure must be opened, repaired, or reinforced — including future work on additions, remodels, and post-event damage assessment.

The connector-installation and inspection habits built in Chapters 10 through 16 apply directly here: a sistered joist or replacement header is only as good as its bearing, its fastening schedule, and its documented inspection, exactly as with new construction.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • documenting one instance of damaged residential framing and its immediate safety concerns
  • determining, from stated criteria, whether the condition requires engineering direction before any repair proceeds
  • planning and installing temporary support under qualified direction
  • removing damaged material only to the extent required and only in the approved sequence
  • installing one replacement or sistered member under a supplied approved repair detail
  • reinstating disturbed blocking, bridging, sheathing, and fire blocking
  • transferring load back to the repaired framing under observation and completing required inspection and documentation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a repair method, member size, fastening schedule, or connector without an approved repair detail supplied by the plans, prescriptive code provisions applicable to the condition, the product manufacturer, or a qualified structural professional
  • repair engineered members, truss members, fire damage, widespread decay, foundation-related displacement, or any condition involving major structural movement without first obtaining engineering direction
  • remove temporary support before receiving written or documented release
  • conceal a repair before required structural or jurisdictional inspection is complete

Any condition involving an uncertain load path, active water intrusion, active insect infestation, or occupant safety risk must stop work in that area until direction is obtained from a qualified professional or the authority having jurisdiction.

Trade Note

A framing repair is only as reliable as the load path it restores. Every step in this chapter exists to protect that load path: from the moment the damaged member is identified until the moment temporary support is released, someone must always be able to state, in specific terms, where the load is currently being carried.

Setup Standard

Do not remove any damaged member, connector, blocking, or sheathing until temporary support is installed, verified stable, and confirmed to be carrying the intended load (Steps 9–10). Do not proceed with a repair of uncertain scope, an engineered member, a truss member, or a condition involving major structural movement without documented engineering direction (Step 5).

Shop Tip

Photograph the damaged condition from at least three angles, including one wide shot that shows the member's location relative to identifiable building features, before installing any temporary support. Once shoring, cribbing, or replacement material is in place, the original damage is harder to document clearly.

Prerequisites

  • An identified area of damaged residential framing accessible for inspection, documentation, and repair
  • An approved repair detail, or a confirmed path to obtain one from a qualified structural professional, before material is removed
  • Shoring posts, beams, cribbing, or other temporary-support material rated for the anticipated load
  • Replacement or sister lumber and the specified fasteners, bolts, plates, straps, or hangers matching the approved detail
  • A repair record and photograph log available for field entries
  • A means to contact the responsible design professional, general contractor, or authority having jurisdiction for conditions outside this project's authorized scope

Common Field Fundamentals

Every framing repair in residential work follows the same sequence regardless of which member is damaged: define and document the condition, determine whether engineering direction is required, install verified temporary support, remove only what the approved detail requires, install the repair to the specified bearing and fastening pattern, transfer load back under observation, and document the completed, inspected repair. Learning that sequence here, on one supplied repair, prepares the learner to apply it correctly to future conditions that this book cannot anticipate in advance.

Temporary supportShoring installed, verified stable, and confirmed carrying load
Repair installationDamaged material removed; sister or replacement member fastened per approved detail

Damage Assessment and the Engineering-Direction Threshold

Not every repair in this project can be completed from a prescriptive detail. A member is structural if it carries floor, wall, roof, or lateral load; it is part of a lateral system if it participates in a braced wall line or shear wall; and it is engineered if it is an engineered wood product, prefabricated truss, or manufacturer-rated component. Any of these conditions, combined with major movement, fire damage, widespread decay, or foundation-related displacement, requires a stop-work condition and engineering direction before repair proceeds.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Uncertain Load Paths

Stop work immediately and obtain engineering direction for uncertain load paths, major structural movement, truss damage, engineered-member damage, fire damage, widespread decay, or foundation-related displacement. Do not remove any damaged material or install a repair under these conditions using a generic or assumed detail.

Safety Cue — Restrict Access and Stop Loading

Restrict access to the affected area and stop applying load — foot traffic, stored material, furniture, or equipment — as soon as damage is identified. Maintain the restriction until temporary support is installed and verified.

Field Safety Cue — Utilities and Finishes

Identify wiring, piping, ducts, and finishes near the damaged member before planning temporary support or removal. Protect these systems throughout the repair, and do not cut, drill, or displace a utility without confirming it is de-energized, depressurized, or otherwise made safe by a qualified person.

Code Alert — Structural and Jurisdictional Inspection Requirements Vary

The requirement for permits and for structural or jurisdictional inspection before concealment varies by jurisdiction, occupancy, and the extent of the repair. Verify current requirements with the authority having jurisdiction before concealing any repaired framing.

Measurement Cue — Overlap and Bearing Come From the Approved Detail

Measure sister-member overlap, replacement-member bearing length, and fastener spacing directly from the approved repair detail. Do not estimate these values from the appearance of the surrounding framing or from a similar repair completed elsewhere.

C. Repair Materials and Consumables

  • Temporary shoring: posts, beams, and cribbing sized and rated per the temporary-support plan for the anticipated load.
  • Repair lumber: replacement or sister material matching the species, grade, size, and treatment specified by the approved repair detail.
  • Fasteners and connectors: bolts, structural screws, nails, plates, straps, or hangers exactly as specified by the approved detail; no substitution without a documented revision.
  • Restoration materials: blocking, bridging, fire-blocking, and sheathing stock sufficient to reinstate everything disturbed during the repair.
  • Documentation: one repair record and photograph log per repair, including the original condition, approved detail, and inspection results.

E. Assessment and Access Control (Steps 1–6)

  1. Define the repair location, observed damage, affected building systems, occupancy condition, and immediate safety concerns. Record the location by plan reference or a field-assigned identification number on the repair record.
  2. Restrict access and stop loading the affected area. Install a physical barrier or signage and remove stored material, furniture, or equipment from the affected area.
  3. Photograph and document cracks, decay, fire damage, insect damage, splits, cuts, holes, deflection, movement, failed connectors, and moisture sources. Take at least three photographs per condition, including one wide reference shot, and log each with location and date.
  4. Determine whether the member is structural, load-bearing, engineered, part of a lateral system, or connected to regulated utilities. Record this determination and its basis on the repair record before proceeding.
  5. Stop work and obtain engineering for uncertain load paths, major movement, truss damage, engineered-member damage, fire damage, widespread decay, or foundation-related displacement. Do not proceed past this step without a documented engineering determination when any of these conditions is present.
  6. Identify and correct or isolate the source of water, insects, impact, overload, or other deterioration before closing the repair. Record the identified source and the correction or isolation method used.

F. Temporary Support Planning and Installation (Steps 7–10)

  1. Obtain the approved repair detail specifying temporary support, member material, length, bearing, fasteners, connectors, and sequence. Confirm the detail matches the specific damage condition documented in Section E.
  2. Plan temporary support so loads are transferred to adequate bearing without damaging finishes or utilities. Identify the bearing surface, required post or beam size, and cribbing needed to distribute the load.
  3. Install shoring, posts, beams, cribbing, or other temporary supports under qualified direction. Set each support plumb, on adequate bearing, and at the spacing specified by the temporary-support plan.
  4. Verify temporary supports are stable and carrying the intended load before removing material. Check for plumb, tightness, and bearing contact, and confirm no deflection occurs when the support is loaded.

G. Removal of Damaged Material (Steps 11–13)

  1. Expose the damaged member only to the extent required for inspection and repair. Remove only the finish or sheathing material identified in the approved detail.
  2. Protect wiring, piping, ducts, finishes, and occupied areas. Cover, relocate, or de-energize systems as required before cutting or demolition begins.
  3. Remove damaged connectors, blocking, sheathing, or member sections in the approved sequence. Cut and remove material only at the locations and to the extent specified by the approved repair detail.

H. Repair Installation and Load Transfer (Steps 14–19)

  1. Prepare replacement or sister members from the specified material. Cut each member to the length, bearing, and orientation specified by the approved detail.
  2. Fit the repair member to the required bearing, overlap, and contact surfaces. Dry-fit the member and confirm its dimensions match the approved detail before fastening.
  3. Clamp or temporarily secure the repair without forcing the structure out of alignment. Verify surrounding framing remains in its documented position while the repair member is clamped.
  4. Install the specified fasteners, bolts, adhesive, plates, straps, or hangers in the required pattern. Follow the exact type, size, spacing, and installation direction stated in the approved detail.
  5. Reinstate blocking, bridging, sheathing, fire blocking, and lateral restraint affected by the repair. Restore each disturbed assembly to its original specification before load transfer begins.
  6. Transfer load from temporary support to the repaired framing gradually and under observation. Release temporary support incrementally while observing the repaired member for movement or distress.

I. Inspection and Closeout (Steps 20–24)

  1. Inspect for movement, cracking, connector distress, gaps, or loss of bearing. Complete this inspection immediately after load transfer and again after a defined observation period if required by the approved detail.
  2. Verify repair dimensions, overlap, bearing, fastener installation, alignment, and restored load path. Compare the completed repair against the approved detail and the Acceptance Standards table.
  3. Complete required structural or jurisdictional inspection before concealment. Schedule and pass the applicable inspection before installing finish material over the repair.
  4. Remove temporary support only after written or documented release. Confirm the release authority and date on the repair record before removing any shoring.
  5. Record the original condition, approved detail, materials, fasteners, photographs, inspection, and closeout. Complete and sign the repair record before the project is considered closed.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the completed repair using the same checks regardless of which member or damage type is involved.

  • Documentation check: confirm the original condition, engineering-direction determination, and approved repair detail are all recorded on the repair record.
  • Temporary-support check: confirm shoring was installed, verified stable, and carrying load before any damaged material was removed, and that release was documented before removal.
  • Removal-extent check: confirm only the material specified by the approved detail was removed.
  • Repair-member check: confirm bearing, overlap, fasteners, and connectors match the approved detail exactly.
  • Restoration check: confirm all disturbed blocking, bridging, fire blocking, and sheathing were reinstated.
  • Post-transfer check: confirm no new movement, cracking, or connector distress was observed after load transfer.

Complete the Repair Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before concealing the repaired area.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Temporary Support Deflects or Shifts Under Load

Probable cause: undersized shoring, inadequate bearing beneath the shoring, or a support that was not installed plumb.
Confirmation: re-check the shoring against the temporary-support plan for size and bearing area, and verify each post is plumb and fully seated.
Correction: do not proceed with removal of damaged material; add additional shoring, cribbing, or bearing area as directed by qualified supervision until the support is stable and carrying the intended load without deflection.
Reinspection: repeat the temporary-support verification before continuing to Section G.

More Damage Is Found Than the Approved Detail Anticipated

Probable cause: the original assessment did not capture the full extent of decay, cracking, or prior damage, which was only discovered after exposing the member.
Confirmation: document the newly discovered condition with photographs and compare it against the scope of the approved repair detail.
Correction: stop work and obtain a revised repair detail or additional engineering direction; do not extend the removal or repair beyond the original approved scope without a documented revision.
Reinspection: repeat the assessment steps in Section E for the newly discovered condition before proceeding.

Sister or Replacement Member Does Not Fit the Required Bearing or Overlap

Probable cause: the repair member was cut to an incorrect length, or the existing framing dimensions differ from the approved detail's assumed dimensions.
Confirmation: re-measure the repair member and the existing bearing and overlap surfaces against the approved detail's stated dimensions.
Correction: recut or replace the repair member to match the approved detail; if the existing framing dimensions genuinely differ from the detail's assumptions, obtain a documented revision before installing the repair.
Reinspection: repeat the dry-fit check before clamping and fastening.

New Movement, Cracking, or Connector Distress Appears During Load Transfer

Probable cause: the repair does not yet carry the intended load, a fastener or connector was not fully seated, or an unanticipated condition affects the load path.
Confirmation: stop the load-transfer process immediately, re-install or restore temporary support, and inspect every fastener, connector, and bearing surface on the repair.
Correction: correct any identified installation deficiency; if the cause is not clearly an installation error, obtain engineering direction before resuming load transfer.
Reinspection: repeat the load-transfer step in a controlled, incremental manner once the cause is corrected or confirmed safe.

Required Structural or Jurisdictional Inspection Was Missed Before Concealment

Probable cause: finish material or sheathing was installed over the repair before the required inspection was scheduled or completed.
Confirmation: review the repair record and the authority having jurisdiction's requirements to confirm whether inspection was required and whether it occurred.
Correction: stop further finish work and contact the authority having jurisdiction; expose the repair again if directed, and complete the required inspection before re-concealing it.
Reinspection: repeat the full inspection and documentation sequence in Section I once the missed inspection is completed.

Cleanup

This project ends with a documented, inspected repair and, where authorized, released temporary support — not with shoring left in place indefinitely or debris left in the work area.

  • Remove temporary support only after documented release, and remove all shoring, cribbing, and bracing material from the work area once released
  • Dispose of removed damaged material according to its condition, including separate handling for decayed, insect-infested, or contaminated material where required
  • Clear sawdust, offcuts, fasteners, and debris from the work area and any occupied space affected by the repair
  • Store the signed repair record, photographs, and inspection results with the project's permanent documentation
  • Communicate the completed repair and any remaining finish work to the responsible design professional, general contractor, or occupant

Field Terms

Damaged Member
The specific framing member identified for repair.
Approved Repair Detail
The specification for a repair supplied by plans, prescriptive code, manufacturer, or a qualified structural professional.
Temporary Support
Shoring, posts, beams, or cribbing that carries load while a permanent repair is completed.
Sister Member
A new member fastened alongside an existing member to add or restore capacity.
Replacement Member
A new member that fully replaces a removed damaged member at the same location and orientation.
Load Transfer
The controlled process of moving load from temporary support back onto a completed, inspected repair.
Engineering Direction
Instruction from a licensed design professional required for uncertain, major, or engineered-member damage.
Stop-Work Condition
A defined condition that requires work to stop until direction is obtained.
Concealment
Covering a repair with finish material or sheathing; must not occur before required inspection.
Repair Record
The signed document confirming original condition, approved detail, materials, fasteners, and inspection results.

Project Closeout

You have completed the framing-repair project in the residential framing sequence: repairing damaged residential framing under an approved repair detail. This project required you to identify and document damaged framing and determine whether engineering direction was required (FR-109), plan and install verified temporary support (FR-110), remove damaged material only to the approved extent and sequence (FR-111), prepare and install a sistered or replacement member fitted to the approved bearing and overlap (FR-112), install the specified fasteners and connectors and reinstate disturbed assemblies (FR-113), and inspect and document the completed repair through required inspection (FR-114).

You now have a documented, inspected repair record demonstrating that you can safely open, support, repair, and close out existing residential framing — a skill set distinct from, and building on, the new-construction sequence taught in every earlier chapter of this book. That combination of skills carries directly into the next project: the Chapter 20 capstone, which requires you to inspect and verify an existing floor platform before framing new walls and a new roof on top of it.

Retain the repair record, photographs, and inspection results with the project's permanent documentation. Future work on this structure will depend on knowing exactly what was repaired, how, and under which approved detail.

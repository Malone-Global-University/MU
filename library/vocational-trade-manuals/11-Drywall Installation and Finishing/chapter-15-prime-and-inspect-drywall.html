Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to clean, prime, inspect, touch up, and approve a finished drywall area for paint or the specified texture. The finished work product is one completely primed room or wall system with a documented inspection record showing every identified defect corrected, spot-primed, and reinspected, and a final approval for paint or texture.

You will confirm sanding and dust cleanup are complete, HEPA-vacuum and hand-check remaining dust, apply drywall primer at the specified coverage with a uniform wet edge, inspect the cured primer under normal and raking light, mark and correct minor surface defects with compatible touch-up compound, sand and spot-prime the repairs, and issue a documented approval for finish paint or texture.

This chapter depends on Chapter 14, which sands and cleans the finished compound surface. It controls the readiness decision for Chapter 16, which applies approved texture, and it controls the primer standard used again in Chapters 17 through 19 whenever a repair is spot-primed.

Where This Skill Fits

Primer is a diagnostic tool as much as a coating. A drywall primer film reveals joint-edge lines, sanding scratches, pinholes, exposed tape fibers, fastener dimples, and texture differences that are difficult to see over bare, dusty compound. This chapter uses that revealing quality intentionally: primer goes on first, the surface is inspected under raking light, and only then is a defect marked, repaired, and spot-primed.

The raking-light inspection method you use here is the same method used in Chapter 13 to judge final-coat readiness and in Chapter 14 to judge sanding completeness. The touch-up and spot-prime procedure in this chapter is the same procedure required at the end of Chapters 17, 18, and 19 whenever a repair must be blended into a primed or painted wall.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming that sanding and dust cleanup are complete before priming begins
  • HEPA-vacuuming and hand-checking the surface for residual dust
  • protecting floors, trim, boxes, fixtures, windows, doors, and adjacent finishes before priming
  • mixing and applying the approved drywall primer at the specified coverage, with a uniform wet edge and film
  • inspecting the dried primer under normal and raking light for ridges, scratches, pinholes, exposed paper, joint edges, fastener dimples, bead defects, and texture differences
  • marking defects lightly without damaging the primer film
  • applying compatible touch-up compound to minor defects, drying, sanding, and cleaning localized repairs
  • spot-priming repaired areas and reinspecting for uniformity
  • issuing a documented approval for finish paint or the specified texture

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • use paint buildup to conceal joint-finishing defects that a proper primer, touch-up, and spot-prime cycle should have corrected
  • apply finish paint or a specified texture over a surface that has not been inspected and approved under this chapter
  • skip HEPA vacuuming and hand-checking for dust before priming, regardless of how clean the surface appears
  • select or substitute a primer other than the type identified in the approved finish specification without confirming compatibility with the specified paint or texture system

Any defect that reappears after touch-up and spot-priming, or that suggests an unresolved joint, tape, or fastener problem beneath the primer, must be referred back to the compound-finishing procedures in Chapters 10 through 13 rather than concealed with additional primer or paint.

Trade Note

This chapter produces a primed, inspected, and approved surface, not a finish-painted or textured room. A wall that has not received the Step 20 approval is not ready for Chapter 16 texture application or for finish-trade paint.

Setup Standard

Do not begin priming (Step 6) until sanding and dust cleanup from Chapter 14 are confirmed complete and residual dust has been removed by both HEPA vacuum and hand check. Primer applied over dusty compound will not bond uniformly and must be removed and reapplied.

Shop Tip

Inspect primer under raking light at a low angle from at least two directions, the same way you sighted framing planes in Chapter 1 and joint flatness in Chapters 12 and 13. A ridge or edge line that is invisible under overhead room lighting is often obvious the moment the light rakes across the surface at a shallow angle.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every priming and inspection sequence in this book follows the same pattern: confirm the surface is clean and dry, protect surrounding finishes, apply primer uniformly with a maintained wet edge, allow full cure, inspect under raking light, mark defects without damaging the film, correct with compatible touch-up compound, dry, sand lightly, spot-prime, and reinspect before final approval.

Dust Removal Before Priming

Primer applied over residual gypsum dust will not bond to the paper and compound surface beneath it and will require complete removal and reapplication. Confirm the Chapter 14 dust-control procedure is complete, then perform an independent hand check with a clean cloth across representative wall and ceiling areas before mixing any primer.

Manufacturer Cue — Confirm Coverage Rate and Recoat Time

Confirm the specified primer's coverage rate in square feet per gallon and its recoat and cure time before beginning application. Applying primer beyond the manufacturer's coverage rate produces a thin, uneven film that will not reveal defects consistently and may not seal the surface adequately for the specified finish.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilation During Priming

Maintain the ventilation specified by the primer manufacturer's safety data sheet throughout application and cure. Do not seal off a room's only ventilation path to control dust from an adjacent area while priming is in progress.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Suspected Ridges With a Straightedge

When raking light reveals a suspected ridge or edge line, confirm it with a straightedge before deciding whether it requires touch-up compound or additional sanding. Do not mark a defect for touch-up based on visual impression alone when a measurable check is available.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Conceal Joint Defects With Paint Buildup

Do not use paint buildup, additional primer coats, or thick touch-up applications to conceal an unacceptable joint-finishing defect. If a defect traces back to an unresolved taping, fastening, or compound problem, refer it back to the applicable Chapter 7 through 13 procedure rather than concealing it at the primer stage.

E. Preparation Procedure (Steps 1–5)

  1. Review the required finish level, primer type, texture sequence, paint specification, ventilation, and environmental limits. Confirm the specified primer and touch-up compound against the approved finish specification before proceeding.
  2. Confirm sanding and dust cleanup are complete. Verify the Chapter 14 surface-smoothness inspection was passed for the room or wall system.
  3. Inspect walls and ceilings for residual powder using a clean cloth or hand check. Wipe representative areas of every plane and check the cloth for visible dust.
  4. HEPA-vacuum remaining dust. Vacuum walls, ceilings, ledges, boxes, and fixtures until the hand check in Step 3 shows no residual dust.
  5. Protect floors, trim, boxes, fixtures, windows, doors, and adjacent finishes. Mask and cover every surface not being primed before mixing any primer.

F. Priming Procedure (Steps 6–10)

  1. Stir or mix the approved drywall primer. Follow the manufacturer's stated mixing method and do not thin beyond the product instructions.
  2. Cut in corners, edges, and penetrations. Apply primer by brush at every corner, edge, box, and penetration before rolling the field area.
  3. Apply primer at the specified coverage without excessive rolling pressure. Maintain the manufacturer's stated coverage rate and avoid pressing the roller hard enough to thin the film unevenly.
  4. Maintain a wet edge and uniform film. Work in sections small enough to blend each new section into the previous wet edge without a visible lap line.
  5. Allow primer to dry fully. Confirm the manufacturer's stated dry time before beginning the inspection in Step 11.

G. Inspection and Defect-Marking Procedure (Steps 11–13)

  1. Inspect under normal and raking light. Check every wall and ceiling plane first under normal room lighting, then under a strong raking angle from at least two directions.
  2. Identify ridges, scratches, pinholes, exposed paper, joint edges, fastener dimples, bead defects, and texture differences. Confirm any suspected ridge with a straightedge before marking it for touch-up.
  3. Mark defects lightly without damaging the primer. Use a light pencil mark or removable marker adjacent to each defect rather than marking directly over it.

H. Touch-Up, Spot-Prime, and Approval Procedure (Steps 14–20)

  1. Apply compatible touch-up compound to minor defects. Fill each marked pinhole, ridge, or dimple with the specified touch-up compound in a thin, feathered application.
  2. Allow touch-ups to dry. Confirm the touch-up compound is fully dry before sanding.
  3. Sand and clean localized repairs. Sand only the repaired area enough to blend it with the surrounding primed surface, then remove dust from the repair and surrounding area.
  4. Spot-prime repaired areas. Apply primer only to the repaired area, matching the surrounding cured primer film.
  5. Reinspect for uniformity and finish readiness. Repeat the raking-light inspection from Section G across every repaired area and confirm no defect remains.
  6. Do not use paint buildup to conceal unacceptable joint finishing. Refer any defect that reappears after touch-up and spot-priming back to the applicable Chapter 7 through 13 procedure rather than concealing it with additional coats.
  7. Record primer, coverage, defects, repairs, and approval status. File the completed primer and inspection record with the room's project documentation before releasing the surface to Chapter 16 or the finish-paint trade.

Common Priming and Inspection Checks

Inspect every primed room or wall system using the same checks regardless of size or configuration.

  • Dust check: confirm no residual powder remains on any surface before primer is mixed.
  • Coverage check: confirm primer was applied at the specified coverage rate with a uniform, wet-edge film.
  • Cure check: confirm the primer reached its full manufacturer-stated dry time before inspection began.
  • Defect check: confirm every ridge, scratch, pinhole, exposed paper area, joint edge, fastener dimple, bead defect, and texture difference was identified under raking light.
  • Repair check: confirm every marked defect was corrected, dried, sanded, spot-primed, and reinspected.
  • Approval check: confirm the surface is approved for finish paint or the specified texture only after every defect is resolved.

Complete the Primer and Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before releasing the room to Chapter 16 or the finish-paint trade.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Primer Peels or Fails to Bond in Isolated Areas

Probable cause: residual dust remained on the surface in that area before priming.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area for a chalky or powdery layer beneath the failed primer film.
Correction: remove the failed primer, HEPA-vacuum and hand-check the area again, and reprime once the dust check passes.
Reinspection: reinspect the reprimed area under raking light after full cure.

A Visible Joint Edge Appears Under Raking Light After Priming

Probable cause: insufficient feathering during the Chapter 12 or Chapter 13 finish coats left a measurable ridge that the primer film revealed.
Confirmation: confirm the ridge with a straightedge across the joint.
Correction: apply thin, feathered touch-up compound extending beyond the ridge on both sides rather than a single thick pass.
Reinspection: sand lightly, spot-prime, and reinspect under raking light after the touch-up dries.

Primer Shows a Lap Line Between Sections

Probable cause: the primer's leading edge dried before the adjacent section was rolled in, breaking the wet edge.
Confirmation: view the affected area under raking light and compare film thickness across the line.
Correction: apply a full additional primer coat across the affected wall or ceiling plane, working in smaller sections to maintain a wet edge.
Reinspection: reinspect the full plane under raking light after the additional coat cures.

A Spot-Primed Touch-Up Remains Visible as a Sheen or Texture Difference

Probable cause: the touch-up compound was sanded unevenly or the spot-prime did not extend far enough to blend with the surrounding cured film.
Confirmation: inspect the repair under raking light from multiple angles and compare sheen and texture to the surrounding surface.
Correction: sand the repair to match the surrounding surface profile and extend the spot-prime coat further beyond the repair's edges.
Reinspection: reinspect after the extended spot-prime coat cures.

A Defect Reappears After Touch-Up and Spot-Priming

Probable cause: the underlying cause is an unresolved taping, fastening, or compound defect rather than a surface-level imperfection.
Confirmation: inspect beneath the touch-up area for loose tape, an unset fastener, or insufficient compound buildup.
Correction: refer the defect back to the applicable Chapter 7 through 13 procedure for correction rather than applying additional touch-up compound or primer coats.
Reinspection: reprime the corrected area and reinspect under raking light before approval.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with an approved, primed surface and a filed inspection record.

  • Clean rollers, brushes, and mixing tools according to the primer manufacturer's instructions
  • Remove masking materials and drop cloths once the primer and all touch-ups have fully cured
  • Dispose of primer and compound waste according to local requirements
  • File the completed primer and inspection worksheet with the room's project records
  • Retain the approval record with the Chapter 16 texture-application documentation or the finish-paint trade handoff for the same room or wall system

Field Terms

Raking Light
Light directed across a surface at a low, shallow angle to reveal ridges, edges, and irregularities.
Cut-In
Primer applied by brush at corners, edges, and penetrations before rolling the field area.
Wet Edge
The leading edge of applied primer kept workable enough to blend with the adjoining section without a lap line.
Surface Defect
A ridge, scratch, pinhole, exposed paper area, visible joint edge, fastener dimple, bead defect, or texture difference identified after priming.
Touch-Up
Localized compound applied to correct a marked surface defect after the first primer coat.
Spot-Prime
Primer reapplied only to a repaired touch-up area to match the surrounding cured film.
Coverage Rate
The manufacturer-specified area a primer covers per unit volume at the correct film thickness.
Feathering
Tapering a compound edge to a thin, gradual transition with the surrounding surface.
PVA Primer
A common drywall primer type formulated to seal porous compound and paper before paint or texture.
Finish-Readiness Approval
The documented decision confirming a primed surface is free of defects and ready for finish paint or specified texture.

Performance Check

With priming, inspection, and all touch-up repairs complete, review the room or wall system against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that no residual dust remained before priming, primer coverage and film were uniform and fully cured, every ridge, scratch, pinhole, exposed paper area, joint edge, fastener dimple, bead defect, and texture difference was identified under raking light, every marked defect was corrected, sanded, spot-primed, and reinspected, and no defect was concealed with paint buildup rather than corrected at its source. Issue the finish-readiness approval only after every defect is resolved and reinspected.

Project Closeout

You have completed the priming and finish-readiness inspection stage of the drywall finishing sequence. This chapter required you to clean drywall dust (DW-085), apply primer (DW-086), identify surface defects (DW-087), mark touch-up areas (DW-088), repair minor imperfections (DW-089), and approve the surface for finish paint (DW-090).

You now have a uniformly primed drywall surface with every defect identified, corrected, spot-primed, and reinspected, along with a documented approval for finish paint or the specified texture. That approval is the controlling document for the next chapter: applying the approved basic wall texture, or, on a smooth-finish project, completing the required texture-practice panel.

File your completed primer and inspection worksheet with the room's project records. Chapter 16 texture application, or handoff to the finish-paint trade on a smooth-finish project, may not begin until this chapter's finish-readiness decision is approved or conditionally approved with a documented completion date for any remaining items.

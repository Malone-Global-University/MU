Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to select, cut, fit, and secure corner bead and edge trim on a controlled set of outside corners and exposed drywall terminations. The finished work product is a set of straight, securely attached corner-bead and edge-trim runs, correctly aligned to the intended finished plane and ready to receive joint compound.

You will confirm that surrounding panels are fully fastened and aligned before bead installation begins; correct panel defects rather than concealing them under bead; measure, cut, and dry-fit bead and trim to the required clearances; secure the bead using the listed method for its material; form clean intersections at three-way corners and openings; and inspect straightness, alignment, and attachment under raking light before releasing the corner for compound.

Chapter 7 fastening standards govern any fastener used to secure bead in this chapter. Chapter 8 corner and return framing must be complete and inspected before bead is installed on those assemblies. The bead and trim installed in this chapter are covered by the taping and compound work of Chapters 10 through 13, so a defect left uncorrected here is concealed, not fixed, by later coats.

Where This Skill Fits

Corner bead and edge trim establish the finished plane at every outside corner and exposed panel termination. A bead that is bowed, twisted, or misaligned when installed cannot be corrected with joint compound alone; compound can only feather to a crooked line, it cannot straighten one. The straightedge and raking-light inspection methods used in Chapter 1 to check framing planes are the same methods used here to check bead alignment before compound is applied.

The corners and returns framed and backed in Chapter 8 are the substrate for this chapter's bead installation. The bead and trim installed here become the reference edge that Chapters 11 through 13 feather compound against, so any bead defect carried forward becomes a visible flaw at every subsequent coat.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • selecting the specified bead and trim type for standard corners, arches, reveals, tear-away edges, expansion joints, and exposed terminations
  • inspecting corners for broken gypsum, projecting paper, fastener heads, and plane misalignment before bead installation
  • measuring, cutting, and dry-fitting bead and edge trim to the specified clearances
  • aligning bead to the approved finished plane and securing it using the listed crimp, fastener, adhesive, compound, or embedded-paper method
  • installing edge trim at exposed panel ends and forming clean intersections at three-way corners and openings
  • maintaining control joints and movement details specified in the approved plans
  • inspecting straightness, flange attachment, corner alignment, and damage under raking light
  • documenting special trim and rated-system locations

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • install bead or trim over panel defects, broken gypsum, projecting paper, or unaddressed plane misalignment; correct the panel defect first or refer it to the responsible party
  • substitute a different bead or trim product, material, or attachment method than the one specified for the location without confirming an approved equivalent
  • alter or omit a control joint, expansion joint, or rated-assembly detail shown in the approved plans
  • bury a bent, twisted, or misaligned bead in thick compound rather than replacing it

Any condition outside the learner's qualification, including panel-plane deviations requiring framing correction or rated-assembly questions, must be referred to the responsible trade or an engineer or architect as identified in the applicable steps below.

Trade Note

This chapter assumes the wall and ceiling panels covered by Chapters 5, 6, and 8 are already fastened, aligned, and inspected. A corner that has not passed those chapters' inspections is not ready for bead installation under this chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not begin bead installation (Step 6) until surrounding panels are confirmed fastened and aligned and corner defects identified in Step 3 are corrected under Step 4. Bead installed over an uncorrected panel defect must be removed and reinstalled after the defect is corrected.

Shop Tip

Dry-fit the full length of bead before securing any fastener. A bead that looks straight held loosely against the corner can still reveal a bow or twist when you sight down its length from floor to ceiling.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every corner-bead and edge-trim installation in this book, from a single practice corner to the full Chapter 20 capstone room, follows the same sequence: inspect and correct the substrate, measure each corner independently, cut and dry-fit the bead or trim, align it to the finished plane, secure it using the listed method, form clean intersections, and inspect under raking light before releasing the corner for compound. Substrate Inspection Before Bead Installation

Inspect every corner for broken gypsum, projecting paper, proud fastener heads, and plane misalignment before any bead is cut or dry-fit. A defect corrected after bead installation requires removing the bead; a defect corrected before installation costs only the time to inspect. Correct panel defects using the same fastening and panel-repair methods taught in Chapters 6 and 7 before proceeding to bead work.

Measurement Cue — Measure Every Corner Independently

Do not assume that corners of the same nominal height are identical. Measure each corner from end to end before cutting bead, and confirm the specified clearance at floors, ceilings, and any intersecting trim before making the cut.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Bury a Defective Bead

Do not conceal a bent, twisted, dented, or misaligned bead under thick joint compound. Replace bead that fails the straightedge and raking-light inspection rather than attempting to correct its alignment with additional compound thickness, which will not hold and will crack or telegraph through the finish.

Field Safety Cue — Cut Edges and Tool Use

Wear eye protection when cutting metal, vinyl, or composite bead, and work gloves when handling cut edges. Keep fingers clear of the crimping tool's jaws and confirm the tool is set for the specified bead gauge before use.

Code Alert — Control Joints and Rated Assemblies

Do not omit or relocate a control joint, expansion joint, or rated-assembly trim detail shown in the approved plans. Where a corner falls within a fire-rated, smoke-rated, or other protected assembly, use only the bead and attachment method specified for that assembly.

G. Preparation and Substrate Verification Procedure (Steps 1–4)

  1. Review the specified bead and trim types for standard corners, arches, reveals, tear-away edges, expansion joints, and exposed terminations. Confirm the approved bead and trim schedule matches the room or wall system to be trimmed.
  2. Confirm that surrounding panels are fully fastened and aligned. Verify the Chapter 6 and Chapter 8 inspection records for every corner and termination before proceeding.
  3. Inspect corners for broken gypsum, projecting paper, fastener heads, and plane misalignment. Check the full length of every corner and termination to be trimmed.
  4. Correct panel defects before bead installation. Repair or replace damaged panel edges and reset proud fasteners using Chapter 7 methods before cutting any bead.

H. Measuring, Cutting, and Dry-Fitting Procedure (Steps 5–9)

  1. Measure each corner from end to end. Measure independently at each corner or termination; do not assume matching lengths from a plan dimension alone.
  2. Cut bead using the tool appropriate to metal, vinyl, paper-faced metal, or composite material. Use aviation snips or the bead-cutting tool specified for the material, and square the cut end unless a mitered intersection is required.
  3. Leave the specified clearance at floors, ceilings, and intersecting trim. Confirm the clearance dimension against the approved bead and trim schedule before securing.
  4. Dry-fit the bead and verify that both flanges contact the panel. Hold the bead in place without fasteners or adhesive and check for gaps or rocking along the full length.
  5. Align the bead nose to the approved finished plane. Reference the surrounding panel faces and any adjoining bead already installed at intersecting corners.

I. Securing and Intersection Procedure (Steps 10–15)

  1. Secure the bead using the listed crimp, fastener, adhesive, compound, or embedded-paper method. Use only the attachment method specified for the bead material in Section D.
  2. Follow the product-specific spacing and sequence. Confirm fastener or crimp spacing against the manufacturer instructions before completing the run.
  3. Avoid twisting, denting, or bowing the bead. Apply even pressure along the length during attachment and check for distortion after each fastener or crimp.
  4. Install edge trim at exposed panel ends using the required reveal and backing. Confirm backing is present at the termination before installing trim.
  5. Form clean intersections at three-way corners and openings. Cope or miter adjoining bead runs so no gap or overlap is visible at the intersection.
  6. Maintain control joints and movement details. Install control-joint bead at the exact approved location without bridging it with rigid bead or compound.

J. Inspection and Documentation Procedure (Steps 16–20)

  1. Sight the bead under raking light. Check the full length of every installed run for bow, twist, wave, or shadow lines indicating misalignment.
  2. Check straightness with a long straightedge. Confirm no gap exceeding the acceptance standard appears between the straightedge and the bead nose.
  3. Inspect flange attachment, corner alignment, damage, and adequate compound space. Verify both flanges are fully seated and that sufficient reveal remains for the compound coats specified in Chapters 11 through 13.
  4. Replace bent, loose, or incorrectly aligned bead rather than burying defects in thick mud. Remove and reinstall any run that fails the straightedge or raking-light inspection.
  5. Document special trim and rated-system locations. Record control joints, rated-assembly bead, and any approved equivalent product substitutions on the installation log before releasing the corner for taping.

Common Corner and Trim Inspection

Inspect every corner and termination using the same checks regardless of bead type or wall configuration.

  • Substrate check: confirm no broken gypsum, projecting paper, or proud fasteners remain beneath the bead location.
  • Straightness check: confirm no visible bow, twist, or wave along the full run under a straightedge and raking light.
  • Flange check: confirm both flanges are fully seated with no gap or rocking.
  • Clearance check: confirm floor, ceiling, and intersecting-trim clearances match the approved schedule.
  • Intersection check: confirm three-way corners and openings form clean, tight intersections.
  • Control-joint check: confirm control joints and rated-assembly details are installed at the exact approved location.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Bead Reads Straight by Eye but Shows a Gap Under the Straightedge

Probable cause: a shallow bow introduced during shipping, storage, or dry-fitting that is not visible from a normal viewing distance.
Confirmation: recheck the full run with a 6-ft straightedge at multiple points and sight it again under raking light.
Correction: remove and replace the bead rather than attempting to correct the bow with additional fasteners or compound.
Reinspection: repeat the straightedge and raking-light check on the replacement bead before securing it.

A Flange Rocks or Shows a Gap at One End

Probable cause: a panel-plane deviation beneath the bead that was not corrected before installation, or a bead that was twisted during handling.
Confirmation: remove the bead and check the panel plane with a straightedge; check the bead itself on a flat reference surface.
Correction: refer an uncorrected panel-plane deviation to the framing trade; replace a twisted bead.
Reinspection: reverify flange contact after the panel or bead correction is complete.

A Three-Way Corner Intersection Shows a Gap or Overlap

Probable cause: the intersecting bead runs were cut to length without coordinating the miter or cope at the intersection point.
Confirmation: dry-fit the intersecting runs together before final attachment and check for a tight, gap-free fit.
Correction: recut the affected run to close the gap or correct the overlap; do not fill the intersection with thick compound as a substitute for a proper cut.
Reinspection: reverify the intersection fit before securing the final run.

A Control Joint Appears Bridged by Rigid Bead or Compound

Probable cause: the control-joint location was trimmed with standard corner bead instead of the specified control-joint product, or compound was applied across the joint gap.
Confirmation: compare the installed location against the approved plans and verify the product used matches the bead and trim schedule.
Correction: remove the incorrect bead or compound and install the specified control-joint product at the exact approved location.
Reinspection: reverify the control joint remains unbridged before proceeding to taping.

Paper-Faced Bead Shows Bubbles or Lifted Edges After Embedding

Probable cause: insufficient compound beneath the paper flange or inconsistent knife pressure during embedment.
Confirmation: press the affected area and check for hollow sound or visible lift at the flange edge.
Correction: lift and reset the flange while the compound remains workable, or remove and replace the affected section after the compound dries.
Reinspection: recheck the flange for full bond before proceeding to Chapter 10 taping.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with installed corner bead and edge trim ready for compound, and a filed installation log.

  • Sweep and collect metal offcuts, paper-faced bead scraps, and packaging from the work area

  • Retain the installation log with the Chapter 10 taping documentation for the same room or wall system

Field Terms

Corner Bead
A metal, vinyl, paper-faced metal, or composite strip installed at an outside corner to protect and define the finished edge.
Edge Trim
Trim installed at an exposed panel termination to protect the edge and establish a finished reveal or transition.
Bead Nose
The rounded or profiled leading edge of corner bead that establishes the finished corner line.
Flange
The flat wing of the bead or trim that contacts the panel face and receives compound or fasteners.
Reveal
An intentional recessed trim detail that exposes a defined shadow line rather than a flush corner.
Control Joint
A manufactured joint that permits controlled movement in a large drywall plane without cracking the finish.
Three-Way Corner
The intersection of three bead or trim runs at a single point.
Tear-Away Bead
Bead with a removable paper or plastic strip that is stripped away after compound application to leave a clean edge.
Crimping Tool
A hand tool that dimples metal bead flanges into the panel to secure the bead without additional fasteners.
Finished Plane
The intended flat surface that the bead nose and surrounding panel faces must align to after finishing.

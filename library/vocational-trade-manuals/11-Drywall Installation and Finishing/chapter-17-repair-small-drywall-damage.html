Repair a controlled set of minor drywall defects — one nail pop, one dent, and one small hole — by correcting the underlying cause, fastening or filling correctly, feathering and sanding the compound, matching texture, and priming the repair so it does not telegraph through the finished surface.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to repair the three most common minor drywall defects encountered in finished rooms: a nail or screw pop, a surface dent, and a small hole. The finished work product is one completed repair set — one repaired nail pop, one repaired dent, and one repaired small hole — each corrected at its cause, filled or patched, sanded, texture-matched where required, and primed so the defect does not telegraph through the surrounding finish.

You will identify the cause of each defect before repairing it; correct fastening or framing movement where that is the cause; fill or patch the defect using thin, feathered coats of compound; sand only enough to blend the repair; match existing texture using an approved sample method; and prime the repair so it is ready for finish paint.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 7 fastening standards, Chapters 10 through 13 taping and coating sequence, Chapter 14 sanding controls, Chapter 15 priming and inspection, and Chapter 16 texture matching. It is the first repair chapter in the book and establishes the cause-correction, fill, sand, texture-match, and prime sequence that Chapters 18 and 19 extend to larger and more structurally involved repairs.

Where This Skill Fits

Minor drywall damage is the most frequent repair call in an occupied building: a fastener works loose as framing dries and shrinks, a doorknob or moved furniture dents a wall, or a hung picture leaves a small hole. Each of these defects has a specific, correctable cause. Filling the visible symptom without correcting that cause is the single most common reason a repair fails or reappears.

The fastening correction in this chapter uses the same screw-selection and depth-setting standards taught in Chapter 7. The thin, feathered compound coats you apply here use the same knife technique as Chapters 11 through 13. The sanding, texture-matching, and priming steps use the exact controls established in Chapters 14, 15, and 16. Chapter 18 extends this sequence to a large hole requiring backing, and Chapter 19 extends it to full section replacement.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying the damage type, cause, wall assembly, existing paint or texture, and moisture condition for each defect
  • confirming that movement, leakage, impact, or fastening causes have been corrected before patching
  • repairing one nail or screw pop by installing a new fastener at the correct offset and filling the resulting depression
  • repairing one dent by removing loose material, sealing exposed gypsum where required, and filling with thin compound coats
  • patching one small hole using an approved patch method matched to hole size and substrate
  • sanding each repair only enough to blend it with the surrounding surface
  • matching existing texture using the approved sample method from Chapter 16
  • cleaning dust, spot-priming the repair, and inspecting the finished blend under normal and raking light

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • patch over an unresolved moisture source, active leak, mold, structural movement, or unaddressed impact cause; the cause must be corrected first, by a qualified trade if it falls outside the learner's competence
  • repair a hole large enough to require backing or a replacement panel section; those repairs are covered in Chapters 18 and 19
  • alter a fire-rated, smoke-rated, or other protected assembly while repairing a defect within it, without the exact approved assembly detail
  • repaint or apply full-wall finish coatings; this chapter ends at an approved, primed, texture-matched repair ready for the painting trade

Any condition outside the learner's qualification — including a suspected moisture source, structural movement, or an electrical or plumbing cause behind the damage — must be referred to the responsible trade or a qualified professional before the repair proceeds.

Trade Note

A repair that reappears within a few weeks almost always means the cause was not corrected, not that the compound was defective. Confirm cause correction before every repair in this chapter, and document it on the repair record described in Section I.

Setup Standard

Do not begin filling or patching (Steps 5–13) until the cause of each defect has been confirmed corrected under Step 2. A repair filled over an uncorrected cause must be removed and redone once the cause is addressed.

Shop Tip

Feather every repair coat at least twice the width of the coat before it — a nail pop filled with a 2-inch dab of compound should have its final coat feathered to roughly 6 to 8 inches across. Wide, thin feathering blends far better under raking light than a thick, narrow patch.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every repair in this chapter follows the same underlying sequence regardless of defect type: identify the cause, confirm the cause is corrected, prepare the damaged area, fill or patch in thin coats, allow full drying between coats, sand only enough to blend, match existing texture, and prime the finished repair. Learning this sequence on small, common defects prepares you for the larger patch and section-replacement procedures in Chapters 18 and 19.

Nail popsFastener replacement at correct offset, depression fill
DentsLoose-material removal, thin fill coats, feathering
Small holesListed patch product, thin fill coats, feathering
BlendSanding limits, texture match, spot-priming, raking-light inspection

Cause Identification Before Repair

Before touching any compound, determine why each defect occurred. A nail pop usually indicates framing shrinkage or an undriven fastener that was never properly seated. A dent usually indicates an impact from furniture, a doorknob, or a tool. A small hole usually indicates a removed fixture, anchor, or accidental puncture. Record the suspected cause and confirm it will not recur before proceeding.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Patch Over an Unresolved Cause

Do not fill or patch any defect where the suspected cause is an active water leak, mold, structural movement, or damaged wiring or plumbing behind the wall. Stop work and refer the condition to the responsible trade or a qualified professional. A repair applied over an unresolved cause will fail and may conceal a hazard.

Safety Cue — De-energize Before Drilling or Driving Near Boxes

Confirm the location of any electrical box, cable, or pipe before driving the replacement fastener for a nail pop or before enlarging a small hole for patching. Where the defect is near a known electrical location, confirm the circuit is de-energized before proceeding.

Measurement Cue — Offset the Replacement Fastener

Do not drive the replacement fastener into the exact hole left by the failed one. Install it at the offset distance specified in the approved project specification, into solid, undamaged framing, so the new fastener does not rely on the same weakened wood or gypsum.

Manufacturer Cue — Follow the Patch Product's Size Limit

Every listed small-hole patch product states a maximum hole dimension it is rated to bridge. Do not use a small-hole patch on a hole that exceeds the manufacturer's stated limit; treat it as a large-hole repair under Chapter 18 instead.

DW-097 – DW-102

Minor Drywall Damage Repair

Nail Pop, Dent, and Small-Hole Repair Set

Difficulty: Beginner to Intermediate

Estimated Time: 2–4 hours active work

Estimated Cost: $15–$50

Finished Work: Three repaired defects — nail pop, dent, small hole — sanded, texture matched, and primed

A. Documentation and Assessment Procedure (Steps 1–3)

  1. Identify the damage type, cause, wall assembly, paint or texture, and moisture condition. Record each of the three defects on the repair worksheet with its location, defect type, suspected cause, wall type, and existing finish.
  2. Confirm that movement, leakage, impact, or fastening causes have been corrected. For any defect with a suspected moisture, structural, electrical, or plumbing cause, obtain confirmation from the responsible trade or qualified professional before proceeding. Do not proceed on an unresolved cause.
  3. Protect the area and establish dust control. Cover flooring, trim, and furnishings within the work radius and establish HEPA vacuum access consistent with Chapter 14 controls.

B. Nail-Pop Repair Procedure (Steps 4–7)

  1. For a nail or screw pop, inspect for framing movement and loose panel edges. Confirm the panel itself is still securely fastened elsewhere and that the pop is limited to the single fastener location.
  2. Install a new approved fastener into framing at the specified offset. Drive the replacement screw perpendicular to the panel, into solid framing, at the offset distance stated in the Fastener and Hardware Schedule, setting the head slightly below the paper surface without breaking it.
  3. Remove or reset the failed fastener only as permitted. Where the approved method calls for removing the failed fastener, back it out without enlarging the surrounding gypsum; where it calls for resetting, drive it to the correct depth only if it still engages solid framing.
  4. Fill the resulting depression with compound. Apply a thin first coat over both the new and the corrected original fastener location, feathering the edges. Allow it to dry fully before recoating.

C. Dent Repair Procedure (Steps 8–10)

  1. For a dent, remove loose paper and gypsum without enlarging sound material unnecessarily. Trim only the damaged, loose, or torn material back to a sound edge using the utility knife or rasp.
  2. Seal exposed gypsum or damaged paper where the repair system requires it. Apply the specified sealer to any exposed gypsum core before filling, following the compound manufacturer's instructions.
  3. Apply thin compound coats to fill the dent. Build the fill in thin layers rather than one thick application, allowing each layer to dry before the next, and feather each coat progressively wider than the one before it.

D. Small-Hole Patch Procedure (Steps 11–14)

  1. For a small hole, select the approved patch method based on hole size and substrate. Confirm the hole falls within the listed patch product's maximum dimension before proceeding; refer larger holes to Chapter 18.
  2. Install backing or a listed patch if required. Apply the patch product per its manufacturer instructions, ensuring full contact and adhesion around the hole perimeter.
  3. Apply compound in thin coats and feather each coat beyond the previous edge. Build the patch surface in progressively wider, thinner coats consistent with the fastening and coating standards from Chapters 10 through 13.
  4. Allow each coat to dry. Do not apply a subsequent coat until the previous coat is fully dry or set per the compound manufacturer's stated time.

E. Finishing and Approval Procedure (Steps 15–20)

  1. Sand only enough to blend the repair. Using the least aggressive abrasive that corrects the defect, sand each repair per the Chapter 14 dust-control and paper-protection standards.
  2. Match the existing texture using the approved sample method. Where the surrounding surface is textured, prepare and approve a sample per Chapter 16 before applying texture to the repair.
  3. Clean dust and spot-prime the repair. HEPA-vacuum all sanding dust, then apply drywall primer to each repair and a margin of surrounding surface.
  4. Inspect under normal and raking light. Check each repair for flatness, texture match, and any remaining defect once the primer is dry.
  5. Verify that the repaired panel is stable and the defect does not telegraph through primer. Confirm with a hand pass and straightedge that no ridge, hollow, or edge line remains detectable.
  6. Document cause, repair method, materials, and final blend. Complete the repair worksheet for all three defects, including the cause-correction confirmation obtained in Step 2.

Common Repair Inspection

Inspect every repair using the same checks regardless of defect type.

  • Cause check: confirm the moisture, structural, fastening, or impact cause was corrected and documented before filling began.
  • Fastening check: confirm the replacement fastener is seated in solid framing at the correct offset with no face-paper breakage.
  • Flatness check: confirm no ridge, hollow, or edge line is detectable by hand or straightedge across the repaired plane.
  • Sanding check: confirm face paper is intact and not fuzzed, cut, or exposed by oversanding.
  • Texture check: confirm the repaired texture matches the approved sample under normal and raking light.
  • Primer check: confirm uniform primer coverage over the repair and surrounding margin with no visible sheen difference.

Complete the Repair Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before considering the repair set closed out.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Nail Pop Reappears After Repair

Probable cause: the replacement fastener was driven at the same location as the failed one, or framing shrinkage or movement was not resolved before repair.
Confirmation: inspect the framing behind the repair for continued movement and confirm the replacement fastener's offset and depth.
Correction: install a new fastener at a corrected offset into solid, unmoved framing, and refer continued framing movement to the framing trade before refilling.
Reinspection: recheck fastener seating and monitor the location before final compound and primer are applied.

A Ridge or Hollow Remains Visible Under Raking Light

Probable cause: insufficient feathering width, a single thick coat instead of thin progressive coats, or sanding that removed material unevenly.
Confirmation: check the repair with a straightedge under raking light to locate the exact high or low point.
Correction: apply an additional thin, wider feathered coat over the defect, allow it to dry fully, and resand lightly.
Reinspection: recheck with the straightedge and raking light before priming.

Sanding Has Exposed or Fuzzed the Face Paper

Probable cause: sanding continued past the point where the compound was blended, cutting into the paper surface.
Confirmation: inspect the sanded area closely for fuzzing, torn fibers, or exposed gypsum.
Correction: apply a thin skim coat of compound over the damaged paper area, allow it to dry, and resand using a lighter touch and finer abrasive.
Reinspection: reinspect for intact, smooth paper before priming.

The Texture Does Not Match the Surrounding Surface

Probable cause: the texture sample was not approved before application, or application settings drifted from the approved sample during the repair itself.
Confirmation: compare the repaired texture directly against the approved sample and the adjacent wall under raking light.
Correction: scrape or sand the mismatched texture, reapply using the approved sample settings, and blend the edges into the surrounding pattern.
Reinspection: recheck the blended area under normal and raking light after the corrected texture dries.

The Repair Telegraphs Through the Primer

Probable cause: the compound was not fully dry before priming, or the feathered edge was too abrupt for the primer film to conceal.
Confirmation: inspect under raking light after the primer has fully dried and confirm whether the defect is a moisture shadow or a true surface irregularity.
Correction: allow additional drying time and reprime if moisture is the cause; sand and apply an additional feathered skim coat if the edge is the cause, then reprime.
Reinspection: recheck under raking light after the corrected area dries fully.

Cleanup

  • HEPA-vacuum all sanding dust from the repair areas, floors, and adjacent surfaces
  • Remove protective coverings from flooring, trim, and furnishings
  • Seal, label, and store remaining compound, patch product, primer, and texture material for future use
  • Clean tools and knives before compound dries on them
  • File the completed repair worksheet with the room's project records

Field Terms

Nail Pop
A fastener that has worked loose from framing, projecting the panel face and cracking or ridging the compound above it.
Dent
A localized depression in the panel face where the gypsum core or face paper is compressed or torn but not penetrated.
Small Hole
A penetration through the panel small enough to bridge with a listed patch product without backing or panel replacement.
Feathering
Progressively thinning a compound coat outward from a repair so its edge is imperceptible against the surrounding plane.
Telegraphing
A repair defect becoming visible through primer or paint due to insufficient feathering, sanding, or texture match.
Cause Correction
Resolving the structural, moisture, fastening, or impact condition responsible for a defect before it is filled or patched.
Setting-Type Compound
A joint compound that hardens through a chemical reaction on a fixed schedule rather than by drying.
Drying-Type Compound
A joint compound that hardens through evaporation and is sensitive to temperature and humidity.
Repair Worksheet
The document recording location, defect type, cause, correction, materials, and final blend for each repair.
Raking Light
Light directed at a low angle across a surface to reveal ridges, hollows, and texture irregularities not visible under normal lighting.

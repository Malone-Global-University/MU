Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to apply the final joint-compound coat over flat seams, butt joints, inside corners, outside corners, and any remaining fastener dimples in the room or area coated under Chapter 12. The finished work product is a fully dried final-coat surface with smooth transitions, minimal ridges, and a limited, clearly identified list of remaining sanding requirements.

You will confirm the second coat is completely dry before beginning; inspect under strong raking light to mark shallow hollows, edge lines, pinholes, and corner defects; apply a thin final coat that extends and feathers beyond the previous coat's edges; fill remaining dimples and minor surface defects; and correct lap marks, heavy edges, and knife chatter while the coat is still workable rather than leaving them for sanding.

This chapter follows Chapter 12, which widened and leveled every joint to a straightedge standard, and depends on that second coat being fully dry and free of hollows, ridges, or edge buildup before this chapter's thin final coat is applied. It feeds directly into Chapter 14, which sands the dried final coat under dust control. No area may be sanded under Chapter 14 until this chapter's coat has dried fully and passed the sanding-readiness decision issued in Step 20.

Where This Skill Fits

The final coat is the last opportunity to control the finished surface with a knife instead of an abrasive. Every ridge, lap mark, or thick edge left standing under this chapter's raking-light inspection becomes sanding work in Chapter 14 — work that removes material, generates dust, and risks cutting through face paper if it is used to correct what the knife should have corrected here.

The thin, wide, heavily feathered pass you use in this chapter is the same feathering discipline introduced in Chapter 11 and built on in Chapter 12, extended one final time to leave the smallest possible transition at every joint edge. The sanding-readiness decision you issue in this chapter's final step is the same type of documented release the Chapter 1 pre-drywall inspection introduced: work does not proceed to the next stage until the responsible party confirms, in writing, that the surface is ready.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the second coat is completely dry before recoating
  • inspecting the surface under strong raking light and marking shallow hollows, edge lines, pinholes, and corner defects
  • scraping ridges, crumbs, and isolated projections before the final coat is applied
  • applying a thin final coat over flat tapered joints, extended and feathered beyond the previous coat's edges
  • applying a wide, shallow final coat over butt joints
  • finishing inside corners using the approved one-side or corner-tool method
  • applying a thin final coat over outside corners and edge trim
  • filling remaining fastener dimples and minor surface defects
  • correcting lap marks, heavy edges, and knife chatter while the coat is workable
  • applying localized touch-up compound where required and issuing a sanding-readiness decision

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin sanding under Chapter 14 before this chapter's coat, including any touch-up compound, has dried fully
  • leave material for sanding to correct rather than removing excess compound with the knife during application
  • apply this chapter's coat over a joint, fastener, or bead defect that Chapters 7, 9, 10, or 12 identify as requiring correction rather than concealment
  • claim a Level 4 or Level 5 finish designation at this stage; finish-level determination occurs only after sanding, primer, and the inspection criteria in the later chapters are actually met

Any defect outside the correction methods described here must be referred back to the identified earlier chapter before this chapter's coat is applied over it.

Trade Note

This chapter produces a dried, inspected final-coat surface, not a sanded or primed finish. A surface that has not received the sanding-readiness decision in Step 20 is not ready for Chapter 14 dust-controlled sanding.

Setup Standard

Do not begin scraping or recoating (Step 3) until the second coat is confirmed completely dry, as required by Step 1. A final coat applied over a second coat that has not finished drying must be removed and reapplied after the underlying coat is confirmed dry.

Shop Tip

Hold the raking light at a low angle and move it slowly along each joint rather than standing it in one place. A hollow or ridge that is invisible under one light position is often obvious the moment the light angle changes.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every final-coat application in this book follows the same sequence: confirm the prior coat is dry, inspect under raking light and scrape only what would interfere with the new coat, apply a thin coat that extends and feathers beyond the previous edges, finish corners and remaining dimples, correct workable defects immediately, and inspect again before allowing the coat to dry. Learning that sequence here carries forward unchanged into Chapter 17, 18, and 19 repair work, and into the Chapter 20 capstone.

Applying a Thin Coat That Removes Rather Than Leaves Material

The final coat is not a thick layer meant to be sanded down; it is a thin layer meant to already be close to the finished plane. Remove excess compound with the knife as you go rather than leaving it for Chapter 14 to remove with abrasive. A coat that requires heavy sanding was applied too thick, too narrow, or without adequate feathering.

Measurement Cue — Extend Beyond the Previous Coat's Edge

Extend the final coat's feathered edge beyond the edge of the second coat at every flat seam, butt joint, and corner. A final coat that stops at the same edge as the second coat concentrates buildup in one line instead of spreading the transition over a wider, less visible area.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Bury Uncorrected Fastening, Bead, or Tape Defects

Do not apply this chapter's coat over a fastener, corner bead, or taped joint that Chapter 7, Chapter 9, or Chapter 10 identifies as requiring correction. A final coat conceals a defect visually without correcting it, and the defect will resurface as cracking, telegraphing, or bond failure after primer and paint.

Field Safety Cue — Overhead Corners and Extended Reach

Wear eye protection when scraping or coating overhead ceiling-to-wall corners. Use a stable platform rather than reaching from a ladder at an angle, and keep the mixing area clear of the path used to carry a loaded mud pan along the wall.

Code Alert — Rated-Assembly Joints

Where a joint receiving the final coat is part of a documented fire-rated, smoke-rated, or sound-rated assembly, apply only the compound type and joint treatment specified in the approved rated-assembly detail. Do not substitute a different compound or method at a rated joint without a documented and approved assembly detail permitting the substitution.

F. Inspection and Preparation Procedure (Steps 1–5)

  1. Confirm the second coat is completely dry. Check representative flat seams, butt joints, and corners from Chapter 12 for full drying and the absence of moisture discoloration.
  2. Inspect the surface under strong raking light. Direct the light nearly parallel to the wall or ceiling and move it slowly along every joint, corner, and dimple location.
  3. Scrape ridges, crumbs, and isolated projections. Remove only what would interfere with the new coat lying flat; do not sand at this stage.
  4. Mark shallow hollows, edge lines, pinholes, and corner defects. Record each location on the finishing log before mixing compound.
  5. Prepare clean finishing compound to the approved consistency. Mix to the manufacturer's stated consistency using clean water within the stated addition limit.

G. Flat-Joint, Butt-Joint, and Corner Coating Procedure (Steps 6–10)

  1. Apply a thin final coat over flat tapered joints. Load the 10-inch knife and draw a single controlled pass along the joint length.
  2. Extend and feather the coat beyond previous edges. Lighten pressure as the pass reaches the outer limit so the coat thins to nothing beyond the second-coat edge.
  3. Apply a wide, shallow final coat over butt joints. Use the 12-inch knife or wide trowel to spread the coat over a broad area so no visible hump remains.
  4. Finish inside corners according to the approved one-side or corner-tool method. Complete one side of the corner in a single pass before finishing the opposite side, or use the corner tool for both sides in one motion where the method specifies it.
  5. Apply a thin final coat over outside corners and edge trim. Use the bead nose and panel plane as guides, matching the technique used in Chapter 11 and Chapter 12.

H. Defect Fill and Correction Procedure (Steps 11–15)

  1. Fill remaining fastener dimples and minor surface defects. Draw the knife across each marked location with enough pressure to fill it flush with the panel surface.
  2. Remove excess compound rather than leaving material for sanding. Wipe the knife clean between passes and avoid building a raised mound at any location.
  3. Maintain clean bead noses, reveals, and trim edges. Wipe stray compound immediately with a damp cloth before it sets.
  4. Check transitions with a straightedge while the coat is workable. Confirm every feathered edge and corner line reads flat and straight before the coat sets.
  5. Correct lap marks, heavy edges, and knife chatter. While the compound remains workable, knife down any irregular pass rather than leaving it to be sanded.

I. Drying and Sanding-Readiness Decision Procedure (Steps 16–20)

  1. Allow the final coat to dry fully. Follow the manufacturer's stated drying or setting time under the temperature and humidity conditions recorded on the finishing log.
  2. Inspect for shrinkage, cracking, exposed tape, pinholes, ridges, and visible edges. Direct the raking light along every joint, corner, and dimple location a second time after drying.
  3. Apply localized touch-up compound where required. Coat only the specific defect locations identified in this inspection.
  4. Allow touch-ups to dry. Follow the same manufacturer drying conditions used for the main coat.
  5. Issue a sanding-readiness decision. Approve the surface for Chapter 14 only after every defect identified under raking light, including touch-up locations, is corrected, dried, and reinspected.

Common Final-Coat Inspection

Inspect every coated area using the same checks regardless of room size or joint length.

  • Flat-joint check: confirm the coat extends and feathers beyond the second-coat edge with no detectable ridge.
  • Butt-joint check: confirm the coat is wide and shallow enough that no hump is visible under raking light.
  • Corner check: confirm inside and outside corners read straight against a straightedge.
  • Dimple and defect check: confirm every remaining depression or flaw is filled flush.
  • Surface check: confirm no unresolved lap marks, heavy edges, or knife chatter remain.
  • Drying check: confirm the coat and any touch-up material have dried fully before Chapter 14 begins.

Complete the Final-Coat Application Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 14.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Shallow Hollow Appears Along a Flat Joint After the Final Coat Dries

Probable cause: the final coat was applied too thin at that location, or the knife pass lifted away from the surface before reaching the joint center.
Confirmation: sight the joint under raking light from at least two directions and confirm the hollow location against the finishing log.
Correction: apply localized touch-up compound to the hollow and feather it into the surrounding dried coat.
Reinspection: allow the touch-up to dry and reinspect under raking light before issuing the sanding-readiness decision.

A Visible Edge Line Remains Where the Final Coat Meets Bare Panel

Probable cause: the feathering pass ended abruptly instead of thinning gradually to nothing.
Confirmation: run a hand across the transition and check it under raking light for a step or ridge.
Correction: knife down the edge while the compound remains workable, or lightly scrape the dried edge and apply a thin corrective feather coat.
Reinspection: recheck the transition under raking light after the correction dries.

Knife Chatter Leaves a Series of Short Ridges Along a Butt Joint

Probable cause: an unsteady or interrupted pass with the wide knife or trowel across the broad butt-joint area.
Confirmation: sight the joint under raking light and confirm the ridge pattern matches an inconsistent pass rather than a hollow.
Correction: knock down the chatter with a clean knife while workable, or scrape lightly once dry and apply a thin corrective skim coat.
Reinspection: recheck the joint under raking light after the correction dries.

An Inside Corner Reads Wavy Rather Than Straight

Probable cause: uneven pressure between the two sides of the corner-tool pass, or the tape crease from Chapter 10 was not centered.
Confirmation: check the corner against a straightedge held along its length and compare to the original tape-crease location.
Correction: apply a corrective one-side pass to the affected side, feathering into the existing corner line.
Reinspection: recheck straightness with the straightedge after the correction dries.

The Final Coat Was Applied Over an Uncorrected Fastening or Bead Defect

Probable cause: the raking-light inspection in Step 2 did not identify the underlying defect, or it was noted but not referred back to the responsible chapter before coating.
Confirmation: scrape the final coat from the affected location and inspect the fastener or bead condition against Chapter 7 or Chapter 9 standards.
Correction: correct the underlying defect under the responsible chapter's methods, then reapply and feather the final coat at that location.
Reinspection: confirm the underlying correction before recoating, then reinspect the dried final coat under raking light.

Cleanup

  • Scrape and rinse taping knives, the wide finishing trowel, the corner tool, and the mud pan before compound dries on them
  • Seal the compound container to prevent premature drying or contamination
  • Wipe stray compound from bead noses, trim reveals, floors, and adjoining finished surfaces
  • File the completed finishing log, including the sanding-readiness decision, with the room's project records
  • Retain the log with the Chapter 14 sanding documentation for the same area

Field Terms

Sanding-Readiness Decision

Finishing Log
The document recording compound type, coated areas, application date, drying conditions, and readiness decisions for each finishing stage.

Performance Check

With the final coat complete and fully dry, review the coated area against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that every flat joint's coat extends and feathers beyond the second-coat edge, every butt joint is wide and shallow enough to show no hump under raking light, every inside and outside corner reads straight against a straightedge, every remaining dimple and minor defect is filled flush, no unresolved lap marks, heavy edges, or knife chatter remain, and all stray compound has been cleaned from bead noses, trim reveals, and adjoining surfaces. Issue the sanding-readiness decision only after every defect identified under raking light, including touch-up locations, has dried and been reinspected.

Project Closeout

You have completed the final-coat application stage of drywall finishing. This chapter required you to apply a final skim coat (DW-073), feather joint edges (DW-074), fill minor defects (DW-075), smooth corner transitions (DW-076), reduce sanding workload (DW-077), and inspect finish readiness (DW-078).

You now have a dried, inspected final-coat surface with every joint, corner, and dimple corrected and a documented sanding-readiness decision. That decision is the controlling document for the next chapter: sanding the surface safely under dust-controlled conditions with minimal material removal.

File your completed Final-Coat Application Log, including the sanding-readiness decision, with the room's project records. Chapter 14 sanding may not begin until this chapter's coat and any touch-up compound have dried fully and the sanding-readiness decision has been issued as approved or conditionally approved with a documented completion date for any remaining items.

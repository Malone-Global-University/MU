Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to apply the second joint-compound coat that widens and levels every flat seam, butt joint, inside corner, and outside corner in the area coated under Chapter 11. The finished work product is a dried, inspected second-coat surface: flat seams extended and feathered beyond the first coat, butt joints built wide and gradual to reduce visible buildup, inside corners coated with a straight line maintained, outside-corner bead flanges widened evenly, and recoated fasteners filled flush, all confirmed flat with a straightedge before the compound sets.

You will confirm the first coat is fully dry and defect-free before recoating; repair any loose tape, bead movement, cracking, or fastening defect before proceeding; select the correct knife or trowel width for each joint type; apply the second coat with feathered edges and a straight corner line; check flatness with a straightedge while the material remains workable; and correct hollows, heavy centers, edge ridges, and uneven corner buildup before the coat dries.

This chapter follows Chapter 11, which covered fasteners, corner bead, edge trim, and any assigned joints with the first coat, and it depends on that coat being fully dry before this chapter begins. It feeds directly into Chapter 13, which applies the final skim coat to the joints and edges this chapter widens. No joint may receive the final coat under Chapter 13 until this chapter's second coat is fully dry and inspected.

Where This Skill Fits

The second coat is where a joint stops looking like a repair and starts reading as a continuous plane. Widening a flat seam beyond the first coat's edge, building a butt joint gradually across a wider span, and holding a straight line on an inside corner are the moves that let the final coat in Chapter 13 do its job with a thin, easy pass instead of trying to correct an undersized second coat.

The straightedge flatness check you use here — sighting and measuring hollows, heavy centers, edge ridges, and uneven corner buildup before the material sets — is the same check Chapter 13 uses to confirm the final coat and the same check Chapter 14 relies on to identify what still needs sanding rather than more compound. A second coat that is checked and corrected here directly reduces the sanding workload in Chapter 14.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the first coat is fully dry and free of moisture discoloration before recoating
  • inspecting for and repairing loose tape, bead movement, cracking, or fastening defects before second coating
  • selecting knife or trowel widths appropriate to flat seams, butt joints, and corners
  • preparing compound to the product-controlled consistency
  • applying the second coat over tapered flat seams, extending beyond the first coat and feathering both edges to the panel face
  • applying a wider, gradual coat over butt joints to reduce visible buildup
  • coating inside corners while maintaining a straight corner line
  • widening both flanges of outside-corner bead evenly
  • recoating fasteners where shrinkage or dimples remain
  • checking joint flatness with a straightedge before the material sets and correcting hollows, heavy centers, edge ridges, and uneven corner buildup

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • apply the second coat over tape, bead, or fastening defects that have not been repaired; defects identified in Step 3 must be corrected in Step 4 before proceeding
  • apply the final coat under Chapter 13 before this chapter's coat has dried completely, as required by Step 18
  • leave joint flatness uncorrected on the assumption that sanding in Chapter 14 will remove hollows, heavy centers, or uneven corner buildup
  • claim a Level 4 or Level 5 finish designation at this stage; finish-level determination occurs only after the operations and inspection criteria in the later chapters are actually met

Any tape, bead, or fastening defect outside the correction methods described here must be referred back to Chapter 7, Chapter 9, or Chapter 10 before this chapter's coat is applied.

Trade Note

This chapter produces a widened, leveled second-coat surface, not a finished skim. Joints and corners coated under this chapter still require the final coat in Chapter 13 before sanding begins in Chapter 14.

Setup Standard

Do not begin applying the second coat (Step 7) until the first coat is confirmed fully dry and free of moisture discoloration, and every identified tape, bead, or fastening defect is repaired, as required by Steps 1–4. A second coat applied over an unrepaired defect or over compound that has not finished drying must be removed and reapplied after the underlying condition is corrected.

Shop Tip

Widen a butt joint gradually across a much wider span than a flat seam — feather it out far enough that the eye cannot find where the buildup begins. A butt joint that is only widened as much as a flat seam will always read as a visible hump under raking light.

Widening Joints and Checking Flatness

Select a wider knife or trowel for the second coat than was used for the first, and let the wider tool set the span of the widened joint. Apply the coat so it extends beyond the edge of the first coat on both sides, and feather each edge into the panel face rather than stopping abruptly. Check flatness with a straightedge across the joint while the compound remains workable, so hollows, heavy centers, and edge ridges can be corrected before the material sets rather than after.

Measurement Cue — Check Flatness Before the Material Sets

Lay the straightedge across each coated joint while the compound is still workable. A gap under the straightedge at the center indicates a hollow; a gap at the edges with contact at the center indicates a heavy center. Correct either condition immediately rather than waiting for the coat to dry.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Recoat Over an Unrepaired Defect

Do not apply the second coat over loose tape, bead movement, cracking, or an uncorrected fastening defect. Repair the condition using the applicable Chapter 7, Chapter 9, or Chapter 10 method before proceeding. A second coat applied over an active defect will fail again once the defect moves.

Field Safety Cue — Overhead Coating and Housekeeping

Wear eye protection when coating overhead ceiling-to-wall corners or scraping high ridges from the prior stage. Keep the mixing area separate from the application area to avoid tripping over buckets, paddles, and cords while carrying a loaded mud pan across the room.

Code Alert — Rated-Assembly Joints

Where a joint being widened is part of a documented fire-rated, smoke-rated, or sound-rated assembly, apply only the compound type and joint treatment specified in the approved rated-assembly detail. Do not substitute a different compound or coating method at a rated joint without a documented and approved assembly detail permitting the substitution.

F. Preparation and Defect-Repair Procedure (Steps 1–6)

  1. Verify the first coat is fully dry and free from moisture discoloration. Check representative fasteners, bead runs, and assigned joints from Chapter 11 for dryness and normal color before proceeding.
  2. Scrape high ridges and remove dust without damaging tape. Use the 10-inch knife to remove only what would prevent the new coat from lying flat.
  3. Inspect for loose tape, bead movement, cracks, or fastening defects. Check every flat seam, inside corner, and bead run against the Chapter 9, Chapter 10, and Chapter 7 acceptance standards.
  4. Repair defects before second coating. Reset loose tape, replace a moved or crooked bead, or correct a fastening defect using the applicable chapter method before proceeding.
  5. Select knife or trowel widths appropriate to flat seams, butt joints, and corners. Use the 10-inch knife for flat seams and fastener recoating, the 12-inch knife for butt joints and corner-bead flanges, and the corner trowel or applicator for inside corners.
  6. Prepare compound to the product-controlled consistency. Mix to the manufacturer's stated consistency using clean water within the stated addition limit.

G. Flat-Seam, Butt-Joint, and Corner Coating Procedure (Steps 7–13)

  1. Apply the second coat over tapered flat seams, extending beyond the first coat. Center the wider knife on the joint and let the coat spread past both edges of the Chapter 11 application.
  2. Feather both edges to the panel face. Lighten knife pressure as the pass reaches the outer edge so the coat thins to nothing at the panel surface.
  3. Apply a wider, gradual coat over butt joints to reduce visible buildup. Spread the coat across a span noticeably wider than a flat seam so the buildup is not concentrated at the joint center.
  4. Coat one side of inside corners at a time when the system requires it. Allow the first side to set before coating the second side if the product or corner tool requires it.
  5. Maintain a straight corner line. Sight along the corner as you coat and correct the knife path immediately if the line begins to wander.
  6. Apply the second coat over outside-corner bead, widening both flanges evenly. Use the bead nose and panel plane as guides, matching the technique used in Chapter 11.
  7. Recoat fasteners where shrinkage or dimples remain. Fill any depression still visible after the first coat dried, using the same technique as the initial fill.

H. Flatness Inspection and Correction Procedure (Steps 14–20)

  1. Maintain consistent pressure to avoid ridges and lap marks. Keep knife angle and pressure steady across the full length of each pass.
  2. Clean compound from bead noses, trim edges, and adjoining surfaces. Wipe drips and overspread immediately with a damp cloth before they set.
  3. Check joint flatness with a straightedge before material sets. Lay the straightedge across each coated joint and corner while the compound remains workable.
  4. Correct hollows, heavy centers, edge ridges, and uneven corner buildup. Add compound to hollows and knife down heavy centers, edge ridges, or uneven buildup before the material sets.
  5. Allow the second coat to dry completely. Follow the manufacturer's stated drying or setting time before proceeding to Chapter 13.
  6. Inspect under raking light and mark areas needing final-coat correction. Identify any remaining low spot, ridge, or inconsistent corner for attention in Chapter 13.
  7. Document drying conditions and unusual defects. Complete the finishing log entry for the coated area, noting any condition that requires attention in the final coat.

Common Second-Coat Inspection

Inspect every coated area using the same checks regardless of room size or joint length.

  • Flat-seam check: confirm the coat extends beyond the first coat and feathers cleanly to the panel face on both edges.
  • Butt-joint check: confirm the buildup is spread gradually across a wide span with no abrupt hump.
  • Inside-corner check: confirm the corner line remains straight along its full length.
  • Outside-corner check: confirm both bead flanges are widened evenly with consistent thickness.
  • Fastener check: confirm every recoated fastener is filled flush with no visible depression.
  • Flatness check: confirm no hollow, heavy center, edge ridge, or uneven corner buildup remains under the straightedge.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Straightedge Reveals a Hollow at the Center of a Flat Seam

Probable cause: insufficient compound was drawn into the center of the pass, or the knife rode too high above the panel surface.
Confirmation: lay the straightedge across the joint at several points along its length and check for a gap at the center.
Correction: apply additional compound to the hollow while the material remains workable and reknife the pass with consistent pressure.
Reinspection: recheck the joint with the straightedge before the coat sets and again after it dries.

A Butt Joint Reads as a Visible Hump Under Raking Light

Probable cause: the second coat was widened only as much as a flat seam rather than spread across a wider, more gradual span.
Confirmation: sight the joint under raking light from a low angle and confirm the buildup is concentrated rather than gradual.
Correction: feather the coat further outward on both sides using a wider knife, tapering the buildup across a broader span.
Reinspection: resight under raking light after the correction dries.

An Inside Corner Line Wanders Off Straight

Probable cause: the corner tool was not sighted continuously during the pass, allowing the knife to drift.
Confirmation: sight along the corner from one end and compare against a straightedge or chalk line reference.
Correction: scrape the affected section while workable and recoat with continuous sighting along the corner.
Reinspection: resight the corner after the correction dries.

A Recoated Fastener Still Shows a Depression After Drying

Probable cause: the second-coat fill was applied too thin or the surrounding feathering pulled compound away from the depression.
Confirmation: run a hand across the location and compare against side lighting from at least two directions.
Correction: apply an additional thin fill directly over the depression and feather it into the surrounding surface.
Reinspection: reinspect under side lighting after the correction dries.

The Second Coat Was Applied Over Loose Tape or a Cracked Joint

Probable cause: the inspection in Step 3 was skipped or the defect was not repaired before recoating.
Confirmation: scrape the affected area and inspect tape bond or crack extent against the Chapter 10 acceptance standards.
Correction: lift and reset or replace the defective tape under Chapter 10 methods, allow it to dry, then reapply the second coat.
Reinspection: confirm tape bond before recoating, then reinspect the dried second coat.

Cleanup

  • Scrape and rinse taping knives, the corner trowel or applicator, and the mud pan before compound dries on them
  • Seal the compound container to prevent premature drying or contamination
  • Wipe stray compound from bead noses, trim reveals, floors, and adjoining finished surfaces
  • File the completed finishing log with the room's project records
  • Retain the log with the Chapter 13 final-coat documentation for the same area

Corner Line

Heavy Center

Hollow

Feathering
Drawing the knife so the compound edge thins to nothing at the outer limit of the coat.

Raking Light

Performance Check

With the second coat complete and fully dry, review the coated area against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that every flat seam extends beyond the first coat with feathered edges, every butt joint is spread gradually with no visible hump, every inside-corner line remains straight, both flanges of every outside-corner bead run are widened evenly, every recoated fastener is filled flush, and no hollow, heavy center, edge ridge, or uneven corner buildup remains under a straightedge. Do not release the area to Chapter 13 until the coat has dried fully under the manufacturer's stated conditions.

Project Closeout

You have completed the second-coat application stage of drywall finishing. This chapter required you to mix compound to workable consistency (DW-067), widen flat seams (DW-068), coat inside corners (DW-069), coat outside corners (DW-070), feather edges (DW-071), and inspect second-coat flatness (DW-072).

You now have a dried, inspected second-coat surface with every flat seam, butt joint, inside corner, and outside corner widened, feathered, and confirmed flat, and every recoated fastener filled flush. That surface is the controlling condition for the next chapter: applying the final skim coat that produces smooth transitions and a surface requiring only limited corrective sanding.

File your completed Second-Coat Application Log with the room's project records. Chapter 13 final-coat work may not begin until this chapter's coat has dried fully under controlled temperature and humidity and every identified defect has been corrected and reinspected.

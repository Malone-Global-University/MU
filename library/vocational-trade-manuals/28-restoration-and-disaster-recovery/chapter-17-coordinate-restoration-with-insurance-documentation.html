Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build and maintain one complete, factual insurance-support file for a residential restoration project, and to keep that file accurate and current from the initial incident through final closeout. The finished work product is a secure claim folder with indexed photographs, a damaged-material log, labor and equipment and material and disposal logs, an estimate register, a communication log, and a periodic claim-support summary — all organized so an insurer, adjuster, or owner can review exactly what happened, what was done, and what it cost, without you making coverage, cause-of-loss, depreciation, or settlement decisions.

You will confirm claim and contact information, build the folder structure, preserve original photographs and metadata, log every removed material, track labor, equipment, materials, and disposal separately, register every estimate version, record every communication, track approval status, separate insurer-related scope from owner-selected upgrades, document hidden damage as it is discovered, and reconcile the final invoice against the authorized scope.

This chapter draws directly on the damage log and photograph index built in Chapter 1, the equipment records from Chapters 2 and 5, the removal and moisture records from Chapters 4 through 6, and the restoration action plan built in Chapter 16. The file you build here becomes part of the record verified in Chapter 19's final inspection and archived in the Chapter 20 capstone.

  • RD-098 — Preserve damaged-material records with location, quantity, condition, and disposition.
  • RD-099 — Document labor and materials using separate, dated logs.
  • RD-100 — Track estimates by version, scope, and approval status.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter before this one has produced records — the Chapter 1 damage log and photographs, the Chapter 2 readiness inspection, the Chapter 3 through 6 water-loss records, the Chapter 7 through 11 mold and smoke records, the Chapter 12 through 14 exterior stabilization records, and the Chapter 15 debris logs, and the Chapter 16 restoration action plan. This chapter does not create new field work; it organizes and maintains those records into one insurance-support file that supports the claim without controlling it. Chapter 18's rebuild and Chapter 19's final inspection both draw on this file, and the Chapter 20 capstone archives it as the closing record of the entire project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • building a secure, organized claim folder linked to the project record
  • preserving and indexing photographs, damaged-material records, labor, equipment, material, and disposal logs
  • creating and maintaining an estimate register across versions, scopes, and approval status
  • recording communications and confirming verbal direction in writing
  • separating insurer-related scope from owner-selected improvement and unrelated repair
  • documenting hidden damage discovered after removal
  • preparing periodic and final claim-support summaries and reconciling the final invoice

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • determine coverage, cause of loss, liability, depreciation, or settlement value; those decisions belong to the insurer and policy process
  • interpret an adjuster's or insurer's silence as approval, or represent a pending estimate as an approved one
  • alter, crop, or delete original photographs, notes, or communications to change what the record shows
  • disclose policy information, financial information, access codes, occupant information, or private photographs outside the approved distribution

Any coverage, cause, or settlement question that arises while building this file must be routed to the insurer or adjuster and recorded as a pending item, not answered by the person maintaining the file.

Trade Note

An insurance-support file is only useful if it is accurate and complete before anyone needs it. Treat every entry as something that may be reviewed months after the work is done, by someone who was not present. If a log entry cannot stand on its own without your memory to explain it, it is not complete yet.

Setup Standard

Do not begin building the claim file until claim number, insurer contact, adjuster contact, and policyholder contact are confirmed. Do not represent any preliminary amount, pending estimate, or verbal statement as an approved cost or coverage decision.

Shop Tip

Confirm every important verbal direction in writing the same day you receive it, even a single follow-up sentence. A confirmed verbal direction is a record; an unconfirmed one is only your recollection.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every insurance-support file in this book follows the same discipline: separate observed fact from opinion, separate emergency work from mitigation and rebuild, separate insurer-related scope from owner-selected improvement, and never let a coverage or settlement decision creep into a documentation task. That discipline is the same one built in Chapter 1's damage log and it governs every log this chapter requires.

Coverage Belongs to the Insurer, Not the Documentation Task

This chapter's file supports the claim; it does not decide it. Coverage, cause-of-loss determination, depreciation, and settlement value are the insurer's and the policy process's decisions. Recording facts, dates, quantities, and communications accurately is the whole job; interpreting what those facts mean for payment is not.

Documentation Cue — Index Every Photograph and Log Entry to the Claim

Every photograph, log entry, and document added to the claim folder must be indexed by phase, date, room or area, and identifier consistent with the Chapter 1 damage log. Do not add an unindexed photograph or an undated log entry to the file.

Critical Safety Cue — Documentation Does Not Substitute for Hazard Response

If building this file requires revisiting an active work area, treat every structural, electrical, gas, or contamination hazard under this chapter's authority the same as any other chapter: stop, establish an exclusion zone, and notify the responsible party. Do not photograph or sample a hazard you are not authorized to approach.

Code Alert — Coverage and Settlement Decisions Are Not Yours to Make

Do not assign insurance coverage, cause-of-loss determination, liability, depreciation, or claim value in any log, summary, or communication. Route coverage and settlement questions to the insurer or adjuster and record the question as pending.

Field Safety Cue — Protect Private Information

Store policy information, financial information, access codes, occupant information, and private photographs only within the secure claim folder, and share them only with the parties identified in the communication instructions.

F. Preparation — Claim Setup and Folder Structure

  1. Obtain claim number, insurer contact, adjuster contact, policyholder contact, authorized contractor contact, and communication instructions. Record each on the claim cover sheet before proceeding.
  2. Clarify that coverage, cause, depreciation, deductible, and settlement decisions belong to the insurer and policy process. Note this limitation on the cover sheet so it is visible to anyone reviewing the file.
  3. Create a secure claim folder linked to the project record. Use the project's existing identifier from the Chapter 1 damage log so the two records stay linked.
  4. Create subfolders for incident, emergency work, mitigation, contents, demolition, drying, temporary repair, estimates, rebuild, payments, and closeout. Confirm every subfolder exists before filing any document.

G. Photograph and Damaged-Material Records

  1. Preserve original photographs with date and metadata where available. Do not edit, crop, rotate, or delete an original photograph before it is filed.
  2. Create a photograph index by room, component, phase, and damage identifier. Use the same identifiers established in the Chapter 1 damage log.
  3. Keep before, during, exposed-cavity, drying, repair, and final photographs separate and traceable. File each phase's photographs in its matching subfolder.
  4. Create a damaged-material log with location, material, quantity, condition, disposition, and photograph reference. Complete one entry per removed material as it is removed.
  5. Preserve representative pieces, labels, serial numbers, and samples only when authorized and safe. Label and store each retained item with its damaged-material log entry number.

H. Emergency, Labor, Equipment, Material, and Disposal Records

  1. Record emergency actions taken to prevent additional damage. Describe the action, the date and time, and the person who authorized or performed it.
  2. Record why immediate action could not await adjuster inspection when applicable. State the specific condition that required action before inspection.
  3. Create a daily labor log by person, company, date, task, location, and hours. Complete this log at the end of each work day.
  4. Create an equipment log by item, serial number, location, start, stop, setting, and operating day. Update this log whenever equipment is installed, adjusted, or removed.
  5. Create a material log by product, quantity, supplier, date, purpose, and cost. Complete this log at the time of purchase or delivery.
  6. Create a disposal log with debris category, quantity, destination, ticket, manifest, and cost. Attach the disposal ticket or manifest to the corresponding log entry.
  7. Store moisture maps, readings, environmental data, drying goals, and completion records. File these in the drying subfolder, cross-referenced to the Chapter 5 drying-completion record.
  8. Store permits, inspection records, professional reports, source-repair invoices, and utility records. File each in the subfolder matching the phase of work it supports.

I. Estimate and Communication Records

  1. Create an estimate register for emergency, mitigation, contents, rebuild, supplements, and owner upgrades. Add a new row for every distinct estimate produced.
  2. Identify estimate version, date, preparer, scope, assumptions, and status. Update the status field whenever the estimate's approval condition changes.
  3. Track insurer or adjuster requests for photographs, measurements, samples, invoices, and clarification. Log each request with the date received and the date fulfilled.
  4. Respond with factual records and avoid unsupported opinions. Limit responses to what is documented in the file; do not add interpretation of coverage or cause.
  5. Record every phone call, meeting, email, portal upload, inspection, and site visit. Complete a communication log entry the same day the contact occurs.
  6. Confirm material verbal direction in writing. Send a written confirmation summarizing the verbal direction and file the confirmation with the communication log entry.
  7. Track approval, partial approval, denial, pending review, and owner authorization separately. Use a distinct status field for each estimate and each scope item.
  8. Do not interpret silence as approval. Mark an item pending until an explicit approval, partial approval, or denial is received and logged.

J. Scope Separation and Closeout Reconciliation

  1. Identify work proceeding under emergency authority, owner authorization, insurer approval, or contractor risk. Tag every work package in the file with its authorization basis.
  2. Separate insurer-related scope from owner-selected improvement and unrelated repair. Maintain this separation in every log, not only in the estimate register.
  3. Document hidden damage discovered after removal with photographs, measurements, and professional findings. File hidden-damage records in the subfolder for the phase in which they were discovered, cross-referenced to the change-control record from Chapter 4 or Chapter 16.
  4. Prepare supplement requests through the approved estimate and authorization process. Do not submit a supplement outside the estimate register or without supporting photographs and measurements.
  5. Track payments, deductibles, deposits, retainage, invoices, and disputed amounts according to contract and accounting controls. Record each transaction with date, amount, and reference document.
  6. Protect policy information, financial information, access codes, occupant information, and private photographs. Restrict access to the secure claim folder to the parties identified on the cover sheet.
  7. Prepare periodic claim-support summaries by phase. Issue a summary at each major phase transition identified in the Chapter 16 restoration action plan.
  8. Reconcile the final invoice with authorized scope, changes, equipment, labor, materials, tax, and payments. Cross-check every invoiced item against a corresponding log entry or approved estimate line.
  9. Prepare the final insurance-support package without representing it as a coverage determination. State plainly in the package that coverage and settlement decisions remain the insurer's.
  10. Archive communications, records, and acknowledgment of delivery. Confirm the package was delivered and record who received it and when.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed insurance-support file using the same checks regardless of incident type or project size.

  • Folder check: confirm the claim folder contains every required subfolder and is linked to the Chapter 1 project record.
  • Traceability check: confirm every photograph is indexed by room, component, phase, and damage identifier.
  • Log-separation check: confirm labor, equipment, material, and disposal entries are never combined in one log.
  • Estimate check: confirm every estimate version is registered with a current status and no version is missing.
  • Communication check: confirm every contact is logged and every material verbal direction has a written confirmation on file.
  • Scope-separation check: confirm insurer scope, owner upgrades, and unrelated repair remain distinguishable throughout the file.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm the final invoice matches authorized scope, changes, and payments before the package is delivered.

Complete the Insurance-Support File Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 18.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Estimate Version Cannot Be Matched to Its Approval Status

Probable cause: a revised estimate was produced without a new version entry in the estimate register, or the status field was never updated after a response was received.
Confirmation: compare every estimate document in the estimates subfolder against the estimate register to find any estimate without a matching version row or current status.
Correction: add the missing version entry with date, preparer, scope, assumptions, and status, and update the status field from the most recent communication log entry.
Reinspection: repeat the estimate check across the full register and subfolder.

A Verbal Direction Was Never Confirmed in Writing

Probable cause: the communication log entry recorded that a call occurred but did not include or reference a written confirmation of the direction given.
Confirmation: review each communication log entry describing a decision or direction and confirm whether a written confirmation is attached or referenced.
Correction: send a written confirmation summarizing the direction as understood, and attach it to the original communication log entry with the date sent.
Reinspection: repeat the communication check across the full log.

Insurer Scope and Owner Upgrades Were Combined in One Log Entry

Probable cause: a labor, material, or estimate entry recorded insurer-related work and an owner-selected improvement together without separating cost or scope.
Confirmation: review entries against the Chapter 16 restoration action plan to identify any item that mixes insurer scope with an owner upgrade.
Correction: split the entry into separate line items, one for insurer-related scope and one for the owner-selected improvement, each with its own cost and authorization basis.
Reinspection: repeat the scope-separation check across the full file.

Hidden Damage Was Repaired Without a Documented Change

Probable cause: concealed damage was discovered and corrected during removal or rebuild, but no photographs, measurements, or change-control entry were created before the area was closed.
Confirmation: compare the removal and rebuild photograph sets against the damaged-material log and change-control records to find any repaired condition without supporting documentation.
Correction: document the condition as completely as remaining evidence allows, note that photographs were not taken before closure, and add the item to the estimate register as a documented change.
Reinspection: repeat the damaged-material completeness check across the full log.

The Final Invoice Does Not Reconcile Against the Logs

Probable cause: an invoiced item was never entered in the labor, equipment, material, or disposal log, or a logged cost was omitted from the final invoice.
Confirmation: cross-check every line of the final invoice against a corresponding log entry or approved estimate line, and cross-check every log entry against the invoice.
Correction: add the missing log entry or correct the invoice to match the documented record, and note the correction with a date and reason.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check before the final claim-support package is delivered.

Silence From the Insurer Was Treated as Approval

Probable cause: work proceeded on a submitted estimate or supplement because no response had been received, and the item was marked approved without a documented approval.
Confirmation: check the estimate register and communication log for a documented approval, partial approval, or denial matching the item marked approved.
Correction: correct the status to pending, follow up in writing, and record the follow-up in the communication log; do not change the status to approved until a documented response is received.
Reinspection: repeat the estimate check across the full register.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a complete, filed insurance-support record, not with any physical work area to clean.

  • Back up the claim folder, photographs, and all logs before closing out any work session
  • Return original invoices, tickets, manifests, and communications to the claim folder without altering the originals
  • Store the photograph index, damaged-material log, labor/equipment/material/disposal logs, estimate register, and communication log together under the same claim folder
  • Confirm access to the secure claim folder remains limited to the parties identified on the claim cover sheet
  • Deliver periodic claim-support summaries as required by Section J and record delivery acknowledgment

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to stop or control one limited, previously classified water source, address the electrical and structural hazards that surround it, remove approved standing clean water, and produce a complete emergency-stabilization record. The finished work product is a stabilized water-loss area with the source stopped or reliably controlled, electrical and structural hazards addressed, standing water removed from safe accessible points, affected boundaries documented, and a clear next-step authorization for selective removal, drying, or professional mitigation.

You will confirm the water source and its contamination classification, refer any water outside the approved clean-water scope, locate and operate shutoffs only through an approved procedure, coordinate electrical isolation with the utility or a qualified electrician when wet conditions require it, install only approved temporary catches or diversions, extract standing water from safe accessible points, and document every action against the initial damage record built in Chapter 1.

This chapter depends directly on Chapter 1's damage log, safety register, and affected-area map, and on Chapter 2's controlled work area. Chapter 4 begins from this chapter's stabilization record to remove non-salvageable wet materials, and the Chapter 20 capstone performs this exact source-control sequence as its own controlling step.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-013 — Identify the water source and its contamination classification before further action.
  • RD-014 — Shut off water at the correct point only through an approved procedure.
  • RD-015 — Stop active water intrusion using an approved temporary control that does not conceal the source.
  • RD-016 — Remove standing water safely from approved accessible areas.
  • RD-017 — Protect electrical hazards in or near the wet area without entering water or touching wet equipment.
  • RD-018 — Document stabilization work in a form that authorizes the next restoration step.

Where This Skill Fits

Source control is the hinge point of every water-damage project in this book. Nothing in Chapter 4's removal, Chapter 5's drying, or Chapter 6's rebuild is valid until the source is stopped or reliably controlled and the surrounding hazards are addressed. This chapter also carries forward Chapter 1's classification and safety-register habits: every decision here — whether to shut off a valve, whether to enter standing water, whether to extract from a given point — depends on information already established and documented, not on a fresh guess made at the water's edge.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming a known or investigable water source that has already been classified as clean water under the approved process
  • operating a main or fixture shutoff only through an approved, non-forced procedure
  • installing approved temporary catches, diversions, caps, pans, or barriers that do not conceal the source
  • coordinating electrical isolation with the utility or a qualified electrician when wet-area conditions require it
  • removing standing clean water from safe, accessible points using extraction equipment appropriate to the classification
  • documenting source status, stabilization actions, and the next authorized step

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • handle sewage, floodwater, groundwater, chemical water, fire-suppression runoff, or any water not classified as clean under the approved process
  • force a damaged, leaking, unsupported, or corroded valve, or make any unapproved permanent repair to the source
  • enter standing water or touch wet electrical equipment to reach a disconnect, or restore power without utility or qualified-electrician clearance
  • remove finish materials, begin drying, or perform any reconstruction; those actions belong to Chapters 4 through 6 and require their own authorization

Any condition that reveals sewage, floodwater, unknown contamination, structural instability, or energized wet equipment must be referred immediately, not stabilized under this chapter's basic scope.

Trade Note

Stabilization is not a repair. A capped supply line, a diverted drip, or a shop-vacuumed floor is a controlled condition, not a finished project. Every temporary control installed in this chapter must be labeled, monitored, and scheduled for a permanent, qualified repair — confusing the two is how temporary controls get forgotten and fail later.

Setup Standard

Do not enter standing water or approach wet electrical equipment until the source classification, structural stability, and electrical status from Chapter 1 and Chapter 2 have been reconfirmed. Do not force any shutoff valve, and do not make an unapproved permanent repair during stabilization.

Shop Tip

Photograph the shutoff valve's condition before you touch it — corrosion, a missing handle, or a weeping packing nut tells you whether the valve is likely to close cleanly or fail. Deciding this after the valve is already leaking is the more expensive way to learn it.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every water-damage response in this book follows the same order: classify the source, refer anything outside the approved scope, address electrical and structural hazards before touching water, control the source through an approved procedure, remove standing water only from safe points, and document the result as a specific status rather than an assumption. Learning that order here, on a limited clean-water loss, is what makes Chapter 4's removal decisions and Chapter 5's drying goals reliable later.

Stopped, Controlled, Intermittent, Unknown, or Referred

Every source status entry in the stabilization record must be one of these five categories, not a general note that "the leak was fixed." A valve that has been closed but not observed for a return of flow is intermittent, not stopped. A source that has not been located is unknown, not assumed minor. Recording the wrong category here misleads every later chapter that depends on it.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Enter Water to Reach a Disconnect

Do not enter standing water or touch wet electrical equipment, receptacles, wiring, panels, appliances, extension cords, pumps, or tools to reach a disconnect. Have power isolated by the utility or a qualified electrician whenever wet-area conditions require it, and treat all such equipment as hazardous until it has been isolated and evaluated.

Code Alert — Sewage, Floodwater, and Unknown Water Are Referred

Stop work and refer sewage, floodwater, groundwater, chemical water, or any water that has not been confirmed as clean under the approved classification process. This chapter's basic scope does not include contaminated or unknown water.

Field Safety Cue — Do Not Force a Damaged Valve

Inspect the shutoff for access, damage, corrosion, and support before operating it. Do not force a damaged, leaking, unsupported, or corroded valve. If the valve cannot be operated safely, refer the shutoff to qualified plumbing or utility personnel and document the condition.

Documentation Cue — Record Extraction by the Project Method

Record extracted water quantity or equipment operating time using the same method established for the project, and tie each entry to the room or area identifier from the Chapter 1 damage log. Do not leave extraction activity undocumented because the water is "mostly gone."

Do not leave any of these fields blank, and do not record a status category the observed evidence does not support.

F. Preparation — Source Confirmation and Referral Screening

  1. Confirm the water source is known or establish a source-investigation plan. Use the Chapter 1 damage log and safety register as the starting reference.
  2. Classify the water source and contamination risk through the approved process. Record the classifying party and the classification result before continuing.
  3. Stop work and refer sewage, floodwater, groundwater, chemical water, or unknown contaminated water outside the limited scope. Document the referral, the referred party, and the date.
  4. Inspect exterior weather, roof, wall, plumbing, appliance, condensate, drain, and fire-suppression conditions related to the loss. Record each inspected condition and its relevance to the source.

G. Source Shutoff

  1. Locate the main water shutoff and local fixture or appliance shutoffs. Record the location and type of each.
  2. Inspect shutoffs for access, damage, corrosion, support, and likelihood of safe operation. Photograph the shutoff before operating it.
  3. Coordinate utility interruption with occupants, property management, medical needs, fire protection, and essential equipment. Record who was notified and when.
  4. Shut off water only through the approved procedure. Do not deviate from the manufacturer or utility-specified operating method.
  5. Do not force a damaged, leaking, unsupported, or corroded valve. Refer any valve that cannot be safely operated.
  6. Confirm that flow has stopped or reduced and identify residual drainage. Observe and record the result over a defined monitoring period.
  7. Arrange qualified plumbing, roofing, envelope, mechanical, or utility repair for the source. Record the referral and scheduled date if available.
  8. Use an approved temporary catch, diversion, cap, pan, or barrier only when it will not increase damage or conceal the source. Label the temporary control and record its installation date.
  9. Do not make an unapproved permanent repair during emergency stabilization. Confirm the installed control is temporary and monitored.

H. Electrical Protection

  1. Inspect electrical equipment, cords, devices, panels, and circuits in or near the wet area. Use the non-contact voltage detector from a safe, dry position.
  2. Do not enter water or touch wet electrical equipment to reach a disconnect. Maintain a safe distance until isolation is confirmed by a qualified party.
  3. Have power isolated through the utility or qualified electrician when required. Record the isolating party, method, and time.
  4. Establish safe temporary lighting and protected power outside the wet zone. Confirm cords and connections remain dry and protected.

I. Structural Protection and Water Removal

  1. Inspect ceilings, walls, floors, cabinets, and contents for collapse, delamination, and falling hazards. Record any condition requiring restricted access.
  2. Support or restrict access beneath sagging or water-loaded materials. Post warning signage at the restricted area.
  3. Move approved unaffected contents out of the wet area or elevate them on compatible supports. Record the disposition of each moved item.
  4. Protect floors and finishes along the equipment and extraction route. Install floor protection before moving extraction equipment.
  5. Select extraction equipment appropriate to the water classification, depth, access, and electrical conditions. Confirm the equipment is rated for the confirmed classification.
  6. Confirm the approved discharge location will not overload drains, spread contamination, flood the site, or affect neighbors. Record the confirmed discharge location before extraction begins.
  7. Remove standing water from the safest accessible point. Begin extraction only after the discharge location is confirmed.
  8. Work from shallow or dry areas and do not enter unstable, electrically uncertain, or contaminated water. Maintain the restricted zones established in Section I.1–I.2.
  9. Monitor for shifting materials, hidden holes, floating debris, and equipment leakage. Stop and reassess if any of these conditions appears.
  10. Remove water from accessible low points, pans, cabinets, and protected cavities only within the approved scope. Do not extend extraction beyond areas already documented in the Chapter 1 damage log without a change record.
  11. Document extracted quantity or equipment time using the project method. Record this on the extraction record for each area.

J. Post-Extraction Documentation and Handoff

  1. Inspect for water migration into adjacent rooms, floors below, wall cavities, crawlspaces, and insulation. Record each migration path found.
  2. Create an initial moisture map after standing water removal. Use labeled, repeatable measurement points consistent with the Chapter 1 record.
  3. Install temporary leak monitoring, collection, or weather protection where required. Label each installed item as temporary.
  4. Post warnings and maintain restricted access until hazards are resolved. Confirm signage remains in place at project closeout.
  5. Photograph source, shutoff, temporary controls, extraction, affected boundaries, and remaining conditions. Index each photograph to the corresponding record entry.
  6. Record the source status as stopped, temporarily controlled, intermittent, unknown, or referred. Select exactly one category and support it with the documented evidence.
  7. Prepare the next-step authorization for selective removal, drying, environmental assessment, or professional mitigation. State the specific next action and the party authorized to perform it.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed stabilization work using the same checks regardless of source type or property size.

  • Classification check: confirm every source has a recorded classification and, if referred, a documented reason and referral party.
  • Shutoff check: confirm every shutoff attempt is recorded with location, condition, and result, and no valve was forced.
  • Electrical check: confirm every wet electrical device or circuit has a recorded isolation status before any power decision is made.
  • Extraction check: confirm every area with standing water has an extraction record with quantity or time and a confirmed discharge location.
  • Moisture-map check: confirm a labeled post-extraction moisture map exists for every affected area.
  • Authorization check: confirm the next-step authorization is specific, supported by the documented source status, and assigned to a named party.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Damaged Valve Was Forced and Began Leaking

Probable cause: the shutoff was operated without first inspecting for corrosion, damage, or support, and was forced past resistance.
Confirmation: inspect the valve body, packing nut, and surrounding piping for new leakage, cracking, or movement.
Correction: stop operating the valve, isolate flow at the next upstream shutoff if available, and refer the valve to qualified plumbing repair.
Reinspection: confirm flow is stopped at the alternate shutoff and document the referral before continuing.

Extraction Began Before the Discharge Location Was Confirmed

Probable cause: extraction equipment was staged and started without first confirming the discharge point could safely receive the volume.
Confirmation: check the extraction record for a confirmed discharge-location entry against the time extraction began.
Correction: stop extraction, confirm or redirect the discharge location, and resume only once confirmed.
Reinspection: verify no overflow, backflow, or unapproved discharge occurred during the affected period.

A Wet Electrical Device Was Left Unaddressed

Probable cause: the electrical inspection in Section H was skipped or only partially completed before extraction began.
Confirmation: cross-check the electrical protection record against every device, cord, and panel visible in the affected area.
Correction: stop work in the affected area, coordinate isolation with the utility or a qualified electrician, and record the result before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the electrical check across the full affected area before further work proceeds.

The Source Status Was Recorded as "Fixed" Instead of a Defined Category

Probable cause: the inspector recorded a general impression rather than selecting stopped, temporarily controlled, intermittent, unknown, or referred.
Confirmation: review the stabilization record for a specific status category supported by observed evidence.
Correction: reclassify the source using the defined categories and support the entry with the monitoring period and evidence observed.
Reinspection: repeat the source-status review before preparing the next-step authorization.

A Temporary Control Concealed Rather Than Controlled the Source

Probable cause: a cap, patch, or barrier was installed in a way that hid the leaking component from later inspection instead of managing the flow.
Confirmation: verify the temporary control can be removed or opened for inspection without additional damage, and that the source remains visible or accessible for monitoring.
Correction: remove and reinstall the control using an approved method that preserves access to the source.
Reinspection: confirm the revised control is labeled, monitored, and does not obscure the source from the next inspection.

Removal Began Before Stabilization Was Documented

Probable cause: the team proceeded directly to material removal without completing the source status record and next-step authorization.
Confirmation: check whether a completed stabilization record and next-step authorization exist before any Chapter 4 activity began.
Correction: stop removal work, complete the stabilization record retroactively using photographs and notes taken during the work, and obtain the required authorization.
Reinspection: confirm the stabilization record is complete and authorized before removal work resumes.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a stabilized area and a complete stabilization record, not with a finished repair.

  • Confirm all extraction equipment is shut down, drained, and removed from the wet area before leaving the property
  • Confirm all temporary controls are labeled, secured, and scheduled for permanent repair
  • Confirm warning signage and restricted-access barriers remain in place around any unresolved hazard
  • Back up all photographs and stabilization records before leaving the property or immediately upon return
  • Store the source classification record, shutoff and stabilization log, electrical protection record, extraction record, moisture map, and next-step authorization together with the Chapter 1 damage log as one continuing restoration file

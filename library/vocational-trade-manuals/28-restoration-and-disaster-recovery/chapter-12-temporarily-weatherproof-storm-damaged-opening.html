Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to install one small, accessible, temporary weatherproofing assembly over a storm-damaged window, door, or wall opening after the storm has passed and the structure has been confirmed sound enough for limited work. The finished work product is a secure, drainable, clearly labeled temporary covering that sheds water, resists the expected wind, avoids trapping water against the structure, and preserves the evidence and access a permanent repair will need later.

You will confirm safe conditions before approaching the opening, inspect the opening and surrounding structure, remove only loose debris that can be removed safely, select and install temporary sheeting or covering material fastened into verified sound framing, direct water away from the opening, secure the covering against expected wind, and document the installation, its limitations, and the required permanent-repair referral.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1's damage inspection and Chapter 2's safe work-area setup. It hands its documented condition, materials, and monitoring schedule forward into Chapter 13's roof-leak protection, Chapter 14's exterior repair work, and the insurance-documentation practices of Chapter 17. A storm-damaged opening you temporarily protect here is not a permanent repair; it is a monitored, referred, and clearly bounded stabilization.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-067 — Inspect a storm-damaged opening for structural stability, hazards, and safe working conditions before disturbing it.
  • RD-068 — Remove loose debris from the damaged opening without enlarging the damage or creating additional hazards.
  • RD-069 — Cover a broken window using a temporary, secured, weather-resistant method.
  • RD-070 — Tarp or sheet a small wall opening using an approved temporary material and attachment method.
  • RD-071 — Secure temporary protection against expected wind, water intrusion, and unauthorized removal.
  • RD-072 — Inspect installed temporary protection for weather resistance and document its condition and limitations.

Where This Skill Fits

Every storm-damaged opening in this book is stabilized the same way: confirm the storm has passed and the structure is sound, protect the interior, remove only loose debris, install a labeled temporary covering fastened into sound framing, direct water away from the building, and schedule monitoring and permanent repair. This chapter teaches that sequence on one small opening. Chapter 13 applies the same low-access discipline to roof leaks, and Chapter 14 uses this chapter's documentation when it repairs the wind-damaged siding, trim, and flashing around the same opening.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming that storm conditions have passed and the opening is within the approved emergency-work limits before approaching it
  • a ground- or low-access inspection and limited loose-debris removal at one small, accessible window, door, or wall opening
  • installing a removable, secured, drainable temporary covering over the opening
  • directing water away from the opening and securing the covering against expected wind
  • inspecting the completed protection and documenting installation, monitoring schedule, and permanent-repair referral

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on a displaced header, leaning wall, failed roof support, large impact-damaged opening, or unstable masonry; those conditions must be referred to a qualified structural professional before any temporary work begins
  • pull embedded glass or framing that may destabilize the opening, or fasten into unknown cavities or damaged structural members
  • climb an unstable structure or work at height beyond a ladder or step stool rated for the reach required by this chapter's small, accessible opening
  • represent the finished temporary covering as a permanent repair, or remove it before permanent work is ready and weather permits

Any opening that cannot be safely and temporarily protected under this chapter's scope must be excluded, referred, and documented, not forced into an unsafe or unstable installation.

Trade Note

A temporary covering that is not labeled, dated, and scheduled for monitoring is easy to forget. Treat the label and the monitoring schedule as part of the finished project, not an optional add-on — an unmonitored temporary repair is one of the most common causes of secondary storm damage.

Required PPE for this chapter: closed-toe footwear at all times; cut-resistant gloves and eye protection during debris removal, cutting, and fastening; fall protection appropriate to any elevated work within the approved low-access scope.

Setup Standard

Do not approach the opening until storm conditions have passed and the exclusion zone, structural stability check, and utility hazard check (Steps 1–6) are complete. Do not fasten temporary material into any framing that has not been verified sound.

Shop Tip

Cut the covering panel roughly two inches larger than the opening on each side so the fastening line lands on solid framing rather than on damaged trim or cracked sheathing. A covering fastened only to damaged material will fail in the next wind event.

Temporary Protection Is Not a Repair

A secured, weather-resistant covering stops immediate rain and wind intrusion. It does not restore the wall, window, or door assembly, and it does not replace the structural, electrical, or envelope inspections a permanent repair requires. Every covering installed under this chapter must be labeled as temporary and carried forward into a permanent-repair referral.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop for Structural Instability

Stop and refer displaced headers, leaning walls, failed roof support, large impact damage, or unstable masonry to a qualified structural professional. Do not install temporary protection over an opening that is not structurally stable enough for limited work.

Safety Cue — Sharp and Falling Debris

Wear cut-resistant gloves and eye protection when removing loose glass, splintered framing, or sheet metal. Do not pull embedded glass or framing that may destabilize the opening; leave it in place and note it in the damage record.

Field Safety Cue — Ground Exclusion Zone

Establish a ground-level exclusion zone before working at the opening and keep it clear of foot traffic for the duration of the installation. Do not stand beneath suspended sheeting or panels during placement.

Code Alert — Egress and Emergency Function

Confirm the covering does not block a required emergency escape opening, ventilation path, or security function without an approved alternative. Verify local requirements before covering any egress-rated window or door.

E. Reference Conventions

  • Weather side: the exterior face exposed to prevailing wind and rain; covered first where safely possible.
  • Head, jamb, sill: the top, side, and bottom framing members of the opening; drainage must be planned from head to sill.
  • Sound attachment area: verified undamaged framing at least 2 inches beyond the opening on every side.
  • Shingle lap direction: upper courses or layers overlap lower courses; never reversed.

G. Preparation — Confirm Safe Conditions and Assess the Opening

  1. Confirm storm conditions have passed or are within the approved emergency-work limits. Check current weather conditions and forecast before approaching the opening.
  2. Check for downed conductors, gas odor, unstable structure, broken glass, loose cladding, trees, and overhead debris. Walk the exterior perimeter from a safe distance before approaching the opening itself.
  3. Establish an exclusion zone around the damaged opening. Mark the ground-level area to be kept clear of foot traffic during installation.
  4. Inspect from a protected ground or interior location before approaching. Use the flashlight and, where available, binoculars to assess the opening before standing beneath it.
  5. Determine whether the opening is structurally stable enough for limited temporary work. Confirm framing, header, and surrounding wall show no displacement, leaning, or collapse indicators.
  6. Stop for displaced headers, leaning walls, failed roof support, large impact damage, or unstable masonry. Refer any such condition to a qualified structural professional before proceeding.

H. Documentation and Measurement

  1. Identify electrical, plumbing, gas, security, alarm, and mechanical systems near the opening. Record the location of each on the opening's documentation sheet.
  2. Photograph interior and exterior conditions before moving debris. Capture the full opening, surrounding wall, and any visible interior effects.
  3. Measure the opening and surrounding sound attachment area. Record the opening width, height, and the sound framing width available on each side.
  4. Identify rain direction, drainage path, wind exposure, and expected duration of protection. Note these on the documentation sheet to guide material selection and orientation.

I. Loose Debris Removal

  1. Remove only loose material that can be removed safely without enlarging damage. Work from the sound framing inward toward the damaged area.
  2. Use tools, gloves, eye protection, and containers appropriate to broken glass and sharp debris. Confirm PPE is in place before beginning debris removal.
  3. Do not pull embedded glass or framing that may destabilize the opening. Leave embedded material in place and note its location and condition on the documentation sheet.
  4. Collect and contain fragments rather than dropping them outside. Use the puncture-resistant container for all glass and sharp debris.
  5. Protect interior floors, contents, and adjacent finishes. Lay down protective sheeting along the interior work path before continuing.

J. Material Selection and Cutting

  1. Select approved plywood, panel, sheeting, membrane, framing, fasteners, tape, or other temporary materials. Match the selection to the opening type and measured dimensions from Section H.
  2. Do not use material that creates excessive wind load, traps water, or blocks required emergency egress without approved alternatives. Confirm the selected material and size against Section D and F before cutting.
  3. For broken glazing, protect both interior and exterior fall or cut hazards. Select the poly sheeting or panel option based on whether the frame itself is damaged or only the glazing.
  4. Cut the covering panel to the dimension recorded in Section H, adding the 2-inch attachment margin on each side specified in the Component Schedule.
  5. Cut backing cleats to the lengths required to span the sound attachment area, and pre-drill fastener locations per Section D.

K. Installation

  1. Install temporary backing or support only into verified sound framing. Confirm each fastener location lands on sound material identified in Section G before driving any fastener.
  2. Do not fasten into unknown cavities or damaged structural members. Reroute the fastening line if a planned location falls on damaged material.
  3. Cover the opening from the weather side where safely possible, shingling layers to shed water. Orient any layered material so upper layers overlap lower layers.
  4. Provide head, side, and sill drainage without directing water into the wall. Install the drip strip at the head per the Component Schedule before setting the panel.
  5. Secure edges against expected wind using approved fasteners and spacing. Drive fasteners at the full perimeter spacing specified in Section D.
  6. Do not rely on tape alone for high-wind or long-duration protection. Use tape only as a supplemental seal on the poly-sheeting weather layer, never as the primary attachment for a panel.
  7. Protect fastener penetrations as required by the temporary detail. Seal exposed fastener heads on the weather side where the material selection calls for it.

L. Labeling, Inspection, and Handoff

  1. Label the covering as temporary and record installation date. Attach the weather-resistant label in a visible location.
  2. Maintain required emergency escape, ventilation, fire, and security functions. Confirm no required function is blocked without an approved alternative.
  3. Inspect from inside during a controlled water test or rain event when safe. Observe the covering during light water exposure applied from a safe position.
  4. Check for leakage, billowing, loose edges, ponding, sharp projections, and interior condensation. Record findings against the Acceptance Standards in Section F.
  5. Correct only within the approved temporary scope. Re-seat fasteners, re-tape edges, or adjust laps as needed; do not begin permanent repair work.
  6. Photograph completed protection and attachment. Capture the full covering, drainage detail, and label.
  7. Create a monitoring schedule after wind, rain, temperature change, or occupant use. Record the specific triggers and the reinspection interval on the documentation sheet.
  8. Prepare permanent-repair referral, measurements, and damage documentation. Compile the opening dimensions, photographs, and referral contact into the restoration file.
  9. Remove temporary protection only when permanent work is ready and weather permits. Do not remove the covering before the scheduled permanent repair unless directed by the responsible party.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed temporary protection using the same checks regardless of opening type or building size.

  • Attachment check: confirm every fastener seats into verified sound framing at the specified spacing.
  • Coverage check: confirm the panel or sheeting fully covers the opening with no gap exceeding 1/4 inch.
  • Drainage check: confirm the head drip strip and shingled layers direct water outward and downward with no ponding.
  • Wind check: confirm the panel does not flex, rattle, or shift under firm hand pressure at the center and edges.
  • Label check: confirm the temporary label is legible, dated, and visible.
  • Function check: confirm no required egress, ventilation, or security function is blocked without an approved alternative.

Complete the Temporary Weatherproofing Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 13.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Covering Flexes or Rattles Under Hand Pressure

Probable cause: fasteners were spaced wider than specified, or one or more fasteners missed sound framing.
Confirmation: inspect fastener spacing against the Section D schedule and probe each fastener location for solid backing.
Correction: add fasteners at the specified spacing, relocating any fastener that missed sound framing to the nearest verified solid location.
Reinspection: repeat the wind check across the full perimeter.

Water Pools on the Panel Face During the Water Test

Probable cause: the head drip strip was omitted or installed under rather than over the panel, or the shingle lap direction was reversed.
Confirmation: observe water flow during a controlled, safe water test and trace the path where pooling begins.
Correction: reinstall the drip strip over the panel face at the head, and correct the lap direction so upper layers overlap lower layers.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check during a second controlled water test.

The Covering Blocks a Required Egress Window

Probable cause: the opening's egress function was not identified before the covering was installed.
Confirmation: review the documentation sheet from Section H for a recorded egress determination.
Correction: remove or modify the covering to restore the required egress function, or install an approved alternative escape provision identified with the responsible party.
Reinspection: repeat the function check and record the approved alternative if one was used.

The Covering Was Installed Without a Temporary Label

Probable cause: the label step was skipped during installation.
Confirmation: inspect the completed covering for a visible, legible, dated label.
Correction: attach the label immediately, recording the actual installation date rather than the date of correction.
Reinspection: repeat the label check and photograph the corrected condition.

A Fastener Was Driven Into Unverified or Damaged Framing

Probable cause: the sound attachment area was not measured or confirmed before fastening began.
Confirmation: probe each fastener location and compare against the sound-framing measurements recorded in Section H.
Correction: remove the fastener, relocate to verified sound framing, and patch the unneeded penetration per the approved temporary detail.
Reinspection: repeat the attachment check across the full perimeter.

Work Proceeded Before Structural Stability Was Confirmed

Probable cause: the installer approached and began work on the opening without completing the Section G stability determination.
Confirmation: check the documentation sheet for a recorded stability confirmation with name and timestamp.
Correction: stop work, obtain a qualified structural determination, and only resume if the opening is confirmed stable enough for limited work.
Reinspection: confirm the stability record is complete before the installation is accepted into the restoration file.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a secured, labeled, documented temporary installation and a cleared work area.

  • Sweep and remove all glass fragments, sawdust, and fastener debris from the interior and exterior work path
  • Return tools to storage and confirm the puncture-resistant debris container is sealed and labeled
  • Back up all photographs and documentation before leaving the property
  • Confirm the exclusion zone marking is removed only after the covering is confirmed secure
  • Deliver the documentation, monitoring schedule, and permanent-repair referral to the responsible party as required by Step L.8

Performance Check

With the covering installed, review the finished work against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every fastener seats into verified sound framing at the specified spacing; the covering fully closes the opening with no gap exceeding 1/4 inch; the head drip strip and shingled layers direct water outward with no ponding; the panel does not flex, rattle, or shift under firm hand pressure; the temporary label is legible and dated; and no required egress, ventilation, or security function is blocked without an approved alternative. Complete the Temporary Weatherproofing Review and correct any failed item before beginning Chapter 13. If an item fails, identify whether the error occurred in structural assessment, debris removal, material selection, fastening, drainage detail, or labeling before correcting it.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to test, clean, and evaluate one limited, minor smoke-affected nonstructural area after a fire has been released and structurally, electrically, and gas-cleared for entry. The finished work product is a cleaned and deodorized surface set with a complete test-area record: protected unaffected surfaces, dry particulate removal completed before any liquid is introduced, a product-compatible wet-cleaning pass on washable materials only after the product has been tested, controlled ventilation without spreading residue, an odor source physically removed before any deodorizer is applied, and a documented, honest post-ventilation odor evaluation.

You will confirm fire-department release and hazard assessment before touching any surface, exclude heavy soot, char, burned structure, and widespread residue from this basic project, photograph residue patterns before cleaning, identify residue types only to the extent the assessment supports, run every cleaning method through an inconspicuous test area first, progress from dry to wet cleaning without skipping steps, contain and control runoff and waste, and evaluate the finished result under real ventilation rather than while a masking product is still working.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1's damage documentation and depends on a completed fire-residue assessment. It sits ahead of Chapter 10, which removes and replaces drywall or trim that this chapter's cleaning trials prove is non-salvageable, and ahead of Chapter 11, which seals and repaints surfaces this chapter has cleaned, dried, and released.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-049 — Identify smoke residue by type only to the extent supported by the fire-residue assessment, without diagnosing beyond the approved scope.
  • RD-050 — Protect unaffected areas, contents, HVAC openings, floors, and access routes before cleaning begins.
  • RD-051 — Dry-clean surfaces using HEPA-filtered vacuuming and dry-cleaning sponges before any liquid is introduced.
  • RD-052 — Wet-clean washable materials using a tested, product-compatible solution and a controlled least-to-most-affected sequence.
  • RD-053 — Ventilate the work area safely using approved outdoor air and filtration without redistributing residue.
  • RD-054 — Inspect the finished surface and evaluate odor reduction honestly after ventilation and drying.

Where This Skill Fits

Smoke cleaning is a test-then-apply skill, not a single universal method. The same room can contain dry soot on a ceiling, oily residue on a range hood, and firefighting residue on a carpet, and each requires its own test area, its own product, and its own pass/fail decision before broad application. The discipline built here — never skip the dry pass, never apply a wet product without testing it first, and never call an odor "gone" while a deodorizer is still masking it — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone applies to every cleaning decision across a full restoration project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming fire-department release and structural, electrical, gas, and fire-residue clearance before entry
  • establishing clean, transition, and work zones and protecting unaffected surfaces, contents, and HVAC openings
  • identifying residue type only to the extent the approved assessment supports
  • dry-cleaning with a HEPA-filtered vacuum and approved dry sponges before any liquid is used
  • testing and applying a product-compatible wet-cleaning solution on washable, nonporous or sealed materials
  • ventilating safely and evaluating odor after drying under normal airflow

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on heavy soot, char, burned structure, melted plastics, chemical fires, HVAC-contaminated systems, or widespread residue; these conditions are excluded from this basic project and require the qualified assessment and specialty methods identified in Chapter 10 and beyond
  • mix cleaning chemicals under any circumstance, or wet-clean porous, unsealed, electrical, paper, fabric, or other water-sensitive materials without a specific approved method
  • use ozone, hydroxyl, or other reactive deodorization equipment in occupied spaces or outside a qualified plan
  • apply deodorizing product as a substitute for physically removing the odor source, or represent a masked odor as a resolved odor

Any material or condition that falls outside this scope must be stopped, documented, and referred, not cleaned by improvisation.

Trade Note

Every wet-cleaning product used in this chapter must pass its own inconspicuous test on the exact material and finish being cleaned before it touches a visible surface. A product that performs well on painted drywall in one room can still streak, swell, or discolor a different finish two rooms over. Testing is not optional caution; it is the method.

Setup Standard

Do not introduce any liquid cleaning product until dry particulate removal is complete and inspected. Do not apply any wet-cleaning solution beyond its tested area until the test result has been inspected and passed. Never mix cleaning chemicals.

Shop Tip

Label each test area with the same identifier used on the cleaning record, and photograph it before and after. When a product performs differently on the broad surface than it did on the test spot, the labeled photograph is what tells you whether the surface changed or the method did.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every smoke-cleaning task in this chapter follows the same order: protect first, document before disturbing, remove dry particulate before any liquid, test every wet method before broad use, control runoff and waste, ventilate without spreading residue, and evaluate odor only after the space is dry and normally ventilated. Skipping the order — wetting a surface before it is dry-cleaned, or applying a product broadly before it is tested — is the most common cause of smeared soot, damaged finishes, and false odor readings.

Dry Before Wet, Always

Liquid turns dry soot into a smear that is far harder to remove than the original residue. Every surface in this chapter's scope receives a complete, inspected dry pass before any wet-cleaning solution is tested or applied. A surface that still shows dry residue after vacuuming and sponging is not ready for liquid, no matter how much time pressure exists.

Documentation Cue — Label the Test Area Before You Test It

Assign a test-area identifier and photograph the location before applying any product. Use the same identifier on the cleaning record, the before photograph, and the after photograph. Do not test a method without recording where it was tested.

Critical Safety Cue — Never Mix Cleaning Chemicals

Never combine bleach, ammonia, acids, solvents, oxidizers, or any other cleaning chemicals in the same container, on the same surface, or in the same waste stream. Select one product-compatible solution per task, follow its label exactly, and do not improvise a stronger mixture.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilate Without Spreading Residue

Ventilate using approved outdoor air and filtration equipment positioned to exhaust away from openings, air intakes, and occupied areas. Do not open a path that pulls loose dry residue into an unaffected room or the building's return-air path.

Code Alert — Heavy Residue Is Outside This Chapter's Scope

Stop and refer any area with heavy soot, char, burned structure, melted plastics, chemical-fire residue, or HVAC contamination. This chapter's methods are validated for minor, limited smoke residue only; using them on excluded conditions can spread contamination and damage material that a qualified specialist could otherwise have addressed.

F. Preparation — Release, Assessment Review, and Scope Confirmation

  1. Confirm fire-department release, structural safety, electrical safety, gas safety, and approved fire-residue assessment. Record each confirmation on the cleaning-record cover sheet before proceeding.
  2. Exclude heavy soot, char, burned structure, melted plastics, chemical fire, HVAC contamination, or widespread residue from the basic project. Mark any excluded area on the scope record and refer it separately.
  3. Review the fire source, fuel type, extinguishing method, affected materials, duration, and prior cleaning. File this information with the cleaning record before selecting products.
  4. Identify possible hazardous combustion products and required PPE. Confirm PPE selection against the fire-residue assessment before entry.

G. Zone Setup, Protection, and Pre-Cleaning Documentation

  1. Establish clean, transition, and work zones. Mark each zone boundary and confirm the entry and debris path before cleaning begins.
  2. Protect unaffected surfaces, contents, HVAC openings, floors, and access routes. Cover or remove items according to the approved contents plan before any residue is disturbed.
  3. Photograph residue patterns and affected boundaries before cleaning. Index each photograph to the area identifier used on the test-area log and cleaning record.
  4. Identify dry soot, oily soot, protein residue, ash, firefighting residue, and mixed deposits only to the extent supported by the assessment. Record the identification with the supporting assessment reference; do not diagnose beyond it.
  5. Select inconspicuous test areas for each material and residue type. Choose a location representative of the finish and out of normal view, and record its identifier on the test-area log.

H. Dry Cleaning (Progressive Residue Removal)

  1. Begin with approved dry particulate removal before introducing liquid. Confirm no liquid has touched the surface before starting this pass.
  2. Use a HEPA-filtered vacuum with appropriate attachment and low-contact technique. Select the attachment matched to the surface texture and residue type.
  3. Do not drag a brush or tool across fragile soot-coated surfaces. Use a lifting or dabbing motion instead of a dragging stroke on fragile finishes.
  4. Use approved dry-cleaning sponges or residue tools with controlled passes. Work in one direction per pass and avoid re-crossing a soiled section of the sponge.
  5. Rotate or replace cleaning surfaces to avoid redistributing soot. Fold to a clean face or replace the sponge once its working surface is loaded with residue.
  6. Collect and contain spent sponges, wipes, filters, and debris. Bag and label waste at the point of removal before it leaves the work zone.
  7. Inspect whether dry cleaning improved the surface or caused smearing. Record the inspection result on the dry-cleaning record before proceeding to any wet-cleaning step.

I. Wet Cleaning (Test-and-Verify Method)

  1. Select a product-compatible wet-cleaning solution for washable surfaces. Match the product to the substrate and finish identified in the residue and test schedule.
  2. Read product labels, dilution, dwell, ventilation, rinse, and disposal requirements. Follow the label exactly; do not estimate dilution or dwell time.
  3. Never mix cleaning chemicals. Use one product at a time in a dedicated, labeled container.
  4. Protect porous, unsealed, electrical, paper, fabric, and water-sensitive materials from inappropriate wet cleaning. Exclude these materials from this chapter's wet-cleaning scope and refer them separately.
  5. Test wet cleaning for color change, finish damage, swelling, streaking, and residue. Inspect the test area after the manufacturer's specified dwell and dry time before any broad application.
  6. Clean from least affected to most affected and from lower-risk test areas outward. Begin broad application only after the adjacent test area has passed.
  7. Change solution, towels, and rinse water frequently. Replace before visible loading or reduced effectiveness, not after.
  8. Control runoff and prevent contaminated liquid from entering unapproved drains or materials. Route and collect runoff along the approved path established in Section G.

J. Ventilation, Odor Source Correction, and Deodorization

  1. Ventilate using approved outdoor air and filtration without distributing residue. Confirm exhaust direction and filtration before opening any ventilation path.
  2. Do not use ozone or reactive deodorization in occupied spaces or outside a qualified plan. Refer any condition that appears to require reactive treatment.
  3. Remove odor sources physically before applying deodorizing products. Confirm the physical source has been cleaned or removed before any product is applied.
  4. Apply approved deodorizer only after cleaning and only to compatible materials. Confirm material compatibility against the product label before application.
  5. Allow surfaces to dry under controlled conditions. Maintain the ventilation and access controls established earlier until drying is complete.

K. Final Inspection, Documentation, and Restoration of Protected Areas

  1. Inspect under normal and raking light for residue, streaking, finish damage, and remaining staining. Record findings by area identifier on the cleaning record.
  2. Evaluate odor after ventilation and drying rather than while products are masking it. Do not record an odor finding while an active deodorizer is still working on the surface.
  3. Identify materials requiring additional cleaning, sealing, replacement, or professional treatment. List each on the cleaning record with the reason and recommended next step.
  4. Photograph results and record products, dilution, test areas, methods, waste, ventilation, and odor findings. Complete the full cleaning record before closing the project.
  5. Restore protected areas and maintain follow-up monitoring. Remove containment and coverings in a controlled sequence and schedule a monitoring check consistent with the fire-residue assessment.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed cleaning project using the same checks regardless of residue type or surface material.

  • Protection check: confirm every unaffected surface, content item, HVAC opening, and floor along the work path was protected before cleaning began.
  • Sequence check: confirm every surface received a complete, inspected dry-cleaning pass before any wet-cleaning solution was applied.
  • Test check: confirm every wet-cleaning product has a passed test-area result recorded before its broad-application entry.
  • Chemical check: confirm no cleaning chemicals were mixed at any point in the project.
  • Waste check: confirm spent sponges, wipes, and filters are collected, labeled, and contained, and runoff was controlled.
  • Odor check: confirm the final odor evaluation was recorded after drying and normal ventilation, without an active masking product present.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Wet Cleaning Was Applied Before the Dry Pass Was Complete

Probable cause: the crew moved to liquid cleaning to save time, or assumed a light residue did not need a full dry pass.
Confirmation: compare the dry-cleaning record against the wet-cleaning record for the same area and confirm the dry-cleaning inspection result was logged before the wet-cleaning start time.
Correction: stop wet cleaning on the affected surface, assess whether smearing occurred, and complete or repeat the dry pass before resuming wet cleaning.
Reinspection: repeat the sequence check across every surface in the project.

A Wet-Cleaning Product Was Applied Broadly Without a Passed Test Result

Probable cause: the test area was skipped, or the test result was not inspected before broad application began.
Confirmation: review the test-area log for the specific material and product and confirm a recorded pass/fail result exists with before and after photographs.
Correction: stop broad application immediately, inspect the surface for color change, finish damage, swelling, streaking, or residue, and run the required test before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the test check across every wet-cleaning entry on the record.

Two Cleaning Chemicals Were Combined on the Same Surface

Probable cause: a second product was applied to improve results without rinsing or clearing the first, or two different crew members used different products on the same area without coordination.
Confirmation: interview the crew and review the wet-cleaning record for the area to identify every product applied and its sequence.
Correction: stop work immediately, ventilate the area, and follow the applicable safety data sheet guidance for the specific chemical combination before any further work in that area.
Reinspection: confirm the chemical-control check passes across the full project before resuming.

Odor Was Recorded as Resolved While a Deodorizer Was Still Active

Probable cause: the evaluation was performed immediately after applying deodorizer, before the masking effect had cleared and before normal ventilation resumed.
Confirmation: compare the deodorizer application time on the record against the odor-evaluation time and confirm sufficient ventilation and drying occurred between them.
Correction: re-evaluate odor after the surface is fully dry and the space has been ventilated under normal conditions without an active masking product.
Reinspection: repeat the odor check and update the record with the corrected finding.

Runoff or Waste Reached an Unapproved Drain or Unaffected Material

Probable cause: containment sheeting or a runoff-collection path was not established before wet cleaning began, or waste bags were left open along the debris path.
Confirmation: inspect the debris and cleaning-solution path from the work zone to the waste area for uncontained liquid or open waste containers.
Correction: stop wet cleaning, contain and clean the affected path, and reestablish approved containment and runoff routing before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the waste and runoff check across the full work path.

Excluded Material Was Cleaned Using This Chapter's Wet-Cleaning Method

Probable cause: a porous, unsealed, electrical, paper, fabric, or other water-sensitive material was mistaken for a washable surface.
Confirmation: review the residue and test schedule for the material's classification and compare it against the cleaning method actually used.
Correction: stop wet cleaning on that material immediately, document the condition, and refer it to the appropriate specialty method or professional rather than continuing under this chapter's scope.
Reinspection: repeat the protection and scope check for every material in the project.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with cleaned, dried surfaces and a filed cleaning record, not with additional demolition or repair.

  • Remove containment sheeting and coverings in a controlled sequence once surfaces are dry and inspected
  • Confirm all spent sponges, wipes, filters, and cleaning waste are bagged, labeled, and moved to approved disposal
  • Decontaminate reusable tools and buckets before returning them to clean storage
  • Store the residue photographs, test-area log, dry- and wet-cleaning records, ventilation and waste record, and odor evaluation together as one dated cleaning file
  • Deliver the finished results and any professional-referral items to the responsible party as required by Section K

Field Terms

HEPA-filtered vacuuming and dry-sponge cleaning performed before any liquid is introduced.
Test Area
A small, inconspicuous location where a cleaning method or product is applied and inspected before broad use.
Least-to-Most-Affected Sequence
Cleaning that begins at the lowest-residue area and moves outward toward heavier residue.
Odor Source
The physical residue or material producing the odor, which must be removed or cleaned before deodorizing.
Masked Odor
An odor temporarily concealed by a deodorizing product rather than physically resolved.
Post-Ventilation Evaluation
An odor assessment performed after drying and normal ventilation, without an active masking product present.
Dry Soot
A powdery combustion residue typically produced by fast, high-oxygen fires.
Oily Soot
A sticky, harder-to-remove combustion residue typically produced by slow, low-oxygen fires.
Firefighting Residue
Residue left by suppression agents or extinguishing water, distinct from combustion residue.
Cleaning Record
The complete written and photographic file documenting products, tests, methods, waste, ventilation, and odor findings for the project.

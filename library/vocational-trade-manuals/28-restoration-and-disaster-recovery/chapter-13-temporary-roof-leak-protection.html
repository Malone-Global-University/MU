Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to install one limited temporary roof-leak protection system for a small, accessible storm-damaged roof area using a ground-based, interior, eave-access, or platform low-access method, without walking an unsafe roof. The finished work product is a secured, drainable, monitored temporary covering that directs water away from the structure, an interior collection setup where needed, and a complete documentation record supporting the permanent roofing referral.

You will confirm that storm, lightning, wind, ice, and darkness conditions are acceptable for the work; assess the roof from the ground using binoculars or a camera; correlate the interior leak with the probable exterior area without claiming a confirmed source; select and deploy an approved low-access covering method; secure the covering against wind while directing water to a safe discharge point; and document the temporary protection, its limitations, and the referral for permanent roofing repair.

This chapter follows Chapter 12's temporary weatherproofing of storm-damaged openings and uses the same exclusion-zone, ground-assessment, and temporary-protection-monitoring discipline. Chapter 14 uses this chapter's exterior assessment habits when repairing wind-damaged siding, trim, and flashing.

  • RD-074 — Evaluate safe access and select a ground-based, interior, eave, or platform low-access method.

Where This Skill Fits

Roof leaks are one of the few conditions in this book where the correct response is deliberately limited: a temporary, monitored, ground-or-low-access covering rather than a roof-walked repair. The exterior hazard assessment you perform here uses the same exclusion-zone and stop-work discipline taught in Chapter 1 and Chapter 12. The documentation you create becomes the referral record for the qualified roofing inspection required before any permanent repair, and it feeds directly into Chapter 16's restoration action plan and Chapter 17's insurance-support file.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • ground-based visual roof assessment using binoculars, camera, or approved remote equipment
  • correlating an interior leak with a probable roof-slope, valley, penetration, wall, or drainage location without confirming the source
  • selecting and deploying a ground-based tarp, interior catch-and-diversion, eave-access covering, or contractor board-up method
  • securing a temporary covering that sheds water, resists expected wind, and avoids trapping water or overloading damaged roofing
  • directing runoff to a safe discharge point
  • documenting the covered area, materials, interior protection, and unresolved conditions, and arranging qualified roofing inspection

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • climb or walk a wet, steep, damaged, brittle, storm-affected, or electrically exposed roof
  • work during active storm, lightning, high wind, ice, snow, darkness, or other unsafe conditions
  • anchor to gutters, plumbing vents, weak fascia, chimney caps, antennas, or other damaged or unrated components
  • confirm the roof-leak source, perform permanent roofing repair, or clear structural, electrical, or tree hazards; those determinations belong to qualified roofing, structural, electrical, or tree-service professionals

Any roof, electrical, tree, structural, or high-access condition that cannot be safely addressed from the ground or an approved low-access method must be marked unsafe and referred, not attempted.

Trade Note

A temporary roof covering is a stopgap, not a repair. Every covering installed under this chapter must be labeled as temporary, monitored on a defined schedule, and replaced by a qualified roofer's permanent repair. Representing a tarp as a completed roof repair is a documentation failure this chapter does not permit.

Setup Standard

Do not deploy any covering until the ground-based hazard assessment (Steps 1–9) confirms the area is safe for the selected low-access method. Do not climb or walk a wet, steep, damaged, brittle, storm-affected, or electrically exposed roof under any circumstance in this chapter.

Shop Tip

Photograph the roof from at least two ground positions before selecting an access method. A single vantage point can hide a downed conductor, a cracked rafter tail, or a second damaged area on the far slope.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every temporary roof-leak protection project in this book follows the same sequence: confirm weather and access safety, assess from the ground, correlate the interior leak without confirming the source, select the lowest-risk access method that can achieve coverage, deploy the covering to shed rather than trap water, secure it against wind, direct discharge to a safe point, and document the limitation and referral. This sequence keeps the worker off an unsafe roof while still stopping active water intrusion.

Probable Source, Not Confirmed Source

Correlating an interior stain with a roof slope, valley, or penetration is a reasonable working estimate, not a confirmed diagnosis. Water travels along framing and sheathing before it appears indoors, so the visible drip point is often several feet from the actual roof opening. Record the correlation as probable and let the qualified roofing inspection confirm the source.

Critical Safety Cue — No Roof-Walking Under Any Condition in This Chapter

Do not climb or walk a wet, steep, damaged, brittle, storm-affected, or electrically exposed roof. This chapter authorizes ground-based, interior, eave-access, and platform low-access methods only. If none of these can achieve safe coverage, stop and refer the work to a qualified roofing or emergency-board-up contractor.

Safety Cue — Stop for Downed Conductors and Tree Contact

Stop and refer immediately if the ground assessment identifies a downed conductor, a tree in contact with the structure or a power line, an unstable chimney, loose roofing that could fall, a sagging deck, or damaged trusses.

Field Safety Cue — Ground and Line-Handling Zones

Establish a ground exclusion zone and a separate line-handling zone before deploying ropes, weights, or a ladder. Do not stand beneath suspended material during deployment, and keep ladders, platforms, lifts, and poles clear of overhead power lines at all times.

Code Alert — Coordinate Contractor Board-Up When Required

Larger, steeper, or higher roofs, or roofs where no ground-based or eave-access method can achieve safe coverage, require a licensed roofing or emergency-board-up contractor. Confirm this threshold before attempting any low-access method that appears marginal.

C. Material and Supply Takeoff

  • Covering materials: one reinforced tarp or membrane sized to the measured protection area plus a minimum 12-inch overlap on all sides, battens sufficient to secure every edge and intermediate fold line, and grommet-compatible rope or strap for every anchor point identified in the attachment plan.
  • Attachment hardware: fasteners compatible with the verified sound anchor material, weighted anchors sized for the expected wind exposure, and corrosion-resistant hardware suitable for exterior exposure for the duration of the temporary installation.
  • Interior protection supplies: catch containers sized to the expected drip rate, protective sheeting for floors and contents beneath the leak path, and towels or absorbent material for ongoing collection.
  • Documentation supplies: roof-leak assessment forms, drainage-record forms, temporary-protection tags, and the referral form, with a camera or smartphone camera for the required photographs.

D. Access-Method and Attachment Schedule

For the selected low-access method, record the following before deployment: method name (ground-based tarp deployment, interior catch and diversion, eave-access covering, or contractor board-up), the specific sound anchor points to be used, the fastener or line type at each anchor point, the wind-exposure rating assumed for the installation, and the discharge point confirmed for runoff. Do not substitute an anchor point that has not been verified as sound, and do not select a method beyond this chapter's low-access scope without referring the work to a licensed roofing or emergency-board-up contractor.

F. Pre-Deployment Safety Assessment

  1. Confirm active storm, lightning, high wind, ice, snow, darkness, and emergency conditions are acceptable for the planned work. Record the confirming source and time on the assessment cover sheet.
  2. Inspect the building and roof from the ground using binoculars, camera, or approved remote equipment. Assess from at least two ground vantage points.
  3. Identify downed conductors, tree contact, unstable chimney, loose roofing, sagging deck, damaged trusses, and overhead debris. Record each condition found, with its approximate location.
  4. Stop and refer all unsafe roof, electrical, tree, structural, or high-access conditions. Notify the responsible party and identified professional immediately and record the referral.
  5. Correlate interior leak location with roof slope, valleys, penetrations, walls, and drainage without claiming a confirmed source. Record the correlation as a probable source area.
  6. Protect interior contents and install safe water collection before exterior work. Position catch containers and sheeting beneath the active or expected drip path.
  7. Photograph roof, exterior, interior stain, active drip, and surrounding conditions. Index each photograph to the property record.
  8. Select a ground-based tarp deployment, interior catch and diversion, eave-access covering, contractor board-up, or other approved low-access method. Record the selected method and the reason it was chosen over the alternatives.
  9. Do not climb or walk a wet, steep, damaged, brittle, storm-affected, or electrically exposed roof. Confirm this limitation is understood by every person on site before proceeding.

G. Material Selection and Attachment Planning

  1. Measure the area requiring protection and the path needed to shed water below the suspected opening. Record the measured dimensions on the drainage-record form.
  2. Select tarp, membrane, battens, ropes, weights, fasteners, anchors, or proprietary cover system approved for the method. Confirm the selection matches the Access-Method and Attachment Schedule.
  3. Inspect temporary materials for tears, UV damage, weak grommets, incompatible coatings, and insufficient strength. Reject and replace any material that fails this inspection.
  4. Plan attachment to verified sound structure without penetrating critical concealed systems. Identify each anchor point before deployment begins.
  5. Do not anchor to gutters, plumbing vents, weak fascia, chimney caps, antennas, or damaged components. Confirm every planned anchor point against this exclusion list.
  6. Establish ground exclusion and line-handling zones. Mark both zones before any rope, ladder, or platform is set up.
  7. Control ladders, platforms, lifts, poles, ropes, and materials against power-line and wind hazards. Verify clearance from overhead lines before raising any equipment.
  8. Deploy the covering without standing beneath suspended material. Confirm the deployment path keeps every worker clear of the suspended load.

H. Deployment and Drainage

  1. Extend protection upslope or above the suspected entry path according to the approved method. Confirm the covering extends beyond the probable source area on the upslope side.
  2. Lap and orient the covering to shed water rather than collect it. Verify shed condition from the ground before securing final edges.
  3. Direct runoff to gutters, roof drainage, or a safe exterior location. Confirm the discharge point on the drainage-record form.
  4. Prevent concentrated discharge onto foundations, walks, neighbors, or damaged walls. Adjust the covering orientation if concentrated discharge is observed.
  5. Secure edges and intermediate points against expected wind. Use the battens, ropes, and weights specified in the Attachment Schedule at every identified point.
  6. Avoid wrinkles, low pockets, and tension that may tear material or overload damaged roofing. Adjust tension and lapping until the covering lies flat and drains freely.
  7. Do not block roof drains, scuppers, valleys, plumbing vents, combustion vents, or required ventilation. Confirm each of these features remains clear before final securing.

I. Inspection, Documentation, and Referral

  1. Inspect attachment from the safe access point. Confirm every anchor point remains secure and every edge is properly lapped.
  2. Observe performance during controlled water exposure or rainfall only when safe. Do not create an artificial water test that risks additional damage or unsafe footing.
  3. Check interior collection, leak rate, new water paths, and ceiling loading. Record observed changes in the interior protection log.
  4. Label the protection as temporary and record installation date and access limitation. Attach the temporary-protection tag at a visible point.
  5. Create a post-storm inspection schedule. Define the trigger conditions — wind, rain, temperature change, or occupant report — that require reinspection.
  6. Document suspected source, covered area, attachment, materials, interior protection, and unresolved conditions. Complete the roofing referral form with this information.
  7. Arrange qualified roofing inspection and permanent repair. Record the referral contact, date requested, and any scheduling constraints.
  8. Remove the temporary system only under safe approved conditions. Do not remove the covering until the permanent repair is ready and weather conditions permit safe removal.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed temporary protection using the same checks regardless of roof type or access method used.

  • Access check: confirm no roof-walking occurred and the selected low-access method matches the ground assessment.
  • Anchor check: confirm every attachment point is verified sound structure and no excluded component was used.
  • Drainage check: confirm the covering sheds water to a confirmed safe discharge point without concentrated runoff.
  • Wind-resistance check: confirm edges and intermediate points are secured beyond tape alone.
  • Blocked-path check: confirm no drain, scupper, valley, or required vent is blocked.
  • Documentation check: confirm the temporary-protection tag, drainage record, and roofing referral are all complete.

Complete the Roof-Leak Protection Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before removing any exclusion zone or leaving the property.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Covering Billows or Pulls Loose in Wind

Probable cause: insufficient anchor points, tape used as the primary fastening method, or a batten spacing wider than the expected wind exposure allows.
Confirmation: inspect every edge and intermediate point against the Attachment Schedule and compare to the observed wind exposure at the site.
Correction: add battens, ropes, or weighted anchors at the failed points, and replace tape-only fastening with a mechanical method.
Reinspection: repeat the wind-resistance check after the correction and during the next monitoring visit.

Water Pools on the Covering Instead of Shedding

Probable cause: the covering was secured with a low pocket, insufficient lap, or reversed orientation relative to the roof slope.
Confirmation: observe the covering from the ground during light rain or controlled water exposure, or inspect for standing-water residue after a rain event.
Correction: loosen the affected section, re-lap in the shedding direction, and re-secure edges and intermediate points.
Reinspection: repeat the water-shedding check under the next safe observation opportunity.

Runoff Discharges Directly onto the Foundation or Walkway

Probable cause: the discharge point was not verified before securing the covering, or the covering shifted after installation.
Confirmation: observe the discharge path from the ground during rainfall or trace the dry-weather runoff pattern using water poured at the low edge.
Correction: adjust the covering orientation or add a diversion element to redirect runoff to the confirmed safe discharge point.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check and update the drainage-record form.

Interior Leak Continues After Exterior Covering Is Installed

Probable cause: the probable source area was incorrectly correlated, water is migrating along framing from a different opening, or the covering does not fully extend upslope of the actual entry point.
Confirmation: recheck interior collection volume and new water paths, and compare against the covered area from the ground.
Correction: extend or reposition the covering upslope within the approved low-access method, or escalate the referral to the qualified roofing contractor for confirmed source identification.
Reinspection: repeat interior collection monitoring after the correction.

An Anchor Point Was Later Found to Be an Excluded Component

Probable cause: a gutter, vent, chimney cap, or antenna was used as an anchor point under time pressure without verifying it against the exclusion list.
Confirmation: review the Attachment Schedule against the excluded-component list and inspect the actual installed anchor points from the ground.
Correction: relocate the anchor to verified sound structure and re-secure the affected section.
Reinspection: repeat the anchor-security check across every attachment point.

The Covering Was Removed Before the Permanent Repair Was Ready

Probable cause: the covering was removed based on appearance or occupant request without confirming the roofing contractor's readiness or a safe weather window.
Confirmation: check the roofing referral record for a confirmed permanent-repair date and current weather conditions.
Correction: reinstall temporary protection immediately using this chapter's full procedure until the permanent repair is actually ready.
Reinspection: repeat the full acceptance-standards review on the reinstalled covering.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a secured temporary installation and a complete documentation set, not with a finished roof repair.

  • Remove and properly store unused materials, tools, ropes, and weights from the ground work area
  • Confirm interior catch containers are positioned for ongoing collection and are checked on the monitoring schedule
  • Back up all photographs and documentation before leaving the property
  • Confirm ground exclusion zones and temporary hazard markers remain in place if the property will not be immediately re-checked
  • Deliver the roof-leak assessment, drainage record, and roofing referral to the responsible party as required by Step 32

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to convert one already-documented incident into a phased restoration action plan. The finished work product is a written restoration action plan that separates emergency life-safety work, property mitigation, investigation, contents handling, demolition, drying, cleaning, temporary repair, permanent repair, rebuild, and owner-selected improvement into distinct, sequenced work packages, each with an assigned trade, a deliverable, an acceptance condition, and a documented hold point.

You will collect the incident, hazard, moisture, and environmental records already created; build a complete damage register; separate incident damage from pre-existing conditions; prioritize life-safety and source-control work ahead of cosmetic repair; break the project into trade-assigned work packages with dependencies; identify pending owner and insurer decisions; and produce a phased sequence with inspection hold points and a risk register.

This chapter sits between the technical restoration chapters and the insurance-documentation and rebuild chapters that follow. Chapter 16 does not perform mitigation, drying, or rebuild work itself. It organizes the findings of Chapters 1 through 15 into the governing plan that Chapters 17 through 20 execute against.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-091 — Separate emergency work from repair and rebuild work using the documented incident record.
  • RD-092 — Identify the trade tasks required to complete the restoration project.
  • RD-093 — Prioritize water and source control ahead of cosmetic and finish work.
  • RD-094 — Prioritize safety hazards using the classifications established in the damage register.
  • RD-095 — Build a repair sequence with dependencies, hold points, and responsible parties.
  • RD-096 — Prepare a restoration action plan with scope, exclusions, assumptions, and closeout criteria.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1's damage register, Chapter 3's source-control record, Chapters 4 through 6's removal and drying records, and Chapters 7 through 14's mold, smoke, and storm findings all feed directly into this chapter's work-package schedule. The plan built here becomes the controlling document that Chapter 17's insurance-support file tracks against, that Chapter 18's rebuild follows, that Chapter 19's final inspection verifies, and that the Chapter 20 capstone assembles in miniature for a single limited project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • collecting and consolidating incident reports, photographs, hazard assessments, moisture maps, environmental findings, emergency invoices, permits, and owner objectives already produced in earlier chapters
  • building a complete damage register and separating incident damage from pre-existing or unrelated conditions
  • separating emergency life-safety work, mitigation, investigation, contents handling, demolition, drying, cleaning, temporary repair, permanent repair, rebuild, code-required work, and owner-selected upgrades
  • prioritizing active hazards and source correction ahead of finish repair
  • breaking the project into work packages by trade, deliverable, dependency, and acceptance condition
  • creating inspection hold points, a risk register, and a phased restoration action plan

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform mitigation, demolition, drying, cleaning, or rebuild work; those actions belong to the chapters that produced the underlying records and to the trades assigned in the plan
  • determine insurance coverage, cause-of-loss determination, liability, depreciation, or claim value
  • authorize rebuilding, closing cavities, or finish work before environmental, moisture, structural, utility, and required inspection releases are documented as complete
  • present preliminary quantities, durations, or costs as approved claim values or fixed contract prices

Any phase or work package that cannot be planned from information already documented must be marked as a pending decision or referral, not estimated by guess.

Trade Note

A restoration action plan is only as reliable as the damage register it is built from. If a Chapter 1 damage-log entry, a Chapter 3 source-status record, or a Chapter 7 mold-risk finding is missing or incomplete, the corresponding work package in this chapter's plan will be missing or incomplete as well. Confirm the upstream record before building the plan, not after.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the work-package schedule until the current source, utility, structural, contamination, and occupancy status has been confirmed against the latest records from Chapters 1 through 15. Do not authorize any phase of the plan beyond the next approved phase.

Shop Tip

Build the damage register as a single running list keyed to the Chapter 1 room and hazard identifiers. Every later chapter's findings attach to that same identifier. A plan built on a second, disconnected numbering system is the most common cause of missed work packages.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every restoration plan in this book follows the same discipline: separate what must happen immediately from what must happen later, separate stabilization from permanent repair, and never let rebuilding begin before the releases it depends on are documented. Applying that discipline at the whole-project level, rather than one room or one hazard at a time, is what this chapter adds.

Damage registerConsolidated record of every room, system, component, and condition
Scope categoriesEmergency, mitigation, investigation, cleaning, demolition, drying, temporary repair, rebuild, closeout
Work-package scheduleTrade, deliverable, dependency, and acceptance condition for every package
Phased sequenceEmergency, 24-hour, short-term, mitigation, rebuild, and final milestones
Risk registerSafety, contamination, hidden-damage, drying, weather, access, supply, price, and schedule risks
Restoration action planScope, exclusions, assumptions, priorities, sequence, responsibilities, approvals, closeout criteria

Emergency, Mitigation, and Rebuild Are Not Interchangeable Categories

A work package that stops an active hazard is emergency work. A work package that limits further damage after the hazard is controlled is mitigation. A work package that restores a finished, code-consistent condition is rebuild. Combining these into one undifferentiated "repair" description is the most common source of stalled approvals and disputed scope later in the project.

Documentation Cue — Key Every Entry to the Chapter 1 Identifier

Carry the room and hazard identifiers assigned in Chapter 1 into the damage register, the work-package schedule, and the risk register without renumbering. A plan that cannot be traced back to the original damage-log entry cannot be verified against it later.

Critical Safety Cue — Rebuild Waits for Every Required Release

Do not sequence closing cavities, installing insulation, hanging drywall, laying flooring, painting, sealing, or rebuilding ahead of documented source correction, cleanliness, dryness, structural acceptance, utility acceptance, and required inspections. A plan that schedules rebuild before these releases is not a valid restoration action plan.

Field Safety Cue — Confirm Current Status Before Planning

Do not build the plan from stale records. Confirm the current source, utility, structural, contamination, and occupancy status as of the planning date, and note the confirmation source and date on the cover sheet.

Code Alert — Permits and Insurer Approvals Vary by Project

Identify permits, inspections, insurer approvals, owner approvals, and utility coordination required for each work package. Do not assume a permit or approval obtained for one work package satisfies the requirement for another.

D. Scope-Category and Priority Schedule

For every damage-register entry, assign one scope category (emergency, mitigation, investigation, contents, demolition, drying, cleaning, temporary repair, permanent repair, rebuild, code-required, or owner-selected improvement) and one priority tier (immediate hazard, source control, mitigation, repair, or monitoring). Do not assign an entry to more than one scope category. An entry with no supporting record from an earlier chapter must be marked pending investigation rather than assigned a category by assumption.

Immediate HazardFire, electrical, gas, structural, sewage, active water intrusion, or blocked egress — sequenced first, ahead of every other package
Source ControlStopping or correcting the water, weather, fire, or contamination source — sequenced before any drying or finish work
Mitigation and RepairSelective removal, drying, cleaning, and rebuild — sequenced only after source control and required releases

F. Preparation — Records Collection and Damage Register

  1. Collect incident reports, photographs, hazard assessments, moisture maps, environmental findings, emergency invoices, permits, and owner objectives. Assemble every record produced in Chapters 1 through 15 for this project.
  2. Confirm the current source, utility, structural, contamination, and occupancy status. Record the confirmation source and date on the cover sheet.
  3. Create a complete damage register by room, system, component, and condition. Use the Chapter 1 room and hazard identifiers without renumbering.
  4. Separate incident damage from pre-existing condition and unrelated maintenance where reasonably supported. Label each register entry accordingly.
  5. Separate emergency life-safety work from property stabilization. Mark each register entry as one or the other where applicable.
  6. Separate mitigation from permanent repair and reconstruction. Add this distinction to the register entry.
  7. Separate contents handling from building work. Track contents entries on a distinct register line.
  8. Separate code-required work from like-kind restoration and owner-selected upgrades. Flag each register entry with the correct designation.

G. Priority and Referral Identification

  1. Identify active hazards requiring immediate action. Pull these directly from the Chapter 1 safety register and any later chapter's stop-work findings.
  2. Prioritize fire, electrical, gas, structural, sewage, water intrusion, weather exposure, and blocked egress. Sequence these ahead of every other package.
  3. Prioritize source correction before finish repair. Confirm no rebuild package precedes its source-control dependency.
  4. Identify environmental testing, engineering, roofing, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, and specialty restoration referrals. List each referral against the specific register entry that triggered it.
  5. Identify permits, inspections, insurer approvals, owner approvals, and utility coordination. Record the requirement against the affected work package.

H. Work-Package and Dependency Development

  1. Break the scope into work packages by emergency, mitigation, demolition, drying, cleaning, temporary enclosure, rebuild, finishes, and closeout. Create one schedule line per package.
  2. Identify each trade, contractor, consultant, supplier, authority, and responsible party. Assign one responsible party per package.
  3. Define the deliverable and acceptance condition for each work package. State the observable result that closes the package.
  4. Identify predecessors and dependencies. List every package or approval that must precede each package.
  5. Prevent rebuilding before environmental, moisture, structural, utility, and inspection releases. Place a hold point on every rebuild package with an open dependency.
  6. Identify long-lead materials, custom finishes, temporary housing, security, and weather constraints. Note these against the affected package.
  7. Identify salvage, cleaning, repair, replacement, and disposal decisions still pending. List each as an open decision with the party responsible for resolving it.
  8. Create allowances or alternate decisions for concealed conditions and unavailable matching materials. Record the allowance basis and the trigger for revisiting it.
  9. Identify owner decisions required for finishes, upgrades, depreciation, and out-of-pocket work. List each with a target decision date.
  10. Identify insurer documentation required before removal or replacement. Cross-reference the Chapter 17 insurance-support file requirements.
  11. Estimate preliminary quantities, equipment, labor, duration, access, and disruption. Record these as preliminary figures on the package schedule.
  12. Do not present preliminary amounts as approved claim values or fixed contract prices. Label every preliminary figure as such on the schedule.

I. Sequencing, Risk, and Plan Finalization

  1. Create a phased sequence with emergency, 24-hour, short-term, mitigation, rebuild, and final milestones as appropriate. Order every work package into the applicable phase.
  2. Create inspection and documentation hold points. Place each hold point on the sequence at the dependency it protects.
  3. Create change-control procedures for newly exposed damage. Define how a new register entry is added and how it re-enters the scope-category and package process.
  4. Create communication and approval channels among owner, contractor, insurer, adjuster, consultants, and authorities. Record the channel and contact for each.
  5. Create daily or periodic reporting requirements. State the frequency and recipient of progress reporting.
  6. Create a risk register for safety, contamination, hidden damage, drying, weather, access, supply, price, and schedule. Record likelihood, impact, and response for each risk.
  7. Prepare the restoration action plan with scope, exclusions, assumptions, priorities, sequence, responsibilities, approvals, and closeout criteria. Assemble every prior deliverable into the final plan document.
  8. Review the plan with qualified stakeholders. Record who reviewed the plan and the date.
  9. Record unresolved issues, deferred work, and owner decisions. Add these to the plan as an open-items list.
  10. Authorize only the next approved phase rather than assuming all phases are released. State the authorized phase on the cover sheet and the condition for authorizing the next one.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed restoration action plan using the same checks regardless of incident type or project size.

  • Register check: confirm every room, system, component, and condition from Chapters 1–15 appears in the damage register with its original identifier.
  • Category check: confirm every register entry is assigned to exactly one scope category.
  • Priority check: confirm immediate-hazard and source-control packages precede every mitigation, repair, and rebuild package they affect.
  • Package check: confirm every work package has a trade, deliverable, acceptance condition, and dependency list.
  • Hold-point check: confirm a hold point exists before every rebuild package with an open environmental, moisture, structural, utility, or inspection dependency.
  • Risk check: confirm every identified risk has a likelihood, impact, and response.

Complete the Restoration Action Plan Review in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 17.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Damage-Register Entry Has No Scope Category

Probable cause: the entry was carried forward from an earlier chapter's record but never sorted into a scope category during plan development.
Confirmation: cross-check the damage register against the scope-category worksheet for any entry without a category assignment.
Correction: assign the missing entry to the correct category using the supporting record from the originating chapter, or mark it pending investigation if no supporting record exists.
Reinspection: repeat the category check across the full damage register.

A Rebuild Package Was Sequenced Ahead of Its Dependency

Probable cause: the work-package schedule listed a rebuild package without checking whether its source-control, drying, or inspection dependency was already closed.
Confirmation: review each rebuild package's dependency list against the actual status of the referenced source-control, drying, or inspection record.
Correction: insert a hold point before the rebuild package and do not authorize it until the dependency is documented as complete.
Reinspection: repeat the hold-point check across the full phased sequence.

Emergency Work Was Blended into a General Repair Description

Probable cause: the planner described stabilization and permanent repair together as one undifferentiated work package.
Confirmation: review each work package description for language that mixes life-safety action with finished-condition restoration.
Correction: split the package into separate emergency and rebuild packages, each with its own deliverable and acceptance condition.
Reinspection: repeat the category separation check across the full work-package schedule.

A Preliminary Estimate Was Treated as an Approved Value

Probable cause: a preliminary quantity, labor, or duration figure was recorded on the schedule without the required preliminary label.
Confirmation: review every quantity, labor, or duration figure on the work-package schedule for the preliminary label.
Correction: add the preliminary label and route the figure through the Chapter 17 estimate-register process before it is treated as approved.
Reinspection: repeat the plan review with the responsible stakeholder.

The Plan Authorized More Than the Next Approved Phase

Probable cause: the plan document stated that all phases were released for work rather than identifying the single next authorized phase.
Confirmation: check the cover sheet and phased sequence for a statement of which phase is currently authorized.
Correction: revise the plan to identify only the next approved phase and the condition required to authorize the following phase.
Reinspection: repeat the plan review and obtain renewed stakeholder acknowledgment.

Cleanup

Skill Check

  1. Why must emergency work be separated from mitigation and rebuild work rather than described as one general repair?
  2. What distinguishes mitigation from permanent repair, and why does the sequence depend on that distinction?
  3. Why must every damage-register entry be assigned to exactly one scope category?
  4. What must a work package define at minimum before it can be considered complete for scheduling purposes?
  5. Why must a hold point be placed before any rebuild package with an open source-control, moisture, structural, or inspection dependency?
  6. What is the difference between a preliminary estimate and an approved claim value, and why must the plan never blur that distinction?
  7. What must happen to a damage-register entry that has no supporting record from an earlier chapter?
  8. Why does the risk register require a likelihood, impact, and response for each identified risk rather than a bare list of concerns?
  9. Why must the plan authorize only the next approved phase rather than releasing the entire project at once?
  10. Who must review and acknowledge the restoration action plan before it governs field work?

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to repair one limited, accessible, wind-damaged section of siding, trim, or flashing after the storm has passed and the area has been assessed for structural and envelope safety. The finished work product is a secure, weather-managed exterior repair with compatible materials and fasteners, restored water-resistive barrier and flashing continuity, preserved drainage, and documented concealed conditions.

You will confirm the storm has passed and access is safe, review the damage assessment and repair authorization, remove only loose or non-salvageable exterior material, inspect the exposed water-resistive barrier and flashing, restore drainage and flashing sequence, install matching replacement siding and trim, prepare and seal joints correctly, finish the repair to match, and verify water control through inspection and, where conditions allow, a controlled water observation.

This chapter builds on Chapter 12's temporary weatherproofing and Chapter 13's temporary roof-leak protection, both of which stabilize storm damage until permanent repair is possible. This chapter is that permanent repair for siding, trim, and flashing. It feeds directly into Chapter 19's final inspection, which verifies that exterior weatherproofing was restored correctly, and into Chapter 20's capstone, which may use this repair sequence when the approved scenario includes a storm-damaged exterior section.

  • RD-081 — Repair trim using matching profile, joinery, and fastening.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 12 stabilized a storm-damaged opening. Chapter 13 stabilized a roof leak from a safe low-access method. This chapter closes out the exterior envelope repair for siding, trim, and flashing so the temporary protections built in those chapters can be removed and replaced with a permanent, drainage-correct repair. The habit built here — restore flashing and drainage sequence before finish material, and never rely on sealant as a substitute for proper flashing — carries directly into Chapter 18's rebuild work and Chapter 19's final weatherproofing inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • repairing one limited, accessible, wind-damaged siding, trim, or flashing-transition area after the storm has passed
  • removing only loose or non-salvageable exterior material within a controlled boundary
  • inspecting exposed water-resistive barrier (WRB), flashing, sheathing, and framing before closure
  • restoring shingle-lap drainage, flashing sequence, and sealant joints designed for sealant
  • installing matching replacement siding and trim with correct laps, clearances, and fastening
  • inspecting the completed repair for water-control continuity

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on structural wall damage, high-elevation areas, major water entry, masonry failure, or broad cladding detachment; those conditions require professional structural or envelope assessment
  • repair electrical, gas, window, door, or roof-to-wall damage discovered during this work; those conditions require the applicable licensed trade or professional referral
  • use sealant as a substitute for missing head, sill, step, or kickout flashing
  • fasten into unverified, damaged, or unknown substrate, or close the assembly before required WRB and flashing repairs are complete

Any condition discovered during removal that exceeds this chapter's scope must stop work and be referred, not repaired by extending the approved boundary without change control.

Trade Note

Flashing sequence controls drainage, not appearance. A repair that looks correct but reverses a lap, blocks a weep, or substitutes sealant for flashing will leak later even though it passes a casual visual check. Verify shingle-lap order and open drainage paths before judging the repair by its finish coat.

Setup Standard

Do not begin exterior work until the storm has passed, ground and public exclusion zones are established, and the damage assessment confirms this repair does not involve structural wall damage, high elevation, major water entry, masonry failure, or broad cladding detachment. Common Field Fundamentals

Every exterior repair in this book follows the same order: confirm safe access and authorization, remove only what is damaged, inspect what removal exposes, restore concealed drainage and flashing before finish material, fasten into verified sound substrate, and inspect the finished assembly for water-control continuity — not just appearance. That order protects the building whether the repair is one siding course or a full elevation.

Sealant Is Not a Flashing Substitute

Sealant belongs only at joints specifically designed to receive it. Head, sill, step, and kickout flashing manage water through overlap and geometry, not adhesion. A joint that should be flashed but is instead sealed will fail as the sealant ages, and the failure will be hidden behind finish material until the next storm.

Measurement Cue — Shingle-Lap Order Before Fastening

Confirm every course, trim piece, and flashing transition laps in the shingle-fashion drainage direction — upper over lower, outer over inner — before driving a single fastener. Reversing a lap during a rushed repair is the most common cause of a repair that fails its first rain event.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop for Structural or Utility Damage

Stop work immediately if removal exposes structural wall damage, torn or extensively missing WRB beyond the approved boundary, damaged sheathing, displaced window or door openings, roof-to-wall damage, or exposed electrical, gas, or plumbing components. Refer these conditions to the qualified professional identified in the damage assessment before proceeding.

Field Safety Cue — Ground and Overhead Controls

Establish ground and public exclusion zones before work begins, and control sharp edges, falling pieces, fasteners, and wind-caught panels throughout removal and installation. Do not work above unprotected walkways or occupied areas without additional overhead protection.

Code Alert — Scope Boundary Is Not Optional

This chapter covers limited, accessible repairs only. Structural wall damage, high-elevation work, major water entry, masonry failure, or broad cladding detachment require a licensed contractor, structural engineer, or other qualified professional. Extending the approved boundary without documented change control is outside this chapter's scope.

C. Material and Fastener Takeoff

  • Siding and trim stock: matched type, profile, thickness, exposure, and finish, quantified to the approved removal boundary with a 10 percent waste allowance for field cuts and matching adjustments.
  • Flashing stock: matched metal or membrane type, gauge or thickness, and finish, quantified to each exposed transition with minimum 6-inch end laps.
  • Fasteners: corrosion-resistant exterior fasteners compatible with the siding, trim, and substrate materials; do not mix corrosive metal combinations at any fastening point.
  • WRB, backer rod, and sealant: matching or manufacturer-compatible WRB patch material; backer rod sized to each designed sealant joint; sealant compatible with substrate and finish coating per product instructions.
  • Finish materials: primer and finish coating matched to the existing material system, sufficient for the repaired area plus feathered transition into sound existing finish.

D. Fastener and Attachment Schedule

Fasten siding and trim only into verified sound substrate identified during the exposed-cavity inspection. Use corrosion-resistant fasteners compatible with the siding, trim, and substrate materials, at the spacing and edge distance specified by the material manufacturer's instructions. Do not overdrive fasteners, pin siding intended to float with seasonal movement, split material at fastening points, or mix corrosive metal combinations. Restore flashing transitions in shingle-lap order before installing finish siding and trim over them.

F. Assessment and Access

  1. Confirm the storm has passed and exterior access, wind, temperature, precipitation, electrical, and overhead conditions are safe. Record conditions on the repair cover sheet before proceeding.
  2. Review the damage assessment, material type, wall assembly, flashing detail, manufacturer instructions, and repair authorization. Confirm all required information is available before removal begins.
  3. Confirm the work does not involve structural wall damage, high elevation, major water entry, masonry failure, or broad cladding detachment. Stop and refer if any of these conditions is present.
  4. Establish ground and public exclusion zones. Mark the zone before staging tools or materials.
  5. Photograph the damaged elevation, loose components, exposed wall, and surrounding transitions. Capture overview and close photographs before touching any material.
  6. Identify siding, trim, flashing, WRB, sheathing, fasteners, sealant, and penetrations affected. Record each on the repair cover sheet.
  7. Inspect for hidden water, torn WRB, damaged sheathing, displaced openings, and unsafe utilities. Use non-invasive methods before removal wherever possible.
  8. Stop for structural, electrical, gas, window, door, roof-to-wall, or extensive envelope damage. Refer to the qualified professional identified in the damage assessment.

G. Removal and Exposed-Cavity Inspection

  1. Remove only loose or non-salvageable exterior material. Stop at the approved removal boundary at stable laps, joints, or transitions.
  2. Control sharp edges, falling pieces, fasteners, and wind-caught panels. Use gloves, eye protection, and controlled handling throughout removal.
  3. Preserve representative pieces for matching and insurance documentation. Set aside one sample of each removed material type.
  4. Inspect exposed WRB, flashing, sheathing, furring, insulation, and framing. Photograph every exposed condition before proceeding.
  5. Dry and clean the area before closure. Do not close over damp or debris-covered material.

H. Concealed Repair — WRB, Flashing, and Drainage

  1. Repair limited WRB tears using the approved compatible method. Install patch material shingle-fashion, lapping 6 inches beyond sound WRB on all sides.
  2. Restore shingle-lap drainage and approved flashing sequence. Verify each course and transition laps upper-over-lower, outer-over-inner, before proceeding.
  3. Do not use sealant as a substitute for missing head, sill, step, or kickout flashing. Install the correct flashing component in every location that requires one.

I. Replacement Material Selection and Fitting

  1. Select replacement siding and trim matching type, profile, thickness, exposure, finish, and movement requirements. Confirm the match against the preserved sample piece.
  2. Condition or acclimate material where required. Follow the manufacturer's stated acclimation period before installation.
  3. Cut replacement pieces with safe support and dust controls. Support material fully during cutting and control dust per the material's hazard requirements.
  4. Treat field cuts and exposed end grain where required. Apply the specified sealant, primer, or edge treatment before installation.
  5. Dry-fit siding and trim while maintaining laps, clearances, expansion, and drainage openings. Confirm fit before final fastening.

J. Fastening and Flashing Integration

  1. Fasten into verified sound substrate with compatible exterior fasteners. Confirm substrate condition from the Section G inspection before driving fasteners.
  2. Do not overdrive, pin movement, split material, or mix corrosive metals. Inspect each fastener as it is driven.
  3. Restore flashing transitions around openings, penetrations, roof lines, and horizontal trim. Confirm each transition maintains the correct lap sequence.

K. Sealant Joints and Finish

  1. Prepare sealant joints only where designed for sealant. Confirm the joint is a designed sealant joint, not a lapped or flashed transition, before proceeding.
  2. Install backer material and compatible sealant at the specified geometry. Follow the manufacturer's joint-width and depth requirements.
  3. Keep weeps, laps, vents, and drainage paths open. Verify no drainage path was sealed or blocked during this step.
  4. Prime, coat, or touch up repaired materials according to the finish system. Feather the finish into sound existing material.

L. Inspection and Verification

  1. Inspect attachment, plane, alignment, laps, clearances, flashing, sealant, and finish. Complete the Acceptance Standards table before proceeding to water testing.
  2. Perform a controlled water observation only when the assembly and weather permit it. Do not perform water testing in unsafe or freezing conditions.
  3. Inspect the interior for related leakage after repair. Check the corresponding interior area during and after any water observation.
  4. Photograph concealed and completed work. Capture before-closure and after-completion photographs for the same locations.
  5. Document materials, fasteners, flashing, sealant, weather, access, and remaining referrals. Complete the repair record before leaving the site.
  6. Schedule reinspection after the next significant wind or rain event. Record the planned reinspection date on the repair record.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed repair using the same checks regardless of material type or elevation.

  • Boundary check: confirm only the approved removal boundary was disturbed and no sound adjacent material was affected.
  • Drainage check: confirm every lap, transition, and flashing component follows correct shingle-fashion order with open weeps.
  • Attachment check: confirm all fasteners are driven into verified sound substrate at the specified schedule with no splitting or corrosive combinations.
  • Sealant check: confirm sealant appears only at designed sealant joints and was never substituted for missing flashing.
  • Finish check: confirm the repaired area matches existing plane, alignment, and finish within tolerance.
  • Water-control check: confirm interior inspection and, where performed, controlled water observation show no leakage.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Repaired Area Leaks After the Next Rain Event

Probable cause: a lap was reversed, a flashing component was omitted, or sealant was used in place of required flashing.
Confirmation: remove the affected finish material and trace the drainage path from the leak point outward, checking each lap and transition against the correct shingle-fashion order.
Correction: correct the lap direction, install the missing flashing component, and remove any sealant that was substituted for flashing.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check and perform a controlled water observation before final closure.

A Fastener Split the Replacement Siding or Trim

Probable cause: the fastener was driven too close to an edge, overdriven, or installed without a required pilot hole.
Confirmation: inspect the split location against the material manufacturer's edge-distance and pilot-hole requirements.
Correction: remove the damaged piece, replace it with new stock, and reinstall using the correct edge distance, pilot hole, and drive depth.
Reinspection: repeat the attachment check across the full repair.

A Weep or Drainage Opening Was Sealed Shut

Probable cause: sealant was applied broadly across a transition instead of only at the designed sealant joint.
Confirmation: inspect every weep, vent, and drainage opening in the repaired area for sealant blockage.
Correction: remove the sealant from the drainage opening and confirm the path is fully open.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check and confirm no other openings were affected.

Replacement Material Does Not Match the Existing Exposure or Plane

Probable cause: the replacement stock has a different profile, thickness, or exposure than the existing material, or courses were not aligned to the existing reference line.
Confirmation: measure the repaired area's exposure, plane, and alignment against the preserved sample piece and adjacent sound material.
Correction: remove and replace the mismatched material with correctly matched stock, re-establishing the reference line from sound adjacent courses.
Reinspection: repeat the finish check across the repaired area and its transition into sound material.

Removal Exposed Damage Beyond the Approved Boundary

Probable cause: hidden decay, prior damage, or storm damage extended further than the initial visual assessment identified.
Confirmation: compare the exposed condition against the approved removal boundary and repair authorization.
Correction: stop work, document the discovered condition with photographs and measurements, and process a documented scope change before extending removal or repair.
Reinspection: confirm the revised boundary is authorized before resuming work.

Work Proceeded Without Confirming Storm Conditions Had Passed

Probable cause: the crew began exterior work before confirming wind, precipitation, and overhead conditions were safe.
Confirmation: check the repair cover sheet for a recorded confirmation of safe conditions before work began.
Correction: stop work immediately if conditions are unsafe, withdraw to a safe location, and resume only after conditions are reconfirmed and recorded.
Reinspection: confirm the cover sheet is complete before any further work is accepted into the repair record.

Field Terms

Shingle-Lap Drainage
The overlapping arrangement that sheds water downward and outward, upper course over lower course.
WRB
Water-resistive barrier; the building-wrap or felt layer beneath siding providing the secondary drainage plane.
Designed Sealant Joint
A joint specifically detailed to receive backer rod and sealant, distinct from a lapped or flashed joint.
Weep
An intentional gap or opening allowing incidental water behind cladding to drain back out.
Verified Sound Substrate
Framing or sheathing confirmed by inspection to be free of decay, damage, or moisture before fastening.
Field Cut
A cut made on-site to fit a replacement piece, as distinct from a factory edge.
Kickout Flashing
A flashing component that diverts roof runoff away from a wall at a roof-to-wall termination.
Backer Rod
Compressible material installed behind sealant to control joint depth and sealant performance.
End Lap
The overlap between two pieces of flashing or siding at their ends, sized to maintain drainage continuity.
Controlled Water Observation
A monitored water test performed under safe conditions to verify a completed repair sheds water correctly.

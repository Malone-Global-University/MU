Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare one controlled restoration work area from the incident record built in Chapter 1. The finished work product is a documented work area with clean, transition, work, equipment, waste, and restricted zones; protected unaffected floors, walls, contents, and common areas; staged PPE and tools; a controlled debris path; and a completed, signed restoration safety inspection authorizing work to begin.

You will review the Chapter 1 hazard classification and authorized scope, confirm that structural, electrical, gas, and contamination conditions have been cleared for setup, define who may enter each zone, install containment and protection appropriate to the hazard, stage PPE and equipment, establish safe debris and equipment routes, and complete a documented safety inspection before any disturbance begins.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1's damage log, safety register, and preliminary scope. Chapter 3 begins its source-control and stabilization work inside the zones this chapter establishes. Chapter 4's material removal and every later chapter's containment, PPE, and debris-handling requirements depend on the readiness record produced here, and the Chapter 20 capstone repeats this exact setup sequence.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-007 — Establish clean, transition, work, equipment, waste, and restricted containment zones appropriate to the hazard.
  • RD-008 — Stage personal protective equipment by task and contamination zone.
  • RD-009 — Isolate damaged or hazardous areas without blocking required building or life-safety functions.
  • RD-010 — Protect unaffected rooms, contents, and common areas from debris, moisture, and contamination.
  • RD-011 — Control debris paths from the work zone to the waste container.
  • RD-012 — Complete a documented restoration safety inspection and obtain readiness approval.

Where This Skill Fits

Every restoration project in this book that involves entering a work area more than once uses the zones, protection, and readiness inspection built in this chapter. Chapter 3's source-control work, Chapter 4's material removal, Chapter 5's drying setup, and every mold-safe, smoke-cleaning, or debris-handling chapter that follows depend on the containment and staging habits established here. The discipline of confirming readiness before disturbance — rather than setting up while work is already underway — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone applies across an entire restoration project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the Chapter 1 incident assessment, hazard classification, and authorized scope before any setup begins
  • defining and physically establishing clean, transition, work, equipment, waste, and restricted zones
  • protecting unaffected floors, walls, contents, and common areas
  • staging PPE, tools, and equipment appropriate to the classified hazard
  • establishing safe debris, cord, and hose routing
  • completing a documented restoration safety inspection before work is authorized

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with setup in any area where structural, electrical, gas, fire, sewage, chemical, or hazardous-material conditions have not been cleared
  • begin water extraction, material removal, cleaning, drying, or repair; those actions belong to later chapters and require their own authorization
  • isolate or operate building HVAC, fire-protection, or life-safety systems beyond an approved temporary measure confirmed with qualified personnel
  • select respiratory protection without the applicable hazard assessment and respiratory-program requirements

Any condition discovered during setup that falls outside the Chapter 1 authorized scope must be stopped, recorded, and referred, not worked around.

Trade Note

A work area is not ready simply because containment sheeting is hung. It is ready when every zone has a defined boundary and entry rule, every hazard from the Chapter 1 safety register has been addressed or excluded from the setup, and a named responsible person has signed the readiness record. Skipping the signed readiness record is the most common way avoidable contamination or injury enters an otherwise well-intentioned setup.

Setup Standard

Do not begin containment installation until the Chapter 1 hazard classification and authorized scope have been reviewed and structural, electrical, gas, sewage, chemical, and hazardous-material conditions have been cleared for the planned setup. Do not authorize work in the completed area until the responsible person signs the readiness record.

Shop Tip

Walk the planned debris path before you install a single barrier. Deciding the route from work zone to waste container after containment is up almost always forces a compromise — a route through a clean room, a blocked doorway, or a path that crosses the equipment zone.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every work-area setup in this book, from this chapter's first zone layout to the Chapter 20 capstone, follows the same sequence: confirm the incident record and clearance, define zones, protect the unaffected building, install containment, stage PPE and equipment, control the debris path, and complete a documented readiness inspection before disturbance. Learning that sequence here, before any material is removed, means every later chapter builds on a work area you already trust.

Setup Follows the Hazard, Not the Habit

The number and boundary of zones depend on the hazard classification carried forward from Chapter 1, not on a fixed template. A small clean-water loss may need only a work zone and a waste zone; a suspected mold or smoke condition may need a full clean-to-restricted zone sequence with a transition zone for decontamination. Do not install more containment than the hazard requires, and do not install less.

Documentation Cue — Confirm Zones Against the Chapter 1 Record

Before installing any barrier, confirm each zone boundary against the Chapter 1 affected-area map and safety register. Do not define a zone boundary from memory or from a quick walk-through that skips the documented record.

Critical Safety Cue — Cleared Conditions Before Setup

Do not begin containment or protection installation until structural, electrical, gas, fire, sewage, chemical, and hazardous-material conditions identified in Chapter 1 have been cleared for the planned setup. If any condition remains unresolved, stop and refer it before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Egress Before Enclosure

Confirm that every containment entry point preserves required emergency egress before sealing any barrier. Do not install containment that blocks a required exit path without an approved alternate egress plan.

Code Alert — HVAC and Life-Safety Systems

Do not isolate, block, or operate building HVAC, combustion-air, return-air, smoke-control, or fire-protection systems without coordination with qualified personnel and the restoration plan. Confirm what is permitted before touching any building system.

C. Material and Supply Takeoff

  • Containment supplies: containment poles or framing, sheeting sized to each zone boundary, tape and containment fasteners compatible with the surface, and zippered access points sufficient for the planned zone count, with a 15 percent reserve for repair or reconfiguration.
  • Protection supplies: floor runners, board, or film sufficient to cover the full access and debris route; corner and edge protection for door frames, stair nosings, and cabinet edges along that route.
  • Staging supplies: a designated clean storage container for replacement PPE, filters, and consumables; labeled bins or racks for tools and equipment by zone.
  • Signage and barriers: zone-identification signs for each established zone, exclusion barriers for the restricted zone, and public-protection barriers where the setup is visible or accessible to non-workers.
  • Documentation stock: restoration safety inspection forms, protection-record forms, and PPE/equipment staging log forms sufficient for one full work area with a 20 percent reserve.

D. Zone and Entry Schedule

For each established zone, record the following: zone name, physical boundary description, containment method, authorized occupants, entry rule (sign-in, escort, or unrestricted), and decontamination requirement if any. Do not leave any zone without a stated entry rule, and do not permit an occupant category to enter a zone not listed as authorized for that category.

F. Preparation — Record Review and Clearance Confirmation

  1. Review the incident assessment, hazard classification, authorized scope, occupancy plan, utility status, environmental requirements, and emergency contacts. Confirm the Chapter 1 damage log, safety register, and preliminary scope are complete and available before setup begins.
  2. Confirm that structural, electrical, gas, fire, sewage, chemical, and hazardous-material conditions have been cleared for the planned setup. Record who confirmed each clearance and the date.
  3. Define who may enter each area and establish sign-in, escort, and communication requirements. Write the entry rule for each planned zone on the zone plan before installing any containment.

G. Zone Definition and Containment Installation

  1. Select the clean zone, transition zone, work zone, equipment zone, waste zone, and restricted zone. Mark each boundary on the zone plan referencing the Chapter 1 sketch.
  2. Establish a controlled entry point that does not block emergency egress. Verify the entry point against the building's marked egress path before installing containment.
  3. Protect unaffected floors, walls, doors, stairs, elevators, cabinets, contents, landscaping, and common areas. Install floor and surface protection along the full access and debris route before any containment is installed.
  4. Remove or cover unaffected contents according to the approved contents plan. Record each moved or covered item in the protection record.
  5. Install containment barriers compatible with the hazard and required airflow plan. Select barrier material and seam method matched to the classified hazard.
  6. Seal or isolate penetrations only where the plan requires it and without blocking life-safety or building functions. Confirm each sealed penetration against the plan before sealing.
  7. Coordinate HVAC isolation or operation with qualified personnel and the restoration plan. Record the name of the qualified person consulted and the decision reached.
  8. Do not block combustion air, return-air paths, smoke control, required ventilation, or fire-protection systems without approved temporary measures. Verify each affected system remains functional or has an approved temporary measure in place.

H. Airflow, PPE, and Equipment Staging

  1. Set up negative-air or filtration equipment only when the hazard assessment, power supply, and exhaust location are approved. Confirm approval before operating any negative-air equipment.
  2. Verify that exhaust does not contaminate occupied areas, neighboring property, air intakes, or public routes. Inspect the exhaust discharge point before startup.
  3. Stage required personal protective equipment by task and contamination zone. Place PPE at the transition zone entry appropriate to each zone's classified hazard.
  4. Create clean storage for respirators, filters, gloves, suits, eye protection, and replacement items. Locate this storage inside the clean zone only.
  5. Establish donning, doffing, handwashing, and decontamination procedures where required. Post the procedure at the transition zone entry.
  6. Set up tools and equipment on dry stable surfaces with protected power and cord routing. Verify each surface before placing equipment on it.
  7. Inspect pumps, extractors, air movers, dehumidifiers, scrubbers, vacuums, lights, extension cords, and temporary power equipment. Record the inspection result for each item on the PPE and equipment staging log.
  8. Verify electrical capacity, grounding, circuit protection, cord condition, and dry connections. Do not energize any circuit or cord that fails this verification.
  9. Establish equipment discharge, condensate, and hose routes that cannot cause leaks, trips, freezing, or contamination. Route each hose and cord along the protected access path only.

I. Debris Path, Public Protection, and Readiness Inspection

  1. Create a debris path from work zone to waste container. Mark the path on the zone plan before any material is moved.
  2. Protect the path and separate clean traffic from contaminated debris movement. Confirm the debris path does not cross the clean zone at any point.
  3. Select bags, wraps, containers, labels, carts, and lifting methods appropriate to the debris. Match each selection to the anticipated debris type from the Chapter 1 preliminary scope.
  4. Establish sharp-material, wet-material, contaminated-material, hazardous-material, recyclable, and salvage categories. Label the waste zone containers by category before work begins.
  5. Install barriers, signs, lighting, floor-edge protection, and public exclusion controls. Verify visibility and stability of each sign and barrier.
  6. Establish a ladder, scaffold, roof-access, or elevated-work plan if included. Confirm this plan only when elevated work is part of the authorized scope.
  7. Establish fire extinguisher, first aid, emergency eyewash where required, communication, and evacuation locations. Record the location of each on the zone plan.
  8. Create daily housekeeping, equipment inspection, containment inspection, filter check, and shutdown procedures. Write these procedures into the setup record for use in later chapters.
  9. Test containment and airflow where the approved plan requires it. Record the test method and result.
  10. Photograph the completed setup before disturbance begins. Index each photograph to the zone it documents.
  11. Complete a documented restoration safety inspection. Check every item on the restoration safety inspection form.
  12. Correct every failed access, containment, utility, electrical, respiratory, fire, debris, equipment, or public-protection item. Record the correction and re-check the item.
  13. Authorize work only after the responsible person signs the readiness record. Do not permit entry to the work zone for any purpose beyond this setup until the readiness record is signed.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed work area using the same checks regardless of hazard type or property size.

  • Zone check: confirm every required zone is established with a defined boundary, entry rule, and authorized occupant list.
  • Egress check: confirm no containment entry point blocks required emergency egress.
  • Protection check: confirm every unaffected floor, wall, content group, and common area along the access route is protected and photographed.
  • Staging check: confirm every PPE and equipment item is staged in the correct zone and inspected.
  • Debris path check: confirm the debris path is continuous, protected, and does not cross a clean zone.
  • Readiness check: confirm every inspection item is checked, every failed item is corrected, and the responsible person has signed the readiness record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Zone Was Installed Without a Stated Entry Rule

Probable cause: the zone boundary was installed from the containment plan but the entry rule (sign-in, escort, or unrestricted) was never written down.
Confirmation: compare the zone plan against the physical containment and confirm every zone has a matching entry rule.
Correction: add the missing entry rule to the zone plan and post it at the zone entry point before permitting further entry.
Reinspection: repeat the zone check across every established zone.

Containment Blocked a Required Emergency Egress

Probable cause: a barrier or sealed entry point was installed across a marked exit path without an approved alternate egress plan.
Confirmation: walk every marked egress path in the affected area and confirm it remains clear or has an approved alternate route.
Correction: reposition or modify the containment to restore the required egress path, or obtain an approved alternate egress plan before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the egress check across the full work area.

Debris Path Crosses a Clean Zone

Probable cause: the debris path was chosen for convenience after containment was already installed, rather than planned before setup.
Confirmation: trace the planned debris route on the zone plan and confirm it does not enter any area marked clean zone.
Correction: reroute the debris path through the transition zone or an alternate route that avoids the clean zone, and re-protect the new route.
Reinspection: repeat the debris path check before any debris movement begins.

PPE Was Staged in the Wrong Zone

Probable cause: replacement PPE was stored in the work zone instead of the clean zone, exposing clean equipment to contamination.
Confirmation: check the PPE and equipment staging log against the physical storage location of each item.
Correction: relocate replacement PPE to clean storage inside the clean zone and discard or clean any item exposed to contamination per the approved method.
Reinspection: repeat the staging check before permitting further entry.

Equipment Was Energized Before Electrical Verification

Probable cause: a pump, air mover, or dehumidifier was plugged in and started before cord condition, grounding, and circuit capacity were verified.
Confirmation: check the PPE and equipment staging log for a completed electrical verification entry for each energized item.
Correction: de-energize the equipment, complete the required electrical verification, and correct any failed condition before re-energizing.
Reinspection: repeat the electrical verification for every equipment item before continuing.

Work Began Before the Readiness Record Was Signed

Probable cause: setup appeared complete and work began without a final documented restoration safety inspection and signature.
Confirmation: check the restoration safety inspection form for a completed checklist and a dated signature from the responsible person.
Correction: stop work, complete the restoration safety inspection, correct any failed item, and obtain the required signature before resuming.
Reinspection: confirm the signed readiness record is on file before any further work is accepted into the restoration file.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, ready work area, not with any material removal or repair.

  • Confirm all containment seams, entry points, and floor protection remain secure at the end of the setup session
  • Store staged PPE, tools, and equipment in their assigned zones according to the staging log
  • Return original zone plans, protection records, staging logs, photographs, and the restoration safety inspection to the restoration file without editing the originals
  • Confirm signage, barriers, and public-protection controls remain in place if the property will not be immediately re-entered
  • Deliver the signed readiness record to the responsible party as required by Step 33 of the work order

Skill Check

  1. Why must structural, electrical, gas, and contamination conditions be cleared before containment installation begins, rather than during it?
  2. What is the difference between a transition zone and a restricted zone, and why must a work area not skip the transition zone when contamination is present?
  3. Why must every containment entry point be checked against the building's marked egress path before sealing?
  4. Why must the debris path be planned before containment is installed, rather than chosen afterward for convenience?
  5. What must be verified about a pump, air mover, or dehumidifier before it is energized in the work area?
  6. Why must replacement PPE be stored in the clean zone rather than the work zone?
  7. What must happen when HVAC isolation or operation is required by the setup plan?
  8. Why must every zone have a stated entry rule rather than an assumed one?
  9. What must occur before work is authorized in the completed work area, and who must sign the readiness record?
  10. Why must a failed item on the restoration safety inspection be corrected and re-checked rather than noted and carried forward?

Performance Check

With the containment, protection, staging, and debris-path work complete, review the finished work area against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every required zone is established with a defined boundary and entry rule; no containment blocks required emergency egress; every unaffected floor, wall, content group, and common area along the access route is protected and photographed; every PPE and equipment item is staged in the correct zone and inspected; the debris path is continuous and does not cross a clean zone; and every item on the restoration safety inspection is checked, corrected where needed, and signed. Complete the Restoration Safety Inspection and correct any failed item before beginning Chapter 3. If an item fails, identify whether the error occurred in zone definition, containment installation, protection coverage, PPE or equipment staging, debris-path routing, or readiness sign-off before correcting it.

Chapter Closeout

You have completed the second chapter in the residential restoration and disaster recovery sequence: setting up a safe restoration work area. This chapter required you to establish clean, transition, work, equipment, waste, and restricted zones appropriate to the hazard (RD-007), stage PPE by task and contamination zone (RD-008), isolate damaged areas without blocking required building or life-safety functions (RD-009), protect unaffected rooms, contents, and common areas (RD-010), control the debris path from work zone to waste container (RD-011), and complete a documented restoration safety inspection with a signed readiness record (RD-012).

You now have a controlled, documented, and formally authorized work area. That work area carries directly into the next chapter: identifying and controlling the source of water damage inside the zones you have just established, and stabilizing the area without beginning unauthorized reconstruction.

File your zone plan, protection record, PPE and equipment staging log, debris path record, photographs, and signed restoration safety inspection together as one dated setup record. Chapter 3 begins from this exact file.

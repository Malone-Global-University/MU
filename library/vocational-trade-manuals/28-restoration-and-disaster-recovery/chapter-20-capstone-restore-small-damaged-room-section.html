Job Overview

This capstone chapter qualifies you to carry one approved limited residential water- or storm-damaged room section through the complete Book 28 restoration workflow: incident assessment and documentation, safe work-area setup, source control, selective wet-material removal, structural drying, repair-plan preparation, concealed-assembly rebuild, drywall, trim, and flooring-edge finish restoration, final quality and safety inspection, and formal owner closeout. The finished work product is one safe, dry, clean, repaired, inspected, documented, and owner-accepted limited restoration project, complete with its incident record, moisture logs, equipment logs, photographs, punch list, insurance-support documentation, and maintenance instructions.

You will select an approved capstone scenario before disturbing the area; confirm the source is corrected or safely controlled; assess, photograph, and document the damage using the Chapter 1 method; set up a contained work area using the Chapter 2 method; remove non-salvageable materials using the Chapter 4 method; dry the affected assembly using the Chapter 5 method; prepare a phased repair plan using the Chapter 16 method; rebuild concealed layers, drywall, trim, and flooring using the Chapter 18 method; complete a final inspection using the Chapter 19 method; and close the project with a complete insurance-support and owner-handoff record using the Chapter 17 method.

This is the last chapter in the book. Every earlier chapter feeds directly into this one: Chapter 1 supplies the assessment method, Chapters 2–6 supply the water-response sequence, Chapters 7–11 supply the moisture and smoke-related boundaries that keep the scenario within the approved limited scope, Chapters 12–14 supply the exterior-stabilization discipline used if the storm-damage alternative is selected, Chapters 15–17 supply the debris, scope, and insurance-documentation discipline, and Chapters 18–19 supply the rebuild and final-inspection method this capstone repeats in full.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RD-115 — Integrate damage inspection and documentation into a single controlling incident record for a full restoration project.
  • RD-116 — Establish containment and control the water or storm-damage source before any wet-material removal begins.
  • RD-117 — Remove wet or damaged materials selectively and dry the affected assembly to a documented, defensible goal.
  • RD-118 — Prepare a repair plan and rebuild concealed assemblies, drywall, trim, and flooring to match the approved design.
  • RD-119 — Perform a final restoration quality and safety inspection and close every punch item.
  • RD-120 — Compile complete restoration records and obtain formal owner acceptance at closeout.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter in Book 28 has been building toward this single integrated project. The damage log and photograph discipline from Chapter 1, the containment and readiness discipline from Chapter 2, the source-control and drying discipline from Chapters 3 through 6, the moisture-risk and smoke-boundary discipline from Chapters 7 through 11, the exterior-stabilization discipline from Chapters 12 through 14, the debris and insurance-documentation discipline from Chapters 15 through 17, and the rebuild and final-inspection discipline from Chapters 18 and 19 all converge here. Completing this capstone independently is the demonstration that you can run a complete limited restoration project from first contact to owner handoff without missing a required control.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers restoring one approved limited residential room section using either of the following scenarios, selected before work begins:

  • a small clean-water-damaged wall and floor section after a verified plumbing leak has been corrected, involving limited baseboard, drywall, insulation, and compatible flooring-edge repair
  • a small storm-damaged wall or window-adjacent interior section, used only when the exterior opening has been safely stabilized, the permanent envelope repair is documented or completed, the structure is sound, and water intrusion is limited and clean enough for the approved scope

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • use fire debris, widespread smoke contamination, sewage, groundwater flooding, large mold growth, trauma scenes, chemical contamination, major roof damage, collapsed structure, damaged gas systems, energized wet electrical systems, asbestos, lead abatement, or occupied-space remediation beyond the approved training setup as the capstone scenario
  • proceed past any stop-work condition identified in Chapters 1 through 19 without the required professional referral and documented resolution
  • rebuild before source correction, removal, drying, structural and utility acceptance, and required inspections are documented and accepted
  • represent this capstone as a substitute for licensed, certified, or specialty restoration work where the scenario or hidden conditions exceed the approved limited scope

Any condition discovered during the capstone that falls outside the approved scenario must stop the affected portion of work, be documented, and be referred, exactly as required in the earlier chapters that govern that condition.

Trade Note

This capstone does not introduce new technical methods. It sequences the methods already taught in Chapters 1 through 19 into one continuous project. Treat every reference to an earlier chapter's method as a requirement to perform that method in full, not as a summary to be shortened.

Setup Standard

Do not select or enter the capstone scenario until it has been confirmed to exclude sewage, floodwater, widespread mold, fire debris, hazardous materials, structural damage, wet energized systems, gas damage, and unsafe roof access. Do not begin removal before the Chapter 1 assessment and Chapter 2 readiness inspection are both complete and documented.

Shop Tip

Open the restoration file on day one and add to it after every phase — assessment, setup, source control, removal, drying, rebuild, and inspection — rather than reconstructing the record at the end. A capstone file assembled after the fact is the most common reason photographs and readings cannot be matched to the correct phase.

F. Scenario Selection and Authorization

  1. Select an approved capstone scenario before entering or disturbing the area. Record the selected scenario on the authorization cover sheet.
  2. Use the preferred limited clean-water wall and floor-edge loss after a verified plumbing repair, or the approved small storm-water alternative. Confirm the alternative meets its exterior-stabilization prerequisites if selected.
  3. Confirm the project excludes sewage, floodwater, widespread mold, fire debris, hazardous materials, structural damage, wet energized systems, gas damage, and unsafe roof access. Record confirmation against the Scope-Exclusion Schedule.
  4. Obtain owner authorization, incident history, source-repair record, utility status, environmental clearance, repair documents, and insurer instructions. File each in the opening restoration record.
  5. Identify the responsible restoration lead and approval authority. Record names, roles, and contact information.
  6. Establish the incident date, discovery time, affected rooms, occupancy, weather, and emergency actions. Enter these on the incident cover sheet.

G. Chapter 1 Assessment and Documentation

  1. Perform the Chapter 1 exterior and interior damage assessment. Follow the full assessment sequence without abbreviation.
  2. Photograph all elevations, room overviews, damage boundaries, source area, contents, utilities, and pre-existing conditions. Index every photograph to a room or hazard identifier.
  3. Create the room sketch, damage register, photograph index, and access-limitations statement. Use the Chapter 1 forms.
  4. Identify immediate safety, structural, electrical, gas, contamination, sharp, fall, and egress hazards. Record each in the safety register.
  5. Stop and refer any condition outside the approved capstone. Document the referral and the affected boundary before proceeding elsewhere.

H. Chapter 3 Source Control and Utility Safety

  1. Confirm the source is permanently corrected or establish an approved temporary control with permanent-repair schedule. Record the source status on the source-control record.
  2. Verify electrical safety and isolate affected circuits or equipment through qualified procedures. Do not enter water or touch wet electrical equipment directly.
  3. Protect contents and unaffected surfaces. Move or cover approved contents before further work begins.

I. Chapter 2 Work-Area Setup

  1. Set up the Chapter 2 clean, transition, work, equipment, waste, and restricted zones. Mark and confirm each zone boundary.
  2. Install approved containment and protect HVAC openings as required. Verify airflow direction and exhaust location.
  3. Stage PPE, tools, extraction, drying, monitoring, waste, lighting, and emergency equipment. Confirm each item is present and functional before disturbance begins.
  4. Complete the restoration safety inspection and obtain readiness approval. Do not proceed to removal without the signed readiness record.

J. Chapter 3–4 Extraction and Selective Removal

  1. Remove approved standing clean water if present. Work from the safest accessible point using approved equipment.
  2. Create the initial moisture map with labeled material locations and reference points. Use consistent, repeatable measurement points.
  3. Determine affected material and approved removal boundaries. Mark boundaries at stable joints, seams, or framing.
  4. Complete required lead, asbestos, mold, or other hazardous-material assessment before disturbance. Stop for any suspect regulated material.
  5. Photograph and label trim, drywall, insulation, flooring, and contents before removal. Index each photograph to the removal record.
  6. Remove trim selectively with fastener and profile preservation. Preserve representative pieces for matching.
  7. Create exploratory openings and verify wiring, plumbing, ducts, framing, and fire blocking. Locate concealed systems before broad removal.
  8. Remove only approved non-salvageable drywall and insulation. Do not remove material solely because it is near the affected area.
  9. Remove approved localized flooring or underlayment only through the product-specific method. Stop for suspect regulated flooring materials.
  10. Bag, label, quantify, and route debris through the controlled path. Separate salvage, insurer-review, and disposal categories.
  11. Preserve insurer-review items and representative material where required. Label and store separately from disposal debris.
  12. Inspect exposed framing, sheathing, subfloor, utilities, fasteners, insulation, and adjacent cavities. Photograph every concealed condition found.
  13. Stop for concealed contamination, decay, structural damage, unsafe wiring, or damage beyond the planned boundary. Do not continue removal in the affected area until resolved.
  14. Process a documented scope change before extending removal. Record the reason, new boundary, and approval.
  15. Physically clean exposed retained surfaces using the approved method. Do not dry sweep contaminated dust.

K. Chapter 5 Structural Drying

  1. Define drying goals from reference materials, product requirements, and qualified direction. Do not use a single universal moisture percentage for dissimilar materials.
  2. Select and place dehumidification, air movement, filtration, and drainage equipment. Follow the approved restoration method for equipment selection.
  3. Record equipment identification, location, setting, power source, and start time. Enter each item on the equipment log.
  4. Verify electrical capacity, cord safety, condensate routing, heat, noise, and public protection. Correct any unsafe equipment condition before startup.
  5. Monitor equipment operation, temperature, relative humidity, and material readings at defined intervals. Use the same labeled points at each visit.
  6. Inspect adjacent rooms, wall plates, floor edges, cabinets, and concealed areas for hidden moisture. Record findings at every monitoring visit.
  7. Investigate stalled or increasing readings. Do not assume equipment operation alone will resolve an unexplained reading.
  8. Adjust the drying plan only through documented qualified review. Record the reason for any adjustment.
  9. Record every visit, reading, equipment change, source condition, and weather event. Maintain the drying log continuously.
  10. Confirm final drying goals and complete the drying-release inspection. Do not proceed to rebuild without this documented release.
  11. Remove equipment and inspect for condensate, leaks, cord damage, and residual moisture. Record the equipment removal time and final condition.

L. Chapter 16 Repair Planning and Chapter 6/18 Rebuild

  1. Prepare the Chapter 16 restoration repair plan with scope, trades, sequence, permits, inspections, and closeout criteria. Review the plan with the responsible party before rebuild begins.
  2. Verify framing, structure, utilities, cleanliness, moisture, environmental conditions, and repair boundaries before closure. Confirm every item is accepted before covering any cavity.
  3. Complete required rough, concealed, and pre-closure inspections. Do not close any cavity before the applicable inspection is signed.
  4. Photograph all concealed conditions and approvals. Index each photograph to its corresponding inspection.
  5. Restore backing, fire blocking, air sealing, vapor control, insulation, and other required concealed layers. Match the pre-loss assembly design.
  6. Install matching drywall with supported edges and approved fasteners. Follow the manufacturer's fastening schedule.
  7. Complete tape, compound, sanding, texture, primer, and paint. Match existing texture only after flatness and joint quality are accepted.
  8. Prepare the flooring substrate and install the approved flooring-edge or localized patch. Confirm substrate flatness, strength, and dryness first.
  9. Maintain movement, thickness, transition, adhesive, and cure requirements. Follow the flooring product's approved method exactly.
  10. Install matching trim and shoe molding without pinning floating flooring. Verify alignment, reveals, and floor clearance before fastening.
  11. Apply stain-blocking primer only after cleaning, drying, and test approval. Test on an inconspicuous area first.
  12. Reinstall approved devices, plates, registers, hardware, and accessories. Do not conceal junction boxes, shutoffs, or inspection openings.
  13. Test doors, windows, plumbing, electrical devices, alarms, ventilation, and other disturbed components. Record the result of each test.
  14. Inspect the permanent source repair or approved exterior weatherproofing. Confirm it remains intact and effective.
  15. Allow coatings, adhesives, compounds, sealants, and flooring systems to cure. Follow the manufacturer's stated cure time before normal use.

M. Chapter 19 Final Inspection and Punch List

  1. Remove containment through the approved clean-to-dirty sequence. Inspect for residual dust or debris during removal.
  2. Clean and inspect the room, access route, exterior route, equipment, and waste area. Confirm all areas are returned to a clean condition.
  3. Complete the Chapter 19 moisture, odor, surface, trim, flooring, utility, weather, safety, and documentation inspection. Use the full inspection category list without omission.
  4. Prepare a categorized punch list with photographs and acceptance criteria. Classify each item by safety, source, moisture, structural, finish, or documentation category.
  5. Correct every safety, source, moisture, utility, weather, finish, and documentation deficiency. Do not close the project with any open safety or active-water item.
  6. Reinspect and close every punch item. Record the correction and reinspection result for each item.

N. Records, Insurance Documentation, and Owner Handoff

  1. Compile incident records, photographs, moisture maps, readings, equipment logs, labor, materials, disposal, permits, inspections, products, warranties, and communications. Assemble the complete restoration file.
  2. Prepare insurance-support documentation without making coverage conclusions. Follow the Chapter 17 documentation standard exactly.
  3. Prepare owner maintenance and monitoring instructions. Cover moisture monitoring, ventilation, exterior checks, and future warning signs.
  4. Conduct the owner walkthrough and explain repaired areas, exclusions, temporary or permanent controls, and future warning signs. Answer questions using the documented record.
  5. Obtain written acceptance and document deferred owner or insurer items separately. Record the acceptance date and signatory.
  6. Archive the restoration file and close the project as safe, dry, clean, repaired, inspected, documented, and accepted. Store the archived file according to the project's record-retention requirement.

Common Inspection Verification

Inspect the completed capstone project using the same discipline applied at the end of every earlier chapter, now applied across the whole project.

  • Sequence check: confirm every phase — assessment, containment, source control, removal, drying, rebuild, and final inspection — has its own signed, dated record in the restoration file.
  • Boundary check: confirm no removal, drying, or rebuild step extended beyond an approved and documented scope change.
  • Stop-work check: confirm every concealed-condition stop encountered was documented, referred where required, and resolved before work resumed in that area.
  • Drying-release check: confirm the drying-release inspection was completed and signed before any cavity was closed.
  • Punch-list check: confirm every punch item from the final inspection was corrected and reinspected before closeout.
  • Acceptance check: confirm the owner acceptance record is signed and the complete restoration file has been delivered or archived.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Rebuild Began Before the Drying-Release Inspection Was Signed

Probable cause: the crew proceeded to Section L before the Chapter 5 drying-release inspection in Section K was completed and documented.
Confirmation: check the restoration file for a signed drying-release record dated before the first rebuild photograph.
Correction: stop rebuild work, uncover the affected assembly if closure has already occurred, verify current moisture readings against the drying goal, and obtain the release before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the drying-release check and the sequence check across the full restoration file.

A Concealed Condition Was Found but Removal Continued in the Same Area

Probable cause: decay, suspect contamination, or structural damage was found during Section J removal, but the crew treated it as a minor variation instead of a stop-work condition.
Confirmation: compare removal photographs against the safety register and scope-change log for a matching entry and referral.
Correction: stop work in the affected area immediately, document the condition, and obtain the required professional referral or documented scope change before any further disturbance.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check and stop-work check for the affected area.

The Restoration File Is Missing Records From an Earlier Phase

Probable cause: documentation was deferred to the end of the project instead of being added after each phase, and a phase's photographs or logs were never filed.
Confirmation: walk the Deliverable Schedule in Section B and confirm each deliverable is present, dated, and complete.
Correction: reconstruct the missing record only from contemporaneous evidence such as timestamps, receipts, and equipment logs; if the record cannot be reconstructed, note the gap explicitly rather than inventing the missing content.
Reinspection: repeat the sequence check across the full restoration file.

A Punch Item Was Marked Corrected Without Reinspection

Probable cause: the correction was completed, but no one reinspected the item against its original acceptance criterion before closing it on the punch list.
Confirmation: check each closed punch item for a reinspection date, method, and result separate from the correction entry.
Correction: reopen the item, reinspect it against the original criterion, and record the result before closing it again.
Reinspection: repeat the punch-list check across the full final-inspection record.

Owner Acceptance Was Obtained Before the File Was Complete

Probable cause: the owner walkthrough and signature occurred before the insurance-support documentation, warranties, or maintenance instructions were finished and delivered.
Confirmation: compare the owner acceptance date against the completion dates of every deliverable in Section B.
Correction: complete and deliver the remaining records, and obtain a supplemental acknowledgment confirming their delivery.
Reinspection: repeat the acceptance check against the completed Deliverable Schedule.

The Capstone Scenario Drifted Beyond Its Approved Scope

Probable cause: the removal or drying boundary expanded gradually without a documented scope change, until the project no longer matched the scenario confirmed in Section F.
Confirmation: compare the current removal and drying boundary against the original scenario selection and the Scope-Exclusion Schedule in Section D.
Correction: stop work, document the actual current boundary and condition, and either process a formal documented scope change within the approved capstone limits or refer the excess scope to the appropriate professional outside this chapter.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check and the scope-exclusion confirmation before resuming.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a closed and archived restoration file, not with any physical work area to clean beyond the room's own final condition.

  • Confirm containment, temporary protection, and equipment have been fully removed and the room and access routes are clean per the Chapter 19 standard
  • Back up all photographs, logs, and digital records before leaving the property or immediately upon return
  • Return original notes, sketches, forms, and communications to the restoration file without editing the originals
  • Store the complete restoration file — incident record, logs, plan, insurance-support documentation, punch list, and owner acceptance — together as one archived project record
  • Deliver copies of the maintenance and monitoring instructions to the owner as required by Section N

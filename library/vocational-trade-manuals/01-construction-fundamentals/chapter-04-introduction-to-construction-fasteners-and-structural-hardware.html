Job Overview

This project qualifies you to identify, select, and install the fasteners and structural hardware used throughout the rest of this book. The finished work product is one complete 30-by-48-inch Construction Fastener and Structural Hardware Practice Panel containing eight labeled installation stations: correct installations, controlled failure examples, and the required hole-preparation sequence for each connector shown.

You will prepare and label sample blocks using the Project 03 cutting method, lay out eight stations on a plywood backer, install a common nail, a general-purpose wood screw, a structural wood screw, a lag screw, a through-bolt, a joist hanger, and a framing angle or equivalent connector, create controlled failure examples, and inspect and explain every installation.

This is the fourth project in the book. It is performed inside the jobsite established in Project 01, using the reference-face and verification methods from Project 02, and the measuring, marking, and cutting system from Project 03. The panel becomes a permanent reference artifact for Project 05 and the Chapter 8 capstone, where the same fastener and connector types are installed under load-bearing conditions.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CF-019 — Identify common nails, wood screws, structural screws, lag screws, through-bolts, and connector fasteners by intended use and installation requirement.
  • CF-020 — Select and prepare pilot, clearance, countersink, and bolt holes for the specified joint.
  • CF-021 — Install nails, wood screws, structural screws, lag screws, and through-bolts without splitting, stripping, bending, or crushing the connected material.
  • CF-022 — Install the specified joist hanger, framing angle, strap, tie, or bracket using manufacturer-approved fasteners.
  • CF-023 — Distinguish structural hardware from nonstructural convenience hardware and identify prohibited substitutions.
  • CF-024 — Inspect, label, and explain correct installations and representative failure conditions.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 03 gave you a repeatable method for measuring, marking, and cutting the sample blocks used at every station on this panel. This project applies that method to fastener and hardware installation for the first time: driving nails and screws, drilling pilot and clearance holes, tightening a through-bolt, and installing manufacturer-approved connector fasteners into a joist hanger and a framing angle.

Every joint in the Project 05 sawhorses and every connection in the Chapter 8 capstone floor platform uses one of the fastener or connector types introduced at this panel's eight stations. The panel remains available as a reference during both projects.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying common nails, general-purpose wood screws, structural wood screws, lag screws, through-bolts, and connector fasteners by intended use
  • selecting and preparing pilot, clearance, countersink, and through-bolt holes for a specified joint
  • installing each fastener type without splitting, stripping, bending, or crushing the connected material
  • installing one joist hanger and one framing angle, strap, tie, or bracket using only manufacturer-approved connector fasteners
  • distinguishing structural hardware from nonstructural convenience hardware
  • creating, labeling, and explaining controlled failure examples

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • substitute generic nails, deck screws, drywall screws, roofing nails, undersized fasteners, mixed metals, or any other unapproved fastener at a connector station
  • publish or assume a load rating, structural capacity, or code-approved connection for any panel installation
  • use the practice panel itself as a load-bearing component in any later project; it is a training and reference artifact only
  • install a connector using any fastener not listed in that connector's identified manufacturer instructions

Any fastener, connector, or installed joint that fails its inspection in this chapter must be corrected, replaced, or clearly labeled as a controlled failure example before the panel is considered complete.

Trade Note

This chapter identifies one exact connector type for the joist-hanger and framing-angle stations and requires you to reproduce that connector's own manufacturer installation instructions. A connector's rated capacity depends on its complete fastener schedule; installing the wrong fastener, the wrong quantity, or leaving a required hole empty changes what the connector can safely carry, even though the panel itself carries no load.

The Controlling Hardware Rule

This chapter identifies the exact connector type used in the training panel and reproduces the applicable manufacturer installation instructions for that connector. Generic nails, deck screws, drywall screws, roofing nails, undersized fasteners, mixed metals, and unapproved substitutions are prohibited at connector stations. Hardware capacity depends on the complete listed fastener schedule; not every hole is optional. Fastener diameter, length, head, shank, coating, and material compatibility matter, and treated lumber or exterior exposure may require compatible corrosion-resistant hardware. A training panel is not a substitute for project-specific structural design.

Measurement Cue — Pilot Before Clearance

At every lag-screw or through-bolt joint, drill the pilot hole in the receiving member and the clearance hole in the attached member as two separate, correctly sized operations. Drilling both members with the same bit undersizes the clearance hole, prevents the joint from drawing tight, and can split the attached member as the fastener is tightened.

Critical Safety Cue — Drill and Driver Control

Disconnect power or remove the battery before changing bits. Keep both hands in control of the drill or driver during operation, keep loose clothing and lanyards clear of the chuck, and never force a bit that has stalled; release the trigger, back the bit out, and clear the obstruction before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Supported, Clamped Work

Clamp every sample block to a stable surface before drilling or driving. Never hold a block only in your hand while drilling a pilot, clearance, countersink, or through-bolt hole.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Connector Fastener Schedules

Install only the fastener type, diameter, length, and quantity specified in the identified manufacturer instructions for the joist hanger and framing angle used at Stations 6 and 7. Fill every required hole in the connector's fastener schedule; do not leave a required hole empty or substitute a different fastener because it appears similar.

F. Preparation — Backer, Sample Blocks, and Layout

  1. Cut or verify the 30-by-48-inch plywood backer. Inspect it for flatness, damage, and contamination before laying out any station.
  2. Prepare and label the required 2×4 and 2×6 sample blocks. Cut every block using the Project 03 reference-face, marking, and cutting method, and mark each block with its intended station number.
  3. Lay out all eight stations before fastening any block to the backer. Position each station so its label, connector, and controlled examples will remain visible and accessible after installation.
  4. Fasten the sample blocks from the back. Drive panel-mounting screws from the back of the backer so they do not interfere with the fasteners displayed at the front of each station.
  5. Label every fastener, connector, hole type, and intended installation before beginning the demonstration work. Apply station labels and the fastener schedule reference before driving or drilling anything.

G. Station 1 — Common Nail and Nail-Set Station

  1. Mark the nail location on the labeled block. Use the identified common nail type and mark its driving location per the fastener schedule.
  2. Drive one correct nail and set it as specified. Seat the nail head flush or set it below the surface as required, without splitting the block.
  3. Create one bent and one overdriven controlled example. Drive these on the same or an adjacent labeled block, clearly marked as controlled failure examples, not as correct installations.

H. Station 2 — General-Purpose Wood-Screw Station

  1. Drill the specified pilot and countersink. Size both to the identified wood screw before installing it.
  2. Install the wood screw flush. Drive it so the head seats flush with the surrounding surface without stripping the head or the wood.
  3. Create one stripped-head or overdriven example on separate scrap. Label this example clearly as a controlled failure, not as the correct installation.

I. Station 3 — Structural Wood-Screw Station

  1. Install the selected structural screw according to its manufacturer's requirements. Follow the identified pilot-hole, drive-bit, and seating requirements exactly as published.
  2. Show correct head seating. Confirm the head is seated per the manufacturer's requirement, neither proud nor overdriven.

J. Station 4 — Lag-Screw, Pilot-Hole, Clearance-Hole, and Washer Station

  1. Mark the lag-screw joint. Identify the receiving member and the attached member before drilling.
  2. Drill the pilot in the receiving member. Size the pilot to the identified lag screw's thread diameter.
  3. Drill the clearance hole in the attached member. Size the clearance hole so the lag screw's shank passes through freely.
  4. Install the washer. Place the washer under the head before driving.
  5. Tighten without crushing the wood. Stop tightening once the washer and head are fully seated against the wood surface without deforming the fibers.

K. Station 5 — Carriage-Bolt or Hex-Bolt Through-Connection Station

  1. Clamp the joint. Hold both members in final position before drilling.
  2. Drill the through-hole square to the faces. Size the hole to the identified 5/16-inch or 3/8-inch bolt diameter and drill through both members in one pass.
  3. Install the carriage or hex bolt. Insert the bolt and place washers correctly at the head and at the nut.
  4. Tighten the locknut until the joint is secure. Stop tightening before the washer is embedded excessively into the wood surface.

L. Station 6 — Joist-Hanger Station

  1. Position the joist hanger at the required height. Set the hanger so the supported 2×6 member's top will align with the required elevation on the mock joint.
  2. Hold the supported member in place. Clamp or otherwise secure the 2×6 sample block in the hanger before fastening.
  3. Install only the approved connector fasteners in the required holes. Fill every pre-punched hole specified in the joist hanger's manufacturer instructions; do not substitute a different fastener and do not leave a required hole empty.

M. Station 7 — Framing-Angle, Strap, Tie, or Bracket Station

  1. Install the selected angle, strap, tie, or bracket on the controlled mock joint. Position the connector per the supplied fastener map for its identified model.
  2. Fill every required hole with the manufacturer-approved connector fastener. Confirm the fastener type, length, and quantity match the identified manufacturer instructions.

N. Station 8 — Failure Diagnosis and Nonstructural Repair Station

  1. Create one stripped nonstructural screw hole. Use a separate labeled block reserved for this station.
  2. Complete only the approved nonstructural repair method. Do not apply this repair method to any structural connector station.

O. Controlled Failure Examples and Final Labeling

  1. Add controlled examples of a split pilot hole, incorrect screw type, missing washer, proud fastener, overdriven fastener, and unfilled required connector hole. Place each on a clearly labeled block or station area separate from the correct installations.
  2. Label every correct and failed condition. Confirm no label is missing or ambiguous before inspection.
  3. Compare the installed panel against the fastener and connector schedule. Verify every station matches Section D above.
  4. Check edge distance, end distance, hole alignment, head seating, washer placement, nut engagement, wood damage, and connector contact. Record the condition of each at every station.
  5. Correct every unintended failure while preserving the deliberately labeled teaching examples. Do not correct or remove a controlled failure example that is properly labeled.
  6. Complete the inspection worksheet and explain why each failed sample failed. Record the explanation for every controlled failure example on the worksheet.
  7. Store the panel where it can be referenced during Project 05 and the Chapter 8 capstone. Place it in the finished-work protection and storage zone established in Project 01.

Common Installation Inspection

Inspect every station using the same checks regardless of which fastener or connector it displays.

  • Identification check: confirm the fastener or connector at each station matches the fastener and hardware schedule in Section D.
  • Hole-preparation check: confirm pilot, clearance, countersink, and through-bolt holes are sized and located correctly for their joint.
  • Installation-condition check: confirm no unintended splitting, stripping, bending, or wood crushing is present at any correct-installation station.
  • Connector-fastening check: confirm every required hole in the joist hanger and framing angle is filled with the manufacturer-approved fastener.
  • Substitution check: confirm no generic, undersized, or mismatched fastener has been installed at any connector station.
  • Labeling check: confirm every correct installation and every controlled failure example is clearly and accurately labeled.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Wood Splits at a Fastener Location

Probable cause: no pilot hole was drilled, the pilot hole was undersized for the fastener, or the fastener was driven too close to the end or edge of the block.
Confirmation: inspect the split for its origin point and measure the fastener's distance from the nearest end or edge against the fastener schedule.
Correction: discard the split block or relocate the joint, drill a correctly sized pilot hole, and reinstall the fastener at the correct edge and end distance.
Reinspection: repeat the installation-condition check on the corrected joint.

A Wood-Screw Head Strips During Driving

Probable cause: an incorrect or worn driver bit, excessive driver speed, or a pilot hole that was too small or absent.
Confirmation: inspect the screw head and driver bit for wear or mismatch, and confirm whether a correctly sized pilot hole was drilled.
Correction: replace the stripped screw, verify the driver bit matches the screw head, drill a correctly sized pilot hole, and reinstall at a controlled speed.
Reinspection: repeat the installation-condition check on the replacement screw.

A Lag Screw or Through-Bolt Crushes the Wood Fibers

Probable cause: the fastener was overtightened beyond the point of full washer seating, or the washer was undersized for the fastener.
Confirmation: inspect the wood surface beneath the washer for visible fiber crushing or a washer embedded below the original surface.
Correction: back off the fastener, confirm the washer is correctly sized, and retighten only until the washer and head or nut are fully seated without further crushing.
Reinspection: repeat the washer and locknut check on the corrected joint.

A Connector Fastener Hole Is Left Unfilled

Probable cause: the installer treated one or more pre-punched holes as optional instead of filling every hole required by the manufacturer's fastener schedule.
Confirmation: compare the installed connector against its identified manufacturer instructions and count the filled and unfilled holes.
Correction: install the correct approved fastener in every remaining required hole.
Reinspection: repeat the connector-fastening check on the corrected station.

A Prohibited Fastener Substitution Is Discovered at a Connector Station

Probable cause: a generic nail, deck screw, drywall screw, roofing nail, undersized fastener, or mismatched-metal fastener was installed instead of the manufacturer-approved connector fastener.
Confirmation: compare the installed fastener's type, diameter, length, and material against the identified manufacturer instructions for that connector.
Correction: remove the substituted fastener and install the correct manufacturer-approved fastener in its place.
Reinspection: repeat the substitution check on the corrected station.

A Through-Bolt Hole Is Drilled Out of Square to the Joint Faces

Probable cause: the drill was not held perpendicular to the joint face during drilling, or the joint shifted while the hole was being drilled.
Confirmation: inspect the bolt for uneven bearing against the clearance hole and check the hole angle against both faces with a square.
Correction: clamp the joint securely, redrill the hole using a square or guide to keep the bit perpendicular to both faces, and reinstall the bolt.
Reinspection: repeat the hole-preparation check on the corrected joint.

Field Terms

Pilot Hole
An undersized hole drilled to guide a fastener's threads and reduce the risk of splitting the wood.
Clearance Hole
A hole sized to let a fastener shank pass through freely so the fastener draws the joint tight.
Countersink
A shallow, cone-shaped recess allowing a screw head to seat flush with the surface.
Bolt Hole (Through-Hole)
A hole drilled completely through both connected members, square to both faces.
Structural Hardware
A connector, fastener, or bracket engineered for load-carrying installations and installed only with its approved fastener schedule.
Nonstructural (Convenience) Hardware
A fastener or bracket intended for light-duty, non-load-bearing use.
Edge Distance / End Distance
The minimum distance from a fastener center to the nearest edge or end of the connected member.
Fastener Schedule
The complete, connector-specific list of fastener type, size, and quantity required for a connector to perform as designed.
Controlled Failure Example
A deliberately created and clearly labeled incorrect installation used for teaching diagnosis.
Installation Inspection Worksheet
The document recording the condition of every correct and failed installation on the panel.

This project required you to identify common nails, wood screws, structural screws, lag screws, through-bolts, and connector fasteners by intended use and installation requirement (CF-019), select and prepare pilot, clearance, countersink, and bolt holes for the specified joint (CF-020), install nails, wood screws, structural screws, lag screws, and through-bolts without splitting, stripping, bending, or crushing the connected material (CF-021), install the specified joist hanger and framing angle, strap, tie, or bracket using manufacturer-approved fasteners (CF-022), distinguish structural hardware from nonstructural convenience hardware and identify prohibited substitutions (CF-023), and inspect, label, and explain correct installations and representative failure conditions (CF-024).

You now have a labeled fastener and hardware reference panel and a repeatable method for selecting, preparing, and installing every fastener and connector type used in the rest of this book. That system carries directly into the next project: combining the safe work system, layout methods, cutting methods, and fastener and hardware practices from Projects 01–04 into the first complete construction assembly.

Store the completed panel in the finished-work protection zone. You will reference this exact panel while building the Project 05 sawhorses and the Chapter 8 capstone floor platform.

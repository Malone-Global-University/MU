Job Overview

This project qualifies you to measure, mark, and cut dimensional lumber accurately and repeatably with a circular saw. The finished work product is one complete varied cut set — five individually dimensioned pieces — and one matched production set of six pieces cut from straight 2×4 stock, each inspected and recorded against a stated tolerance.

You will complete a tape-reading qualification, inspect and select stock, establish a reference face, edge, and end on each board, lay out and verify cut lines, set up and inspect a circular saw, make controlled crosscuts on supported stock, and measure and record every finished piece against its target length and square condition.

This is the third project in the book. It is performed inside the jobsite established in Project 01, using the reference-face and square-verification methods from Project 02. Every board you cut in Project 04, Project 05, and the Chapter 8 capstone depends on the measuring, marking, and cutting system taught here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CF-013 — Inspect dimensional lumber and select a stable reference face, edge, and end.
  • CF-014 — Read and mark dimensions through 1/16 inch using a consistent reference method.
  • CF-015 — Identify keeper side, waste side, kerf, long point, short point, and cut sequence.
  • CF-016 — Set up, inspect, support, and operate a circular saw for controlled crosscuts.
  • CF-017 — Produce individual and matched components within the stated dimensional and square tolerances.
  • CF-018 — Inspect finished cuts and diagnose errors caused by reading, marking, support, kerf placement, or saw control.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 02 gave you a verified set of squares and levels and a reference-face method. This project applies that method to a moving tool for the first time: a circular saw cutting real stock to real tolerances. Every fastener joint in Project 04, every sawhorse component in Project 05, and every framing member in the Chapter 8 capstone starts as a board measured, marked, and cut using the exact sequence taught here.

The matched-set discipline in this chapter — comparing pieces directly against each other, not only against the target dimension — is the same discipline used to compare the paired sawhorses in Project 05 and the interior joists in the Chapter 8 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reading a tape measure accurately through 1/16 inch
  • inspecting dimensional lumber for defects that disqualify a board or a section of a board
  • selecting and marking a reference face, reference edge, and reference end on inspected stock
  • identifying keeper side, waste side, kerf, long point, and short point before cutting
  • inspecting, setting up, and operating a circular saw for supported crosscuts
  • producing five individually dimensioned pieces and one six-piece matched production set within stated tolerances
  • diagnosing and correcting dimensional and square errors

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform rip cuts, bevel cuts, compound cuts, or pocket cuts, which are outside this chapter's scope
  • cut engineered lumber, treated lumber requiring special blade or dust-control precautions, or any material other than straight 2×4 dimensional lumber
  • operate a circular saw without completing the tape-reading qualification and the tool-inspection procedure in this chapter
  • use a cut component from this project in a load-bearing assembly; the pieces produced here are measurement-and-cutting practice stock only, unless specifically reused as Project 04 practice stock per that chapter's material list

Any saw, blade, or support that fails its inspection in this chapter must be corrected, repaired, or replaced before cutting continues.

Trade Note

Every measurement in this chapter is taken from a single reference end, edge, or tick — never from the previous cut. Measuring from a prior cut, rather than from the fixed reference, is the single most common source of the cumulative error diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not connect power to the circular saw or place any board on the cutting supports until the tape-reading qualification (Step 1) and the saw inspection (Step 12) are both complete. A cut made before either is complete must be discarded.

Shop Tip

Mark the waste side of every layout line with a clear X before you pick up the saw. Deciding which side of the line is waste after the saw is already running is how keeper pieces end up cut short.

Establish these references before any layout begins, and do not change them mid-board.

Keeper Side, Waste Side, and Kerf

Every layout line marks the finished edge of the keeper piece. Mark the waste side with a clear X immediately after drawing the line, and confirm the blade teeth will ride entirely on the waste side before starting the saw. The kerf — the material removed by the blade — comes out of the waste side only; a cut line placed correctly but cut on the wrong side of the blade will leave the keeper piece one kerf width short.

Measurement Cue — Read From the Reference End Every Time

Hook the tape on the reference end for every measurement on that board. Do not measure the next target length from the end of the previously cut piece unless the cut plan specifically calls for it. Measuring from a prior cut allows small errors to compound across the matched sets.

Critical Safety Cue — Blade Contact and Kickback

Disconnect power or remove the battery before adjusting blade depth, changing the blade, or clearing debris. Keep both hands on the saw during the cut, keep the cord or battery clear of the blade path, and never reach under the stock while the blade is exposed. Support the offcut so the kerf cannot close and pinch the blade, which is a leading cause of kickback.

Field Safety Cue — Supported Stock Only

Never cut a board that is not fully supported on both sides of the cut line, and never cut a board held only in your hands or across your knee. Use the stable cutting supports established in Project 01's cutting zone for every cut in this chapter.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Blade Depth and Guard

Set blade depth according to the saw manufacturer's instructions so only the portion of the blade required to cut through the stock projects below the base plate. Confirm the lower guard returns fully and freely before every cut; do not wedge, tape, or otherwise defeat the guard.

E. Preparation — Tape Reading, Stock Inspection, and Cut Planning

  1. Complete the supplied tape-reading qualification before handling the saw. Correctly read and call out whole-inch, half-inch, quarter-inch, eighth-inch, and sixteenth-inch marks on the tape before proceeding to Step 2.
  2. Inspect every board for defects. Check for embedded metal, splits, severe bow, crook, cup, twist, loose knots, rot, and contamination along the full length of each board.
  3. Reject stock that cannot produce the required components safely and accurately. Set rejected boards aside and select replacement stock from the material allowance.
  4. Sight each usable board and select one reference face, one reference edge, and one reference end. Sight down the length of the board to confirm the selected face and edge are the straightest available.
  5. Mark the reference surfaces. Place a small "R" tick on the reference face, edge, and end so they remain identifiable after the first cut.
  6. Review keeper side, waste side, kerf, long point, short point, and cumulative-error control. Confirm you can identify each on the board before marking any cut line.
  7. Prepare a cut plan for each board. Sequence the cuts so that, except where the cut plan specifically requires it, no piece is measured from a previously cut end.

F. Layout and Verification Procedure (Repeat for Pieces A–E)

  1. Hook the tape on the reference end and mark the target length with a fine tick. Read the tape to the nearest 1/16 in. and mark the tick precisely at that reading.
  2. Seat the speed square against the reference edge and draw a thin 90-degree line through the tick. Hold the square firmly against the reference edge while drawing.
  3. Mark the waste side with a clear X and identify the intended saw-blade side of the line. Confirm the X falls on the offcut side, not the keeper side.
  4. Verify the layout line with the combination square before cutting. Confirm the line reads square to the reference edge along its full length.

G. Circular Saw Setup and Crosscut Procedure (Repeat for Each Piece)

  1. Disconnect power or remove the battery and inspect the saw. Check the blade, arbor, lower guard, base plate, cord or battery, and trigger for damage or malfunction before every use.
  2. Set the blade depth according to the saw manufacturer's instructions. Only the portion of the blade required to cut through the 2×4 should project below the base plate.
  3. Support the keeper piece securely and arrange the offcut so the kerf can open without pinching the blade. Position the cutting supports on both sides of the layout line.
  4. Clamp the stock where clamping does not interfere with the saw base or blade path. Confirm the clamp will not contact the saw during the cut.
  5. Position the saw base fully on the supported side and align the blade teeth on the waste side of the line. Confirm alignment before starting the saw.
  6. Start the saw clear of the material and allow it to reach full speed. Do not contact the stock until the blade is at full speed.
  7. Advance steadily without twisting or forcing the saw and keep the base plate flat. Maintain a steady feed rate through the full length of the cut.
  8. Release the trigger after the blade clears the stock and allow the blade to stop before setting the saw down. Do not set the saw down while the blade is still spinning.
  9. Measure the finished piece and check the cut face with the combination square. Record the actual length and square condition immediately.
  10. Record target length, actual length, dimensional difference, and square condition on the cut inspection worksheet.

H. Matched Production Sets (Pieces F and G)

  1. Prepare the four matched F pieces using one consistent reference and repeatable marking method. Use the same reference end, edge, and marking sequence from Section F and G above for all four pieces.
  2. Prepare the two matched G pieces using the same method. Compare the two G pieces directly against each other in addition to checking each against the 24-in. target.
  3. Identify the longest and shortest piece in each matched set. Record both and calculate the difference between them.

I. Recut and Closeout Procedure

  1. Recut every failed component. Classify each failure as reading, marking, reference loss, kerf placement, support, blade condition, or saw-control error before recutting.
  2. Label and retain passing pieces. Store them for use as measurement references or as Project 04 practice stock per that chapter's material list.

Common Cutting Inspection

Inspect every finished piece using the same checks regardless of which piece or matched set it belongs to.

  • Dimensional check: confirm each piece is within ±1/16 in. of its target length, measured from the same reference end used to lay it out.
  • Square check: confirm each cut face is within 1/16 in. of square across the 3 1/2-in. face using the combination square.
  • Matched-set check: confirm no two F pieces differ by more than 1/16 in. and the two G pieces differ by no more than 1/16 in.
  • Cut-face condition check: confirm no uncontrolled tear-out, blade burn, split ends, or unsafe partial cut is present on any piece.
  • Procedure check: confirm the saw inspection, support setup, cutting, and post-cut measurement were completed independently for every piece.

Complete the Cut Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 04.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Repeated Pieces Grow or Shrink Across a Matched Set

Probable cause: each piece was measured from the end of the previously cut piece instead of from the reference end, allowing small errors to compound.
Confirmation: re-measure each piece in the set from the original reference end and compare to the recorded actual lengths.
Correction: recut the affected pieces, measuring every length from the reference end rather than from a prior cut.
Reinspection: repeat the dimensional check and the matched-set check for the full set.

A Cut Is Accurate on One Face but Not Square

Probable cause: the saw base plate did not stay flat on the supported side during the cut, or the speed square shifted while the line was being drawn.
Confirmation: re-verify the speed square by the reversal method from Project 02 and check the cut face with the combination square in multiple positions across its width.
Correction: if the square fails reversal, remove it from service and redraw the line with a verified square; if the square passes, recut the piece while keeping the saw base plate flat and fully supported throughout the cut.
Reinspection: repeat the square check on the recut piece.

The Blade Was Placed on the Keeper Side of the Line

Probable cause: the waste side was not marked with an X before cutting, or the saw was aligned to the wrong side of the line during setup.
Confirmation: measure the finished piece; a keeper-side cut typically produces a piece shorter than the target by approximately one kerf width.
Correction: discard the undersized piece, mark the waste side with an X on every future layout line before cutting, and recut from new stock.
Reinspection: repeat the dimensional check on the recut piece.

Stock Moved During the Cut

Probable cause: inadequate clamping, or a cutting support that did not fully contact the board along its length.
Confirmation: inspect the cut face for a wandering or angled cut line and check the cutting supports for full, stable contact with the board.
Correction: reclamp the stock so the clamp does not interfere with the saw base, confirm both supports contact the board fully, and recut.
Reinspection: repeat the square check and the cut-face condition check on the recut piece.

Kerf Closure and Blade Pinching

Probable cause: the offcut was not supported so the kerf could open freely, allowing the material to settle and pinch the blade.
Confirmation: inspect the cut edge for burn marks or a stalled-cut appearance and confirm how the offcut was supported during the cut.
Correction: reposition the cutting supports so the offcut is supported independently of the keeper piece and the kerf can open without closing on the blade, then recut.
Reinspection: repeat the cut-face condition check on the recut piece.

Excessive Tear-Out on the Cut Face

Probable cause: a dull or incorrect blade, excessive feed rate, or an unsupported area directly beneath the cut line.
Confirmation: inspect the blade for dullness or damage and confirm the correct wood-cutting blade is installed per the manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: replace a dull or incorrect blade, add support directly beneath the cut line, and recut using a steady, unforced feed rate.
Reinspection: repeat the cut-face condition check on the recut piece.

Reference Face or Reference End Was Lost

Probable cause: the "R" tick marks were not placed at Step 5, or the board was flipped or rotated during handling without checking the marks.
Confirmation: inspect the board for "R" tick marks; if absent or ambiguous, the reference cannot be confirmed.
Correction: re-sight the board, reselect the reference face, edge, and end, mark them clearly, and repeat any layout completed without a confirmed reference.
Reinspection: repeat the dimensional and square checks on any piece cut without a confirmed reference.

Field Terms

The one board face all layout marks and measurements are taken from for that board.
Reference Edge
The one straight edge the speed square and combination square are seated against.
Reference End
The one board end every length measurement is hooked from, unless the cut plan specifically requires otherwise.
Keeper Side

Waste Side

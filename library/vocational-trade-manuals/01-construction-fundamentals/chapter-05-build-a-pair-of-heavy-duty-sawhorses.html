Job Overview

This project qualifies you to combine safe work practices, layout, cutting, and fastening into one complete construction assembly. The finished work product is one matching pair of fixed, trestle-style heavy-duty sawhorses, each 36 inches long, 24 inches wide, and 30 inches tall, built from square-cut 2×6 and 2×4 stock, plywood gussets, carriage bolts, and structural screws.

You will cut and label every component for both sawhorses, assemble two matching end frames, join them with side stretchers using bolted and screwed connections, install a centered top rail, fit diagonal anti-racking braces, and verify both sawhorses match each other in height, width, length, and stability.

This is the fifth project in the book and the first integrated assembly. It is performed inside the jobsite established in Project 01, using the reference-face and verification methods from Project 02, the measuring, marking, and cutting system from Project 03, and the fastener and connector installation practices from Project 04. The completed sawhorses become working shop equipment used to support material in Projects 06 through 08.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • CF-025 — Prepare and label every sawhorse component from the controlled cut and hardware schedules.
  • CF-026 — Assemble matching end frames square, aligned, and to the specified dimensions.
  • CF-027 — Install stretchers, bolts, structural screws, gussets, and anti-racking braces in the required sequence.
  • CF-028 — Maintain finished height, width, length, level, foot contact, and pair consistency within the stated tolerances.
  • CF-029 — Diagnose and correct rocking, twist, racking, joint gaps, fastener errors, and pair-height mismatch.
  • CF-030 — Complete the approved stability inspection and document the permitted and prohibited uses.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 03 gave you a repeatable method for measuring, marking, and cutting square components. Project 04 qualified you to select, prepare, and install the fasteners and structural hardware used in this assembly: structural screws, carriage bolts with washers and locknuts, and plywood gusset connections. This project applies both skills for the first time to a complete, matched, load-bearing piece of shop equipment.

The finished sawhorses are used as work supports in every remaining project in this book, including holding stock during the Project 06 footprint layout preparation and supporting material during the Chapter 8 capstone construction.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • preparing and labeling every sawhorse component from the controlled cut and hardware schedules
  • assembling two matching end frames square, aligned, and to the specified dimensions
  • installing stretchers, carriage bolts, structural screws, plywood gussets, and diagonal anti-racking braces in the required sequence
  • maintaining finished height, width, length, level, foot contact, and pair-to-pair consistency within stated tolerances
  • diagnosing and correcting rocking, twist, racking, joint gaps, fastener errors, and pair-height mismatch
  • completing the stability inspection and documenting permitted and prohibited uses

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • use the finished sawhorses as scaffolds, steps, ladder supports, vehicle stands, lifting stands, seating, or equipment for supporting people
  • publish or assume a person-support rating, load rating, or structural capacity beyond ordinary lumber, sheet-goods, door, and construction-assembly support
  • substitute a different fastener, bolt size, gusset material, or brace arrangement than the one specified in the controlling component and joint schedules
  • build the pair from mismatched dimensions, mixed component sizes, or an unverified design

Any component or joint that fails inspection must be corrected before the sawhorse pair is placed into service.

Trade Note

This design uses square cuts throughout so you can concentrate on accurate production cutting, matched assembly, structural hardware, level, square, and stability rather than angled joinery. Both sawhorses in the pair must be built from the identical controlled design in this chapter; do not modify dimensions, joints, or fasteners on one sawhorse without applying the same change to the other.

Common Field Fundamentals

A sawhorse pair only performs correctly when both units match. Every measurement in this project is taken from a controlled reference face or edge, every joint uses the exact fastener specified in the joint schedule, and every completed frame is checked against its mate before the pair is placed into service. Losing a reference face, substituting a fastener, or skipping a diagonal check at any point in the sequence produces a rocking, racked, or mismatched sawhorse that must be disassembled and corrected.

Finished Dimensions for Each Sawhorse

  • Overall length: 36 in.
  • Overall width: 24 in.
  • Target overall height: 30 in.
  • Top work surface: 5 1/2 in. wide
  • Lower stretcher top edge: 10 in. above the reference floor
  • Top centered across both end frames
  • Pair height difference: not more than 1/8 in.
  • No rocking on a verified flat reference surface

Measurement Cue — One Reference Per Frame

Mark one reference face and edge on every crossbar, leg, and stretcher before cutting. Measure the leg-inset marks, the stretcher top-edge line, and every bolt and screw location from those same reference surfaces. Switching references partway through a frame is the most common cause of an out-of-square or mismatched sawhorse.

Critical Safety Cue — Circular Saw and Drill Control

Disconnect power or remove the battery before changing blades or bits. Support and clamp every board before cutting or drilling; never hold stock only in your hand. Keep hands clear of the blade path and the bit's rotation, and allow the saw blade to fully stop before setting the tool down.

Field Safety Cue — Two-Frame Work Area

Build the two end frames for one sawhorse on the flat assembly surface established in Project 01, with enough clear space to stand both completed frames upright side by side without crossing walking routes or blocking exits.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Fastener Match

Install only the fastener types and sizes listed in the component and joint schedules: 3-inch exterior structural screws at leg-to-crossbar and top-rail joints, 5/16-inch carriage bolts with washers and locknuts at stretcher-to-leg joints, and 1 5/8-inch exterior wood screws at gusset and brace connections. Do not substitute a longer, shorter, or different-head fastener because it is available.

F. Preparation — Stock Selection, Cutting, and Layout

  1. Review the complete drawing, component schedule, joint schedule, and inspection standard before cutting. Confirm every dimension in Sections B and D before touching the saw.
  2. Select straight stock and reject severe bow, twist, splits, loose knots, decay, or damaged ends. Inspect every board per the Project 03 method before marking a reference face and edge.
  3. Mark one reference face and edge on every board. Keep this mark visible through every subsequent layout and cutting operation.
  4. Cut two 36-inch 2×6 top rails. Use the Project 03 measuring, marking, and cutting sequence.
  5. Cut four 24-inch end crossbars. Verify each cut square within the Project 03 tolerance before proceeding.
  6. Cut eight 28 1/2-inch legs. Compare all eight directly for matched length.
  7. Cut four 30-inch side stretchers. Compare all four directly for matched length.
  8. Cut four 36-inch 1×4 diagonal-brace blanks. Do not trim these to final length yet; they are cut to fit during Assembly Step 19.
  9. Cut eight matching 8-by-10-inch right-triangular plywood gussets from the supplied panel layout. Ease all cut edges before labeling.
  10. Compare all matched components and recut any piece outside the Project 03 tolerances. Set aside every piece that fails before continuing.
  11. Label all first-sawhorse components A and all second-sawhorse components B. Keep the two labeled sets physically separated on the material zone shelving.
  12. Mark the leg locations on every crossbar so each leg's outside face is 1 1/2 inches in from the crossbar end. Measure from the reference end on each crossbar.
  13. Mark the stretcher top-edge line 10 inches above the bottom of every leg. Measure from the same reference end used on each leg.
  14. Mark controlled fastener and bolt locations using the supplied drilling template. Apply the template identically to the A-set and B-set components.

G. Assembly — First End Frame (Repeat for Second End Frame)

  1. Place the first 24-inch crossbar on edge on the flat assembly surface. Orient its reference face outward per the drilling template.
  2. Position two legs against the controlled face of the crossbar with their upper ends flush with the crossbar top and their outside faces on the 1 1/2-inch inset marks. Confirm both legs sit on the marked inset before clamping.
  3. Clamp the first end frame and verify both legs are parallel and perpendicular to the crossbar. Use the framing square and combination square per the Project 02 verification method.
  4. Drill the required pilots and install the two structural screws at each leg-to-crossbar joint. Drive both screws per Joint Schedule Section D without splitting the leg or crossbar.
  5. Position one plywood gusset over each joint using the supplied edge offsets. Align the gusset before marking its screw pattern.
  6. Install the gusset screws in the controlled pattern without overdriving. Confirm a minimum of six screws per gusset, fully seated and flush.
  7. Repeat Steps 1–6 to build the second matching end frame. Compare the two completed frames directly for matched leg spacing and square before continuing.

H. Assembly — Stretchers, Top Rail, and Level Check

  1. Stand the two end frames upright with their controlled faces oriented correctly and their inside leg faces 30 inches apart. Measure the 30-inch inside spacing at both the top and bottom of the legs before clamping.
  2. Position the first 30-inch side stretcher between the legs with its top edge on the 10-inch line. Confirm the top-edge line matches on both end frames.
  3. Clamp the stretcher, verify it is level, and drill the controlled through-holes square to the joint. Use the level per the Project 02 verification method before drilling.
  4. Install one carriage bolt, washer, and locknut at each end and tighten without crushing the wood. Stop tightening once the washer is fully seated against the wood surface.
  5. Install the specified anti-rotation screw at each stretcher connection. Drive one structural screw at each stretcher-to-leg joint per Joint Schedule Section D.
  6. Repeat Steps 2–5 for the opposite side stretcher. Confirm both stretchers are level with each other before continuing.
  7. Measure the partial assembly diagonally and adjust until square within 1/8 inch. Compare both diagonals across the open frame before proceeding.
  8. Center the 36-inch top rail over both crossbars with its ends flush to the overall 36-inch length. Confirm equal overhang on both sides before clamping.
  9. Clamp the top, verify equal side overhang, and install three structural screws into each crossbar. Drive all six screws per Joint Schedule Section D.
  10. Place the assembly on the verified reference floor and check all four feet for contact. Identify and correct any foot that does not contact the floor before continuing.

I. Assembly — Diagonal Braces and Final Squaring

  1. Hold the first diagonal brace from the upper end-frame region to the opposite lower stretcher region as shown in the side elevation. Position the 1×4 blank across the open side of the assembly.
  2. Mark the brace in place from the outside face of the first end-frame leg near the crossbar to the outside face of the opposite leg immediately above the lower stretcher, cut it to fit, reinstall it, and fasten it using the supplied pattern. Install a minimum of three 1 5/8-inch screws at each end per Joint Schedule Section D.
  3. Install the second brace on the opposite long side in the opposing direction so the two braces resist racking in both directions. Confirm the second brace crosses in the opposite diagonal direction from the first.
  4. Recheck square, top level, end-frame plumb, overall dimensions, joint seating, and rocking. Correct any deviation before moving to the second sawhorse.
  5. Build the second sawhorse using the same sequence and setup controls (Sections F–I) with the B-labeled components. Do not begin the second sawhorse until the first passes every check in Step 4.
  6. Place both sawhorses side by side and compare top height, width, length, and foot contact. Record any measured difference on the stability inspection worksheet.
  7. Lay a verified straight board across both tops and check for rocking or loss of contact. Correct any gap before final inspection.
  8. Correct every mismatch before completing the final inspection. Repeat the affected check after each correction.

Common Installation Inspection

Inspect every joint and both completed sawhorses using the same checks regardless of build order.

  • Dimension check: confirm overall length, width, and height are within ±1/8 in. of the specified 36 × 24 × 30 in. on both sawhorses.
  • Square and level check: confirm both end frames are square, legs are parallel, and the top is centered and level across its width.
  • Foot-contact check: confirm all four feet contact a verified flat floor with no rocking.
  • Fastener check: confirm no unintended split lumber, crushed fibers, stripped screws, or loose bolts, and that washers and locknuts are fully seated and engaged.
  • Bracing check: confirm braces and gussets are fully seated with the correct screw pattern and that no racking is visible under a firm hand-applied material-support stability check.
  • Pair-consistency check: confirm a straight reference board contacts both sawhorse tops without rocking.

Complete the Stability Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before placing the pair into service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Assembly Rocks on a Verified Flat Floor

Probable cause: unequal leg length, twist in one leg, or a leg-to-crossbar joint that shifted during fastening.
Confirmation: measure matching legs against each other, inspect joint seating at each leg-to-crossbar connection, and check the assembly on the reference floor with all four feet marked.
Correction: loosen and realign the shifted joint, or replace the leg that is out of tolerance.
Reinspection: repeat the dimension, square, and foot-contact checks.

An End Frame Is Out of Square

Probable cause: the crossbar or a leg was not clamped against its reference face during fastening, or the inset marks were measured from inconsistent reference ends.
Confirmation: re-verify the frame with the framing square and combination square per the Project 02 method, and check both inset marks against the reference end.
Correction: remove the structural screws at the affected joint, realign the leg against the 1 1/2-inch inset mark, reclamp, and reinstall the fasteners.
Reinspection: repeat the end-frame square check on the corrected joint.

The Assembly Racks Under a Hand-Applied Stability Check

Probable cause: a diagonal brace is missing, undersized for its screw pattern, installed in the wrong orientation, or the brace screws are not fully seated.
Confirmation: inspect both braces for correct opposing orientation and count the screws at each brace end against the minimum of three per end.
Correction: install the missing screws, or remove and correctly reinstall a brace that is in the wrong orientation.
Reinspection: repeat the bracing check with a firm hand-applied material-support stability test.

A Carriage Bolt Crushes the Wood at the Stretcher Connection

Probable cause: the locknut was overtightened beyond the point of full washer seating.
Confirmation: inspect the wood surface beneath the washer for visible fiber crushing or a washer embedded below the original surface.
Correction: back off the locknut, confirm the washer is correctly seated, and retighten only until the washer is fully seated against the wood without further crushing.
Reinspection: repeat the fastener check on the corrected joint.

The Two Sawhorses Do Not Match in Height

Probable cause: the top rail was not seated flush against both crossbars on one sawhorse, or a leg length differs between the A-set and B-set.
Confirmation: measure both sawhorses side by side at each corner and compare individual leg lengths between the two sets.
Correction: loosen and reseat the top rail flush against the crossbars, or replace the out-of-tolerance leg and rebuild the affected end frame.
Reinspection: repeat the pair-height check and the straight-reference-board check across both tops.

A Stretcher Bolt Loosens Under Use

Probable cause: the anti-rotation screw was omitted, or the locknut was not fully engaged during installation.
Confirmation: inspect the stretcher for rotation around the bolt and confirm the locknut is fully threaded onto the bolt.
Correction: install the missing anti-rotation screw, or remove, re-seat, and retighten the locknut to full engagement.
Reinspection: repeat the fastener check on the corrected joint.

Field Terms

Trestle-Style Sawhorse
A fixed sawhorse design using splayed or square-cut leg-and-crossbar end frames joined by stretchers and a top rail.
End Frame
The assembly of one crossbar, two legs, and their gussets that forms each end of the sawhorse.
Side Stretcher
The horizontal member connecting the legs of the two end frames along each long side of the sawhorse.
Anti-Rotation Screw
The structural screw installed at a bolted stretcher joint to prevent the stretcher from rotating around the bolt.
Anti-Racking Brace
A diagonal member installed to resist the sawhorse leaning or twisting out of square under lateral load.
Gusset
A triangular plywood plate installed over a joint to add rigidity and resist joint movement.
Inside Leg Face
The face of a leg that faces the opposite leg across the sawhorse width, used as the reference for the 30-inch stretcher spacing.
Pair Consistency
The requirement that two matched sawhorses agree in height, width, length, and foot contact within the stated tolerance.
Controlled Face
The identified face of a component against which other parts are positioned during assembly.
Stability Inspection Worksheet
The document recording dimensions, square, level, fastener condition, and stability results for the finished pair.

Performance Check

With both sawhorses assembled, braced, and recorded, review the finished pair against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm overall length, width, and height are within ±1/8 inch on both units, the pair height difference is not more than 1/8 inch, both end frames are square with parallel legs, both tops are centered and level, all four feet on each sawhorse contact a verified flat floor, no correct-installation joint shows unintended splitting, crushed fibers, stripped screws, or loose bolts, washers are seated and locknuts fully engaged, gussets and braces are fully seated with the correct screw pattern, no racking appears under a firm hand-applied material-support stability check, and a straight reference board contacts both sawhorse tops without rocking. If a check fails, identify whether the error occurred in stock selection, layout, end-frame assembly, stretcher installation, top-rail installation, bracing, or fastener installation before correcting and re-running the affected check.

This project required you to prepare and label every sawhorse component from the controlled cut and hardware schedules (CF-025), assemble matching end frames square, aligned, and to the specified dimensions (CF-026), install stretchers, bolts, structural screws, gussets, and anti-racking braces in the required sequence (CF-027), maintain finished height, width, length, level, foot contact, and pair consistency within the stated tolerances (CF-028), diagnose and correct rocking, twist, racking, joint gaps, fastener errors, and pair-height mismatch (CF-029), and complete the approved stability inspection and document the permitted and prohibited uses (CF-030).

You now have one matching pair of heavy-duty sawhorses and a repeatable method for combining safe work practices, layout, cutting, and structural fastening into a complete construction assembly. That system carries directly into the next project: laying out the exact 8-foot by 10-foot capstone footprint and skid-support lines, where these sawhorses will serve as working shop equipment.

Place the completed sawhorse pair into service in the finished-work protection and storage zone. You will use this exact pair to support material during Project 06, the Project 07 cut planning, and the Chapter 8 capstone construction.

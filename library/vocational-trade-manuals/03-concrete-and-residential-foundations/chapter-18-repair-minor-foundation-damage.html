Repair Cracks, Spalls, and Minor Foundation Damage

Evaluate and repair approved nonstructural cracks, shallow spalls, edge damage, tie holes, and localized surface defects on completed foundation concrete, using a repair system compatible with the substrate, exposure, and finish that the defect requires — without treating any sign of structural movement as a cosmetic repair.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to evaluate and repair approved nonstructural cracks, shallow spalls, edge damage, tie holes, and localized surface defects on completed foundation concrete. The finished work product is a documented, mapped, and repaired set of minor defects: a completed Foundation Defect Map and Repair Record, cured and inspected repairs, and photographs of every repair before and after work.

You will obtain the repair history and approved repair scope, inspect and map every defect, measure and classify each one, sound the surrounding concrete for delamination, select a compatible repair material, prepare the substrate, apply and finish the repair, cure and protect it, and inspect the finished bond and appearance.

This project assumes the foundation concrete has already been placed, cured, stripped, and inspected under Chapters 4 through 9, and that any waterproofing, drainage, insulation, backfill, or slab work already completed remains undisturbed unless a defect requires reopening a protected system with authorization.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FN-103 — Evaluate minor concrete damage against the approved repair scope.
  • FN-104 — Clean repair areas to the profile and condition the selected repair product requires.
  • FN-105 — Route cracks to the dimensions required by the selected repair system.
  • FN-106 — Patch spalls using approved repair geometry and preparation.
  • FN-107 — Apply repair mortar, sealant, or injection material within manufacturer limits.
  • FN-108 — Inspect repair bond, profile, and finish before closeout.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 8 addressed defects found immediately after form stripping, before curing, waterproofing, and backfill were complete. This project addresses defects found or developing later — after curing, after backfill, after slab placement, or during a later inspection cycle — using the same defect classification discipline but a wider range of approved repair materials and a stricter boundary against treating structural symptoms cosmetically.

The classification method used here — map, measure, sound, and classify before ever preparing a repair surface — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone relies on when compiling the final punch list and repair record for the complete foundation system.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining the repair history, concrete age, exposure conditions, and approved repair scope before any defect is touched
  • inspecting and mapping every crack, spall, void, tie hole, edge defect, and previous repair
  • measuring and recording crack width, length, orientation, location, pattern, moisture, displacement, and apparent activity
  • sounding surrounding concrete to identify loose or delaminated material
  • classifying each defect as within the minor-repair scope or requiring qualified structural or materials review
  • selecting and applying a compatible repair material within manufacturer limits
  • curing, protecting, and inspecting the finished repair

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform cosmetic treatment of active structural movement, settlement, displaced walls, wide or growing cracks, water-driven deterioration, corrosion-related delamination, exposed or damaged reinforcing steel, major honeycombing, fire damage, or chemical attack
  • repair a defect of unknown cause without first obtaining qualified structural or materials evaluation
  • prepare exposed reinforcing steel for repair unless specifically authorized and directed by an approved corrosion-repair detail
  • select or substitute a repair material that is not compatible with the substrate, exposure, movement, thickness, orientation, moisture condition, and required finish

Any defect that exceeds the approved minor-repair limits, or that indicates structural movement, corrosion, or continuing water entry, must be stopped and escalated to a qualified professional before repair work continues.

Trade Note

A crack that looks minor on the surface can still indicate an active structural or moisture condition below it. Classification comes before preparation in every case in this chapter — never prepare a repair surface for a defect that has not yet been measured, sounded, and classified.

Setup Standard

Do not prepare or apply any repair material to a defect that has not been measured, sounded, and classified in Steps 2 through 5. Any defect classified as requiring qualified review must be stopped and escalated in Step 6 before repair work proceeds on that defect.

Shop Tip

Photograph every defect before preparation begins, again after preparation, and again after the finished, cured repair. A defect map without before-and-after photographs cannot later demonstrate that a crack was stable and nonstructural at the time it was repaired.

Prerequisites

  • Completed foundation concrete that has passed the required curing, form-stripping, and Chapter 8 or Chapter 9 inspection, with a documented repair history if one exists
  • Written confirmation of the approved minor-repair scope, including which defect types and sizes are authorized for learner repair without additional review

  • Access to the areas requiring repair without disturbing occupied space, active utilities, or protected waterproofing and drainage systems unless authorized

Common Field Fundamentals

Every minor-repair task in this chapter follows the same sequence regardless of whether the defect is a hairline crack, a shallow spall, or an old tie hole: map and measure the defect, sound the surrounding concrete, classify it against the approved scope, prepare only what the classification authorizes, apply a compatible material within manufacturer limits, cure and protect it, and inspect the finished bond and profile before closeout.

Classification Before Preparation

No repair surface may be prepared until the defect it belongs to has been measured, sounded, and classified. A defect classified as requiring qualified review must be escalated in Step 6 before any grinding, routing, or chiseling begins on that specific defect. Working ahead of classification is the single most common cause of a cosmetic repair concealing a condition that needed structural evaluation.

Measurement Cue — Record Every Defect Before Classifying It

Measure and record crack width using the crack comparator gauge, length using the tape measure, and orientation, location, pattern, moisture condition, and apparent displacement for every defect before assigning a classification. Do not classify a defect from memory or from a prior inspection cycle without re-measuring it.

Critical Safety Cue — Signs of Structural Movement

Stop work immediately on any defect showing active displacement, a widening crack, exposed or corroded reinforcing steel, major honeycombing, or continuing water entry. Do not prepare, patch, or seal such a defect. Obtain qualified structural or materials review before any further work in that area.

Field Safety Cue — Dust and Silica Control

Wear the specified silica-rated respirator and eye protection during every grinding, routing, or chiseling operation. Use the shop vacuum or an equivalent dust-control method at the point of cutting. Treat accumulated concrete dust as a respiratory hazard regardless of the size of the repair area.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Product Compatibility and Limits

Select a repair material compatible with the substrate, exposure, expected movement, repair thickness, orientation, substrate moisture, and required finish or coating. Do not substitute a different manufacturer's primer, bonding agent, or repair material without confirming compatibility. Apply, cure, and protect every product within the limits stated in its manufacturer instructions.

E. Preparation — Document Review and Defect Mapping

  1. Obtain the repair history, concrete age, exposure, moisture conditions, structural information, and approved repair scope. Confirm the approved scope in writing before evaluating any defect.
  2. Inspect and map each crack, spall, void, tie hole, edge defect, and previous repair. Record every defect's location on the Foundation Defect Map before measuring it.
  3. Measure and record crack width, length, orientation, location, pattern, moisture, displacement, and apparent activity. Use the crack comparator gauge and tape measure for every crack, and record dimensions and condition for every spall, void, tie hole, and edge defect.

F. Sounding and Classification

  1. Sound the surrounding concrete using the approved method to identify loose or delaminated material. Tap the area around every mapped defect with the specified hammer and record any hollow or dull response.
  2. Classify defects as within the minor-repair scope or requiring qualified review. Compare each measured and sounded defect to the Defect Classification Schedule in Section B.
  3. Stop work on any defect that exceeds the approved limits or indicates structural movement, corrosion, or continuing water entry. Escalate every qualified-review defect to the named structural or materials professional before proceeding on that defect.

G. Material Selection and Area Preparation

  1. Select a repair material compatible with the substrate, exposure, movement, thickness, orientation, moisture, coating, and finish requirements. Confirm compatibility using the manufacturer instructions before purchasing or opening any product.
  2. Establish dust, silica, debris, ventilation, and adjacent-surface controls. Mask and protect adjacent finished surfaces before any grinding, routing, or chiseling begins.
  3. Remove loose, unsound, contaminated, or feather-edged concrete to the required repair geometry. Grind or chisel to a square or beveled edge as required by the selected system; do not leave a feather edge.
  4. Route or prepare approved cracks to the dimensions required by the selected system. Use the crack router or chisel to the width and depth stated in the manufacturer instructions.

H. Substrate Preparation and Application

  1. Clean the repair area using the specified mechanical, air, vacuum, or washing method. Remove all dust and loose material before checking substrate condition.
  2. Prepare exposed steel only when authorized and according to the approved corrosion-repair detail. Do not clean, treat, or coat exposed reinforcing steel without specific written authorization.
  3. Condition the substrate to the moisture state required by the repair product. Verify with the moisture meter before applying primer or bonding agent.
  4. Apply primer, bonding agent, sealant, injection material, patching mortar, or repair concrete as specified. Mix and apply within the manufacturer's stated proportions and working time.
  5. Consolidate and finish the repair without trapping voids or creating thin edges. Work the material fully into the prepared geometry and tool the surface to eliminate air pockets.
  6. Match the required surface profile for waterproofing, flooring, coating, or exposed appearance. Finish the repair to the texture and plane required by the surface's intended use.

I. Curing and Closeout Inspection

  1. Cure and protect the repair for the required period. Apply the specified curing compound or wet-cure covering and maintain it for the full manufacturer-stated duration.
  2. Inspect for bond, shrinkage, cracking, debonding, hollow areas, profile, finish, and water tightness where applicable. Sound the cured repair and compare its profile to the surrounding surface.
  3. Correct failed areas using the manufacturer-approved procedure. Remove and re-prepare any repair that fails the bond, profile, or finish check.
  4. Record defect maps, measurements, product data, batch information, preparation, application, curing, photographs, and inspection results. Complete every field on the Foundation Defect Map and Repair Record before closeout.

Common Repair Inspection

Inspect every repair using the same checks regardless of defect type.

  • Classification check: confirm every repaired defect was measured, sounded, and classified as minor repairable before preparation began.
  • Preparation check: confirm loose, unsound, or contaminated material was removed to the required geometry with no feather edges.
  • Application check: confirm primer, bonding agent, and repair material were applied within manufacturer proportions and limits.
  • Bond and profile check: sound the cured repair and confirm the finished profile matches the required surface condition.
  • Curing check: confirm the repair was cured and protected for the full manufacturer-stated period.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Crack Reopens or Continues to Grow After Repair

Probable cause: the crack was classified as stable and nonstructural without adequate confirmation of its activity, or the repair material selected does not accommodate the actual movement at the crack.
Confirmation: re-measure the crack width and length against the original record and compare current readings to the classification made in Step 5.
Correction: stop work and obtain structural or materials review; do not reapply the same repair material without a revised classification.
Reinspection: repeat the crack-measurement and classification check after the professional issues direction.

Repair Material Debonds From the Substrate

Probable cause: the substrate moisture, temperature, or cleanliness did not meet the manufacturer's stated limits at the time of application, or the bonding agent was omitted or incompatible.
Confirmation: sound the debonded area and compare the original substrate-preparation record to the manufacturer instructions for the product used.
Correction: remove the debonded repair material, re-prepare the substrate to the required condition, and reapply the specified primer or bonding agent before repatching.
Reinspection: repeat the bond check after the repair cures.

Sounding Reveals a Hollow Area Beyond the Mapped Defect

Probable cause: delamination extends beyond the originally identified defect boundary.
Confirmation: sound the full surrounding area in a grid pattern and mark the actual boundary of the hollow response.
Correction: stop work on the enlarged area and obtain qualified structural or materials review; do not extend the minor repair to cover undocumented delamination.
Reinspection: repeat the sounding check after the professional issues direction, using the revised boundary.

Repair Surface Does Not Match the Required Profile

Probable cause: the repair was finished before confirming the profile required for the adjoining waterproofing, flooring, coating, or exposed-surface system.
Confirmation: compare the finished repair profile to the specification for the adjoining system.
Correction: re-tool or re-finish the repair surface to the required profile while the material remains workable, or remove and reapply if it has already cured.
Reinspection: repeat the profile check against the adjoining system's requirement.

A Defect Was Prepared Before Classification Was Complete

Probable cause: preparation began before measurement, sounding, and classification steps were finished for that defect.
Confirmation: review the Foundation Defect Map and Repair Record for the affected defect and confirm whether classification was completed before preparation began.
Correction: stop further preparation, complete the measurement, sounding, and classification steps retroactively, and obtain qualified review if the completed classification places the defect outside the minor-repair scope.
Reinspection: repeat the classification check before resuming any repair work on that defect.

Cleanup

This project ends with cured, inspected repairs and a completed record, not simply an empty work area.

  • Remove all ground, chiseled, or routed debris and dispose of it according to the project's waste-handling requirements
  • Clean mixing tools and equipment before repair material cures on them
  • Store unused repair material according to the manufacturer's shelf-life and storage instructions
  • Confirm every curing compound or wet-cure covering remains in place for its full required duration before removal
  • Return the Foundation Defect Map and Repair Record and all before-and-after photographs to the project record

Field Terms

Minor Repairable Defect
A crack, spall, void, tie hole, or edge defect within the approved repair scope that shows no sign of structural movement, corrosion, or continuing water entry.
Qualified-Review Defect
Any defect exceeding the approved minor-repair limits or showing signs requiring structural or materials evaluation.
Apparent Activity
Evidence that a crack is currently moving, widening, or displacing.
Sounding
Tapping concrete with a specified hammer to identify loose or delaminated material by its hollow or dull response.
Repair Geometry
The routed or ground profile, width, and depth of a prepared crack or spall required by the selected repair system.
Feather Edge
A repair edge tapered to an unreliable bonding thickness; prohibited at every repair perimeter in this chapter.
Bonding Agent
A manufacturer-specified product applied to the prepared substrate to promote adhesion of the repair material.
Delamination
Separation of near-surface concrete from the sound substrate beneath it, identified by sounding.
Foundation Defect Map and Repair Record
The document recording every mapped defect, its measurements, classification, repair materials, and inspection results.
Closeout
The completed, signed record confirming every repair has cured, been inspected, and passed the acceptance standards.

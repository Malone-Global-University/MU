Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete the slab placement begun in Project 16 by bringing the wet, screeded surface through bull-floating, edging, jointing, hand and machine finishing, texturing, curing, and protection. Project 16 and this project are separate instructional and assessment sections of one continuous slab operation. The concrete placed and screeded in Project 16 does not stop for a chapter break; the finishing crew must be staged and ready before discharge begins so the slab moves directly from screeding into the operations described here.

The finished work product is a floated, edged, jointed, textured, cured, and protected residential concrete slab meeting the surface-finish specification called for by the approved plans, together with a completed Slab-Finish Inspection Record documenting the timing, methods, joint installation, curing start, and any repairs made before the crew left the site.

You will evaluate the concrete's readiness for each finishing operation by its actual condition rather than by elapsed time, bull-float or darby the surface, edge the perimeter and formed openings, tool or plan for sawed control joints at the correct locations, sequence hand and machine finishing passes to the required texture, protect embedded items and drains during finishing, begin curing at the correct surface condition, and inspect the finished slab for tearing, scaling, dusting, and joint defects before turning the work over for continued curing.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FN-097 — Bull-float or darby the screeded slab surface using the approved direction and overlap, correcting ridges and low areas while the surface remains workable.
  • FN-098 — Edge the slab perimeter and formed openings to the specified radius and depth.
  • FN-099 — Cut or tool control, construction, and isolation joints at the planned locations, timing, depth, and spacing.
  • FN-100 — Finish the surface texture using hand floats, power floats, trowels, or broom/texture tools as required by the finish specification.
  • FN-101 — Protect the slab surface by beginning the specified curing method at the correct concrete condition and barricading against traffic, weather, and early loading.
  • FN-102 — Inspect the finished slab for tearing, scaling, dusting, ridges, birdbaths, joint defects, and finish inconsistency, and document the result.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 16 placed, consolidated, and screeded the slab and brought it to the correct elevation and initial surface condition. This project takes over at the exact point Project 16 ends: the same wet concrete, the same crew, the same continuous operation. Nothing in this project may wait for a later work session. The floating, edging, jointing, and finishing sequence taught here depends entirely on reading the concrete's actual bleed-water and stiffening condition, a skill you will use again any time this book calls for timed concrete work.

The curing and protection steps performed at the end of this project connect directly to the curing discipline taught in Project 09 and carried forward through the capstone in Project 20, where the finished slab is inspected one final time before the foundation is handed off to framing.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • evaluating concrete condition before each finishing operation rather than working solely by elapsed time
  • bull-floating or darbying the screeded surface and correcting ridges, low areas, and screed marks while the surface remains workable
  • allowing bleed water and sheen to dissipate to the required condition before later finishing
  • edging the slab perimeter and formed openings to the specified radius and depth
  • tooling joints where the design uses tooled joints, and saw-cutting control joints at the specified timing, depth, spacing, and sequence where the design uses sawed joints
  • sequencing hand float, power float, trowel, fresno, and texture passes according to concrete condition and the specified finish
  • protecting drains, depressions, edges, penetrations, and embedded items during finishing
  • beginning the specified curing method and barricading the slab
  • inspecting the finished slab and correcting only conditions that can be repaired without causing greater damage

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin finishing operations on concrete that has not reached the required condition for that operation, regardless of schedule pressure
  • select a joint location, spacing, or depth different from the approved joint plan
  • substitute a curing method different from the one specified without authorization
  • attempt a structural-level repair of tearing, scaling, or cracking that exceeds a documentable minor correction; such conditions are referred to Project 18 or to qualified review
  • operate a power float/trowel machine or a concrete saw without the tool-specific instruction required in Section H

Any surface condition that cannot be corrected without causing greater damage must be documented and referred for evaluation rather than concealed by additional working of the surface.

Trade Note

Every finishing operation in this project is gated by the concrete's condition, not by the clock. Working a bull float pass, an edging pass, or a trowel pass at the wrong condition is the single most common cause of the surface defects diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section — closing the surface too early traps bleed water beneath it; waiting too long tears the surface or leaves it too hard to finish.

Shop Tip

Test the concrete's readiness for each finishing pass with a firm thumbprint or foot-pressure check on an inconspicuous area rather than by watching the clock. A surface that still yields water to the touch is not ready for edging, jointing, or troweling regardless of how much time has elapsed since placement.

Reading Concrete Condition

Evaluate the concrete's condition before every operation in this project rather than relying on elapsed time since placement. Ambient temperature, humidity, wind, sun exposure, mixture, and slab thickness all change how quickly concrete becomes ready for each finishing step. A thumbprint or firm foot-pressure test on an inconspicuous area of the slab is the required method for judging readiness for edging, jointing, and troweling.

Measurement Cue — Evaluate Condition Before Every Pass

Before bull-floating, edging, jointing, or each floating and troweling pass, check the concrete's actual condition using a thumbprint or firm foot-pressure test on an inconspicuous area. Do not begin an operation solely because a set amount of time has elapsed since placement or since the previous operation.

Critical Safety Cue — Power Float and Trowel Machines

Do not operate a power float/trowel machine without the manufacturer's guarding in place, without confirming blade or pan condition, and without keeping hands and loose clothing clear of the rotating head. Shut down and lock out the machine before clearing a jam, adjusting blade pitch, or performing any maintenance.

Field Safety Cue — Working on Fresh Concrete

Use knee boards when working from the slab surface and confirm each board is stable before shifting weight onto it. Keep the finishing crew's tool cords, hoses, and saw power supply clear of the wet surface and clear of walking paths used during finishing.

Code Alert — Joint Location and Timing

Required control-joint spacing, depth, and saw-cutting timing vary by slab thickness, aggregate, mixture, and jurisdiction, and are set by the approved joint plan rather than by general practice. Never estimate joint spacing or delay a saw-cut window based on convenience; verify the specified timing and sequence before placement begins and follow it exactly.

E. Preparation — Confirming Specifications and Readiness

  1. Confirm the specified surface finish, edge profile, joint locations, joint method, flatness or slope requirements, curing method, weather controls, and access restrictions. Record each specification on the Slab-Finish Inspection Record before concrete discharge begins in Project 16.
  2. Evaluate the concrete condition before each finishing operation rather than working solely by elapsed time. Use a thumbprint or firm foot-pressure test on an inconspicuous area before every operation in this sequence.

F. Bull-Floating and Initial Surface Correction

  1. Bull-float or darby the screeded surface using the approved direction and overlap. Work in overlapping passes across the full slab area immediately after screeding is complete.
  2. Correct ridges, low areas, aggregate exposure, and screed marks while the surface remains workable. Stop and correct any area that will not close properly before moving to the next section.
  3. Avoid trapping bleed water, overworking the surface, or adding dry cement or unauthorized water. Do not continue floating an area that is still yielding visible water at the surface.
  4. Allow bleed water and sheen to dissipate to the required condition before later finishing. Check the surface at regular intervals using the thumbprint or foot-pressure test rather than a fixed wait time.

G. Edging and Jointing

  1. Edge the slab perimeter and formed openings using the specified radius and depth. Run the edging tool continuously along every perimeter form and opening before jointing begins.
  2. Tool construction, isolation, and contraction joints at the planned locations where the design uses tooled joints. Use the straightedge to guide the groover and match the joint plan exactly.
  3. Maintain straight joint lines, consistent depth, intersection details, and edge condition. Recheck each tooled joint against the straightedge before moving to the next.

H. Floating, Troweling, and Texturing

  1. Use hand floats, power floats, trowels, fresnos, brooms, texture tools, or other equipment only as required by the finish specification. Confirm the specified finish before selecting a tool sequence.
  2. Sequence floating and troweling passes according to the concrete condition and desired finish. Do not begin a troweling pass before the surface has reached the condition required for that pass.
  3. Apply broom or other texture in the specified direction and uniformity. Complete texturing immediately after the final floating or troweling pass and before the surface hardens further.
  4. Protect drains, depressions, edges, penetrations, and embedded items during finishing. Work around each protected feature without displacing it or filling it with finishing residue.

I. Verification Before Sawing or Curing

  1. Check slope, drainage, surface plane, edge condition, joint location, texture, and transitions while correction remains possible. Complete this check before the surface hardens past the point of correction.
  2. Saw-cut control joints at the specified timing, depth, spacing, and sequence where the design uses sawed joints. Begin saw-cutting only within the specified early-strength window; do not delay past the window or cut before it.

J. Curing, Protection, and Cleanup

  1. Begin curing immediately when the surface can receive the specified method without damage. Do not delay curing start once the surface condition permits it.
  2. Install wet coverings, curing compound, plastic, insulation, weather protection, or other specified controls. Apply the specified method continuously across the full slab area.
  3. Barricade the slab against foot traffic, equipment, rain, freezing, rapid drying, contamination, and early loading. Post barricades before leaving the area unattended.
  4. Inspect for tearing, scaling, dusting, ridges, birdbaths, joint defects, and finish inconsistency. Walk the full slab and formed openings and record every observed condition.
  5. Correct only conditions that can be repaired without causing greater damage; document defects requiring later evaluation. Refer any condition beyond minor correction to Project 18 or to qualified review.
  6. Clean tools and equipment using an approved washout method. Prevent cement residue from entering soil, drains, or waterways.
  7. Record finishing times, operations, weather, joint installation, curing start, defects, and protection measures. Complete every field on the Slab-Finish Inspection Record.
  8. Complete the slab-finish inspection and continued-curing handoff. Sign and date the release section of the record before leaving the site.

Common Finish Inspection

Inspect every finishing item using the same checks regardless of which operation produced it.

  • Flatness check: confirm ridges, low areas, birdbaths, and screed marks were corrected while the surface was workable.
  • Edge check: confirm the specified radius and depth are continuous along the perimeter and every formed opening.
  • Joint check: confirm every joint is straight, consistent in depth, correctly intersected, and matches the planned location, timing, and spacing.
  • Finish check: confirm the surface texture matches the approved finish specification in direction and uniformity.
  • Protection check: confirm curing began at the correct surface condition and that barricades, coverings, and weather protection remain in place and continuous.

Complete the Slab-Finish Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before turning the work over for continued curing.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Surface Tears or Pulls During Floating or Troweling

Probable cause: the finishing pass was performed before the concrete reached the required condition, or the tool was worked over an area still yielding bleed water.
Confirmation: check an adjacent untouched area with a thumbprint or foot-pressure test to compare readiness.
Correction: stop working the torn area, allow it to reach the correct condition, then refloat or retrowel only that area using light pressure.
Reinspection: repeat the finish check across the corrected area and confirm texture consistency with the surrounding surface.

Blisters, Delamination, or Dusting Appear After Finishing

Probable cause: bleed water or air was trapped beneath the surface by closing it too early, or by a power-trowel pass performed while bleed water was still present.
Confirmation: sound the surface for hollow areas and compare affected locations to the timing recorded on the Slab-Finish Inspection Record.
Correction: remove and refinish blistered or delaminated areas that can be corrected without further damage; document areas that cannot be corrected for evaluation under Project 18.
Reinspection: repeat the finish check and sound the corrected area again after curing begins.

Joint Line Is Crooked or Inconsistent in Depth

Probable cause: the groover was worked freehand without a straightedge, or the saw drifted from the planned line during cutting.
Confirmation: measure the joint against the straightedge or the marked saw-cut line and check depth at several points along its length.
Correction: for tooled joints still plastic, correct the line and depth immediately using the straightedge; for a hardened or sawed joint outside tolerance, document the condition and obtain direction before further cutting.
Reinspection: repeat the joint check along the full length of the corrected joint.

Saw-Cutting Window Is Missed

Probable cause: cutting was delayed past the specified early-strength window, risking random cracking before the joint is cut, or attempted before the concrete reached sufficient strength to prevent raveling.
Confirmation: compare the actual cutting time recorded on the Slab-Finish Inspection Record to the specified window and inspect the slab for random cracks that may have formed before cutting.
Correction: cut the joint as close to the planned location as the surface condition allows; document any random crack found and refer it for evaluation if it falls outside the minor-repair scope.
Reinspection: repeat the joint check and document the actual cutting time against the specified window for the project record.

Surface Finish Does Not Match the Specification

Probable cause: the wrong tool sequence was used, or texturing was applied before or after the correct concrete condition for that texture.
Confirmation: compare the actual finish to the approved finish specification and to the tool sequence recorded in this project's steps.
Correction: while the surface remains workable, refinish the affected area using the correct tool sequence; if the surface has hardened past correction, document the deviation for evaluation.
Reinspection: repeat the finish check across the corrected area and compare texture and direction to the surrounding surface.

Curing Was Delayed After Finishing

Probable cause: curing materials were not staged before placement began, or the crew moved to cleanup before beginning the specified curing method.
Confirmation: compare the recorded curing-start time to the time the surface reached the correct condition for curing.
Correction: begin curing immediately upon discovery of the delay and inspect the surface for drying, cracking, or dusting caused by the delay.
Reinspection: repeat the surface inspection after the specified curing period and document any damage attributable to the delay.

Cleanup

This project ends with a finished, jointed, cured, and barricaded slab, not a cleared job site.

  • Clean bull floats, hand floats, trowels, edging tools, groovers, and power-float equipment before concrete residue hardens on them
  • Wash out saw-cutting equipment and dispose of slurry using an approved washout method that does not discharge cement residue into soil, drains, or waterways
  • Confirm curing coverings, compound, or insulation are continuous across the full slab area before leaving the site
  • Confirm barricades are posted and stable against foot traffic, equipment, rain, freezing, and early loading
  • Return the Slab-Finish Inspection Record and photographs to the project record with the Project 16 placement log

Field Terms

Bull Float
A large flat tool used to close and level the screeded surface before hand finishing.
Darby
A hand-held straightedge tool used for initial surface leveling, typically on smaller slab areas.
Bleed Water
Water that rises to the concrete surface after placement and must dissipate before further finishing.
Tooled Joint
A control or isolation joint formed with a hand or walk-behind groover while the concrete is plastic.
Sawed Joint
A control joint cut into the slab surface with a saw within a specified early-strength window.
Fresno
A long-handled steel finishing trowel used for smooth or hard-troweled finishes.
Curing Start
The point at which the specified curing method may be applied without damaging the finished surface.
Birdbath
A shallow depression in a finished slab surface that collects standing water.
Slab-Finish Inspection Record
The document recording finishing times, operations, joint installation, curing start, defects, and protection measures.
Continuous Operation
The requirement that Project 16 placement and this project's finishing proceed without pausing at the chapter boundary.

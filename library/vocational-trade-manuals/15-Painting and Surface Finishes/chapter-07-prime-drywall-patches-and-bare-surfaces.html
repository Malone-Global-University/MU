Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to select and apply the correct primer for each substrate and defect condition present on a practice wall and trim assembly: bare drywall, repaired patch areas, raw wood, and approved minor stains. The finished work product is a fully and correctly primed practice assembly with every surface documented by substrate, primer type, lot, coverage, and inspection result, ready to receive finish coating in Chapter 8 and Chapter 9.

You will identify each substrate and defect requiring primer, select drywall primer, bonding primer, wood primer, stain-blocking primer, or another specified product for each condition, prepare a test patch where compatibility or stain blocking is uncertain, spot-prime repaired areas, prime bare drywall and raw wood, apply and reinspect stain-blocking primer over corrected stains, and inspect the finished primer coverage for adhesion, holidays, lap marks, and bleed-through.

This chapter depends on the readiness decision from Chapter 1, the cleaning completed in Chapter 3, the patching and sanding completed in Chapter 4, and the caulking completed and cured in Chapter 5. It supplies the primed surface that Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 cut in and roll.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-037 — Select the correct primer product for each identified substrate and defect condition.
  • PF-038 — Spot-prime repaired wall areas without leaving visible flashing under the finish coat.
  • PF-039 — Prime bare drywall uniformly at the specified coverage rate.
  • PF-040 — Prime raw wood, end grain, MDF edges, and knots using the required system.
  • PF-041 — Apply stain-blocking primer to identified minor stains after their cause is corrected, and confirm no bleed-through.
  • PF-042 — Inspect primer coverage for adhesion, holidays, runs, lap marks, and bleed-through, and document the results.

Where This Skill Fits

Primer is not a universal first coat poured over every surface without thought. It is a substrate-specific and defect-specific product selected from the readiness decision issued in Chapter 1 and the repairs completed in Chapters 4 and 5. Bare drywall, a patched repair, raw wood, and a corrected stain each require a different primer chemistry to achieve uniform porosity, adhesion, and blocking before the finish coat is applied.

The primer coverage you approve in this chapter becomes the surface Chapter 8 cuts in and Chapter 9 rolls. A primer defect left uncorrected here — a holiday, an unblocked stain, or unsanded raised grain — telegraphs directly through the finish coat and is far harder to correct once topcoat has been applied.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying every substrate and defect on the practice assembly that requires primer
  • selecting drywall primer, bonding primer, wood primer, stain-blocking primer, masonry primer, or another product specified for the condition present
  • preparing a test patch where compatibility or stain-blocking performance is uncertain
  • spot-priming repaired wall areas and priming bare drywall uniformly
  • priming raw wood, end grain, MDF edges, and knots
  • applying stain-blocking primer to approved minor stains whose cause has already been corrected
  • reinspecting for bleed-through and applying an additional coat only where required
  • sanding raised grain after full cure where specified, cleaning dust, and documenting primer type, lot, substrate, coverage, and test results

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • prime any stain whose underlying moisture, leak, or contamination cause has not been corrected and verified; priming over an active cause only delays reappearance of the stain
  • substitute one primer product as a universal layer for every substrate present; each substrate and defect condition requires the primer selected for it in this chapter
  • apply finish topcoat directly to any surface in this chapter; cutting in and rolling belong to Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 and may not begin until this chapter's primer coverage is inspected and accepted
  • prime any surface where the Chapter 1 readiness decision, Chapter 4 patch cure, or Chapter 5 caulk cure has not been confirmed complete

Any surface that fails the primer acceptance standards in this chapter must be corrected and reinspected before Chapter 8 begins.

Trade Note

A primer is chosen for what it must do — seal porosity, promote adhesion, or block a stain — not simply because it is the next can on the shelf. Confirm the substrate and defect condition before opening any primer container.

Setup Standard

Do not open any primer container until the substrate and defect list for the practice assembly (Step 1) has been reviewed against Chapter 1's readiness decision. Do not begin cutting in or rolling finish coat until this chapter's primer inspection (Steps 18–20) is complete and accepted.

Shop Tip

Group the day's priming by product rather than by location. Prime every bare-drywall area with drywall primer first, then every raw-wood area with wood primer, rather than switching containers repeatedly, to reduce cross-contamination and keep coverage rate consistent.

Common Field Fundamentals

Priming is a matching exercise, not a default step. Every substrate and defect identified in Chapter 1 and repaired in Chapters 3 through 5 is matched here to the primer chemistry that seals its porosity, promotes its adhesion, or blocks its stain. A primer applied to the wrong condition — for example, a standard drywall primer applied over an uncorrected tannin stain — will not block the defect it was never designed to block.

Primer Selection Is Substrate- and Defect-Specific

Confirm the substrate category and defect condition for each area before selecting a primer. Do not apply one primer product across every substrate in the practice assembly. Drywall primer, bonding primer, wood primer, and stain-blocking primer are each formulated for a different condition, and using one as a universal substitute risks poor adhesion, unblocked stains, or wasted coats.

Measurement Cue — Coverage Rate and Coat Count

Apply each primer at the coverage rate stated on its manufacturer data sheet. Do not stretch primer beyond its stated coverage to make a container last longer, and do not apply additional coats beyond the number specified for the identified stain or substrate condition without a documented reason.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Prime Over an Uncorrected Stain Cause

Do not apply stain-blocking primer over any stain whose underlying moisture, leak, corrosion, or contamination cause has not been corrected and verified. Priming over an active cause conceals the stain temporarily and allows it to reappear through the finish coat after this chapter is complete.

Manufacturer Cue — Mixing and Recoat Windows

Mix each primer according to its product instructions before application. Observe the manufacturer's stated dry time before recoating or applying finish coat, and do not shorten the recoat window to save time.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilation and Overhead Work

Establish ventilation that controls primer fumes without drawing dust into occupied spaces or backdrafting combustion appliances. Use a stable ladder or platform and a painter's pole for ceiling and upper-wall priming rather than overreaching.

E. Substrate Identification and Primer Selection

  1. Identify each substrate and defect requiring primer. Walk the practice assembly and confirm the substrate inventory against the Chapter 1 readiness decision and the Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 repair records.
  2. Review topcoat compatibility, stain type, porosity, adhesion, tannin risk, corrosion risk, and environmental limits. Cross-check each substrate against the manufacturer data sheet for the intended finish coat.
  3. Select drywall primer, bonding primer, wood primer, stain-blocking primer, masonry primer, or other specified product. Assign one primer to each substrate and defect condition on the Selection Schedule.
  4. Do not use one primer as a universal substitute. Confirm no single product has been assigned across substrates with different porosity, adhesion, or blocking requirements.
  5. Prepare a test patch where compatibility or stain blocking is uncertain. Apply the selected primer to a hidden or scrap area and allow it to dry fully before proceeding to full application.

F. Setup and Mixing

  1. Protect adjacent surfaces and establish ventilation. Confirm masking from Chapter 6 remains intact and ventilation controls fumes without spreading dust.
  2. Mix primer according to the product instructions. Stir each primer to full uniformity before loading a brush or roller.

G. Application to Patches, Drywall, and Wood

  1. Spot-prime repaired wall areas. Apply the selected patch primer to each repaired area, feathering the edge into the surrounding field.
  2. Prime bare drywall uniformly without overworking the paper. Roll the full field at the manufacturer's coverage rate, cutting in edges and corners with a brush first.
  3. Prime raw wood, end grain, MDF edges, and knots using the required system. Brush wood primer into end grain and edges, working it fully into the grain rather than leaving a surface film only.

H. Stain-Blocking Primer

  1. Apply stain-blocking primer to identified minor stains after their cause is corrected. Confirm the stain's cause has been corrected and verified before applying primer over it.
  2. Allow the first stain-blocking coat to dry. Observe the manufacturer's full dry time before inspecting.
  3. Reinspect for bleed-through. Examine the dried coat under work light for any reappearance of the stain's color or shape.
  4. Apply the specified additional coat only if required. Recoat only areas where bleed-through is confirmed, not the full surrounding field.

I. Coverage Control and Dry Time

  1. Maintain wet edge and specified coverage. Work in sections sized to maintain a wet edge at the product's stated coverage rate.
  2. Do not stretch primer beyond its coverage rate. Reload the brush or roller rather than thinning the film to extend a container.
  3. Allow primer to dry within environmental limits. Confirm surface and air temperature and humidity remain within the manufacturer's stated range during dry time.

J. Inspection, Sanding, and Documentation

  1. Inspect for holidays, runs, poor adhesion, lap marks, bleed-through, and raised fibers. Use a work light at a raking angle across every primed surface.
  2. Sand raised grain only after full cure and where specified. Use fine sandpaper lightly, without cutting through the primer film.
  3. Clean dust and approve for finish coating. HEPA-vacuum all sanding dust and confirm every surface meets the Acceptance Standards before releasing the assembly to Chapter 8.
  4. Document primer type, lot, substrate, coverage, and test results. Complete the Primer Log entry for every area primed in this chapter.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every primed surface regardless of substrate or primer type.

  • Selection check: confirm every substrate and defect condition was matched to the primer specified for it on the Selection Schedule, with no universal substitution.
  • Coverage check: confirm no holidays or thin areas remain visible under work-light inspection.
  • Stain-blocking check: confirm no bleed-through is visible after the specified dry time, and that any additional coat was applied only where bleed-through was confirmed.
  • Adhesion check: confirm primer shows no lifting or flaking after cure.
  • Recoat-timing check: confirm the manufacturer's stated dry time was observed before any additional coat or finish coat.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Primer Log has a complete entry for every primed area, with no blank fields.

Complete the Primer Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 8.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Stain Bleeds Through After the Finish Coat Is Applied

Probable cause: the stain-blocking primer was applied over an uncorrected moisture or contamination cause, or bleed-through was not reinspected before the finish coat was applied.
Confirmation: inspect the original Primer Log entry for the stain and check whether a cause-correction and reinspection result was recorded.
Correction: identify and correct the underlying cause, then reapply stain-blocking primer and reinspect for bleed-through before recoating the finish.
Reinspection: confirm no bleed-through is visible after the corrected primer coat is fully dry.

Primer Lifts or Flakes Shortly After Application

Probable cause: a bonding or wood primer was applied over a glossy, dusty, or contaminated surface without the required cleaning or scuff-sanding from earlier chapters.
Confirmation: inspect the failed area for residual gloss, dust, or contamination beneath the lifted primer.
Correction: remove the failed primer, reclean or scuff-sand the substrate, and reapply the correct primer.
Reinspection: confirm adhesion on the corrected area after full cure before proceeding.

A Holiday Appears Under the Finish Coat

Probable cause: a section of the primer field was missed during rolling, or the roller was allowed to run dry without reloading.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area under raking work light and compare against the original Primer Log entry for that section.
Correction: spot-prime the missed area, feathering into the surrounding primed field, and allow it to dry fully.
Reinspection: reinspect the corrected area under raking light before Chapter 8 begins.

Raised Grain Telegraphs Through the Finish Coat

Probable cause: raised grain from the wood primer coat was not sanded after full cure before the finish coat was applied.
Confirmation: run a hand across the affected wood surface to feel for texture inconsistent with the surrounding area.
Correction: sand the raised grain lightly with fine sandpaper after full primer cure, without cutting through the primer film, then reclean dust.
Reinspection: confirm a smooth, consistent surface before Chapter 8 begins.

One Primer Was Used Across Substrates That Required Different Products

Probable cause: the Selection Schedule was not consulted before application, and a single primer on hand was applied to every substrate for convenience.
Confirmation: compare the Primer Log entries against the Selection Schedule for each substrate.
Correction: identify affected areas, confirm whether the applied primer meets that substrate's adhesion or blocking requirement, and reprime with the correct product where it does not.
Reinspection: confirm each corrected area meets the Acceptance Standards before proceeding.

Primer Log Shows Blank Coverage or Lot Entries

Probable cause: priming was completed under time pressure and documentation was deferred rather than completed at the point of application.
Confirmation: review the Primer Log line by line for blank fields.
Correction: return to the affected area, confirm the product and lot used, measure or estimate the coverage applied, and complete the missing entry.
Reinspection: confirm every Primer Log entry is complete before releasing the assembly to Chapter 8.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a fully primed, inspected practice assembly and a completed Primer Log retained for the next chapter.

  • Clean brushes and rollers according to the primer manufacturer's instructions; do not pour rinse water or solvent into drains
  • Close and store primer containers upright, labeled, and within their temperature limits
  • Dispose of any oily rags or solvent-soaked materials in an approved fire-safe container
  • HEPA-vacuum sanding dust from raised-grain corrections and confirm floors and adjacent protection remain intact
  • File the completed Primer Log and test-patch records with the Chapter 1 readiness decision for reference by later chapters

Field Terms

Spot-Prime
Applying primer only to a repaired or bare area rather than the full surrounding field.
Full Prime
Applying primer across an entire wall, ceiling, or trim run.
Bleed-Through
A stain reappearing through a dried stain-blocking primer coat.
Holiday
A skipped or missed area within the primer film where the substrate remains unsealed.
Raised Grain
Wood fiber that swells and stands upright after primer wets the surface.
Coverage Rate
The area a stated volume of primer will cover at the manufacturer's specified film thickness.
Bonding Primer
A primer formulated to promote adhesion on glossy, dense, or otherwise low-porosity surfaces.
Drywall Primer
A primer formulated to seal bare drywall paper and joint-compound repairs at uniform porosity.
Wood Primer
A primer formulated to seal raw wood, end grain, MDF edges, and knots.
Stain-Blocking Primer
A primer formulated to block water, tannin, smoke, or grease stains from bleeding through a finish coat.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to cut in one wall-and-ceiling area by brush: loading a cutting-in brush correctly, cutting a consistent ceiling line, cutting inside corners without excessive buildup, cutting cleanly around casing, baseboard, windows, doors, receptacles, fixtures, and penetrations, and maintaining a wet edge into the section that will be rolled. The finished work product is a completed cut-in band around the full perimeter of the accepted area — ceiling line, corners, and every trim and fixture boundary — free of holidays, sags, brush ridges, crooked lines, and heavy corners, and wide enough for the roller to overlap.

You will confirm the primer and repairs from earlier chapters are accepted, stir and box the paint, load the brush to a controlled depth, cut the ceiling line, cut inside corners, cut around casing and fixtures without coating hardware or device faces, work in sections sized to stay wet for rolling, and inspect the finished cut line against a documented standard.

This chapter depends on the accepted primer from Chapter 7 and the masking completed in Chapter 6. Chapter 9 begins immediately from the cut-in band you produce here: rolling cannot proceed correctly against a cut line that is inconsistent, dry, or improperly placed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-043 — Load a cutting-in brush to a controlled depth without starving or overloading it.
  • PF-044 — Cut a consistent ceiling line at the correct band width.
  • PF-045 — Cut inside corners without excessive paint buildup.
  • PF-046 — Cut around casing, baseboard, windows, doors, receptacles, fixtures, and penetrations without coating hardware, seals, or device faces.
  • PF-047 — Maintain a wet edge across a cut-in section sized for rolling.
  • PF-048 — Inspect a completed cut-in line for holidays, sags, brush ridges, crooked lines, and heavy corners.

Where This Skill Fits

Cutting in is the brush-applied edge work that rolling depends on. A roller cannot reach cleanly into ceiling lines, inside corners, or tight trim boundaries, so the cut-in band establishes both the edge line and a wet margin the roller will overlap. Chapter 9 rolls each section only while its cut-in band remains workable; a cut-in section left to dry before rolling begins will show a visible lap line at the overlap.

The masking completed in Chapter 6 and the primer accepted in Chapter 7 are prerequisites this chapter does not repeat. The cut-line standard documented at the end of this chapter is the same standard Chapter 19 checks during the final quality inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • loading a cutting-in brush to the correct depth for controlled paint release
  • cutting a consistent ceiling line by the approved freehand or masked method
  • cutting inside corners without excessive buildup
  • cutting around casing, baseboard, windows, doors, receptacles, fixtures, and penetrations
  • sequencing sections so the cut-in band remains wet for rolling
  • inspecting the finished cut line against a documented acceptance standard

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin cutting in before the primer and repairs covered in Chapter 7 are accepted, or before the masking completed in Chapter 6 is in place and inspected
  • coat electrical receptacles, switches, grounding surfaces, detector openings, appliance labels, sprinkler heads, fire-alarm devices, hinges, latches, weather seals, or moving hardware
  • roll a section whose cut-in band has already dried; that condition is addressed in this chapter's troubleshooting section and requires resequencing, not coating over a dry edge
  • substitute a different coating, sheen, or color than the one specified on the approved finish and color schedule

Any section where the cut-in band cannot be kept wet through rolling, or where trim, fixture, or hardware protection is incomplete, must be corrected before cutting in continues in that section.

Trade Note

A cutting-in brush is loaded, not dipped. Excess paint tapped or wiped from one side of the bristles, rather than left to drip, is the single most common factor separating a clean, controlled cut line from a line with holidays on one pass and sags on the next, as identified in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cutting in (Step 1) until the primer and repairs from Chapter 7 are accepted and the masking from Chapter 6 is inspected and in place. Do not begin rolling any section (Chapter 9) until this chapter's cut-in band for that section is documented as complete and still wet.

Shop Tip

Cut only as much wall or ceiling as can still be rolled while the cut-in band remains workable. Working in sections matched to your rolling pace, rather than cutting the entire room before rolling begins, is what keeps the wet-edge requirement in Step 13 achievable.

Common Field Fundamentals

Cutting in and rolling are coordinated operations, not separate tasks performed in isolation. The cut-in band you produce in this chapter must still be wet when Chapter 9 rolls into it; a dry cut-in edge produces a visible lap line no amount of rolling technique can hide. Working in sections sized to your rolling pace, rather than cutting an entire room before any rolling begins, is the fundamental discipline this chapter teaches.

Brush Loading and Wet-Edge Timing

A correctly loaded brush carries paint on both sides of the bristle tips without excess heavy enough to drip. Tap or wipe the brush against the inside of the cut bucket rather than the rim alone, and reload before the stroke begins to drag or thin. Because the wet-edge requirement governs section size, plan how much ceiling line, corner, and trim boundary you can cut and still roll before the leading edge begins to set.

Measurement Cue — Consistent Band Width

Maintain a cut-in band wide enough for the roller to overlap it by the margin specified in the approved application procedure. A band that is too narrow forces the roller to touch unpainted substrate at the edge; a band that is too wide creates a visible texture difference between the brushed and rolled areas.

Critical Safety Cue — Hardware, Devices, and Life-Safety Components

Do not coat electrical receptacles, switches, grounding surfaces, detector openings, appliance labels, sprinkler heads, fire-alarm devices, hinges, latches, weather seals, or moving hardware while cutting around them. Cut cleanly to the edge of each device or opening and stop; do not bridge the gap with paint to save time.

Field Safety Cue — Ladder Stability and Overhead Work

Use a stable, rated ladder or platform positioned on level footing for every ceiling-line cut. Do not overreach to extend a cut line; reposition the ladder instead. Keep the cut bucket secured on a stable surface or hook, not balanced on a ladder step.

Code Alert — Coating Restrictions Near Life-Safety Devices

Requirements prohibiting the coating of smoke detectors, sprinkler heads, and fire-alarm devices are enforced by adopted fire and building code and do not vary by preference. Verify the exact devices and clearances that must remain uncoated in this room before cutting in around them.

E. Setup and Brush Loading

  1. Review color sequence, sheen, brush type, coating, masking, and room order. Confirm the approved finish and color schedule and the room's planned section order before opening any paint.
  2. Confirm primer and repairs are accepted. Verify the Chapter 7 acceptance record for every surface to be cut in.
  3. Stir and box paint where required. Mix thoroughly and box multiple containers of the same color into a common supply per the manufacturer's instructions.
  4. Pour a controlled working quantity into a cut bucket. Fill the bucket to a level that allows brush loading without dipping past the ferrule.
  5. Load the brush to the specified depth. Dip the bristles to roughly one-third of their length.
  6. Tap or wipe excess without starving the brush. Tap against the inside of the bucket or wipe one side against the rim; do not scrape both sides bare.

F. Cutting the Ceiling Line and Corners

  1. Begin away from the final line and spread paint toward it. Start the stroke a short distance from the ceiling line and draw the brush into position.
  2. Cut the ceiling line using the approved freehand or masked method. Follow the method specified in the finish schedule for this room.
  3. Maintain a consistent cut-in band wide enough for the roller overlap. Check band width periodically against the specified overlap margin.
  4. Cut inside corners without excessive buildup. Use the brush tip to lay a thin, even line into the corner rather than working paint back and forth repeatedly.

G. Cutting Around Trim, Hardware, and Fixtures

  1. Cut around casing, baseboard, windows, doors, receptacles, fixtures, and penetrations. Work the brush tip to each boundary and stop precisely at the protected or masked edge.
  2. Avoid coating hardware, seals, device faces, or service labels. Lift the brush before contact with any component identified in the Critical Safety Cue above.

H. Sequencing, Wet Edge, and Reload

  1. Work in sections that can be rolled while the cut-in remains wet where the coating system requires it. Size each section to match the rolling pace planned for Chapter 9.
  2. Feather the open edge of the cut-in band. Lighten brush pressure at the inner edge of the band so it blends without a visible ridge when rolled.
  3. Reload before brush drag creates thin or ragged lines. Return to the cut bucket at the first sign of a thinning stroke.

I. Inspection, Correction, and Documentation

  1. Inspect for holidays, sags, brush ridges, crooked lines, and heavy corners. Use the handheld light under raking conditions along the full cut-in band.
  2. Correct defects while the coating remains workable. Re-brush thin or missed areas immediately; do not wait for the section to dry.
  3. Clean or wrap the brush between extended pauses. Wrap the loaded brush in plastic film or an airtight bag to prevent it from drying out during breaks in the same shift.
  4. Document the approved cut-line standard. Record band width, method used, and inspection results on the Cut-In Work Record.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every cut-in boundary in the room regardless of location or trim type.

  • Band-width check: confirm the cut-in band is consistently wide enough for the specified roller overlap along its full length.
  • Corner check: confirm inside corners show no excessive buildup or visibly heavy paint.
  • Hardware check: confirm no paint has reached receptacles, switches, hinges, latches, weather seals, labels, or other protected hardware.
  • Surface check: confirm no holidays, sags, brush ridges, or crooked lines are visible under raking light.
  • Wet-edge check: confirm each section's cut-in band is still workable at the moment rolling begins on that section.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Cut-In Work Record lists method, band width, and inspection result for every boundary type present in the room.

Complete the Cut-In Work Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 9.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Visible Lap Line Appears Where Rolling Meets the Cut-In Band

Probable cause: the cut-in band was allowed to dry before the adjoining section was rolled, so the roller could not blend into a wet edge.
Confirmation: compare the time the cut-in band was applied against the time rolling began on that section; inspect the lap line under raking light.
Correction: re-cut and re-roll the affected section as one continuous operation, sized to a section the painter can complete without the band drying.
Reinspection: check the corrected section under raking light for a blended, lap-free transition.

Holidays Appear Along the Cut-In Band

Probable cause: the brush was starved of paint before the stroke was complete, or the section was rushed without reloading.
Confirmation: inspect the band under raking light for thin or missed spots and compare their spacing with the painter's reload intervals.
Correction: reload the brush and recoat the affected area while the surrounding paint remains workable.
Reinspection: re-check the corrected area under raking light once dry.

Corners Show Heavy Buildup or Visible Ridges

Probable cause: the brush was worked back and forth repeatedly in the corner instead of laid in with a single controlled stroke.
Confirmation: inspect the corner under raking light and compare paint film thickness with the adjoining band.
Correction: while still workable, lightly draw the brush tip through the corner to redistribute the excess; if already set, sand lightly per the finish schedule and recoat.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected corner matches the band's thickness and shows no ridge under raking light.

Paint Reaches a Receptacle, Hinge, or Other Protected Hardware

Probable cause: the cut line was extended past the protected edge without lifting the brush, or masking over the hardware was incomplete.
Confirmation: inspect the affected hardware and the surrounding masking for gaps or missed coverage.
Correction: remove wet paint from the hardware immediately using the approved cleanup method; if cured, follow the correction method in Chapter 18.
Reinspection: confirm the hardware is free of paint and operates correctly before continuing.

The Cut-In Band Is Inconsistent in Width Along the Ceiling Line

Probable cause: the painter varied brush angle or pressure without checking band width periodically during the pass.
Confirmation: measure band width at several points along the ceiling line and compare against the specified roller-overlap margin.
Correction: re-cut narrow sections to bring them to the specified width before that section is rolled.
Reinspection: re-check band width at the same points after correction.

The Brush Dries Out During an Extended Pause

Probable cause: the loaded brush was left exposed to air during a break instead of wrapped or cleaned.
Confirmation: inspect the bristles for stiffness or partially dried paint before resuming work.
Correction: clean the brush per the manufacturer's instructions if paint has begun to set, or reload a properly wrapped brush before continuing.
Reinspection: test the reloaded brush on a small area to confirm it lays down a clean, even line before resuming the cut-in band.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with an inspected cut-in band ready for Chapter 9 rolling, not a fully closed-out project.

  • Wrap or clean the cutting-in brush according to the paint manufacturer's instructions
  • Seal or store the cut bucket to prevent skinning if work will resume the same day
  • Wipe drips from protected floors, trim, and fixtures immediately per Chapter 2 protection procedures
  • Confirm the Cut-In Work Record is complete for every boundary type in the room
  • Flag any section whose cut-in band could not be kept wet for immediate rolling

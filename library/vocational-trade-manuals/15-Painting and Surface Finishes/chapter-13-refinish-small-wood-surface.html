Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to refinish one small wood surface — a tabletop, shelf, panel, or trim component — from a failed or unwanted existing finish through to a fully cured, protected, approved-color finish. The finished work product is one refinished wood component with the old finish removed without gouging the wood or sanding through veneer, minor damage repaired, an approved stain or color applied and matched to a sample, and a protective topcoat built to the specified number of coats.

You will identify the wood species, veneer thickness where present, and existing finish; select and test a removal method; strip or sand off the old finish; repair minor damage; prepare the bare grain; apply an approved color; build a protective topcoat; and inspect the finished surface for color match, film integrity, and smoothness.

This chapter builds on the sanding, staining, and clear-coating methods taught in Chapter 12. Where Chapter 12 finished bare or already-prepared wood trim, this chapter begins one step earlier: removing an existing finish that is failed, unwanted, or incompatible with the new finish before any of that preparation can begin. The finish-removal, damage-repair, and color-matching method taught here applies to any small wood component refinished later in this book or in field practice.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-073 — Strip or sand the old finish from a small wood component without gouging the wood or sanding through veneer.
  • PF-074 — Repair minor damage, including dents, scratches, loose joints, and edge damage, within the stated project scope.
  • PF-075 — Prepare the wood grain through progressive sanding and complete dust removal.
  • PF-076 — Apply an approved stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil finish matched to a sample.
  • PF-077 — Apply a protective topcoat built to the specified coat count with sanding between coats where required.
  • PF-078 — Inspect the refinished surface for color match, sheen consistency, film defects, adhesion, and smoothness.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 12 taught staining and clear-coating on bare interior trim that had no existing finish to remove. This chapter applies the same sample-approval, staining, and topcoat method to a component that starts in worse condition: an old finish that is peeling, discolored, incompatible, or simply no longer wanted. The finish-removal method taught here — test first, remove without damaging the substrate, neutralize residues, and confirm dryness before any repair begins — governs every later refinishing task in this book and in the Chapter 18 failure-repair work, where the same diagnose-before-you-remove principle applies to painted surfaces.

The color-and-sheen sample method used here is the same discipline enforced in Chapter 12: no stain or topcoat is applied to the finished component until a sample on scrap or a hidden area has been approved. That discipline prevents a mismatched or blotched result on a visible surface that cannot be corrected without starting the entire removal-through-topcoat sequence again.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying wood species, veneer thickness, existing finish, damage, and the desired appearance for one small wood component
  • selecting and testing a finish-removal method — sanding, scraping, or chemical stripping
  • removing the old finish without gouging the wood or sanding through veneer
  • repairing minor dents, scratches, loose joints, and edge damage within the stated project scope
  • preparing the wood grain through progressive sanding
  • applying an approved stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil finish matched to a sample
  • applying a protective topcoat to the approved build with sanding between coats where required
  • inspecting the refinished surface for color match, sheen consistency, film defects, adhesion, and smoothness

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • sand, scrape, or otherwise disturb any coating suspected of containing lead without the required testing and controls; test suspect older finishes for lead where age and condition require it before any disturbance begins
  • repair damage beyond minor dents, scratches, loose joints, and edge damage — structural breaks, missing structural members, or major veneer loss are outside this chapter's scope and require a different repair plan
  • apply stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil finish to the finished component before a sample on scrap or a hidden area has been approved
  • apply a protective topcoat before the applied color has reached its full specified dry time
  • reassemble or return the component to service before the topcoat has reached full cure

Any component with damage beyond minor dents, scratches, loose joints, and edge damage, or any component with a suspect lead-based finish that has not cleared testing, must be referred to a qualified professional or a different project plan before this chapter's procedure continues.

Trade Note

Removal method, repair, finish, and waste handling are recorded on the project record as each step is completed — not reconstructed afterward. A refinished component with no removal-method or cure-time record cannot be defended if the finish fails prematurely.

Setup Standard

Do not begin finish removal (Step 3 area) until the wood species, existing finish, damage, desired appearance, and project limits have been identified (Step 1) and any required lead testing has cleared (Step 2). Do not apply stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil to the component (Step 15) until the sample color has been approved (Step 14).

Shop Tip

Prepare at least two sample pieces from wood scrap of the same species and grain character as the component, if any is available. One sample confirms the removal and sanding sequence; the second confirms the color match. Testing both on the same piece can mask problems that only appear when removal dust or stripper residue is not fully cleared before staining.

Common Field Fundamentals

Refinishing a small wood surface follows the same underlying sequence as new stain-and-clear-coat work in Chapter 12, with one addition at the front: the existing finish must come off cleanly before any of that sequence can begin. Identify the wood and finish, test the removal method, remove without damaging the substrate, repair only what is within scope, sand progressively, sample the color, apply the color, build the topcoat, cure fully, and inspect.

Removal Method Selection

Sanding, scraping, and chemical stripping remove an existing finish by different mechanisms and each carries different risks to the substrate. Sanding removes finish and a small amount of wood together, and is the highest-risk method for sanding through thin veneer. Scraping lifts already-softened or already-failing finish with minimal wood removal, but only works where the finish is no longer well bonded. Chemical stripping dissolves the finish chemically and requires neutralization or rinsing before any further work, plus ventilation and chemical protection matched to the product. Select the method specified for the component's substrate and finish condition; do not substitute one method for another without confirming it will not damage the wood.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspected Lead-Based Finish

Test for lead or other hazardous coating components where the age and condition of the existing finish require it. Do not sand, scrape, or apply chemical stripper to a suspect coating until the required testing and controls are complete.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilation, Chemical Protection, and Fire Control

Establish ventilation before opening any stripper, solvent, stain, or oil-based finish product. Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection during removal, and select respiratory protection matched to the specific product, since a dust mask does not protect against solvent vapor. Dispose of oil-soaked rags and applicators in an approved fire-safe container; do not leave them piled or folded where spontaneous heating can occur.

Manufacturer Cue — Stripper Neutralization and Rinse Requirements

Follow the stripper manufacturer's instructions for neutralization or rinsing before sanding or applying any repair or finish. A stripper residue left on the surface will interfere with adhesion of filler, stain, and topcoat.

Measurement Cue — Sample Approval Before Application

Apply the selected stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil finish to a sample piece of the same wood species and sanding sequence, or to the least-visible area of the component if no scrap is available, and confirm color and grain match before applying it to the visible finished surface.

E. Identification and Hazard Screening

  1. Identify wood species, veneer thickness, existing finish, damage, desired appearance, and project limits. Record each on the Component Identification Record before proceeding.
  2. Test for lead or hazardous coating where age and condition require it. Do not proceed to removal until any required test result is recorded and clearance is documented.

F. Finish Removal

  1. Select sanding, scraping, chemical stripping, or another approved finish-removal method. Base the selection on the substrate, veneer condition, and existing finish identified in Step 1.
  2. Establish ventilation, chemical protection, fire control, and waste handling. Confirm these controls are in place before opening any stripper or solvent product.
  3. Test the removal method in an inconspicuous area. Confirm the method removes the finish without gouging the wood or exposing veneer substrate before working the full surface.
  4. Remove hardware and label components. Tag each removed item with the component identity recorded in Step 1.
  5. Remove the old finish without gouging wood or sanding through veneer. Work systematically across the surface, checking veneer areas frequently for substrate exposure.
  6. Neutralize or rinse stripper residues where required. Follow the stripper manufacturer's instructions exactly; do not proceed to repair or sanding until residue is fully removed.
  7. Allow the wood to dry. Confirm the surface is fully dry before repair work begins.

G. Repair and Grain Preparation

  1. Repair minor dents, scratches, loose joints, and edge damage within the project scope. Use filler compatible with the substrate and the intended finish system; do not attempt repairs beyond this chapter's stated scope.
  2. Sand progressively with the grain. Work through the specified abrasive sequence, always sanding in the direction of the grain.
  3. Preserve crisp edges and profiles. Avoid rounding over edges or profile details that should remain sharp.
  4. Remove all dust and residue. Use a tack cloth or HEPA vacuum across the entire surface, including profile recesses, before any color is applied.

H. Color Sample and Application

  1. Prepare sample color on scrap or hidden area. Apply the selected stain, dye, toner, paint, or oil finish to the sample piece or least-visible component area and confirm the match before proceeding.
  2. Apply stain, dye, toner, paint, oil, or other approved finish. Use the applicator and method specified for the selected product, working in the grain direction.
  3. Blend color and grain. Maintain even coverage and blend any overlaps before the product sets.
  4. Allow full dry time. Confirm the specified dry time has passed before applying the topcoat.

I. Topcoat, Cure, and Reassembly

  1. Apply the specified protective topcoat. Use the applicator and film thickness specified for the selected topcoat product.
  2. Sand between coats where required. Use the abrasive grade specified by the topcoat manufacturer and remove all resulting dust before the next coat.
  3. Apply additional coats to the approved build. Continue through the specified coat count, observing recoat timing between each.
  4. Allow cure before reassembly or service. Confirm full cure has been reached before handling the finished surface.
  5. Reinstall hardware. Match each labeled item back to its recorded position from Step 4.

J. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect color, sheen, repair visibility, film defects, adhesion, and surface smoothness. Compare the finished surface against the approved sample under normal and raking light.
  2. Document removal method, repair, finish, waste handling, and cure. Complete the project record before returning the component to storage or service.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every refinished wood component regardless of species or removal method.

  • Substrate check: confirm no veneer substrate is exposed and no gouging is visible where the old finish was removed.
  • Hazard-screen check: confirm any required lead test has a recorded, cleared result before physical work began.
  • Repair check: confirm every repaired dent, scratch, loose joint, and edge is sanded flush and free of visible ridges.
  • Grain check: confirm sanding direction followed the grain throughout, with no cross-grain scratches visible under raking light.
  • Color-match check: confirm the finished component matches the approved sample in color and grain clarity.
  • Cure check: confirm full cure was reached before hardware reinstallation or return to service.

Complete the Refinishing Project Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the component to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Veneer Substrate Becomes Visible During Sanding

Probable cause: the veneer thickness was not confirmed before sanding, or sanding pressure was too aggressive for a thin veneer layer.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area under normal light and compare its color and texture with the surrounding veneer.
Correction: stop sanding the affected area immediately, switch to a less aggressive method or hand-sand only, and consult a qualified finisher if substrate exposure is significant.
Reinspection: confirm no further substrate exposure occurs before continuing to the remaining surface.

Stain Blotches Unevenly After Application

Probable cause: the wood was not sanded to a consistent grit across the entire surface, or residual stripper or sanding dust remained before staining.
Confirmation: inspect the sample piece result against the finished surface and check the sanding record for consistency.
Correction: allow the stain to dry fully, sand back to bare wood, resand to a consistent grit, remove all dust, and reapply stain following the approved sample.
Reinspection: compare the corrected area with the approved sample under normal and raking light before proceeding to topcoat.

Topcoat Shows Dust Nibs or Bubbles

Probable cause: dust was not fully removed between coats, or the topcoat was stirred in a way that introduced air bubbles.
Confirmation: inspect the surface under raking light for texture irregularities.
Correction: allow the coat to cure fully, sand the affected area smooth using the specified between-coat abrasive, remove all dust, and apply an additional coat.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected area is smooth and free of nibs and bubbles before considering the topcoat build complete.

Repaired Area Remains Visible After Finishing

Probable cause: the filler used was not compatible with the stain or did not accept color the same way as the surrounding wood.
Confirmation: compare the repaired area with the surrounding finish under raking light and review the filler product record.
Correction: remove the incompatible filler where possible, replace it with a filler product confirmed compatible with the specified stain system, and reapply color and topcoat to the affected area.
Reinspection: confirm the repaired area blends with the surrounding finish before final inspection.

Finish Feels Tacky After the Specified Cure Time

Probable cause: the topcoat was applied too heavily, environmental conditions slowed the cure, or an incompatible oil or wax was present under the topcoat.
Confirmation: check the applied film thickness against the manufacturer's specified coverage rate and confirm the environmental conditions during application.
Correction: allow additional cure time within the manufacturer's stated range; if tackiness persists beyond that range, consult the manufacturer's instructions for the specific product before proceeding to further handling.
Reinspection: confirm the surface has reached full cure before reassembly or service.

Oily Rags Show Signs of Heat Buildup in Storage

Probable cause: oil-soaked rags or applicators were piled or folded together instead of stored in an approved fire-safe container.
Confirmation: inspect the storage location and container used for rag disposal.
Correction: immediately separate and safely dispose of the rags according to the product's waste-handling instructions, and provide an approved fire-safe container for all future oil-soaked material.
Reinspection: confirm fire-safe rag storage is in place before any further oil-based product is used on this or any other component.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a fully cured, reassembled component and a completed project record.

  • Dispose of stripper residue, sanding dust, oil-soaked rags, and applicators using approved fire-safe and waste-handling methods
  • Clean and store sanding equipment, applicators, and mixing containers
  • Confirm all photographs are labeled with their corresponding project-record entries
  • Store the approved sample piece or photograph with the project record for future reference
  • File the completed project record with the component's identity and cure date

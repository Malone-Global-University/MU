Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to repair the minor wall defects recorded on the Chapter 1 Room Inspection Worksheet — nail holes, dents, gouges, small cracks, and chipped edges — using filler or patch compound applied in thin, feathered layers, sanded with the least aggressive abrasive that corrects the surface, and cleaned of dust before the repair is approved for primer. The finished work product is a set of repaired wall defects that are flush, feathered beyond the original damage, free of ridges and sanding dust, and visually integrated with the surrounding surface under raking light.

You will classify each defect, confirm its underlying cause has already been corrected, remove loose material without enlarging sound drywall, fill and patch in thin layers, feather and sand each layer, match texture where required, remove all dust with HEPA vacuuming, and inspect the repair under raking light before approving it for Chapter 7 priming.

This chapter depends directly on the Chapter 1 readiness decision and the Chapter 3 cleaning results. Only defects already recorded on the Room Inspection Worksheet, and located on surfaces that have already been cleaned and dried, may be patched in this chapter. Chapter 5 caulks trim and wall joints only after this chapter's patches have cured. Chapter 7 selects and applies primer only after this chapter's repairs are sanded, cleaned, and approved.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-019 — Fill nail holes and shallow fastener defects using controlled pressure and a compatible filler.
  • PF-020 — Patch small wall defects — dents, gouges, small cracks, and chipped edges — with compatible compound applied in thin layers.
  • PF-021 — Feather patching compound beyond the defect so the repair blends into the surrounding plane.
  • PF-022 — Sand repairs using the least aggressive abrasive that corrects the surface without cutting through surrounding coating or paper.
  • PF-023 — Remove sanding dust from walls, trim, floors, and ledges using HEPA vacuuming.
  • PF-024 — Inspect wall smoothness under raking light and approve the repair for primer.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 identified and recorded every wall defect in the room. Chapter 3 cleaned the surfaces so compound can bond to a residue-free substrate. This chapter closes the gap between a documented defect and a paintable surface: every nail hole, dent, gouge, small crack, and chipped edge listed on the Room Inspection Worksheet is filled, patched, feathered, sanded, and cleaned here.

The feathering and sanding standard taught in this chapter — thin layers, least aggressive abrasive, no cutting through face paper — is the same standard Chapter 10 applies to door and trim fillers, Chapter 13 applies to wood-surface repairs, and Chapter 18 applies when diagnosing and correcting coating failures. A patch that is not properly feathered and sanded here will telegraph through every coat applied in Chapters 7 through 9.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • filling nail holes and shallow fastener defects
  • patching dents, gouges, small cracks, and chipped edges recorded on the Room Inspection Worksheet
  • setting proud fasteners or installing corrective fasteners where approved
  • sealing exposed drywall paper or porous damaged areas when the repair system requires it
  • applying patch compound in thin, feathered layers
  • sanding repairs with dust control
  • matching existing texture using an approved sample where required
  • HEPA-vacuuming dust and inspecting the repair under raking light

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • patch a defect whose underlying moisture, structural movement, or hazardous-coating cause has not already been corrected and cleared under the Chapter 1 hazard screen; that defect remains a stop-work condition
  • patch a structural crack, an area of active movement, mold, or water damage — these fall outside this chapter's scope and require the qualified-professional evaluation identified in Chapter 1
  • apply caulk to trim or wall joints; that work belongs to Chapter 5 and follows this chapter's patches
  • apply primer to a repaired area before it is fully dry, sanded, and cleaned of dust; that work belongs to Chapter 7
  • disturb any surface still flagged on the Chapter 1 Hazard-Screening Worksheet as pending lead or other hazardous-coating testing

Any defect that does not meet these conditions must be corrected, tested, or referred to a qualified professional before it is patched under this chapter.

Trade Note

A patch is a system, not a single application. Filler set proud of the surface, sanded flat, and left unfeathered will show as a hard-edged ring once primer and paint cross it. The feathering step in this chapter — extending each layer beyond the defect until it blends into the surrounding plane — is what makes a repair disappear rather than merely fill a hole.

Setup Standard

Do not begin patching any defect that is not already recorded on the Chapter 1 Room Inspection Worksheet, or that remains flagged as a stop-work condition on the Hazard-Screening Worksheet (Step 1). Do not approve a repair for primer (Step 20) until it has been sanded, HEPA-vacuumed, and inspected under raking light.

Shop Tip

Apply patch compound in the thinnest layer that will still build to a flush surface after drying and sanding. A thick single layer takes longer to dry through, is more likely to crack, and requires far more sanding to level than two or three thin layers applied and feathered in sequence.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every wall repair in this chapter follows the same sequence: confirm the cause is corrected, classify the defect, remove only loose material, set or correct fasteners, fill or patch in thin layers, feather each layer, dry fully, scrape ridges, sand with the least aggressive abrasive, match texture where required, remove all dust, inspect under raking light, and approve for primer. Skipping the feathering or dust-removal steps is the most common cause of a patch that telegraphs through the finish coat.

Thin Layers and Feathering

Patch compound applied in a single thick layer shrinks unevenly, cracks, and takes far longer to dry through than several thin layers. Apply each layer thin enough to dry fully within the product's stated time, and feather every layer beyond the edge of the previous one so the repair tapers smoothly into the surrounding surface rather than ending in a hard ridge.

Measurement Cue — Feather Distance

Extend each layer of patch compound at least beyond the visible edge of the defect and the previous layer, tapering the knife pressure toward the outer edge so the final layer leaves no detectable ridge under raking light. Confirm feathering visually and by hand before the layer is allowed to dry.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm the Cause Is Corrected Before Patching

Do not patch any crack, dent, or damaged area whose underlying cause — moisture, structural movement, corrosion, or an uncorrected fastening problem — has not already been corrected and cleared under the Chapter 1 hazard screen. Patching over an uncorrected cause hides the problem without resolving it and the defect will return.

Safety Cue — Dust Control While Sanding

Wear a dust mask during every sanding step in this chapter. Sand with controlled, even pressure and stop periodically to HEPA-vacuum accumulated dust rather than allowing it to spread across the work area.

Manufacturer Cue — Filler and Compound Selection

Select filler or patch compound compatible with the substrate and the finish system identified in the Chapter 1 plan. Follow the manufacturer's stated dry time, recoat window, and sanding readiness before proceeding to the next layer.

E. Cause Confirmation and Defect Classification

  1. Confirm moisture, movement, impact, and fastening causes have been corrected. Cross-check each defect against the Chapter 1 Hazard-Screening Worksheet and confirm no stop-work flag remains for the affected area.
  2. Inspect each defect and classify it as nail hole, dent, gouge, small crack, or chipped edge. Record the classification on the Defect Repair Log using the Defect Classification Schedule.
  3. Stop work for structural cracks, active movement, mold, or water damage outside the chapter scope. Refer any such condition back to the Chapter 1 stop-work process rather than patching it here.

F. Work-Area Preparation and Fastener Correction

  1. Protect the work area and establish dust control. Lay drop cloths beneath each work zone and stage the HEPA vacuum within reach.
  2. Remove loose coating, paper, compound, and debris without enlarging sound material unnecessarily. Use the putty knife to clear only the failed or loose material at each defect.
  3. Set proud fasteners or install corrective fasteners where approved. Drive proud nails or screws slightly below the surface without breaking the face paper; replace any fastener that will not hold.
  4. Seal exposed drywall paper or porous damaged areas when the repair system requires it. Apply the specified sealer or primer-sealer to exposed paper edges before filling if the product system calls for it.

G. Filling and Patching

  1. Select filler or patching compound compatible with the substrate and finish. Confirm the product matches the Defect Classification Schedule selection for each defect type.
  2. Fill nail holes and shallow defects with controlled pressure. Press filler into the hole with the putty knife, drawing the blade flat across the surface to avoid leaving excess proud of the wall.
  3. Apply patch compound in thin layers. Load the taping knife with a controlled amount and spread it evenly across the defect without overworking the material.
  4. Feather each layer beyond the defect. Taper the knife pressure toward the outer edge of each pass so the layer thins to nothing beyond the defect boundary.
  5. Allow each layer to dry or set fully. Observe the manufacturer's stated dry or set time before applying the next layer or beginning to sand.

H. Sanding and Dust Removal

  1. Scrape ridges before sanding. Use the putty knife at a low angle to knock down any hardened ridge or high spot before abrasive contact.
  2. Sand using the least aggressive abrasive that corrects the surface. Begin with 120-grit for initial leveling; move to 220-grit only for final smoothing.
  3. Blend the repair without cutting through surrounding coating or paper. Sand with light, even pressure and check the surrounding sound material frequently during sanding.
  4. Match existing texture using an approved sample where required. Apply the approved texture technique only after the base patch is sanded flat and only where the Chapter 1 plan specifies texture matching.
  5. HEPA-vacuum dust from walls, trim, floors, and ledges. Vacuum the repaired surface first, then surrounding trim, floor, and horizontal ledges where dust has settled.

I. Inspection and Primer Approval

  1. Inspect under raking light. Hold the handheld light low and to the side of each repair and check for ridges, depressions, or visible boundaries.
  2. Apply localized touch-up compound where needed. Fill any remaining low spot with a thin skim coat and allow it to dry fully.
  3. Resand, clean, and approve the repair for primer. Repeat sanding and HEPA vacuuming on any touched-up area, then record the primer-readiness approval on the Defect Repair Log.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every repaired defect in the room regardless of type.

  • Cause check: confirm no patched defect had an uncorrected moisture, structural, or hazardous-coating cause.
  • Flush check: confirm each repair is flush with the surrounding plane by hand and under raking light.
  • Feather check: confirm no hard edge or ridge is visible at the boundary of any repair.
  • Sound-material check: confirm no surrounding texture, paper, or coating was cut through or enlarged.
  • Dust check: confirm walls, trim, floors, and ledges are free of sanding dust before inspection.
  • Approval check: confirm every defect has a recorded primer-readiness approval before Chapter 5 or Chapter 7 begins.

Complete the Defect Repair Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 5.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Ring or Halo Appears Around the Patch After Priming

Probable cause: the patch was sanded flat locally but was not feathered wide enough into the surrounding plane, leaving a hard transition that primer and paint make visible.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area under raking light and compare the feather width against the Defect Repair Log entry.
Correction: apply an additional thin, wider feathered layer over the existing patch, allow it to dry, and resand using the least aggressive abrasive that levels the new layer.
Reinspection: reinspect under raking light and confirm no boundary is detectable before recoating.

The Patch Cracks After Drying

Probable cause: compound was applied in a single layer that was too thick to dry evenly, or the underlying movement cause was not fully corrected before patching.
Confirmation: inspect the crack pattern and re-check the Chapter 1 hazard-screening record for the affected area.
Correction: remove the cracked compound, confirm the movement cause is corrected, and reapply in thinner, properly timed layers.
Reinspection: allow full dry time and inspect for new cracking before sanding.

Sanding Cuts Through the Drywall Face Paper

Probable cause: an abrasive more aggressive than necessary was used, or excessive pressure was applied while sanding the repair.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area for fuzzy, raised paper fiber or exposed gypsum core.
Correction: apply a thin skim coat of compound to reseal the damaged paper, allow it to dry, and resand using lighter pressure and the specified starting grit.
Reinspection: confirm the repaired area is flush and the paper is fully sealed before HEPA vacuuming.

Dust Reappears on the Wall After Vacuuming

Probable cause: dust was vacuumed from the wall surface but not from adjacent trim, ledges, or the floor, allowing it to resettle.
Confirmation: inspect trim tops, window sills, and floor areas beneath the repair for visible dust.
Correction: HEPA-vacuum all adjacent horizontal surfaces, not only the repaired wall.
Reinspection: wipe the repaired area with a clean cloth and confirm no dust transfers before inspection.

Texture Does Not Match the Surrounding Wall

Probable cause: texture matching was attempted without an approved sample, or the technique was applied before the base patch was fully sanded flat.
Confirmation: compare the repaired area with the approved texture sample under normal and raking light.
Correction: sand the base patch flat, prepare a new texture sample matching the surrounding wall, obtain approval, and reapply texture using the approved technique.
Reinspection: compare the corrected texture with the sample and the surrounding wall before approving for primer.

A Repair Is Approved for Primer With an Uncorrected Underlying Cause

Probable cause: the defect was patched without confirming against the Chapter 1 Hazard-Screening Worksheet that its underlying moisture, structural, or hazardous-coating cause had been corrected.
Confirmation: cross-check the Defect Repair Log entry against the Chapter 1 worksheet for the same location.
Correction: withdraw the primer approval, remove the patch if necessary, correct the underlying cause, and repeat the repair sequence.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected cause and reissue the primer approval only after the repair passes raking-light inspection again.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with repaired, dust-free surfaces and a completed repair record, not a finished coating.

  • HEPA-vacuum all walls, trim, floors, and ledges a final time
  • Wipe down sanding tools and return them to storage
  • Dispose of empty filler and compound containers per local waste requirements
  • Confirm all photographs are labeled with their corresponding Defect Repair Log entries
  • File the completed Defect Repair Log with the Chapter 1 Room Inspection Worksheet
  • Flag any repair still pending texture-match approval before Chapter 5 begins

Field Terms

Feathering
Tapering compound progressively thinner beyond a defect so the repair blends into the surrounding plane.
Proud Fastener
A nail or screw sitting above the surrounding drywall surface that must be set or replaced before filling.
Least Aggressive Abrasive
The lowest-grit sandpaper that levels a repair without damaging surrounding sound material.
Sound Material
Wall paper, texture, or coating surrounding a defect that must not be enlarged or damaged during repair.
Raking Light
Light held low and to the side of a surface to reveal ridges, boundaries, and irregularities invisible under normal light.
Setting-Type Compound
A patch compound that hardens through a chemical reaction on a fixed schedule rather than by drying alone.
Skim Coat
A very thin finishing layer of compound applied to level or reseal a surface.
Texture Match
Reproducing the surrounding wall's texture on a repaired area using an approved sample as the standard.
Defect Repair Log
The document recording classification, materials, layer count, sanding, and primer-readiness approval for every repaired defect.
Primer-Readiness Approval
The documented confirmation that a repair has passed raking-light inspection and is cleared for Chapter 7 priming.

Performance Check

With every recorded defect repaired, review the completed Defect Repair Log against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every patched defect had its underlying cause corrected, every repair is flush and feathered with no visible boundary under raking light, no surrounding sound material was cut through or enlarged, all sanding dust has been removed from walls, trim, floors, and ledges, and every defect carries an individual primer-readiness approval.

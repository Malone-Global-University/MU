Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare and paint one interior door-and-trim assembly using a compatible primer and finish system. The finished work product is a fully coated door, casing, stops, and jambs with clean profiles, no sticking or coating damage at the moving joints, working hardware reinstalled after adequate cure, and a completed application and cure record.

You will confirm the door and trim are secure and clean, remove or mask hardware, scuff-sand glossy sound coatings, fill approved defects, prime where required, brush the panels, recesses, rails, stiles, casing, stops, and jambs in the specified sequence while keeping paint off hinges and weather seals, prevent the door from closing against wet paint, apply additional coats within the recoat limits, and reinstall hardware only after sufficient cure.

This chapter depends directly on Chapter 1's inspection and readiness decision, Chapter 3's cleaning and degreasing, Chapter 6's masking, and Chapter 7's primer selection. It applies those preceding chapters to one specific assembly type — an operable door and its surrounding trim — where coating buildup in the wrong place can cause the door to bind, stick shut, or fail to latch.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-055 — Prepare trim surfaces for finish coating, including cleaning, defect repair, and hardware removal or masking.
  • PF-056 — Sand glossy trim using controlled dust methods to promote adhesion without damaging the substrate.
  • PF-057 — Prime trim and door surfaces where required, using a compatible bonding or spot-primer system.
  • PF-058 — Brush trim cleanly, maintaining wet edges and profile-consistent coverage without sags or bridging.
  • PF-059 — Paint a paneled door in the correct sequence — recesses, rails, and stiles — without trapping wet paint against the frame.
  • PF-060 — Inspect the completed trim finish for coverage, adhesion, operation, and hardware function.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 finished the field surfaces of the room — cut-in edges and rolled walls and ceilings. This chapter finishes the operable and high-touch detail surfaces: the door, its casing, and its jambs. Doors and trim differ from wall surfaces in one critical respect — a moving assembly that is coated incorrectly does not just look wrong, it can stop functioning. A door painted shut, a hinge clogged with enamel, or a latch that no longer seats is a direct result of skipping a masking or hardware step taught here.

The preparation method in this chapter — clean, scuff-sand, fill, spot-prime — is the same method Chapter 11 applies to cabinets and Chapter 14 applies to exterior doors. Learning it correctly on one interior door-and-trim assembly here means every later door, cabinet, or trim project in this book starts from a verified, transferable sequence.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the door and trim are secure, adjusted, clean, and dry before coating begins
  • removing or masking hardware, and labeling doors and hardware where components are removed
  • cleaning grease, hand oils, polish, and dust from door and trim surfaces
  • scuff-sanding glossy sound coatings under dust control, and feathering chips and failed edges
  • filling approved dents and fastener holes and cleaning the resulting sanding dust
  • applying bonding or spot primer where required and sanding lightly if specified
  • painting door edges, paneled-door recesses and profiles, rails and stiles, and casing, stops, and jambs in the specified sequence
  • preventing the door from closing against wet paint and keeping paint off hinges, latches, weather seals, labels, and hardware
  • applying additional coats within the recoat limits and reinstalling hardware after sufficient cure
  • inspecting runs, sags, brush marks, sticking, edge buildup, coverage, and operation

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin work on a door or trim assembly that has not passed the Chapter 1 readiness screening, including hazardous-coating and moisture screening
  • strip or remove an existing finish down to bare wood; that work belongs to Chapter 13
  • coat an exterior door or exterior trim assembly; that work, with its weather and dew-point limits, belongs to Chapter 14
  • coat hinges, latches, strike plates, weather seals, or any other operating hardware, or seal a required clearance gap, drainage path, or ventilation opening with paint
  • close the door against a wet coating or turn a freshly coated door before the coating has dried per the product's stated dry-to-handle time

Any door or trim assembly with structural movement, active moisture, suspected hazardous coating, or an unresolved Chapter 1 stop-work condition must be corrected or cleared before this chapter's project begins.

Trade Note

A door is judged by two different standards at once: how it looks and whether it still operates correctly. This chapter's inspection step checks both. A finish that looks flawless but leaves the door dragging on the strike plate, or a latch that no longer seats because of built-up paint in the strike opening, is not an acceptable result even if the visible coating is otherwise sound.

Setup Standard

Do not begin surface preparation (Step 2) until the Chapter 1 readiness decision for this door and trim assembly is approved or conditionally approved with the relevant prerequisites met. Shop Tip

Paint a door flat whenever the hinge system and jamb clearance allow it. Laying the door across sawhorses or a flat support reduces sags and drips on the panel profiles, and it is far easier to keep a wet edge on a horizontal surface than on a hanging door where gravity works against you.

Common Field Fundamentals

Doors and trim are painted in a fixed sequence for one reason: recessed and profiled surfaces must be coated before the flat surfaces around them, or the wet edge from the flat surface will drag through the detail and leave brush marks across the profile. On a paneled door, that means the panel recesses go first, then the rails, then the stiles, then the edges — always working from the most recessed detail outward to the flattest field.

Coating Sequence and Wet Edge

Cut recesses and profiles first, then flat fields, always keeping a wet edge between the two so the brush blends fresh paint into fresh paint rather than dragging over a partially set edge. On casing and jambs, work the full length of one piece before moving to the next rather than coating short overlapping sections that create visible lap marks under side lighting.

Critical Safety Cue — Hazardous Coatings on Older Doors and Trim

Do not scuff-sand, scrape, or otherwise disturb any coating on a door or trim assembly that was flagged during the Chapter 1 hazard screening as requiring lead or other hazardous-coating testing. Confirm hazardous-material clearance for this specific assembly before beginning any sanding step in this chapter.

Safety Cue — Ventilation and Solvent Exposure

Provide ventilation appropriate to the primer and enamel in use before opening containers. Select respiratory protection based on the product's safety data sheet; a dust mask is not adequate protection against solvent vapor.

Field Safety Cue — Working with the Door Off Its Hinges

If the door is removed for flat painting, support it securely on sawhorses or a stable rack rated for its weight. Keep the removed door and hinge pins clear of the walking path, and label the door and its hardware location before removal so reassembly matches the original fit.

Manufacturer Cue — Bonding Primer and Recoat Windows

Follow the primer manufacturer's stated cure time before applying enamel, and follow the enamel manufacturer's stated recoat window between coats. Coating outside these windows can cause poor intercoat adhesion even when the surface appears dry to the touch.

E. Preparation and Hardware Removal

  1. Review substrate, existing coating, finish type, sheen, door hardware, seals, and recoat requirements. Confirm this against the Chapter 1 readiness decision and the approved finish and color schedule before touching the assembly.
  2. Confirm the door and trim are secure, adjusted, clean, and dry. Check that the door is properly hung and does not currently bind, drag, or fail to latch; correct hardware alignment issues, if any, before coating rather than after.
  3. Remove or mask approved hardware. Remove hinge pins, knobs, locksets, and strike plates where permitted; mask hardware in place where removal is not appropriate.
  4. Label doors and hardware where components are removed. Record each removed item's original location on the Hardware and Labeling Record.
  5. Clean grease, hand oils, polish, and dust. Degrease high-touch areas around the doorknob, kick area, and push points using the specified cleaner from Chapter 3.
  6. Scuff-sand glossy sound coatings using dust control. Sand lightly across the full surface to be coated, using the least aggressive grit that dulls the gloss, and control dust with vacuuming as you go.
  7. Feather chips and failed edges. Blend the edges of any chipped or failed coating area into the surrounding sound coating so no ridge remains.
  8. Fill approved dents and fastener holes. Apply the specified filler with a putty knife, slightly overfilling to allow for sanding shrinkage.
  9. Clean all sanding dust. HEPA-vacuum and wipe the door, trim, floor, and surrounding surfaces before proceeding to primer.

F. Priming

  1. Apply bonding or spot primer where required. Prime any glossy sound coating requiring a bonding primer per the finish schedule, and spot-prime filled defects and any bare repaired wood.
  2. Allow primer to dry and sand lightly if specified. Follow the primer manufacturer's stated dry time before sanding or recoating, and sand only if the product data sheet calls for it.

G. Door Face and Edge Coating Sequence

  1. Paint door edges in the required sequence. Coat the hinge edge, lock edge, top edge, and bottom edge before the door faces, keeping paint off the hinge leaves and strike-plate opening.
  2. Paint paneled-door recesses and profiles first. Cut the panel recesses and their surrounding profile with the sash brush before coating the surrounding rail and stile field.
  3. Paint rails and stiles in the specified order. Coat the horizontal rails first, then the vertical stiles, blending each into the wet edge left by the panel recesses.
  4. Maintain wet edges and brush with the grain or profile direction. Keep each section's leading edge wet until the adjoining section is coated, and finish each stroke in the direction of the wood grain or profile line.

H. Casing, Stops, and Jambs

  1. Paint casing, stops, jambs, and trim without bridging moving joints. Work the full length of each piece before moving to the next, and keep the coating out of the clearance gap between the door and the jamb or stop.
  2. Keep paint off hinges, latches, weather seals, and hardware. Check each pass against the masked or removed hardware locations before moving to the next section.
  3. Prevent the door from closing against wet paint. Prop the door open on a doorstop or wedge positioned so it does not touch a freshly coated surface.
  4. Allow the coating to dry before turning or closing the door. Observe the product's stated dry-to-handle time before any operation of the door.

I. Additional Coats, Cure, and Reassembly

  1. Apply additional coats within the recoat limits. Recoat only within the manufacturer's stated recoat window, and maintain the same coverage and sequence used for the first coat.
  2. Reinstall hardware after sufficient cure. Confirm the coating has reached the manufacturer's stated cure time for hardware contact before reinstalling hinges, knobs, locksets, and strike plates.

J. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect runs, sags, brush marks, sticking, edge buildup, coverage, and operation. Side-light the finished door and trim, and operate the door through a full open-close-latch cycle.
  2. Document products, coats, cure, and hardware restoration. Record the primer and enamel products, coat count, cure times observed, and confirmation that hardware was restored to its original, labeled position.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every door-and-trim assembly regardless of room or hardware type.

  • Sequence check: confirm recesses and profiles were coated before rails and stiles, and rails and stiles before edges.
  • Hardware check: confirm no coating is present on hinges, latches, strike plates, weather seals, or labels.
  • Bridging check: confirm no paint film spans the clearance gap between the door and the jamb or stop.
  • Operation check: confirm the door opens, closes, and latches without sticking or dragging.
  • Cure check: confirm hardware was reinstalled only after the manufacturer's stated cure time, not simply "dry to the touch."
  • Coverage check: confirm no holidays, thin spots, runs, sags, or brush ridges are visible under raking side light.

Complete the Door and Trim Finish Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before proceeding to Chapter 11.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Door Sticks or Will Not Latch After Reassembly

Probable cause: coating buildup on the door edge, jamb, stop, or strike-plate opening was not controlled during application, or hardware was reinstalled before the coating fully cured.
Confirmation: inspect the hinge edge, lock edge, and strike opening for visible film thickness, and check the recorded cure time against the manufacturer's stated requirement.
Correction: sand the built-up area to restore clearance, spot-coat only if the substrate is exposed, and allow full cure before retesting operation.
Reinspection: operate the door through a full cycle and confirm smooth movement and full latch engagement before closing out the project.

Paint Bridges the Door-to-Jamb Clearance Gap

Probable cause: the coating was applied too heavily near the stop or jamb, or the door was closed before the coating dried.
Confirmation: inspect the clearance gap along the full perimeter of the door for a continuous film connecting the door edge to the stop.
Correction: cut the bridged film free with a sharp blade along the clearance line and allow the area to cure fully before further operation.
Reinspection: operate the door and confirm the clearance gap remains free along its full length.

Brush Marks or Lap Lines Appear on the Door Face

Probable cause: the wet edge was lost between the panel recess and the rail/stile field, or between adjoining casing sections.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area under raking side light and compare against the coating sequence used.
Correction: allow the coat to fully cure, sand the affected area lightly, and recoat the full plane rather than spot-touching the lap line.
Reinspection: re-inspect under raking light after the correction coat cures.

Primer or Enamel Fails to Bond to a Glossy Sound Coating

Probable cause: the existing gloss was not adequately scuff-sanded, or dust from sanding was not fully removed before priming.
Confirmation: perform a tape adhesion test on the affected area.
Correction: re-sand the affected area to properly dull the gloss, remove all dust, and reapply the specified primer before recoating.
Reinspection: repeat the adhesion test on the corrected area before final coating.

Hardware Is Difficult to Reinstall or Does Not Sit Flush

Probable cause: paint was allowed to build up in a hinge mortise, strike-plate recess, or fastener hole during masking or application.
Confirmation: inspect the mortise or recess for paint film thickness compared to the hardware's original fit.
Correction: clear the excess coating from the recess using a sharp blade or scraper without gouging the substrate, and test-fit the hardware before final reinstallation.
Reinspection: confirm the hardware seats flush and functions correctly after clearing.

Cleanup

Complete cleanup only after the final coat has reached its stated recoat or cure time and all inspection items are resolved.

  • Clean brushes and tools with the method appropriate to the coating used, without pouring rinse water, solvent, or waste into a drain
  • Store leftover primer and enamel closed, upright, labeled, and within the product's temperature limits
  • Dispose of solvent-soaked rags and applicators in an approved fire-safe container
  • Remove masking from hardware and adjacent surfaces at the specified time and angle
  • Return removed and reinstalled hardware to its labeled, original position and confirm operation one final time
  • File the completed Door and Trim Finish Worksheet with the Chapter 1 readiness decision and finish schedule

Field Terms

Casing

Stop

Jamb
The frame members that a door hinges to and closes into.
Scuff-Sand
Light abrasion of a glossy sound coating to promote adhesion without cutting to bare substrate.
Bridging
Paint film spanning a moving joint or clearance gap, restricting or preventing operation.
Bonding Primer
A primer formulated to adhere to a glossy or otherwise low-porosity existing coating.
Recoat Window
The manufacturer-stated time range within which an additional coat may be applied for proper intercoat adhesion.

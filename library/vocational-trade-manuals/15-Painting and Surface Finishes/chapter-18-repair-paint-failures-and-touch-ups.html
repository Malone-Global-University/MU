Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to diagnose and repair a controlled set of coating failures — peeling, bubbling, and chips — and to correct brush or roller marks and blend touch-up work into a finished surface. The finished work product is a documented set of repairs in which every failure's cause was identified and corrected before any material was removed, sanded, patched, primed, or recoated, and every repair is visually and physically integrated with the surrounding coating.

You will record each defect with its location, substrate, coating history, and exposure; screen for active leaks, condensation, alkalinity, contamination, movement, corrosion, and incompatible coatings; test suspect older coatings for lead where required; remove only unsound material; feather sound edges; prime and patch as required by the failure type; match color, sheen, texture, and application direction; blend touch-up boundaries; correct brush and roller defects after full cure; and verify the repair holds under a repeated exposure to the original failure condition where practical.

This chapter depends on the substrate identification and coating-condition method from Chapter 1, the cleaning and dust-control methods from Chapter 3, the patching and sanding methods from Chapter 4, and the primer-selection method from Chapter 7. Chapter 19 uses the results of this chapter's repairs as part of the final quality inspection, and Chapter 20 applies this chapter's cause-based repair method to any defect discovered during the capstone room.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-103 — Diagnose peeling paint by identifying the failure interface and its underlying cause.
  • PF-104 — Diagnose bubbling paint by identifying the underlying cause before removing any material.
  • PF-105 — Repair chipped areas by stabilizing edges, filling the defect, and preparing it for finish.
  • PF-106 — Blend touch-up work into the surrounding coating using matched color, sheen, texture, and application method.
  • PF-107 — Correct brush or roller marks after full cure using sanding and recoating rather than repeated local touch-up.
  • PF-108 — Inspect the repaired area for integration, durability, and recurrence risk.

Where This Skill Fits

Every repair in this book, and every repair a working painter performs afterward, follows the same discipline: identify the cause before touching the coating, correct the cause, then repair only after the cause is gone. A patch or recoat applied over an uncorrected leak, an uncorrected contamination source, or an unresolved application error will fail again in the same place, often faster than the original coating did.

This chapter also teaches the boundary between a defect that can be spot-touched and one that requires recoating a full plane. Sheen and color differences between a small touch-up and a surrounding field coat are common, visible, and — once the surrounding coat has aged — usually impossible to hide with more spot touch-up. Chapter 19 relies on this chapter's judgment to determine whether a punch-list item is correctable with a touch-up or requires a full recoat before final acceptance.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • recording defect location, substrate, coating history, age, and exposure before any repair begins
  • screening for active leaks, condensation, alkalinity, contamination, movement, corrosion, and incompatible coatings
  • testing suspect older coatings for lead where age and condition require it
  • diagnosing peeling paint by failure interface: substrate, primer, intercoat, or topcoat
  • diagnosing bubbling paint by cause: moisture, heat, solvent entrapment, contamination, or application error
  • repairing chips by stabilizing edges and filling the defect
  • matching color, sheen, texture, tool, and application direction for touch-up work
  • blending touch-up boundaries using an approved full-section or feather method
  • correcting brush marks, roller lines, sags, or drips after full cure through sanding and recoating
  • inspecting repairs under normal and raking light and verifying durability where practical

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • remove, sand, or disturb any coating before the cause of failure has been identified and corrected
  • disturb any coating suspected of containing lead until the required testing and clearance are complete
  • repeat local touch-up on an area where sheen or color differences from the surrounding field are already visible; that condition requires a full-plane recoat
  • proceed past a stop-work condition, including an active leak, unresolved condensation source, or unidentified prior coating, without correction or qualified-professional evaluation

Any repair area where the cause cannot be confirmed and corrected must be flagged and referred rather than patched over.

Trade Note

A repair that looks correct on the day it is completed but was applied over an uncorrected cause is not a completed repair — it is a delayed failure. This chapter's troubleshooting section exists because the single most common repeat-repair call in the trade is a patch applied without first correcting the moisture, contamination, or application error that caused the original failure.

Setup Standard

Do not remove, sand, or disturb any failed coating (Step 6) until the cause of failure has been recorded and corrected (Steps 1–4). Do not apply primer or finish coat over any area where the cause has not been confirmed as corrected.

Shop Tip

Photograph every defect before touching it. A photograph of the failure pattern — the shape of a peel, the size and spacing of bubbles, the location of a chip relative to a door swing or a window sill — is often the clearest evidence of cause, and it disappears the moment you start scraping.

Common Field Fundamentals

Coating failures repeat when the repair addresses only the visible symptom. This chapter's method is consistent regardless of failure type: record the defect and its context, screen for the underlying cause, stop and correct that cause, then remove only unsound material, prepare, prime, match, blend, and reinspect. Applying that sequence out of order — patching before the cause is corrected, or priming before feathering is complete — produces a repair that fails again, often in the same location.

Diagnosing Before Disturbing

Every defect in this chapter is recorded and diagnosed before any material is removed. Peeling is classified by the layer at which separation occurred; bubbling is classified by its most likely cause; chips are inspected for the stability of their edges. None of these classifications requires removing sound material — they are made through visual inspection, gentle probing at the defect's existing edge, and, where required, the moisture meter and the adhesion test from Chapter 1.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspected Lead-Based Paint and Other Hazardous Coatings

Test suspect older coatings for lead based on the age, location, and condition factors established in Chapter 1 before scraping, sanding, or otherwise disturbing any failed coating. Do not proceed with removal until the required testing and controls are complete.

Critical Safety Cue — Uncorrected Cause of Failure

Stop repair work on any area where an active leak, ongoing condensation, unresolved alkalinity, contamination, substrate movement, or corrosion source has not been corrected. Do not patch, prime, or recoat over an uncorrected cause; the repair will fail again at the same location.

Measurement Cue — Record Cause Before Correction

Record the defect, its probable cause, and the confirmation method used, before recording the correction. A repair record that lists only the correction, without the cause and its confirmation, does not demonstrate that the underlying condition was actually addressed.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Lighting, and Footing

Use a stable, rated ladder or platform for upper-wall and ceiling repairs. Confirm adequate lighting, including raking light, before diagnosing or inspecting any repair. Keep the work path clear of tools, patching materials, and sanding debris.

Manufacturer Cue — Primer and Patching Compatibility

Select the patching compound and primer identified for the substrate and defect type in Chapter 7. Do not substitute a different product because it is on hand; an incompatible patch or primer is itself a cause of repeat failure.

E. Defect Recording and Cause Screening

  1. Record the defect, location, substrate, coating history, age, and environmental exposure. Enter every field on the Repair and Touch-Up Worksheet before touching the defect.
  2. Inspect for active leaks, condensation, alkalinity, contamination, movement, corrosion, and incompatible coatings. Use the moisture meter and visual inspection on and around the defect; record every finding.
  3. Test suspect older coatings for lead where required. Apply the age, location, and condition screen from Chapter 1; do not proceed with removal until clearance is documented.
  4. Stop work until the cause of failure is corrected. Mark the defect as excluded from repair until the identified moisture, contamination, structural, or application-error cause is confirmed corrected.

F. Peeling Diagnosis and Repair

  1. For peeling, determine whether failure is at the substrate, primer, intercoat, or topcoat interface. Gently lift an existing loose edge to identify which layer released from which.
  2. Remove only loose and unsound material using the approved controlled method. Scrape or lift failed coating back to sound, adhered material without enlarging the defect into sound coating.
  3. Feather sound coating edges. Sand the transition at the sound edge smooth so no ridge or step remains after recoating.
  4. Clean and dry the exposed substrate. Remove dust and residue and confirm the substrate is dry before priming.
  5. Apply the required primer. Select the primer identified in Chapter 7 for the exposed substrate and confirmed cause.

G. Bubbling Diagnosis and Repair

  1. For bubbling, determine whether the cause is moisture, heat, solvent entrapment, contamination, or application error. Puncture one representative bubble to check for trapped moisture or solvent, and record the finding.
  2. Remove failed bubbles after the cause is corrected. Do not open or scrape bubbles until the underlying cause is confirmed corrected.
  3. Patch and prime the area. Fill any resulting depression with compatible patching compound and apply the required primer once dry.

H. Chip Repair

  1. For chips, stabilize edges and fill the defect. Remove any loose material at the chip edge, fill with compatible patching compound in thin layers.
  2. Sand and clean the repair. Sand the cured patch level with the surrounding surface and remove all dust before finishing.

I. Matching, Blending, and Full-Plane Correction

  1. Match color, sheen, texture, tool, and application direction. Draw touch-up paint from the retained color formula and lot record; use the same application tool and direction as the surrounding finish.
  2. Blend touch-up boundaries using the approved full-section or feather method. Choose feather-blend touch-up only where the boundary will not be visible under normal and raking light; otherwise plan a full-section recoat.
  3. Correct brush marks, roller lines, sags, or drips after full cure using sanding and recoating. Do not attempt to correct a cured application defect with additional spot touch-up.
  4. Avoid repeated local touch-up when sheen differences require recoating the full plane. Recognize when a second or third spot touch-up in the same area signals that a full-plane recoat is required instead.

J. Final Repair Inspection

  1. Allow repairs to dry and cure. Observe the full recoat window for the products used before inspecting the finished repair.
  2. Inspect under normal and raking light. Check the repair and its boundary for color, sheen, texture, and surface-profile consistency with the surrounding finish.
  3. Repeat the cause condition where practical to verify durability. Where feasible and safe, reproduce the original exposure (for example, a controlled water test near a corrected leak location) and reinspect.
  4. Document cause, preparation, materials, blend method, and final result. Complete the Repair and Touch-Up Worksheet entry, including photographs, for every defect addressed.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every repair regardless of defect type.

  • Cause-correction check: confirm the recorded cause was corrected and reinspected before any material was removed or recoated.
  • Removal check: confirm only loose and unsound material was removed and sound edges were feathered, not cut through.
  • Hazard-clearance check: confirm lead-testing clearance is documented for any suspect older coating before it was disturbed.
  • Match check: confirm touch-up color, sheen, texture, tool, and application direction match the surrounding finish.
  • Blend-boundary check: confirm the repair boundary is not visible under normal or raking light, or that a full-section recoat was performed instead.
  • Durability check: confirm the cause condition was repeated where practical and the repair held under reinspection.

Complete the Repair and Touch-Up Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before treating any repair as final.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Peeling Returns in the Same Location After Repair

Probable cause: the patch and recoat were applied without correcting the underlying moisture, alkalinity, or contamination source that caused the original peel.
Confirmation: re-inspect the location with the moisture meter and review the original repair record for a documented cause correction.
Correction: identify and correct the actual cause before removing and repairing the coating again.
Reinspection: repeat the moisture screen and confirm a clear result before repriming the area.

Bubbles Reappear Shortly After Recoating

Probable cause: bubbles were removed and patched before the trapped-moisture, heat, or solvent-entrapment cause was corrected.
Confirmation: puncture a new bubble and check for trapped liquid or solvent odor; compare with the original diagnosis entry.
Correction: correct the actual cause — ventilation, moisture source, or application timing — before repatching.
Reinspection: allow the corrected area to dry fully and reinspect before recoating.

Touch-Up Is Visible as a Distinct Patch Under Raking Light

Probable cause: a feather-blend touch-up was used where the surrounding coating had already aged or changed sheen, making the boundary visible.
Confirmation: inspect the boundary under raking light and compare the touch-up sheen and color with the surrounding field.
Correction: recoat the full plane rather than repeating a local touch-up.
Reinspection: inspect the recoated plane under normal and raking light for uniform sheen and color.

A Repaired Chip Shows a Visible Depression After Cure

Probable cause: the patching compound was applied in a single thick layer that shrank during cure instead of thin, built-up layers.
Confirmation: inspect the patch under raking light and check depth with a straightedge.
Correction: apply an additional thin layer of compatible compound, allow it to cure, and resand level with the surrounding surface.
Reinspection: confirm the patch is flush with the surrounding surface before matching and blending the finish coat.

Removal of Failed Coating Extends Into Sound Material

Probable cause: scraping was not stopped at the sound coating edge, enlarging the defect unnecessarily.
Confirmation: inspect the perimeter of the repair for adhesion using the tape adhesion test from Chapter 1.
Correction: feather the enlarged edge properly, clean, and prime the larger exposed area; do not attempt to disguise the extra removal with a thicker patch.
Reinspection: confirm the feathered edge and full-primer coverage before recoating.

Repair Was Documented Without a Recorded Cause

Probable cause: the worksheet entry lists the correction performed but does not identify what caused the original failure or how that cause was confirmed.
Confirmation: review the worksheet entry for the defect and check for a completed cause and confirmation-method field.
Correction: return to the repair location, identify the probable cause using the diagnostic method for that defect type, and record it along with how it was corrected.
Reinspection: confirm the worksheet entry is complete before treating the repair as final.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, documented set of repairs and a filed worksheet, in addition to a physically clean work area.

  • Remove all scraped material, sanding dust, and patching debris using HEPA vacuuming or the approved method
  • Clean brushes, rollers, and mixing containers using the approved method; do not pour rinse water or waste into drains
  • Dispose of scraped coating debris, oily rags, and unused mixed product using approved fire-safe and hazardous-waste procedures where applicable
  • Confirm all photographs are labeled with their corresponding worksheet entries
  • File the completed Repair and Touch-Up Worksheet with the retained color and lot record
  • Flag any repair pending durability reinspection for follow-up before Chapter 19 begins

Field Terms

Failure Interface
The layer at which a peeling coating has separated from the layer beneath it.
Sound Coating Edge
The point at which surrounding coating remains fully adhered and does not lift under the adhesion test.
Feathering
Sanding a sound coating edge to a smooth transition so no ridge or step remains after recoating.
Full-Section Blend
Repainting an entire wall, door face, or trim run rather than a small spot, used when sheen or color differences would otherwise be visible.
Feather-Blend Touch-Up
A small, localized touch-up whose edges are worked out to nothing, used only where the boundary will be invisible.
Stop-Work Condition
An uncorrected cause of failure or a suspected hazardous coating that halts repair until corrected, tested, or referred.
Bubbling
Localized loss of adhesion causing raised, hollow areas in a coating, typically from trapped moisture, heat, solvent, or contamination.
Peeling
Separation and lifting of a coating from the layer beneath it at an identifiable failure interface.
Repair and Touch-Up Worksheet
The document recording defect, cause, confirmation, correction, materials, blend method, and final inspection result for every repair.
Durability Verification
Repeating the original cause condition, where practical, to confirm a repair holds before final acceptance.

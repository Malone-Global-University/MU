Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare, prime, paint, and inspect one exterior door-and-trim assembly under weather-exposed conditions. The finished work product is a fully coated, functioning exterior door and its surrounding trim, with complete coverage, preserved drainage and weather-seal contact, and documented weather and product conditions at the time of application.

You will confirm that leaks, rot, corrosion, failed flashing, and door-adjustment problems have already been corrected before coating begins; clean and dry the assembly; scrape and feather only sound coating; spot-prime bare substrate and stains; paint door panels, recesses, rails, stiles, casing, and trim in the required sequence; observe weather and recoat limits; and inspect the finished assembly for adhesion, coverage, drainage, and hardware function.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 10, which taught interior door and trim painting technique, and Chapter 7, which taught primer selection. This chapter applies those skills under exterior exposure, where weather, surface temperature, and dew point govern whether the coating can be applied or must wait.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-079 — Clean exterior door and trim surfaces of dirt, chalk, mildew residue, grease, and pollutants using an approved method.
  • PF-080 — Sand and feather peeling exterior paint using controlled dust procedures without damaging sound coating.
  • PF-081 — Spot-prime bare exterior wood, metal, composite, and stain areas with a compatible exterior primer.
  • PF-082 — Paint exterior door panels, recesses, rails, stiles, and edges in the specified sequence with complete coverage.
  • PF-083 — Paint exterior trim and casing while maintaining wet edges and required film coverage.
  • PF-084 — Inspect the weather-exposed coating for adhesion, coverage, drainage, weather-seal contact, and hardware function.

Where This Skill Fits

Exterior coating work carries the same substrate, cleaning, sanding, priming, and application principles taught for interior doors and trim in Chapter 10, but adds weather, surface-temperature, and dew-point limits that can stop the work entirely regardless of how well the surface was prepared. A coating applied outside its product's temperature or dew-point limits can fail even when every preparation step was performed correctly.

This chapter also introduces the exterior-specific rule that drainage gaps, threshold weeps, and weather-seal movement must never be sealed with caulk or paint — a rule that differs from interior trim work, where most joints are caulked for a clean paintable line. The next chapter, Chapter 15, extends these same weather and moisture principles to siding.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • cleaning dirt, chalk, mildew residue, grease, and pollutants from one exterior door-and-trim assembly
  • scraping and feathering sound, cleared peeling coating using dust control
  • spot-priming bare wood, metal, composite, and stained areas with a compatible exterior primer
  • sealing exposed end grain and joints designated on the finish schedule
  • painting door panels, recesses, rails, stiles, edges, casing, and trim in the specified sequence
  • observing weather, surface-temperature, dew-point, and recoat limits
  • reinstalling hardware after sufficient cure and confirming door operation
  • inspecting the finished assembly for adhesion, coverage, drainage, weather-seal contact, and hardware function

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin any preparation or coating step until active leaks, rot, corrosion, failed flashing, and door-adjustment problems identified in Chapter 1 or a current inspection have been corrected
  • scrape, sand, grind, burn, heat-gun, blast, or otherwise disturb any coating suspected of containing lead until required testing and controls are complete
  • seal drainage gaps, threshold weeps, or weather-seal movement with caulk or paint under any circumstance
  • apply any coating outside the surface-temperature, air-temperature, humidity, dew-point, or forecast limits stated on the product's approved data sheet
  • coat electrical devices, detector openings, hinges, latches, weather seals, or moving hardware unless the finish schedule explicitly permits it

Any assembly that fails the moisture, hazardous-coating, or weather-condition screening in this chapter must be corrected, tested, or postponed before coating proceeds.

Trade Note

Every weather reading — surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew point — is recorded on the work order at the time it is taken, not estimated afterward from memory. A coating applied without a recorded dew-point margin is the single most common cause of exterior adhesion failure identified in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cleaning or scraping until leaks, rot, corrosion, failed flashing, and door-adjustment problems have been confirmed corrected (Step 2) and the current weather forecast has been reviewed (Step 1). Do not apply any coat until the surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew-point margin have been measured and recorded within the product's approved limits.

Shop Tip

Check the forecast for the full recoat window, not just the moment of application. A coating that goes on within limits but faces rain, frost, or heavy dew before it cures can fail even though the application conditions were correct.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before recording any weather reading or beginning preparation, and use them consistently on the work order.

TermDefinition
Surface temperatureThe temperature of the door or trim substrate itself, measured with a surface thermometer, not the surrounding air temperature
Dew pointThe temperature at which moisture will condense on the surface; the coating manufacturer's required margin between surface temperature and dew point must be measured and recorded before application
Sound coatingExisting coating that remains bonded, with no lifting or flaking, confirmed by scraping and feathering only where loose material is found
FeatheringSanding a scraped edge so the transition between bare substrate and remaining sound coating is smooth and not detectable under the new finish coat
Drainage gap / weepAn intentional opening at a threshold, door bottom, or trim detail that allows water to escape; never sealed with caulk or paint
Recoat windowThe manufacturer's stated minimum and maximum time between coats, measured from the time each coat was applied

Common Field Fundamentals

Exterior door and trim painting follows the same clean-scrape-prime-paint sequence as interior trim work, with two conditions added: the substrate must be corrected and dry before coating begins, and every coat must go on within the product's surface-temperature, humidity, and dew-point limits. Skipping either condition produces a coating that looks correct at application but fails within one season of weather exposure.

Weather Windows and Recoat Limits

The approved product data sheet states minimum surface temperature, minimum air temperature, maximum humidity, required dew-point margin, and minimum and maximum recoat time. Record all four readings before every coat, not only the first. A surface that was within limits at the first coat can fall outside limits by the time the recoat window opens, particularly at dusk or during a change in weather.

Measurement Cue — Record Every Weather Reading Before Each Coat

Measure and record surface temperature, air temperature, relative humidity, and dew-point margin immediately before applying primer and immediately before applying each finish coat. Do not rely on a single reading taken at the start of the day to cover the entire application window.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspected Lead-Based Paint on Exterior Coatings

Do not scrape, sand, grind, burn, heat-gun, blast, or otherwise disturb any exterior coating suspected of containing lead until the required testing and controls are complete. Exterior coatings on older structures carry the same hazard-testing requirements as interior coatings.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Seal Drainage Gaps or Weather Seals

Do not caulk or paint over threshold weeps, door-bottom drainage gaps, or weather-seal movement joints. Sealing these details traps water against the door and frame and accelerates rot and coating failure.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Landings, and Weather Exposure

Use a stable, rated ladder or platform with a spotter for any work above the first step or landing. Stop work if wind, rain, or failing light make safe footing or accurate application impossible.

Code Alert — Exterior Exposure and Disclosure Requirements

Requirements for lead-paint disclosure, testing, and remediation on exterior surfaces vary by jurisdiction and by the age and occupancy of the structure. Verify applicable requirements before disturbing any coating on a pre-1978 structure or one with an unknown coating history.

E. Review and Cause Verification

  1. Review door material, existing coating, exposure, weather forecast, surface temperature, dew point, color restrictions, hardware, and seals. Record all findings on the work order before starting.
  2. Confirm that leaks, rot, corrosion, failed flashing, and door-adjustment problems are corrected. Do not proceed until this confirmation is documented.
  3. Test existing coatings for lead where required. Base the testing decision on structure age, location, and condition as taught in Chapter 1.

F. Protection, Cleaning, and Scraping

  1. Protect thresholds, siding, glass, hardware, weather seals, masonry, and landscaping. Mask and cover every surface not receiving the new coating.
  2. Clean dirt, chalk, mildew residue, grease, and pollutants using the approved method. Use the cleaning solution rated for the substrate and rinse where the product requires it.
  3. Allow the assembly to dry. Confirm dryness before scraping begins; do not scrape a damp surface.
  4. Scrape and feather only sound, cleared coatings. Remove loose material first, then feather the transition edge.
  5. Sand peeling edges under controlled dust procedures. Collect dust with a vacuum attachment or drop cloth; do not dry-sweep fine dust.
  6. Repair minor substrate defects. Use a compatible exterior filler for small dents, holes, or checking within the project scope.

G. Priming and Joint Sealing

  1. Prime bare wood, metal, composite, and stains with compatible exterior primer. Confirm surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew point are within the primer's approved limits before mixing.
  2. Seal exposed end grain and approved joints. Apply the specified sealant only to joints identified on the finish schedule.
  3. Do not seal drainage gaps, threshold weeps, or weather-seal movement. Confirm these details remain open after sealing is complete.

H. Hardware Removal and Door-Panel Painting

  1. Remove or mask hardware and labels. Label each removed component and its position for reassembly.
  2. Paint door panels, recesses, rails, stiles, and edges in the specified sequence. Coat recessed panel profiles first, then rails, then stiles, then edges.
  3. Paint exterior casing and trim. Move to casing and trim only after the door panel sequence is complete for the current coat.
  4. Maintain wet edges and required film coverage. Work in sections that allow the wet edge to be maintained across each component.
  5. Protect the door from closing against wet surfaces. Prop the door open using a method that does not mark the wet finish.

I. Weather Observation, Additional Coats, and Reassembly

  1. Observe weather and recoat limits. Record surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew point before each additional coat.
  2. Apply additional coats. Apply only within the manufacturer's stated recoat window and weather limits.
  3. Reinstall hardware after sufficient cure. Confirm the cure time stated on the product data sheet has elapsed before handling the coated surface.
  4. Operate the door. Open and close the door fully to confirm it does not stick, bind, or contact wet or under-cured paint.
  5. Inspect adhesion, coverage, runs, edge buildup, weather-seal contact, drainage, and hardware function. Use the Acceptance Standards table to confirm every item.
  6. Document weather, products, coats, and cure. Complete the weather log and product record before closing out the work order.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every exterior door-and-trim assembly regardless of substrate or exposure.

  • Cause-correction check: confirm leaks, rot, corrosion, flashing, and adjustment problems were corrected and documented before coating began.
  • Weather-compliance check: confirm every recorded application falls within the product's surface-temperature, humidity, and dew-point limits.
  • Sequence check: confirm door recesses and panel profiles were coated before rails, stiles, and trim.
  • Drainage check: confirm no threshold weep, drainage gap, or weather-seal movement joint was sealed or coated.
  • Hardware check: confirm hardware was reinstalled only after sufficient cure and the door operates without sticking or binding.
  • Cure check: confirm the door was not closed or handled against a wet or under-cured finish.

Complete the Exterior Door and Trim Work Order in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the assembly to normal use.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

New Paint Peels Within One Season

Probable cause: a coat was applied without a recorded dew-point margin, or moisture was trapped beneath the coating at application.
Confirmation: review the weather log for the affected application date and check for a recorded dew-point margin meeting the product's stated minimum.
Correction: scrape and feather the failed area back to sound coating or bare substrate, allow the surface to dry fully, and reapply only after confirming surface temperature, humidity, and dew-point margin meet the product's limits.
Reinspection: recheck the repaired area after the full recoat and cure window for continued adhesion.

Door Sticks or Binds After Reinstallation

Probable cause: hardware was reinstalled before the finish coat reached the manufacturer's stated cure time, or excess film built up at the hinge-side edge.
Confirmation: check the cure-time record against the product data sheet and inspect the hinge-side edge for excess film thickness.
Correction: allow additional cure time before reoperating the door, and sand back excess film at the binding edge without exposing bare substrate.
Reinspection: reoperate the door fully and confirm smooth operation before returning it to service.

Water Pools at the Threshold After Coating

Probable cause: a threshold weep or drainage gap was unintentionally sealed with caulk or paint during masking or application.
Confirmation: inspect the threshold and door-bottom clearance for blocked or coated drainage details.
Correction: clear the blocked drainage path completely; do not merely thin the blockage.
Reinspection: confirm water drains freely from the threshold detail before the assembly is considered complete.

Feathered Edge Becomes Visible Under the Finish Coat

Probable cause: the scraped edge was not sanded enough to create a smooth transition, or the primer coat was too thin to bridge the remaining ridge.
Confirmation: run a hand across the dried area and inspect under raking light for a visible ridge or shadow line.
Correction: sand the ridge further, reprime the area, and allow full dry time before recoating.
Reinspection: inspect the area again under raking light after the corrective coat has dried.

Finish Coat Applied Outside the Recoat Window

Probable cause: the primer or previous finish coat exceeded the manufacturer's maximum recoat time before the next coat was applied, reducing intercoat adhesion.
Confirmation: check the coat-application timestamps against the product's stated minimum and maximum recoat window.
Correction: follow the manufacturer's instructions for recoating outside the window, which may require light sanding or an additional bonding coat before proceeding.
Reinspection: perform an adhesion check on the affected area after the corrected coat has cured.

Rain or Frost Affects a Coat Before It Cures

Probable cause: the recoat and cure window was not checked against the full forecast, only the conditions at the moment of application.
Confirmation: inspect the affected coat for spotting, wash-out, blushing, or a soft, tacky film.
Correction: allow the coat to dry fully, then sand and reapply the affected area following the product's instructions for weather-damaged film.
Reinspection: confirm full cure and uniform sheen on the corrected area before final inspection.

Cleanup

  • Depressurize and clean brushes and mixing tools using the approved method for the coating type
  • Dispose of scraped-paint debris, sanding dust, and used rags in approved fire-safe or hazard-appropriate containers
  • Remove masking and protection only after the finish coat has reached the stated recoat or handling time
  • Return the surface thermometer, hygrometer, and other instruments to storage
  • File the completed weather log, product record, and photographs with the project documentation

Field Terms

Surface Temperature
The temperature of the substrate itself, measured directly, not inferred from air temperature.
Dew Point
The temperature at which moisture condenses on a surface; a required margin above dew point must be confirmed before coating.
Feathering
Sanding a scraped edge so the transition to sound coating is not detectable under the new finish.
Drainage Gap / Weep
An intentional opening that allows water to escape; never sealed with caulk or paint.
Recoat Window
The manufacturer's stated minimum and maximum time between coats.
Spot-Priming
Applying primer only to bare substrate and stain areas rather than the full surface.
Wet Edge
The leading edge of freshly applied coating that must be maintained to avoid visible lap marks.
Weather Log
The documented record of surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew point taken before each coat.

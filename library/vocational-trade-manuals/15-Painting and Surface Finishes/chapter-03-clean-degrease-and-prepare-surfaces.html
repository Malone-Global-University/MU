Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to clean and prepare a controlled set of walls, trim, and high-touch surfaces so that later patching, sanding, caulking, priming, and coating work bonds to a residue-free substrate. The finished work product is a set of dust-free, residue-free, dry surfaces with a documented cleaning record, ready for Chapter 4 patching and Chapter 7 priming.

You will remove loose dust, wash walls with an approved cleaner, degrease kitchen, bath, door, handrail, and high-touch trim surfaces, rinse cleaning residue where the product requires it, allow surfaces to dry fully, and inspect for residue, streaks, grease, chalk, soap film, and water damage before signing off on the surfaces as ready for the next chapter.

This chapter follows the Chapter 1 room inspection and readiness plan and the Chapter 2 protected work area. It precedes Chapter 4 patching, Chapter 5 caulking, Chapter 6 masking, and Chapter 7 priming. No coating in this book may be applied to a surface that has not passed this chapter's cleanliness inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-013 — Remove loose dust from walls, trim, and adjacent surfaces using HEPA vacuuming or an approved clean microfiber method.
  • PF-014 — Wash walls using an approved cleaner and controlled moisture without saturating drywall, wood, joints, cabinets, or electrical boxes.
  • PF-015 — Degrease kitchen, bath, door, handrail, and high-touch trim surfaces using the specified degreasing product.
  • PF-016 — Clean glossy surfaces before sanding so contamination is not driven into the coating, and remove wax, polish, silicone residue, smoke film, and soap film using compatible methods.
  • PF-017 — Rinse preparation residue where the cleaning product requires it, changing rinse water or cloths frequently until cleanliness is verified.
  • PF-018 — Inspect surface cleanliness using a water-break, wipe, adhesion, or other required check, and document cleaners, test areas, residue removal, and final acceptance.

Where This Skill Fits

Every patch, sanding pass, caulk bead, primer coat, and finish coat in this book depends on a substrate free of dust, grease, wax, silicone, chalk, and soap film. Coating applied over any of these contaminants fails to bond correctly, regardless of how carefully the later steps are performed. This chapter is the point in the sequence where that contamination is removed and verified before any further preparation begins.

The cleaning record produced here becomes part of the documentation Chapter 19 reviews during the final quality inspection, and it is the baseline Chapter 18 checks first whenever a coating failure traces back to inadequate surface preparation rather than a materials or application problem.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • removing loose dust from walls, trim, and adjacent surfaces before any wet cleaning begins
  • washing walls with an approved cleaner and controlled moisture
  • degreasing kitchen, bath, door, handrail, and other high-touch trim surfaces
  • cleaning glossy surfaces before sanding
  • removing wax, polish, silicone residue, smoke film, and soap film using compatible methods
  • rinsing preparation residue where the cleaner requires it
  • inspecting and documenting final surface cleanliness before releasing surfaces to Chapter 4

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin patching, sanding, caulking, priming, or coating any surface before it passes this chapter's cleanliness inspection
  • use a cleaner, degreaser, or rinse method not identified as compatible with the substrate and existing coating in the Chapter 1 plan
  • saturate drywall, wood, joints, cabinets, or electrical boxes with wash water or rinse water
  • proceed on any surface where cleaning reveals a stop-work condition — such as active moisture, suspected hazardous coating, or unresolved contamination — identified in Chapter 1 but not yet corrected or cleared

Any surface that fails the water-break, wipe, or adhesion cleanliness check must be recleaned or referred back to the Chapter 1 plan before this chapter's cleanliness acceptance may be recorded.

Trade Note

Clean before you sand, and clean before you patch. Sanding or patching over grease, wax, or silicone drives the contaminant into the substrate, where it will telegraph through every later coat. This chapter's sequence — dust removal first, then wet cleaning, then rinse, then dry, then inspect — is not interchangeable.

Setup Standard

Do not begin wet cleaning (Step 6) until the approved cleaner has been tested on an inconspicuous area (Step 4) and adjacent finishes and electrical devices are protected (Step 5). Do not release any surface to Chapter 4 until it has passed the final cleanliness inspection (Step 20).

Shop Tip

Work from clean areas toward contaminated areas, not the reverse. Starting in the dirtiest section and moving outward spreads residue into surfaces that were already clean, doubling the rinse-and-recheck work later in the chapter.

Common Field Fundamentals

Cleaning always precedes sanding, patching, priming, or coating. Dust removal always precedes wet cleaning, and wet cleaning always precedes rinsing, drying, and inspection. Skipping or reordering any of these stages leaves contamination that a later coat cannot bond to, no matter how carefully that later coat is applied.

Working From Clean Toward Contaminated Areas

Begin each wash or degrease pass in the least contaminated area of the room and work toward the most contaminated area. This prevents redepositing grease, soil, or residue onto surfaces that have already been cleaned, and it reduces the number of rinse-water or cloth changes required to reach an acceptable result.

Measurement Cue — Document Cleaners, Test Areas, and Acceptance

Record the cleaner and degreaser product used, the location of every test area, the method used to remove residue, and the final cleanliness-check result for every surface. A surface without a recorded final acceptance is not ready for Chapter 4.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Dry-Sweep Fine Sanding or Surface Dust

Do not dry-sweep fine dust from walls, trim, or floors at any point in this chapter. Dry sweeping redistributes fine particulate into the air rather than removing it, and it can spread contamination from a surface with an unresolved hazard screen. Use HEPA vacuuming or a clean, slightly damp microfiber method instead.

Safety Cue — Test the Cleaning Solution First

Test every cleaner and degreaser on an inconspicuous area before general use. Some cleaners dull sheen, soften certain coatings, or react with unknown existing finishes. Confirm compatibility on the test area before proceeding to the full surface.

Field Safety Cue — Wet Surfaces and Electrical Devices

Protect electrical receptacles, switches, and other devices from wash and rinse water. Avoid saturating any area around an electrical box. Keep footing dry where practical and clean up spills immediately to prevent slipping.

Code Alert — Wash and Rinse Water Disposal

Do not pour wash water, rinse water, or cleaning-residue slurry into drains, soil, or storm systems. Requirements for disposal of cleaning byproducts vary by jurisdiction and by product; verify the approved disposal method before generating wash or rinse waste.

E. Preparation and Dust Removal

  1. Review substrate, contamination, existing coating, cleaner, rinse, ventilation, and disposal requirements. Confirm the Chapter 1 plan identifies the approved cleaner and degreaser for each surface category.
  2. Remove loose dust using HEPA vacuuming or a clean microfiber method. Work walls, trim, and ledges before any wet cleaning begins.
  3. Do not dry-sweep fine dust. Confirm no dry-sweeping method is used at any point in this chapter.

F. Test Area and Protection

  1. Test the cleaning solution on an inconspicuous area. Apply the approved cleaner to a small, hidden area and observe the result before general use.
  2. Protect adjacent finishes and electrical devices. Cover or mask receptacles, switches, and finishes not scheduled for this chapter's cleaning.

G. Wall Washing

  1. Wash walls using the approved cleaner and controlled moisture. Apply the cleaner with a sponge or cloth, using only enough moisture to lift dirt without saturating the surface.
  2. Work from clean areas toward contaminated areas. Begin in the least soiled section of the wall and move toward the most soiled section.
  3. Avoid saturating drywall, wood, joints, cabinets, and electrical boxes. Wring cloths and sponges before applying them to porous or jointed surfaces.

H. Degreasing and Glossy-Surface Cleaning

  1. Degrease kitchen, bath, door, handrail, and high-touch trim surfaces using the specified product. Apply the approved degreaser and work it into textured or grooved areas with a soft-bristle brush where needed.
  2. Repeat cleaning until rinse cloths or test wipes show acceptable cleanliness. Change cloths as soon as they no longer lift additional residue.
  3. Clean glossy surfaces before sanding so contamination is not driven into the coating. Complete this step before any sanding scheduled in Chapter 4 or Chapter 10 begins.
  4. Remove wax, polish, silicone residue, smoke film, and soap film only with compatible methods. Confirm the method selected for each contaminant is identified as compatible in the Chapter 1 plan.

I. Rinsing and Drying

  1. Rinse surfaces where the cleaner requires it. Follow the product instructions for whether and how rinsing is required.
  2. Change rinse water or cloths frequently. Replace rinse water or cloths as soon as they show visible residue or reduced effectiveness.
  3. Allow surfaces to dry fully. Do not proceed to inspection until all washed, degreased, and rinsed surfaces are dry to the touch.

J. Inspection, Correction, and Documentation

  1. Inspect for residue, streaks, grease, chalk, soap, and water damage. Inspect under normal light and, for surfaces scheduled for a new finish coat, under raking light as well.
  2. Perform a water-break, wipe, adhesion, or other cleanliness check where required. Apply the check specified for the surface and cleaner combination and record the result.
  3. Reclean failed areas. Repeat washing, degreasing, or rinsing on any area that fails the cleanliness check, then repeat the check.
  4. Mark defects that become visible after cleaning. Record any newly visible defect on the worksheet with its location for the Chapter 4 patching plan.
  5. Document cleaners, test areas, residue removal, and final acceptance. Complete the Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet in full before releasing the room to Chapter 4.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every cleaned surface regardless of substrate or location.

  • Dust-removal check: confirm HEPA vacuuming or dry microfiber cleaning was performed before any wet cleaning, with no dry-sweeping used.
  • Test-area check: confirm every cleaner and degreaser was tested on an inconspicuous area before general use.
  • Saturation check: confirm drywall, wood, joints, cabinets, and electrical boxes were not saturated during washing.
  • Rinse check: confirm rinsing was performed wherever the product required it, with fresh water or cloths used until residue was removed.
  • Dryness check: confirm every surface was fully dry before the cleanliness check was performed.
  • Cleanliness-check verification: confirm a water-break, wipe, adhesion, or other required check was performed and passed on every surface, with failed areas recleaned and rechecked.

Complete the Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 4.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Primer or Paint Fish-Eyes or Fails to Bond After Cleaning

Probable cause: silicone, wax, or oil residue remained on the surface after cleaning, most often on a glossy or high-touch surface that was not fully degreased.
Confirmation: perform a water-break check on the affected area; beading or uneven sheeting indicates remaining residue.
Correction: reclean the affected area with the approved degreaser, rinse if required, allow full drying, and repeat the water-break check before recoating.
Reinspection: confirm a passing water-break result on the corrected area and on adjacent surfaces cleaned by the same method.

Streaks or Soap Film Remain Visible After Drying

Probable cause: rinse water or cloths were not changed frequently enough, leaving diluted cleaner residue on the surface.
Confirmation: inspect the surface under raking light for streak patterns and compare against the recorded rinse-cloth-change frequency.
Correction: rewash or rerinse the affected area with fresh water and cloths, changing them more frequently than the previous attempt.
Reinspection: reinspect under raking light and repeat the wipe check before marking the area accepted.

Drywall or Trim Shows Swelling or Soft Spots After Washing

Probable cause: the wash or rinse step saturated a porous or jointed surface instead of using controlled moisture.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area for softness, swelling, or discoloration and compare with the pre-cleaning condition recorded in Chapter 1.
Correction: allow the area to dry fully, reassess for damage, and refer any structural softening to the appropriate repair chapter before proceeding; adjust the wash method to reduce moisture on subsequent surfaces.
Reinspection: confirm the area is dry and structurally sound before including it in the cleanliness acceptance.

A New Stain or Defect Appears Only After Cleaning

Probable cause: surface dirt or dust was masking a stain, crack, or coating failure that the Chapter 1 inspection did not detect.
Confirmation: compare the newly visible condition against the Chapter 1 Room Inspection Worksheet to confirm it was not previously recorded.
Correction: mark the defect on the Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet with its location and classification, and route it to the appropriate chapter (patching, caulking, or stain-blocking primer) before coating.
Reinspection: confirm the newly recorded defect is included in the Chapter 4 or later preparation plan before proceeding.

Cleaner Dulls or Softens an Existing Sound Coating

Probable cause: the cleaner was applied to the full surface without first testing it on an inconspicuous area, and it is incompatible with the existing coating.
Confirmation: inspect the test-area result, if one was recorded, and compare it against the condition of the affected surface.
Correction: stop using the incompatible cleaner immediately, select an alternate approved cleaner, and retest it on an inconspicuous area before resuming.
Reinspection: confirm the replacement cleaner passes the test-area check before it is used on any remaining surface.

Cleanliness Acceptance Is Recorded Without a Documented Check

Probable cause: the surface was judged clean by visual inspection alone, without performing the required water-break, wipe, or adhesion check.
Confirmation: review the Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet for a recorded check result on every surface.
Correction: perform the required cleanliness check before treating the surface as accepted, and record the result.
Reinspection: confirm every surface entry on the worksheet has a recorded check result before releasing the room to Chapter 4.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with dry, documented surfaces ready for Chapter 4, plus a cleaning area that is itself clean and organized for the next phase of work.

  • Dispose of used wash water, rinse water, and cleaning cloths using the approved method; do not pour wash or rinse water into drains, soil, or storm systems
  • Rinse and store buckets, brushes, and sponges for reuse
  • Remove and store protective drop cloths used during cleaning
  • Confirm all photographs of newly visible defects are labeled with their corresponding worksheet entries
  • File the completed Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet with the Chapter 1 readiness decision

Field Terms

Loose Dust
Dry surface dust removable by HEPA vacuuming or a dry microfiber wipe, without water or cleaner.
Wash
Applying the approved general cleaner with controlled moisture to remove surface dirt and light contamination.
Degrease
Applying the approved degreasing product to remove grease, oils, and hand-contact residue from high-touch surfaces.
Rinse
Removing cleaner or degreaser residue with clean water or cloths, repeated until no residue remains.
Water-Break Check
Applying clean water to a cleaned surface and observing whether it sheets evenly or beads, indicating remaining contamination.
Cleanliness Acceptance
The documented result confirming a surface is dry, residue-free, and ready for patching, sanding, caulking, or primer.
Clean-to-Dirty Direction
Working a cleaning pass from the least contaminated area of a surface toward the most contaminated area.
Test Area
A small, inconspicuous location used to confirm a cleaner or degreaser is compatible with the surface before general application.

Skill Check

  1. Why must loose dust be removed by HEPA vacuuming or dry microfiber before any wet cleaning begins, and why is dry-sweeping never an acceptable substitute?
  2. Why is a cleaner or degreaser always tested on an inconspicuous area before general use?
  3. Why must wall washing avoid saturating drywall, wood, joints, cabinets, and electrical boxes, and what controlled-moisture technique prevents this?
  4. Why must glossy surfaces be cleaned before sanding rather than after?
  5. What is a water-break check, and what result indicates that residue remains on the surface?
  6. Why must rinse water or cloths be changed frequently rather than reused until visibly dirty?
  7. What must the learner do when a new defect becomes visible only after a surface is cleaned?
  8. Why is coating applied over a surface with grease, wax, or silicone residue likely to fail regardless of the application method used?
  9. Why must every surface entry on the Cleaning and Inspection Worksheet include a recorded cleanliness-check result before the room is released to Chapter 4?
  10. Why does this chapter's sequence — dust removal, then wet cleaning, then rinse, then dry, then inspect — control the order of work rather than allowing steps to be reordered for convenience?

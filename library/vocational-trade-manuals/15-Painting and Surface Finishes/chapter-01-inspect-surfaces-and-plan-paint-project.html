Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect an interior room or practice area, correctly identify every substrate and surface condition present, screen for hazards that must stop work, and produce a documented paint-project plan. The finished work product is a completed Room Inspection and Paint-Readiness Worksheet covering every wall, ceiling, trim run, door, cabinet, and adjacent surface in the room, along with a written readiness decision: approved, conditional, or rejected.

You will identify each surface's substrate, inspect walls and ceilings under normal and raking light, inspect trim, doors, cabinets, and built-ins for gloss and contamination, test existing coating adhesion, screen for active moisture and hazardous-coating conditions, classify stains by probable cause, select the required preparation and coating systems, calculate material quantities, and issue a documented readiness decision.

This is the first chapter in the book. No prior painting chapter is required, but every later chapter in this book depends on the inspection, hazard-screening, and planning method taught here. Chapter 2 protects and stages the room you inspect in this chapter. Chapters 3 through 20 all begin from the plan produced here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-001 — Inspect drywall and plaster surfaces under normal and raking light and record defects.
  • PF-002 — Inspect trim, doors, cabinets, and built-ins for gloss, contamination, and coating condition.
  • PF-003 — Identify stains and defects by probable cause using a documented classification method.
  • PF-004 — Identify existing coating condition through observation and an adhesion or compatibility test.
  • PF-005 — Select a coating and preparation system appropriate to the identified substrate and defect conditions.
  • PF-006 — Document paint-project readiness and issue an approved, conditional, or rejected decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every coating project in this book begins the same way: a documented inspection that identifies the substrate, the defects, the hazards, and the required systems before any product is purchased or opened. The hazard-screening and stop-work method taught here — for suspected lead-based paint, active moisture, and unknown coatings — applies without exception through Chapter 20.

The readiness decision you issue at the end of this chapter is the same document Chapter 2 uses to plan protection and ventilation, Chapter 7 uses to select primers, and Chapter 19 uses as the baseline for the final quality inspection. A project that skips this chapter's screening step has no verified basis for any later material selection.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying drywall, plaster, wood, MDF, metal, laminate, masonry, cabinet finish, door, and other approved substrates present in the room
  • inspecting walls, ceilings, trim, doors, cabinets, and built-ins for defects, gloss, and contamination
  • screening for active leaks, condensation, mold, substrate movement, and corrosion
  • screening for conditions requiring lead or other hazardous-coating testing
  • classifying stains by probable cause
  • selecting cleaning, patching, sanding, caulking, stain-blocking, priming, and finish-coating systems appropriate to the findings
  • calculating estimated surface area and material quantities
  • producing a documented, defensible paint-project readiness decision

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • scrape, sand, grind, burn, heat-gun, blast, or otherwise disturb any suspect lead-based paint or other hazardous coating; that disturbance requires the testing and controls addressed later in this book and, where indicated, a lead-safe certified contractor
  • begin cleaning, patching, priming, or coating any surface; those tasks belong to Chapters 2 through 20 and may not begin until this chapter's readiness decision is approved or conditionally approved
  • proceed past a stop-work condition identified in this chapter, including active leaks, condensation, widespread mold, structural movement, or unknown industrial coatings, without the required correction or qualified-professional evaluation
  • issue a final readiness decision for a room where a required hazard test has not yet returned a result

Any room, surface, or condition that fails the screening steps in this chapter must be corrected, tested, or referred to a qualified professional before this chapter's readiness decision may be approved.

Trade Note

Every finding in this chapter is recorded on the worksheet at the point of inspection — never reconstructed from memory afterward. A defect, stain, or hazard condition not written down at the point it was observed is the single most common source of missed preparation identified in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the room inspection until the approved finish schedule, color schedule, and any known room-use conditions have been reviewed (Step 1). Do not issue a readiness decision (Step 20) until every hazard-screening item in this chapter has a recorded result.

Shop Tip

Walk the room once before writing anything down. A first pass without a clipboard, looking only at overall condition and light direction, helps you plan where raking light will reveal the most, before you commit to a room-by-room inspection order.

Normal Light and Raking Light

A normal-light pass identifies overall condition, obvious damage, and gross contamination. A raking-light pass — light held low and to the side of the surface — reveals joint ridges, texture inconsistencies, nail pops, and surface irregularities that normal room lighting hides. Every wall and ceiling in this chapter must be inspected under both conditions; a raking-light pass is not optional for any surface receiving a new finish coat.

Measurement Cue — Record Findings at the Point of Inspection

Write every defect, stain, and hazard-screen result directly on the worksheet at the moment you observe it, with its location. Do not rely on memory to reconstruct the inspection after leaving the room. A finding recorded later, from memory, is a common source of missed preparation identified in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspected Lead-Based Paint and Other Hazardous Coatings

Do not scrape, sand, grind, burn, heat-gun, blast, or otherwise disturb any coating suspected of containing lead or other hazardous components during this inspection. Determine whether testing is required based on the age, location, and condition of the coating, and stop further disturbance of the affected surface until the required testing and controls are complete.

Critical Safety Cue — Active Moisture, Mold, and Structural Movement

Stop the inspection of any area showing active leaks, condensation, widespread mold, substrate movement, or corrosion. Do not proceed to defect classification or system selection for that area until the moisture source, structural cause, or contamination is corrected and reinspected.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Lighting, and Footing

Use a stable, rated ladder or platform for ceiling and upper-wall inspection. Confirm adequate lighting before inspecting closets, corners, and areas behind furniture. Keep the inspection path clear of cords, tools, and loose materials.

Code Alert — Disclosure and Testing Requirements

Requirements for lead-paint disclosure, testing, and remediation vary by jurisdiction and by the age and occupancy of the structure. Verify the applicable disclosure and testing requirements before issuing a readiness decision for any pre-1978 structure or any structure with an unknown coating history.

E. Document Review and Substrate Identification

  1. Obtain the approved finish schedule, color schedule, product requirements, substrate information, and room-use conditions. Record which documents were supplied and which were not.
  2. Identify each surface as drywall, plaster, wood, MDF, metal, laminate, masonry, cabinet finish, door, or another approved substrate. Record the substrate category for every wall, ceiling, trim run, door, cabinet, and built-in on the Room Inventory Schedule.

F. Wall, Ceiling, and Trim Inspection

  1. Inspect walls and ceilings under normal and raking light. Walk each surface twice: once under normal room lighting, once with a handheld light held low and to the side.
  2. Record nail pops, cracks, dents, holes, joint ridges, texture defects, stains, chalking, peeling, bubbling, and moisture marks. Note the exact location and approximate size of each finding on the worksheet.
  3. Inspect trim, doors, cabinets, and built-ins for gloss, grease, wax, silicone, damage, open joints, loose coatings, and hardware conflicts. Record each finding with its location.
  4. Inspect floors, windows, fixtures, appliances, and adjacent surfaces requiring protection. List every surface that will require protection when Chapter 2 stages the room.

G. Coating Condition and Adhesion Testing

  1. Test existing coating adhesion using the approved method where required. Apply painter's tape firmly to a representative area of the existing coating, press it down, and remove it in one steady motion; record whether the coating remains sound or lifts with the tape.
  2. Identify whether the existing coating is sound, glossy, chalky, stained, contaminated, unknown, or failing. Record one condition category for each inspected surface.

H. Moisture, Structural, and Hazardous-Coating Screening

  1. Identify active leaks, condensation, mold, substrate movement, or corrosion. Use the moisture meter on any surface showing discoloration, softness, or a musty odor.
  2. Stop work until active moisture and structural causes are corrected. Mark the affected area on the worksheet as excluded from this chapter's readiness decision pending correction and reinspection.
  3. Determine whether lead or other hazardous-coating testing is required based on age, location, and condition. Record the structure's approximate age, the condition of the existing coating, and any location factors (window and door surrounds, high-friction areas) that increase hazard-testing priority.
  4. Stop disturbance until hazardous-material clearance is obtained where required. Mark any surface requiring testing as excluded from further physical inspection — visual observation only — until clearance is documented.

I. Stain Classification and System Selection

  1. Identify stains as water, tannin, smoke, grease, rust, ink, marker, or unresolved. Record the probable cause for every stain found in Section F.
  2. Select the required cleaning, patching, sanding, caulking, stain-blocking, priming, and finish-coating systems. Match each identified substrate and defect condition to the system category that addresses it; do not select a single universal system for every surface.
  3. Confirm coating compatibility, sheen, washability, moisture resistance, exterior exposure, and cure requirements. Cross-check the selected systems against the approved finish and color schedule where one was supplied.
  4. Determine sample-board or test-patch requirements. Record which surfaces require an approved sample before full application, per the finish schedule.

J. Estimating, Sequencing, and Readiness Decision

  1. Calculate surface area and estimated material quantities using approved coverage information. Measure and record wall, ceiling, and trim area using the tape measure, and apply the coverage rate stated on the selected product's approved data sheet.
  2. Plan room sequence, ventilation, occupancy controls, drying, and recoat timing. Record the intended order of work and any occupancy or ventilation constraints that later chapters must plan around.
  3. Prepare a deficiency and prerequisite list. List every condition that must be corrected, tested, or cleared before Chapter 2 may begin.
  4. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected paint-project readiness decision. Approve the room only if every hazard screen is clear; issue a conditional decision listing exact prerequisites if selected areas require correction or testing; reject the plan if stop-work conditions affect the room broadly.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every surface in the room regardless of substrate or location.

  • Substrate check: confirm every wall, ceiling, trim run, door, cabinet, and built-in has one recorded substrate category.
  • Raking-light check: confirm every wall and ceiling scheduled for a new finish coat was inspected under raking light, not normal light alone.
  • Adhesion check: confirm the tape adhesion test was performed and recorded on every representative existing-coating area.
  • Hazard-screen check: confirm every applicable item on the Hazard-Screening Worksheet has a recorded result, with no blank entries, before the readiness decision is issued.
  • Stain-classification check: confirm every recorded stain has an assigned probable cause.
  • Decision check: confirm the readiness decision states approved, conditional, or rejected, with prerequisites or reasons listed for any conditional or rejected decision.

Complete the Room Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Defect Is Discovered After the Readiness Decision Was Issued

Probable cause: a wall or ceiling surface was inspected under normal light only, without a raking-light pass.
Confirmation: re-inspect the affected surface under raking light and compare the result with the original worksheet entry.
Correction: record the newly found defect, reclassify it, and reissue the readiness decision as conditional until the defect is addressed in the appropriate later chapter.
Reinspection: repeat the raking-light check on all remaining surfaces in the room before proceeding.

Coating Removal Begins Before Hazard-Screening Is Complete

Probable cause: the hazard-screening worksheet was left incomplete, or a surface was assumed safe based on appearance alone.
Confirmation: review the Hazard-Screening Worksheet for blank entries and confirm the age, location, and condition factors were recorded for every applicable surface.
Correction: stop any disturbance of the affected surface immediately, complete the required testing or professional referral, and do not resume until clearance is documented.
Reinspection: repeat the hazard-screening step for the affected surface and confirm the result is recorded before further work continues.

A Stain Reappears After the Room Is Later Coated

Probable cause: the stain was classified without identifying and correcting its underlying cause, such as an active leak or ongoing condensation source.
Confirmation: re-inspect the stain location for moisture, review the original classification entry, and check whether a moisture correction was recorded as a prerequisite.
Correction: identify and correct the underlying moisture or contamination source before any stain-blocking primer is reapplied in a later chapter.
Reinspection: reclassify the stain and confirm no active moisture source remains before recoating.

The Adhesion Test Was Skipped on an Existing Coating

Probable cause: the existing coating appeared sound by visual inspection alone, and the tape test was not performed.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for a recorded adhesion-test result on every representative coated surface.
Correction: perform the tape adhesion test before proceeding, and record the result even if the visual inspection already suggested a sound coating.
Reinspection: confirm the adhesion-test entry is complete for every representative area before issuing or reissuing the readiness decision.

Material Estimate Does Not Match the Room After Later Measurement

Probable cause: wall, ceiling, or trim area was estimated by eye instead of measured with the tape measure, or the wrong product coverage rate was applied.
Confirmation: re-measure the affected surfaces and re-check the coverage rate against the approved product data sheet.
Correction: recalculate the material estimate using the measured area and the correct coverage rate, and update the worksheet.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected estimate against the finish schedule before Chapter 2 orders or stages materials.

Readiness Decision Is Approved With Unrecorded Blank Worksheet Fields

Probable cause: the inspection was completed quickly and some worksheet fields, particularly hazard-screening entries, were left blank rather than marked "not applicable" or given a recorded result.
Confirmation: review the completed worksheet line by line for blank fields.
Correction: return to the affected surface, complete the missing inspection or screening step, and record the result before the readiness decision stands.
Reinspection: confirm every worksheet field has an entry before the readiness decision is treated as final.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, documented plan retained for the next chapter, not a physical work area to restore.

  • Return the moisture meter, adhesion-test tape, camera, and clipboard to storage
  • Confirm all photographs are labeled with their corresponding worksheet entries
  • File the completed Room Inspection Worksheet and Hazard-Screening Worksheet with the approved finish and color schedule
  • Flag any conditional or rejected items for follow-up before Chapter 2 begins
  • Store the readiness decision where it will be available to every later chapter in this book

Field Terms

Normal Light
Standard room lighting used for a first-pass, general-condition inspection.
Raking Light
Light held low and to the side of a surface so it grazes across it, revealing texture defects invisible under normal light.
Substrate
The base material of a surface, identified separately for every surface in the room.
Sound Coating
An existing coating that passes the adhesion test with no lifting, flaking, or removal of material.
Failing Coating
An existing coating that lifts, flakes, chalks heavily, or otherwise fails inspection or testing.
Stop-Work Condition
Any finding that halts further inspection or work on the affected area until it is corrected, tested, or referred to a qualified professional.
Adhesion Test
The tape-based method used to determine whether an existing coating remains bonded to its substrate.
Hazard Screening
The documented review of age, location, and condition factors used to determine whether hazardous-coating testing is required.
Readiness Decision
The documented approved, conditional, or rejected outcome of this chapter's inspection and planning process.
Room Inspection Worksheet
The document recording substrate, defect, condition, and system-selection findings for every surface in the room.

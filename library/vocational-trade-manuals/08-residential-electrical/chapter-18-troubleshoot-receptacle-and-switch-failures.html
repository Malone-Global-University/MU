Job Overview

This project qualifies you to diagnose and correct one controlled receptacle or switch failure on an existing residential branch circuit. The finished work product is one completed Troubleshooting and Repair Record: a confirmed symptom, a completed intact-cover test, a de-energized inspection of the affected device, a documented cause, a completed repair using listed terminal methods, and a passed functional retest.

You will record the reported symptom and affected loads; confirm the symptom with covers intact using a plug-in tester or normal device operation; rule out tripped GFCI or AFCI protection, switched receptacles, and upstream controls before assuming a device failure; de-energize and verify absence of voltage before removing any cover; diagnose a dead receptacle, reversed polarity, an open equipment-grounding path, or a faulty switch using de-energized inspection and testing; correct loose terminations or replace a damaged device; and restore power to confirm the repair with the same tester used to confirm the original symptom.

This project builds directly on the live-dead-live verification from Chapter 1, the receptacle and GFCI installation methods from Chapters 9 and 10, the switch and fixture wiring from Chapters 11 and 12, and the circuit-fault diagnosis method from Chapter 17. It prepares you for the Chapter 19 subpanel project and the Chapter 20 capstone, both of which require the same disciplined sequence: confirm the symptom, de-energize, verify, diagnose, repair, and retest.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-103 — Diagnose a dead receptacle by confirming line continuity and upstream connection condition in a de-energized circuit.
  • EL-104 — Diagnose reversed polarity by tracing grounded and ungrounded conductor terminations.
  • EL-105 — Diagnose an open equipment-grounding path by inspecting grounding continuity without creating a neutral-ground substitute.
  • EL-106 — Diagnose a faulty switch by isolating it and using an approved de-energized continuity test.
  • EL-107 — Correct loose terminations using listed terminal methods and required torque.
  • EL-108 — Verify the repair through a complete functional retest of the affected device and its upstream and downstream connections.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 17 taught branch-circuit-level fault diagnosis: opens, shorts, and damaged cable sections found through de-energized continuity testing along the cable route. This project narrows the same discipline to the two most common callback items in residential electrical work: a receptacle or switch that has stopped functioning correctly, and a device that tests unsafe even though it still operates. A dead receptacle, reversed polarity, an open ground, and a faulty switch are diagnosed and repaired at the device box rather than by replacing cable, but they are proven safe using the identical live-dead-live method from Chapter 1.

The habit of confirming a symptom with covers intact before removing anything protects both the learner and the occupant: it prevents unnecessary cover removal on a circuit that is not actually the source of the reported problem, and it establishes a documented starting condition against which the finished repair is tested.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • recording the reported symptom, affected loads, breaker status, recent work, and intermittent behavior
  • confirming the symptom with covers intact using a listed plug-in tester or normal device operation
  • ruling out tripped GFCI or AFCI protection, switched receptacles, timers, and upstream controls before opening any cover
  • de-energizing and verifying absence of voltage using the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure before removing any cover
  • diagnosing a dead receptacle, reversed polarity, an open equipment-grounding path, or a faulty switch using de-energized inspection and testing
  • correcting loose terminations or replacing a damaged device using listed terminal methods and required torque
  • restoring power and completing a full functional retest of the affected device and its upstream and downstream connections

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • repeatedly reset a breaker that trips, or bypass a tripped GFCI or AFCI device without diagnosing the cause
  • perform continuity or resistance testing on an energized circuit, or remove a cover before the circuit has been verified de-energized
  • reuse a damaged, overheated, or scorched device
  • substitute a neutral-to-ground jumper, bootleg ground, or unlisted connector for a missing or open equipment-grounding path
  • proceed past any stop-work condition identified in this chapter without correcting it or escalating to a qualified electrical professional

Any fault that cannot be positively diagnosed at the device box, or that indicates concealed cable damage, aluminum branch wiring, or a shared-neutral condition beyond the scope of this project, must be escalated to the Chapter 17 circuit-diagnosis procedure or to a qualified electrical professional.

Trade Note

Diagnosis in this chapter always follows the same order: confirm the symptom with covers intact, rule out tripped protection and upstream controls, de-energize and verify, inspect and test, correct, restore, and retest. Skipping the intact-cover confirmation step and opening the cover first is a common cause of misdiagnosis, because a tripped GFCI or a switched receptacle can look identical to a failed device until it is checked.

Setup Standard

Do not remove any device cover until the reported symptom has been confirmed with covers intact, tripped GFCI or AFCI protection and upstream controls have been ruled out, and the circuit has been de-energized and verified using the live-dead-live procedure at the target conductors. Work started before verification is complete must stop immediately.

Shop Tip

Photograph the existing terminations before disconnecting anything, even on a device you plan to replace. A photograph of the original wiring is the fastest way to confirm that the replacement device has been wired to match the original circuit function rather than an assumed one.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every diagnosis in this chapter follows the same order: confirm the symptom with covers intact, rule out upstream controls, de-energize and verify, inspect and test at the device, correct the confirmed cause, restore power, and retest. Skipping a step in this order risks either an unsafe cover removal on a live circuit or a repair built on the wrong diagnosis.

Distinguishing a Device Failure from an Upstream Control

A dead receptacle or switch is not always a failed device. A tripped GFCI or AFCI device upstream, a switched-receptacle configuration, a timer, or another control can remove power from the affected device while the device itself remains fully functional. Check every upstream control before removing a cover or concluding that the device has failed.

Measurement Cue — Confirm the Symptom Before Opening Any Cover

Use the listed plug-in tester or the device's normal operation, with the cover intact, to confirm the reported symptom before doing anything else. A symptom confirmed only by the occupant's description, without direct testing, is not a confirmed symptom.

Critical Safety Cue — De-Energize and Verify Before Removing Any Cover

Do not remove a device cover until the circuit has been de-energized and verified using the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure at the target conductors. Do not repeatedly reset a breaker that trips; a breaker that trips on reset indicates a fault that must be diagnosed, not defeated by re-closing the breaker.

Field Safety Cue — Working in Occupied Spaces

Notify occupants before interrupting power to the affected circuit, and protect the work area against entry, dropped hardware, and accidental contact while the device box is open. Restore lighting and other critical loads promptly if the diagnostic work extends the circuit's downtime.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Replacement Device Rating and Torque

Select a replacement device with the same voltage, amperage, and grounding rating as the original, matched to the conductor material and gauge present in the box. Tighten every terminal to the manufacturer-specified torque or approved torque method; a loose termination is one of the most common causes of the failures diagnosed in this chapter and must not be repeated in the repair.

E. Symptom Record and Intact-Cover Confirmation

  1. Record the failed device, symptom, affected loads, breaker status, recent work, and intermittent behavior. Write the reported symptom exactly as described before testing.
  2. Inspect for heat, discoloration, odor, cracked covers, moisture, or damaged cords. Record every visible condition found with the cover still in place.
  3. With covers intact, use the approved plug-in tester or normal switch operation to confirm the symptom. Do not proceed until the reported failure has been directly observed.

F. Upstream-Control Check

  1. Check for tripped GFCI devices, AFCI or GFCI breakers, switched receptacles, timers, and upstream controls. Reset any tripped device using its listed procedure and retest before concluding the device itself has failed.
  2. Do not repeatedly reset a breaker that trips. A breaker that trips again on reset indicates a fault that must be diagnosed under the Chapter 17 procedure before this project continues.
  3. Map the likely upstream and downstream devices from the circuit record. Identify every device that could be affected by the same cause.

G. De-Energization and Cover Removal

  1. De-energize and verify absence of voltage before removing covers or devices. Complete the full Chapter 1 live-dead-live test at the target conductors before touching the cover screw.
  2. Inspect the device box for loose conductors, failed backstab connections, broken pigtails, reversed conductors, missing grounding, damaged insulation, and overheating. Record every condition observed before disturbing any connection.

H. Diagnosis

  1. Diagnose a dead receptacle by confirming line continuity and upstream connection condition in the de-energized circuit. Test from the device terminals back toward the identified upstream connection point.
  2. Diagnose reversed polarity by tracing grounded and ungrounded conductor terminations. Confirm which conductor is connected to which terminal against the approved circuit documentation.
  3. Diagnose open grounding by inspecting equipment-grounding continuity without creating a neutral-ground substitute. Test the grounding path directly; do not bond the neutral to the box or device ground to mask an open path.
  4. Diagnose a faulty switch by isolating it and using an approved de-energized continuity test. Disconnect the switch from the circuit before testing its internal contacts.

I. Correction

  1. Replace damaged or overheated devices rather than reusing them. Do not reinstall a device showing scorching, cracked housing, or melted parts.
  2. Correct loose terminations using listed terminal methods and required torque. Retighten or reterminate using the manufacturer-specified method for the conductor and terminal type.
  3. Move approved conductors from unreliable push-in terminations to listed screw or clamp terminals when included in the repair specification. Do not leave a conductor on a backstab termination identified as the confirmed cause.
  4. Restore pigtails and grounding continuity. Replace any damaged pigtail using a listed connector and conductor matching the circuit gauge and material.

J. Reassembly and Functional Retest

  1. Verify box fill and conductor condition before reassembly. Confirm the box volume remains adequate with the repaired or replaced device installed.
  2. Mount the device and install the cover. Fasten the yoke square to the box before installing the cover plate.
  3. Restore power through the approved sequence. Remove the lockout only after the repair and reassembly are complete.
  4. Repeat the original functional test and use the listed receptacle tester where applicable. Confirm the same test method used in Step 3 now passes.
  5. Test upstream protection and downstream operation. Confirm GFCI or AFCI devices reset and trip correctly and that downstream outlets regain power.
  6. De-energize for any failed result, heat, arcing, noise, or breaker operation. Return to the diagnosis phase if the retest fails.
  7. Document symptom, confirmed cause, repair, and final test results. Complete every field on the Troubleshooting and Repair Worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed repair using the same checks regardless of which device or failure type the project involves.

  • Symptom check: confirm the original symptom was directly observed with the cover intact before any cover was removed.
  • De-energization check: confirm the live-dead-live test was completed at the target conductors before the cover was removed.
  • Diagnosis check: confirm the identified cause was proven by de-energized inspection and testing, not assumed.
  • Termination check: confirm every terminal is tightened to the manufacturer-specified torque with no exposed copper or trapped insulation.
  • Grounding check: confirm the equipment-grounding path is continuous without a neutral-to-ground jumper or bootleg ground.
  • Retest check: confirm the original test method was repeated and passed after restoration, including upstream and downstream devices.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Troubleshooting and Repair Worksheet is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Receptacle Tests Dead Even Though the Breaker Is Closed

Probable cause: a tripped GFCI device upstream, an open connection at an upstream junction or pigtail, or a loose termination at the affected device itself.
Confirmation: check and reset any upstream GFCI device first; if the symptom persists, de-energize, verify, and test line continuity back toward the identified upstream connection point.
Correction: reset the upstream GFCI device where applicable, or correct the loose or open termination using listed terminal methods and required torque.
Reinspection: repeat the intact-cover test after restoration and confirm both the affected device and any downstream devices regain power.

The Plug-In Tester Indicates Reversed Polarity

Probable cause: the grounded and ungrounded conductors were terminated on the wrong terminals during a prior installation or repair.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify, and trace each conductor from the device terminal back to its identified source using the approved circuit documentation.
Correction: reterminate the grounded conductor to the identified neutral terminal and the ungrounded conductor to the identified line terminal.
Reinspection: repeat the plug-in tester check after restoration and confirm correct polarity indication.

The Plug-In Tester Indicates an Open Ground

Probable cause: a missing, disconnected, or corroded equipment-grounding conductor or bonding connection at the box or device.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify, and perform a de-energized continuity test from the device grounding terminal back through the box and branch equipment-grounding path.
Correction: repair or reconnect the grounding path using a listed connector and pigtail; do not install a neutral-to-ground jumper or bootleg ground as a substitute.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity test and the plug-in tester check after restoration.

A Switch Operates the Fixture Intermittently or Not at All

Probable cause: a loose or failed switch terminal, an open traveler or switched-leg conductor, or a failed switch mechanism.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify, isolate the switch from the circuit, and perform a de-energized continuity test across its terminals in both positions.
Correction: correct the loose termination or replace the switch if the continuity test shows a failed internal contact.
Reinspection: restore power and cycle the switch through repeated operations, confirming correct switching and fixture operation.

A Downstream GFCI-Protected Outlet Loses Power After the Repair

Probable cause: the line and load conductors at the GFCI device were interchanged during the repair, or the downstream connection was not restored to its original terminals.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify, and inspect the GFCI device's line and load terminal connections against the approved circuit documentation.
Correction: correct the line and load conductor connections to match the approved design; do not interchange line and load conductors.
Reinspection: restore power, reset the GFCI device, and test each downstream outlet for power and correct trip and reset function.

The Device Shows Heat, Arcing, or an Abnormal Odor After Restoration

Probable cause: an undiagnosed loose termination, a damaged device that was not identified for replacement, or an incompatible or damaged conductor.
Confirmation: de-energize immediately and inspect the device, terminations, and conductors for scorching, melted insulation, or discoloration.
Correction: replace the damaged device or conductor rather than reusing it, and correct any termination not tightened to the manufacturer-specified torque.
Reinspection: repeat the full de-energized inspection, correct any remaining defect, restore power, and repeat the functional retest.

Cleanup

This project ends with the affected device restored to power, tested, and documented, and the work area returned to its normal condition.

  • Confirm the cover plate is installed with no gap exposing energized parts
  • Confirm the affected device and all upstream and downstream devices have been retested and pass

  • Dispose of any replaced device or damaged conductor according to the property owner's or site's disposal procedure
  • Store the Troubleshooting and Repair Worksheet with the circuit map and panel directory for future reference

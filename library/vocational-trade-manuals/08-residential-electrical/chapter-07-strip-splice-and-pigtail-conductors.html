Job Overview

This project qualifies you to prepare, splice, and pigtail a de-energized conductor set inside one approved electrical box using listed wire connectors. The finished work product is one completed box assembly: every ungrounded, grounded, equipment-grounding, traveler, switched, and control conductor identified, stripped without damage, connected using a listed connector matched to the conductor combination, pigtailed to the assigned device or grounding point where required, folded into the box without insulation damage, and verified against the calculated box-fill allowance.

You will verify the circuit is de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Project 01; inspect the box for adequate volume, grounding provisions, cable entries, and conductor length; identify every conductor by function using the approved circuit documentation; strip each conductor to the connector-controlled length without nicking or cutting strands; install listed connectors using their approved method; build device and grounding pigtails; perform an individual-conductor pull check on every splice; and fold the finished conductor set into the box while confirming the calculated box-fill allowance is not exceeded.

This project builds directly on the box installation skills from Projects 03 and 04, the connector and cable-support skills from Project 05, and the cable-routing skills from Project 06. It is the direct prerequisite for bonding and grounding in Project 08, and for every device, switch, and fixture termination taught in Projects 9 through 15 and assembled again in the Project 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-037 — Strip conductors to the connector-controlled length without nicking, scoring, or cutting strands.
  • EL-038 — Make mechanical splices using listed wire connectors matched to the conductor combination and installation method.
  • EL-039 — Use wire connectors correctly, including twist-on, lever, push-in, and crimp types, according to their listed instructions.
  • EL-040 — Create device and equipment-grounding pigtails of the correct material, gauge, length, and identification.
  • EL-041 — Fold conductors into the box using controlled bends while preserving bending space and verified box fill.
  • EL-042 — Inspect splice quality, including pull-check results, exposed copper, insulation damage, and box-fill compliance.

Where This Skill Fits

Every device installed later in this book — the duplex receptacle in Project 09, the GFCI receptacle in Project 10, the switch and fixture wiring in Projects 11 through 14, and the capstone practice wall in Project 20 — depends on the splicing and pigtailing skill taught here. A loose connector, a nicked conductor, or an overfilled box created in this project will surface later as a failed device, an intermittent connection, or a failed inspection.

The identification discipline used here — labeling ungrounded, grounded, equipment-grounding, traveler, switched, and control conductors before connecting anything — is the same discipline the three-way switching circuit in Project 12 and the subpanel work in Project 19 depend on.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • verifying the circuit is de-energized before opening any box or handling any conductor
  • identifying conductor function using the approved circuit documentation before stripping or connecting anything
  • stripping conductors to the connector-controlled length without insulation or strand damage
  • installing listed wire connectors using their approved twist, lever, push-in, or crimp method
  • building device and equipment-grounding pigtails of the correct material, gauge, length, and identification
  • performing the individual-conductor pull check on every splice
  • folding the finished conductor set into the box without insulation damage, and verifying box fill

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • splice or handle conductors on a circuit that has not been verified de-energized by the live-dead-live procedure
  • reuse a single-use connector or exceed a connector's listed conductor range
  • mix aluminum and copper conductors or components unless the exact connector and procedure are listed for the combination
  • energize the completed splice set before the box-fill, grounding, and connection inspection required by this project is complete

Any box, conductor, or connector condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected before the box is closed or the circuit is energized.

Trade Note

A splice is only as reliable as the strip. A nicked or scored conductor concentrates mechanical stress at the nick, and that conductor can fail months later under normal thermal cycling even though the connector was installed correctly. Inspect every stripped conductor under adequate light before it goes into a connector, not after.

Setup Standard

Do not strip, cut, or connect any conductor until the circuit has been verified de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Project 01 and every conductor in the box has been identified by function. Work started before identification is complete must stop immediately and restart from conductor identification.

Shop Tip

Label conductors whose function is not obvious the moment you identify them, before you strip anything. A conductor set that looks identical after stripping is far harder to re-identify correctly than one labeled while the circuit documentation is still in hand.

Conductor Function, Connector Selection, and Pigtailing

Identify every conductor's function from the approved circuit documentation before stripping anything. A splice made before identification is complete risks joining a switched leg to a grounded conductor, or omitting a required equipment-grounding pigtail. Select the connector listed for the exact conductor material, gauge range, and count present at the splice, and never place more conductors under a connector or terminal than it is listed to accept.

Measurement Cue — Strip to the Connector-Controlled Length, Not by Eye

Use the stripping opening or gauge setting matched to the conductor size, and expose only the length specified by the connector manufacturer's instructions. A strip that is too short leaves insulation trapped under the connector; a strip that is too long leaves exposed copper beyond the connector body.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify De-Energized Before Handling Any Conductor

Repeat the live-dead-live test from Project 01 at this box before stripping, splicing, or pigtailing any conductor. Do not assume the circuit remains de-energized because it was verified earlier in the project; re-verify at this box before conductors are handled.

Field Safety Cue — Adequate Light Before Every Splice

Inspect every stripped conductor under adequate light before it enters a connector. A nick, score, or partially cut strand is difficult to see in poor lighting and becomes invisible once the connector is installed.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Connector Range and Installation Method

Install each connector using its listed twist, lever, push-in, or crimp method exactly as specified. Do not reuse a single-use connector or exceed its listed conductor range. A connector installed outside its listed range or method is a stop-work condition for that splice, not a splice to be closed into the box anyway.

E. Verification and Box Inspection

  1. Review conductor material, insulation type, gauge, circuit function, connector listing, pigtail size, box fill, and terminal requirements. Confirm every value against the approved circuit documentation before proceeding.
  2. Verify the circuit is de-energized using the approved live-dead-live procedure. Repeat the full test from Project 01 at this box.
  3. Inspect the box for adequate volume, grounding, cable entries, damage, and conductor length. Confirm the box condition from Project 03, 04, or 05 remains intact.

F. Conductor Identification

  1. Identify ungrounded, grounded, equipment-grounding, traveler, switched, and control conductors by the approved circuit documentation. Do not rely on conductor color alone.
  2. Label conductors whose function is not obvious. Apply labels before stripping begins.

G. Stripping

  1. Cut damaged ends back only when sufficient required free length remains. Confirm remaining length meets the box's free-conductor requirement before cutting.
  2. Select the correct stripping opening or tool setting for the conductor. Match the tool setting to the conductor gauge before making the first cut.
  3. Strip the manufacturer-controlled length without nicking, scoring, or cutting strands. Remove insulation cleanly in a single controlled motion.
  4. Inspect every stripped conductor under adequate light. Reject and re-strip any conductor showing a nick, score, or cut strand.

H. Splicing

  1. Prepare equal-gauge or approved mixed-gauge conductors for the listed connector. Confirm the connector's listed range covers the exact combination present.
  2. Align conductor ends as required by the connector instructions. Match the alignment method to the connector type.
  3. Install the connector using its listed twist, lever, push-in, or other controlled method. Follow the manufacturer's exact installation sequence.
  4. Do not reuse single-use connectors or exceed their conductor range. Discard any connector that has already been used once if it is not listed for reuse.

I. Pigtailing

  1. Create pigtails of the correct material, gauge, length, and identification. Match the pigtail to the branch-circuit conductor exactly.
  2. Connect one device pigtail to the branch conductors rather than using the device as an unintended pass-through where the project requires pigtailing. Confirm the device is never the sole path for downstream conductors when pigtailing is required.
  3. Prepare equipment-grounding pigtails for metal-box and device bonding. Build a separate pigtail for the box and, where required, a separate pigtail for the device.

J. Verification, Folding, and Documentation

  1. Perform the approved individual-conductor pull check. Pull on each conductor in every completed splice individually to confirm it cannot be withdrawn.
  2. Inspect for exposed copper beyond the connector, loose strands, damaged insulation, or mixed incompatible conductors. Correct any splice that fails this inspection before continuing.
  3. Arrange splices behind device conductors while preserving bending space. Confirm the finished arrangement will not obstruct later device installation.
  4. Fold conductors into the box using controlled bends rather than force. Avoid sharp bends or forcing conductors into position.
  5. Verify box fill after all connectors, pigtails, clamps, grounds, and devices are accounted for. Confirm the calculated allowance from Project 03 is not exceeded.
  6. Install a blank cover or proceed to the assigned device installation. Match this step to the project's current stage.
  7. Record connector type, conductor combination, box-fill result, and inspection findings. Complete every field on the Conductor and Splice Record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed splice and pigtail set using the same checks regardless of which box or circuit the project involves.

  • De-energized check: confirm the live-dead-live test was repeated at this box before any conductor was handled.
  • Identification check: confirm every conductor's function was confirmed by circuit documentation before stripping.
  • Strip check: confirm every stripped conductor shows no nicking, scoring, or cut strands under adequate light.
  • Connector check: confirm each connector is listed for the exact conductor combination and installed by its listed method.
  • Pigtail check: confirm device and grounding pigtails are present wherever required and correctly identified.
  • Pull-check: confirm every conductor in every splice was individually pulled and could not be withdrawn.
  • Box-fill check: confirm the calculated allowance is not exceeded after all components are accounted for.

Complete the Conductor and Splice Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 08.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Conductor Pulls Free During the Individual Pull Check

Probable cause: the connector was not fully seated, the strip length was incorrect, or the connector's listed range did not match the conductor combination.
Confirmation: remove the connector, inspect the conductor ends for correct strip length, and compare the connector's listed range against the actual conductor gauge and count.
Correction: re-strip if needed, select a connector rated for the actual combination, and reinstall using the listed method.
Reinspection: repeat the individual-conductor pull check on the new splice.

Exposed Copper Remains Visible Beyond the Connector Body

Probable cause: the conductor was stripped longer than the connector-controlled length, or the connector was not seated fully over the insulation line.
Confirmation: measure the exposed copper against the connector's listed strip length.
Correction: remove the connector, trim the conductor end if excess length permits, or replace the conductor if insufficient free length remains, then re-strip to the correct length and reinstall.
Reinspection: inspect under adequate light and repeat the pull check.

A Stripped Conductor Shows a Nick, Score, or Cut Strand

Probable cause: the wrong stripper gauge opening was used, or excessive force was applied during stripping.
Confirmation: inspect the conductor under adequate light and compare against the stripper's gauge markings.
Correction: cut back the damaged section if sufficient free length remains and re-strip using the correct gauge opening; if insufficient length remains, replace the conductor.
Reinspection: inspect the new strip under adequate light before installing the connector.

The Box Exceeds Its Calculated Fill Allowance

Probable cause: too many conductors, connectors, or devices for the box's marked volume, or pigtails were not accounted for in the original calculation.
Confirmation: recalculate box fill using all connectors, pigtails, clamps, grounds, and devices actually present.
Correction: install a larger listed box, reduce the pigtail count where the design allows, or relocate a splice to an approved junction point.
Reinspection: repeat the box-fill calculation and confirm the result meets the acceptance standard before closing the box.

A Conductor's Function Cannot Be Confirmed from the Circuit Documentation

Probable cause: incomplete circuit mapping, an unlabeled conductor, or a discrepancy between the documentation and the actual cable count.
Confirmation: compare the box against the circuit map from Project 02 and trace the conductor back toward its source if necessary.
Correction: do not strip or splice an unidentified conductor; escalate to the circuit documentation record or a qualified electrical professional before proceeding.
Reinspection: repeat conductor identification for the full box before resuming stripping or splicing.

The Grounded Conductor Was Connected to a Device Terminal Not Intended for Pass-Through

Probable cause: pigtailing was skipped and the device was used as an unintended splice point.
Confirmation: compare the actual connection against the approved wiring diagram and the pigtail requirement stated in this project's specification.
Correction: disconnect the device, install the required pigtail, and reconnect the device to its own pigtail only.
Reinspection: repeat the pull check and box-fill verification after the correction.

Cleanup

This project ends with the box's conductor set completed, verified, and either closed with a blank cover or ready for the assigned device installation.

  • Confirm every splice has passed the individual-conductor pull check
  • Confirm the box remains within its calculated fill allowance
  • Store unused connectors and pigtail stock in a protected, organized condition
  • Confirm conductor labels remain legible and attached where identification was required
  • Store the Conductor and Splice Record with the project's other documentation for reference by later projects

Field Terms

Pigtail
A short conductor connecting the branch-circuit splice to a single device or grounding point, used instead of the device serving as an unintended pass-through.
Connector-Controlled Strip Length
The exact length of exposed conductor required by the listed connector's instructions.
Individual-Conductor Pull Check
Pulling on each conductor in a completed splice individually to confirm it cannot be withdrawn from the connector.
Box Fill
The calculated volume allowance for a box based on conductors, connectors, pigtails, clamps, grounds, and devices present.
Equipment-Grounding Pigtail
A pigtail connecting the equipment-grounding conductor to a metal box or device grounding terminal.
Single-Use Connector
A connector not listed for reuse once installed and removed.
Traveler Conductor
A conductor carrying switching information between two three-way or four-way switches, identified by function rather than color alone.
Conductor and Splice Record
The document recording connector type, conductor combination, box-fill result, and inspection findings for a completed splice set.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to assemble one complete, indexed residential project closeout package: the record set the owner will rely on for permits, warranties, maintenance, and future work, and the record set the contractor relies on to demonstrate that the contracted scope was completed and properly closed. The finished work product is one reviewed, corrected, and delivered closeout package, supported by a closeout deliverable matrix and a closeout log tracking every required item from requested through delivered.

You will review contract, permit, lender, insurer, utility, manufacturer, and company closeout requirements; collect approved permits and inspection reports; collect warranties, operation and maintenance manuals, and product data; organize as-built markups and record documents; inventory spare materials and closeout hardware; collect training records and final photographs; collect financial closeout documents; index the package by owner use; review every document for correctness and completeness; and prepare, test, and deliver the finished package.

This project depends on records built throughout the project rather than assembled at the end. The permit and inspection records collected in Project 16, the change and allowance records maintained in Project 11, the punch-list closure verified in Project 14, and the communication records preserved in Project 17 all become source material here. The finished package feeds directly into Project 19, the final walkthrough and owner handoff, where these documents are demonstrated and transferred.

Where This Skill Fits

The closeout deliverable matrix built in this project should already exist in draft form by this point in the project, since the universal master instructions and the Project 02 job-file setup both require closeout planning to begin during project setup rather than at the end. This project is where that matrix is completed, reconciled against everything actually collected during construction, and turned into a single delivered package.

The package assembled here is the direct input to Project 19's final walkthrough and owner handoff, where the contractor demonstrates systems, transfers documents and access items, and obtains signed acceptance. It is also the source record for the Chapter 20 capstone's closeout requirements and for any future warranty claim, remodel, or resale the owner undertakes.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing contract, permit, lender, insurer, utility, manufacturer, and company closeout requirements and completing the closeout deliverable matrix
  • collecting permits, permit cards, inspection reports, correction closures, test reports, certificates, and completion or occupancy approvals
  • collecting product and workmanship warranties with owner, address, model, serial, installation date, installer, term, and claim procedure
  • collecting operation and maintenance manuals, product data, care instructions, and finish, color, and stain formulas
  • organizing as-built markups, field dimensions, concealed routing notes, shutoff locations, and equipment locations at their actual level of verification
  • inventorying spare materials, keys, access cards, remotes, filters, and specialty tools
  • collecting training records, final photographs, punch closure, and financial closeout documents
  • indexing, reviewing, correcting, preparing, and delivering the finished closeout package

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • certify as-built drawings as design-professional record documents unless a licensed design professional has prepared and approved them under the required standard
  • represent a document as complete, signed, or approved when it has not actually been received, reviewed, and accepted
  • release lien releases, financial closeout documents, or final payment records without the authority and review the contract and company policy require
  • withhold or alter a warranty, permit, or inspection record to make the package appear more complete than the underlying construction record supports

Any conflict between contract closeout requirements, permit conditions, lender or insurer requirements, and manufacturer documentation requirements must be identified and resolved before the package is represented to the owner as complete.

Trade Note

A closeout package assembled entirely after the crews leave the site is already late. Every chapter in this book that produces a permit, warranty, manual, inspection record, as-built note, or financial release has instructed the responsible party to route a copy into the closeout section of the job file as it is produced. This project's real work is reconciliation, verification, and indexing of what should already be substantially collected — not a scramble to recreate missing records from memory.

Common Field Fundamentals

A closeout package is organized around the owner's future use of the project, not around the contractor's internal trade structure. Every deliverable in this chapter, from a warranty card to a lien release, has one requirement in common: it must be genuine, complete, correctly labeled to this project, and traceable to the party who produced it.

As-Built Notes Versus Record Documents

Field markups made by the contractor or trades are as-built notes, not certified record drawings, unless a licensed design professional has prepared and approved them under the required professional standard. Label every as-built item according to its actual level of verification. Presenting a field markup as a certified as-built drawing misrepresents the document to the owner and to anyone who relies on it later for design, permitting, or emergency response.

Documentation Cue — Index by Owner Use, Not Only by Trade

Organize the delivered package with a closeout index the owner can search by room, system, or topic, in addition to any internal trade or cost-code structure the contractor needs. An owner should be able to locate the water heater warranty, the paint color formula, or the alarm code without first identifying which subcontractor performed the work.

Critical Closeout Cue — Do Not Represent Incomplete Records as Complete

Do not mark a closeout deliverable as received, reviewed, or accepted until it has actually been received, reviewed, and found complete. Do not deliver a package representing full closeout while permits, inspections, warranties, or financial releases remain outstanding without disclosing the specific open items and their status.

Field Coordination Cue — Request Closeout Documents Before Demobilization

Request warranties, manuals, spare materials, and as-built markups from each trade and supplier before that party demobilizes from the site. A trade that has already left the project is significantly harder to reach for a missing or corrected closeout document than one still on site.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Follow Warranty Registration Requirements

Where a manufacturer requires warranty registration, startup documentation, or a specific submission method to activate coverage, follow that method exactly and confirm registration was completed before including the warranty in the closeout package. Do not assume a warranty is active without confirming the manufacturer's specific activation requirement was satisfied.

E. Preparation — Confirm Requirements and Build the Matrix

  1. Review contract, specifications, permit, owner, lender, insurer, utility, manufacturer, and company closeout requirements. Identify every category of required closeout deliverable before collection begins.
  2. Create the closeout deliverable matrix during active construction if not already established. Build it now from every source in Step 1 if it does not already exist.
  3. Assign each deliverable to the responsible trade, supplier, designer, tester, inspector, or project team member. Record one responsible party per deliverable.
  4. Identify required format, original, signature, certification, language, number of copies, and due date. Record these requirements individually for each deliverable rather than assuming a default format.
  5. Create a closeout log with required, requested, received, reviewed, corrected, accepted, and delivered status. Enter every deliverable from the matrix into the log before requesting documents.

F. Collect Permits, Warranties, and Manuals

  1. Collect approved permits, permit cards, inspection reports, correction closures, test reports, certificates, and occupancy or completion approvals. Verify against the Project 16 inspection register that every required approval is present.
  2. Collect special-inspection, engineering, commissioning, balancing, startup, and manufacturer reports. Confirm each report is signed and dated by the responsible party.
  3. Collect product warranties and confirm owner, address, model, serial, installation date, installer, term, and claim procedure. Reject a warranty missing any of these fields and request a corrected copy.
  4. Collect contractor and subcontractor workmanship warranties. Confirm each states its term and claim procedure.
  5. Collect operation and maintenance manuals for equipment, fixtures, finishes, controls, and systems. Confirm the manual matches the actual installed model.
  6. Collect product data, care instructions, finish schedules, color formulas, stain formulas, and cleaning limitations. Record supplier and formula-code information exactly as provided.
  7. Collect filter, belt, battery, lamp, consumable, maintenance, and service schedules. Confirm each schedule states interval and part or service identification.
  8. Collect supplier and service contact information. Confirm current contact details rather than outdated sales contacts.

G. Organize As-Built Information and Spare Materials

  1. Collect as-built markups, field dimensions, concealed routing notes, shutoff locations, valve schedules, panel schedules, and equipment locations. Assemble these from field records maintained throughout construction.
  2. Label as-built notes according to their actual level of verification and do not overstate them as certified drawings. Mark each item as field markup, verified measurement, or design-professional record document, as applicable.
  3. Obtain design-professional record documents where contractually required. Confirm the design professional has prepared and approved these under the required standard before including them as record documents.
  4. Collect approved substitutions, RFIs, bulletins, sketches, and changes affecting operation or future work. Include only the items that affect how the owner will operate or maintain the completed work.
  5. Collect attic stock, spare tile, flooring, paint, hardware, keys, access cards, remotes, filters, and specialty tools. Confirm quantities match contract or allowance requirements.
  6. Inventory spare materials by product, quantity, location, batch, and storage requirement. Record this inventory on the spare-material inventory form.
  7. Collect training agendas, attendance, recordings where authorized, and signoff. Confirm the owner or designated occupant actually attended and signed the training record.

H. Photographs, Financial Closeout, and Package Assembly

  1. Collect final photographs by exterior elevation, room, system, equipment, shutoff, access point, and concealed reference where available. Confirm photographs are labeled with location and date.
  2. Confirm photographs do not expose private or security-sensitive information improperly. Review each photograph for visible security codes, personal documents, or other sensitive content before inclusion.
  3. Collect final punch-list closure, unresolved warranty list, deferred items, and owner responsibilities. Confirm this matches the current status of the Project 14 punch log.
  4. Collect lien releases, affidavits, consent of surety, final invoices, credits, allowance reconciliation, and financial closeout documents as required. Confirm these reconcile with the Project 15 job-cost report.
  5. Collect utility releases, final meter or service records, HOA approvals, and agency documentation. Confirm each authority release matches the permits collected in Step 6.
  6. Organize documents by owner use rather than only by internal trade structure. Group the package by room, system, and topic in addition to any internal cost-code structure.
  7. Create a closeout index with document, revision, date, responsible party, warranty expiration, and file location. Build this index from the completed closeout log.
  8. Review every document for correct project, address, product, signature, completeness, legibility, and consistency. Flag any document that fails this review rather than including it as-is.
  9. Reject incomplete, generic, wrong-project, unsigned, or superseded closeout submissions. Return rejected items to the responsible party with the specific correction needed.
  10. Create a missing-document and correction list. Record every outstanding item with responsible party and due date.
  11. Escalate late closeout items before final payment or demobilization where permitted. Use the contractually available leverage before the responsible party is no longer reachable on site.
  12. Prepare digital and physical packages in the required formats. Match the format, copy count, and certification requirements identified in Step 4.
  13. Test links, file names, searchability, backups, and media readability. Open a sample of files from the finished package to confirm they function as delivered.
  14. Prepare an owner quick-reference sheet for emergency contacts, shutoffs, filters, alarms, service, and warranties. Keep this sheet to a single accessible page or screen.
  15. Obtain internal closeout approval before delivery. Confirm the responsible project manager or supervisor has reviewed and approved the finished package.
  16. Record transmittal and owner receipt. Document the delivery date, method, and recipient acknowledgment.
  17. Preserve the complete project archive according to retention policy. Retain the full source record set separately from the delivered owner package.

Common Closeout Package Inspection

Inspect every closeout package using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Matrix check: confirm every contract, permit, lender, insurer, utility, manufacturer, and company closeout requirement appears on the deliverable matrix with a responsible party and due date.
  • Permit and approval check: confirm every required permit and inspection shows final approval, correction closure, or a documented pending status.
  • Warranty and manual check: confirm each warranty and manual identifies the correct owner, address, and product, with term and claim procedure stated.
  • As-built check: confirm every as-built item is labeled to its actual verification level and no field markup is presented as a certified record document.
  • Spare-material check: confirm spare materials, keys, and access items are inventoried individually by quantity and location.
  • Completeness and delivery check: confirm every deliverable is received, reviewed, and marked accepted or documented as an open item, and that delivery and receipt are recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Product Warranty Is Missing or Incomplete When the Owner Files a Claim

Probable cause: the warranty was collected but not reviewed against the Step 8 field standard for owner, address, model, serial, installation date, installer, term, and claim procedure before it was accepted into the package.
Confirmation: re-check the closeout log entry for the warranty and confirm whether the review status was actually verified or only marked received.
Correction: contact the supplier or manufacturer for a corrected warranty document, confirm registration status, and update the closeout log and delivered package.
Reinspection: repeat the warranty completeness check across every warranty in the package, not only the disputed item.

An As-Built Note Is Treated as a Certified Record Drawing

Probable cause: the field markup was not labeled according to its actual verification level at Step 15, or the package was assembled without distinguishing field notes from design-professional record documents.
Confirmation: check whether a licensed design professional actually prepared and approved the document under the required standard, or whether it is a contractor or trade field markup.
Correction: relabel the document accurately as a field markup, and obtain a true record document from the design professional if the contract requires one.
Reinspection: repeat the as-built labeling check across the full as-built section of the package.

The Owner Cannot Locate a Specific Document After Handoff

Probable cause: the package was indexed only by internal trade or cost-code structure at Step 27, rather than by the owner-use organization this chapter requires.
Confirmation: check the closeout index for a room- or system-based entry point to the document in question.
Correction: reorganize or cross-reference the index by owner use and redeliver the corrected index to the owner.
Reinspection: test the reorganized index by searching for three sample documents using only owner-facing terms.

A Trade Cannot Be Reached for a Missing Closeout Document

Probable cause: the closeout document was not requested before the trade demobilized, as required at the Field Coordination Cue in this chapter, and no escalation occurred while the trade remained reachable.
Confirmation: check the closeout log for the request date relative to the trade's demobilization date recorded in the daily reports.
Correction: escalate through the subcontract terms, contact the supplier directly for product-level documents, and document the delay and resolution attempt.
Reinspection: repeat the missing-document escalation check for every trade that has already demobilized.

A Lien Release or Financial Closeout Document Does Not Match the Job-Cost Report

Probable cause: the financial closeout documents were collected at Step 25 without reconciliation against the current Project 15 job-cost report and Project 11 change log.
Confirmation: compare the amount and scope stated on the lien release or final invoice against the current approved contract value and change log.
Correction: resolve the discrepancy with the subcontractor, supplier, or accounting department before including the document in the package, and update both records.
Reinspection: repeat the financial reconciliation check across all financial closeout documents before final delivery.

The Delivered Package Is Represented as Complete While Items Remain Outstanding

Probable cause: the missing-document and correction list from Step 31 was not reconciled against the closeout log before delivery, or an outstanding item was not disclosed to the owner.
Confirmation: compare the closeout log's accepted-status items against everything actually delivered in the package.
Correction: disclose the specific outstanding items, their responsible party, and expected resolution date to the owner in writing, and do not represent the package as fully complete until they close.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check once the outstanding items are resolved and confirm the package is updated and redelivered if required.

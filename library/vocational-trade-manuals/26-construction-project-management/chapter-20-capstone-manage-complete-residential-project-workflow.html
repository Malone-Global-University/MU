Job Overview

This capstone project qualifies you to plan, coordinate, control, document, and close one complete approved residential project workflow, from the scope baseline through owner handoff and lessons learned. It does not teach a new control system. It requires you to operate every control system built in Projects 01 through 19 — scope, job file, WBS, sequence logic, baseline schedule, lookahead, procurement, trade coordination, coordination meetings, RFIs, change and allowance control, safety and housekeeping, quality control, punch list, job cost, inspections, communication, closeout package, and final walkthrough — as one integrated system governing a single approved capstone project, rather than as separate exercises.

The finished work product is one complete, auditable residential project-management record: a reconciled scope baseline; a controlled job file; an approved WBS; a sequenced and baselined schedule with a maintained lookahead; a live procurement log; a documented trade-coordination and handoff system; a complete meeting, action, and decision record; a closed RFI log; a controlled change and allowance log; an active safety and housekeeping plan; a complete quality-control record; a closed punch list; a current job-cost report; a complete inspection-approval record; a preserved communication register; an indexed closeout package; a signed owner handoff record; and a documented lessons-learned report.

This is the last project in Book 26. It draws directly on the Book 23 plan-interpretation package, the Book 24 estimate, and the Book 25 permit-and-inspection plan, and it produces the complete project-management record that carries forward into Book 27, The Professional Contractor.

Where This Skill Fits

Every earlier project in this book produced one control system in isolation: the scope statement in Project 01, the job file in Project 02, the WBS in Project 03, sequence logic in Project 04, the baseline schedule in Project 05, and so on through the final walkthrough in Project 19. This capstone does not introduce a new document type. It requires you to operate all nineteen systems together, on one real approved project, exactly as a working project manager must — keeping scope, schedule, cost, procurement, safety, quality, and communication aligned to a single baseline from mobilization through archive.

The discipline built here — one current scope baseline, one current schedule baseline, one approved budget baseline, and one active field-document set, updated together rather than separately — is the exact discipline Book 27, The Professional Contractor, assumes the learner already has. Nothing taught in this project is new; what is new is running every system at once, without letting one control fall out of alignment with the others.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • selecting one approved capstone residential project and confirming the delivery method, contract relationships, and decision hierarchy that govern it
  • reconciling the Book 23 plan-interpretation package, Book 24 estimate, and Book 25 permit-and-inspection plan against the executed agreement before issuing the project-management baseline
  • producing and freezing the scope statement, job file, WBS, sequence logic, and baseline schedule as one aligned set
  • operating the two-week lookahead, procurement log, trade-coordination matrix, and coordination-meeting cycle throughout construction
  • operating the RFI log, change and allowance log, safety and housekeeping plan, and quality-control plan throughout construction
  • operating the job-cost system, inspection-approval record, and communication register throughout construction
  • producing the punch list, closeout package, final walkthrough, and signed owner handoff
  • completing a full project-management closeout, archive, and lessons-learned report, and documenting the transition to Book 27

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a major commercial, high-rise, industrial, institutional, heavy-civil, complex multifamily, hazardous, public-works, or highly specialized project as the capstone without the contracts, management systems, licenses, insurance, bonding, staffing, professional services, and regulatory controls that project type requires
  • make design, engineering, or code-compliance decisions; those remain with the licensed design professional, engineer, or authority having jurisdiction
  • perform, direct, or accept licensed, permitted, or safety-controlled work outside the license, permit, insurance, training, or contractual scope of the responsible party
  • reset a schedule or cost baseline, bypass a required inspection or quality hold point, or expand the approved scope without the written authorization required by the change-control system built in Project 11

Any conflict between the reconciled Book 23–25 package, the executed agreement, and current field conditions must be identified, documented, and resolved in writing before the affected baseline is issued or revised.

Single-Baseline Discipline Across the Full Project

At every stage of this capstone, maintain exactly one current scope baseline, one current schedule baseline, and one approved budget baseline. When any control system is updated — a change order, an RFI response, an approved substitution — update every other system that depends on it in the same work session, not on a delayed or separate schedule. A change log that does not update cost and time the same day it is approved is not functioning as change control.

Critical Scope Cue — Do Not Let One Control System Drift From the Others

The most common capstone failure is allowing one system to fall out of alignment with the rest: a schedule updated without updating procurement release dates, a change order priced without updating the job-cost forecast, or a punch item closed without updating the closeout matrix. Before closing any coordination meeting, verify that every affected log has been updated, not only the one directly discussed.

Documentation Cue — Preserve Every Baseline and Revision

Preserve the original scope, WBS, and schedule baselines even as forecasts and revisions are issued. Do not overwrite a baseline to hide delay, cost growth, or a missed commitment. Record why a forecast date or cost moved, and keep the original baseline retrievable in the archive.

Field Coordination Cue — Keep One Master Index of Current Revisions

Maintain one master index page listing the current revision number and date of every baseline, log, and register used in this capstone. Update the index immediately when any document is revised. A project team working from different revisions of the same document is the single most common cause of field disputes on integrated projects.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow the Executed Agreement's Authority and Notice Rules Throughout

Every stage of this capstone — scope changes, schedule notices, payment applications, inspection coordination, and final acceptance — must follow the notice method, timeframe, and decision authority stated in the executed agreement. Do not substitute a faster informal channel for a method the contract requires at any stage of the project.

E. Project Setup — Reconcile the Controlling Documents and Issue the Baseline

  1. Select the approved capstone residential project and identify the authorized project manager. Confirm the project meets the Capstone Project Control conditions in the book outline before proceeding.
  2. Collect the Book 23 plan-interpretation package, Book 24 estimate, Book 25 permit-and-inspection plan, executed agreement, and current project documents. Assemble every controlling document before drafting the integrated baseline.
  3. Confirm delivery method, contract relationships, permit holder, design authority, owner representative, lender, insurer, and project decision hierarchy. Record this confirmation in writing before assigning any project responsibility.
  4. Create the project information sheet, contact directory, emergency information, and authority matrix. Distribute it to every stakeholder identified in the scope statement.
  5. Perform a full document and revision reconciliation. Compare every Book 23–25 document, the executed agreement, and prior correspondence for conflicts before issuing the baseline.
  6. Remove superseded documents from active use. Move every outdated draft to the archive location established in the Project 02 job file.
  7. Define project objectives, success criteria, physical deliverables, milestones, and owner priorities. Confirm these match the Project 01 scope-statement standard exactly.
  8. Create the Chapter 1 scope statement with inclusions, exclusions, allowances, alternates, assumptions, constraints, stakeholders, and change rules. Use the same document schedule and acceptance standards established in Project 01.
  9. Reconcile the scope with estimate, permits, inspections, drawings, specifications, and owner selections. Record every discrepancy found and resolve it before approval.
  10. Obtain approval and freeze the scope baseline. Assign the baseline number and date, and confirm the approver matches the decision authority recorded in Step 3.

F. Job File, WBS, and Sequence Logic

  1. Set up the Chapter 2 project binder or digital job file. Use the folder structure, access rules, and file-naming convention established in Project 02.
  2. Create document registers, revision logs, transmittals, photograph protocol, field notes, and controlled logs. Confirm every log required in Project 02 is present and active before mobilization.
  3. Establish access, permissions, cybersecurity, backup, retention, and active-versus-archive rules. Test recovery of one current document and one archived document before proceeding.
  4. Build the Chapter 3 WBS and WBS dictionary. Break the frozen scope into deliverable-based work packages exactly as taught in Project 03.
  5. Assign work packages, cost codes, schedule identifiers, procurement identifiers, responsibility, quality checks, inspections, and closeout deliverables. Confirm every code aligns with the job-cost and schedule systems built later in this sequence.
  6. Compare the WBS with scope, estimate, permit plan, and contracts. Resolve any work package without a matching scope item, and any scope item without a matching work package.
  7. Approve and issue the WBS baseline. Freeze the revision and distribute it to the project team.
  8. Create the Chapter 4 construction-sequence logic. Convert the WBS into activities using the physical, regulatory, and trade-handoff logic taught in Project 04.
  9. Identify physical, permit, inspection, procurement, cure, testing, access, protection, and handoff relationships. Confirm no activity is sequenced without a documented logic reason.
  10. Review sequence logic with field supervision and key trades. Record comments and revise the logic before it becomes the baseline schedule's foundation.

G. Baseline Schedule and Lookahead

  1. Build the Chapter 5 baseline schedule. Use the approved WBS and sequence logic as the complete activity source, exactly as taught in Project 05.
  2. Enter calendars, durations, resources, procurement, inspections, owner decisions, milestones, and logic. Confirm every activity has a defined start and finish condition.
  3. Identify critical path, float, near-critical work, and schedule risks. Record mitigation for the highest-risk items before freezing the baseline.
  4. Obtain schedule commitments and baseline approval. Confirm approval from the authority identified in the scope statement.
  5. Create the initial two-week lookahead. Convert the baseline schedule into daily field commitments using the Project 06 method.
  6. Create the constraint log and assign owners and due dates. Enter every unresolved readiness item before the lookahead is published.
  7. Confirm drawings, labor, material, equipment, access, safety, quality, inspections, and owner conditions for near-term work. Do not place a task on the lookahead until every readiness condition is checked.

H. Procurement, Trade Coordination, and Mobilization Readiness

  1. Create the Chapter 7 procurement register. Build it from the approved estimate, takeoffs, specifications, and baseline schedule.
  2. Identify long-lead and owner-selection items. Flag every item requiring submittal, approval, fabrication, or extended lead time.
  3. Calculate submittal, approval, release, fabrication, shipping, and delivery dates backward from installation. Confirm every release date is tied to a required-on-site date from the baseline schedule.
  4. Issue approved purchase orders and subcontracts. Confirm each matches the current scope, estimate, and delivery requirements before release.
  5. Track supplier acknowledgment, fabrication, shipping, receipt, inspection, storage, and installation readiness. Update the procurement register at every coordination meeting.
  6. Create the trade-responsibility and interface matrix. Resolve every scope gap or overlap in writing before mobilization.
  7. Define measurable handoff conditions by area and system. Use the same handoff-condition standard taught in Project 08.
  8. Conduct preinstallation coordination for structure, enclosure, MEP, wet areas, finishes, and other high-risk interfaces. Record commitments and unresolved constraints.
  9. Verify trade licenses, insurance, safety documents, approved scope, current documents, and closeout obligations. Confirm every subcontractor meets these conditions before mobilization.
  10. Create the Chapter 12 jobsite safety and housekeeping plan. Confirm the plan coordinates, but does not replace, each employer's own safety responsibilities.

I. Safety, Quality, and Kickoff

  1. Complete initial site logistics, access, emergency, protection, storage, sanitation, and housekeeping readiness. Perform the initial site-readiness inspection before mobilization.
  2. Conduct orientations and recurring site inspections through the responsible safety process. Record acknowledgment from every subcontractor.
  3. Create the Chapter 13 quality-control plan. Identify acceptance criteria for every high-risk, concealed, or owner-visible work package.
  4. Identify preparatory, first-work, in-process, preconcealment, preinspection, handoff, and final checks. Link every check to the WBS and schedule.
  5. Conduct mockup or first-work approval for selected high-risk work. Record who attended and what was approved.
  6. Create the inspection register from the Book 25 requirements. Confirm every required authority, special, and manufacturer inspection is listed.
  7. Link quality checks and authority inspections to the schedule. Confirm no concealed-work activity proceeds without its linked quality and inspection approval.
  8. Mobilize only after permit, safety, access, document, procurement, and baseline conditions are satisfied. Confirm every Section E through I condition above is met before this step.
  9. Conduct the project kickoff and record decisions and actions. Use the meeting standard built in Project 09.
  10. Issue current documents, schedule, lookahead, site rules, quality criteria, and communication plan. Confirm distribution to every stakeholder and trade.

J. Field Execution — Daily Control, Meetings, RFIs, and Changes

  1. Begin daily reporting of weather, manpower, work, deliveries, visitors, inspections, delays, incidents, photographs, and decisions. Maintain this record without interruption for the life of the project.
  2. Update the two-week lookahead at the approved interval. Confirm actual starts, actual finishes, and remaining duration are entered before each new lookahead is published.
  3. Run recurring Chapter 9 coordination meetings. Follow the agenda, decision, and action-item standard built in Project 09.
  4. Track commitments, constraints, safety, quality, procurement, inspections, RFIs, changes, and owner decisions. Confirm every open item has one responsible owner and one due date.
  5. Create and route RFIs for actual ambiguities or conflicts. Use the standard built in Project 10; do not use an RFI to transfer ordinary contractor means and methods.
  6. Distribute responses and update active field documents. Confirm every affected drawing, submittal, or schedule item is revised before affected work proceeds.
  7. Create potential-change records for scope, cost, or time impacts. Identify every change at its earliest indication.
  8. Operate the Chapter 11 change and allowance log. Price, authorize, and track every change using the standard built in Project 11.
  9. Obtain written authorization before changed work except under approved emergency or directive procedures. Confirm the approver matches the decision authority recorded in Step 3.
  10. Update scope, WBS, schedule, procurement, cost, drawings, inspections, and communication after approved changes. Complete every update in the same work session as the approval.

K. Field Execution — Construction Sequence Through Substantial Completion

  1. Manage sitework and foundation handoffs, inspections, concealed records, and quality checks. Confirm every concealed item is photographed and approved before covering.
  2. Manage structural framing, bracing, connectors, roof structure, inspections, and dry-in sequence. Confirm the weather-tight milestone conditions defined in Project 04 are met before releasing successor trades.
  3. Manage roofing, windows, doors, WRB, flashing, siding, and envelope quality and weather protection. Verify manufacturer installation instructions where they control acceptance.
  4. Manage MEP rough-in coordination, supports, penetrations, testing, inspections, and preclosure documentation. Confirm rough-in inspections occur before concealment.
  5. Manage insulation, air sealing, fire blocking, energy tests, and pre-drywall inspections. Confirm every pre-drywall hold point is verified before drywall begins.
  6. Manage drywall, cabinets, trim, flooring, paint, fixtures, devices, equipment, and finish protection. Coordinate finish-trade sequencing to prevent damage and rework.
  7. Manage exterior work, final grading, drainage, flatwork, decks, fences, landscaping, and site restoration. Confirm access and weather conditions before scheduling.
  8. Coordinate permanent utilities, equipment startup, testing, commissioning, balancing, owner training, and final inspections. Confirm every system is operational before the final walkthrough is scheduled.

L. Cost, Punch, and Progressive Closeout

  1. Operate the Chapter 15 job-cost system throughout the project. Maintain original budget, current budget, commitments, actual cost, pending exposure, and forecast at every reporting cycle.
  2. Update commitments, actual costs, pending exposure, forecast, billing, allowances, and change revenue. Reconcile these figures against field progress at each cost-reporting cycle.
  3. Investigate cost and productivity variance before it becomes unrecoverable. Identify the cause by quantity, price, productivity, scope, schedule, waste, damage, rework, or timing.
  4. Update the master schedule with actual progress, forecast, delay, recovery, and approved time changes. Confirm every update is recorded against the current baseline.
  5. Do not overwrite the original baseline. Preserve it and record the reason for every forecast change separately.
  6. Create area-based pre-punch lists before trades demobilize. Begin punch planning before substantial completion, using the standard built in Project 14.
  7. Verify corrections through the Chapter 14 punch process. Reinspect every corrected item against its stated acceptance criterion.
  8. Reconcile contractor, owner, designer, inspector, and warranty items. Consolidate duplicate items across all four sources.
  9. Begin closeout collection as each trade completes work. Do not wait until project completion to request closeout documents.
  10. Collect permits, approvals, tests, warranties, manuals, record notes, spare materials, keys, photographs, training, and financial releases. Confirm each item against the closeout deliverable matrix built in Project 18.

M. Final Closeout, Handoff, and Lessons Learned

  1. Prepare the Chapter 18 indexed closeout package. Organize the package by owner use, following the standard built in Project 18.
  2. Complete final cleaning, testing, inspections, correction closure, utility releases, and occupancy conditions. Confirm every condition is satisfied before scheduling the final walkthrough.
  3. Prepare the Chapter 19 final walkthrough route and owner demonstration plan. Confirm the punch list and closeout package are ready for review before the walkthrough date.
  4. Conduct the walkthrough and categorize owner concerns. Distinguish incomplete contract work from preference, warranty, and new-change items using the Project 19 standard.
  5. Complete remaining legitimate contract items and verify closure. Confirm every item is reinspected and verified, not merely reported complete.
  6. Deliver closeout documents, maintenance guidance, access items, and warranty information. Obtain owner acknowledgment of receipt.
  7. Obtain signed handoff, acceptance, and completion records as applicable. Confirm the signature is from the decision authority recorded in Step 3.
  8. Reconcile final contract value, approved changes, allowances, credits, commitments, actual cost, forecast, invoices, retainage, and payments. Confirm the final job-cost report matches the final contract value.
  9. Close purchase orders, subcontracts, permits, inspections, punch items, RFIs, changes, safety actions, and quality deficiencies. Confirm no open item remains in any log without a documented reason.
  10. Archive the complete project record with controlled access and retention. Confirm every baseline, log, and register from Projects 01 through 19 is included in the archive.
  11. Conduct a lessons-learned meeting covering scope, estimate, schedule, procurement, trades, safety, quality, inspections, cost, communication, and closeout. Record findings from every control system used in this capstone.
  12. Record what should be standardized, corrected, avoided, or added to future project controls. Assign each lesson a category and a recommended action.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Schedule Shows a Date the Procurement Log Cannot Support

Probable cause: a schedule update at Step 69 was made without recalculating the backward-scheduled release dates in the procurement register from Step 30.
Confirmation: compare the current installation date on the schedule against the current required-release date on the procurement register for the same item.
Correction: recalculate the release date from the revised installation date, and if the item cannot meet the new date, raise it as a schedule risk in the next coordination meeting.
Reinspection: repeat the cross-system currency check between the schedule and procurement register for every long-lead item.

An Approved Change Order Is Not Reflected in the Job-Cost Forecast

Probable cause: the change and allowance log at Step 56 was updated, but the job-cost report at Step 66 was not updated in the same work session.
Confirmation: compare the current contract value and forecast in the job-cost report against the approved-change total in the change log.
Correction: update the current budget, committed cost, and forecast fields to include the approved change, and note the correction date in the cost-report history.
Reinspection: repeat the cross-system currency check between the change log and the job-cost report for every approved change.

Work Is Concealed Before an Inspection Hold Point Is Verified

Probable cause: the quality-control plan's preconcealment hold point from Step 42 was not linked to the schedule activity, or field supervision proceeded on schedule pressure.
Confirmation: check the inspection register and quality checklist for the affected work package and confirm whether the required hold-point approval was recorded before concealment.
Correction: stop further concealment, expose the work if safely possible, obtain the required inspection or engineering review, and document the corrective action.
Reinspection: repeat the preconcealment and handoff check across all concealed work packages completed in the same period.

Two Baselines Are Both Being Treated as Current

Probable cause: a revised schedule, WBS, or scope document was issued without archiving the superseded version, breaking the single-baseline discipline required in Step 6 and the master revision index.
Confirmation: check the master revision index against the document actually in use by field supervision, procurement, and accounting for the same control system.
Correction: identify the correct current baseline, archive every superseded version, and redistribute the current index to the full project team.
Reinspection: repeat the single-baseline check across every control system, not only the one where the conflict was found.

Closeout Documents Cannot Be Located at Project Completion

Probable cause: closeout collection at Step 75 did not begin as each trade completed work, so documents were never requested before demobilization.
Confirmation: check the closeout log for required, requested, received, and delivered status against the trade-completion dates recorded in the daily reports.
Correction: contact the responsible trade or supplier directly, request the missing document, and escalate before final payment or demobilization where the contract permits.
Reinspection: repeat the progressive closeout check against the full closeout deliverable matrix.

The Owner Disputes an Item as Incomplete Contract Work During the Walkthrough

Probable cause: the item was not clearly categorized as included, excluded, allowance, or alternate in the original scope statement, or a later change was not properly documented and distributed.
Confirmation: check the frozen scope baseline and the change log for the specific item and confirm which category it falls under and whether any change record affects it.
Correction: resolve the categorization in writing with the owner and decision authority, and if legitimate, assign it an identifier, responsible party, due date, and acceptance criterion in the punch process.
Reinspection: repeat the walkthrough categorization check once the item is resolved and documented.

The Lessons-Learned Report Repeats the Same Finding as a Prior Project

Probable cause: a corrective action identified in an earlier project's lessons-learned report was not actually implemented as a standard practice at Step 87.
Confirmation: check whether the recommended standardize, correct, avoid, or add action from the prior report was incorporated into the company's current templates, logs, or procedures.
Correction: implement the corrective action as a standard company practice, not only as a one-time note, and confirm it going forward.
Reinspection: confirm the corrective action appears in the current templates or procedures before closing this capstone's lessons-learned report.

Field Terms

Project-Management Baseline
The combined, aligned set of the approved scope, WBS, and schedule baselines that governs the capstone project.
Integrated Control Record
The complete set of active logs and registers kept current and cross-referenced to the baseline.
Single-Baseline Discipline
The rule that only one current scope baseline, one current schedule baseline, and one approved budget baseline may govern the project at any time.
Reconciliation
The documented comparison of two or more controlling records to identify and resolve gaps, duplications, or conflicts before a baseline is issued.
Cross-System Currency
The requirement that every log or register affected by an approved change is updated in the same work session as the approval.
Progressive Closeout
The practice of collecting closeout documents as each trade completes work, rather than reconstructing them after the team leaves the site.
Master Revision Index
A single current listing of the revision number and date of every baseline and log in active use on the project.
Lessons Learned
The documented record of what should be standardized, corrected, avoided, or added to future project controls, captured at project closeout.

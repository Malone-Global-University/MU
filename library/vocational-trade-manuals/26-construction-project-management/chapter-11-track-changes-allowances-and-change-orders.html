Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and operate a change-control system for a residential construction project — the controlling process that captures every potential change at the earliest indication, prices it accurately, obtains written authorization before work proceeds, and updates every affected project document once a change is approved. The finished work product is one operating change log, one allowance log, and a set of priced, authorized change-order records reconciled to current contract value.

You will create a change-event numbering system, identify change sources, document conditions before changed work begins, price labor, material, equipment, and schedule effects separately, route proposals through authorized review, update every affected project document after approval, operate an allowance log tied to owner selections, and reconcile pending, approved, billed, and closed amounts through project closeout.

This project depends directly on the scope baseline from Project 01, the RFI process from Project 10, and the cost codes established in Project 15. It also protects the change-control rule created in Project 01, Step 36, which prevents informal expansion of the approved baseline. Every later project in this book — cost reporting in Project 15, inspection coordination in Project 16, communication control in Project 17, and closeout in Project 18 — depends on this change and allowance system being accurate and current.

Where This Skill Fits

The scope statement approved in Project 01 establishes the baseline this project protects. The RFI process in Project 10 frequently generates the potential changes this project prices and authorizes. The job-cost system in Project 15 depends on this project's change log and allowance log to keep committed cost, forecast cost, and contract value current. The closeout package assembled in Project 18 cannot be reconciled unless every change event created here reaches a documented closure.

The discipline of separating entitlement from pricing, and price from schedule effect, taught in this project, is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone requires when integrating change control with the rest of the project-management workflow.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • creating a change-event numbering system and central change log with defined statuses
  • identifying change sources and documenting conditions at first notice, including photographs and measurements of existing conditions
  • confirming whether an event is within original scope, excluded, allowance, alternate, unit-price, or a potential change before promising entitlement or price
  • issuing required notice within contractual time limits and documenting owner requests in writing
  • pricing labor, material, equipment, supervision, and markup using approved methods, and stating price and schedule effects separately
  • routing change proposals through authorized review and approval, and updating every affected project document after approval
  • operating an allowance log tied to owner selections, with committed cost, forecast, tax, markup, and remaining balance
  • reconciling pending, approved, billed, paid, and closed change amounts through project closeout

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • direct changed work without the required written authorization, except under a documented emergency or directive procedure identified in the executed agreement
  • promise entitlement, price, or schedule relief before the contract and scope have been reviewed by the party holding that authority
  • render a legal opinion on contract entitlement, delay damages, or claim validity; unresolved entitlement disputes must be routed to the person or role identified in the executed agreement
  • treat a verbal owner conversation as authorization to proceed with changed work

Any conflict between a change request and the approved scope baseline, drawings, or specifications must be identified and resolved in writing before changed work proceeds.

Trade Note

Every change event in this project begins as a documented condition, not a verbal agreement. Confirming discussion is not authorization, and pricing an event before confirming entitlement, are the two habits this chapter's troubleshooting section identifies most often as the cause of disputed billing.

Setup Standard

Do not price a change event (Step 13) until entitlement has been confirmed against the approved scope baseline (Step 6) and required notice has been issued (Step 8). A change priced before entitlement is confirmed must be re-verified before it is submitted for authorization.

Shop Tip

State price effect and time effect as two separate numbers on every change-order proposal. A proposal that bundles cost and schedule impact into one narrative paragraph is difficult to authorize accurately and difficult to reconcile later.

Entitlement Before Price

Confirm whether a change event is within original scope, excluded, allowance, alternate, unit-price, or a genuine potential change before any price is discussed with the owner or a trade. Promising a price, or implying a price is likely, before entitlement is confirmed puts the contractor in the position of having created an expectation it may not be authorized to fulfill.

Documentation Cue — Photograph or Measure Before Changed Work Begins

Photograph or measure the existing condition before changed work begins, for every change event. A change priced or billed without a documented existing condition is difficult to defend if the scope, quantity, or condition is later disputed.

Critical Change Cue — Written Authorization Before Changed Work

Do not direct changed work without the required written authorization, except under a documented emergency or directive procedure identified in the executed agreement. Do not promise entitlement or price before contract and scope review. Treating an owner's verbal agreement as authorization is a leading cause of unpriced and unauthorized changed work.

Field Coordination Cue — Notify Affected Trades of Pending Status

Notify affected trades and field supervision when a change event is pending, and identify clearly whether work in the affected area may continue, must be protected, or must stop. Do not allow a pending change to create ambiguity about what may proceed in the field.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow the Notice Deadline

Where the executed agreement specifies a time limit for issuing notice of a change event, issue notice within that time limit exactly. Missing a contractual notice deadline can affect entitlement even when the underlying change is legitimate.

E. Preparation — Establish the Change Log and Statuses

  1. Create a change-event numbering system and central change log. Assign a unique number to every change event before any other action is taken on it.
  2. Define change statuses such as identified, under review, pricing, submitted, approved, rejected, withdrawn, proceeding under directive, billed, and closed. Use these statuses consistently for every entry.
  3. Identify change sources including owner request, design revision, hidden condition, code correction, utility requirement, substitution, allowance selection, field conflict, delay, and scope transfer. Record the source category for each event.
  4. Record the date, originator, location, description, document reference, and affected work at first notice. Complete this entry before further action on the event.
  5. Photograph or measure the existing condition before changed work begins. Attach the record to the change-log entry.

F. Confirm Entitlement and Issue Notice

  1. Confirm whether the event is within original scope, excluded, allowance, alternate, unit-price, or potential change. Compare the event against the approved scope baseline from Project 01.
  2. Do not promise entitlement or price before contract and scope review. Keep discussion factual until the review is complete.
  3. Issue required notice within contractual time limits. Confirm the notice method and deadline in the executed agreement before issuing it.
  4. Identify whether work must stop, continue unchanged, be protected, or proceed under written directive. Record the determination and communicate it to affected trades.
  5. Document owner requests in writing and confirm that discussion is not authorization. Record the request exactly as stated by the owner.
  6. Define the changed scope and the unchanged baseline scope. State clearly what is being added, deleted, or modified.
  7. Identify demolition, protection, redesign, permit, inspection, procurement, rework, and closeout effects. Record every secondary effect the change creates.

G. Price the Change and Prepare the Proposal

  1. Obtain subcontractor and supplier quotations with scope, quantity, labor, material, equipment, exclusions, validity, and schedule. Confirm each quotation matches the defined changed scope.
  2. Calculate self-performed labor, material, equipment, supervision, cleanup, disposal, tax, and markup using approved methods. Use the company's approved pricing policy; do not invent markup rates.
  3. Identify credits, restocking, cancellation, return, salvage, and deleted work. Record any reductions to price alongside additions.
  4. Calculate direct schedule effect and secondary effects on sequence, access, weather, overhead, and other trades. State the effect in calendar days or a defined unit.
  5. Identify delay, acceleration, remobilization, overtime, standby, or extended general-condition effects. Record each effect as a separate line item where it applies.
  6. State price effect and time effect separately. Do not combine them into a single narrative figure.
  7. Prepare the change proposal with assumptions, exclusions, validity, and supporting detail. Attach the quotations and calculations supporting the proposal.

H. Authorize, Implement, and Update Project Documents

  1. Route the proposal through authorized review and approval. Submit it to the person or role confirmed in the Prerequisites.
  2. Record approval, rejection, modification, or pending status. Update the change log immediately after the determination is received.
  3. Do not direct changed work without the required authorization except under documented emergency or directive procedures. Confirm the directive procedure exactly matches the executed agreement before relying on it.
  4. Issue revised scope, drawings, schedule, budget, purchase orders, subcontracts, and field instructions after approval. Update every document affected by the change.
  5. Update contract value, committed cost, forecast, billing, schedule, WBS, procurement, inspections, and closeout requirements. Confirm each linked system reflects the approved change.

I. Operate the Allowance Log

  1. Create an allowance log with original allowance, approved selection, committed cost, forecast, tax, markup, and balance. Establish one entry per allowance category from the estimate.
  2. Track allowance-decision dates and warn when delayed selections affect schedule or price. Compare selection deadlines against the schedule and procurement dates.
  3. Distinguish allowance overrun, scope change, upgrade, and quantity change. Record the correct category for each variance from the original allowance.
  4. Track pending changes in forecast cost even before approval according to company policy. Do not treat unapproved but likely exposure as zero cost.
  5. Prevent duplicate pricing across RFIs, owner requests, subcontractor changes, and allowance reconciliations. Check the change log before pricing an item that may already be captured elsewhere.

J. Verify, Bill, and Close the Change Record

  1. Link invoices and subcontractor change orders to the approved change identifier. Confirm the change number appears on the invoice or billing record.
  2. Verify completed changed work and required inspections. Confirm the work matches the approved change scope before billing.
  3. Capture revised as-built notes, warranties, manuals, and closeout information. Update closeout records affected by the change.
  4. Reconcile pending, approved, billed, paid, and closed change amounts. Confirm the change log and job-cost report agree.
  5. Obtain final signature or closure evidence. Record the closure date and responsible party on the change log.
  6. Archive the complete change record and update lessons learned. Preserve the full record, including rejected and withdrawn events, in the project archive.

Common Change-Log Inspection

Inspect the change and allowance system using the same checks regardless of project size or number of change events.

  • Entitlement check: confirm every priced change was compared against the approved scope baseline before price was discussed.
  • Documentation check: confirm existing conditions were photographed or measured before changed work began for every event.
  • Authorization check: confirm no changed work proceeded without written authorization, except under a documented directive procedure.
  • Pricing check: confirm price and time effects are stated separately and traceable to quotations or calculations.
  • Document-update check: confirm every affected project document was revised after approval.
  • Allowance check: confirm every allowance category shows a current original amount, selection, committed cost, forecast, and balance.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm pending, approved, billed, paid, and closed amounts agree between the change log and job-cost report.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Trade Performs Changed Work Without a Signed Change Order

Probable cause: a verbal owner or field conversation was treated as authorization, or the directive procedure was used without meeting its documented conditions.
Confirmation: check the change log for a matching entry and confirm whether written authorization or a valid directive exists for the work performed.
Correction: document the event immediately, confirm entitlement and price after the fact, and obtain written ratification from the authorized approver; review the notice and directive procedure with field staff.
Reinspection: confirm the change-control rule has been reissued to the field team and acknowledged.

An Owner Disputes the Price of an Approved Change

Probable cause: the change proposal did not include traceable supporting detail, or price and time effects were combined into one figure instead of stated separately.
Confirmation: review the change-order proposal against Step 19 and confirm whether quotations, calculations, and separated price and time effects are attached.
Correction: reconstruct the pricing detail from source quotations and calculations, and reissue the proposal with price and time effects stated separately.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing check on the reissued proposal before requesting approval again.

The Job-Cost Report and Change Log Do Not Agree

Probable cause: a change was priced or billed without being linked to its change-log number, or pending exposure was not carried in the forecast according to company policy.
Confirmation: compare the change log against the job-cost report line by line for the disputed period and identify the specific change number causing the discrepancy.
Correction: correct the missing link between the invoice or billing record and the change number, and update the forecast to include pending exposure.
Reinspection: repeat the full reconciliation check between the change log and the job-cost report.

An Allowance Category Shows an Unexplained Overrun

Probable cause: the variance was not correctly distinguished among allowance overrun, scope change, upgrade, and quantity change at Step 27, or a related scope change was priced separately without checking the allowance log for duplication.
Confirmation: review the allowance log entry and the change log for the same period, and identify whether the variance is a true allowance overrun or a miscategorized scope change.
Correction: recategorize the variance correctly, remove any duplicate pricing, and update the allowance log balance.
Reinspection: repeat the allowance check across the full log, not only the affected category.

A Change Event Has No Existing-Condition Documentation

Probable cause: the photograph or measurement requirement at Step 5 was skipped because the change appeared minor or was identified after work had already begun.
Confirmation: check the change-log entry for an attached existing-condition record and confirm whether the record predates the changed work.
Correction: document the current condition immediately, note the gap in the change-log entry, and use available secondary evidence such as prior photographs, schedule records, or witness statements to support the record.
Reinspection: repeat the documentation check for every open change event in the log.

A Change Is Never Closed and Remains Open at Project Closeout

Probable cause: the change was billed and paid but never carried to final signature or closure evidence at Step 34, or the closure step was not assigned a responsible party.
Confirmation: check the change log status field and confirm whether closure evidence is attached for the disputed item.
Correction: obtain the missing signature or closure evidence before final project closeout, or document the item as a carried-forward open risk with an assigned responsible party.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check across the full change log before beginning Project 18.

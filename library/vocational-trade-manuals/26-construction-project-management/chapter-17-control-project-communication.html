Job Overview

This project qualifies you to create and operate a project communication plan — the controlling system that determines which channel carries which kind of information, who has authority to speak for the project, how decisions and notices are documented, and how communication records are preserved. The finished work product is one issued communication plan supported by an owner-decision log, trade and supplier notification templates, a communication register, and a completed audit of a sample of records.

You will identify every stakeholder and their information needs, define channels for emergencies, formal notices, owner decisions, field coordination, schedule updates, RFIs, changes, billing, safety, quality, and closeout, define response-time and escalation rules, build an owner-decision log and schedule-notice procedure, create trade and supplier notification templates, establish a field-conversation confirmation process, protect confidential information, build a communication register, and audit a sample of communications for completeness before archiving the plan.

This project depends on the scope baseline from Project 01, the job file from Project 02, the schedule and lookahead from Projects 05 and 06, the trade-handoff system from Project 08, the coordination-meeting record from Project 09, the RFI system from Project 10, the change-control system from Project 11, the safety plan from Project 12, the quality plan from Project 13, and the inspection-approval record from Project 16. It supplies the controlled communication record that Project 18 closeout, Project 19 owner handoff, and the Chapter 20 capstone all depend on.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior project in this book generates communication that this plan must route and preserve: schedule updates from Project 05, lookahead publication from Project 06, procurement notices from Project 07, trade handoffs from Project 08, meeting minutes from Project 09, RFI distribution from Project 10, change notices from Project 11, safety and quality communication from Projects 12 and 13, and inspection results from Project 16. This project does not create new project content; it creates the controlled channels, authority rules, and records that carry the content already produced.

The discipline built here — one controlling channel per subject, written confirmation of verbal direction, and a preserved register — is the same discipline the Project 18 closeout package and the Project 19 owner handoff depend on to prove that decisions, notices, and approvals actually occurred.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying every stakeholder and the information they need, provide, approve, or receive
  • defining communication channels for emergencies, formal notices, owner decisions, field coordination, schedule updates, RFIs, changes, billing, safety, quality, and closeout
  • defining which channel controls when duplicate messages exist, response-time expectations, and escalation paths
  • defining who may issue direction to subcontractors, suppliers, designers, and field staff, and preventing unauthorized direct owner-to-trade scope changes
  • building an owner-decision log, a schedule-notice procedure, and trade and supplier notification templates
  • creating a field-conversation confirmation process and confirming important verbal direction in writing
  • protecting confidential information and restricting payroll, medical, personnel, pricing, claims, security, and financial records
  • building a communication register, auditing a sample of records, and archiving the plan and its records

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • issue direction to a subcontractor, supplier, designer, or field employee outside the authority defined in Step 6 of the procedure below
  • treat a schedule forecast communicated informally as an approved time extension or a contractual notice
  • bypass the architect, engineer, owner representative, contractor, or permit authority when their role controls a communication channel
  • disclose payroll, medical, personnel, security, access-code, or other confidential project information outside the restrictions established in Step 18 of the procedure below

Any conflict between an informal communication and the contractually required notice method must be identified and resolved in writing before the informal communication is treated as controlling.

Trade Note

Every communication plan in this chapter is built from the executed agreement's notice provisions and the stakeholder list approved in Project 01 — never from an assumption about how a particular owner or trade prefers to communicate. Treating a convenient channel as controlling, rather than the channel the contract or project rule requires, is the single most common source of the missed-notice and unauthorized-direction problems diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin issuing trade or supplier notifications (Step 12 and Step 13) until the channel definitions and authority rules (Steps 3–7) are approved. A notification issued before these steps are complete must be reissued once the plan is approved, and any direction it contained must be reconfirmed through the correct channel.

Shop Tip

State the controlling channel for every subject in one sentence a new team member could follow without asking. "Schedule delays are communicated through the weekly written schedule notice; a verbal mention in a hallway conversation is not the notice" is clear. "Keep everyone updated on schedule" is not, because it does not say which channel controls when messages conflict.

Channels, Authority, and the Controlling-Channel Rule

Every communication plan must state, for each subject, which channel is controlling when duplicate or conflicting messages exist. Write the controlling-channel rule immediately next to the channel it governs so a reader cannot mistake a convenient message for the one that carries contractual or scheduling effect. A subject left without a stated controlling channel becomes a disputed notice once a deadline or delay is contested.

Documentation Cue — Record Sender, Recipient, Subject, and File Location

Every communication register entry must carry a date, sender, recipient, subject, type, required action, due date, and file location. Do not log a communication without its file location. Recording only the subject line, without the file reference, allows a critical notice or decision to become unlocatable later in the project.

Critical Scope Cue — Prevent Unauthorized Owner-to-Trade Direction

Do not allow an owner request made directly to a trade or supplier to be treated as authorization to change scope, price, or schedule. Every such request must be routed through the authorized channel and decision maker identified in Step 6, confirmed in writing, and evaluated through the change-control process from Project 11 before work proceeds.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Contact Information Before Distribution

Confirm the current name, role, organization, and preferred contact method for every stakeholder before a distribution list is used. A notification routed to an outdated contact, or missing a required recipient, delays action and creates confusion about who received what information.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow the Executed Agreement's Notice Terms

Where the executed agreement specifies a particular method, address, or timeframe for a formal notice, follow that method exactly. Do not substitute an informal communication channel, such as a group message, for the notice method the contract requires, even when it would be faster.

E. Preparation — Identify Stakeholders and Define Channels

  1. Identify every stakeholder and the information they need, provide, approve, or receive. Build this list from the Project 01 stakeholder list and confirm it is current.
  2. Identify contractual notice addresses, representatives, and required delivery methods. Record these directly from the executed agreement.
  3. Define communication channels for emergencies, formal notices, owner decisions, field coordination, schedule updates, RFIs, changes, billing, safety, quality, and closeout. State the channel for each subject individually.
  4. Define which channel is controlling when duplicate messages exist. State the rule directly next to each channel definition.
  5. Define response-time expectations and escalation paths. State the expected response time and the next contact if it is missed.
  6. Define who may issue direction to subcontractors, suppliers, designers, and field staff. Name the role or person for each category.

F. Authority, Owner Decisions, and Schedule Notices

  1. Prevent unauthorized direct owner-to-trade scope changes. State the rule and the required routing through the change-control process.
  2. Create an owner-decision log for selections, approvals, access, changes, and deferred items. Set up the log fields before recording begins.
  3. Record decision requested, options, cost and time effect, due date, decision maker, response, and distribution. Complete every field for each logged decision.
  4. Create a schedule-notice procedure for baseline issue, updates, lookaheads, delays, recovery, and milestone changes. State the required frequency and recipients for each notice type.
  5. Distinguish forecast information from approved time extension or contractual notice. Label every schedule communication with its correct status.
  6. Create trade-notification templates for mobilization, readiness, delivery, inspection, delay, correction, protection, and demobilization. Draft one template per subject.
  7. Create supplier-notification templates for release, shipping, delivery, damage, shortage, replacement, and return. Draft one template per subject.
  8. Create a field-conversation confirmation process. Define the form and the timeframe for confirming a verbal conversation in writing.
  9. Confirm important telephone or verbal directions in writing. Apply the confirmation process to every direction that could affect scope, cost, schedule, or safety.
  10. Distinguish observation, recommendation, request, instruction, decision, and approval. Label every logged communication with its correct category.

G. Distribution, Confidentiality, and Special Conditions

  1. Create distribution lists by subject and project phase. Build one list per subject rather than a single all-purpose list.
  2. Protect confidential information and restrict payroll, medical, personnel, pricing, claims, security, access-code, and financial records. Apply the company confidentiality rule to each restricted category.
  3. Define photograph and video sharing rules for occupied homes and private property. State what may be shared, with whom, and under what restriction.
  4. Define document-transmittal and acknowledgment requirements. State which documents require a recorded acknowledgment of receipt.
  5. Define communication during outages, emergencies, incidents, utility strikes, leaks, weather events, and site closures. State the emergency channel and required notifications.
  6. Define communication with inspectors, utilities, neighbors, HOA, and public authorities through authorized representatives. Name the authorized representative for each external party.
  7. Define meeting, RFI, submittal, change, inspection, and closeout record integration. State how this plan's register connects to the existing logs from Projects 09, 10, 11, and 16.
  8. Create a communication register with date, sender, recipient, subject, type, required action, due date, and file location. Set up the register before routine communication begins.

H. Issue, Operate, and Audit the Communication Plan

  1. Use clear subject lines and project identifiers. Apply this standard to every message sent through a defined channel.
  2. Avoid ambiguous pronouns, undocumented attachments, and unreferenced field locations. Review outgoing messages against this standard before sending.
  3. Issue current schedule and project status at the approved frequency. Confirm the frequency matches the schedule-notice procedure from Step 10.
  4. Notify affected parties promptly of material delay, access change, inspection result, design clarification, or approved scope change. Route each notice through its defined channel.
  5. Do not distribute unapproved drafts as field direction. Confirm a document's approval status before it is distributed as controlling.
  6. Request acknowledgment for critical instructions and decisions. Apply the transmittal and acknowledgment requirement from Step 20.
  7. Monitor overdue responses and escalate according to the plan. Apply the escalation path defined in Step 5.
  8. Reconcile conflicting communications and issue one controlling clarification. Apply the controlling-channel rule from Step 4.
  9. File records promptly in the job system. Confirm every communication is entered in the register at the time it is sent or received.
  10. Audit a sample of owner, trade, supplier, and formal communications for completeness. Select a representative sample and check it against the register and confidentiality rules.
  11. Correct channel misuse, missing confirmation, unauthorized direction, and distribution gaps. Document each correction and the date it was made.
  12. Archive the communication plan, register, decisions, notices, and final project correspondence. Preserve the complete record for use in Project 18 and Project 19.

Common Communication Plan Inspection

Inspect every communication plan using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Channel check: confirm every listed subject has one stated controlling channel and a defined escalation path.
  • Authority check: confirm direction authority is named for subcontractors, suppliers, designers, and field staff, and that no unauthorized owner-to-trade direction is recorded.
  • Decision check: confirm every owner-decision log entry contains options, cost and time effect, due date, decision maker, response, and distribution.
  • Confirmation check: confirm important verbal or telephone direction is confirmed in writing before it is treated as controlling.
  • Confidentiality check: confirm payroll, medical, personnel, security, and financial records are restricted according to the confidentiality rule.
  • Register and audit check: confirm the communication register is current and that a sample audit found no uncorrected channel misuse, missing confirmation, or distribution gap.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Trade Begins Work Based on a Direct Owner Request

Probable cause: the authority rule from Step 6 was not communicated to the trade, or the owner's request was treated as equivalent to written authorization.
Confirmation: check whether a written change record exists for the work and whether it was routed through the person identified in Step 6.
Correction: stop work pending written authorization where safely possible, document the request, and route it through the change-control process from Project 11.
Reinspection: confirm the authority rule has been reissued to the trade and acknowledged.

A Schedule Delay Notice Is Disputed as Never Having Been Given

Probable cause: the delay was communicated through an informal channel instead of the schedule-notice procedure from Step 10, or the message was not distinguished as a forecast versus a formal notice.
Confirmation: check the communication register for a schedule-notice entry matching the date and subject, and confirm which channel it was sent through.
Correction: reissue the notice through the controlling channel identified in Step 4, and confirm receipt from the required recipient.
Reinspection: repeat the schedule-notice check against the procedure for the remainder of the project.

Two Parties Give Conflicting Instructions on the Same Subject

Probable cause: the controlling-channel rule from Step 4 was not defined for that subject, or it was defined but not followed.
Confirmation: check the channel definitions for the subject and confirm which channel is stated as controlling.
Correction: issue one controlling clarification through the correct channel, and notify all parties who received the conflicting instructions.
Reinspection: repeat the channel check for the disputed subject and confirm no further conflicting messages were sent.

Confidential Information Is Found in an Unrestricted Distribution

Probable cause: the confidentiality rule from Step 18 was not applied to the specific document or message, or the distribution list from Step 17 included a recipient without access rights.
Confirmation: check the distributed communication against the confidentiality categories and the distribution list used.
Correction: restrict further distribution, notify management according to company policy, and correct the distribution list.
Reinspection: repeat the confidentiality check on a new sample of communications involving the same subject.

A Verbal Direction Is Later Denied by the Party Who Gave It

Probable cause: the field-conversation confirmation process from Step 15 was not applied to the conversation.
Confirmation: check whether a written confirmation was sent following the conversation and whether it was acknowledged.
Correction: document the conversation as accurately as possible from available records, and require written confirmation for all future direction from that party.
Reinspection: confirm the confirmation process is being applied consistently going forward.

A Critical Communication Cannot Be Located

Probable cause: the communication was not entered in the register at Step 24, or it was filed without the required file-location reference.
Confirmation: check the communication register for the entry and confirm whether the file-location field is complete.
Correction: locate the original communication, enter it in the register with a complete file location, and correct the filing procedure with the person responsible.
Reinspection: repeat the register-completeness check on a new sample of recent communications.

An Overdue Response Is Not Escalated

Probable cause: the escalation path from Step 5 was not applied, or overdue responses were not being monitored as required in Step 31.
Confirmation: check the communication register for the original request date and confirm whether the response-time expectation was exceeded without escalation.
Correction: escalate the item immediately according to the defined path and document the escalation.
Reinspection: confirm overdue-response monitoring is being performed at the required frequency going forward.

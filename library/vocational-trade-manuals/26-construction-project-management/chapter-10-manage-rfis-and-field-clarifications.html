Job Overview

This project qualifies you to identify, write, route, track, and close a formal request for information or approved field clarification on a residential project without making an unauthorized design or scope decision in the field. The finished work product is one closed and auditable RFI record: a numbered question with document references, a tracked routing and response history, a distributed and incorporated response, and an evaluated cost and schedule impact.

You will confirm that a genuine ambiguity, conflict, or omission exists rather than an already-defined coordination task, inspect and document the affected field condition, write a factual question tied to specific document references, route it through the contractually required channel, track it to a timely response, verify the response actually answers the question, distribute it to every affected party, update the active field documents, and close the record only after field implementation is verified.

This project builds directly on the document-control discipline established in Project 02 and the sequencing logic from Project 04, and it feeds directly into the change-control system in Project 11: an RFI response that affects cost or time becomes a potential-change record the moment that effect is identified.

Where This Skill Fits

The RFI process protects the scope baseline approved in Project 01 from unauthorized field improvisation. Every RFI in this project must be checked against the current documents organized in the Project 02 job file and the sequencing logic built in Project 04 before it is written, so that a question already answered by an existing document is never submitted as new. When a response changes cost, time, or scope, it becomes the first entry in the Project 11 change-control system rather than a silent field adjustment.

The distribution and document-update discipline taught here — closing an RFI only after the response is incorporated into the active drawing set, schedule, and affected logs — is the same discipline used later for change orders, inspection corrections, and every other controlled field document in this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming that a document conflict, omission, dimension issue, coordination issue, or required design clarification is genuine before submitting an RFI
  • inspecting the field condition and collecting supporting photographs, measurements, and sketches
  • writing a factual RFI with exact document references, affected-work description, and a required response date
  • routing the RFI through the contractually required channel and tracking it by age and schedule impact
  • receiving, verifying, and recording the response, and identifying any new effects it creates
  • distributing the response to every affected party and obtaining acknowledgment for critical changes
  • updating active field documents, logs, and schedules to reflect the incorporated response
  • verifying field implementation and closing the RFI record

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • make a design, engineering, or code-compliance decision in place of the architect, engineer, or authority having jurisdiction
  • answer a design question through unauthorized field improvisation while a response is pending
  • bypass the architect, engineer, owner representative, contractor, or permit authority when their role controls the response
  • use an RFI to transfer ordinary contractor means, methods, coordination, or already-defined scope to the designer
  • treat a response as authority to proceed beyond its stated scope, or treat an unauthorized proposal embedded in a question as an approved solution

Any conflict between the current drawings, specifications, schedules, addenda, and prior RFI responses must be identified and referenced specifically in the RFI rather than resolved by field assumption.

Trade Note

An RFI is a document-control instrument, not a coordination shortcut. Before drafting one, confirm the answer is not already available in the current drawings, specifications, schedules, details, addenda, submittals, or a prior RFI response — checking this first is the single step that most reduces disputed or duplicate RFIs later in the chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not draft the RFI (Step 6) until the field inspection and document check (Steps 1–5) are complete. An RFI submitted before these steps are complete must be withdrawn and reissued once the required references and field information are confirmed.

Shop Tip

State the observed conflict, not the preferred fix. "Detail A-4.2 shows a 6-inch header depth; the framing plan on S-2.1 calls for an 8-inch header at the same opening" is a clear RFI. "Please confirm the header size is correct" without the reference is not, because it gives the responder nothing specific to check.

Genuine Ambiguity Versus Means-and-Methods

An RFI exists to resolve document ambiguity, conflict, omission, or a required design clarification — not to transfer ordinary contractor means, methods, sequencing, or already-defined scope to the designer. Before drafting, confirm the question cannot be answered from current drawings, specifications, schedules, details, addenda, submittals, or a prior RFI, and confirm it is not a coordination decision the contractor is responsible to make.

Documentation Cue — Record the RFI Number, Reference, and Response Date

Every RFI must carry a unique number, the exact document reference it concerns, and the required response date established from the schedule. Do not distribute an unnumbered question as though it were a tracked RFI. Recording only the question, without the controlling reference and date, prevents the item from being tracked for age and schedule impact.

Critical RFI Cue — No Unauthorized Field Improvisation

Do not answer a design question through unauthorized field improvisation while a response is pending. If work in the affected area cannot safely continue without the answer, hold that work and identify which unaffected areas may safely proceed. Proceeding on a field guess is a leading cause of rework and disputed cost documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Field Coordination Cue — Notify Affected Trades of the Work Hold

Notify every trade whose work is affected by a pending RFI, and identify exactly what is held and what may continue. A trade that is not notified may install over, around, or in conflict with the unresolved condition, creating additional rework once the response is received.

Contract Instruction Cue — Route Through the Contractually Required Channel

Where the executed agreement specifies a particular RFI channel, form, platform, or addressee, follow that method exactly. Do not bypass the architect, engineer, owner representative, contractor, or permit authority when their role controls the response, even when a faster informal answer seems available.

E. Preparation — Confirm the Ambiguity and Inspect the Field Condition

  1. Identify a genuine ambiguity, conflict, omission, dimension issue, coordination issue, or required design clarification. Confirm the issue affects actual field work rather than a hypothetical condition.
  2. Confirm the answer is not already available in current drawings, specifications, schedules, details, addenda, submittals, or prior RFIs. Search the current document register and RFI log before proceeding.
  3. Confirm the issue is not solely contractor means, methods, sequencing, or an already-defined responsibility. Do not submit an RFI for a decision the contractor is responsible to make.
  4. Inspect the field condition and collect photographs, measurements, sketches, and affected-area information. Date and locate every piece of evidence collected.
  5. Identify the exact drawing, detail, note, specification, schedule, and prior communication references. Record each reference by sheet number, detail number, and revision.

F. Draft and Route the RFI

  1. Describe the observed conflict or missing information factually. State exactly what the documents show or fail to show, without characterizing intent.
  2. Describe the affected work, trade, area, milestone, material, inspection, cost, and schedule condition. Identify everything that depends on the response.
  3. Do not conceal a preferred solution inside a misleading question. If a solution is suggested, label it separately as a proposal.
  4. Present a proposed solution only when authorized and label it clearly as a proposal. Confirm authorization before including a proposed solution.
  5. Identify the date by which a response is needed to avoid impact. Calculate this date from the current schedule or lookahead.
  6. Identify whether work can continue safely in unaffected areas. State this explicitly in the RFI record.
  7. Identify temporary protection or hold requirements pending response. Record what must be protected or held and by whom.
  8. Assign the RFI number and enter it in the log. Use the project's controlling numbering convention.
  9. Route the RFI through the contractually required channel. Confirm the channel matches the executed agreement.
  10. Do not bypass the architect, engineer, owner representative, contractor, or permit authority when their role controls the response. Confirm the addressee holds the required authority before submitting.

G. Track, Receive, and Verify the Response

  1. Record submission date, responsible responder, contractual response time, and required response date. Enter each field in the RFI log.
  2. Notify affected trades that the item is pending and identify the work hold. Confirm every affected trade has received this notice.
  3. Track the RFI by age and schedule impact. Update the log at each schedule update and coordination meeting.
  4. Escalate overdue RFIs through the approved notice process. Do not allow an overdue RFI to remain unescalated past the required response date.
  5. Receive the response and verify it answers the complete question. Compare the response against every part of the original question.
  6. Identify new ambiguity, cost, time, scope, safety, permit, or coordination effects created by the response. Record any new effect as a separate finding.
  7. Request clarification when the response is incomplete or unauthorized. Do not treat an incomplete or unauthorized response as final.
  8. Confirm whether revised drawings, sketches, calculations, permit revisions, or product approvals are required. Identify each required follow-up document.
  9. Do not treat a response as authority to proceed beyond its stated scope. Confirm the response's scope before authorizing any field action based on it.

H. Distribute, Incorporate, and Close the RFI

  1. Distribute the response to every affected party. Use the distribution list identified in Step 7.
  2. Obtain acknowledgment from field supervision and responsible trades for critical changes. Confirm acknowledgment is recorded, not assumed.
  3. Mark or replace affected field documents according to document-control rules. Follow the Project 02 document-control system exactly.
  4. Update the drawing register, revision log, submittals, procurement, WBS, schedule, lookahead, quality plan, inspection plan, and safety plan as affected. Update every log the response actually affects.
  5. Create a potential-change record when cost or time may change. Route this record into the Project 11 change-control system.
  6. Photograph or document field implementation where appropriate. Record evidence that the response was actually built as answered.
  7. Verify the work conforms to the response and approved documents. Inspect the completed or in-progress work against the incorporated response.
  8. Close the RFI only after distribution, incorporation, impact evaluation, and field resolution are complete. Do not close a record with any of these four conditions outstanding.
  9. Preserve the question, attachments, response, distribution, changes, and closure evidence in the job file. File the complete record in the Project 02 job file under the controlling RFI section.

Common RFI Record Inspection

Inspect every RFI record using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Ambiguity check: confirm the question was genuinely unanswered by current documents and was not a means-and-methods question before it was submitted.
  • Reference check: confirm every document reference cited is exact — sheet, detail, note, specification section, and revision.
  • Routing check: confirm the RFI was submitted through the contractually required channel to the responsible responder.
  • Tracking check: confirm age and required response date were tracked and overdue items were escalated.
  • Response check: confirm the response was verified as complete and authorized before being treated as controlling.
  • Distribution and incorporation check: confirm every affected party received the response and every affected field document was updated before closure.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Trade Proceeds on a Field Guess While an RFI Is Pending

Probable cause: the work hold from Step 12 was not communicated to the affected trade, or the trade was not included in the notification required by Step 17.
Confirmation: check the RFI record for a documented work-hold notice and confirm the trade in question was on the distribution list.
Correction: stop the affected work where safely possible, document the field condition as installed, and route the discrepancy through the RFI response and, if needed, the Project 11 change-control process.
Reinspection: repeat the work-hold notification check for all currently pending RFIs.

An RFI Is Rejected as a Means-and-Methods Question

Probable cause: Step 3 was not completed before drafting, or the RFI asked the designer to make a contractor-responsibility decision.
Confirmation: re-read the question against the standard in Step 3 and confirm whether it asks for a design clarification or a contractor coordination decision.
Correction: withdraw or revise the RFI to state only the genuine document ambiguity, or resolve the coordination decision internally without resubmitting.
Reinspection: repeat the ambiguity check on the revised RFI before resubmission.

The RFI Response Is Overdue

Probable cause: the required response date from Step 16 was not tracked against the schedule, or the overdue item was not escalated under Step 19.
Confirmation: check the RFI log for the required response date and confirm whether an escalation notice was issued.
Correction: issue the required escalation notice through the approved contractual process and identify the schedule impact of the continued delay.
Reinspection: repeat the age and escalation check across the full RFI log, not only the item in question.

The Response Is Treated as Authorizing More Than It States

Probable cause: Step 24 was not applied, and field staff assumed a response to one condition authorized a broader change.
Confirmation: compare the field action taken against the exact scope of the response as recorded.
Correction: stop any work exceeding the response's stated scope, submit a new RFI or potential-change record for the additional condition, and correct the field record.
Reinspection: repeat the response-scope check for the corrected work.

A Trade Is Working From an Unincorporated Document

Probable cause: Step 27 was not completed before the response was distributed, or the affected drawing sheet or log was not identified in Step 28.
Confirmation: check the document register for the current revision status of the affected sheet or log and compare it against the RFI response date.
Correction: mark or replace the affected document immediately, redistribute it to every affected party, and confirm receipt.
Reinspection: repeat the document-incorporation check for every document listed as affected in the RFI record.

An RFI Is Closed Before Field Implementation Is Verified

Probable cause: Step 31 or Step 32 was skipped, and the record was closed based on distribution alone rather than verified field conformance.
Confirmation: check the RFI record for field verification evidence, such as a photograph or inspection note, dated after the response was incorporated.
Correction: reopen the RFI, inspect the affected work against the response and approved documents, and document the verification before closing again.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check requiring all four closure conditions from Step 32 before any RFI is marked closed.

A Cost or Time Effect From an RFI Response Is Never Priced

Probable cause: Step 29 was not completed when the response was received, so the effect was never captured as a potential-change record.
Confirmation: compare the RFI response against the affected work and check whether a potential-change record exists in the Project 11 change log.
Correction: create the potential-change record now, evaluate the cost and time effect, and route it through the required authorization process.
Reinspection: repeat the impact-evaluation check across the full RFI log to identify any other unrecorded effects.

Cleanup

This project ends with the RFI record closed and filed, not left as an open question with an unverified answer.

  • Confirm the RFI log shows the item as closed with all four closure conditions satisfied
  • Confirm every affected field document has been marked or replaced and matches the RFI response
  • Confirm distribution and, where required, acknowledgment records are complete
  • Confirm any identified cost or time effect has a corresponding potential-change record in the Project 11 log
  • Provide the closed RFI record, updated documents, and any potential-change record to Project 11 for change-control processing

Field Terms

RFI
A numbered, written request for clarification of a genuine document ambiguity, conflict, omission, or required design decision.
Field Clarification
An approved written answer to a field question that does not require a formal RFI but is still recorded and incorporated.
Controlling Response
The most recent written answer that governs the affected work, superseding prior assumptions or verbal discussion.
Work Hold
A defined stop on affected work, or a defined limit on which unaffected work may continue, pending an RFI response.
Potential Change
A record created when an RFI response is identified as affecting cost, time, or scope.
Incorporation
The act of marking, replacing, or updating an active field document so the RFI response becomes the controlling instruction.
Closure
The state of an RFI record after distribution, incorporation, impact evaluation, and verified field resolution are all complete.
Means and Methods
Contractor-responsibility decisions about how work is performed, not a proper subject for an RFI to the designer.
Escalation
The approved notice process used to raise an overdue RFI to the level required to obtain a timely response.
RFI Log
The controlled record tracking every RFI by number, date, reference, response date, and status.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and operate one inspection-management system for a residential project. Inspections are not a single event at the end of a phase — they are a coordinated sequence of required approvals from building, zoning, trade, utility, special, manufacturer, lender, and owner authorities, each with its own readiness conditions, request procedure, and correction path. The finished work product is one operating inspection register, a preinspection readiness checklist for each inspection type, requested and completed inspection records, a correction log linked to reinspection, and an approval archive preserved in the job file.

You will import the Book 25 inspection matrix and permit conditions, build a register linking every required inspection to the WBS and schedule, identify work that must remain exposed until approval, confirm readiness before requesting each inspection, request and attend inspections through the approved channel, record results accurately, track correction notices to verified reinspection, and reconcile all required inspections before final inspection and owner handoff.

This project depends directly on the scope baseline from Project 01, the job file from Project 02, the WBS from Project 03, the sequence logic from Project 04, the baseline schedule from Project 05, the quality-control plan from Project 13, and the permit-and-inspection plan carried forward from Book 25. Its output — a complete, reconciled approval record — becomes a required input to the closeout package in Project 18 and the final walkthrough in Project 19.

Where This Skill Fits

The quality-control plan built in Project 13 defines contractor QC hold points; this project coordinates the authority, trade, utility, special, manufacturer, lender, and owner inspections that QC prepares work for. The two-week lookahead from Project 06 and the coordination meetings from Project 09 both depend on accurate inspection status to sequence upcoming work correctly. The closeout package in Project 18 cannot be assembled without the complete, reconciled approval record this project produces, and the final walkthrough in Project 19 cannot proceed until required final inspections are complete or clearly documented as pending.

The distinction taught here — between contractor QC, design review, code inspection, testing, manufacturer inspection, and owner acceptance — is the same distinction that governs every quality and closeout decision for the rest of the project.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • importing the Book 25 inspection matrix, permit conditions, approved plans, and authority contacts into an active inspection register
  • identifying building, zoning, grading, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, gas, fire, energy, utility, environmental, special, and final inspections required for the project
  • identifying testing, engineer observation, manufacturer inspection, lender draw, and owner witness points
  • linking every inspection to the WBS and schedule and identifying work that must remain exposed until approval
  • building and completing a preinspection readiness checklist for each inspection type
  • requesting inspections through the approved channel and confirming attendance by required licensed trades or design professionals
  • recording inspection results, correction notices, and reinspections to verified closure
  • reconciling all required inspections before final inspection and owner handoff and archiving the complete approval record

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform, waive, or substitute for a licensed authority inspection, special inspection, engineer observation, or manufacturer inspection; those approvals remain with the licensed inspector, authority having jurisdiction, or qualified professional identified by permit or contract
  • conceal inspected or uninspected work before the required approval is recorded and any partial-approval limitations are understood
  • accept a correction as closed based on a trade's verbal statement or an uncorroborated photograph without adequate verification
  • schedule an inspection merely to obtain a correction list for work that is not genuinely complete, clean, safe, and ready

Any conflict between the permit conditions, the approved plans, and the current inspection register must be identified and resolved in writing before the affected inspection is requested.

Trade Note

Contractor quality control, built in Project 13, is the contractor's own planned verification of work before acceptance. It does not replace design review, code inspection, special inspection, testing, manufacturer inspection, or owner acceptance. This project coordinates those separate approvals; it does not substitute for any of them.

Setup Standard

Do not request an authority inspection (Step 18) until contractor QC has been completed for that scope (Step 12) and the full preinspection readiness checklist for that inspection type (Step 10) has been confirmed. An inspection requested before these steps are complete must be documented as a readiness failure, not treated as a routine correction list.

Shop Tip

Link every inspection to its exact predecessor work and successor release in the register, not only to a general schedule date. "Rough electrical inspection — after rough-in complete, before insulation" is a working link. "Electrical inspection" is not, because it does not tell the next trade when it is safe to proceed.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every inspection managed in this project follows the same sequence: identify, link, prepare, request, attend, record, correct, reinspect, archive. Learning that sequence here, applied to one residential project's full set of required inspections, means the same discipline governs the final inspection and owner-witness points at project closeout.

Contractor QC, Authority Inspection, and Owner Acceptance Are Separate

State clearly, for every inspection type entered in the register, which party performs the check and what that check does and does not establish. Contractor QC verifies the contractor's own work is ready. An authority inspection verifies code or permit compliance for the specific scope inspected. Owner acceptance verifies the owner is satisfied the contracted work is complete. Treating one of these as a substitute for another is a leading cause of concealed work that later fails inspection or is disputed at handoff.

Documentation Cue — Preserve the Official Inspection Result as Controlling

Record inspection date, inspector, type, result, approved areas, any partial approval, and verbal comments, but treat the official written or system-recorded result as the controlling record. Do not rely on a verbal comment alone to establish that work has been approved.

Critical Scope Cue — Do Not Conceal Work Before Approval Is Recorded

Do not cover, close, backfill, or otherwise conceal work that requires inspection until the required approval is recorded and any partial-approval limitations are understood. If corrections are issued, do not conceal the corrected work before the reinspection approval is recorded. Concealing inspected or uninspected work before approval is one of the most difficult and costly failures to correct once discovered.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Access, Occupancy, and Security Before Requesting

Confirm owner access, occupant schedules, security systems, and site conditions before requesting an inspection at an occupied home or a site with limited access. An inspector who cannot access the area on the scheduled date creates an avoidable delay and, in some jurisdictions, an avoidable fee.

Authority Alert — Confirm Jurisdiction-Specific Request Rules

Inspection request lead time, cutoff time, cancellation rules, access requirements, and fee schedules vary by jurisdiction and inspection type. Confirm the current rule with the specific authority before assuming a prior project's procedure still applies.

E. Preparation — Import Requirements and Build the Register

  1. Import the Book 25 inspection matrix, permit conditions, approved plans, and authority contacts. Confirm the imported information matches the current permit and plan revision.
  2. Identify building, zoning, grading, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, gas, fire, energy, utility, environmental, special, and final inspections required. List every inspection type individually rather than as a general category.
  3. Identify testing, engineer observation, manufacturer inspection, lender draw, and owner witness points. Record each as its own register entry alongside the authority inspections.
  4. Create an inspection register with permit, type, area, predecessor work, required documents, test, responsible trade, request method, and status. Populate one row per required inspection.
  5. Link each inspection to the WBS and schedule. Confirm the linkage identifies the exact predecessor work package and successor release condition.
  6. Identify work that must remain exposed until approval. Record the exact scope and location that must not be concealed before the linked inspection is approved.

F. Confirm Readiness Before Each Request

  1. Identify partial, phased, underground, above-ceiling, pressure, pre-pour, pre-cover, and final inspection conditions. Record the specific condition governing each inspection type in the register.
  2. Confirm approved plans, permit card, engineering, truss documents, product approvals, test forms, and revisions are on site. Verify the documents present match the current approved revision.
  3. Confirm inspection request lead time, cutoff, cancellation, access, contact, and fee rules. Record the current rule for the specific authority and inspection type.
  4. Create a preinspection readiness checklist for each inspection type. Include documents, cleanliness, safety, access, and required test equipment.
  5. Assign internal preinspection responsibility. Name the person who confirms readiness before a request is submitted.
  6. Complete contractor QC before requesting authority inspection. Confirm the linked quality-control hold point from Project 13 has been passed.
  7. Confirm the full inspection scope is complete, clean, safe, lit, accessible, and test-ready. Walk the exact area to be inspected before submitting the request.
  8. Confirm ladders, platforms, excavation protection, panels, valves, test gauges, equipment, and identification are available. Stage every item the inspector will need to perform the inspection.
  9. Confirm required licensed trades, competent persons, special inspectors, or design professionals will attend. Obtain a specific commitment rather than assuming availability.
  10. Confirm the work matches current approved documents. Check for any revision issued after the work was installed.
  11. Do not schedule merely to obtain a correction list for incomplete work. Verify readiness against the checklist before submitting the request, not after.

G. Request, Attend, and Record

  1. Request the inspection through the approved channel and record confirmation. Enter the request date and confirmation in the register.
  2. Notify trades, owner, occupants, security, and site supervision. Confirm access and schedule coordination in advance of the inspection date.
  3. Protect the inspection area from concealment, damage, shutdown, or material storage. Maintain the readiness condition until the inspection occurs.
  4. Attend the inspection with current documents and factual answers. Provide only confirmed information; do not speculate on unconfirmed conditions.
  5. Record inspection date, inspector, type, result, approved areas, partial approval, verbal comments, and official record. Complete every field before closing the register entry.
  6. Preserve the official inspection result as controlling. Do not rely on a verbal comment alone as the record of approval.
  7. Photograph approval marks or retained conditions where permitted. Confirm photography is authorized before photographing occupied or security-sensitive areas.

H. Track Corrections, Reinspect, and Archive

  1. If corrections are issued, create correction-log items with exact citation or note, location, responsible party, and due date. Record each correction individually rather than as a general list.
  2. Obtain design clarification or permit revision when correction requires it. Route the item through the RFI or change process rather than improvising a field solution.
  3. Verify corrective work internally before reinspection. Confirm the correction meets the cited requirement before requesting reinspection.
  4. Request reinspection and record fees or schedule effects. Enter the reinspection request, date, and any fee or schedule impact in the register.
  5. Do not conceal the corrected work before approval. Maintain the exposed condition until the reinspection result is recorded.
  6. Update the schedule, lookahead, cost, change, quality, and communication logs. Reflect the inspection result and any correction impact across the connected project-control systems.
  7. Record approval, conditional approval, partial approval, expiration, or remaining hold. Confirm the recorded status matches the official result exactly.
  8. Distribute approval status to affected trades. Notify the successor trade that the area is released according to the recorded status.
  9. Maintain permits, inspection cards, reports, tests, letters, certificates, and utility releases in the active job file. File each record as it is received rather than reconstructing the archive later.
  10. Reconcile all required inspections before final inspection and owner handoff. Confirm every register entry shows a final approved status or a documented, agreed exception.
  11. Archive the complete approval record in the closeout package. Provide the reconciled register and supporting documents to Project 18.

Common Inspection-Record Inspection

Inspect every inspection register using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Identification check: confirm every required authority, trade, utility, special, manufacturer, lender, and owner inspection is listed individually with its controlling reference.
  • Linkage check: confirm every inspection is linked to a WBS package, predecessor completion condition, and schedule date.
  • Readiness check: confirm contractor QC and the preinspection checklist were completed before each request was submitted.
  • Result check: confirm date, inspector, type, result, approved areas, and any partial-approval limitation are recorded for every completed inspection.
  • Correction check: confirm every correction is tracked with a responsible party, due date, and independently verified reinspection.
  • Archive check: confirm permits, cards, reports, tests, letters, and certificates are filed in the active job file and reconciled before final inspection.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Inspection Is Requested and Fails Immediately

Probable cause: the preinspection readiness checklist in Step 10 was not completed, or contractor QC in Step 12 was skipped for the linked scope.
Confirmation: check the register entry for a completed readiness checklist and a recorded QC sign-off before the request date.
Correction: complete the readiness checklist and internal QC before resubmitting the request, and document why the original request was submitted early.
Reinspection: repeat the readiness check for the corrected scope before requesting reinspection.

Work Is Concealed Before the Inspection Result Is Recorded

Probable cause: the area-protection requirement in Step 20 was not communicated to the trade, or a successor trade proceeded without confirming approval status.
Confirmation: check the register for a recorded result and compare it against the date the concealing work began.
Correction: stop the concealing work where safely possible, expose the area if required by the authority, and obtain the inspection or engineering evaluation needed to establish compliance.
Reinspection: repeat the concealment-protection check for the remaining inspection points in the register.

A Correction Item Is Marked Closed but Later Fails Reinspection

Probable cause: the correction in Step 27 was accepted based on a trade's statement rather than independent internal verification before reinspection was requested.
Confirmation: check the correction log for a documented internal verification step and compare the corrected condition against the cited requirement.
Correction: reopen the correction item, verify the actual corrected condition, and request reinspection only after verification is documented.
Reinspection: repeat the correction-verification check across the full correction log, not only the failed item.

An Inspector Cannot Access the Work Area on the Scheduled Date

Probable cause: owner, occupant, or security coordination in Step 19 was not confirmed before the request was submitted.
Confirmation: check the register for a documented access confirmation and compare it against the actual site condition on the inspection date.
Correction: reschedule through the approved channel, confirm access in writing before the new date, and record any resulting fee or schedule effect.
Reinspection: repeat the access-confirmation step for every remaining occupied-area inspection.

Successor Work Begins Before the Predecessor Inspection Is Approved

Probable cause: the WBS and schedule linkage in Step 5 was not communicated to the successor trade, or the two-week lookahead was not updated with the inspection status.
Confirmation: check the register's linked predecessor status and compare it against the successor trade's actual start date.
Correction: stop the successor work where safely possible, confirm the predecessor inspection status, and do not resume until approval is recorded or an authorized exception is documented.
Reinspection: repeat the linkage check for every inspection with an active successor trade.

The Closeout Package Cannot Be Assembled Because an Approval Record Is Missing

Probable cause: the archive step in Step 33 was not completed as records were received, so a permit, report, or certificate was never filed in the active job file.
Confirmation: check the approval archive index against the completed register entries and identify the specific missing document.
Correction: request a duplicate or replacement record from the issuing authority or professional, and file it in the archive immediately upon receipt.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check across the complete register before resubmitting the closeout package.

A Reinspection Fee or Schedule Delay Is Disputed

Probable cause: the original correction notice in Step 25 was not recorded with an exact citation, location, and due date, making it unclear whether the delay was caused by a genuine correction or an avoidable readiness failure.
Confirmation: check the correction log for the citation, the responsible party, and the date the correction was completed and verified.
Correction: document the sequence of events from original inspection through correction and reinspection, and route any resulting cost or schedule question through the Project 11 change-control process.
Reinspection: repeat the correction-log completeness check for all open and recently closed items.

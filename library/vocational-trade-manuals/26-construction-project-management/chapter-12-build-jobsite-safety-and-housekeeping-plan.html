Job Overview

This project qualifies you to create and issue a project-specific jobsite safety and housekeeping coordination plan — the document that identifies phase-specific hazards, establishes site logistics and access rules, sets daily and recurring housekeeping standards, defines material-storage zones, and creates a recurring inspection and corrective-action process for one residential project. The finished work product is one issued plan with a site logistics diagram, hazard identification by phase, an inspection checklist, and a documented corrective-action log, reviewed and acknowledged by field supervision and subcontractors.

You will collect company and subcontractor safety programs, hazard assessments, and authority requirements; identify project phases and their associated hazards; build a site logistics plan for access, parking, deliveries, and emergency response; establish housekeeping, storage, and daily-closing standards; conduct an initial site-readiness inspection before mobilization; and set up the recurring inspection and correction cycle that continues through demobilization.

This project depends directly on the scope baseline from Project 01, the WBS from Project 03, and the schedule from Project 05, which identify the phases and high-risk work this plan must address. It feeds the quality-control plan in Project 13, the coordination meetings in Project 09, and the two-week lookahead in Project 06, where upcoming high-risk activities must be checked against this plan before work is committed.

Where This Skill Fits

This plan coordinates safety and housekeeping across trades; it does not create or transfer the safety obligations that belong to employers, competent persons, licensed contractors, and permit holders under law and contract. Every hazard control, competent-person assignment, and training requirement in this chapter comes from the governing safety programs you collect in Step 2 — this project organizes and coordinates those programs at the project level, it does not invent them.

The site logistics plan you build here becomes the reference every later trade-coordination activity in this book uses for access, laydown, and exclusion zones — including the trade handoffs in Project 08 and the recurring coordination meetings in Project 09. The recurring inspection process you set up here is the direct model for the quality-control inspection points built in Project 13.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting company safety programs, subcontractor plans, hazard assessments, permits, emergency information, and authority requirements
  • identifying project phases, high-risk work, occupied areas, public interfaces, and hazards applicable to the project
  • building a site logistics plan for access, parking, deliveries, pedestrian and equipment routes, emergency access, and the muster point
  • establishing access rules, orientation and sign-in procedures, and posted emergency information
  • coordinating first aid, sanitation, lighting, and weather-protection provisions through the responsible safety program
  • establishing material-storage zones and daily housekeeping standards
  • creating a recurring inspection checklist and corrective-action process, and completing the initial site-readiness inspection before mobilization

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform the duties of a competent person, qualified person, or licensed safety professional; those roles must be filled by the individuals identified in Step 6, per the governing safety program and applicable law
  • set fall-protection, excavation, electrical, hot-work, confined-space, or other technical hazard-control specifications; those specifications come from the responsible employer's safety program and applicable regulation, not from this coordination plan
  • direct unsafe work, conceal a hazard, bypass a control, or allow schedule pressure to override a safety requirement
  • treat this project-coordination plan as a substitute for any subcontractor's or employer's own safety program, training, or legal responsibility

Any condition that presents an imminent hazard must be stopped and escalated through the approved safety process immediately; do not wait for the next scheduled inspection cycle described in this chapter.

Trade Note

Safety management remains the responsibility of employers, competent persons, licensed contractors, permit holders, and other parties as established by law and contract. This project coordinates site conditions, communication, and inspection across those parties. It does not transfer their duties, and it does not authorize the learner to perform work that requires a license, certification, or competent-person designation the learner does not hold.

Setup Standard

Do not issue the plan until the initial site-readiness inspection (Step 32) is complete and every access, protection, sanitation, emergency, storage, and housekeeping deficiency identified in that inspection has been corrected. A plan issued before the site-readiness inspection is complete must be corrected and reissued before mobilization proceeds.

Shop Tip

Write every stop-work condition as an exact trigger and an exact required response. "Stop work in the excavation if the protective system is not in place" is usable. "Be careful around the excavation" is not, because it gives the crew nothing to check and nothing to do.

Coordination Without Transfer of Duty

Every hazard control this plan references must trace back to a governing safety program, employer policy, or authority requirement identified in Step 2. This plan schedules, communicates, and verifies those controls at the site level. It does not create a new control that no governing program requires, and it does not remove a control a governing program does require.

Critical Safety Cue — Imminent Hazards Are Stopped and Escalated Immediately

Do not wait for a scheduled inspection to address an imminent hazard. Stop the affected work, secure the area, and escalate through the approved safety process defined in Step 28 immediately. The recurring inspection cycle in this chapter supplements, and does not replace, the duty to stop and escalate an imminent hazard the moment it is observed.

Code Alert — Verify Authority Requirements Before Assuming a Control Is Standard

Fall-protection, excavation, electrical, and other technical hazard-control requirements vary by jurisdiction and by the specific conditions of the work. Confirm the applicable requirement with the responsible competent person, employer safety program, or authority having jurisdiction rather than assuming a control used on a prior project applies here.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Access Before Posting the Site Logistics Plan

Confirm parking, delivery routes, and pedestrian paths with neighboring property owners, the municipality, and any HOA before posting the site logistics plan. A logistics plan that conflicts with a public right-of-way or a neighboring access easement will need correction after workers and deliveries have already begun using it.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow Each Subcontractor's Safety Plan for Their Own Scope

Where a subcontractor's own safety plan governs their scope of work, coordinate around that plan rather than substituting a generic project rule. This project's plan sets the shared site conditions; it does not override a subcontractor's specific program for their own crew and equipment.

E. Preparation — Identify Responsibility and Collect Governing Programs

  1. Identify the controlling contractor, employer, competent persons, permit holders, and safety responsibilities established by law and contract. Record each by name or role before drafting begins.
  2. Collect company safety programs, subcontractor plans, hazard assessments, permits, emergency information, and authority requirements. Assemble every governing document before hazard content is written.
  3. Clarify that the project plan coordinates safety but does not transfer employer duties. State this clearly in the plan's introduction section.
  4. Identify project phases, high-risk work, occupied areas, public interfaces, neighboring property, and emergency access. Draw phases directly from the current WBS and schedule.
  5. Identify excavation, fall, electrical, fire, hot-work, tool, lifting, silica, lead, asbestos, confined-space, weather, traffic, and equipment hazards as applicable. Map each identified hazard to the phase and area where it occurs.
  6. Identify required competent persons, qualified persons, training, certifications, and permits. Record the individual or role assigned to each requirement.

F. Site Logistics, Access, and Emergency Information

  1. Create a site logistics plan showing entrances, exits, fencing, gates, parking, deliveries, pedestrian routes, equipment routes, emergency access, and muster point. Confirm the plan against neighboring access before posting it.
  2. Create access rules for workers, visitors, owners, inspectors, delivery drivers, children, pets, and the public. State the rule for each category separately.
  3. Create an orientation and sign-in procedure. Define what the orientation must cover and how attendance is recorded.
  4. Post emergency contacts, address, utility shutoffs, evacuation routes, and incident-reporting procedure. Confirm the posted information matches the current site condition.
  5. Coordinate first aid, fire extinguishers, sanitation, drinking water, lighting, temporary heat, and weather protection. Identify the responsible party for maintaining each item.
  6. Define fall-protection, ladder, scaffold, lift, roof, opening, stair, and guard controls through the responsible safety program. Reference the governing program rather than inventing a control.

G. Hazard-Specific Coordination

  1. Define excavation, trench, soil, spoil, access, protective-system, and inspection controls where applicable. Confirm the responsible competent person and inspection frequency.
  2. Define temporary electrical, cord, panel, lighting, lockout, and wet-location controls. Reference the governing electrical safety requirement.
  3. Define hot-work permits, fire watch, combustible control, cylinder storage, and end-of-shift checks. State the permit and monitoring requirement for hot work.
  4. Define dust, silica, lead, asbestos, mold, chemical, ventilation, and respiratory coordination. Reference the governing exposure-control program.
  5. Define crane, hoisting, rigging, forklift, delivery, spotter, and exclusion-zone controls. State the exclusion-zone boundary and spotter requirement for each lifting operation.
  6. Define weather triggers for heat, cold, wind, rain, lightning, snow, ice, and air quality. State the condition that triggers a work stoppage or modification.
  7. Define occupied-home protection, temporary barriers, secure openings, utility interruptions, and daily restoration. Apply this step only where the project involves occupied or adjacent occupied space.

H. Storage, Housekeeping, and Inspection Setup

  1. Establish material-storage zones by weight, exposure, fire risk, security, and installation phase. Assign each zone to a specific location on the logistics diagram.
  2. Keep materials clear of exits, stairs, electrical panels, equipment, roads, drains, excavations, and public paths. State this as a standing rule applicable to every storage zone.
  3. Define waste streams, dumpsters, sharp-object control, hazardous waste, and disposal responsibility. Identify the responsible party for each waste stream.
  4. Create daily housekeeping standards for floors, stairs, access, cords, fasteners, dust, debris, spills, and stored tools. State each standard as an observable, measurable condition.
  5. Define end-of-shift weatherproofing, utility, fire, security, access, and housekeeping checks. Assign responsibility for completing the end-of-shift check.
  6. Define weekly or scheduled site-inspection responsibility and frequency. State the assigned inspector and the inspection interval.
  7. Create an inspection checklist with location, condition, responsible party, severity, due date, and verification. Base the checklist on the hazard and housekeeping content already established in this plan.
  8. Define immediate stop-and-correct conditions and escalation. State the exact trigger condition and the exact required response for each stop-work item.
  9. Define incident, injury, property damage, utility strike, near-miss, and environmental reporting. State the reporting timeline and the responsible recipient.
  10. Define preservation of evidence and communication rules after an incident. State what must be preserved and who may communicate externally about the incident.

I. Review, Site-Readiness Inspection, and Issuance

  1. Review the plan with field supervision, subcontractors, owner, and affected neighbors or occupants as appropriate. Distribute the draft far enough in advance for informed review.
  2. Record subcontractor acknowledgment and project-specific additions. Preserve a record of every acknowledgment and any additional hazard or control a subcontractor identifies.
  3. Conduct the initial site-readiness inspection before mobilization. Use the inspection checklist created in Step 27 as the basis for this inspection.
  4. Correct access, protection, sanitation, emergency, storage, or housekeeping deficiencies before work begins. Do not authorize mobilization until every identified deficiency is corrected and verified.
  5. Perform recurring inspections and track corrective actions. Log every finding in the corrective-action log with responsible party and due date.
  6. Review upcoming high-risk activities during lookahead and coordination meetings. Confirm this plan's applicable controls before the activity is committed to the two-week lookahead.
  7. Update the plan for phase, weather, access, incident, design, and scope changes. Issue a new revision and communicate the change rather than editing the posted plan silently.
  8. Archive orientations, inspections, corrective actions, incidents, revisions, and closeout records according to required confidentiality. Store incident and medical information according to the confidentiality rule established in Step 3 of the job-file setup.

Common Safety-Plan Inspection

Inspect every safety and housekeeping plan using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Responsibility check: confirm the controlling contractor, employer, competent persons, and permit holders are identified and that the plan states it coordinates rather than transfers their duties.
  • Hazard check: confirm every applicable hazard category is mapped to the phase and area where it occurs, sourced from the governing hazard assessments.
  • Logistics check: confirm the site logistics diagram shows access, routes, exclusion zones, and the muster point, and has been confirmed against neighboring access.
  • Access check: confirm orientation, sign-in, and access rules are defined for every category of person who may be on site.
  • Housekeeping and storage check: confirm daily standards and storage zones are measurable and assigned to a specific location.
  • Inspection and correction check: confirm the recurring inspection frequency, checklist, and stop-work conditions are defined, and that the corrective-action log tracks findings to verified closure.

Complete the Safety and Housekeeping Plan Inspection Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 13.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Material Is Stored Blocking an Exit or Electrical Panel

Probable cause: the storage zone assigned in Step 20 was not clearly marked on the logistics diagram, or material was staged temporarily without checking the storage-zone plan.
Confirmation: check the logistics diagram for the assigned storage zone and compare it against the current physical condition.
Correction: relocate the material to the assigned zone immediately, and reissue the storage-zone diagram to affected trades if the zone was unclear.
Reinspection: repeat the housekeeping and storage check across every active storage zone, not only the corrected item.

A Subcontractor's Crew Was Not Oriented Before Starting Work

Probable cause: the orientation and sign-in procedure from Step 9 was not enforced before mobilization, or a late-arriving crew member began work before completing orientation.
Confirmation: check the orientation and sign-in log for the individual's name and date.
Correction: stop the individual's work until orientation is completed, and correct the mobilization procedure to prevent unoriented access.
Reinspection: audit the sign-in log against the current site roster.

An Inspection Finding Is Not Corrected Before the Next Scheduled Inspection

Probable cause: the corrective-action log entry from Step 27 was not assigned a responsible party and due date, or the due date passed without follow-up.
Confirmation: check the corrective-action log for the item's responsible party, due date, and current status.
Correction: assign or reassign responsibility, set a new due date, and escalate through the process defined in Step 28 if the item remains open past its deadline.
Reinspection: verify the correction physically before marking the item closed in the log.

Work Continues During a Defined Weather Trigger Condition

Probable cause: the weather trigger from Step 19 was not communicated to the affected crew, or field supervision did not monitor conditions against the stated trigger.
Confirmation: compare the current or recorded weather condition against the trigger defined in the plan.
Correction: stop the affected work immediately, move personnel to a safe location, and resume only once the condition clears the stated trigger.
Reinspection: confirm the weather-trigger communication was reissued to the affected crew and acknowledged.

A Visitor or Delivery Driver Is Found in an Exclusion Zone

Probable cause: the access rules from Step 8 were not posted at the relevant entrance, or the visitor was not directed to the correct route by site staff.
Confirmation: check the posted access signage at the point of entry the visitor used.
Correction: escort the visitor to a safe area immediately, correct or add signage at the entry point, and brief site staff responsible for greeting visitors and deliveries.
Reinspection: confirm signage and staff briefing at every active entrance.

An Incident Occurs and the Reporting Procedure Is Not Followed

Probable cause: the incident-reporting procedure from Step 29 was not communicated clearly, or field staff were uncertain who the responsible recipient was.
Confirmation: check whether the incident was reported within the stated timeline and to the stated recipient.
Correction: complete the required report immediately, preserve any remaining evidence, and reissue the incident-reporting procedure to the project team.
Reinspection: confirm the reissued procedure has been acknowledged by all affected personnel.

The Plan Specifies a Hazard Control No Governing Program Requires

Probable cause: a hazard control was drafted directly into the plan without tracing it to a governing safety program, employer policy, or authority requirement.
Confirmation: check the plan section against the source document identified for it in the document schedule.
Correction: remove or revise the control to match the governing program, or obtain the governing program's written update if the control is genuinely needed.
Reinspection: repeat the responsibility check across the full plan, confirming every hazard control traces to a governing source.

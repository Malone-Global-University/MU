Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and operate a residential job-cost control system — the controlling record that reconciles what the project was budgeted to cost, what has been committed, what has actually been spent, what exposure remains unrecorded, and what the project is forecast to cost at completion. The finished work product is one current, cost-code-aligned job-cost report supported by a locked original budget, commitment records, actual-cost entries, a pending-cost log, and a preserved history of every issued report.

You will import the approved estimate and contract value, align cost codes with the WBS and accounting system, lock the original budget, create current-budget, commitment, actual-cost, pending-cost, forecast, and revenue fields, enter purchase orders, subcontracts, labor, material, and equipment costs against the correct codes, reconcile invoices against the accounting cutoff, identify and investigate variance, prepare a job-cost report with a written narrative, and preserve each issued report as a period record.

This project depends directly on the estimate produced under Book 24, the WBS and cost codes established in Project 03, the procurement records built in Project 07, and the change and allowance log operated in Project 11. It supplies the current cost picture used in every Project 09 coordination meeting from this point forward, and its final reconciliation becomes part of the Project 18 closeout package.

Where This Skill Fits

The job-cost system built here is the financial mirror of the WBS from Project 03, the procurement log from Project 07, and the change and allowance log from Project 11: the same cost codes, the same work packages, and the same approved changes drive schedule, procurement, and cost together. A job-cost report that cannot be traced back to the WBS and the approved change log is not a controlled report — it is a disconnected spreadsheet.

The discipline established here — locking the original budget, distinguishing commitment from actual cost, and never treating unapproved exposure as zero cost — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone requires when it reconciles final job cost, contract value, allowances, changes, credits, retainage, and closeout costs before the project can be archived.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • importing the approved Book 24 estimate and contract value and aligning cost codes with the WBS, accounting, purchase orders, subcontracts, labor, equipment, changes, and billing
  • creating and locking the original budget and creating current-budget, commitment, actual-cost, pending-cost, forecast, and revenue fields
  • establishing the accounting cutoff, update frequency, responsible person, and review cycle
  • entering purchase orders, subcontracts, labor, material, equipment, and subcontractor invoices against the correct cost code
  • updating allowance and change status, reconciling invoices against the accounting cutoff, and estimating remaining cost based on field review
  • identifying variance by cause, investigating cost codes with mismatched cost and progress, and preparing a job-cost report with narrative
  • protecting confidential cost information, reviewing the report with the appropriate parties, and preserving each issued report as a period record

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • approve payroll rates, employee classifications, benefits, or tax treatment; those remain the responsibility of the employer of record and company accounting or payroll function
  • commit company funds, issue purchase orders, or approve subcontracts beyond the authority identified in Step 13 of the procedure below
  • render an opinion on lien rights, bonding conditions, insurance coverage, or contract entitlement; those are legal, surety, or insurance matters outside a job-cost report
  • overwrite an issued job-cost report or the locked original budget; corrections are made through a new period record, not by altering history

Any conflict between the estimate, the contract value, and the current approved changes must be identified and resolved in writing before the original budget is locked.

Trade Note

Every job-cost report in this chapter is built from the same cost-code structure used by the WBS, procurement, labor records, and the change log — never from a separate, disconnected cost list built for convenience. Maintaining two different cost structures for the same project is the single most common source of the untraceable variance diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin entering actual costs (Step 12 forward) until the original budget is locked (Step 4) and the cost codes are aligned across the WBS, accounting, procurement, labor, and change systems (Step 2). A job-cost system built before these steps are complete must be re-aligned before it is trusted for reporting.

Shop Tip

Never forecast remaining cost as budget minus actual cost alone. A cost code can show low spending because work has not started, or because work is complete and simply came in under budget. Only a field review of installed quantity and remaining scope tells you which one is true.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every job-cost report in this book, from this chapter's residential cost control system to the Chapter 20 capstone reconciliation, follows the same sequence: align, lock, commit, record, forecast, investigate, report, preserve. Learning that sequence here, on one residential project, means every later cost update starts from a method you already trust.

Commitment, Actual Cost, and Pending Cost

Every job-cost report must separate what has been formally committed, what has actually been paid or invoiced, and what remains a known but unapproved exposure. Write pending-cost entries immediately when a condition is identified so a reader cannot mistake unrecorded exposure for a project that is under budget. An item left out of the pending-cost field becomes a forecast surprise once the exposure is finally invoiced.

Documentation Cue — Preserve Every Issued Report as a Period Record

Every job-cost report must carry a report date, the responsible preparer, and the accounting cutoff it reflects. Do not overwrite a previously issued report with the current update. Preserving only the current version, without the period history, removes the ability to see how the forecast changed and prevents accurate lessons-learned analysis later in the project.

Critical Cost Cue — Do Not Treat Unapproved Exposure as Zero Cost

Do not exclude an unapproved change, an expected invoice, damaged material, rework, or a claim from the forecast merely because it has not been formally approved or billed. Treating known exposure as zero cost is a leading cause of a forecast that looks accurate until the exposure is finally recorded, at which point the overrun appears all at once.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Percent Complete With Field Supervision

Confirm installed quantity and remaining scope directly with field supervision before finalizing forecast-to-complete for a cost code. A forecast built only from office records, without field confirmation, misses productivity, waste, and rework conditions that only field supervision can observe directly.

Company Policy Cue — Follow the Company's Pending-Cost and Accrual Rules

Where company policy specifies a particular method for recognizing pending cost, accruing late invoices, or setting commitment authority limits, follow that method exactly. Do not substitute a personal judgment call for the accrual or authority rule the company requires, even when it would be faster to report a rounder number.

E. Setup — Import, Align, and Lock the Budget

  1. Import the approved Book 24 estimate and contract value into the project cost system. Use the approved figures without modification at this stage.
  2. Align cost codes with the WBS, accounting, purchase orders, subcontracts, labor, equipment, changes, and billing. Confirm every code used by one system has a matching code in every other system.
  3. Create original-budget lines for labor, material, equipment, subcontract, general conditions, allowances, contingency, overhead, and other approved categories. Populate each line from the estimate.
  4. Lock the original budget and preserve revisions separately. Do not allow the original-budget field to be edited once locked.
  5. Create current-budget fields for approved transfers and change orders. Keep this field distinct from the locked original budget.
  6. Create commitment fields for purchase orders, subcontracts, rentals, and approved changes. Populate as commitments are issued, before invoices arrive.
  7. Create actual-cost fields for labor, material invoices, equipment, subcontract invoices, fees, and other costs. Reserve these fields for verified cost only.
  8. Create pending-cost fields for unapproved changes, expected invoices, damaged material, rework, claims, and known exposure. Do not leave this field blank when exposure is known.
  9. Create forecast-to-complete and forecast-final-cost fields. Prepare these fields to be populated from field-reviewed data in Step 22.

F. Entry — Record Commitments and Actual Costs

  1. Create revenue fields for original contract, approved changes, allowances, alternates, billings, retainage, collections, and remaining value. Keep revenue fields distinct from cost fields.
  2. Establish the accounting cutoff, update frequency, responsible person, and review cycle. Record these on the cost-code register.
  3. Enter purchase orders and subcontracts against the correct cost code. Verify the code before entry; do not enter to a convenient or general code.
  4. Prevent commitments from exceeding authority without approval. Confirm the responsible person's authority limit before releasing a commitment above it.
  5. Enter labor hours, wage, burden, crew, phase, and cost code from verified time records. Use only records confirmed by field supervision.
  6. Compare labor production and percent complete with the Book 24 estimate basis. Identify labor cost codes running ahead of or behind the estimated production rate.
  7. Enter material invoices with quantity, supplier, purchase order, delivery, tax, freight, credit, and cost code. Match each invoice to its purchase order before entry.
  8. Enter equipment rental, owned-equipment, fuel, delivery, operator, and standby costs. Assign each to the cost code for the work the equipment supported.
  9. Enter subcontractor invoices only after scope, progress, stored material, change orders, lien documents, and approval are reviewed. Do not enter an unreviewed subcontractor invoice as actual cost.

G. Reconcile, Forecast, and Investigate Variance

  1. Update allowance commitments and forecast balance. Reflect the current selection, committed amount, and remaining balance from the allowance log.
  2. Update approved and pending change events. Reflect current-budget and pending-cost effects from the change log.
  3. Reconcile invoices received after the accounting cutoff through accrual or pending cost according to company policy. Do not omit a late invoice from the period it belongs to.
  4. Estimate remaining cost based on installed quantity, remaining scope, productivity, commitments, procurement, and current conditions. Confirm the estimate with field supervision before finalizing.
  5. Do not forecast remaining cost as budget minus actual without field review. Treat this shortcut as a failed forecast requiring correction.
  6. Identify variance by quantity, unit price, productivity, waste, scope, damage, rework, schedule, freight, tax, and timing. Record the specific cause for each significant variance.
  7. Investigate cost codes with high actual cost but low reported progress. Confirm whether the cause is productivity, waste, damage, rework, or a reporting error.
  8. Investigate high progress with missing invoices or commitments. Confirm whether cost has simply not been entered yet or genuinely was not incurred.

H. Report, Review, and Preserve

  1. Compare cost forecast with schedule progress and procurement status. Confirm the cost picture is consistent with the current schedule and procurement records.
  2. Prepare a job-cost report showing budget, changes, current budget, commitments, actual, pending, forecast, variance, and percent complete. Populate the report from the fields established in Sections E and F.
  3. Prepare a narrative for material variances, corrective action, and management decision. Write the narrative in plain language tied to the specific cost codes involved.
  4. Review cash flow, billing, retainage, collections, supplier terms, and upcoming commitments. Confirm the revenue side supports the cost side.
  5. Protect confidential payroll, vendor, owner, and financial information. Distribute the report only to the parties authorized under the project's confidentiality rules.
  6. Review the report with project management, field supervision, accounting, procurement, and management. Confirm each reviewer's area of the report before distribution ends.
  7. Assign corrective action for overruns, missing commitments, delayed billing, and forecast risk. Record one responsible party and one due date for each corrective action.
  8. Preserve each issued report as a period record rather than overwriting history. Archive the report with its date and accounting cutoff intact.
  9. Reconcile final job cost, contract value, allowances, changes, credits, retainage, and closeout costs before project closure. Complete this reconciliation as the job-cost system's final entry at Chapter 20 closeout.

Common Job-Cost Report Inspection

Inspect every job-cost report using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Cost-code check: confirm every cost code used in the report also appears in the WBS, procurement register, labor records, and change log with no orphan codes.
  • Budget check: confirm the original budget is locked and unchanged, and that every revision appears only in the current-budget field.
  • Commitment and actual check: confirm every purchase order, subcontract, invoice, and labor entry is recorded against the correct cost code.
  • Pending-cost check: confirm every known unapproved or unbilled exposure is recorded and not treated as zero cost.
  • Forecast check: confirm forecast-to-complete is based on field-reviewed data, not budget minus actual alone.
  • Variance check: confirm every significant variance has an identified cause and, where applicable, an assigned corrective action.
  • History check: confirm the current report is preserved as a dated period record without overwriting a previously issued report.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Forecast Understates the Final Cost

Probable cause: the forecast was calculated as budget minus actual cost without a field review of installed quantity and remaining scope, in violation of Step 23.
Confirmation: check whether the forecast-to-complete field was populated from a field review or from a simple subtraction, and compare against actual field progress.
Correction: conduct a field review with supervision for the affected cost codes and recalculate forecast-to-complete from installed quantity and remaining scope.
Reinspection: repeat the forecast check across all active cost codes, not only the one identified.

A Known Exposure Does Not Appear in the Report

Probable cause: the pending-cost field was left blank for an unapproved change, expected invoice, damaged material, rework, or claim, in violation of Step 8.
Confirmation: check the change log, field reports, and correspondence for known exposure items and compare against the pending-cost field for the affected cost code.
Correction: enter the exposure in the pending-cost field with its estimated amount and update the forecast-final-cost accordingly.
Reinspection: repeat the pending-cost check across the full cost-code register.

Cost Cannot Be Traced Between the Job-Cost Report and the WBS or Procurement Log

Probable cause: the cost-code alignment in Step 2 was not completed, or a cost code was created in one system without a matching code in another.
Confirmation: check the cost-code register for the item in question and confirm whether it exists in the WBS, procurement register, labor records, and change log.
Correction: create or correct the missing code across all systems, reassign the misfiled entry, and document the correction.
Reinspection: repeat the cost-code check across the full register, not only the disputed item.

A Cost Code Shows High Actual Cost but Low Reported Progress

Probable cause: the variance investigation in Step 25 was not completed, or the actual cause among productivity, waste, damage, rework, or a reporting error was never identified.
Confirmation: compare the cost code's actual cost, committed cost, and reported percent complete, and check field records for productivity, waste, damage, or rework conditions.
Correction: identify the specific cause, record it in the variance narrative, and assign corrective action with a responsible party and due date.
Reinspection: repeat the variance check for the cost code after the next reporting period.

A Prior Job-Cost Report Cannot Be Located or Was Overwritten

Probable cause: the period-history convention from Step 34 was not followed, and the current update was saved over the previous report instead of preserved as a new period record.
Confirmation: check the period-report archive for a complete, dated sequence of issued reports and confirm whether any period is missing.
Correction: reissue the current report as a new dated period record, and correct the archive procedure so future updates are preserved rather than overwritten.
Reinspection: confirm the archive shows an unbroken sequence of period reports going forward.

A Commitment Was Issued Above the Responsible Person's Authority

Probable cause: the authority limit from Step 13 was not confirmed before the purchase order or subcontract was released.
Confirmation: check the commitment amount against the responsible person's documented authority limit and confirm whether the required approval was obtained.
Correction: obtain retroactive approval from the correct authority level, document the exception, and correct the commitment authority process for future purchase orders and subcontracts.
Reinspection: repeat the authority check on the next commitment issued against the affected cost code.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to select, measure, cut, install, and inspect one transition or threshold between two adjoining floor surfaces or between two elevations of the same surface. The finished work product is one completed transition installation: a selected and confirmed transition type, a measured opening and elevation difference, a cut and dry-fitted transition piece, a completed installation into the substrate using the specified track, anchor, adhesive, screws, or clips, and a documented inspection confirming fit, support, movement clearance, and door operation.

You will identify the adjoining floor materials and finished heights; select the approved transition type; measure the opening and the actual elevation difference; verify door swing and clearance; mark the required expansion space for each floor; cut and dry-fit the transition; install it into the substrate using the specified method; and inspect the completed transition for fit, support, movement, fastener concealment, and finish.

This project depends on the finished-floor heights and transition locations identified in Chapter 1 and on the flooring installed in Chapters 6 through 14. It is a required step before Chapter 16 doorway fitting is finalized on either side of a transition, and it supplies the transition record used in the Chapter 19 final quality inspection and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-085 — Select the correct transition type for the adjoining floor materials, elevations, and use conditions.
  • FL-086 — Measure a doorway or floor-to-floor transition opening and the actual elevation difference.
  • FL-087 — Cut a transition strip to length using a method suitable for its material.
  • FL-088 — Fasten a threshold or transition into the substrate using the specified anchoring method.
  • FL-089 — Maintain the required expansion clearance for each adjoining floor at the transition.
  • FL-090 — Inspect a completed transition for fit, support, movement, and finish.

Where This Skill Fits

Every floor-covering chapter in this book ends at a transition. Laminate and engineered floating floors in Chapters 6 and 10 rely on the transition to cover the perimeter expansion gap without pinning the floor. LVP and sheet vinyl in Chapters 7 and 8 rely on the transition to protect an exposed edge. Nail-down hardwood in Chapter 9 and tile in Chapters 11 through 13 rely on the transition to resolve elevation differences at doorways. Carpet in Chapter 14 relies on the transition or edge bar to secure the carpet edge where it meets another material.

The measurement and clearance discipline established here — confirming actual elevation difference rather than assuming it, and never fastening through a floating floor — is reused directly in Chapter 16 doorway fitting, Chapter 17 stair and landing finishes, and the Chapter 20 capstone, where multiple flooring types meet at coordinated transitions.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the adjoining floor materials, finished heights, movement requirements, traffic direction, doorway, accessibility, and moisture exposure at one transition location
  • selecting the approved T-molding, reducer, threshold, end cap, seam binder, carpet bar, Schluter-type profile, or other transition specified for the adjoining floors
  • measuring the opening width and the actual elevation difference
  • verifying door swing and clearance where the transition falls beneath a door
  • cutting, dry-fitting, and installing the transition using the track, anchor, adhesive, screws, or clips specified for the substrate
  • maintaining required movement space beneath or beside the transition for each adjoining floor
  • inspecting the completed transition for fit, support, edge coverage, movement, fastener concealment, and finish

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • alter the finished-flooring installation on either side of the transition — that work belongs to Chapters 6 through 14
  • undercut door jambs or casing — that work is covered in Chapter 16
  • fasten a transition through a floating floor, or trap a floating floor beneath a rigid transition where the flooring manufacturer prohibits it
  • alter stair rise, run, tread depth, nosing, or landing geometry — stair transitions are covered in Chapter 17
  • proceed with installation when the measured elevation difference, door clearance, or accessibility condition conflicts with the selected transition

Any elevation conflict, door-clearance conflict, or accessibility conflict identified during this project must be resolved by selecting a compatible transition or escalating to the responsible trade before installation continues.

Trade Note

A transition is not a cosmetic strip. It is the component that resolves elevation differences, covers a required expansion gap, and protects an exposed floor edge at the point of greatest foot traffic in a room. A transition installed before the actual elevation difference is measured, or fastened without regard for the movement requirement of the floor it covers, is the most common cause of a loose, rocking, or trip-hazard transition documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not cut or install a transition until the opening width and the actual elevation difference have been measured, the transition type has been confirmed compatible with both adjoining floors, and the required expansion space for each floor has been marked. A transition installed against an assumed elevation difference must be treated as incomplete and reinstalled once the actual measurement is taken.

Shop Tip

Dry-fit every transition before final installation, even a short one. A transition that appears to fit by eye can still bind against a door, rock on an unsupported low spot, or fall short of the required expansion coverage once it is anchored. Confirming the dry fit first prevents removing a fastened transition later.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every transition in this project resolves one of three conditions: two different floor materials meeting at the same elevation, two floor materials meeting at different elevations, or a floating floor's required expansion gap that must be covered without being pinned. Identifying which condition applies determines the correct transition type before any measurement or cutting begins.

Transition Selection and Compatibility

Every transition type selected in this chapter is based on the confirmed materials, finished heights, and movement requirements of both adjoining floors, not on appearance or convenience. Where the flooring plan or product instructions specify a particular transition, that specification controls. Where no plan exists, select the transition type from the compatible options identified in the manufacturer instructions for each adjoining floor, and record the selection reasoning on the Transition Installation Record.

Measurement Cue — Measure the Actual Elevation Difference, Do Not Assume It

Measure the actual elevation difference between the two adjoining floors at the transition location using a straightedge and feeler gauge or graduated shim, at more than one point along the opening if the floors are not perfectly flat. Do not select a reducer, threshold, or other elevation-specific transition based on a nominal product thickness alone.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Fasten Through a Floating Floor

Do not drive a screw, nail, or staple through a laminate or engineered floating floor to anchor a transition. A fastener driven through a floating floor's field pins the floor in place, prevents its required seasonal movement, and can cause buckling, peaked joints, or locking-system failure. Anchor the transition into the subfloor at the expansion gap, or use a transition system specifically designed to float with the flooring.

Field Safety Cue — Cutting Metal and Composite Transitions

Cut metal and composite transition profiles with a blade rated for that material, supported on a stable cutting surface, and clear of your free hand. Dress every cut edge with a file or deburring tool before handling it further. An unfilled cut edge on a metal transition is sharp enough to cause a laceration during dry-fitting or installation.

Code Alert — Accessibility and Trip-Hazard Limits

Verify the accessibility, threshold-height, and trip-hazard requirements that apply to this transition location. Requirements for maximum threshold height, bevel slope, and abrupt vertical change vary by jurisdiction and by accessible-route requirements. Confirm the applicable limit before selecting a threshold profile.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Anchoring Method and Movement Clearance

Use the exact track, anchor, adhesive, screw, or clip system specified by the transition manufacturer for the substrate, and the exact expansion-space dimension specified by each adjoining floor's manufacturer. Do not substitute a different anchoring method or assume a clearance dimension that is not stated in the manufacturer's instructions or the approved chapter project specification.

E. Transition Identification and Selection

  1. Identify the adjoining floor materials, finished heights, movement requirements, traffic direction, doorway, accessibility, and moisture exposure. Record each condition on the Transition Installation Record before selecting a product.
  2. Select the approved T-molding, reducer, threshold, end cap, seam binder, carpet bar, Schluter-type profile, or other transition. Base the selection on the identified materials, heights, and movement requirements.
  3. Confirm transition compatibility with both floor systems. Check the transition manufacturer's instructions against each adjoining floor's manufacturer instructions before proceeding.

F. Measurement and Door-Clearance Verification

  1. Measure opening width and actual elevation difference. Take the elevation measurement with a straightedge and feeler gauge or shim at more than one point if the floors are not perfectly flat.
  2. Verify door swing and clearance. Operate the door through its full range and confirm it will clear the transition once installed.
  3. Determine whether the transition is centered under the door or located at another approved line. Mark the confirmed reference line before cutting.
  4. Mark the required expansion space for each floor. Use the dimension specified by each adjoining floor's manufacturer, not an estimated gap.

G. Cutting and Dry-Fitting

  1. Cut the transition to length using a blade suitable for wood, metal, vinyl, or composite material. Support the transition fully during the cut and keep hands clear of the blade path.
  2. Dress sharp edges and protect finishes. File or deburr every cut edge before handling the transition further.
  3. Dry-fit the transition. Set the transition in place without fastening it and confirm fit, length, and door clearance before proceeding.

H. Installation and Anchoring

  1. Install the track, anchor, adhesive, screws, or clips into the substrate as specified. Follow the exact anchoring method and spacing specified by the transition manufacturer.
  2. Do not fasten through a floating floor. Anchor into the subfloor within the expansion gap, or use a transition system designed to float with the flooring.
  3. Maintain required movement space beneath or beside the transition. Recheck the marked expansion clearance immediately before final seating.
  4. Seat the transition fully without rocking. Confirm full contact and support along the entire length.
  5. Install thresholds with proper support and moisture seal where required. Follow the specified sealing method for exterior or wet-area thresholds.
  6. Preserve accessibility limits and avoid abrupt trip hazards. Confirm the installed height and bevel comply with the applicable accessibility limit identified in Section F.

I. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Check door operation. Operate the door through its full range again with the transition fully installed.
  2. Inspect fit, support, edge coverage, movement, fastener concealment, and finish. Confirm every item against the Acceptance Standards table.
  3. Correct loose, high, short, sharp, or binding transitions. Remove and reinstall or replace any transition that fails inspection.
  4. Document transition type, substrate attachment, and clearances. Complete the Transition Installation Record before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed transition using the same checks regardless of which floor materials or transition type the project involves.

  • Material check: confirm both adjoining floor materials and finished heights are correctly identified and recorded.
  • Compatibility check: confirm the selected transition is compatible with both adjoining floor systems.
  • Fit check: confirm the transition covers the full opening width within the manufacturer-specified tolerance.
  • Support check: confirm the transition is fully seated with no rocking or unsupported sections.
  • Movement check: confirm each adjoining floating or expanding floor retains its required movement clearance.
  • Fastener check: confirm no fastener penetrates a floating floor's field and all substrate fasteners are seated flush.
  • Door check: confirm the door opens, closes, and latches without binding.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Transition Installation Record is fully completed.

Complete the Transition Installation Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 16.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Transition Rocks or Flexes Underfoot After Installation

Probable cause: an unsupported low spot beneath the transition, an incomplete anchoring pattern, or a track that was not fully seated before the cap or profile was installed.
Confirmation: press along the full length of the transition and identify the specific unsupported section.
Correction: remove the transition, correct the substrate support or anchoring pattern per the manufacturer's instructions, and reinstall.
Reinspection: repeat the support and seating check along the full length after reinstallation.

A Floating Floor Buckles or Develops a Peaked Joint Near the Transition

Probable cause: a fastener was driven through the floating floor's field, or the transition was installed without the required expansion clearance.
Confirmation: inspect the transition anchoring for any fastener penetrating the floor field and re-measure the expansion clearance against the manufacturer's specification.
Correction: remove the offending fastener or the transition, restore the required expansion clearance, and reinstall using a method that does not penetrate the floating floor.
Reinspection: confirm the floor lies flat and the joint condition improves once the floor is free to move.

The Door Binds or Will Not Fully Close After the Transition Is Installed

Probable cause: the transition thickness or profile height was not verified against door clearance before installation, or the transition was located off the confirmed reference line.
Confirmation: operate the door through its full range and identify the exact point of contact.
Correction: select a lower-profile transition, adjust the transition location within the approved reference line, or escalate a door-clearance conflict to the responsible trade.
Reinspection: repeat the full door-operation check after correction.

A Gap Appears at One or Both Ends of the Transition

Probable cause: the opening width was measured incorrectly, or the transition was cut short of the measured dimension.
Confirmation: re-measure the opening width and compare it against the actual cut length of the installed transition.
Correction: replace the transition with a correctly measured and cut piece; do not fill a structural gap with sealant or filler as a substitute for a correctly sized transition.
Reinspection: repeat the fit check along the full opening after replacement.

The Installed Threshold Exceeds the Applicable Height or Trip-Hazard Limit

Probable cause: the threshold profile selected did not account for the applicable accessibility or trip-hazard limit for this location.
Confirmation: re-measure the installed threshold height and bevel slope and compare against the limit identified during transition selection.
Correction: replace the threshold with a compliant profile or escalate the conflict for resolution before the transition is finalized.
Reinspection: re-measure the corrected installation against the applicable limit.

A Sharp or Unfinished Edge Remains on a Cut Transition

Probable cause: a cut edge on a metal or composite transition was not filed or deburred before installation.
Confirmation: run a gloved hand carefully along every cut edge and visually inspect for burrs.
Correction: remove the transition if already installed, dress the edge with a file or deburring tool, and reinstall.
Reinspection: recheck every cut edge before final acceptance.

Cleanup

This project ends with the transition fully installed, the door confirmed operational, and the work area free of cutting debris and packaging.

  • Remove and properly store cutting tools, drill/driver, and fastening hardware not used
  • Vacuum or sweep metal filings, vinyl shavings, or wood dust from the transition location
  • Remove protective or masking tape from adjacent finishes
  • Store the Transition Installation Record with the project documentation for reference by later projects

The document recording the adjoining floor materials, selected transition type, measurements, anchoring method, clearances, and final inspection.

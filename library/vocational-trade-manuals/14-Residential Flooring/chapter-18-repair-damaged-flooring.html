Job Overview

This project qualifies you to repair a controlled set of flooring damage on four different material systems: a damaged click-lock plank, a damaged glued-vinyl area, a cracked tile with loose grout, and a damaged section of stretch-in carpet. The finished work product is a set of five completed repairs — plank replacement, vinyl patch, tile replacement, grout replacement, and carpet patch — each restored to sound support, secure attachment, and a blended appearance, with the identified cause of damage corrected before the cosmetic repair begins.

You will identify the flooring material and damage type; correct the underlying cause of damage before repairing the surface; remove damaged material without harming adjacent sound material; prepare the substrate; install a matching replacement using the method specific to that material; and inspect the repair for color, pattern, plane, seam, movement, and adhesion.

This project depends on the substrate, moisture, and installation knowledge built in Chapters 1 through 17. Every repair method used here assumes the surrounding flooring was installed correctly; a repair is not a substitute for correcting a substrate, moisture, or installation defect that caused the damage in the first place.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-103 — Replace damaged plank flooring using a method appropriate to the locking or fastening system.
  • FL-104 — Patch vinyl damage using a matched pattern and compatible adhesive.
  • FL-105 — Replace cracked tile without damaging adjacent tile or substrate.
  • FL-106 — Repair loose grout while preserving movement joints.
  • FL-107 — Patch carpet damage using a matched donor piece and approved seam method.
  • FL-108 — Inspect the repair blend for color, pattern, plane, seam, movement, and adhesion.

Where This Skill Fits

Repair work follows a different discipline than new installation. Chapters 6 through 14 taught you to install flooring across an open field with full access to every edge. This project teaches you to work inside an existing, occupied floor: isolating damage, protecting adjacent finished material, and matching a repair to surrounding material you did not install and may not have selected.

The diagnostic discipline in this chapter — identifying cause before repairing symptom — is the same discipline used in the Chapter 19 final quality inspection and in the Chapter 20 capstone repair demonstration. A repair performed without correcting its cause is treated in this book as an incomplete repair, regardless of how well it is finished cosmetically.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the flooring material, damage type, cause, moisture condition, and substrate condition for each repair area
  • correcting the identified cause of damage — leakage, movement, impact, adhesive failure, substrate damage, or environmental condition — before repairing the surface
  • removing a damaged click-lock plank by disassembly or cut-out replacement without damaging adjacent locking edges
  • cutting and installing a matched vinyl patch with compatible adhesive and seam treatment
  • isolating and removing a cracked tile, removing old mortar, and setting a matching replacement tile
  • removing unsound grout without chipping tile, regrouting, and preserving movement joints
  • cutting and fitting a matched carpet patch using an approved seam method with blended pile direction
  • inspecting each repair for color, pattern, plane, seam, movement, and adhesion

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with any repair before the cause of damage has been identified and corrected
  • disturb suspect legacy flooring, felt backing, mastic, or other unknown materials before hazardous-material screening is complete
  • repair a floor showing widespread moisture intrusion, structural subfloor failure, or an unknown adhesive without escalation to a qualified professional
  • substitute a mismatched plank, tile, vinyl pattern, or carpet dye lot without documenting the visible difference to the responsible party

Any repair area showing an uncorrected leak, unresolved structural movement, suspect legacy material, or unknown adhesive must be stopped and escalated before this project's repair methods are used.

Trade Note

Every repair in this chapter begins with a cause determination, not a material selection. A cracked tile over an unresolved deflection point, a lifted vinyl seam over a slow leak, or a worn carpet patch over a binding transition will fail again if the replacement material is installed without correcting what damaged the original material.

Setup Standard

Do not begin any material removal until the cause of damage has been identified and, where required, corrected. A repair installed over an uncorrected leak, an unresolved structural movement point, or an unknown adhesive must be treated as incomplete and is not an acceptable finished repair under this chapter.

Shop Tip

Photograph each damage area before removal begins. A photograph of the original pattern, seam location, and pile direction is the single most reliable reference for matching a replacement piece once the damaged material has been cut out and discarded.

Damage Identification and Cause Correction

Identify the flooring material, the type of damage, and the moisture, substrate, and surrounding-finish condition before selecting a repair method. Every repair in this chapter is preceded by a cause determination: correct leakage, structural movement, impact source, adhesive failure, substrate damage, or environmental cause before any cosmetic repair begins. A repair completed without correcting the identified cause must be documented as incomplete.

Measurement Cue — Confirm the Boundary Before Cutting

Mark the repair boundary at the nearest sound, supported edge before making any cut. Extending a cut into undamaged material to make the work easier increases the size of the visible repair and is not an acceptable shortcut.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspect Legacy Material and Unknown Adhesive

Stop work if the repair area reveals suspect legacy flooring, felt backing, black mastic, or an adhesive of unknown composition beneath the damaged material. Do not sand, grind, scrape, or otherwise disturb the material further. Escalate to a qualified hazardous-material professional before continuing the repair.

Field Safety Cue — Dust and Slurry Control During Tile Removal

Control silica dust and mortar slurry during tile grinding, cutting, and mortar removal using wet cutting, local extraction, or HEPA vacuuming as appropriate to the method. Do not dry-sweep fine dust generated during tile or grout removal.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Adhesive, Mortar, and Grout Compatibility

Use only adhesive, mortar, or grout products confirmed compatible with the existing flooring material, substrate, and surrounding installation. Do not substitute a different product family or add unapproved water, solvents, or accelerators outside the product's mixing and working-time limits.

E. Damage Identification and Cause Correction

  1. Identify the flooring material, damage type, cause, moisture condition, substrate condition, and surrounding finish. Record each factor on the Flooring Repair Record before removing any material.
  2. Correct leakage, movement, impact source, adhesive failure, substrate damage, or environmental cause before repair. Verify the correction using the moisture meter or the applicable method from Chapters 1, 3, and 4.
  3. Stop work for hazardous legacy material, structural failure, widespread moisture, or unknown adhesive. Escalate to a qualified professional before continuing, per the Failure Points section.

F. Click-Lock Plank Repair

  1. For click-lock plank, determine whether disassembly from an edge or an approved cut-out replacement is required. Base the decision on the plank's location relative to a wall, doorway, or field position.
  2. Remove the damaged plank without harming adjacent locking edges. Use the oscillating multi-tool to relieve the locking profile where a cut-out method is required.
  3. Prepare and install a matching replacement while preserving expansion. Engage the replacement plank's locking profile fully and confirm perimeter expansion space is unchanged.

G. Glued Vinyl Repair

  1. For glued vinyl, define the patch boundary and verify adhesive compatibility. Mark the boundary at the nearest pattern repeat line or seam where possible.
  2. Cut a matching pattern patch and remove damaged material. Align the donor material's pattern with the surrounding floor before cutting the final patch.
  3. Prepare the substrate and install the patch with approved adhesive and seam treatment. Remove old adhesive residue, apply the specified adhesive, and seat the patch without trapping air.

H. Cracked Tile Repair

  1. For cracked tile, isolate and remove the tile without damaging adjacent pieces or substrate. Score the grout joints fully around the damaged tile before removal.
  2. Remove old mortar to the required depth. Clean the substrate to a flat, sound surface suitable for the new mortar bed.
  3. Set a matching tile with the approved mortar and joint spacing. Match the replacement tile's plane and joint width to the surrounding field.

I. Grout Repair

  1. For loose grout, remove unsound grout without chipping tile. Use the grout saw to remove only the affected joint length to a consistent depth.
  2. Regrout and preserve movement joints. Pack the repaired joints with matching grout and confirm no rigid grout has entered a designated movement joint.

J. Carpet Repair

  1. For carpet, identify donor material and pile direction. Confirm the donor patch matches the surrounding carpet's color, texture, and pile direction before cutting.
  2. Cut a controlled patch and fit it using the approved seam method. Cut the damaged section and the donor patch to matching dimensions using a straightedge and utility knife.
  3. Blend pile and seam orientation. Orient the patch so pile direction matches the surrounding carpet, then join the seam using the specified tape or hot-melt method.

K. Protection, Inspection, and Documentation

  1. Protect each repair through cure or set. Restrict traffic across each repair area for the period required by the adhesive, mortar, or grout product used.
  2. Inspect color, pattern, plane, seam, movement, adhesion, and surrounding damage. Compare each completed repair against the Acceptance Standards table.
  3. Document cause, repair material, method, and final blend. Complete the Flooring Repair Record for each of the five repair areas before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed repair area using the same checks regardless of which material the repair involves.

  • Cause check: confirm the identified cause of damage was corrected and documented before the cosmetic repair began.
  • Boundary check: confirm removal was limited to damaged material with no unnecessary harm to adjacent sound material.
  • Attachment check: confirm the replacement material is fully engaged, bonded, or fastened with no rocking, lifting, or movement.
  • Blend check: confirm color, pattern, plane, and pile direction are matched to the surrounding material at normal viewing distance.
  • Movement check: confirm floating-floor expansion and tile movement joints are preserved and not filled or trapped.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Flooring Repair Record is fully completed for every repair area.

Complete the Flooring Repair Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 19.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Replacement Plank Rocks or Clicks After Installation

Probable cause: the locking profile was not fully engaged, debris is trapped in the joint, or the subfloor beneath the repair is not flat within tolerance.
Confirmation: lift and reseat the replacement plank, inspect the locking profile for debris, and check the subfloor flatness at the repair location.
Correction: clean the joint, reseat the plank using the tapping block, or correct the underlying flatness deviation per Chapter 4 before reinstalling.
Reinspection: recheck joint engagement and walk the repair area to confirm the movement is resolved.

Vinyl Patch Edge Lifts After Adhesive Cure

Probable cause: insufficient adhesive coverage at the patch edge, incompatible adhesive, or trapped air beneath the patch during installation.
Confirmation: lift the affected edge carefully and inspect adhesive transfer and coverage.
Correction: clean the area, apply the specified compatible adhesive, and reseat the patch while rolling out trapped air.
Reinspection: recheck the edge after the adhesive's specified cure period.

Replacement Tile Sits Proud or Low of the Surrounding Field

Probable cause: uneven mortar bed thickness, incomplete old-mortar removal, or an unlevel substrate beneath the repair.
Confirmation: place a straightedge across the repair tile and adjacent tiles to measure the plane deviation.
Correction: remove the tile, correct the mortar bed thickness, and reset the tile while checking plane continuously.
Reinspection: recheck lippage across the repair boundary once the mortar has cured.

New Grout Cracks or Falls Out Shortly After Repair

Probable cause: the joint was regrouted at a designated movement joint, the joint depth was inconsistent, or the grout was mixed or cured outside product limits.
Confirmation: inspect the affected joint location against the original movement-joint plan and review the mixing and cure record.
Correction: remove the failed grout, confirm the joint is not a movement joint, and repack using the correct mixing ratio and cure protection.
Reinspection: recheck the joint after the specified cure period.

Carpet Patch Seam Is Visible at Normal Viewing Distance

Probable cause: pile direction mismatch between the donor patch and surrounding carpet, an uneven cut edge, or a dye-lot difference.
Confirmation: view the seam under raking light and compare pile direction and color against the surrounding carpet.
Correction: re-cut the patch with corrected pile orientation, or select a donor piece from a matching dye lot.
Reinspection: recheck the seam under raking light once the corrected patch is seated.

Damage Recurs at the Same Location After Repair

Probable cause: the underlying cause of damage — a leak, movement point, impact source, or adhesive failure — was not corrected before the cosmetic repair was completed.
Confirmation: re-inspect the repair area for the original moisture, movement, or substrate condition using the applicable method from Chapters 1, 3, or 4.
Correction: remove the repair, correct the underlying cause, and reinstall the repair using the applicable material-specific method.
Reinspection: confirm the cause is resolved before reinstalling any replacement material.

Cleanup

This project ends with every repair area protected through its cure period and the work area clear of removed material and repair debris.

  • Remove and properly dispose of damaged plank, vinyl, tile, mortar, grout, and carpet waste
  • Clean adhesive, mortar, and grout residue from surrounding sound flooring before it cures
  • Store unused donor and patch material for future matched repairs where practical
  • Store the Flooring Repair Record with the PPE and documentation station for reference by Chapter 19

Field Terms

Repair Boundary
The marked outer edge of the area to be removed and replaced, extending only to sound, supported material.
Donor Material
Matching plank, tile, vinyl, or carpet material used to complete a repair, selected from the same product, color, and lot when available.
Cause of Damage
The underlying condition that produced the visible flooring damage, which must be corrected before cosmetic repair.
Pattern and Pile Match
The alignment of a replacement piece's pattern, grain, or pile direction with the surrounding material at the repair boundary.
Movement Joint
A tile or grout joint intentionally left free of rigid grout to accommodate movement.
Cut-Out Replacement
A plank-repair method that removes a damaged board by relieving its locking profile rather than disassembling the floor from an edge.
Lippage
A difference in plane height between adjacent tiles at a shared joint.
Repair Blend
The visual and physical match between the completed repair and the surrounding original flooring.

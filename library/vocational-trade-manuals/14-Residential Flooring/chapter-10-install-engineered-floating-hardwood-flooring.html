Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install a complete engineered floating hardwood floor in one small room. The finished work product is one fully assembled floating floor with a straight, aligned starter row; fully engaged locking joints throughout the field; the required end-joint stagger; a fitted doorway crossing; preserved perimeter and obstacle expansion; and a verified free-floating condition confirmed by walking and inspecting the completed floor.

You will review the product's construction and locking method, confirm substrate and environmental readiness carried forward from Chapter 1 and Chapter 5, install compatible underlayment where required, establish plank direction and a reference line, calculate starter- and final-row widths, assemble the floor course by course while maintaining stagger and expansion, fit the floor around the door jamb using the Chapter 16 undercut method, install transitions and perimeter trim without fastening into the field, and confirm the completed floor can move freely at every edge.

This project depends directly on the substrate and moisture findings from Chapter 1, the work-area preparation from Chapter 2, and the underlayment and moisture-control system from Chapter 5. It supports the doorway fitting taught in Chapter 16, the transition work in Chapter 15, and the final quality inspection in Chapter 19.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-055 — Select underlayment compatible with the engineered floating-floor system, substrate, and any radiant-heat condition.
  • FL-056 — Assemble floating boards using the product's locking or glue-joint method with correct stagger and alignment.
  • FL-057 — Maintain expansion gaps at the perimeter and around fixed objects throughout the installation.
  • FL-058 — Cut and install final rows to fit the room width while preserving joint engagement and expansion.
  • FL-059 — Install flooring around doorways using the undercut-jamb method with preserved movement space.
  • FL-060 — Inspect a completed floating floor for unrestricted movement, joint engagement, and alignment.

Where This Skill Fits

Engineered floating hardwood is one of the plank flooring systems introduced in Chapters 6 through 10. Unlike nail-down hardwood in Chapter 9, a floating floor is never fastened to the substrate; it relies entirely on engaged locking joints and a preserved gap at every perimeter, obstacle, and fixed object to move as ambient humidity changes. The layout, stagger, and expansion discipline built in Chapter 6 for laminate flooring transfers directly to this project, with the addition of engineered-wood-specific locking profiles and, on some systems, end-joint glue.

The doorway-fitting procedure in this chapter follows the Chapter 16 undercut-jamb method exactly. The transition and perimeter-trim installation follows the Chapter 15 transition procedure. Both must preserve the floor's ability to move; neither may pin the floor to the substrate.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing product construction, locking or glue-joint method, underlayment requirement, moisture limits, radiant-heat compatibility, acclimation, and expansion requirement
  • confirming substrate and environmental readiness carried forward from Chapters 1, 2, and 5
  • installing compatible underlayment where the flooring system requires a separate layer
  • establishing plank direction, a straight reference line, and calculated first- and last-row widths
  • assembling the floating floor course by course with fully engaged joints, correct end-joint stagger, and clean finish faces
  • fitting the floor around a door jamb using the Chapter 16 undercut method
  • maintaining expansion around the perimeter, pipes, cabinets, islands, columns, and fixed objects
  • installing transitions and perimeter trim without fastening into the floating field
  • inspecting the completed floor for movement, hollow spots, clicking, binding, open joints, and trapped edges

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • install this floor over a substrate that has not passed the Chapter 1 moisture and flatness inspection
  • fasten the floating floor through the field, or trap it beneath cabinets, partitions, or fixed equipment
  • fasten shoe molding or transitions through the floor unless the approved system explicitly permits it
  • alter door-jamb structural components, fire-rated components, weather seals, or thresholds beyond the Chapter 16 undercut procedure
  • install nail-down or glue-down hardwood methods in place of the specified floating system

Any substrate, moisture, or environmental condition that has not passed the required prior-chapter inspection must be corrected before this project begins.

Trade Note

A floating floor is a complete system, not a series of individually placed boards. Every board depends on the boards around it for support and alignment. A joint that is not fully engaged, a row that drifts off the reference line, or a spacer removed too early can compound into misalignment, open joints, or a floor that cannot move as intended. Verify each row before starting the next.

Setup Standard

Do not begin plank assembly until the Chapter 1 substrate and moisture findings are confirmed current, the Chapter 2 work area is prepared, and the Chapter 5 underlayment or moisture-control system, if required, has passed inspection. Confirm the room has been within the flooring manufacturer's required temperature and humidity range for the specified acclimation period before the first board is placed.

Shop Tip

Dry-lay several rows before assembling any of them permanently. Confirm the stagger pattern, board color distribution, and final-row width on paper or on the floor before committing a single joint. Correcting a layout decision before assembly costs minutes; correcting it after several rows are locked together costs hours.

Locking Systems and Movement Discipline

Engineered floating floors rely entirely on their locking joints and preserved perimeter gap to remain sound. Every joint must be fully engaged before the next board is placed, and every spacer must remain in place until the entire field is assembled. A floor that is fastened, glued to the substrate, or trapped beneath a fixed object at any point loses its ability to expand and contract, which can produce buckling, peaking, or joint separation as conditions change.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Locking Method and Underlayment

Confirm whether the specified engineered product uses a click-lock, drop-lock, tongue-and-groove with glue, or another proprietary joining method, and confirm the exact underlayment or attached-pad requirement before installation begins. Do not substitute a different locking technique or underlayment product without manufacturer approval.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Expansion Space at Every Edge

Maintain the manufacturer-specified expansion space at every wall, column, pipe, cabinet, island, and door frame. Verify spacer placement continuously during assembly rather than only at the start and end of the installation.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Trap the Floating Floor

Do not fasten the flooring field to the substrate, install cabinets or fixed partitions directly on top of the field without the required expansion allowance, or install trim that pins the flooring in place. A trapped floating floor can buckle or delaminate under normal seasonal movement, and correcting a trapped floor after finish work is far more disruptive than preventing it during installation.

E. Product Review and Readiness Confirmation

  1. Review product construction, locking or glue-joint method, underlayment, moisture limits, radiant-heat compatibility, acclimation, and expansion. Record the exact locking method, required underlayment, and expansion space on the installation record.
  2. Confirm substrate and environment readiness. Verify the Chapter 1 moisture and flatness findings remain current and that the room has held the required temperature and humidity range for the specified acclimation period.
  3. Inspect boards for finish, tongue, groove, locking-profile, and dimensional defects. Set aside any board with a damaged locking edge, finish flaw, or out-of-tolerance dimension.
  4. Mix material from approved cartons. Draw boards from multiple cartons throughout the installation to distribute color and grain variation evenly across the room.

F. Layout and Underlayment

  1. Establish plank direction and reference line. Confirm the direction matches the approved plan and mark a chalk line square to the starting wall.
  2. Calculate first- and last-row widths. Measure the room perpendicular to plank direction and adjust the starter-row width so the final row is not left narrower than the manufacturer's minimum usable width.
  3. Install compatible underlayment where required. Roll or place the underlayment per the Chapter 5 procedure, maintaining flat, unwrinkled coverage and the specified seam treatment.
  4. Place perimeter spacers. Set spacers at the reference wall and along both ends before placing the first board.

G. Starter Row and Field Assembly

  1. Assemble the first row straight. Engage each board's end joint fully and confirm the row remains against the reference line and spacers along its full length.
  2. Use end-joint adhesive only if the system requires it. Apply the specified adhesive at end joints only where the manufacturer's instructions call for it, and observe the stated open time.
  3. Maintain specified end-joint stagger. Select the starting board length for each new row so every end joint falls at or beyond the required minimum offset from the joints in adjacent rows.
  4. Assemble subsequent rows without damaging locking edges. Engage each long-side joint using the tapping block, striking only over solid backing and never directly on an unsupported edge.
  5. Keep glue residue off the finish and remove it promptly where applicable. Wipe any adhesive squeeze-out from the finish surface before it cures.
  6. Check alignment every few rows. Measure from the reference line at multiple points along each completed row to confirm the field remains square and straight.

H. Doorway and Obstacle Fitting

  1. Fit around door jambs using Chapter 16. Undercut the jamb and casing per the Chapter 16 procedure before installing the flooring piece that crosses the doorway.
  2. Maintain expansion around pipes, cabinets, islands, columns, and fixed objects. Cut each piece to clear the obstacle by the manufacturer-specified expansion distance, marking the cut before removing material.
  3. Do not trap the floor beneath built-ins or partitions. Confirm the flooring field stops short of any fixed cabinet, partition, or built-in by the required expansion space rather than running the field beneath it.

I. Final Rows and Perimeter Completion

  1. Rip and install the final row. Measure the remaining width at multiple points along the wall, rip the final row planks to that width, and install using the pull bar where a tapping block cannot be used against the wall.
  2. Remove spacers after the installation is complete. Remove every spacer only after the entire field is assembled and confirmed straight.
  3. Install transitions and perimeter trim without fastening into the floor field. Fasten transitions and trim to the wall, subfloor beyond the flooring edge, or track system per Chapter 15, never through the flooring planks themselves.

J. Movement Verification

  1. Walk the floor and listen for movement, hollow spots, clicking, or binding. Walk the entire field methodically, noting any location with an unexpected sound or feel.
  2. Confirm the floor can move at the perimeter. Visually and physically confirm the expansion gap remains open and unobstructed at every wall, column, pipe, and fixed-object location after trim installation.
  3. Correct open joints, trapped edges, and damaged boards. Address every deficiency found during the walk inspection before proceeding to closeout.
  4. Protect the floor and document installation conditions. Record the product, underlayment, layout, stagger, expansion measurements, and inspection results on the installation record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed floating-floor installation using the same checks regardless of room size or plank pattern.

  • Underlayment check: confirm continuous, compatible coverage with no wrinkles or gaps where required.
  • Alignment check: confirm the starter row and subsequent rows remain straight and square to the reference line.
  • Joint check: confirm every long-side and end joint is fully engaged with no visible gap.
  • Stagger check: confirm every end joint meets the manufacturer's minimum offset from adjacent rows.
  • Expansion check: confirm the specified gap is present and unobstructed at every wall, column, pipe, and fixed object.
  • Doorway check: confirm the flooring passes beneath the undercut jamb without being trapped or pinched, and that the door operates correctly.
  • Movement check: confirm the completed floor is free of fasteners, adhesive, or trim that restricts expansion at any edge.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Joint Will Not Fully Engage

Probable cause: debris trapped in the locking profile, a damaged tongue or groove, or the adjoining board not seated flat before engagement.
Confirmation: inspect the locking profile of both boards for debris or damage, and confirm the adjoining board lies flat against the underlayment.
Correction: clear debris from the profile, reposition the board, or replace a damaged board with one set aside during the defect inspection.
Reinspection: re-engage the joint and confirm full closure with no visible gap.

The Floor Rows Drift Off the Reference Line

Probable cause: accumulated small misalignments from row to row, an inaccurate starter row, or a reference line that was not confirmed square.
Confirmation: measure from the reference line at several points along the affected rows and compare to the recorded layout plan.
Correction: stop assembly, identify the row where drift began, and disengage and realign rows back to that point before continuing.
Reinspection: re-measure alignment at the corrected rows and at several subsequent rows before resuming full-speed assembly.

The Final Row Is Narrower Than the Manufacturer's Minimum Usable Width

Probable cause: the starter-row width was not adjusted during layout planning to balance the first and last rows.
Confirmation: re-measure the room width and recalculate the starter- and final-row widths against the manufacturer's minimum.
Correction: where the field has already been assembled, this deficiency must be corrected in the layout stage for future rooms; for the current room, consult the manufacturer's guidance on ripping both the starter and final row to balance the remaining width.
Reinspection: confirm the revised final-row width meets the manufacturer's minimum before installing the last row.

The Floor Clicks, Binds, or Feels Restricted When Walked

Probable cause: the floor is trapped at a wall, cabinet, transition, or trim location, or a spacer was removed and the resulting gap was closed by an installed item.
Confirmation: inspect the full perimeter and every obstacle location for contact between the flooring field and a fixed item.
Correction: remove or adjust the item causing contact and restore the required expansion space.
Reinspection: walk the floor again after correction and confirm the sound or binding no longer occurs.

The Flooring Is Trapped or Pinched Beneath the Door Jamb

Probable cause: the jamb was undercut to an insufficient height, or the flooring piece beneath the trim was cut without the required hidden expansion space.
Confirmation: inspect the piece beneath the jamb for direct contact with the trim and confirm the cut height against the Chapter 16 gauge method.
Correction: re-undercut the jamb to the correct height or re-cut the flooring piece to restore the hidden expansion space.
Reinspection: confirm the door operates without binding and the flooring piece is not trapped after correction.

Field Terms

Floating Floor
A flooring assembly that is not fastened, glued, or otherwise fixed to the substrate and relies on locking joints for structural continuity.
Locking Profile
The tongue, groove, or proprietary click-lock geometry milled into each plank edge that joins adjacent boards.
End-Joint Stagger
The minimum required offset between end joints in adjacent rows, stated by the flooring manufacturer.
Expansion Space
The gap maintained between the flooring field and any wall, cabinet, column, pipe, or fixed object to allow the floor to move.
Tapping Block
A tool used to transfer mallet force evenly across a plank edge to engage a locking joint without damaging it.
Pull Bar
A tool used to engage the final row's locking joint where a tapping block cannot be positioned against the wall.
Reference Line
The straight marked line the starter row is assembled against, confirmed square to the starting wall.
Trapped Floor
A floating floor that has been fastened, glued, or physically restricted at a point that prevents it from expanding and contracting as intended.

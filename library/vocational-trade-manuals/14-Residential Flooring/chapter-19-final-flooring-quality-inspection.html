Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect a completed flooring installation and issue a documented acceptance, conditional acceptance, or rejection decision before the space is handed off to full traffic and following trades. The finished work product is one completed Final Flooring Quality Inspection Report: a raking-light and normal-light surface inspection, a joint and seam inspection for every installed material, an expansion-gap and floating-floor movement verification, a transition and threshold inspection, a stair and landing inspection where included, a recorded punch list with assigned corrections, and a documented final flooring status.

You will inspect the floor under normal and raking light; inspect plank, board, sheet, tile, and carpet joints and seams for their respective defects; verify floating-floor perimeters and movement space; inspect thresholds, reducers, T-moldings, and door clearances; check floor vents, drains, appliances, cabinets, and service access; confirm baseboard and shoe-molding coordination with floating flooring; record every deficiency by room and location; assign a correction method and responsible trade; reinspect corrected items; and issue the final status.

This is the twentieth-to-last project in the book, and it is the formal release inspection referenced by every earlier chapter. Chapter 20's capstone room depends on the inspection method taught here to complete its own final walkthrough before handoff to Book 15.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-109 — Inspect seams in plank, board, sheet, and tile flooring for openings, peaking, chips, and adhesive telegraphing.
  • FL-110 — Inspect expansion gaps and floating-floor perimeters for required movement space.
  • FL-111 — Inspect transitions, thresholds, and reducers for fit, support, and clearance.
  • FL-112 — Inspect lippage, unevenness, and surface-plane defects across finish-flooring systems.
  • FL-113 — Verify secure installation, including attachment, movement, sound, and stair or landing safety.
  • FL-114 — Document punch-list corrections and issue a final flooring-status decision.

Where This Skill Fits

This inspection reuses the moisture, flatness, and documentation discipline established in Chapter 1 and applies it to a completed installation rather than a bare substrate. It also draws directly on the material-specific defect knowledge built across Chapters 6 through 14 — laminate and LVP joints, sheet-vinyl seams and bubbles, nail-down and floating hardwood movement, tile lippage and grout condition, and carpet stretch and seam quality — and on the transition, doorway, and stair work from Chapters 15 through 17.

The punch-list and reinspection method taught here is the same method the Chapter 20 capstone uses to complete its own final inspection before the practice room is handed off for Book 15 painting and surface finishes. A flooring installation that has not passed this inspection sequence is not ready for full traffic or following trades regardless of how complete it otherwise appears.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • assembling approved plans, product instructions, moisture records, flatness records, acclimation records, installation photographs, and prior inspection reports before beginning the walkthrough
  • confirming required cure times and traffic restrictions have passed before inspection begins
  • inspecting the completed floor under normal and raking light
  • inspecting plank and board joints, sheet vinyl, tile, and carpet for their material-specific defects
  • inspecting floating-floor perimeters, transitions, thresholds, stairs, and landings
  • checking floor vents, drains, appliances, cabinets, and service access, and confirming baseboard and shoe-molding coordination with flooring movement
  • performing specified sound, bond, flatness, load, or operational checks
  • recording every deficiency by room and location, assigning a correction method and responsible trade, and reinspecting corrected items
  • compiling final photographs and test records and issuing an approved, conditional, or rejected flooring status

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform structural, moisture, or flatness corrections identified during this inspection — that work belongs to Chapters 3, 4, and 18
  • install, replace, or refasten flooring, transitions, or trim as part of this inspection itself — deficiencies are recorded and assigned, not corrected during the walkthrough
  • issue an approved status for any room where a required cure time or traffic restriction has not yet passed
  • disturb any suspect legacy material discovered during the inspection — treat it as a stop-work condition per Chapter 1 and escalate before continuing

Any deficiency identified in this chapter must be corrected, reinspected, and documented before the flooring status is finalized as approved.

Trade Note

Every final inspection in this chapter is performed under both normal room lighting and raking light — light directed low and across the floor surface. Lippage, seam peaking, and surface telegraphing that are invisible under normal overhead lighting are often clearly visible under raking light. Substituting a normal-light walkthrough for the required raking-light inspection is the most common cause of a defect surfacing only after full traffic has begun.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the final inspection until every product-required cure time and traffic restriction confirmed in prior chapters has passed. An inspection performed before the specified cure time is complete must be treated as preliminary and repeated in full once the cure period has elapsed.

Shop Tip

Walk each room twice: once under normal lighting to check layout, transitions, and general condition, and once under raking light to check joints, seams, and surface plane. Combining both passes into a single walkthrough is the most common reason a lippage or peaking defect is missed until after handoff.

Record Assembly, Cure Confirmation, and Lighting Inspection

Every finding in this chapter depends on a completed and available prior-chapter record. Where a moisture reading, flatness record, or installation photograph from an earlier chapter cannot be located, note the missing record on the report and confirm the underlying condition directly before continuing. Do not proceed with a normal-light-only walkthrough; every room requires both a normal-light and a raking-light pass before its inspection is considered complete.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Lippage and Gaps Against the Specified Tolerance

When raking light reveals a joint or seam irregularity, measure it with a feeler gauge or graduated shim and compare the reading against the tolerance specified for that flooring system in its governing chapter. Do not record a visual impression alone; record the measured value.

Critical Safety Cue — Stairs and Landings

Do not walk a stair or landing during this inspection until it has passed the Chapter 17 structural and finish inspection. Barricade any stair section found to be loose, uneven beyond the approved finish correction, or otherwise unsafe, and provide alternate access before continuing the walkthrough.

Field Safety Cue — Raking-Light Inspection Footing

Position the raking-light source so its cord and stand do not create a trip hazard while walking the inspection area. Use adequate ambient lighting when moving between rooms, and do not rely on the raking-light source as the sole light for general movement through the space.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Cure and Traffic-Restriction Confirmation

Confirm the exact cure time and traffic restriction specified by each installed flooring, adhesive, mortar, or grout product before beginning the inspection of that area. Do not substitute a general assumption about cure time for the specific product instruction, and do not issue an approved status for any area where the specified period has not elapsed.

E. Record Assembly and Cure Confirmation

  1. Assemble approved plans, product instructions, moisture records, flatness records, acclimation records, installation photographs, and prior inspection reports. Note any missing record for direct field confirmation during the walkthrough.
  2. Confirm required cure times and traffic restrictions have passed. Compare the installation date and specified cure period for each product against today's date before inspecting that area.

F. Lighting Inspection

  1. Inspect the floor under normal and raking light. Complete both passes in every room before recording that room's findings as final.

G. Material-Specific Surface and Joint Inspection

  1. Inspect plank and board joints for openings, peaked edges, chips, locking failure, glue residue, and pattern drift. Measure any flagged joint against the tolerance specified for that flooring system.
  2. Inspect wood floors for squeaks, movement, fastener damage, surface defects, and moisture-related change. Walk and load the area methodically as taught in Chapter 3.
  3. Inspect floating-floor perimeters and transitions for required movement space. Measure the gap at walls, columns, cabinets, and fixed objects.
  4. Confirm cabinets, trim, thresholds, and fasteners do not trap floating flooring. Check every location where trim or built-ins meet a floating-floor edge.
  5. Inspect sheet vinyl for bubbles, wrinkles, adhesive telegraphing, open seams, and edge lift. Use raking light at every seam and perimeter edge.
  6. Inspect tile for lippage, cracked tile, hollow areas, joint consistency, grout defects, haze, and movement joints. Tap representative tiles and measure any flagged lippage.
  7. Inspect carpet for wrinkles, loose edges, seam visibility, pattern alignment, tack exposure, and transition security. Check stretch by hand-testing representative field areas.

H. Stair, Transition, and Service Access Inspection

  1. Inspect stairs and landings for movement, nosing security, trip edges, and geometry. Barricade and escalate any unsafe stair condition per the Critical Safety Cue above before continuing.
  2. Inspect thresholds, reducers, T-moldings, edge profiles, and door clearances. Operate each affected door and confirm clearance and transition seating.
  3. Check floor vents, drains, appliances, cabinets, and service access. Confirm each remains unobstructed and functional.
  4. Confirm baseboard, shoe molding, and toe kicks are coordinated with flooring movement. Verify no trim is fastened through a floating floor's field.
  5. Perform specified sound, bond, flatness, load, or operational checks. Use the method and tolerance specified for each applicable flooring system.

I. Punch List, Reinspection, and Final Status

  1. Record every deficiency by room and location. Include a description specific enough for the responsible trade to locate and correct the item without further clarification.
  2. Assign correction method and responsible trade. Reference the governing chapter for each correction where applicable.
  3. Protect accepted areas during corrections. Confirm corrective work in one area does not damage or contaminate an already-accepted area.
  4. Reinspect corrected items. Repeat the original inspection method and location for every corrected punch-list item.
  5. Compile final photographs and test records. Attach photographs of every corrected condition alongside the original deficiency photograph.
  6. Issue approved, conditional, or rejected flooring status. Record the decision, the reasoning, and any conditions that must be satisfied before full traffic or following-trade work begins.
  7. Provide protection, cleaning, maintenance, and traffic instructions. Record these instructions on the report for handoff to the property owner or next trade.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed inspection scope using the same checks regardless of which rooms or flooring types the project involves.

  • Record check: confirm every required prior-chapter record is available or its absence is noted and the condition confirmed directly.
  • Cure check: confirm every specified cure time and traffic restriction has passed before that area's findings are finalized.
  • Lighting check: confirm every room received both a normal-light and a raking-light pass.
  • Joint and seam check: confirm every material-specific defect and lippage measurement is recorded against its specified tolerance.
  • Movement check: confirm every floating-floor perimeter and transition has unobstructed, measured movement space.
  • Stair and transition check: confirm every stair, landing, threshold, and door clearance is inspected and operable.
  • Punch-list check: confirm every deficiency has an assigned correction, responsible trade, and confirmed reinspection result.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Final Flooring Quality Inspection Report is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Joint or Seam Defect Appears Only Under Raking Light

Probable cause: minor peaking, edge lippage, or seam separation that is not visible under normal overhead lighting.
Confirmation: measure the deviation with a feeler gauge or graduated shim and compare it against the tolerance specified for that flooring system.
Correction: record the finding as a punch-list item and assign it to the trade responsible for that flooring system, referencing the governing installation chapter.
Reinspection: repeat the raking-light inspection at the same location after correction.

Floating-Floor Movement Space Is Found Obstructed

Probable cause: trim, cabinetry, or a transition was installed after flooring in a way that traps the floating floor's perimeter.
Confirmation: measure the gap at the obstructed location and compare it against the expansion requirement specified for that flooring system.
Correction: record the finding as a punch-list item and assign correction to the trade responsible for the obstructing trim, cabinetry, or transition.
Reinspection: re-measure the gap at the corrected location once the obstruction is resolved.

A Stair or Landing Fails the Structural or Finish Inspection

Probable cause: movement, an insecure nosing, or a trip edge developed after Chapter 17 installation, or was not fully corrected at that time.
Confirmation: physically inspect every step and landing and compare findings against the Chapter 17 acceptance standards.
Correction: barricade the affected stair section, provide alternate access, and escalate correction to the trade responsible for stair finishes.
Reinspection: reopen the stair to traffic only after the correction is reinspected and confirmed safe.

A Cure Time or Traffic Restriction Has Not Yet Passed

Probable cause: the final inspection was scheduled before the specified product cure or traffic-restriction period elapsed.
Confirmation: compare the installation date and specified cure period against today's date for the affected area.
Correction: postpone the affected area's inspection until the specified period has passed, and note the postponement on the report.
Reinspection: complete the full inspection sequence for that area once the cure period is confirmed complete.

A Prior-Chapter Record Cannot Be Located

Probable cause: a moisture, flatness, acclimation, or product record from an earlier chapter was not retained or was misfiled.
Confirmation: search the documentation station established in Chapter 1 and confirm with the responsible trade whether the record exists elsewhere.
Correction: note the missing record on the report and confirm the underlying condition directly through field measurement before finalizing that item's status.
Reinspection: attach the field-confirmed measurement to the report in place of the missing record.

A Punch-List Item Fails Reinspection After Correction

Probable cause: the assigned correction method did not fully resolve the original deficiency, or a new condition developed during the correction.
Confirmation: repeat the original inspection method and measurement at the same location.
Correction: return the item to the responsible trade with the reinspection findings and do not close the item until it passes.
Reinspection: repeat this cycle until the item passes and the status can be recorded as corrected.

Field Terms

Raking Light
Light directed at a low angle across the floor surface to reveal lippage, peaking, seam openings, and telegraphing not visible under normal overhead lighting.
Lippage
A measurable elevation difference between the edges of two adjacent flooring units at a joint or seam.
Movement Space
The expansion gap or perimeter clearance required around a floating or wood flooring system to allow it to expand and contract without restriction.
Telegraphing
A defect or texture from the substrate or subfloor that becomes visible through a thin finish-flooring material such as sheet vinyl.
Punch-List Item
A single recorded deficiency identified by room, location, description, assigned correction method, and responsible trade.
Reinspection
The repeated check of a corrected punch-list item using the same method and location as the original finding.
Traffic Restriction
The manufacturer-specified period during which a completed flooring installation must be protected from normal foot or equipment traffic.
Final Flooring Status
The documented approved, conditional, or rejected decision issued at the close of the final quality inspection.
Final Flooring Quality Inspection Report
The document recording every inspection finding, measurement, punch-list correction, reinspection result, and the final flooring status.

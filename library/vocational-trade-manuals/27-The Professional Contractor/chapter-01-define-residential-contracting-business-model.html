Job Overview

This project produces one written, approved business model statement for a residential contracting company. The finished work product is a single controlling document — the Business Model Statement — supported by a service-area and project-fit matrix, a customer-fit and project-fit checklist, and a stop-work or decline-work list, all completed on the templates supplied in this chapter.

You will describe the owner's existing trade and management background, identify the jurisdictions in which the business may operate, select and screen candidate services, define the target customer and geographic service area, set project-size and capacity limits, identify a provable competitive advantage, estimate startup and working-capital needs, identify major risks, and write and approve the statement that results.

This is the first project in the book. Nothing in Chapters 2 through 20 may proceed ahead of it: the business structure in Chapter 2, the licensing file in Chapter 3, the insurance file in Chapter 4, the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7, the contract scope in Chapter 9, and every marketing claim in Chapter 17 are all built from the model approved here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-001 — Identify a residential service category supportable by the owner's current authority, competence, and capital.
  • PC-002 — Define a target customer by property type, ownership, and decision process.
  • PC-003 — Define a geographic service area using travel time, jurisdiction, and operational cost.
  • PC-004 — Select a project-size range consistent with staffing, cash flow, and risk capacity.
  • PC-005 — Identify a competitive advantage supported by demonstrable evidence rather than an unproven claim.
  • PC-006 — Write and obtain approval of a business model statement that will control licensing, insurance, pricing, and marketing.

Where This Skill Fits

This project has no prior project in the book to build on; it is the foundation project. It sits ahead of every other system in the Book 27 sequence: the structure and records in Chapter 2 are built for this model's ownership and services, the licensing research in Chapter 3 is scoped to this model's jurisdictions and service list, the insurance file in Chapter 4 is matched to this model's operations and risk appetite, and the pricing, proposal, contract, marketing, and capstone systems in the remaining chapters all reference the service list, customer definition, geography, and project-size limits fixed here.

The discipline used in this chapter — writing down a decision before acting on it, and revising the written record rather than the memory of the decision — is the same discipline used for the change-order log in Chapter 10, the job-cost record in Chapter 18, and the capstone operating system in Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • describing the owner's existing trade experience, management experience, licenses, certifications, equipment, capital, workforce, supplier relationships, and existing customer base
  • identifying the jurisdiction of formation and every jurisdiction where the business may offer or perform work
  • screening candidate residential service categories against current licensing, insurance, training, supervision, equipment, and permit capacity
  • defining a primary service, supporting secondary services, and explicit exclusions
  • defining the target customer, geographic service area, project-size range, and capacity limits
  • identifying a provable competitive advantage and rejecting unprovable claims
  • identifying startup capital needs and major operating risks at a planning level
  • producing an approved, dated Business Model Statement and three supporting documents

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • advertise, bid, contract, or accept payment for any service listed in the model before the licensing file in Chapter 3, the insurance file in Chapter 4, and the applicable structure and registrations in Chapter 2 are in place
  • treat this chapter's cost, revenue, or capacity estimates as guaranteed results; they are planning assumptions only
  • rely on this chapter for legal, tax, accounting, insurance, or licensing advice; every jurisdiction-specific requirement must be verified with the applicable authority or a qualified professional
  • finalize the business model without the professional review required in Section H below

Any service, jurisdiction, or claim that cannot be supported by current licensing, insurance, capital, or competence must be removed from the model before it is approved.

Trade Note

Every decision in this chapter is recorded on the reference worksheet named in that step — never left as an unwritten understanding among the owners. An unwritten business model is the single most common source of the scope creep, underpricing, and unauthorized-service exposure diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Shop Tip

Draft the excluded-services list in Section D at the same time as the included-services list, not afterward. Deciding what the business will not do is as controlling as deciding what it will do, and it is far easier to write down now than to explain to a customer mid-project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every later system in this book, from the Chapter 3 licensing matrix to the Chapter 20 capstone operating system, starts from the same four written boundaries defined here: what the business does, who it does it for, where it does it, and how large a project it is built to deliver. Getting this sequence right on paper, before any license, policy, price, or advertisement is created, means every later system inherits a consistent, defensible foundation.

Service Screening Discipline

A candidate service is only added to the model after it is checked against the owner's current licensing, insurance, training, supervision, equipment, and permit structure. A service that fails this check is removed from the model, not marked as an aspiration; it is only added back after the specific missing authority, training, equipment, or capital is obtained and documented.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Removed Service

When a candidate service is removed from the model in Section B, record the service, the reason it was removed, and the condition that would allow it to be reconsidered. Do not simply delete it from the list. The removed-service record protects the business from silently drifting back into unauthorized work later.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Advertising or Bidding on an Unverified Service

Do not advertise, quote, bid, or accept payment for any service in the model until its licensing, insurance, and permit requirements are confirmed through Chapters 3 and 4. A service that appears in the draft model is not yet an authorized service.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Model, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled copy of the Business Model Statement in the company record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow separate, informal versions to circulate among owners, estimators, or marketing. Every later system in this book references the single approved copy.

Professional Advisor Cue — Financial and Legal Assumptions Require Review

Revenue, gross-profit, overhead-recovery, owner-compensation, and cash-reserve figures drafted in Section E are planning assumptions, not guaranteed results, and startup-capital estimates must use the Book 24 cost structure and qualified financial guidance. Do not present any of these figures to a lender, partner, or customer without CPA or financial-advisor review.

E. Owner and Jurisdiction Profile

  1. Describe the owner's trade experience, management experience, licenses, certifications, equipment, capital, workforce, supplier relationships, and existing customer base. Record each item on the Business Model Statement template's profile section before continuing.
  2. Identify the jurisdiction in which the business will be formed and each jurisdiction in which it may offer or perform work. List every candidate state, county, city, or district by name on the service-area and project-fit matrix.

F. Service Selection and Screening

  1. List the residential service categories under consideration. Record each candidate service — repair, maintenance, remodeling, carpentry, painting, flooring, exterior work, or a regulated specialty trade — as its own row on the service-area and project-fit matrix.
  2. Verify that each proposed service can be performed within the anticipated licensing, insurance, training, supervision, equipment, and permit structure. Mark each row as supported, conditionally supported, or unsupported based on current information; do not mark a service supported without a specific basis.
  3. Remove services that exceed current authority, competence, capital, staffing, safety capacity, or risk tolerance. Move each removed service to the stop-work / decline-work list with the specific disqualifying reason and the condition that would allow reconsideration.
  4. Define the primary service category in terms a customer can understand. Write one sentence naming the single service category the business will be known for.
  5. Define secondary services that support the primary category without creating uncontrolled scope expansion. For each secondary service, state the specific limit that keeps it in a supporting role.
  6. Define explicit excluded services, prohibited work, emergency-work limitations, and referral categories. Record each on the stop-work / decline-work list together with the referral category or contact to be used.

G. Customer, Geography, and Capacity Definition

  1. Define the target customer by property type, ownership, project need, decision process, and service expectations. Write the definition on the Business Model Statement in terms specific enough to accept or decline an actual lead.
  2. Separate homeowners, landlords, property managers, investors, associations, builders, and commercial customers when their requirements differ. State which categories are in scope and which are excluded.
  3. Define the intended geographic service area using travel time, jurisdictions, permit systems, supplier access, labor availability, and vehicle cost. Name the specific boundary — a travel-time radius, county list, or municipal list.
  4. Identify areas excluded because of distance, licensing, tolls, parking, congestion, weather, or operational burden. List each excluded area on the service-area matrix with the specific reason.
  5. Define the minimum and maximum project size by contract value, duration, labor hours, crew size, complexity, and risk. State each limit as a specific figure or range, not a general description.
  6. Define the maximum number of active jobs the business can manage without damaging quality, safety, cash flow, or communication. State the specific number and the basis for it.
  7. Define whether work is self-performed, subcontracted, managed, or delivered through a combination. State the delivery method for the primary service and each secondary service.
  8. Define the expected ratio of repair calls, small projects, larger projects, recurring maintenance, and warranty work. Record the target ratio as a planning assumption to be tested against Chapter 18 job-cost results.

H. Advantage, Capital, Risk, and Documentation

  1. Identify the customer problem the business is designed to solve. Write one specific statement connecting the primary service to a customer need.
  2. Identify measurable advantages such as responsiveness, documented quality, specialized skill, occupied-home controls, predictable scheduling, or maintenance continuity. For each advantage, state the specific evidence the business can produce to support it.
  3. Reject competitive claims that cannot be proven or delivered consistently. Remove any advantage statement lacking specific supporting evidence before the model is finalized.
  4. Identify required tools, vehicles, office systems, software, storage, shop, and field infrastructure. List each item and its current status — owned, needed, or under evaluation.
  5. Identify required employees, supervisors, subcontractors, bookkeepers, advisors, and professional partners. List each role and whether it is currently filled.
  6. Estimate startup and working-capital needs using the Book 24 cost structure and qualified financial guidance. Record the estimate and the reviewing financial professional's name and date of review.
  7. Define target revenue, gross profit, overhead recovery, owner compensation, cash reserve, and capacity assumptions without representing them as guaranteed results. Label this section "Planning Assumptions — Not Guaranteed" on the statement.
  8. Identify major risks including licensing, injury, property damage, warranty, weather, customer concentration, labor shortage, supplier dependence, and cash flow. List each risk with a one-sentence description of its potential effect on the business.
  9. Define the business's risk acceptance, referral, insurance, contract, and contingency strategy. For each risk identified in the previous step, state the planned response: accept, insure, contract-limit, refer, or avoid.
  10. Identify legal, tax, insurance, accounting, employment, and licensing questions requiring professional review. List each open question and the professional responsible for answering it.
  11. Create a one-page service-area and project-fit matrix. Finalize the matrix begun in Section F and G with every candidate service, jurisdiction, and project-size limit in one table.
  12. Create a customer-fit and project-fit checklist. Convert the customer and project-size definitions from Section G into a yes/no screening checklist usable during Chapter 11 intake.
  13. Create a stop-work or decline-work list. Finalize the list begun in Section F with every excluded service, area, and customer category and its reason.
  14. Write the business model statement covering service, customer, geography, project range, delivery, advantage, and limits. Assemble the completed sections into the final one-to-three-page statement.
  15. Review the statement with the owner, attorney, CPA, broker, licensing advisor, or experienced contractor as applicable. Obtain and record specific comments from at least one qualified reviewer.
  16. Revise assumptions and record unresolved dependencies. Update the statement to address reviewer comments and list any item still pending professional confirmation.
  17. Approve the business model as the controlling basis for licensing, insurance, pricing, marketing, and operations. Sign and date the final statement and store it in the company record system.
  18. Assign a review date and conditions that require the model to be updated. Record the next scheduled review date and the specific events — new jurisdiction, new service, licensing change, capital change — that would trigger an earlier review.

Common Model Inspection

Inspect the completed model and its supporting documents using the same checks regardless of which service or jurisdiction is involved.

  • Service check: confirm every listed service has been screened against current licensing, insurance, training, supervision, equipment, and permit capacity, and every removed service is documented with a reason.
  • Customer and geography check: confirm the target customer and service area are defined specifically enough to accept or decline an actual lead without further interpretation.
  • Capacity check: confirm project-size range and maximum active-job count are each stated as a specific value.
  • Advantage check: confirm every claimed advantage is supported by specific, producible evidence.
  • Financial-assumption check: confirm startup-capital, revenue, and capacity figures are labeled as planning assumptions and were built from the Book 24 cost structure with qualified financial guidance.
  • Professional-review check: confirm at least one qualified reviewer's comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Business Model Keeps Expanding After Approval

Probable cause: secondary services were not given a specific limit, or a rejected service was informally re-added without documentation.
Confirmation: compare current advertised or bid services against the approved service-area and project-fit matrix and the stop-work / decline-work list.
Correction: remove any service not on the approved matrix, or formally re-add it only after the disqualifying condition from Section F, Step 3 has been corrected and documented.
Reinspection: repeat the service check on the corrected matrix.

A Lead Does Not Fit the Model but Is Accepted Anyway

Probable cause: the customer-fit and project-fit checklist was not used during intake, or its criteria were too general to apply consistently.
Confirmation: review the customer-fit and project-fit checklist against the actual lead's property type, decision process, and project size.
Correction: rewrite any checklist criterion that is too general to answer yes or no, and apply the corrected checklist to future leads before estimating time is invested.
Reinspection: repeat the customer-fit and project-fit check on the next several leads and confirm consistent screening.

Revenue or Capacity Assumptions Are Treated as Guaranteed

Probable cause: the planning-assumptions section of the statement was not clearly labeled, or was shared with a lender, partner, or customer without that label.
Confirmation: review the statement for the required "Planning Assumptions — Not Guaranteed" label and check how the figures were presented externally.
Correction: relabel the section, correct any external communication that presented the figures as guaranteed, and route future disclosures through the CPA or financial advisor.
Reinspection: repeat the financial-assumption check on the corrected statement.

A Competitive-Advantage Claim Cannot Be Supported

Probable cause: the advantage was written as an aspiration rather than a demonstrated, evidenced capability.
Confirmation: ask what specific evidence the business could produce today to support the claim; if none exists, the claim fails.
Correction: remove the unsupported claim or replace it with a narrower claim the business can currently prove.
Reinspection: repeat the advantage check on the revised statement before marketing in Chapter 17 references it.

The Model Was Approved Without Professional Review

Probable cause: a qualified reviewer was not identified early enough, or the review step was skipped to meet an internal deadline.
Confirmation: check the approval record for a named reviewer, review date, and recorded comments.
Correction: suspend reliance on the model for licensing, insurance, pricing, contract, or marketing decisions until a qualified reviewer's comments are obtained and addressed.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before re-approving the statement.

The Business Operates in a Jurisdiction Not Named in the Model

Probable cause: a customer, referral, or opportunity in a new area was accepted without updating the jurisdiction list or verifying licensing there.
Confirmation: compare the project address against the jurisdiction list on the service-area and project-fit matrix.
Correction: stop work in the unlisted jurisdiction until the Chapter 3 licensing research is completed for that jurisdiction and the model is formally updated.
Reinspection: repeat the customer and geography check on the updated model.

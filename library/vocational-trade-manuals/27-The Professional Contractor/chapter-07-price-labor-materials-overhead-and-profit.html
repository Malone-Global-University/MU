Job Overview

This project produces one controlled contractor pricing worksheet built on the Book 24 estimating workbook structure and the company's current chart of accounts and cost codes. The finished work product is a locked, tested worksheet that converts a defined scope from Chapter 6 into current material cost, burdened labor, equipment, subcontractor cost, jobsite general conditions, company overhead recovery, profit, defined risk, and contingency, resulting in one approved selling price for each pricing type the business uses.

You will define which pricing types the worksheet supports, build every required cost field, separate wage from fully burdened labor cost, build overhead-recovery formulas from actual accounting data, apply the company's approved markup or margin method, build risk and contingency fields tied to specific uncertainty, test the worksheet on one standard service, compare it against historical job costs and current market quotes, and obtain review and approval before locking the formulas.

This worksheet sits between the service menu built in Chapter 6 and the proposal and contract templates built in Chapters 8 and 9. No proposal may be issued and no contract may be signed using a price that did not pass through this worksheet.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-037 — Calculate labor cost using production rates, crew composition, and fully burdened labor rates rather than wage alone.
  • PC-038 — Calculate material cost using measured quantity, waste allowance, current unit price, freight, and tax.
  • PC-039 — Add overhead recovery to a project price using a formula built from the company's actual accounting data.
  • PC-040 — Add profit margin using the company's approved markup or margin method, displayed separately from overhead.
  • PC-041 — Account for risk and contingency using named uncertainty categories and an approved authorization procedure.
  • PC-042 — Create, test, and lock a pricing worksheet capable of pricing the company's standard services.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on the approved business model from Chapter 1, the licensing and insurance limits confirmed in Chapters 3 and 4, the financial-control system built in Chapter 5, and the service menu and scope library built in Chapter 6. Every field in this worksheet must trace back to a cost code in the Chapter 5 chart of accounts and a scope identifier in the Chapter 6 scope library; it may not introduce a disconnected duplicate cost system.

The worksheet produced here becomes the pricing engine for the estimate and proposal templates in Chapter 8, the contract-ready scope of work in Chapter 9, the change-order and allowance pricing in Chapter 10, and the job-cost and profit review in Chapter 18. The discipline of comparing a calculated price against actual historical job-cost results, rather than trusting the formula blindly, is the same discipline used again in Chapter 18 and in the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining which pricing types — time-and-material, unit-price, fixed-price, service-call, maintenance-plan, or cost-plus — the worksheet supports
  • building material, labor, equipment, subcontractor, and general-condition cost fields tied to the Book 24 workbook structure
  • separating wage from fully burdened labor cost using payroll burden, workers' compensation, benefits, vehicle, and small-tool costs
  • building overhead-recovery formulas from the company's actual accounting categories
  • applying the company's approved markup or margin method and displaying each separately
  • building risk, contingency, callback, warranty, and bad-debt cost treatment consistent with company policy
  • building approval thresholds that restrict who may change protected formulas or approve discounts
  • testing, comparing, reviewing, and locking the worksheet

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • issue a customer-facing price before the worksheet is tested, reviewed, and approved under Section H below
  • base a selling price solely on a competitor's price, a simple labor multiplier, a square-foot number, or the customer's stated budget
  • treat contingency as a way to conceal known scope or habitual underestimating
  • promise a fixed price for undefined conditions without an approved allowance, unit price, exclusion, investigation, or risk authorization
  • change a protected formula, overhead rate, or approved markup without the authority defined in the Chapter 2 signing-authority matrix

Any pricing type, cost field, or formula that cannot be supported by the company's actual accounting data or current market cost must be corrected before the worksheet is approved.

Trade Note

A pricing worksheet that recovers labor, material, equipment, and subcontractor cost but forgets office overhead, vehicles, insurance, licenses, software, callbacks, and owner compensation does not produce a price — it produces a number that happens to cover direct cost only. Recovering full cost and risk on every project is the entire purpose of this chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not issue a customer-facing price from an unlocked, untested, or unreviewed worksheet. A price produced before the worksheet passes the Section H acceptance standards must be treated as a draft estimate only, not an approved selling price.

Shop Tip

Build the overhead-recovery formula from the actual chart of accounts before touching markup or margin. An overhead rate estimated from memory, rather than from real office, insurance, vehicle, and administrative cost, is the single most common reason contractors recover profit on paper but run out of cash in practice.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every price this business quotes, from a single service call to the largest project in its approved size range, must pass through the same sequence: measured scope, current direct cost, full labor burden, jobsite general conditions, company overhead recovery, named risk, and an approved profit calculation. Skipping any one of these categories does not make the price simpler — it makes the price wrong, and the business absorbs the difference as unrecovered cost.

Labor Cost Discipline

Labor cost is never the employee's wage alone. Every labor field in this worksheet must include payroll burden, workers' compensation, benefits, vehicle cost, and small-tool cost approved by the company's accounting system before it is added to a price. A wage-only labor number is treated as an incomplete field, not a usable cost.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Wage-Only Labor Pricing

Do not use employee wage alone as labor cost anywhere in this worksheet. Every labor field must resolve to a fully burdened rate approved by the bookkeeper or CPA before the worksheet is tested in Section H.

Documentation Cue — Name Every Contingency

Every contingency field must identify the specific uncertainty it covers and the authority required to release it. A contingency line with no named uncertainty is not a contingency; it is an unexplained price increase and must be removed or corrected before approval.

Operational Discipline Cue — Lock the Formulas, Not the Data

Once approved in Section H, protect the worksheet's formulas from edits by sales or field personnel while still allowing current material, labor, and subcontractor cost data to be updated. Changing a protected formula requires the same signing authority defined in the Chapter 2 signing-authority matrix.

Professional Advisor Cue — Overhead Allocation Requires CPA Confirmation

The overhead-allocation basis used in Section G must be confirmed with the CPA and reconciled against actual accounting data from Chapter 5. Do not adopt an industry rule-of-thumb overhead percentage without confirming it against this company's real cost structure.

E. Worksheet Structure and Identification

  1. Import the Book 24 estimating workbook structure and the current company chart of accounts and cost codes. Confirm the worksheet's categories and field names align with both before any formula is built.
  2. Define whether the worksheet supports time-and-material, unit-price, fixed-price, service-call, maintenance-plan, or cost-plus work. Record the approved pricing types this business uses and build a separate calculation path for each.
  3. Identify the approved pricing date, market, service area, tax treatment, and validity period. Record these on the worksheet header and set an expiration rule.
  4. Create fields for scope identifier, project, customer, location, estimate type, estimator, reviewer, and revision. Link the scope identifier field directly to the Chapter 6 scope library.

F. Direct Cost, Labor, Equipment, and General Conditions

  1. Create material quantity, waste, purchase unit, unit price, freight, delivery, tax, return, and total fields. Tie unit prices to a current, dated supplier quote or price list.
  2. Create labor task, quantity, production rate, skill, crew, direct hours, setup, cleanup, supervision, and total-hour fields. Base production rates on historical job-cost data where available.
  3. Create wage, payroll burden, workers' compensation, benefits, vehicle, small-tool, and other approved labor-cost fields. Confirm each rate with the bookkeeper or CPA before use.
  4. Do not use employee wage alone as labor cost. Verify every labor total resolves through the burden fields before it reaches the cost summary.
  5. Create equipment, rental, fuel, delivery, operator, consumable, and minimum-period fields. Use current rental agreements or dated quotes.
  6. Create subcontractor, permit, inspection, testing, design, disposal, and specialty-service fields. Tie each to a current subcontractor or vendor quote where available.
  7. Create jobsite general-condition fields for protection, sanitation, temporary utilities, supervision, travel, parking, and project administration. Base these on project duration and crew size rather than a flat estimate.

G. Overhead, Profit, Risk, and Contingency

  1. Create company overhead categories from actual accounting data. Import the overhead categories directly from the Chapter 5 chart of accounts.
  2. Determine the approved overhead allocation basis with the CPA and management. Record the chosen basis — such as percentage of direct cost, percentage of labor, or per-project fixed allocation — and the reviewing CPA's name and date.
  3. Create formulas that recover overhead without duplicating costs already in labor, equipment, general conditions, or subcontractor pricing. Cross-check every overhead category against the direct-cost fields built in Section F to confirm no category appears twice.
  4. Create a profit calculation using the approved markup or margin method. Confirm the method matches company policy before building the formula.
  5. Display markup and margin separately to prevent confusion. Label each field clearly with its formula visible to the reviewer.
  6. Create risk categories for scope, existing condition, customer, access, schedule, material, labor, weather, permit, and subcontractor uncertainty. List each category as its own row.
  7. Create approved responses for each risk: investigate, clarify, exclude, allow, alternate, unit price, contingency, or decline. Assign one response to each risk category identified in the previous step.
  8. Create contingency fields that identify the specific uncertainty and approval authority. Do not create a generic, unnamed contingency line.
  9. Do not use contingency for known unmeasured work. Verify every contingency traces to a named, uncertain condition rather than an item that should instead be measured and priced directly.
  10. Create callback, warranty, punch, and bad-debt cost treatment according to company policy. Record the approved treatment and its source policy document.
  11. Create minimum service-call, mobilization, diagnostic, emergency, and after-hours pricing only after legal and market review. Confirm these figures with management and, where required, counsel before adding them to the worksheet.
  12. Create deposit, payment schedule, retainage, financing, and cash-flow considerations without inserting unreviewed customer terms. Leave contract-specific payment language to the Chapter 9 attorney-reviewed scope of work.
  13. Create sensitivity checks for labor hours, material price, schedule, and hidden conditions. Build a simple variance test that shows the effect of a defined percentage change in each input.
  14. Create summary views for direct cost, overhead, profit, selling price, gross margin, labor hours, and cash requirement. Present these as one clear summary table at the top or end of the worksheet.
  15. Create approval thresholds for discounts, margin reduction, contingency use, and nonstandard terms. Record the specific dollar or percentage threshold and the approving role for each.
  16. Prohibit sales personnel from changing protected formulas or approving unauthorized discounts. Apply spreadsheet or software protection consistent with the Chapter 2 signing-authority matrix.

H. Testing, Review, and Approval

  1. Test the worksheet on one standard service from Chapter 6. Run a complete price calculation using current data and record the resulting selling price.
  2. Compare the price with historical job costs, current supplier quotes, subcontractor input, and market position. Record each comparison figure alongside the worksheet's calculated price.
  3. Investigate differences rather than forcing a target price. Identify whether a difference comes from production rate, material price, overhead allocation, or an omitted cost category before adjusting the worksheet.
  4. Review the worksheet with the estimator, field lead, bookkeeper, CPA, and management as applicable. Record each reviewer's name, role, comments, and date.
  5. Approve the worksheet and lock formulas. Obtain the authorized manager's signature and date, then apply formula protection.
  6. Create a pricing revision and expiration procedure. Record the review interval and the specific conditions — cost-code change, overhead change, market shift — that trigger an earlier revision.
  7. Create a post-job feedback process from Chapter 18 to update rates and assumptions. Record how completed-project job-cost results will be routed back into this worksheet's production rates and cost data.

Common Worksheet Inspection

Inspect the completed worksheet using the same checks regardless of which service or pricing type is involved.

  • Labor check: confirm every labor field resolves to a fully burdened rate rather than wage alone.
  • Material check: confirm every material field includes quantity, waste, current unit price, freight, delivery, and tax.
  • Overhead check: confirm the overhead formula is built from actual accounting data and does not duplicate a cost already captured elsewhere.
  • Profit check: confirm markup and margin are calculated with the approved method and displayed separately.
  • Risk check: confirm every contingency names a specific uncertainty and approval authority, and none covers known unmeasured work.
  • Review check: confirm the estimator, field lead, bookkeeper, CPA, and management have reviewed the worksheet and their comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Calculated Price Is Consistently Lower Than Historical Job Cost

Probable cause: production rates are optimistic, a labor-burden component is missing, or an overhead category was omitted from the allocation formula.
Confirmation: compare the worksheet's labor hours, burden rate, and overhead recovery line by line against a completed project's actual job-cost report.
Correction: update the production rate or burden rate from the actual data and add any missing overhead category to the allocation formula.
Reinspection: rerun the test in Section H on the corrected worksheet and compare again.

A Contingency Line Has No Named Uncertainty

Probable cause: the contingency was added as a general cushion rather than tied to a specific risk category from Section G.
Confirmation: review the contingency field against the risk table and confirm whether a specific uncertainty and approval authority are recorded.
Correction: either assign the contingency to a named risk category with a specific approval authority, or remove it and price the known item directly.
Reinspection: repeat the risk check on the corrected worksheet.

Overhead Recovery Duplicates a Cost Already Priced Elsewhere

Probable cause: a cost such as vehicle expense or small tools appears in both the labor-burden fields and the general overhead allocation.
Confirmation: cross-check every overhead category against the direct-cost and general-condition fields to identify duplication.
Correction: remove the duplicated category from whichever field is not the company's designated location for that cost, per the Chapter 5 chart of accounts.
Reinspection: repeat the overhead check on the corrected worksheet.

Sales Personnel Changed a Protected Formula or Approved an Unauthorized Discount

Probable cause: formula protection was not applied, or the discount-approval threshold was not enforced in the software.
Confirmation: review the worksheet's edit history or version log and compare the discount against the approved thresholds from Section G.
Correction: restore the protected formula, correct any price issued under the unauthorized change, and reapply protection consistent with the Chapter 2 signing-authority matrix.
Reinspection: repeat the review check with management confirming the corrected protection is functioning.

The Worksheet Was Approved Without CPA Review of the Overhead Basis

Probable cause: the overhead allocation was estimated internally without reconciling it against actual accounting data.
Confirmation: check the approval record for the CPA's name, review date, and recorded comments on the overhead basis.
Correction: suspend reliance on the worksheet for new proposals until the CPA has reviewed and confirmed the overhead basis.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before re-approving the worksheet.

A Pricing Type Used by the Business Is Missing from the Worksheet

Probable cause: the worksheet was built around only one pricing type when the business actually quotes several.
Confirmation: compare the pricing types listed in Section E, Step 2 against the pricing types actually used in recent proposals.
Correction: build the missing pricing-type calculation path using the same direct-cost, overhead, and profit structure as the existing paths.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing-type coverage check on the corrected worksheet.

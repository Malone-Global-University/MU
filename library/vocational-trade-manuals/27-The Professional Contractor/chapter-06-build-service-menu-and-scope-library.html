Job Overview

This project produces one approved contractor Service Menu and Scope Library. The finished work product is a single controlling document — the Service Menu — supported by one reusable Scope Description for every standard service, a Trade-Boundary and Referral Reference, and a Service-Package Options sheet, all completed on the templates supplied in this chapter.

You will import the approved Chapter 1 business model, Chapter 3 licensing matrix, Chapter 4 insurance file, and current workforce and equipment capability, screen every candidate service against that authority, group services into categories, write a measurable deliverable and boundary statement for each one, define prerequisites and exclusions, define trade boundaries against other licensed trades, define reusable options, and test the finished library against one sample project before publishing it to sales and marketing.

This project sits directly between the business-formation and risk chapters that came before it and the pricing, proposal, and contract chapters that follow. The pricing worksheet in Chapter 7, the proposal templates in Chapter 8, and the contract-ready scope of work in Chapter 9 are all built from the exact service descriptions, boundaries, and exclusions approved here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-031 — Define standard services the business is authorized, insured, and equipped to perform.
  • PC-032 — Define excluded work, prohibited work, and referral categories for each standard service.
  • PC-033 — Write a reusable scope description with measurable deliverables for each standard service.
  • PC-034 — Identify trade boundaries between self-performed work and licensed-trade work.
  • PC-035 — Create reusable service packages and options consistent with operational capacity.
  • PC-036 — Build and publish an approved scope library referenced by estimating, sales, and project management.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 business model's approved primary and secondary services, the Chapter 3 licensing matrix's classification and scope limits, and the Chapter 4 insurance file's covered operations. A service that does not survive the licensing and insurance screen in this chapter must be removed here, not carried forward into pricing or marketing.

The reusable scope descriptions produced here become the estimating inputs for the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet, the scope sections of the Chapter 8 proposal templates, and the deliverable language of the Chapter 9 contract-ready scope of work. The trade-boundary and referral reference is used again in Chapter 11 intake screening and Chapter 16 subcontractor qualification.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • importing the approved business model, licensing matrix, insurance limits, workforce capability, equipment, supplier access, and project-size limits
  • listing and grouping every service the company intends to advertise, estimate, contract, or perform
  • removing services not supported by active license, insurance, supervision, training, tools, capital, or permit authority
  • writing a reusable scope description for each standard service with measurable deliverables, prerequisites, and boundaries
  • defining trade boundaries, referral rules, and excluded work
  • defining reusable service packages, options, and material or finish selection checklists
  • testing the finished library on one sample project and publishing only approved services to sales and marketing

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • publish, advertise, or price a service that has not been verified against the current Chapter 3 licensing matrix and Chapter 4 insurance file
  • treat a scope description as a substitute for the jurisdiction-specific contract language required in Chapter 9
  • rely on this chapter for legal, tax, insurance, or licensing advice; every scope boundary and exclusion must be reviewed by the applicable authority or a qualified professional before publication
  • finalize the scope library without the field-personnel and professional review required in Section H below

Any service, boundary, or exclusion that cannot be supported by current licensing, insurance, capability, or capacity must be removed from the library before it is approved.

Trade Note

Every scope description in this chapter is recorded on the reference worksheet named in that step — never left as an informal understanding between the estimator and the field. An unwritten scope boundary is the single most common source of the disputed exclusions and unpriced work diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not advertise, estimate, or price any service until it has been screened against the current Chapter 3 licensing matrix and Chapter 4 insurance file and recorded as supported on the Service Menu. A service drafted in this chapter is not yet an authorized service until that screen and the Section H review are complete.

Shop Tip

Draft the excluded-work and trade-boundary sections for each service at the same time as its deliverable description, not afterward. Deciding what a service does not include is as controlling as deciding what it does include, and it is far easier to write down now than to negotiate with a customer mid-project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every standard service in the scope library rests on the same four written boundaries: what problem it solves, what deliverable it produces, what it does not include, and where the trade boundary requiring a separately licensed trade sits. Getting this sequence right on paper, before any price, proposal, or contract is created, means every later system inherits a consistent, defensible scope.

Service Screening Discipline

A candidate service is only added to the scope library after it is checked against the company's active license classification, insurance coverage, training, supervision, equipment, and permit-holder authority from Chapters 3 and 4. A service that fails this check is removed from the library, not marked as an aspiration; it is only added back after the specific missing authority, training, equipment, or capital is obtained and documented.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Removed Service

When a candidate service is removed from the scope library in Section F, record the service, the reason it was removed, and the condition that would allow it to be reconsidered. Do not simply delete it from the list. The removed-service record protects the business from silently drifting back into unauthorized work later.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Pricing or Publication of an Unverified Service

Do not price, propose, contract, or advertise any service in the library until its licensing and insurance basis is confirmed against the current Chapter 3 matrix and Chapter 4 file. A service that appears in the draft scope library is not yet an authorized service.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Library, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled copy of the scope library in the company record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow separate, informal scope descriptions to circulate among estimators, field leads, or marketing. Every later system in this book references the single approved copy.

Professional Advisor Cue — Exclusion and Limitation Language Requires Review

Standard exclusions, limitation language, and trade-boundary statements drafted in Section G must be reviewed by counsel before they are relied upon in a customer-facing proposal or contract. Do not present draft exclusion language to a customer as final until that review is complete.

E. Authority Import and Candidate Listing

  1. Import the approved business model, licensing matrix, insurance limits, workforce capability, equipment, supplier access, and project-size limits. Record the reference document names and dates on the Service Menu template's header before continuing.
  2. List every service the company intends to advertise, estimate, contract, or perform. Record each candidate service as its own row on the Service Menu.
  3. Remove services not supported by active license, insurance, supervision, training, tools, capital, or permit authority. Mark each row supported, conditionally supported, or removed, and move every removed service to the removed-service record with its specific disqualifying reason and reconsideration condition.

F. Categorization and Deliverable Definition

  1. Group services into repair, replacement, installation, maintenance, remodeling, inspection, consultation, or project-management categories. Assign each supported service to one category on the Service Menu.
  2. Define the customer problem solved by each service. Write one sentence per service on its Scope Description template.
  3. Define the standard completed deliverable for each service. State the deliverable using measurable nouns, quantities, locations, and completion conditions.
  4. Define the typical property, location, system, material, and condition included. Record this on each Scope Description.
  5. Define prerequisites such as site access, utility shutoff, dry substrate, approved design, permit, inspection, owner selection, or specialty testing. List each prerequisite specifically; do not use "adequate access" or an equivalent unresolved phrase.
  6. Define work by others required before the service begins. Name the specific work and the responsible party.
  7. Define the standard project boundaries by room, area, component, quantity, duration, or contract value. State each boundary as a specific value or range.

G. Exclusions, Trade Boundaries, and Materials

  1. Define explicit excluded work, hazardous materials, hidden conditions, structural work, licensed-trade work, and restoration not included. Record each exclusion on the Scope Description in specific, named terms.
  2. Define common alternates, upgrades, allowances, and unit-price items. List each on the Scope Description with its basis.
  3. Define customer-furnished and contractor-furnished materials. State responsibility for each major material category.
  4. Define demolition, salvage, protection, disposal, delivery, setup, cleanup, testing, inspection, and closeout responsibilities. Assign each task to contractor or owner.
  5. Define access, occupancy, pets, children, parking, noise, dust, water, power, storage, and working-hour assumptions. Record the standard assumption for each service.
  6. Define trade boundaries among carpentry, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, roofing, painting, flooring, cabinets, and specialty contractors. Record each boundary on the Trade-Boundary and Referral Reference.
  7. Identify tasks that may be self-performed only under a specific license or permit. Name the specific license or permit condition.
  8. Create referral rules for services the company does not perform. State the referral category or contact for each excluded trade.
  9. Create a reusable scope description for each standard service. Confirm each Scope Description template is complete before continuing.
  10. Use plain language and measurable deliverables. Review each description and remove vague or unresolved phrases.
  11. Create standard exclusions and limitation language for professional review. Compile the exclusion and limitation language into one section for attorney review.

H. Packages, Testing, Review, and Publication

  1. Create options for good, better, premium, phased, maintenance-plan, or emergency-response packages only where operationally supported. Record each option on the Service-Package Options sheet with its supporting capacity basis.
  2. Create a material and finish selection checklist for each service. Attach the checklist to the applicable Scope Description.
  3. Create a site-visit information checklist tied to each scope. Attach the checklist for use in Chapter 11 intake.
  4. Create an estimating-input checklist tied to Book 24. Confirm every deliverable, quantity, and material item needed for pricing is present.
  5. Create a permit and inspection trigger checklist tied to Book 25. Identify which services trigger a permit or inspection requirement.
  6. Create a project-management startup checklist tied to Book 26. Identify which services require a specific startup sequence.
  7. Create a warranty and maintenance handoff checklist for each service. Attach the checklist to the applicable Scope Description.
  8. Assign a scope-library identifier, revision, owner, and approval date. Record these fields on the Service Menu and each Scope Description.
  9. Review each scope with field personnel, estimator, project manager, licensed trades, attorney, and insurer as applicable. Record specific comments from each reviewer.
  10. Test the scope on one sample project and identify ambiguity, duplication, or missing work. Document the test project and every correction made.
  11. Revise the service package and update pricing assumptions. Update the Scope Description and note the pricing assumptions affected for Chapter 7.
  12. Publish only approved services to sales and marketing systems. Confirm no unapproved or removed service appears in any sales or marketing material.
  13. Create quarterly and incident-triggered scope reviews based on claims, losses, callbacks, or licensing changes. Record the review schedule and trigger conditions on the Service Menu.

Common Scope Library Inspection

Inspect the completed scope library using the same checks regardless of which service or category is involved.

  • Authority check: confirm every listed service has been screened against current licensing, insurance, training, supervision, equipment, and permit capacity, and every removed service is documented with a reason.
  • Deliverable check: confirm each scope description states a measurable deliverable, not a general description.
  • Exclusion check: confirm each scope description names its exclusions, hidden-condition boundary, and licensed-trade work not included.
  • Trade-boundary check: confirm the Trade-Boundary and Referral Reference identifies every task requiring a separately licensed trade and its referral rule.
  • Package check: confirm every offered package or option is supported by current workforce, equipment, and supplier capacity.
  • Publication check: confirm only approved services appear in sales and marketing materials.

Complete the Scope Library Approval Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 07.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Estimator Prices a Service Not on the Approved Menu

Probable cause: an informal or superseded scope description was still in circulation outside the company record system.
Confirmation: compare the estimate against the current approved Service Menu and its revision date.
Correction: withdraw the unapproved estimate, remove the superseded description from circulation, and reissue the estimate from the current approved Scope Description.
Reinspection: repeat the publication check on all active estimating files.

A Customer Dispute Arises Over Excluded Work

Probable cause: the excluded-work statement on the Scope Description was too general, or was not carried into the customer-facing proposal.
Confirmation: compare the disputed item against the Scope Description's exclusion language and the issued proposal.
Correction: rewrite the exclusion in specific, named terms and confirm the corrected language is carried into every proposal template that uses this scope.
Reinspection: repeat the exclusion check on the corrected Scope Description and its linked proposal template.

Field Personnel Perform Licensed-Trade Work Without Referral

Probable cause: the Trade-Boundary and Referral Reference did not clearly identify the task as requiring a separately licensed trade.
Confirmation: compare the performed task against the current Trade-Boundary and Referral Reference and the Chapter 3 licensing matrix.
Correction: stop the self-performed work, correct the boundary reference, and route the task through the referral or qualified-subcontractor procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the trade-boundary check on the corrected reference before further field assignments.

A Service Package Is Sold Beyond Operational Capacity

Probable cause: a package or option was added to the Service-Package Options sheet without confirming current workforce, equipment, or supplier capacity.
Confirmation: compare the sold package against the capacity basis recorded on the Service-Package Options sheet.
Correction: suspend the package until capacity is confirmed or increased, and correct the options sheet to reflect the actual supported capacity.
Reinspection: repeat the package check on the corrected options sheet.

The Scope Library Was Published Without Field or Professional Review

Probable cause: a scope description was drafted and published on an internal deadline before the required field-personnel and attorney review occurred.
Confirmation: check the approval record for named reviewers, review dates, and recorded comments.
Correction: suspend reliance on the unreviewed scope description for pricing, proposals, or contracts until the required reviews are obtained and addressed.
Reinspection: repeat the field-and-professional-review check before re-publishing the description.

A Removed Service Reappears in Marketing

Probable cause: the service was informally re-added to marketing materials without correcting the disqualifying condition recorded in Section E.
Confirmation: compare the marketing material against the current Service Menu and the removed-service record.
Correction: remove the service from marketing until the specific missing authority, insurance, or capacity is obtained and the Service Menu is formally updated.
Reinspection: repeat the authority check on the updated Service Menu.

Job Overview

This project produces one working sales pipeline and follow-up system covering every qualified lead and issued proposal from the point the Chapter 11 site-visit summary is routed to estimating through acceptance, decline, expiration, waitlisting, or referral. The finished work product is a controlled pipeline record — populated with real or representative test data — supported by a contact-log format, follow-up message templates, an objection-and-question library, a discount-approval process, and a management dashboard.

You will define pipeline stages and their entry and exit criteria, build lead and opportunity records with consent and communication-preference fields, create a factual contact-log format, build compliant follow-up templates tied to proposal status, build an objection library that does not disparage competitors, create escalation and discount-approval rules, track decline reasons and do-not-contact status, build a sales-to-operations handoff checklist, and produce a management dashboard that does not treat pipeline value as committed revenue.

The pipeline in this project accepts only leads and proposals produced by the Chapter 11 intake and site-visit workflow and the Chapter 8 proposal templates. It does not create new scope, price, or contract terms; it tracks and communicates about scope and price that were already approved elsewhere.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-067 — Track leads through a defined pipeline with consistent stage, status, and ownership fields.
  • PC-068 — Document customer conversations factually, without speculation or protected-characteristic notes.
  • PC-069 — Send compliant, non-pressuring follow-up messages appropriate to proposal type, status, and validity.
  • PC-070 — Answer common objections factually using an approved question-and-objection library.
  • PC-071 — Update proposal status accurately and route unresolved items to the responsible owner.
  • PC-072 — Maintain a customer pipeline with decline handling, referral procedures, and management reporting.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the customer intake and site-visit workflow from Chapter 11 and the estimate and proposal templates from Chapter 8, and it feeds directly into the job startup packet in Chapter 13. A lead enters this pipeline only after Chapter 11 intake and site-visit screening; a proposal enters this pipeline only after it has been produced on the Chapter 8 template and priced from the Chapter 7 worksheet. This pipeline's job is to track, follow up on, and accurately report the status of that work — not to originate scope, price, or contract terms of its own.

The discipline used here — writing down every material conversation, never promising what has not been authorized, and closing the loop from accepted proposal to signed contract — is the same discipline the Chapter 14 customer-communication process uses once a project is under construction, and the same discipline the Chapter 17 marketing system uses when it measures lead source and conversion.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining pipeline stages, entry and exit criteria, and unique lead and opportunity identifiers
  • building lead and opportunity fields for customer, property, service, source, size, decision authority, budget alignment, timing, risk, and communication consent
  • building a factual contact-log format and applying it to sample conversations
  • building proposal status fields and compliant, non-pressuring follow-up templates
  • building an objection-and-question library and escalation rules
  • building a discount and concession approval process with field-personnel limits
  • building dormant, nurture, lost, do-not-contact, and referral statuses and procedures
  • building a sales-to-operations handoff checklist and a management dashboard
  • testing the system with one new lead, one revised proposal, one decline, and one accepted project

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • change scope, price, payment terms, warranty terms, or schedule commitments during sales follow-up; any such change must route through the Chapter 10 change or Chapter 8/9 proposal-revision process
  • contact a customer through a channel, frequency, or method not covered by the customer's recorded consent and applicable marketing, telemarketing, email, text, and privacy requirements
  • create urgency, scarcity, or discount claims that are not currently true and approved
  • continue contacting a customer who has declined or asked not to be contacted
  • treat pipeline or proposal value as committed revenue in any financial report without the qualification required in Section H

Any communication template, discount rule, or status definition that has not been reviewed under Section H must be treated as a draft, not an approved company procedure.

Trade Note

A pipeline that exists only in memory or in scattered notes is not a pipeline. Every stage change, every conversation, and every follow-up in this project is recorded on the pipeline record at the time it happens — not reconstructed afterward when a customer calls asking why nothing has happened since the site visit.

Common Field Fundamentals

A sales pipeline exists to answer four questions at any moment: what stage is this opportunity in, what was actually said to the customer, what does the customer still need to decide, and who owns the next action. Every field, template, and status built in this project exists to make one of those four questions answerable without relying on memory.

Consent and Privacy Discipline

A customer's contact information is not a standing invitation to contact them by every available channel at any frequency. Every lead and opportunity record carries its own consent and channel-preference fields, and every follow-up respects the applicable marketing, telemarketing, email, text, and privacy requirements for that channel before a message is sent.

Critical Compliance Cue — Do-Not-Contact Is Absolute

Once a customer declines or asks not to be contacted, no further outbound sales communication may be sent regardless of pipeline value, sales pressure, or manager request. The do-not-contact status in Section H overrides every follow-up schedule in Section F.

Business Risk Cue — Scarcity and Urgency Claims Create Liability

A follow-up message that states a discount, deadline, or limited availability that is not currently true and approved is a false or misleading business practice, not an effective sales technique. Every urgency or scarcity statement in a follow-up template must be verified as currently accurate before it is sent.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Record Per Opportunity

Prevent duplicate customer records and conflicting proposals by checking for an existing opportunity before creating a new one. Two proposals for the same customer and property, tracked as separate opportunities, produce conflicting follow-up and an inaccurate dashboard.

Professional Advisor Cue — Discount Authority Requires Approval

Sales personnel may not change protected pricing formulas or approve discounts, concessions, or nonstandard terms beyond the limits set in the Section G approval matrix. Any discount request beyond those limits routes to the authorized manager before it is offered to the customer.

E. Pipeline Structure and Lead Intake Fields

  1. Define pipeline stages from inquiry through qualification, site visit, estimating, proposal, follow-up, accepted, declined, expired, waitlisted, or referred. Record each stage name in the order an opportunity actually moves through it.
  2. Define entry and exit criteria for each stage. State the specific condition that moves an opportunity into the stage and the specific condition that moves it out.
  3. Create unique lead and opportunity identifiers. Assign a non-reusable identifier to every opportunity at the moment it is created.
  4. Create fields for customer, property, service, source, estimated size, decision authority, budget alignment, timing, and risk. Build these as structured fields on the opportunity record, not free-text notes.
  5. Create consent and preference fields for phone, email, text, platform, and postal communication. Record the customer's affirmative consent and preferred channel for each communication type at intake.
  6. Follow applicable marketing, telecommunication, email, text, privacy, and do-not-contact requirements. Confirm the consent fields in the previous step match the current requirements for every channel the business intends to use.

F. Communication Log, Proposal Status, and Follow-Up

  1. Create a contact-log format with date, method, participants, factual summary, decision, commitment, and next action. Build this as a structured, dated entry format attached to each opportunity.
  2. Do not record insults, speculation, protected characteristics, or irrelevant personal information. Write the contact-log standard as an explicit rule reviewed with every person who will use it.
  3. Create proposal issue, revision, expiration, and status fields. Attach these fields to the opportunity record and link them to the specific Chapter 8 proposal document.
  4. Create follow-up timing based on proposal type, customer commitment, validity, and business policy. State a specific follow-up interval for each proposal type and status.
  5. Create professional follow-up templates for receipt, clarification, selection, schedule, price validity, and closing status. Draft each template using the message-purpose categories in Component Schedule row two.
  6. Do not create false scarcity, false deadlines, fabricated discounts, or unsupported urgency. Review every drafted template against this standard before it is approved.
  7. Create fields for unresolved questions, selections, financing, design, permit, and co-owner approval. Attach these as open-item fields on the opportunity record so nothing pending is lost between contacts.

G. Objection Handling, Escalation, and Discount Control

  1. Create a common-question and objection library based on approved scope, price, schedule, warranty, disruption, permits, and process. Populate the library from Component Schedule row five with at minimum one entry per listed category.
  2. Answer objections factually and avoid disparaging competitors. Review each drafted response against this standard.
  3. Create escalation rules for legal, technical, insurance, financing, complaint, discrimination, or safety questions. State the specific role or professional each escalation category routes to.
  4. Create a discount and concession approval process. Build the approval matrix from Component Schedule row six with a specific sales-personnel limit and manager threshold.
  5. Prohibit unapproved changes to scope, price, payment terms, warranty, or schedule during sales follow-up. State this prohibition explicitly in the objection library and the follow-up template set.
  6. Create reminders and ownership for every open opportunity. Confirm every opportunity record carries a next-action date and a named responsible person.

H. Status Management, Referral, Reporting, and Approval

  1. Create dormant, nurture, lost, and do-not-contact statuses. Add these statuses to the stage list from Section E with the specific condition that triggers each.
  2. Record decline reason using consistent business categories. Build a fixed decline-reason list and require one selection per declined opportunity.
  3. Do not pressure a customer who declines or asks not to be contacted. Confirm the do-not-contact status stops every scheduled follow-up in Section F.
  4. Create referral procedures for projects outside the business model. Link this procedure to the Chapter 1 stop-work / decline-work list and Chapter 6 referral rules.
  5. Create a handoff checklist from sales to estimating and from accepted proposal to contract and operations. Build the checklist from Component Schedule row seven and confirm it links to the Chapter 13 job-start packet.
  6. Prevent duplicate customer records and conflicting proposals. Add a duplicate-check step to the opportunity-creation procedure in Section E.
  7. Create a dashboard for lead source, stage, value, age, probability, next action, conversion, and backlog. Build the dashboard from Component Schedule row eight using current pipeline data.
  8. Do not treat speculative pipeline value as cash or committed revenue. Label the dashboard's value fields "Pipeline Value — Not Committed Revenue."
  9. Create monthly sales review procedures. State the specific recurring date, attendees, and agenda for the review.
  10. Compare lead quality, estimate cost, proposal rate, conversion, margin, service type, and source. Add these comparison fields to the monthly review procedure.
  11. Review complaints, misunderstandings, lost jobs, and underpriced wins for process improvement. Add this review item to the monthly review procedure with a named responsible reviewer.
  12. Test the system with one new lead, one revised proposal, one decline, and one accepted project. Run each test case through the full pipeline and record the result.
  13. Verify that communication records and privacy controls function. Confirm the contact log, consent fields, and do-not-contact status behave as specified during the test cases.
  14. Review templates with attorney, marketing, sales, estimating, and customer-service personnel. Obtain and record specific comments from at least one qualified reviewer.
  15. Approve the system and assign pipeline ownership. Sign, date, and name the responsible pipeline owner.
  16. Create record-retention and deletion procedures. State the specific retention period and deletion trigger for lead, contact-log, and proposal records, consistent with the Chapter 2 record-retention controls.

Common Pipeline Inspection

Inspect the completed pipeline and its supporting documents using the same checks regardless of which lead source or service is involved.

  • Stage check: confirm every stage has a specific entry criterion, exit criterion, and responsible owner.
  • Record check: confirm every opportunity carries a unique identifier and complete lead fields, including consent and channel preference.
  • Contact-log check: confirm logged entries are factual, dated, and free of speculation or protected-characteristic notes.
  • Follow-up check: confirm templates contain no false scarcity, false deadlines, fabricated discounts, or unsupported urgency, and match recorded consent.
  • Discount check: confirm every discount above the sales-personnel limit is routed through the approval matrix and documented.
  • Decline and do-not-contact check: confirm decline reasons use the fixed category list and do-not-contact status stops further communication.
  • Professional-review check: confirm at least one qualified reviewer's comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Leads Stall Between Site Visit and Proposal With No Record of Why

Probable cause: the opportunity record has no next-action date or named owner, or the site-visit-to-estimating handoff was informal.
Confirmation: review the opportunity record for a missing next-action date, owner, or stage-exit criterion.
Correction: assign a responsible owner and next-action date immediately, and route the opportunity through the Chapter 11 handoff procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the stage check on the corrected record and confirm it moves to the next stage on schedule.

A Customer Reports Being Contacted After Declining

Probable cause: the do-not-contact status was not set at the time of the decline, or a scheduled follow-up was not checked against current status before sending.
Confirmation: review the opportunity's status history and the contact log for the decline entry and any communication sent afterward.
Correction: set the do-not-contact status immediately, cancel any scheduled follow-up, and correct the workflow step that failed to check status before sending.
Reinspection: repeat the decline and do-not-contact check on the corrected record and on the next several declines.

A Follow-Up Message Promises a Discount or Deadline That Is Not Approved

Probable cause: the template was drafted without review, or field personnel modified an approved template without authorization.
Confirmation: compare the sent message against the approved template library and the discount-approval matrix.
Correction: correct the customer-facing communication, document the correction in the contact log, and reinforce the prohibition on unapproved template changes.
Reinspection: repeat the follow-up check on the corrected template before it is used again.

Management Reports Pipeline Value as Committed Revenue

Probable cause: the dashboard label distinguishing pipeline value from committed revenue was missing or was removed when the report was shared externally.
Confirmation: review the dashboard and any external report for the required "Pipeline Value — Not Committed Revenue" label.
Correction: correct the label, correct any external communication that presented the figure as committed, and route future financial disclosures through accounting.
Reinspection: repeat the reporting check on the corrected dashboard.

Duplicate Opportunity Records Appear for the Same Customer

Probable cause: a new lead was entered without checking for an existing opportunity at the same property.
Confirmation: search the pipeline by customer name and property address for multiple active records.
Correction: merge the records into a single opportunity, reconcile the contact logs, and reinforce the duplicate-check step in Section H.
Reinspection: repeat the record check on the merged opportunity and on the next several new leads.

The System Was Approved Without Professional Review

Probable cause: a qualified reviewer was not identified early enough, or the review step was skipped to meet an internal deadline.
Confirmation: check the approval record for a named reviewer, review date, and recorded comments.
Correction: suspend use of the follow-up templates and discount matrix until a qualified reviewer's comments are obtained and addressed.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before re-approving the system.

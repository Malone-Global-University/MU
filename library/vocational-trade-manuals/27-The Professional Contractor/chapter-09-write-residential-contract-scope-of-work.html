Job Overview

This project produces one attorney-reviewed, contract-ready residential scope of work built from an accepted Chapter 8 proposal. The finished work product is the complete scope-of-work document — measurable deliverables, exact materials and finishes, owner and contractor responsibilities, concealed-condition and hazardous-material boundaries, schedule assumptions, acceptance criteria, and closeout requirements — reconciled against the estimate, permit file, and project-management plan, and reviewed by counsel before it becomes part of an executed contract.

You will collect the accepted proposal and every supporting document, confirm the parties and controlling document date, define the project objective, divide the work into measurable deliverables, define exact materials and product responsibility, define owner, contractor, and third-party responsibilities, define exclusions and concealed-condition boundaries, define completion and acceptance standards, define schedule and change-order boundaries, cross-check the entire scope against the Book 24 estimate, Book 25 permit file, and Book 26 project-management system, and submit the reconciled scope for jurisdiction-specific attorney review before execution.

This project follows directly from the Chapter 8 estimate and proposal templates and precedes the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance system, the Chapter 13 job-start packet, and the Chapter 19 closeout, each of which references the exact deliverables, responsibilities, and boundaries fixed in this scope of work.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-049 — Define project deliverables using measurable nouns, quantities, locations, materials, and completion conditions.
  • PC-050 — Define materials and finishes with exact products, models, grades, and allowance procedures.
  • PC-051 — Define owner responsibilities for access, decisions, selections, utilities, and property control.
  • PC-052 — Define contractor responsibilities for labor, supervision, protection, cleanup, testing, and documentation.
  • PC-053 — Define completion standards, acceptance criteria, punch-list process, and closeout documents.
  • PC-054 — Prepare one contract-ready scope of work, cross-checked and submitted for jurisdiction-specific attorney review.

Where This Skill Fits

This project converts the accepted Chapter 8 proposal into the exact document that will be attached to, or incorporated by reference into, the executed residential contract. It depends directly on the business model from Chapter 1, the service and scope library from Chapter 6, the pricing worksheet from Chapter 7, and the estimate and proposal templates from Chapter 8, and it supplies the fixed deliverables, materials, responsibilities, and boundaries used by the Chapter 10 change-order system, the Chapter 11 through 14 customer workflow, the Chapter 13 job-start packet, the Chapter 18 job-cost review, and the Chapter 19 closeout.

The discipline used here — writing every deliverable, responsibility, and boundary down in measurable terms rather than relying on the proposal's summary language or an unwritten understanding — is the same discipline used for the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7 and the job-cost record in Chapter 18.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting the accepted proposal, drawings, specifications, selections, photographs, site notes, estimate, permit plan, schedule assumptions, and clarifications
  • confirming the legal business name, customer, property owner, project address, contract authority, and authorized signers
  • defining measurable deliverables, exact materials and finishes, and product responsibility
  • defining owner, contractor, subcontractor, supplier, and third-party responsibilities
  • defining exclusions, concealed-condition boundaries, and how field conditions are documented and priced
  • defining completion standards, acceptance criteria, punch-list process, and closeout documents
  • cross-checking the completed scope against the Book 24 estimate, Book 25 permit file, and Book 26 project-management system
  • submitting the reconciled scope for jurisdiction-specific attorney review before contract execution

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin work, mobilize, or represent the scope as final before it has completed attorney review and been incorporated into an executed, counsel-approved contract
  • insert unreviewed payment, deposit, retainage, cancellation, lien, or warranty language copied from the internet, another jurisdiction's form, or a prior project without current review
  • agree to legal conclusions, added scope, or completion standards outside the process defined in this chapter and the Chapter 10 change-order system
  • treat a deliverable, material, or responsibility statement as final if it has not been cross-checked against the estimate, permit file, and project-management plan

Any deliverable, material specification, or responsibility statement that has not been priced in the Book 24 estimate, or that conflicts with the Book 25 permit file or the Book 26 project-management plan, must be resolved before the scope is submitted for attorney review.

Trade Note

The scope of work is not a restatement of the proposal in different words. It is the measurable, enforceable version of the proposal — every deliverable stated as a quantity, location, and material; every responsibility assigned to a named party; every boundary stated as a specific condition. An unmeasurable scope statement is the most common source of the disputes this chapter's troubleshooting section is built to prevent.

Setup Standard

Do not execute a contract, mobilize, or represent a scope of work as final until it has completed attorney review in the applicable jurisdiction and every cross-check against the estimate, permit file, and project-management plan has been resolved.

Shop Tip

Write the exclusions section in Section G at the same time as the deliverables section in Section F, not afterward. Deciding what is not included is as controlling as deciding what is included, and it is far easier to write down now than to negotiate with a customer mid-project.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every deliverable, material, responsibility, and boundary in this scope must trace back to the accepted proposal and must be checked against the estimate, permit file, and project-management plan before the document is submitted for attorney review. A scope statement that cannot be traced to a priced, permitted, and scheduled item is either missing work or unauthorized work, and both must be resolved before contract execution.

Measurable-Deliverable Discipline

A deliverable is only complete in the scope when it states a quantity, location, material, and completion condition a third party could verify without asking the original author. A deliverable that fails this test is rewritten, not accepted as understood.

Documentation Cue — Trace Every Deliverable to the Estimate

When a deliverable is written in Section F, record the corresponding line item or cost code from the Book 24 estimate. A deliverable with no traceable price is flagged for resolution before the scope proceeds to Section H.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Execution Before Attorney Review

Do not execute a contract using this scope of work, and do not begin mobilization, until jurisdiction-specific attorney review is complete and any required revisions have been made. A cross-checked scope is not yet a legally reviewed scope.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Scope, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled copy of the scope of work in the project file established under Chapter 2 records. Do not allow a separate, informal version to circulate among sales, field, or accounting staff once the attorney-reviewed version is executed.

Professional Advisor Cue — Completion and Payment Language Requires Review

Milestone, substantial-completion, final-completion, and acceptance-criteria language in Section H must be reviewed by counsel before it is used in an executed contract. Do not adopt generic completion language from a template that has not been reviewed for the current jurisdiction and project type.

E. Foundation Documents and Project Objective

  1. Collect the accepted proposal, drawings, specifications, selections, photographs, site notes, estimate, permit plan, schedule assumptions, and clarifications. Assemble every source document in the project file before drafting begins.
  2. Confirm the legal business name, customer, property owner, project address, contract authority, and authorized signers. Record each on the scope-of-work template header exactly as it will appear on the contract.
  3. Identify the controlling document date and revision. State which proposal revision, plan set, and clarification date governs the scope.
  4. Define the project objective and completed outcome. Write one specific statement of the finished result the contract is intended to produce.
  5. Divide the work into logical phases, rooms, systems, or trade packages. Organize the scope structure to match how the work will actually be sequenced and inspected.
  6. Write each deliverable using measurable nouns, quantities, locations, materials, and completion conditions. Draft the deliverable list for every phase, room, system, or trade package identified in the previous step.

F. Deliverables, Materials, and Product Responsibility

  1. Identify demolition, salvage, protection, disposal, and existing-condition responsibilities. State which party performs and pays for each activity.
  2. Identify exact materials, products, models, grades, finishes, colors, or allowance procedures. Record the specific product identification or the governing allowance amount for every major material.
  3. Identify who selects, purchases, receives, stores, inspects, and warrants each major product. Assign each responsibility to a named party for every product identified in the previous step.
  4. Identify substitution approval and discontinued-product procedures. State who must approve a substitution and what happens when a specified product is discontinued.
  5. Define contractor labor, supervision, equipment, temporary protection, cleanup, testing, inspection, and documentation. State the contractor's operational responsibilities for each deliverable.

G. Owner, Contractor, and Third-Party Responsibilities

  1. Define owner access, decisions, selections, utilities, property control, pets, children, valuables, parking, and payment responsibilities. State each owner responsibility as a specific, verifiable duty.
  2. Define subcontractor, supplier, designer, engineer, utility, municipality, and third-party responsibilities. Name each third party's role and the contractor's coordination responsibility.
  3. Define permit-holder, inspection, correction, and approval responsibilities. State who holds each permit and who is responsible for scheduling and passing each inspection.
  4. Define work excluded from the contract. List every excluded item in specific, project-specific language.
  5. Distinguish exclusions from owner work, future work, alternates, allowances, and unit-price work. Confirm each excluded item is not mistakenly restated as an allowance or alternate elsewhere in the scope.
  6. Define concealed-condition, hazardous-material, code-discovery, utility, structural, moisture, and damage boundaries. State how each type of discovered condition is documented, priced, and authorized.
  7. Define how field conditions are documented and priced. Reference the Chapter 10 change-order and force-account procedures for any condition discovered after work begins.
  8. Define access, work hours, occupancy, dust, noise, sanitation, security, and daily shutdown expectations. State each expectation as a specific, observable condition.
  9. Define material delivery, storage, staging, protection, and damage risk. State which party bears responsibility for delivered material at each stage.

H. Completion Standards, Cross-Checks, and Legal Review

  1. Define milestone, substantial completion, final completion, and owner-use conditions only through approved contract language. Do not draft this language without attorney-approved terms specific to the jurisdiction.
  2. Define acceptance criteria using plans, specifications, manufacturer instructions, code approvals, samples, and reasonable workmanship standards reviewed by counsel. State the specific standard each deliverable will be measured against.
  3. Define inspection, testing, startup, demonstration, training, manuals, warranties, spare material, and final photographs. State what is required for each deliverable at completion.
  4. Define punch-list process and conditions for legitimate correction. State how a punch item is identified, documented, and closed.
  5. Define cleanup, debris removal, restoration, and final site condition. State the specific condition the site must be left in at completion.
  6. Define schedule assumptions and events that may affect duration without inserting unsupported guarantees. State schedule assumptions as conditional, not guaranteed.
  7. Define allowance selection deadlines and consequences. State the deadline for each allowance selection and what happens if it is missed.
  8. Define change-order process and authorization. Reference the Chapter 10 system as the sole authorized method for changing the scope.
  9. Define closeout documents and final handoff. List every document required at project closeout under Chapter 19.
  10. Cross-check every scope statement against the Book 24 estimate. Confirm every deliverable and material has a traceable price.
  11. Cross-check regulated work against Book 25. Confirm every permit-triggering deliverable is reflected in the permit file.
  12. Cross-check execution and closeout against Book 26. Confirm the scope's sequencing and closeout requirements match the project-management plan.
  13. Identify unpriced, duplicated, ambiguous, or unauthorized work. Record every discrepancy found during the three cross-checks on the discrepancy log.
  14. Resolve discrepancies through written revision before contract execution. Correct the scope, estimate, permit file, or project-management plan as needed and record the resolution.
  15. Submit the complete scope and contract package for attorney review in the applicable jurisdiction. Route the reconciled scope to the reviewing attorney with the cross-check and discrepancy records.
  16. Obtain authorized approval and archive the reviewed template and project-specific version. Record the attorney's approval, address any final comments, and store the executed scope in the project file.

Common Scope Inspection

Inspect the completed scope of work using the same checks regardless of project type or jurisdiction.

  • Deliverable check: confirm every deliverable states a measurable quantity, location, material, and completion condition traceable to the estimate.
  • Material and responsibility check: confirm every major product and every party responsibility is assigned to a named party without gap or overlap.
  • Exclusion and boundary check: confirm exclusions, concealed-condition boundaries, and hazardous-material boundaries are distinguished from allowances and alternates.
  • Completion check: confirm milestone, completion, and acceptance language uses only attorney-approved terms.
  • Cross-check resolution: confirm every discrepancy identified against the estimate, permit file, and project-management plan has been resolved and documented.
  • Legal-review check: confirm a qualified attorney has reviewed the complete scope and comments are recorded and addressed before execution.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Deliverable Cannot Be Verified by a Third Party

Probable cause: the deliverable was written as a general description rather than a measurable quantity, location, material, and completion condition.
Confirmation: ask whether a person unfamiliar with the project could verify the deliverable is complete using only the written statement.
Correction: rewrite the deliverable with specific quantity, location, material, and completion condition, and re-trace it to the Book 24 estimate.
Reinspection: repeat the deliverable check on the corrected scope.

A Scope Item Has No Corresponding Estimate Price

Probable cause: the item was added to the proposal or scope after the estimate was finalized, or was assumed to be included in an adjacent line item without confirmation.
Confirmation: compare the scope item against the Book 24 estimate line items and cost codes.
Correction: price the item, remove it from the scope, or move it to an alternate or allowance with the customer's written agreement before contract execution.
Reinspection: repeat the cross-check against the updated estimate.

An Exclusion Is Restated as an Allowance Elsewhere in the Scope

Probable cause: the exclusions section and the allowance section were drafted separately without cross-reference.
Confirmation: compare every item on the exclusions list against every allowance and alternate item.
Correction: remove the conflicting reference and confirm the item's correct classification with the estimator and customer.
Reinspection: repeat the exclusion and boundary check on the corrected scope.

Completion Language Was Drafted Without Attorney-Approved Terms

Probable cause: generic milestone or completion language was copied from a prior project or an unreviewed template.
Confirmation: check the completion and acceptance sections against the currently approved attorney-reviewed language for the applicable jurisdiction.
Correction: remove the unreviewed language and replace it with the approved terms, then resubmit for attorney review.
Reinspection: repeat the completion check before the scope is executed.

A Responsibility Is Assigned to No One or to Two Parties

Probable cause: the responsibility sections in Section G were drafted independently for owner, contractor, and third parties without a consolidated review.
Confirmation: list every responsibility named in Section G and confirm exactly one party is assigned to each.
Correction: assign the gap to the correct party or resolve the overlap and update all affected sections.
Reinspection: repeat the responsibility check on the corrected scope.

The Scope Was Executed Without Completed Attorney Review

Probable cause: a scheduling deadline or customer pressure led to contract execution before the attorney's comments were received or addressed.
Confirmation: check the attorney review record for a completed date and addressed comments prior to the contract execution date.
Correction: suspend reliance on the executed scope for mobilization until the attorney review is completed and any required amendment is executed by both parties.
Reinspection: repeat the legal-review check before authorizing mobilization under Chapter 13.

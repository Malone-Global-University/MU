Job Overview

This project produces one written, professionally reviewed Insurance, Bonding, and Risk-Management File for the residential contracting business approved in Chapter 1 and structured in Chapter 2. The finished work product is a controlling risk file — the Policy and Bond Summary Register — supported by a certificate-of-insurance issuance procedure, a subcontractor insurance and endorsement tracking log, a renewal calendar, an incident and claim reporting procedure, and a loss-prevention plan, all completed on the templates supplied in this chapter.

You will provide the broker and bond producer with the approved business model and its services, employees, vehicles, tools, and project sizes; build a risk inventory across the company's actual operations; identify mandatory and contract-driven coverage and bond needs; submit complete applications; assemble policy and bond documentation into a controlled summary; build certificate, endorsement, and renewal tracking; build an incident and claim reporting procedure; build a loss-prevention plan; and obtain professional review before the file is approved.

This is the fourth project in the book, and it depends directly on the first three. The business model from Chapter 1 defines which services, geography, and project sizes must be insured. The entity and ownership authority from Chapter 2 determine the named insured on every policy and bond. The licensing matrix from Chapter 3 identifies which classifications carry mandatory insurance, bond, or financial-statement requirements. Nothing in Chapters 5 through 20 may proceed ahead of this file: banking and accounting controls in Chapter 5, pricing and overhead recovery in Chapter 7, proposal and contract insurance statements in Chapters 8 and 9, subcontractor qualification in Chapter 16, and every job-start packet in Chapter 13 all depend on the coverage, bonds, and certificate procedures approved here.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the approved Business Model Statement from Chapter 1, the entity and company record system from Chapter 2, and the licensing matrix from Chapter 3. The risk inventory in this chapter can only be accurate if the services, jurisdictions, employees, and project sizes it covers are the same ones approved in those three projects; a risk file built against an out-of-date model will misprice or omit real exposure.

Every later system in the book references this file rather than duplicating it. The banking and accounting system in Chapter 5 records premiums, deductibles, and claim reserves through the same cost structure. The pricing worksheet in Chapter 7 recovers insurance and bonding cost through overhead. The proposal and contract templates in Chapters 8 and 9 state licensing and insurance information only as confirmed here. The job-start packet in Chapter 13 confirms active coverage before mobilization. The subcontractor qualification system in Chapter 16 uses the same certificate and endorsement tracking procedure built in this chapter. The capstone in Chapter 20 reconciles the entire operating system against this file rather than creating a separate one.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • providing the broker and bond producer with the approved business model, service menu, jurisdictions, project sizes, payroll, sales, subcontracting, vehicles, equipment, and property for review
  • building a risk inventory across injury, property damage, completed operations, vehicle, tool, employee, cyber, pollution, professional, and contractual exposure
  • identifying mandatory workers' compensation, general liability, commercial auto, and other coverage needs, and identifying license, bid, performance, and payment bond needs
  • submitting complete, accurate applications and obtaining policy and bond documentation
  • building a policy summary, a bond summary, a certificate issuance procedure, and a subcontractor certificate and endorsement tracking log
  • building a renewal calendar, an incident and claim reporting procedure, and a loss-prevention plan
  • reviewing the completed file with the broker, surety, and other qualified professionals and obtaining approval

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select coverage limits, exclusions, endorsements, or bond terms without the broker's, carrier's, or surety's professional determination; every limit, endorsement, and bond condition in this chapter's templates must be confirmed by the licensed professional handling the account
  • represent a certificate of insurance as proof that every claim, person, activity, vehicle, tool, location, subcontractor, or contract obligation is covered
  • advertise, bid, contract, or accept payment for any service until the coverage and bonds identified as required for that service are confirmed active
  • modify, fabricate, or alter a certificate of insurance or bond document in any way
  • finalize the risk-management file without the professional review required in Section H below

Any service, project size, or contract obligation that cannot be supported by confirmed active coverage or bonding must be removed from active operations, referred, or postponed until coverage is placed and confirmed.

Trade Note

Every coverage decision, exclusion, and bond term in this chapter is recorded on the Policy and Bond Summary Register named in Section F — never left as an unwritten understanding of what "the insurance covers." An unrecorded exclusion is the single most common source of an uninsured claim diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not accept a contract, mobilize a crew, add an employee, add a vehicle, or take on a subcontractor based on a draft or assumed coverage picture. A commitment made before the applicable coverage or bond is confirmed active by the broker or surety must be treated as unauthorized and unwound.

Shop Tip

Build the risk inventory in Section E before contacting the broker, not after. Arriving at the first broker meeting with a complete, honest list of operations, vehicles, employees, and prior claims produces a faster, more accurate quote than asking the broker to guess at the company's exposure.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every later system in this book that touches money, contracts, employees, or subcontractors, from the Chapter 5 banking system to the Chapter 20 capstone operating system, assumes that the coverage and bonds identified in this chapter are actually in force, not merely intended or pending. Getting the risk inventory, coverage placement, and documentation right here, before any contract is signed or crew is mobilized, means every later system inherits an accurate, defensible risk picture instead of an assumed one.

Coverage Screening Discipline

A candidate coverage or bond is only recorded as active on the Policy and Bond Summary Register after the broker or surety has confirmed the named insured, policy or bond period, limits, and applicable endorsements in writing. A coverage that has been quoted but not bound, or a certificate that has been requested but not yet issued, is recorded as pending, not active; work that depends on it does not proceed until it changes status.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Exclusion and Declined Coverage

When the broker identifies an exclusion, sublimit, or coverage the carrier will not offer, record it on the Policy and Bond Summary Register with the reason and the operational or contractual limit it creates. Do not simply leave the gap unrecorded. The exclusion record protects the business from unknowingly accepting a contract or project that falls outside actual coverage.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Mobilization on Unconfirmed Coverage

Do not mobilize a crew, add a vehicle to active routes, add an employee to payroll and fieldwork, or accept a bonded contract until the applicable coverage or bond is confirmed active by the broker, carrier, or surety in writing. A quote, application, or verbal assurance is not confirmation.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Register, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled copy of the Policy and Bond Summary Register in the company record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow separate, informal summaries of "what's covered" to circulate among estimators, project managers, or field supervisors. Every later system in this book references the single approved register.

Professional Advisor Cue — Coverage and Bond Terms Require Professional Determination

Limits, deductibles, exclusions, additional-insured wording, waiver-of-subrogation requirements, and bond indemnity terms are determined by the licensed broker, carrier, and surety handling the account, not selected independently from this chapter's templates. Do not present any limit, endorsement, or bond term to a customer, lender, or general contractor without the broker's or surety's confirmation that it is actually in force.

E. Provide Business Information and Build the Risk Inventory

  1. Provide the broker or advisor with the approved business model, service menu, jurisdictions, project sizes, payroll, sales, subcontracting, vehicles, equipment, and property. Deliver these items from Chapters 1 and 2 to the broker before the first coverage discussion.
  2. Identify every legal entity, trade name, owner, qualifier, location, vehicle, trailer, tool, and operation requiring coverage review. List each item as its own row on the risk-inventory worksheet.
  3. Create a risk inventory for injury, property damage, completed operations, water, fire, theft, vehicle, tool, employee, cyber, pollution, professional, and contractual exposure. Mark which exposure categories apply to each operation identified in the previous step.
  4. Identify mandatory workers' compensation or approved exemption requirements. Record the jurisdiction's specific rule and the company's current employee count and classifications on the risk-inventory worksheet.
  5. Identify general liability needs by operation, project type, customer, contract, and completed-operations exposure. Record the operations most likely to generate a liability claim and the completed-operations period the business will need after project handoff.
  6. Identify commercial auto needs for owned, hired, non-owned, employee, rented, and trailer use. Record every vehicle and trailer by ownership type and typical use.

F. Identify Additional Coverage, Bonds, and Contractual Requirements

  1. Identify inland marine, tools, equipment, installation floater, property, builders-risk, crime, cyber, employment-practices, umbrella, and other coverage for review. List each candidate coverage with the specific tool, equipment, or exposure it would protect.
  2. Identify pollution, mold, lead, asbestos, roofing, excavation, hot work, water damage, design, or other exclusions that could affect the service menu. Cross-check each exclusion against the Chapter 1 service list and flag any conflict.
  3. Identify contractual insurance requirements from planned customers, general contractors, lenders, and municipalities. Record any minimum limit, additional-insured, or notice requirement found in a template contract or expected customer agreement.
  4. Identify additional insured, waiver, primary and noncontributory, completed operations, notice, and certificate requirements for professional review. List each requirement for the broker to confirm against available endorsements.
  5. Identify license and contract bond requirements. Record the bond type and amount required by each licensing authority identified in Chapter 3.
  6. Identify bid, performance, payment, maintenance, permit, subdivision, or other bond needs for planned work. Record which planned project types or customers are likely to require a project-specific bond.

G. Submit Applications and Assemble Documentation

  1. Provide complete and accurate applications to carriers and bond providers. Submit the risk inventory and entity information from Sections E and F without alteration.
  2. Do not omit subcontracting, hazardous work, vehicles, payroll, revenue, claims, or project types to reduce premium. Confirm every application matches the actual risk inventory before submission.
  3. Obtain policy declarations, forms, endorsements, exclusions, schedules, applications, audits, invoices, and broker correspondence. File each document under its policy or bond in the company record system.
  4. Create a policy summary with carrier, policy, period, limit, deductible, classification, premium, audit basis, and responsible person. Record each active policy as its own row on the Policy and Bond Summary Register.
  5. Create a bond summary with surety, producer, obligee, amount, term, indemnity, renewal, and project conditions. Record each active bond as its own row on the same register.
  6. Create a certificate-of-insurance request and issuance procedure. Record every requestor, recipient, and required endorsement on the certificate log before a certificate is issued.
  7. Do not modify, fabricate, or misrepresent a certificate. Verify every issued certificate against the underlying policy before it is sent to a recipient.
  8. Create a contract-insurance review checklist before signing agreements. List the coverage and limit confirmations required before any contract is executed.
  9. Create subcontractor insurance requirements based on scope and professional advice. Record the minimum coverage and endorsement requirements for each subcontractor trade category.
  10. Create a certificate and endorsement tracking log for subcontractors. Record each subcontractor's current certificate, expiration date, and additional-insured status on the subcontractor tracking log.

H. Renewal, Incident, Loss Prevention, and Approval

  1. Create renewal reminders far enough in advance to prevent lapse. Record each policy and bond expiration date and the advance-notice period on the renewal calendar.
  2. Create procedures for adding vehicles, employees, locations, operations, tools, equipment, and projects. Record the specific broker-notification step required before each addition takes effect.
  3. Create an incident and claim reporting procedure with emergency contacts, photographs, witness records, preservation, and insurer notice. Assemble the incident and claim reporting procedure document with each required step in sequence.
  4. Do not admit liability, promise payment, conceal facts, or destroy evidence after an incident. Add this instruction as a standing rule at the top of the incident and claim reporting procedure.
  5. Create a loss-prevention plan for water shutoff, fire prevention, hot work, theft, keys, driving, ladders, tools, dust, occupied homes, and subcontractors. Assemble the loss-prevention plan with a specific control for each listed hazard category.
  6. Create an annual insurance audit preparation file for payroll, sales, subcontract costs, classifications, and certificates. Record what documentation must be assembled before each policy's audit period.
  7. Create an indemnity and personal-guarantee review requirement before executing bonds. Record that no bond indemnity agreement or personal guarantee is signed without attorney and CPA review.
  8. Review all policies and bonds with qualified professionals and reconcile them with licenses and contracts. Confirm the completed Policy and Bond Summary Register matches the Chapter 3 licensing matrix and any executed contract requirements.
  9. Identify uninsured, excluded, high-deductible, or self-insured risks requiring service limits, pricing, reserves, or contract controls. Record each identified gap and the operational response chosen: exclude the service, raise the price, hold a reserve, or add a contract limitation.
  10. Update the service menu and project limits when coverage does not support planned work. Return to the Chapter 1 business model and remove or limit any service that cannot be supported by confirmed coverage.
  11. Approve the insurance and bonding file and establish quarterly status reviews. Sign and date the completed Policy and Bond Summary Register and schedule the first quarterly review.
  12. Archive proof of coverage while protecting confidential financial and underwriting information. Store the complete file in the Chapter 2 company record system with restricted access to underwriting financials.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Contract Requires Coverage the Business Does Not Have

Probable cause: the contractual insurance requirement was not identified during Section F before the proposal was issued, or the customer's contract template changed after the file was last reviewed.
Confirmation: compare the specific contract clause against the Policy and Bond Summary Register and the broker's most recent coverage confirmation.
Correction: contact the broker before signing to determine whether the requirement can be met by an existing policy, an endorsement, or a new policy; do not sign the contract until the gap is resolved or the clause is revised through the customer.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage and bond check on the updated register before mobilization.

A Subcontractor's Certificate Has Expired Mid-Project

Probable cause: the subcontractor tracking log was not reviewed on a fixed schedule, or the renewal reminder was not set far enough in advance.
Confirmation: check the subcontractor tracking log against the subcontractor's actual current certificate.
Correction: suspend the subcontractor's site access until a current certificate is obtained and logged; do not allow work to continue on an assumption that coverage was renewed.
Reinspection: repeat the subcontractor tracking check weekly until the log shows no expirations within the advance-notice period.

An Incident Occurs and No One Follows the Reporting Procedure

Probable cause: the incident and claim reporting procedure was created but never reviewed with field personnel, or it is not physically accessible at the jobsite.
Confirmation: ask the field crew involved whether they knew the procedure existed and could locate it at the time of the incident.
Correction: review the procedure with all field personnel, post or distribute a jobsite-accessible copy, and repeat the review after any incident where the procedure was not followed.
Reinspection: conduct a announced practice run of the procedure and confirm every field employee can complete it without assistance.

A Certificate Does Not Match the Actual Policy

Probable cause: a certificate was issued from an outdated template, or a requested endorsement was never confirmed as bound before the certificate was sent.
Confirmation: compare the certificate against the current policy declarations and endorsement schedule on file.
Correction: withdraw the inaccurate certificate, obtain a corrected certificate from the carrier or broker, and reissue it to the recipient with an explanation.
Reinspection: repeat the certificate check on the corrected document before it is relied upon for any contract.

A Policy or Bond Lapses Without Notice

Probable cause: the renewal calendar did not include the policy or bond, or the advance-notice period was too short to allow for underwriting delays.
Confirmation: check the renewal calendar against the full Policy and Bond Summary Register for any missing entry.
Correction: add the missing entry with a longer advance-notice period, and immediately contact the broker or surety to determine current status and any gap in coverage.
Reinspection: repeat the renewal check across the entire register and confirm every entry has a responsible person and advance-notice period.

The Business Accepts Bonded Work Without a Bond in Place

Probable cause: the bonding requirement in Section F was identified for the license but not re-checked against a specific project's bid or contract requirement.
Confirmation: compare the project's bid documents or contract against the Policy and Bond Summary Register's bond section.
Correction: stop work or withdraw the bid until the required bond is obtained from the surety and confirmed in writing.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage and bond check before accepting any future bonded project.

Job Overview

This project produces one working customer intake and site-visit workflow for a residential contracting business. The finished work product is a lead-intake form, a privacy notice, a project-fit screening procedure, a site-visit checklist, a photograph and measurement standard, and a site-visit summary template — tested on one live lead from first contact through a completed site visit.

You will collect only the customer information reasonably necessary for qualification and scheduling, screen the request against the Chapter 1 business model and Chapter 6 service menu, schedule and confirm the visit, obtain permission before entering the property or photographing it, document existing conditions and hazards, and produce an estimating-ready summary that is routed to estimating, design, licensing, and project-management review.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 1 Business Model Statement, the Chapter 1 customer-fit and project-fit checklist, and the Chapter 6 scope library. It feeds directly into Chapter 7 pricing, Chapter 8 proposals, and Chapter 12 sales follow-up.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-061 — Collect the customer information reasonably necessary for qualification and scheduling, protected by a privacy notice and access rule.
  • PC-062 — Screen a project request against the approved business model, service menu, and consistent nondiscriminatory fit criteria.
  • PC-063 — Schedule a site visit with confirmed date, arrival window, access, and safety instructions.
  • PC-064 — Document existing conditions using a consistent measurement method and a structured observation record.
  • PC-065 — Photograph project areas with permission, using consistent identifiers linking each photograph to a room, elevation, or scope item.
  • PC-066 — Prepare a site-visit summary that routes to estimating, design, licensing, and project-management review.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 business model, which defines the target customer, geographic service area, and project-size range this workflow screens against, and on the Chapter 6 scope library, which supplies the service categories used to route each lead. It also depends on the Chapter 2 company record system for secure storage of customer information and photographs.

The site-visit summary produced here is the direct input to the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet and the Chapter 8 estimate and proposal templates. The same project-fit screening discipline reappears in the Chapter 12 sales pipeline and the Chapter 13 job startup packet, where a project that was never properly screened here becomes a project that is never properly priced or started there.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting customer, property, and project information necessary for qualification and scheduling, with a privacy notice and access rule
  • screening the request against the Chapter 1 business model and Chapter 6 service menu using consistent, nondiscriminatory criteria
  • identifying emergency or hazardous conditions that require referral rather than a normal sales visit
  • scheduling and confirming the site visit, including access, parking, pets, and safety instructions
  • obtaining permission before entering, photographing, opening access panels, or moving objects
  • documenting existing conditions using a consistent measurement method and photograph identifiers
  • recording customer goals separately from field observations, without unsupported diagnoses
  • producing and routing an estimating-ready site-visit summary

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • quote a firm price, start date, or scope of work during the site visit
  • diagnose structural, moisture, electrical, or other specialty-trade conditions beyond a visual observation; identify these instead as items requiring specialty-trade or professional evaluation
  • enter, photograph, or operate any part of the property without confirmed permission
  • proceed with a lead that fails the Chapter 1 project-fit screening without a documented refer, decline, or waitlist decision

Any hazardous condition discovered during the visit must be documented and escalated using the emergency-referral process in Section F below rather than addressed as part of a normal sales visit.

Trade Note

Every observation recorded during the site visit is written on the site-visit summary at the time it is made — never reconstructed from memory afterward. A summary rebuilt from memory is the most common source of missed conditions, inaccurate measurements, and disputes over what the customer was told, all of which surface later in Chapter 9 contract drafting and Chapter 14 customer-expectation management.

Setup Standard

Do not enter a property, open an access panel, move a customer's belongings, or photograph any area without confirmed permission recorded before the visit begins. A visit conducted without confirmed permission must be treated as unauthorized and the resulting notes and photographs must not be used in the estimate.

Shop Tip

Separate the customer's stated goals from your own field observations on the summary at the time you write them, using two distinct sections. Mixing a customer's opinion about the cause of a problem with your own visual observation creates ambiguity that surfaces later when the estimate or contract is challenged.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every lead moves through the same four checkpoints before it becomes an estimate: intake, screening, scheduled and authorized access, and a documented summary. Skipping or informally handling any checkpoint is the most common cause of wasted estimating time, unsafe or unauthorized property entry, and inconsistent customer screening.

IntakeCustomer, property, and project information — Section F
ScreeningProject-fit against business model and service menu — Section F
Scheduling and AccessConfirmed visit, permission, and safety instructions — Section G
Summary and HandoffObservations, photographs, and routing to estimating — Section G

Screening Discipline

A lead is only scheduled for a site visit after it has been checked against the Chapter 1 business model's target customer, geographic service area, and project-size range, and against the Chapter 6 service menu. A lead that fails this check is marked refer, decline, or waitlist on the intake record; it is not silently dropped and it is not scheduled "to be safe."

Documentation Cue — Record Every Screening Decision

When a lead is accepted, referred, declined, or waitlisted in Section F, record the decision and the specific reason on the intake form. Do not leave the disposition of a lead undocumented. The screening record protects the business from inconsistent, unrecorded, or discriminatory lead handling.

Critical Compliance Cue — Confirmed Permission Before Entry

Do not enter a property, open an access panel, operate a fixture, move an object, or photograph an area without permission confirmed before the visit. Permission assumed from a scheduled appointment alone is not confirmed permission for these specific actions.

Operational Discipline Cue — Emergency Conditions Bypass the Normal Visit

A reported or observed active leak, fire hazard, structural movement, electrical hazard, gas odor, sewage, or other unsafe condition is routed through the emergency-referral process in Section F, Step 7, not scheduled as a routine estimating visit.

Professional Advisor Cue — Privacy and Nondiscrimination Review Required

The privacy notice, data-collection scope, and project-fit screening criteria used in this workflow must be reviewed by an attorney or licensing advisor to confirm they satisfy applicable privacy and nondiscrimination requirements before the workflow is approved for regular use.

F. Intake and Screening

  1. Create a lead-intake form with customer name, contact, property, ownership or authority, referral source, and preferred communication. Build the form using the fields listed and store it in the company record system.
  2. Collect only information reasonably necessary for qualification and scheduling. Remove any field from the form that is not needed for a screening or scheduling decision.
  3. Create a privacy notice and information-access rule for customer data. Draft the notice describing what is collected, why, and who may access it, and route it for professional review.
  4. Record service requested, problem description, desired outcome, affected areas, urgency, and known history. Capture this information on the intake form at first contact.
  5. Record property type, occupancy, tenants, association, lender, insurer, and access constraints where relevant. Add these fields to the intake form and complete them for the test lead.
  6. Record suspected hazardous materials, active leaks, fire, structural movement, electrical hazards, gas odor, sewage, pests, or unsafe areas. Ask directly about these conditions during intake and record the answer.
  7. Create an emergency-referral process for conditions that should not wait for a normal sales visit. Define the specific routing and contact used when Step 6 identifies an unsafe condition.
  8. Screen the request against the Chapter 1 business model and Chapter 6 service menu. Compare the requested service, property type, and location against the approved model and mark the result on the intake form.
  9. Screen jurisdiction, license, project size, budget alignment, schedule, staffing, insurance, and specialty-trade needs. Complete each screening field using the Chapter 1 project-fit checklist.
  10. Use consistent nondiscriminatory project-fit criteria. Apply the same documented criteria to every lead regardless of any protected characteristic.
  11. Identify the decision maker, property owner, co-owner, tenant, manager, designer, insurer, or lender involved. Record the identified decision maker and their authority on the intake form.
  12. Identify whether plans, permits, engineering, selections, or insurance claims already exist. Ask and record the answer before scheduling.
  13. Identify whether the customer is seeking a budget, diagnosis, estimate, insurance documentation, design, or immediate repair. Record the stated purpose of the request.
  14. Explain any consultation, diagnostic, design, site-visit, or estimate fee before scheduling. State the applicable fee, if any, to the customer and record that it was disclosed.
  15. Create accept, refer, decline, waitlist, or request-more-information statuses. Assign one of these statuses to the test lead and record the reason.

G. Site Visit and Summary

  1. Schedule the visit with date, arrival window, expected duration, attendees, access, parking, pets, and safety instructions. Record each scheduling detail on the intake record.
  2. Send a confirmation identifying documents, selections, concerns, and areas the customer should make accessible. Deliver the confirmation and store a copy.
  3. Prepare a project-specific site-visit checklist. Build the checklist from the service category identified in Step 8.
  4. Review license, insurance, privacy, photo, recording, and property-entry requirements. Confirm current status before the visit.
  5. Confirm permission before entering, photographing, opening access panels, or moving objects. Obtain and record explicit permission for each of these actions at the start of the visit.
  6. Conduct an exterior and interior overview before detailed measurements. Walk the property and note the general condition and layout before recording details.
  7. Identify immediate hazards and stop or restrict the visit where required. If a hazard from Section F, Step 6 is confirmed on-site, apply the emergency-referral process and document the condition.
  8. Document the customer's stated goals separately from field observations. Use the two separate sections defined in Section E of this specification.
  9. Measure approved areas using a consistent method. Record each dimension using the stated measurement method from the site-visit checklist.
  10. Photograph overview, detail, context, damage, access, utilities, and concealed-condition indicators. Take photographs only in areas covered by the Step 5 permission.
  11. Use identifiers linking photographs to rooms, elevations, and scope items. Label each photograph in the photograph log with its identifier and description.
  12. Do not photograph unrelated private possessions, people, documents, security systems, or access codes unnecessarily. Review each photograph before leaving the property and delete any that captures unrelated private material.
  13. Record existing finishes, materials, dimensions, access, occupancy, utilities, protection, parking, delivery, storage, and waste conditions. Complete the field-observation section using the site-visit checklist prompts.
  14. Record visible moisture, movement, damage, prior repairs, code concerns, permit indicators, and specialty-trade needs without making unsupported diagnoses. Describe what is observed, not its cause, and flag items requiring specialty-trade evaluation.
  15. Record customer selections, priorities, budget constraints, schedule constraints, and decision deadlines. Add these to the customer-goals section of the summary.
  16. Review likely scope boundaries, exclusions, investigations, and next steps without promising price or start date. Draft the scope-candidates section without stating a firm price or schedule.
  17. Obtain acknowledgment of any urgent condition or referral. Confirm the customer understands any emergency referral made during the visit and record the acknowledgment.
  18. Prepare the site-visit summary with observations, assumptions, missing information, photographs, scope candidates, and follow-up actions. Assemble the completed sections into the final summary document.
  19. Route the summary to estimating, design, licensing, and project-management review. Send the completed summary to each required reviewer and record the routing date.
  20. Archive the record and update lead status. Store the completed intake form, checklist, photographs, and summary in the company record system and update the lead's pipeline status.

Common Workflow Inspection

Inspect the completed workflow and its test-case documents using the same checks regardless of which lead or service category is involved.

  • Intake check: confirm only necessary information was collected and the privacy notice was provided.
  • Screening check: confirm the lead's accept, refer, decline, or waitlist status is recorded with a specific reason tied to the Chapter 1 business model and Chapter 6 service menu.
  • Permission check: confirm entry, photography, panel access, and object movement each have documented permission before they occurred.
  • Documentation check: confirm measurements used a stated consistent method and every photograph carries a room, elevation, or scope-item identifier.
  • Separation check: confirm customer-stated goals and field observations appear in separate sections of the summary, with no unsupported diagnosis.
  • Handoff check: confirm the summary was routed to estimating, design, licensing, and project-management review and archived.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Lead Outside the Business Model Reaches Estimating

Probable cause: the project-fit screening in Section F was skipped or applied inconsistently.
Confirmation: compare the lead's service, location, and project size against the Chapter 1 business model and the recorded screening decision.
Correction: apply the screening criteria and assign the correct accept, refer, decline, or waitlist status before estimating time is spent.
Reinspection: repeat the screening check on the corrected intake record and on the next several new leads.

Photographs Are Taken Without Confirmed Permission

Probable cause: permission was assumed from the scheduled appointment rather than confirmed specifically for photography at the start of the visit.
Confirmation: check the site-visit checklist for a recorded permission confirmation preceding the photograph log entries.
Correction: obtain explicit permission before any further photography and remove any photograph taken without it from the summary.
Reinspection: repeat the permission check on the corrected summary before it is routed to estimating.

Field Observations Contain Unsupported Diagnoses

Probable cause: the observation was written as a conclusion about cause rather than a description of what was visually observed.
Confirmation: review each observation for language that states a cause rather than a visible condition.
Correction: rewrite the observation to describe only what was seen and flag it for specialty-trade evaluation if a cause must be determined.
Reinspection: repeat the summary-separation check on the corrected document.

An Emergency Condition Is Handled as a Routine Visit

Probable cause: the intake screening in Section F, Step 6 did not ask directly about hazardous conditions, or the answer was not escalated.
Confirmation: review the intake record for the hazard-screening questions and compare against the emergency-referral log.
Correction: route the condition through the emergency-referral process immediately and document the escalation.
Reinspection: repeat the emergency-handling check on the corrected record and confirm the referral process functioned.

The Summary Reaches Estimating Without Required Routing

Probable cause: the summary was completed but not sent to every required reviewer, or the routing date was not recorded.
Confirmation: check the summary for a routing record showing estimating, design, licensing, and project-management review.
Correction: route the summary to any missing reviewer and record the date before estimating proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the handoff check on the corrected summary.

Customer Data Collected Exceeds What Is Necessary

Probable cause: the intake form was not reviewed against the qualification-and-scheduling standard before use.
Confirmation: review each field on the intake form and ask whether it is necessary for qualification or scheduling.
Correction: remove any unnecessary field and update the privacy notice to match the corrected data-collection scope.
Reinspection: repeat the intake check on the corrected form before it is used on the next lead.

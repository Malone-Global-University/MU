Job Overview

This project produces one controlled change-order and allowance management system for use on every contracted project. The finished work product is a set of five linked, reusable documents — a potential-change record, a change-order document, a pending-change log, an approved-change log, and an allowance register — each populated with one tested owner-requested change and one tested concealed-condition change.

You will build the procedure for identifying a potential change from any source, documenting the condition separately from any conclusion about fault, pricing the change using the Chapter 7 worksheet and Book 24 controls, stating price and time impact separately, routing the change through a defined approval matrix, and updating the contract sum, schedule, and accounting records only after written authorization is obtained. You will also build the allowance register that tracks every allowance from its original amount through selection, commitment, installation, variance, and remaining balance.

This project builds directly on the Chapter 9 contract-ready scope of work, which defines the change-order process and authorization language the contract itself uses, and on the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet, which supplies the labor, material, equipment, subcontractor, overhead, and profit inputs for every priced change. The system built here is used continuously from Chapter 13 job startup through Chapter 18 job costing and Chapter 19 project closeout.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-055 — Identify a potential scope change from owner request, concealed condition, design revision, code requirement, substitution, damage, delay, or scope transfer, and document it separately from any conclusion about fault.
  • PC-056 — Price added, omitted, or credited work using the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet and Book 24 controls, stating price and schedule impact separately.
  • PC-057 — Track an allowance from its original amount through selection, quote, commitment, installation, tax, markup, variance, and remaining balance.
  • PC-058 — Document written owner, contractor, designer, or other required-party approval before changed work proceeds, except under an approved emergency or directive procedure.
  • PC-059 — Update the contract sum, schedule, budget, purchase orders, subcontracts, invoices, and drawings after a change is approved.
  • PC-060 — Maintain a pending-change log and an approved-change log and close each change only after field work, documentation, cost, schedule, billing, and customer approval are reconciled.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on the attorney-approved change, directive, emergency, allowance, and notice provisions already written into the Chapter 9 contract-ready scope of work, and on the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet used to price every change. It cannot be finalized before those two systems are approved.

The change-order and allowance logs built here become permanent project records referenced in the Chapter 13 job-start packet, the Chapter 14 customer-communication process, the Chapter 18 job-cost review, and the Chapter 19 final walkthrough and invoice. An unpriced or unauthorized change discovered at Chapter 18 job costing is far more expensive to correct than one caught and priced here, at the point the condition is first observed.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying a potential change from any of the sources named in the Chapter 9 contract and documenting the condition with photographs, field notes, measurements, and observations separate from conclusions about entitlement or fault
  • creating a notice procedure that satisfies the contract's and jurisdiction's notice requirements
  • pricing added, omitted, or credited work using the Chapter 7 worksheet, stating price and time impact separately
  • creating an approval matrix, a pending-change log, an approved-change log, and a rejected/withdrawn/no-cost-change status
  • creating an allowance register, owner selection forms, and credit rules for unused allowances consistent with the approved contract
  • creating a unit-price work log and a directive-work log where the contract permits directed work before final price is agreed
  • updating the customer, operations, accounting, procurement, and field team after every approval, and closing each change only after field work, documentation, cost, schedule, billing, and customer approval are reconciled

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with changed work based solely on an informal verbal request, except under the specific emergency procedure approved in the Chapter 9 contract
  • combine multiple unrelated owner selections into one opaque allowance, or use an allowance to avoid defining foreseeable scope
  • insert unreviewed change-order, directive, or notice language copied from another jurisdiction or another company's form
  • mark a change closed before field work, documentation, cost, schedule, billing, and customer approval are all reconciled

Any change-order, directive-work, or notice language used in this project must be reviewed by qualified counsel in the applicable jurisdiction before it is used on an active contract.

Trade Note

A potential change is a fact pattern, not yet a conclusion. Recording what was observed — a photograph, a measurement, a date, a location — before recording any statement about who is responsible protects the pricing and approval process that follows. Conclusions about fault or entitlement are resolved through the approval matrix and, where necessary, the attorney-reviewed contract terms, not through the field note itself.

Shop Tip

Build the allowance register at the same time as the change-order log, not afterward. An allowance that runs over its committed amount is a change in fact, even when no one has called it one, and it will not surface correctly in Chapter 18 job costing unless the register has been tracking variance from the first selection forward.

Entitlement and Fault Discipline

A potential-change record documents what was observed: a location, a measurement, a photograph, a date, and the party who first noticed the condition. It does not include a conclusion about who caused the condition or who must pay for it. That conclusion is reached separately, through the approval matrix and, where the amount or the dispute requires it, through attorney-reviewed contract terms. Recording a conclusion in the same field where the observation is recorded makes the record harder to defend if the change is later disputed.

Documentation Cue — Separate Observation From Conclusion

Record the condition, its location, its dimensions, and its photographs in the observation field of the potential-change record. Record any statement about cause, fault, or entitlement in a separate field, dated and attributed to the person making the statement. Do not merge the two.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Changed Work Without Written Authorization

Do not begin changed work before written authorization is obtained, except under the specific emergency procedure approved in the Chapter 9 contract. Work performed on an informal verbal request must be treated as unauthorized until it is documented, priced, and approved through the matrix in Section G.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Log, Updated at Every Billing Cycle

Maintain a single controlled pending-change log and a single controlled approved-change log in the company record system established in Chapter 2. Review open items at every project meeting and billing cycle identified in the Chapter 14 customer-communication calendar. Do not allow a separate, informal change list to circulate among field, sales, and accounting.

Professional Advisor Cue — Notice, Directive, and Authorization Language Requires Review

The notice procedure, directive-work language, and approval-matrix authorization statements used in this system must match the attorney-reviewed Chapter 9 contract terms exactly. Do not draft new legal language for this project; use only the terms already approved for the contract, or route any new term through the same attorney review before it is used.

E. Identification, Evidence, and Notice

  1. Review the attorney-approved contract change, directive, emergency, allowance, and notice provisions. Record the specific contract section numbers on the potential-change record template header before drafting any procedure.
  2. Create a potential-change record with unique identifier, date, source, location, description, and initiating party. Assign a sequential identifier scheme and record it on the template.
  3. Define change sources such as owner request, concealed condition, design revision, code requirement, substitution, damage, delay, or scope transfer. List each source as a selectable field on the template.
  4. Require photographs, field notes, document references, measurements, and responsible-party observations. Add a mandatory evidence-attachment field to the template.
  5. Separate documentation of the condition from conclusions about fault or entitlement. Create two distinct fields on the template: "Observed Condition" and "Statements on Cause or Entitlement," each separately dated and attributed.
  6. Identify whether work should stop, continue for safety, be protected, or await direction. Add a required decision field with these four options and a space for the responsible person's name.
  7. Create a notice procedure that meets contract and jurisdictional requirements. Draft the notice timing, method, and recipient exactly as stated in the Chapter 9 contract; do not shorten or lengthen the notice period.
  8. Create an RFI or clarification link where design information is required. Add a reference field connecting the potential-change record to any open request for information.

F. Pricing and Time Impact

  1. Define original scope, proposed changed scope, omitted work, added work, and credit. Add these five fields to the change-order document template.
  2. Perform quantity takeoff and pricing using Chapter 7 and Book 24 controls. Link the change-order template directly to the approved Chapter 7 pricing worksheet; do not create a separate, disconnected pricing method.
  3. Include labor, material, equipment, subcontractor, supervision, protection, cleanup, delay, remobilization, tax, overhead, profit, and bond or insurance effects as approved. Confirm each cost category on the worksheet has a corresponding field on the change-order document.
  4. State time effect separately from price. Create a distinct "Time Impact" field on the change-order document, stated in calendar or working days, separate from the price fields.
  5. Identify schedule predecessor, procurement, inspection, access, and completion effects. Add a schedule-impact narrative field describing which specific schedule activities are affected.
  6. Create a change-order document with description, amount, credit, net change, time, assumptions, exclusions, and expiration. Assemble the complete template with all required fields in a fixed order.

G. Authorization, Logs, and Directive Work

  1. Create an approval matrix for customer, contractor, designer, lender, insurer, and other parties. List each party who must approve a change of a given type or dollar threshold, matching the Chapter 9 contract's authorization requirements.
  2. Do not proceed based solely on an informal verbal request except under the approved emergency procedure. Add a required-authorization checkbox to the change-order document that must be completed before field work begins.
  3. Create electronic and paper authorization controls. Define the accepted signature methods and the retention location for each.
  4. Create a pending-change log with status, value, exposure, owner, due date, and impact. Build the log template with one row per potential or priced change not yet authorized.
  5. Create an approved-change log linked to contract sum, schedule, budget, purchase orders, subcontracts, invoices, and drawings. Build the log template with columns linking each approved item to these six records.
  6. Create a rejected, withdrawn, and no-cost-change status. Add these three statuses to both logs so a resolved item is never left showing as pending.
  7. Create a unit-price work log with measured quantity, unit, verification, rate, and authorization. Build the log with a field confirming the measurement method used to verify quantity before payment.
  8. Create a directive-work log where the contract permits directed work before final price. Confirm the Chapter 9 contract permits this method before building the log; if it does not, mark this log not applicable and record the reason.
  9. Create a daily force-account record for labor, material, equipment, subcontractor, and signatures where applicable. Build a daily form requiring same-day signature from the customer's authorized representative.
  10. Create a change-invoice and payment-status link. Add a field connecting each approved change to its corresponding invoice line and payment status in the Chapter 5 accounting system.

H. Allowances, Reconciliation, and System Testing

  1. Create an allowance register with original allowance, selection, quote, tax, delivery, installation, markup, committed amount, forecast, variance, and remaining balance. Build the register with one row per allowance item, matching the allowances stated in the Chapter 9 contract.
  2. Define what each allowance includes. Add a description field to each allowance row stating exactly what is and is not covered by the stated amount.
  3. Do not combine multiple unrelated selections into one opaque allowance. Confirm each row represents one defined selection category; split any combined row that covers unrelated items.
  4. Create owner selection forms with due date, approved product, price effect, lead time, and signature. Build the form and link it to the corresponding allowance-register row.
  5. Create credit rules for unused allowance subject to approved contract language. State the credit calculation exactly as written in the Chapter 9 contract; do not invent a different treatment.
  6. Create a procedure to update the customer, operations, accounting, procurement, and field team after approval. List the specific person or role notified in each department and the required notification method and timing.
  7. Replace superseded scope and drawings in active use while preserving history. Define the version-control method that marks a superseded document while retaining the original in the project archive.
  8. Review open changes at every project meeting and billing cycle. Add a standing agenda item to the Chapter 14 customer-communication calendar referencing the pending-change log.
  9. Do not mark a change closed until field work, documentation, cost, schedule, billing, and customer approval are reconciled. Add a five-part closure checklist to the approved-change log requiring all five items before a change is marked closed.
  10. Test the system with one owner-requested change and one concealed-condition change. Select one candidate of each type from the sample project identified in Section C and process each completely through identification, pricing, authorization, logging, and closure.
  11. Review the system with attorney, estimator, project manager, accounting, and customer-service personnel. Record specific comments from each reviewer on the two test changes and the templates.
  12. Approve the forms and logs and assign administration responsibility. Sign and date the approved system and name the person responsible for maintaining the logs going forward.

Common System Inspection

Inspect the completed system and its two test changes using the same checks regardless of which change source or contract type is involved.

  • Evidence check: confirm each potential-change record separates observed condition from any statement about cause or entitlement, and includes photographs, measurements, and dates where applicable.
  • Pricing check: confirm every priced change uses the Chapter 7 worksheet and current Book 24 cost data, with price and time impact stated in separate fields.
  • Authorization check: confirm no changed work in either test case proceeded before written authorization, except where the approved emergency procedure applied.
  • Allowance check: confirm the allowance register tracks original amount, commitment, and remaining balance for at least one complete selection, with no unrelated items combined.
  • Log check: confirm the pending-change log and approved-change log are current and that the approved test change updated contract sum, schedule, and the linked accounting records.
  • Closure check: confirm the approved test change was marked closed only after field work, documentation, cost, schedule, billing, and customer approval were reconciled.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Changed Work Was Performed Before Written Authorization

Probable cause: field personnel proceeded on a verbal customer request, or treated an informal text message as sufficient authorization.
Confirmation: compare the date changed work began against the date recorded on the change-order document's authorization field.
Correction: stop further changed work until the item is documented, priced, and authorized through the approval matrix; escalate to management and, if the emergency procedure does not apply, treat the performed work as an open exposure item on the pending-change log until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the authorization check on the corrected record and confirm field personnel training addresses the gap.

An Allowance Runs Over Its Committed Amount Without Being Flagged

Probable cause: the allowance register was not updated at the time of selection, or the selection amount was recorded without tax, delivery, or installation cost included.
Confirmation: compare the committed amount on the register against the actual purchase order, invoice, or vendor quote for that selection.
Correction: update the register with the complete committed amount, calculate the variance, and route any overage through the change-order process rather than absorbing it silently.
Reinspection: repeat the allowance check on the corrected register and confirm the variance is now visible.

A Change Is Marked Closed but Billing Does Not Match

Probable cause: the change was closed based on completed field work alone, without confirming the invoice line, payment status, and customer approval were also reconciled.
Confirmation: review the five-part closure checklist on the approved-change log entry and identify which item was not actually completed.
Correction: reopen the change status, complete the missing reconciliation item, and re-close only after all five items are confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check on the corrected log entry and on the next several closed changes to confirm consistent practice.

A Concealed-Condition Record Contains a Fault Conclusion in the Observation Field

Probable cause: the field crew or estimator recorded a statement about who caused the condition in the same field used to describe what was observed.
Confirmation: review the potential-change record for any causal or blame language mixed into the observed-condition field.
Correction: move any causal or entitlement statement into the separate "Statements on Cause or Entitlement" field, dated and attributed to its source, and leave the observed-condition field limited to measurable, photographed fact.
Reinspection: repeat the evidence check on the corrected record before it is used to support pricing or a dispute.

The Pending-Change Log and the Contract Sum Do Not Match

Probable cause: an approved change was not moved from the pending-change log to the approved-change log, or the contract-sum update step was skipped after approval.
Confirmation: compare the current approved-change log total against the contract sum shown in the accounting and project-management records.
Correction: move any approved item to the correct log, update the contract sum, schedule, budget, and linked purchase orders, subcontracts, and invoices, and record the correction date.
Reinspection: repeat the log check after the next full billing cycle to confirm the logs and contract sum remain synchronized.

Directive Work Was Performed Without Contract Authority to Use That Method

Probable cause: field or project-management personnel used the directive-work log without first confirming the Chapter 9 contract permits directed work before final price agreement.
Confirmation: review the Chapter 9 contract's change-order provisions for directive-work authority.
Correction: if the contract does not permit this method, stop using the directive-work log, convert the item to a standard change order with agreed price before further work, and mark the directive-work log not applicable for this contract.
Reinspection: repeat the authorization check to confirm all remaining changes route through the standard approval matrix.

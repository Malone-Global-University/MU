Field Cue System

These cues appear at the point of work. They identify compliance, professional-review, jurisdictional, and documentation issues before an unauthorized transaction, commingled fund, or missing tax record becomes a business risk.

△

Business Risk Cue

A choice that increases financial, legal, or operational exposure if left unaddressed.

✋

Critical Compliance Cue

An action that must not be taken, or must be stopped, until a specific legal, tax, or banking condition is satisfied.

⛑

Operational Discipline Cue

Practical controls that keep financial records accurate as transactions actually occur.

▤

Licensing and Jurisdiction Alert

Tax, payroll, and filing requirements vary by state, county, and municipality. Verify current requirements before relying on this chapter.

▧

Professional Advisor Cue

A point where a CPA, bookkeeper, bank officer, payroll provider, or attorney must be consulted before proceeding.

▱

Documentation Cue

A decision, account, or figure that must be written down, dated, and retained in the company record system.

Job Overview

This project produces one operating financial-control system for a residential contracting company. The finished work product is a set of properly named business bank accounts, a chart of accounts aligned with the Book 24 cost-code structure, documented income and expense categories, receipt and invoice procedures, payroll and subcontractor tax-document controls, monthly reconciliation and reporting procedures, a cash forecast, and tax-document and record-retention folders, all reviewed by the company's CPA, bookkeeper, payroll provider, and bank.

You will open accounts in the correct legal name, define account purposes, create signing authority and dual-control rules, build the accounting chart of accounts, define income and direct-cost and overhead categories, create customer and vendor records, define receipt-capture and petty-cash rules, set up payroll and subcontractor tax-document procedures, identify tax obligations, create reconciliation and reporting procedures, build a cash forecast, run a complete test transaction, and obtain professional review and approval of the finished financial-control checklist.

This project depends directly on the entity, ownership, and signing-authority records approved in Project 02, and it supplies the account structure, cost codes, and controls that every later pricing, proposal, contract, job-costing, and closeout project in this book will reference. No customer deposit, payment, payroll, or job cost may be received or recorded until this system is operating.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-025 — Separate business and personal funds by opening and controlling business accounts in the correct legal name.
  • PC-026 — Organize income records by customer, project, contract, and payment status.
  • PC-027 — Organize expense records by direct cost, overhead, vendor, and cost code.
  • PC-028 — Track receipts using a consistent date, vendor, amount, project, cost-code, and approver format.
  • PC-029 — Prepare tax-document folders organized by filing year and form type.
  • PC-030 — Establish basic financial controls including signing authority, dual approval, reconciliation, and fraud prevention.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the entity, ownership, signing-authority, and company-record system approved in Project 02, and it depends on the licensing status confirmed in Project 03 and the insurance file confirmed in Project 04, since the bank, CPA, and payroll provider must know the company's authorized services, jurisdictions, and coverage before accounts and payroll are opened.

Every later project in this book reads from the account structure and chart of accounts created here: the pricing worksheet in Project 07 recovers overhead using the categories defined in this project's accounting chart, the job-cost review in Project 18 compares estimate to actual cost using these same cost codes, and the final business closeout in the Project 20 capstone reconciles revenue, cost, tax, payroll, and reserves against this system. Do not create a duplicate or informal accounting system later in the book; every subsequent project must reference the single system approved here.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • opening business bank accounts in the correct legal entity and taxpayer identity approved in Project 02
  • defining operating, payroll, tax, savings, and other account purposes with professional advice
  • creating signing authority, approval limits, dual-control, and online-access rules
  • creating fraud controls for vendor changes, wire transfers, ACH, checks, credit cards, and reimbursements
  • building an accounting chart of accounts aligned with the Book 24 and Book 26 cost-code structure
  • creating income, direct-cost, overhead, and owner-equity categories
  • creating customer, vendor, purchase-order, receipt, and payroll and subcontractor tax-document procedures
  • identifying tax obligations and creating tax-liability accounts and payment schedules with qualified professionals
  • creating monthly reconciliation, reporting, cash-forecast, and record-retention procedures
  • running one complete test transaction and obtaining professional review and approval

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • determine worker classification, tax treatment, deductible status, or filing requirements without a qualified CPA, tax professional, or payroll provider; this chapter organizes records, it does not supply tax or legal conclusions
  • deposit customer funds into a personal account, spend payroll withholding, collected tax, trust funds, or restricted project funds under any circumstance
  • rely on a tax form alone as proof that a worker is legally an independent contractor
  • receive a customer deposit, issue payroll, or pay a subcontractor before this system is reviewed and approved in Section H below

Any account, category, or procedure that has not been confirmed with the bank, CPA, bookkeeper, or payroll provider must be marked pending and must not be treated as operational.

Trade Note

A contractor's financial system fails most often not from a missing account but from an undocumented exception — a customer deposit run through a personal account "just this once," an unrecorded cash payment to a helper, or a vendor-payment change made by phone without independent verification. Every exception in this chapter is closed by a written rule before the first transaction occurs.

Fund Separation Discipline

No customer payment, deposit, or reimbursement may be deposited into a personal account under any circumstance, and no business expense may be paid from a personal account without a documented reimbursement procedure reviewed by the CPA. A single commingled transaction is treated as a control failure requiring correction and documentation, not as a convenience to be repeated.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Customer Funds in Personal Accounts

Do not deposit a customer payment, deposit, or retainage into any account other than the approved business operating, payroll, or trust account named in Section E. A deposit made into a personal account must be corrected immediately and documented as an exception.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Account and Its Purpose

Record each bank account's legal name, account purpose, opening date, signers, and approval limits on the Financial-Control Checklist before the account is used for any transaction. Do not use an account for a purpose other than the one recorded.

Professional Advisor Cue — Tax and Classification Questions Require Review

Sales tax, payroll tax, worker classification, and filing-obligation determinations must be made by the CPA, tax professional, or qualified payroll provider identified in Section H, not assumed from this chapter or from a tax form a worker has signed.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Chart of Accounts, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled chart of accounts and cost-code list in the company record system established in Project 02. Do not allow estimators, bookkeepers, or field personnel to use informal or duplicate cost-code lists.

E. Banking Structure and Signing Authority

  1. Provide the bank, CPA, bookkeeper, payroll provider, and tax professional with the approved entity, ownership, authority, licenses, and business model. Record the date and attendees of this initial meeting on the Financial-Control Checklist.
  2. Open business bank accounts in the correct legal name and taxpayer identity. Record the account name, account number reference, and opening date on the bank-account and signing-authority log.
  3. Define operating, payroll, tax, savings, equipment, credit-card, line-of-credit, deposit, trust, or other accounts based on professional advice. Record the specific purpose of each account and mark any account still pending professional confirmation.
  4. Do not deposit customer funds into personal accounts. Record this rule as a standing control on the Financial-Control Checklist with the corrective procedure for any violation.
  5. Create bank-signing authority, approval limits, dual-control, and online-access rules. Record each authorized signer, their approval limit, and any dual-control requirement on the bank-account and signing-authority log.
  6. Create fraud controls for vendor changes, wire transfers, ACH, checks, credit cards, refunds, and employee reimbursements. Record the specific verification step required before each transaction type is executed.

F. Chart of Accounts and Category Structure

  1. Create an accounting chart aligned with the Book 24 cost codes and Book 26 job-cost system. Record each account category and its corresponding Book 24 or Book 26 cost code on the chart-of-accounts worksheet.
  2. Create income categories for contract revenue, approved changes, allowances, service calls, maintenance, deposits, refunds, tax, and other receipts. List each income category as its own row.
  3. Create direct-cost categories for labor, materials, subcontractors, equipment, permits, delivery, disposal, and project-specific expenses. List each direct-cost category and its associated cost code.
  4. Create overhead categories for office, insurance, licenses, vehicles, software, marketing, professional fees, training, and administration. List each overhead category separately from direct cost.
  5. Create owner equity, contribution, distribution, draw, loan, and reimbursement categories with professional guidance. Record each category and the CPA's guidance on its use.

G. Transaction, Payroll, and Subcontractor Controls

  1. Create customer, project, contract, invoice, payment, credit, and receivable records. Record the fields required for each customer and project record on the chart-of-accounts worksheet.
  2. Create vendor, purchase, bill, payment, credit, tax-document, and payable records. Record the fields required for each vendor record.
  3. Create purchase-order and authorization requirements by amount and category. Record the specific dollar thresholds requiring purchase-order approval.
  4. Create a receipt-capture procedure using date, vendor, amount, project, purpose, and approver. Enter the procedure on the receipt-capture and petty-cash log template.
  5. Prohibit undocumented cash spending except through a controlled petty-cash procedure. Record the petty-cash limit, custodian, and reconciliation frequency.
  6. Create employee timekeeping, payroll, reimbursement, benefit, withholding, and personnel-data procedures. Record the payroll provider's procedure and pay schedule.
  7. Create subcontractor tax-document, insurance, invoice, payment, lien-release, and classification records. Record the specific documents required before a subcontractor invoice is paid.
  8. Do not use a tax form as proof that a worker is legally an independent contractor. Record this rule as a standing control with the CPA's classification-review procedure.
  9. Identify sales tax, use tax, gross-receipts tax, payroll tax, income tax, franchise tax, excise, local tax, and filing obligations with qualified professionals. Record each applicable obligation, filing frequency, and responsible preparer.
  10. Create tax liability accounts and payment schedules. Record each tax-liability account and its funding and remittance schedule.
  11. Do not spend payroll withholding, collected tax, trust funds, or restricted project funds. Record this rule as a standing control with the corrective procedure for any violation.

H. Reconciliation, Reporting, Testing, and Approval

  1. Create monthly bank and credit-card reconciliation procedures. Record the responsible person and the target completion date each month.
  2. Create monthly accounts receivable, accounts payable, cash-flow, job-cost, balance-sheet, and income-statement review procedures. Record the review schedule and responsible reviewer.
  3. Create a cash forecast showing expected receipts, payroll, tax, material, subcontract, debt, and overhead. Record the forecast period and update frequency.
  4. Create payment acceptance, refund, chargeback, financing, card-fee, and fraud procedures. Record each procedure and its responsible person.
  5. Create debt, lease, equipment finance, and personal-guarantee records. Record each obligation and its review status with counsel.
  6. Create a fixed-asset and depreciation-information file for professional tax treatment. Record each fixed asset and its acquisition data for the CPA.
  7. Create tax-document folders by filing year and form type. Build the tax-document folder index with a folder for each filing year and form type.
  8. Create record-retention and secure-destruction schedules with professional advice. Record the retention period for each record category.
  9. Create backup, access, password, device, and cybersecurity procedures for financial systems. Record the specific control for each system.
  10. Run a test transaction from estimate through invoice, payment, purchase, payroll, reconciliation, job cost, and financial report. Record the test transaction's project identifier and each processing step completed.
  11. Correct duplicate, miscoded, unreconciled, or unsupported entries. Record each correction and the date it was resolved.
  12. Review the full system with the CPA, bookkeeper, bank, payroll provider, and attorney as applicable. Record each reviewer's name, role, and comments on the Financial-Control Checklist.
  13. Approve the financial-control checklist and assign daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annual responsibilities. Sign and date the checklist, and record the responsible person for each recurring task.

Common System Inspection

Inspect the completed financial system using the same checks regardless of business size or jurisdiction.

  • Fund-separation check: confirm no customer or business fund has been deposited into or paid from a personal account.
  • Authority check: confirm every account has documented signers, approval limits, and dual-control rules.
  • Chart-of-accounts check: confirm income, direct-cost, overhead, and equity categories are aligned with the Book 24 and Book 26 cost-code structure.
  • Record check: confirm every transaction can be traced to a date, vendor, amount, project, cost code, purpose, and approver.
  • Payroll and subcontractor check: confirm classification, payroll, and subcontractor tax-document procedures were reviewed by qualified professionals.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm monthly reconciliation, reporting, and cash-forecast procedures are assigned and operating.
  • Test-transaction check: confirm the test transaction completed every stage without unresolved errors.
  • Professional-review check: confirm every required reviewer's comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Customer Payment Was Deposited Into a Personal Account

Probable cause: the business operating account was not yet open when the payment was received, or an owner used a personal account as a convenience.
Confirmation: compare the deposit record against the approved bank-account log and the customer's payment record.
Correction: transfer the full amount to the correct business account immediately, document the transfer, and notify the CPA and bookkeeper of the correction for tax and reporting purposes.
Reinspection: repeat the fund-separation check on the corrected records.

Transactions Cannot Be Matched to a Project or Cost Code

Probable cause: the receipt-capture procedure was not followed at the point of purchase, or the chart of accounts was not fully aligned with the Book 24 cost codes before transactions began.
Confirmation: review a sample of recent transactions against the receipt-capture log and chart-of-accounts worksheet.
Correction: research and recode the unmatched transactions with the bookkeeper, and retrain the responsible person on the receipt-capture procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the record check on the following month's transactions.

A Subcontractor Invoice Was Paid Without Required Documentation

Probable cause: the subcontractor tax-document, insurance, and lien-release requirements from Section G were not enforced before payment approval.
Confirmation: review the subcontractor's file against the required-document list on the Financial-Control Checklist.
Correction: obtain the missing documents before any further payment, and review payment-approval authority with the responsible approver.
Reinspection: repeat the payroll and subcontractor check on the next subcontractor payment cycle.

Monthly Reconciliation Is Consistently Late or Incomplete

Probable cause: reconciliation was not assigned to a specific responsible person with a specific due date, or transaction volume exceeds the current bookkeeping capacity.
Confirmation: review the reconciliation log for the last three months and compare completion dates against the assigned schedule.
Correction: reassign responsibility, adjust the schedule, or add bookkeeping capacity with CPA guidance.
Reinspection: repeat the reconciliation check for the next two reconciliation cycles.

The System Was Approved Without Professional Review

Probable cause: the CPA, bookkeeper, bank officer, or payroll provider was not identified early enough, or the review step was skipped to meet an internal deadline.
Confirmation: check the approval record for named reviewers, review dates, and recorded comments.
Correction: suspend reliance on the system for customer deposits, payroll, or subcontractor payments until the required reviewers' comments are obtained and addressed.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before re-approving the checklist.

A Vendor Payment Was Sent to a Changed Account Without Verification

Probable cause: a vendor payment-instruction change was accepted by email or phone without independent verification through a known contact.
Confirmation: review the fraud-control log for the transaction and confirm whether the verification step in Section E was completed.
Correction: contact the bank immediately to attempt recovery, notify the CPA and insurer, and document the incident under the fraud-control procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the fraud-control check on the next vendor payment-instruction change.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a coordinated, purchase-ready framing lumber takeoff for a residential structure. The finished work product is one framing takeoff entered into the Chapter 1 workbook's framing cost-code section, organized by species, grade, treatment, engineered product, size, length, location, and function, and reconciled against wall length, floor area, roof area, opening count, and member counts drawn from the approved plan set.

You will identify species, grade, treatment, and engineered-product requirements; separate sill, floor, wall, ceiling, roof, stair, porch, deck, and miscellaneous framing; count studs, plates, headers, beams, joists, rafters, and trusses from the plans, schedules, sections, and details; convert net lengths into available stock lengths; apply waste correctly by member type; create a companion connector and fastener list; estimate labor; price the takeoff from dated quotations; and complete an independent reconciliation review.

This project depends on the workbook built in Chapter 1 and the scope and clarification work completed in Chapter 2. The framing quantities produced here become the structural basis for Chapter 6's sheathing and panel takeoff, and they feed directly into Chapter 17's labor estimate and Chapter 18's pricing.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-025 — Count studs by wall length, spacing, height, type, corner, intersection, and opening condition.
  • ET-026 — Calculate plates by wall length, layer, lap, intersection, and stock length.
  • ET-027 — Estimate joists and rafters by layout, span, spacing, bearing, cantilever, and roof geometry.
  • ET-028 — Count headers and beams by opening, span, material, ply, and size from structural schedules.
  • ET-029 — Estimate blocking, backing, and miscellaneous framing support from documented conditions.
  • ET-030 — Prepare a framing lumber list that is coordinated, reconciled, and ready for supplier quotation.

Where This Skill Fits

The framing takeoff is the first structural material quantity entered into the workbook after sitework and concrete. It establishes the wall length, floor area, roof area, and opening data that Chapter 6 reuses to calculate sheathing, and it establishes the member counts that Chapter 7 and Chapter 8 reference when coordinating roofing and envelope details around structural framing. An error in stud count, header size, or truss quantity here does not stay contained in this chapter; it propagates into every later chapter that reconciles against wall length, floor area, or roof geometry.

The reconciliation discipline introduced in Chapter 1 and used in Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 is applied here to wall length, floor area, roof area, and member counts rather than to volume, and the same independent-review requirement applies before the framing list is priced in Chapter 18.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing architectural, structural, framing, section, schedule, detail, and truss documents to identify species, grade, treatment, and engineered-product requirements
  • counting studs, plates, headers, beams, posts, columns, joists, rafters, ridge and valley framing, stair framing, and blocking from the approved plan set
  • converting net lengths into available stock lengths and applying member-specific waste
  • creating a companion connector and fastener list
  • estimating framing labor by floor, wall, roof, stair, and miscellaneous task
  • pricing the takeoff from dated lumber and engineered-product quotations
  • reconciling wall length, floor area, roof area, openings, and member counts

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a beam, header, or engineered-member size that is not shown on an approved structural schedule or detail; an unassigned structural size must be converted to an RFI, not assumed
  • apply a single generic waste percentage to engineered members priced by exact length
  • substitute an unspecified species, grade, or treatment for the one identified in the structural notes or specifications
  • issue the framing list as a final purchase order without pricing, quotation review, and independent reconciliation

Any wall, opening, or roof plane whose framing cannot be identified from the approved documents must be logged as a clarification and excluded from the priced takeoff until resolved.

Trade Note

A framing takeoff is only as reliable as the wall-length and floor-area reconciliation behind it. Counting studs directly off a plan without first confirming total wall length against the same plan's dimensions is how a shortfall on delivery day gets discovered instead of caught at the desk.

Framing Standard

Do not price any header, beam, post, or engineered member whose size is not shown on an approved structural schedule or detail. A missing structural size is a clarification, not an assumption, and must be logged and resolved before the framing list is priced in Chapter 18.

Shop Tip

Reconcile wall length and floor area before counting a single stud. A takeoff built directly from a stud-by-stud count with no independent check against total wall length is how a missed wall line disappears into the final purchase order.

Common Field Fundamentals

The framing takeoff converts wall lines, floor plans, roof planes, and structural schedules into a purchase-ready member list. Every count in this chapter must reconcile against an independent measurement of wall length, floor area, roof area, or opening count taken from the same approved documents. A stud count that cannot be checked against total wall length, or a rafter count that cannot be checked against roof area, is an unverified number and must not be priced.

Species, Grade, Treatment, and Engineered Products

Identify species, grade, treatment, moisture condition, and engineered-product requirements from the structural notes and specifications before counting a single member. Separate ground-contact, exterior, fire-retardant, appearance, and standard lumber, and do not substitute one for another without an approved clarification. Engineered members are sized from the manufacturer's design table identified in the structural documents, not from a generic span chart.

Traceability Cue — Reconcile Before You Count

Calculate total wall length, floor area, and roof area from the approved plan dimensions before counting a single stud, joist, or rafter. Use that total as the independent check against your member counts in Section G. A takeoff without this independent basis cannot be reconciled and must not be priced.

Critical Control Cue — Do Not Assume an Unassigned Structural Size

Every header, beam, post, column, and engineered member must trace to an approved structural schedule or detail. If a structural size is not shown, do not select one. Log the condition as a clarification per the Chapter 2 process and exclude the item from the priced takeoff until the structural documents are revised or a qualified response is received.

Manufacturer Cue — Engineered Members Use Exact-Length Pricing

Do not apply a generic dimensional-lumber waste percentage to I-joists, floor trusses, LVL, PSL, LSL, or glulam members priced by exact length. Confirm the manufacturer's or supplier's minimum length increment, cutting policy, and any fabrication charge before finalizing engineered-member quantities.

Documentation Cue — Log Stock-Length Assumptions

Record the stock-length assumption used for every dimensional-lumber conversion, including the source confirming that length is available from the intended supplier. An assumed stock length that is not actually stocked becomes a delivery delay or a field cut that was not priced.

Company Policy Alert — Waste Percentages Vary by Member and Company

Confirm the company's approved waste percentage for each member type — dimensional lumber, sheet blocking stock, and engineered members — before applying a value. Do not carry forward a waste percentage from a different company or a different project type without verification.

  • Engineered products: I-joists, floor trusses, LVL, PSL, LSL, glulam, rim boards, squash blocks, stiffeners, and bridging sized from the manufacturer's design table identified in the structural documents; priced by exact length without generic waste.

E. Document Review and Member Identification Procedure

  1. Review architectural, structural, framing, section, schedule, detail, and truss documents. Confirm revision status against the Chapter 2 document register before beginning.
  2. Identify species, grade, treatment, moisture condition, engineered products, stock lengths, and connector requirements. Record each on the framing takeoff worksheet with its source.
  3. Separate sill, floor, wall, ceiling, roof, stair, porch, deck, and miscellaneous framing. Create a labeled section in the workbook for each.
  4. Measure sill plates and separate treated material, seal, anchors, and splices. Record sill length by wall line and treatment type.
  5. Count exterior and interior studs by wall length, spacing, height, and type. Use the approved on-center spacing from the structural notes.
  6. Add studs for corners, intersections, backing, wall ends, and approved details. Use the framing detail identified in the structural documents for each condition.
  7. Count king, trimmer, cripple, jamb, and specialty studs at openings. Cross-reference the opening schedule for each opening's width and height.
  8. Calculate bottom and top plates by wall length, layer, lap, intersection, and stock length. Record the number of plate layers specified for the wall type.
  9. Identify tall, rake, pony, furred, double, and specialty walls. Flag each for its own stud and plate calculation rather than the standard wall formula.
  10. Count headers by opening, span, material, ply, and size from the structural schedule. Do not assign a header size that is not shown on the schedule; log a clarification instead.
  11. Count beams, girders, posts, columns, and engineered members from schedules. Record species or product, size, ply, and bearing condition for each.
  12. Measure joists by layout, span, spacing, bearing, cantilever, rim, and stock length. Confirm spacing against the structural floor framing plan.
  13. Count I-joists, floor trusses, LVL, PSL, LSL, glulam, rim boards, squash blocks, stiffeners, and bridging. Use the manufacturer's design table identified in the structural documents.
  14. Count stair stringers, landings, openings, and supports. Use approved stair details only; do not calculate stringer geometry without a detail.
  15. Measure ceiling joists, ties, backing, and attic platforms. Record spacing and span from the ceiling framing plan.
  16. Count rafters by plane, spacing, span, tail, ridge, valley, hip, jack, and overhang. Separate each roof plane by slope and framing type.
  17. Count ridge, valley, hip, outlookers, subfascia, ladder framing, and roof blocking. Record length and quantity for each roof-plane intersection.
  18. Identify trusses by mark, quantity, span, profile, pitch, heel, and overhang. Use the truss schedule exactly as issued; do not substitute a field-calculated truss count.
  19. Measure fire blocking, draft stopping, nailers, cabinet backing, rail backing, and miscellaneous support. Record location and length for each from the finish and structural notes.

F. Material Separation, Stock Optimization, and Waste Procedure

  1. Separate ground-contact, exterior, fire-retardant, appearance, and standard lumber. Confirm each assignment against the structural notes and specifications.
  2. Convert net lengths into available stock lengths and optimize cuts. Use the approved stock-length reference; record the source confirming availability.
  3. Apply waste by stock length, defects, culling, damage, and framing complexity. Use the member-specific waste rate from Section C for each category.
  4. Do not apply generic waste to engineered members priced by exact length. Price these members from exact length plus the manufacturer's minimum-length or fabrication policy.
  5. Create a companion list for hangers, straps, clips, anchors, bolts, screws, nails, and adhesive. Cross-reference each connector to the structural detail specifying its use.
  6. Include delivery, unloading, staging, protection, field treatment, and disposal. Record each as a separate line item tied to the framing phase.

G. Labor, Pricing, and Reconciliation Procedure

  1. Estimate labor by floor, wall, roof, stair, and miscellaneous tasks. Use the workbook's labor columns and the production-rate basis available at this stage.
  2. Adjust production for height, complexity, crane use, weather, and repetition. Record the adjustment basis for each affected task.
  3. Price lumber and engineered products from dated quotations. Record supplier, quote number, date, expiration, freight, lead time, and minimum order for each.
  4. Reconcile wall length, floor area, roof area, openings, and member counts. Compare the independently calculated totals from Section E's introductory review against the completed member counts.
  5. Prepare the framing list by material, size, length, quantity, area, and phase. Organize the final list for direct use in a supplier quotation request.
  6. Complete independent review of openings, beams, engineered products, blocking, and lead times. Have a second qualified reviewer verify the highest-value and highest-risk items before the list is released for pricing in Chapter 18.

Common Framing Takeoff Inspection

Inspect the finished framing takeoff using the same checks regardless of which framing section is being reviewed.

  • Reconciliation check: confirm stud, plate, and joist counts reconcile against the independently calculated wall length and floor area.
  • Structural-size check: confirm every header, beam, post, column, and engineered member traces to an approved structural schedule or detail.
  • Truss check: confirm truss quantities, marks, and spans match the truss schedule exactly with no field substitution.
  • Waste check: confirm dimensional lumber and engineered members carry separate, member-specific waste treatment.
  • Connector check: confirm the companion connector and fastener list is cross-referenced to the framing details requiring each connector.
  • Documentation check: confirm every count carries a recorded source and the workbook revision log reflects this chapter's entry.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Stud Count Does Not Reconcile With Calculated Wall Length

Probable cause: a wall line was omitted from the count, an incorrect on-center spacing was applied, or the wall-length calculation used a different revision than the stud count.
Confirmation: compare the wall-by-wall length list used for the count against the independently calculated total wall length, and confirm both used the same document revision.
Correction: identify the specific wall line causing the variance, correct the spacing or omission, and recalculate the affected section.
Reinspection: repeat the wall-length reconciliation and confirm the totals match within the workbook's stated tolerance.

A Header or Beam Size Was Assumed Instead of Taken From the Schedule

Probable cause: the structural schedule did not clearly assign a size to the opening, and a size was selected without confirmation.
Confirmation: locate the opening on the structural schedule and confirm whether a size is actually assigned.
Correction: remove the assumed size, log the condition as a clarification per the Chapter 2 process, and exclude the item from the priced takeoff until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the structural-size check across all headers and beams before the list is released for pricing.

Engineered Members Were Priced With Generic Dimensional-Lumber Waste

Probable cause: the engineered-member rows were built from the same waste formula as dimensional lumber instead of exact-length pricing.
Confirmation: review the waste column for I-joist, LVL, PSL, LSL, and glulam rows and compare against the Section C material-category rule.
Correction: remove the generic waste percentage and reprice from exact length plus the manufacturer's minimum-length or fabrication policy.
Reinspection: repeat the waste check on all engineered-member rows.

Truss Quantity Does Not Match the Truss Schedule

Probable cause: a truss mark was double-counted, omitted, or the roof framing plan was read instead of the truss schedule.
Confirmation: compare the takeoff's truss list mark-by-mark against the truss schedule's quantity column.
Correction: correct the takeoff to match the truss schedule exactly; do not substitute a field-calculated count.
Reinspection: repeat the mark-by-mark comparison after correction.

A Connector or Fastener Has No Corresponding Framing Detail

Probable cause: the connector was added from a general assumption rather than a specific structural detail.
Confirmation: locate the structural detail specifying the connection and confirm the connector type, size, and quantity per joint.
Correction: correct the connector entry to match the detail, or remove it and log a clarification if no detail exists.
Reinspection: repeat the connector check across the full companion list.

An Assumed Stock Length Is Not Actually Available From the Intended Supplier

Probable cause: the stock-length conversion used a length from a general reference rather than a confirmed supplier stock list.
Confirmation: check the assumed stock length against the current supplier stock list or quotation.
Correction: revise the stock-length assumption to an available length and recalculate order quantity and waste for the affected rows.
Reinspection: repeat the stock-length verification against the current quotation before final pricing.

Roof-Area Reconciliation Fails After Rafter or Truss Entry

Probable cause: a roof plane was measured using footprint area instead of actual slope area, or a dormer or valley plane was omitted.
Confirmation: compare the roof-plane area list used for the rafter or truss count against the independently calculated slope area for each plane.
Correction: recalculate the affected plane using actual slope area and add any omitted dormer, valley, or irregular plane.
Reinspection: repeat the roof-area reconciliation and confirm the totals match within the workbook's stated tolerance.

Cleanup

This project ends with a reconciled, priced, and independently reviewed framing list entered into the workbook, not an in-progress count.

  • Confirm every structural size traces to an approved schedule or detail with no unresolved assumptions
  • Confirm wall length, floor area, roof area, and opening reconciliation checks pass within the workbook's stated tolerance
  • Confirm the companion connector and fastener list is complete and cross-referenced
  • Log the framing takeoff entry in the workbook's revision log with date, person, reason, and approval status

Field Terms

King Stud
A full-height stud at the side of an opening supporting the trimmer and header.
Trimmer Stud
A stud running from the sole plate to the underside of a header, supporting the header's bearing load.
Cripple Stud
A short stud above or below an opening, filling the space between the header or sill and the plate.
Engineered Member
A manufactured structural component such as an I-joist, LVL, PSL, LSL, glulam, or floor truss, sized from a manufacturer's design table.
Stock Length
The actual purchase length available from the supplier for a given species, grade, and product.
Member-Specific Waste
A waste percentage applied separately by member type rather than one blanket rate for the entire framing package.
Bearing
The structural support condition where a joist, rafter, or beam transfers load to a wall, beam, or foundation.
Cantilever
A framed member extending beyond its bearing support with no support at the extended end.
Truss Mark
The identifying label assigned to each unique truss profile on the truss schedule.
Companion List
The connector and fastener schedule cross-referenced to the framing members and details requiring each item.

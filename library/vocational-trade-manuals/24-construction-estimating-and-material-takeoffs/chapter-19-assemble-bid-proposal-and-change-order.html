Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert an approved estimate into two commercial documents: a bid proposal that a client can sign, and a change-order form that documents a scope change after work is under contract. The finished work product is one proposal that reconciles line-for-line with the frozen estimate workbook from Chapter 18, and one sample change order built from a documented change already recorded in the estimating record.

You will confirm the approved estimate version, review the bid instructions, write the proposal header and project understanding, state scope in trade sequence, state alternates, allowances, and unit prices, list exclusions and assumptions, state expiration and schedule terms, summarize price without exposing confidential cost detail unless intended, reconcile the proposal against the workbook, complete required review, build a change-order form and one sample change order, and archive the issued package.

This is the nineteenth project in the book. It depends directly on the frozen selling price built in Chapter 18 and on the workbook structure built in Chapter 1. The proposal and change-order package you build here becomes the template used again inside the Chapter 20 capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-109 — Write the scope of work in logical trade or phase sequence, matching the approved estimate and documents.
  • ET-110 — List exclusions clearly and specifically, distinguishing them from assumptions and allowances.
  • ET-111 — Summarize costs by base, alternates, allowances, and tax without exposing confidential cost detail unless intended.
  • ET-112 — Prepare a bid proposal that reconciles with the workbook and meets the bid instructions' form, delivery, and attachment requirements.
  • ET-113 — Prepare change-order pricing that identifies the initiating condition, changed scope, quantity, cost, markup, and schedule effect.
  • ET-114 — Document assumptions and expiration dates for both the proposal and the change order.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 18 froze a selling price built from verified quantities, dated pricing, labor, indirect costs, escalation, contingency, and profit. That number has no commercial effect until it is issued as a proposal a client can act on. This project performs that conversion under controlled language, so the price, scope, exclusions, and assumptions that exist in the workbook are the same ones the client receives.

The change-order form built in this project is the same form used for every future scope change on the awarded project, and the reconciliation discipline used here — proposal against workbook, change order against the pending-change log — is the same discipline the Chapter 20 capstone applies at full project scale.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the approved estimate version, selling price, alternates, allowances, unit prices, assumptions, exclusions, tax, and validity before drafting begins
  • reviewing bid instructions for required form, recipient, deadline, delivery method, signatures, bonds, and addenda
  • writing the proposal header, project understanding, scope, alternates, allowances, unit prices, exclusions, assumptions, and terms
  • reconciling the proposal totals with the frozen workbook and completing required review
  • building a change-order form and preparing one sample change order from a documented change already in the estimating record
  • archiving the issued proposal, submission proof, and change-order records

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • change the scope, price, or quantities established in the frozen Chapter 18 workbook while drafting the proposal
  • issue a proposal or change order without completing the legal, management, insurance, or licensing review required by company policy
  • invent payment, deposit, retainage, invoicing, warranty, or closeout terms not supplied by approved company contract language
  • price the sample change order using assumed quantities, rates, or markups not supplied by the frozen workbook, a quotation, or company policy

Any proposal or change-order line that cannot be traced back to the frozen workbook or a documented change must be corrected before the package is issued.

Trade Note

A proposal is a commercial document, not a summary memo. Every number a client can read must trace back to a number the estimator already froze in Chapter 18. A proposal that states a different total, a softened exclusion, or an unstated assumption is not a clean copy of the estimate — it is a new, unreviewed commitment.

Common Field Fundamentals

A proposal is not a rewrite of the estimate; it is a controlled restatement of the same numbers in language a client can act on. A change order is not a new estimate; it is a narrow document tied to one specific, already-documented condition. Both documents fail their purpose the moment they say something the frozen workbook and the estimating record do not already support.

Scope Language and Confidential Cost Detail

Write scope in the sequence the work will be performed, not in the sequence the estimate happens to be organized. State materials, products, finishes, fixtures, equipment, and performance that define the price without restating internal unit costs, labor rates, or markup unless the recipient is intended to see that detail. A proposal that discloses internal cost breakdowns it was never meant to disclose is a document-control failure, not a transparency benefit.

Traceability Cue — Reconcile Every Proposal Figure

Every base price, alternate price, allowance amount, unit price, and tax figure appearing in the proposal must trace to the identical figure in the frozen Chapter 18 workbook. Do not round, adjust, or restate a number while drafting the proposal; if a number appears wrong, correct it in the workbook first and refreeze the estimate before continuing.

Critical Control Cue — Do Not Create Unintended Commitments

Review every sentence in the scope, alternates, allowances, and terms sections for language that could be read as a guarantee, a design decision, or an inclusion the estimate does not price. An unintended commitment in a signed proposal is a contractual obligation, not a drafting error that can be corrected later.

Documentation Cue — Keep Proof of Submission

Record the delivery method, recipient, date, and confirmation for every proposal issued, and store that proof with the issued document. A proposal with no submission record cannot be defended if its validity period or receipt date is later disputed.

Company Policy Alert — Contract Language Is Not Optional

Payment, deposit, retainage, invoicing, warranty, and closeout terms must come from approved company contract language, never from the estimator's own wording. Confirm the current approved language before drafting Section F, Step 12 of the work order below.

E. Preparation — Confirm the Approved Basis

  1. Confirm the approved estimate version, selling price, alternates, allowances, unit prices, assumptions, exclusions, tax, and validity. Verify the workbook version being used is the one management approved and frozen in Chapter 18.
  2. Review bid instructions for form, recipient, deadline, delivery, signatures, bonds, addenda, and attachments. Record each requirement on a proposal-compliance checklist.
  3. Create the proposal header. Include contractor, client, project, address, proposal number, date, plan date, revision, and validity.
  4. Write a concise project understanding matching the documents and estimate purpose. State the estimate class and purpose exactly as recorded on the project-information sheet.

F. Proposal Content Build Procedure

  1. Write scope in logical trade or phase sequence. Follow the cost-code order used in the frozen workbook unless the bid instructions require a different sequence.
  2. Include temporary work, protection, supervision, cleanup, testing, permits, and closeout only where priced. Do not state an inclusion the frozen workbook does not carry a cost for.
  3. Identify materials, products, finishes, fixtures, equipment, and performance defining the price. Use the same product identifications used in the workbook's quotation log.
  4. State owner-furnished and contractor-installed responsibilities. Match the responsibility assignments recorded during Chapter 2's scope review.
  5. State alternates with separate scope and price. Copy each alternate's scope description and price directly from the frozen workbook.
  6. State allowances with amount and included material, labor, tax, delivery, markup, and installation. State explicitly what each allowance excludes.
  7. State unit prices with unit, included work, measurement method, minimum, and validity. Copy the unit basis directly from the frozen workbook.
  8. List exclusions clearly and specifically. Write each exclusion as a standalone, testable statement rather than a general disclaimer.
  9. List assumptions for access, hours, utilities, conditions, dimensions, selections, approvals, and schedule. Copy each assumption from the Chapters 2 through 18 assumptions log.
  10. Identify unresolved clarifications and pricing treatment. State whether each unresolved item was converted to an allowance, unit price, exclusion, or contingency.
  11. State whether permits, fees, tax, bonds, insurance, testing, engineering, and utilities are included. Match the frozen workbook's indirect-cost sections exactly.
  12. State proposal expiration and material-price validity. Use the validity period recorded on the project-information sheet.
  13. State schedule assumptions, duration, lead times, and start dependencies without unsupported promises. Do not state a start or completion date the schedule basis does not support.
  14. State payment, deposit, retainage, invoicing, change-order, warranty, and closeout terms only through approved company contract language. Insert the approved language without independent drafting.
  15. Summarize base, alternates, allowances, tax, and total without exposing confidential cost unless intended. Present the summary in the format the bid instructions require.
  16. Reconcile proposal totals with the workbook. Compare every summary figure against the frozen workbook line by line.
  17. Check every proposal inclusion has cost and every significant estimate item has proposal treatment. Flag any workbook line with no corresponding proposal statement.
  18. Review language for ambiguity, contradiction, unauthorized design, and unintended guarantees. Mark any sentence requiring revision before continuing.

G. Review and Reconciliation

  1. Complete legal, management, insurance, and licensing review where required. Record reviewer name, date, and disposition for each required review.
  2. Prepare addenda acknowledgment and required attachments. Confirm every attachment listed in the bid instructions is included.
  3. Confirm the proposal-compliance checklist from Section E, Step 2 is fully satisfied. Correct any unmet requirement before continuing.

H. Change-Order Form and Sample Verification

  1. Create a change-order form with project, contract, change number, date, initiating party, reference, and description. Use the company change-order template where one exists.
  2. Define original scope, changed scope, reason, and responsibility without unsupported fault. State the initiating condition factually and without assigning blame not supported by the documented condition.
  3. Attach quantity, material, labor, equipment, subcontract, delay, and schedule calculations. Build these from the same formula basis established in Chapters 1 and 17–18.
  4. Apply approved overhead, profit, tax, bond, and insurance markup. Use the same rates approved in Chapter 18 unless company policy specifies a different change-order markup.
  5. Identify credits, restocking, cancellation, remobilization, and work already performed. State each explicitly where the documented change involves any of them.
  6. State added or deducted amount and time effect separately. Do not combine cost and schedule effect into a single undifferentiated statement.
  7. State assumptions, exclusions, validity, and required authorization. Match the same discipline used in the bid proposal.
  8. Create a pending-change log and prevent duplicate pricing. Confirm the sample change is not already priced elsewhere in the estimating record.
  9. Prepare one sample change order from a documented change. Use an actual condition already recorded in the estimating record, not a hypothetical.
  10. Review proposal and change order against the audit trail. Confirm both documents match the frozen workbook, the documented change, and all prior review dispositions.

I. Closeout — Authorization and Archive

  1. Verify delivery instructions, recipient, deadline, format, and authorization. Confirm the proposal is signed by the authorized company representative before issue.
  2. Issue the proposal and save proof of submission. Record delivery method, date, recipient, and confirmation.
  3. Obtain authorization and archive the issued package with the estimate and quotations. Store the proposal, the change-order form, the sample change order, and all review records in the estimating record.

Common Proposal and Change-Order Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which section is being reviewed.

  • Reconciliation check: confirm every base, alternate, allowance, unit-price, tax, and total figure in the proposal matches the frozen Chapter 18 workbook.
  • Completeness check: confirm every significant workbook line has a corresponding proposal statement and every proposal inclusion has a priced workbook basis.
  • Language check: confirm scope, exclusion, and assumption language is specific and testable, with no unresolved ambiguous phrases.
  • Compliance check: confirm the proposal's form, attachments, and delivery method satisfy the bid instructions.
  • Review check: confirm required legal, management, insurance, and licensing review is complete and recorded.
  • Change-order check: confirm the sample change order is built from an actual documented condition and is not duplicated in the pending-change log.

Complete the Proposal and Change-Order Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 20.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Proposal Total Does Not Match the Frozen Workbook

Probable cause: a figure was manually re-typed into the proposal instead of copied from the workbook, or the workbook was revised after the proposal draft began.
Confirmation: compare each base, alternate, allowance, unit-price, tax, and total figure in the proposal against the identical line in the frozen workbook.
Correction: replace the incorrect proposal figure with the frozen workbook figure, or if the workbook itself changed, confirm which version is approved and re-freeze before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the full reconciliation check in Section G before proceeding to review.

A Workbook Line Has No Corresponding Proposal Statement

Probable cause: the scope narrative was drafted from memory or a template rather than directly against the cost-code sections.
Confirmation: walk the frozen workbook cost-code section by cost-code section and confirm each has a matching scope, alternate, allowance, or exclusion statement in the proposal.
Correction: add the missing statement using the same identification used in the workbook and quotation log.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check across the full workbook and proposal.

Proposal Language Creates an Unintended Guarantee

Probable cause: a schedule, performance, or design statement was written more strongly than the estimate basis supports.
Confirmation: compare the statement against the documented schedule, performance, or design basis in the frozen workbook and estimating record.
Correction: revise the statement to match only what the estimate basis actually supports, or convert it to a stated assumption.
Reinspection: repeat the language check across the full scope, terms, and assumptions sections.

The Proposal Omits a Bid-Instructions Requirement

Probable cause: the proposal-compliance checklist from Section E, Step 2 was not fully built or was not checked before drafting.
Confirmation: compare the finished proposal and its attachments against the original bid instructions line by line.
Correction: add the missing form, signature, bond, or attachment before issue.
Reinspection: repeat the compliance check in Section G before issuing the proposal.

A Change Order Is Priced Twice

Probable cause: the sample change was not checked against the pending-change log before pricing began.
Confirmation: search the pending-change log for the same initiating condition or reference document.
Correction: remove the duplicate pricing and consolidate the change into a single authorized change-order entry.
Reinspection: repeat the pending-change log check before finalizing the sample change order.

The Change Order Assigns Fault Not Supported by the Documented Condition

Probable cause: the description was drafted from assumption rather than from the actual document, field report, or RFI response supporting the change.
Confirmation: compare the change-order description against the specific document reference cited as the initiating condition.
Correction: revise the description to state only what the referenced document supports, and remove any unsupported responsibility statement.
Reinspection: repeat the change-order review in Section H, Step 10.

The Proposal Was Issued Without Required Review

Probable cause: Section G, Step 1 was skipped or the review disposition was not recorded before delivery.
Confirmation: check the estimating record for a recorded reviewer name, date, and disposition for each required review type.
Correction: if the proposal has not yet been delivered, complete the missing review before issue; if already delivered, notify management immediately and follow company correction procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the review check for every future proposal before delivery.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert a reconciled residential takeoff and labor estimate into a current, risk-adjusted selling price. The finished work product is one completed pricing summary within the Chapter 1 workbook containing a frozen takeoff reference, a pricing log with dated and sourced quotations, verified material extensions with material-specific waste, freight and tax treatment, priced labor and equipment, leveled subcontractor proposals, jobsite general conditions, a risk register with resolved risk responses, calculated overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit, and a final total selling price with management approval.

You will freeze the approved takeoff, request and record quotations for high-value and long-lead materials, apply unit pricing without duplicating waste, add freight and tax, price labor and equipment, enter leveled subcontractor proposals, calculate general conditions and overhead, build and resolve a risk register, calculate contingency and profit without double-counting risk, run sensitivity scenarios, compare totals with historical benchmarks, and obtain management approval for the final price.

This project uses the completed Chapter 3 through Chapter 16 takeoffs and the Chapter 17 labor estimate already entered in your Chapter 1 workbook. It produces the priced estimate that Chapter 19 turns into a bid proposal and that Chapter 20 assembles into the capstone estimate.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-103 — Apply unit pricing to net or order quantity according to the workbook design, from dated and sourced quotations.
  • ET-104 — Calculate waste factors and confirm each is not duplicated in unit cost or elsewhere.
  • ET-105 — Add delivery and handling, including freight, unloading, staging, hoisting, and current tax treatment.
  • ET-106 — Calculate overhead on the approved company basis without duplicating it in labor burden, equipment, subcontract markup, or general conditions.
  • ET-107 — Calculate profit margin using approved company policy and verify markup versus margin.
  • ET-108 — Add contingency allowances on the approved basis without double-counting risk already carried in waste, labor, allowance, or markup.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 3 through 16 produced net and order quantities. Chapter 17 produced labor hours and burdened labor cost. This project is the point where those quantities and hours become dollars: unit prices, waste-adjusted material cost, freight, tax, leveled subcontractor cost, general conditions, overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit are all calculated here, in this order, on this frozen takeoff.

The pricing summary built in this project becomes the source for the Chapter 19 bid proposal, which must reconcile line-for-line with the total produced here. Chapter 20 repeats this exact pricing procedure against the capstone project's complete takeoff.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • freezing the approved takeoff version and setting the pricing date, market, currency, and bid-validity target
  • requesting, recording, and verifying quotations for high-value, volatile, long-lead, imported, custom, or minimum-order materials
  • applying unit pricing to the correct quantity basis without duplicating waste
  • adding freight, handling, and current tax treatment
  • pricing labor, equipment, and leveled subcontractor proposals
  • calculating jobsite general conditions, company overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit
  • building and resolving a risk register and running sensitivity scenarios
  • preparing a pricing summary and obtaining management approval

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent unit prices, freight charges, tax rates, overhead percentages, profit targets, escalation, or contingency percentages that are not supplied by a dated quotation, current tax guidance, or company policy
  • select the lowest subcontractor proposal before scope gaps and qualifications are resolved
  • issue a client-facing proposal from this pricing summary; that document is prepared separately in Chapter 19
  • use contingency to conceal known scope or double-count risk already carried in waste, labor, allowance, or markup

Any pricing line that lacks a dated, sourced quotation or an approved company basis must be labeled unpriced and flagged for resolution before the pricing summary is issued.

Trade Note

Every price entered in this project must carry a date and a source, exactly like every quantity entered in Chapter 1 through Chapter 16. A price with no recorded date will look correct on the day it is entered and become wrong, without warning, the moment the market moves.

Pricing Standard

Do not calculate overhead, profit, escalation, or contingency until every direct cost line has been priced and the risk register has been built. A markup applied before the underlying cost is settled will be recalculated on a moving base and will not match the frozen takeoff when Chapter 19 issues the proposal.

Shop Tip

Enter the quotation expiration date next to every unit price the day the quotation is recorded. A price that looked current when the takeoff started can expire before the pricing summary is finished, and a missed expiration is one of the most common causes of a proposal issued at a stale price.

Common Field Fundamentals

Pricing follows the same order on every project: freeze the takeoff, price materials with waste and freight and tax, price labor and equipment, enter leveled subcontractor cost, calculate general conditions, resolve the risk register, then apply overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit in that sequence. Reversing this order, or applying markups before the underlying cost is settled, produces a total that will not reconcile with the frozen takeoff.

Waste, Freight, and Tax Are Priced Separately

The workbook built in Chapter 1 keeps waste, freight, and tax in their own columns rather than buried inside unit cost. Confirm this structure is intact before pricing begins. If a supplier quotation already includes freight or a waste allowance inside its unit price, record that fact in the quotation qualification field and do not add a second freight or waste charge on top of it.

Traceability Cue — Date and Source Every Price

Record the supplier or subcontractor, quote number, date, expiration, freight, lead time, minimum order, and qualifications the moment a price is entered. A price without a recorded date and source cannot be defended in a later review and must be treated as unverified until that information is added.

Critical Control Cue — Do Not Duplicate Waste, Overhead, or Contingency

Confirm each waste factor exists in exactly one place before applying a unit price. Confirm overhead is not already embedded in labor burden, equipment rate, subcontract markup, or general conditions before calculating company overhead. Confirm contingency is not covering a risk already priced through waste, an allowance, or a unit price. A duplicated markup silently inflates the total selling price without producing any visible error.

Documentation Cue — Maintain the Pricing Log

Record every quotation and qualification in the pricing log before its price is used in a cost-code section. Update the log whenever a quotation is revised, expires, or is replaced, and record the reason for the change.

Company Policy Alert — Confirm the Overhead, Profit, and Contingency Basis

Company overhead, profit, and contingency calculation bases vary. Confirm whether each is applied to direct cost, a subtotal, or another defined base, and confirm the basis with a supervisor or company policy document before calculating. Do not assume the basis used in a prior project applies here without verification.

C. Pricing Sequence Rule

  • Fixed order: price materials, labor, equipment, and subcontractors before calculating general conditions; calculate general conditions before company overhead; resolve the risk register before calculating contingency; calculate overhead and contingency before profit.
  • No skipped freeze: do not price any line until the takeoff version has been frozen and recorded in Section E, Step 1.
  • No premature markup: do not calculate overhead, escalation, contingency, or profit on a direct-cost total that still contains unresolved or unpriced lines.
  • Non-duplication rule: confirm waste, overhead, and risk are each priced in exactly one place before the pricing summary is finalized.

E. Preparation — Freeze the Takeoff and Build the Pricing Log

  1. Freeze the approved takeoff version. Confirm quantities, units, sources, and scope classifications match the Chapter 3 through 16 reconciled takeoffs and the Chapter 17 labor estimate. Record the frozen version number on the pricing summary sheet.
  2. Set the pricing date, market, currency, escalation period, and bid-validity target. Record each on the pricing summary sheet before requesting any quotation.
  3. Identify high-value, volatile, long-lead, imported, custom, or minimum-order materials requiring quotations. List each on the pricing log before requesting pricing.
  4. Request pricing with exact product, grade, size, quantity, delivery, unloading, tax, lead time, and expiration. Send a written request identifying the exact specification for each listed material.
  5. Record every quotation and qualification in the pricing log. Include supplier, quote number, date, expiration, freight, lead time, minimum order, and qualifications for each.
  6. Verify quoted products meet the documents and approved alternates. Compare each quotation's product description against the specification before using its price.

F. Pricing Procedure — Materials, Labor, Equipment, Subcontractors, and General Conditions

  1. Apply unit pricing to net or order quantity according to the workbook design. Use the order-quantity formula built in Chapter 1 for material extension.
  2. Review each waste factor and confirm it is not duplicated. Check whether the quoted unit price already includes a waste, breakage, or attic-stock allowance before applying the workbook's separate waste column.
  3. Convert quantities into supplier units and round only at the purchase-unit stage. Include cartons, bundles, rolls, pallets, minimum orders, and attic stock in the conversion.
  4. Add freight, fuel, delivery, unloading, staging, hoisting, handling, and return charges. Enter each as its own line item sourced from the quotation.
  5. Add tax according to current qualified guidance and project status. Identify differing tax treatment for owner purchases, resale, subcontract, rental, freight, and labor before applying a single rate to all categories.
  6. Price labor using approved burdened rates and hours from Chapter 17. Confirm hours match the Chapter 17 labor estimate before pricing.
  7. Price equipment with rental or ownership rate, mobilization, fuel, attachments, operator, standby, and minimum rental period. Enter each component as its own line.
  8. Enter qualified subcontractor proposals after scope leveling. Confirm each proposal has been leveled to the same scope as every competing proposal before entering its price.
  9. Add permits, testing, engineering, survey, bonds, insurance, dumpsters, sanitation, temporary utilities, and direct costs. Source each from a quotation, fee schedule, or company policy.
  10. Calculate jobsite general conditions by duration, fixed cost, quantity, or the approved method. Label the specific method used per the Section D schedule.
  11. Review general conditions against schedule and staffing. Confirm the duration or quantity basis matches the project's anticipated schedule.

G. Risk Register, Overhead, Escalation, Contingency, and Profit

  1. Create a risk register for scope, design, quantity, site, productivity, schedule, market, and subcontractors. List each identified risk with a description and affected estimate line.
  2. Assign each risk a response: clarify, exclude, allow, alternate, unit price, contingency, transfer, or accept. Record the assigned response and the reasoning for each risk.
  3. Calculate company overhead on the approved basis. Use the Section D basis schedule and label the application base.
  4. Confirm overhead is not duplicated in labor burden, equipment, subcontract markup, or general conditions. Review each of those categories for an embedded overhead component before finalizing the overhead line.
  5. Identify material escalation between pricing, procurement, and installation. Apply escalation only to the affected costs and periods, using documented market data.
  6. Calculate contingency on the approved basis without double-counting known scope. Confirm each contingency dollar corresponds to a risk-register entry assigned the contingency response, not to a risk already priced through waste, an allowance, or a unit price. Separate internal contingency from client allowance where company policy requires it.
  7. Calculate profit using approved company policy and verify markup versus margin. Confirm which calculation type is required and apply it to the correct base.

H. Review, Sensitivity, and Approval

  1. Run sensitivity scenarios for commodities, productivity, schedule, and unresolved quantities. Record the resulting price range for each scenario on the pricing summary sheet.
  2. Compare totals with relevant historical benchmarks and investigate variance. Treat the benchmark as a secondary check; do not force the estimate to match history without investigating the cause of any variance.
  3. Review cost and selling price by trade, area, unit, phase, and benchmark. Confirm each subtotal is reasonable against the frozen takeoff.
  4. Prepare the pricing summary with direct, indirect, overhead, escalation, contingency, profit, tax, alternates, and total. Present each as its own labeled line.
  5. Document expiration, assumptions, exclusions, and procurement risks. Record these on the pricing summary sheet alongside the total.
  6. Obtain management approval for overhead, profit, contingency, alternates, and final price. Record the approving person, date, and any conditions of approval before the summary is treated as final.

Common Pricing Inspection

Inspect the finished pricing summary using the same checks regardless of which trade or cost-code section is being reviewed.

  • Freeze check: confirm the pricing summary references the frozen takeoff version and no quantity has changed since the freeze.
  • Source and date check: confirm every price in the pricing log carries a supplier, date, and expiration.
  • Waste duplication check: confirm no waste factor is applied twice for the same material.
  • Tax treatment check: confirm tax treatment is verified separately for owner purchases, resale, subcontract, rental, freight, and labor rather than applied as a single rate.
  • Markup duplication check: confirm overhead is not embedded in labor burden, equipment, subcontract markup, or general conditions, and confirm contingency does not duplicate a priced risk.
  • Approval check: confirm overhead, profit, contingency, alternates, and final price carry a recorded management approval.

Complete the Pricing Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 19.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Total Selling Price Is Higher Than Expected With No Obvious Cause

Probable cause: a waste factor, overhead component, or contingency amount is duplicated somewhere in the pricing summary.
Confirmation: trace each material line for a waste factor applied twice, and trace labor, equipment, and subcontract lines for an embedded overhead component alongside the calculated overhead line.
Correction: remove the duplicated waste, overhead, or contingency amount and recalculate the affected subtotal.
Reinspection: repeat the markup-duplication check across every cost-code section.

A Quotation Has Expired Before the Pricing Summary Is Finalized

Probable cause: the pricing cycle extended past the quotation's recorded expiration date.
Confirmation: compare today's date against the expiration field in the pricing log for the affected line.
Correction: request an updated quotation, record the new date and expiration in the pricing log, and update the affected unit price.
Reinspection: repeat the source-and-date check across the full pricing log before finalizing.

Two Subcontractor Proposals Cannot Be Compared

Probable cause: the proposals were not leveled to the same scope before entry.
Confirmation: compare each proposal's included and excluded scope items against the approved documents.
Correction: request scope clarification from each subcontractor and rebuild both proposals to a common scope basis before entering either price.
Reinspection: repeat the leveling comparison before entering the selected proposal into the pricing summary.

Overhead or Profit Was Calculated Before the Risk Register Was Resolved

Probable cause: the pricing sequence in Section C was not followed, and markups were applied before every risk received an assigned response.
Confirmation: check the risk register for any risk without a recorded response, and compare the overhead and profit calculation date against the risk-register completion date.
Correction: resolve every outstanding risk-register entry, then recalculate overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit in the required order.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing-sequence check confirming the required order in Section C.

Contingency Appears to Cover a Risk Already Priced Elsewhere

Probable cause: the same risk was assigned both a unit price or allowance response and a contingency response in the risk register.
Confirmation: review the risk register for any risk with more than one assigned response, and trace the corresponding cost-code line for a matching allowance or unit-price entry.
Correction: assign the risk a single response and remove the duplicated contingency amount.
Reinspection: repeat the review of the risk register for single-response assignment.

The Pricing Summary Total Does Not Match the Frozen Takeoff Quantities

Probable cause: a quantity in a cost-code section was changed after the takeoff freeze in Section E, Step 1.
Confirmation: compare the current quantities in each cost-code section against the recorded frozen-takeoff version.
Correction: restore the frozen quantities, or formally document and log a takeoff revision with a stated reason before re-freezing.
Reinspection: repeat the freeze check across every priced cost-code section.

Tax Was Applied Using a Single Rate Across All Cost Categories

Probable cause: the estimator applied one assumed tax rate without checking differing treatment for owner purchases, resale, subcontract, rental, freight, and labor.
Confirmation: compare the applied tax rate against current qualified tax guidance for each cost category.
Correction: apply the category-specific tax treatment confirmed by current qualified guidance and recalculate affected totals.
Reinspection: repeat the tax-treatment check across all cost categories.

Field Terms

Direct Cost
Material, labor, equipment, and subcontract cost before general conditions, overhead, escalation, contingency, and profit.
General Conditions
Jobsite supervision and site-support cost calculated by duration, fixed cost, quantity, or approved method.
Overhead
Indirect company cost allocated to the project on the approved company basis.
Escalation
The documented change in material or labor price between the pricing date and the anticipated procurement or installation date.
Contingency
A calculated allowance for unresolved risk, applied on the approved basis without duplicating priced risk.
Markup
Profit calculated as a percentage of cost.
Margin
Profit calculated as a percentage of the final selling price.
Leveled Proposal
A subcontractor or supplier proposal adjusted so its scope matches every competing proposal before comparison.
Risk Register
The document tracking identified risks and their assigned response: clarify, exclude, allow, alternate, unit price, contingency, transfer, or accept.
Pricing Log
The document recording supplier, quote number, date, expiration, freight, lead time, minimum order, and qualifications for every quotation used in the estimate.

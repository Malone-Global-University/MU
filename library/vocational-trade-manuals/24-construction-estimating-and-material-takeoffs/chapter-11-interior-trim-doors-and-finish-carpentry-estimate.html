Job Overview

This project qualifies you to produce a coordinated finish-carpentry estimate for one residential project. The finished work product is one estimate covering interior doors, frames, jambs, casing, baseboard, crown and wall trim, closet shelving, stock-length optimization, fasteners and finishing materials, and labor, entered into the cost-code sections of the workbook built in Project 01.

You will reconcile the door schedule against the plans, separate door types and hardware groups, measure casing and baseboard by room, measure crown and wall trim by room and elevation, convert linear quantities into stock lengths with waste, calculate fasteners and finishing materials, coordinate scope boundaries with flooring, cabinets, painting, hardware, and electrical, price every component from dated quotations, reconcile door count and trim quantities against the schedules, and prepare the estimate by room, opening, profile, material, labor, and total.

This project follows Project 10, where wall and ceiling substrates were estimated, and precedes Project 12, where cabinets, countertops, and built-ins are estimated. Interior trim and finish carpentry cannot be measured accurately until wall, opening, and ceiling conditions are established, and cabinet coordination in the next project depends on the opening and trim boundaries set here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • ET-061 — Count interior doors by tag, type, and hardware group, reconciled against the door schedule.
  • ET-062 — Calculate casing quantities by opening perimeter, profile, and stock length.
  • ET-063 — Calculate baseboard quantities by room perimeter with the approved opening-deduction rule.
  • ET-064 — Estimate crown or wall trim by room and elevation, including built-up profiles.
  • ET-065 — Estimate fasteners, adhesive, and caulk for finish-carpentry installation.
  • ET-066 — Prepare a complete finish-carpentry estimate with reconciled quantities, labor, pricing, and documented scope boundaries.

Where This Skill Fits

The drywall estimate from Project 10 establishes finished wall and ceiling surfaces; this project measures every trim element applied to those surfaces along with every door opening cut into them. The door count and hardware groups established here are the same count Project 12 uses to coordinate cabinet, appliance, and built-in openings, and the same count Project 15 uses to verify electrical switch and device locations near door swings.

The stock-length optimization and material-specific waste discipline used here is the same discipline applied to flooring transitions in Project 13 and to paint sundries in Project 14. Errors in door count or trim perimeter carry directly into the labor estimate in Project 17 and the selling price in Project 18.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • counting interior doors by tag, type, swing, core, rating, frame, and hardware group, reconciled against the door schedule
  • measuring casing by opening perimeter, side, head, reveal, profile, and stock length
  • measuring baseboard, shoe, and quarter round by room perimeter with the approved opening-deduction rule
  • measuring crown, picture rail, chair rail, panel molding, wainscot cap, and wall trim by room and elevation
  • measuring closet shelving, rods, cleats, brackets, and supports where assigned
  • converting linear quantities into stock lengths with cut optimization and pattern matching
  • calculating fasteners, adhesive, biscuits, dowels, plugs, shims, filler, and caulk
  • coordinating flooring, cabinet, painting, hardware, and electrical scope boundaries
  • pricing doors, frames, trim, millwork, hardware, fasteners, finishing, and delivery from dated quotations
  • preparing the estimate by room, opening, profile, material, labor, finish, and total

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select door hardware, profiles, or finish grade on the owner's behalf; use only the schedule, specification, or approved selection provided
  • price field cutting of specialty stair components, custom millwork, or shop-fabricated items without a fabricator quotation covering that work
  • assume any stock length, profile, or species not confirmed by the specification, schedule, or a dated supplier quotation
  • issue this estimate as a client-facing proposal; that document is prepared separately in Project 19

Any door count, opening perimeter, or room perimeter that does not reconcile against the schedules must be resolved before pricing begins.

Trade Note

A door count that does not match the door schedule is the single most common source of a finish-carpentry estimate that is wrong at every downstream step — hardware, casing, and labor are all driven by the same count. Reconcile the count before measuring a single foot of casing.

Reconciliation Standard

Do not enter a unit cost for any door, casing, base, or trim line until the count or linear quantity for that line has been reconciled against the door schedule, room perimeter, or opening data. A quantity entered without reconciliation must be marked unverified.

Shop Tip

Build the room schedule and door count before opening the stock-length reference. Optimizing cuts against an unreconciled count only produces a precisely wrong order quantity.

Reconciling the Door Schedule

Compare every door shown on the floor plans against the door schedule before any hardware or casing quantity is calculated. Record the tag, size, swing, core, rating, frame, and hardware group for each door exactly as listed on the schedule. Where a plan shows a door not listed on the schedule, or the schedule lists a door not shown on the plan, treat the discrepancy as an unresolved clarification and do not resolve it by independent judgment.

Traceability Cue — Record the Schedule Source With Every Door Line

Enter the door schedule reference and revision date with every door line the moment the line is created. A door count without a recorded schedule reference cannot be defended in a later review and must be treated as unverified until the reference is added.

Critical Control Cue — Reconcile the Door Count Before Pricing Hardware

Do not price hardware groups, casing, or labor against a door count that has not been reconciled between the plans and the schedule. A miscount here is repeated in every hardware group, every casing opening, and every labor hour calculated from it.

Documentation Cue — Log Coordination Boundaries

Record every scope boundary agreed with flooring, cabinets, painting, hardware, and electrical in the trade-boundary log, including which trade supplies and which trade installs each shared component. Do not leave a shared boundary undocumented.

Manufacturer/Supplier Cue — Confirm Included Scope on Every Door Quotation

Confirm whether a door quotation includes frames, jambs, stops, hinges, locksets, closers, thresholds, seals, and factory painting before using the quoted price. A door price without a stated scope must not be entered into the workbook.

C. Material and Species Categories

  • Species and material separation: separate species, MDF, composite, PVC, finger-jointed, clear, stain-grade, and paint-grade materials into distinct lines; do not combine differing materials under one line item.
  • Stain-grade handling: apply pattern-matching and grain-continuity requirements only where the finish schedule specifies a stain-grade finish.
  • Specialty trim: identify built-up profiles requiring multiple components and record the assembly labor for each component separately from the material quantity.
  • Non-substitution rule: do not substitute a species, profile, or stock length not confirmed by the specification, schedule, or an approved clarification.

E. Document Review and Door Reconciliation

  1. Review plans, door schedule, finish schedule, elevations, sections, details, millwork drawings, and specifications. Record the revision date of each document used.
  2. Identify door type, size, swing, core, rating, frame, jamb, stop, casing, hardware group, finish, and responsibility for each door tag. Record these fields exactly as listed on the schedule.
  3. Count doors by tag and reconcile the schedule with the plans. Record any discrepancy as an unresolved clarification before continuing.
  4. Separate prehung, slab, pocket, bypass, bifold, barn, access, rated, and specialty doors. Assign each its own line so pricing and labor differences are not averaged together.
  5. Identify pairs, transoms, sidelights, louvers, glass, and special hardware. Record each as its own line item linked to the parent door tag.
  6. Confirm whether the quoted price includes frames, jambs, stops, hinges, locksets, closers, thresholds, seals, and painting. Record the confirmed scope on the quotation log for each door line.

F. Casing, Baseboard, and Wall-Trim Takeoff

  1. Measure casing by opening perimeter, side count, head count, reveal, profile, and stock length. Record the opening tag supporting each casing quantity.
  2. Include window casing, stools, aprons, jamb extensions, returns, rosettes, and specialty details. Record each as a distinct line linked to its opening.
  3. Measure baseboard by room perimeter and apply the approved opening-deduction rule. State the deduction rule used on the room schedule.
  4. Add corners, returns, transitions, and stock-length waste. Record the corner and return count separately from the linear footage.
  5. Measure shoe, quarter round, and cap, and flexible trim separately. Do not combine these with the baseboard line.
  6. Measure crown, picture rail, chair rail, panel molding, wainscot cap, and wall trim by room and elevation. Record the elevation reference for each measurement.
  7. Identify built-up profiles requiring multiple components and assembly labor. Record each component of a built-up profile as its own material line, linked under one assembly reference.

G. Shelving, Stair Finish, and Material Categorization

  1. Measure closet shelving, rods, cleats, brackets, and supports where assigned. Record the room and closet type for each shelving line.
  2. Count stair finish items only from approved details. Do not estimate stair stringers, treads, risers, rails, or balusters without a specific approved detail reference; treat missing stair details as an unresolved clarification.
  3. Separate species, MDF, composite, PVC, finger-jointed, clear, stain-grade, and paint-grade materials. Apply the material category rules in Section C.

H. Stock Optimization, Waste, and Consumables

  1. Convert linear quantities into stock lengths with cut optimization and pattern matching. Use the approved stock-length reference and record the assigned stock length for each line.
  2. Apply waste by profile, stock length, stain grade, room geometry, and cut complexity. Record the waste percentage and its basis on each line; do not apply one waste percentage to every profile.
  3. Calculate finish nails, screws, adhesive, biscuits, dowels, plugs, shims, filler, caulk, and touch-up. Base the quantity on linear footage, joint count, and manufacturer coverage.
  4. Calculate primer, paint, stain, clear coat, and shop finishing only when included in this estimate's scope. Record whether finishing is included here or excluded and coordinated with Project 14.

I. Coordination, Labor, and Pricing

  1. Include field measurement, templates, mockups, samples, and shop drawings where required. Record which items require field verification before ordering.
  2. Include delivery, protected storage, acclimation, sorting, culling, handling, and disposal. Record these as separate cost lines, not absorbed into material unit cost.
  3. Estimate labor by door, hardware preparation, casing, base, crown, wall trim, shelving, stair, sanding, and punch. Assign a separate labor line for each task category.
  4. Adjust production for occupancy, high ceilings, stain grade, irregular walls, specialty joints, and protection. Record the adjustment reason on each affected labor line.
  5. Coordinate flooring, cabinets, painting, hardware, and electrical boundaries. Record each boundary in the trade-boundary log with the responsible trade for supply and installation.
  6. Price doors, frames, trim, millwork, hardware, fasteners, finishing, and delivery from dated quotations. Record the quotation date, source, and expiration for every priced line.
  7. Identify long-lead custom doors, profiles, species, hardware, and factory finishes. Record the lead time and its effect on the schedule assumption.

J. Reconciliation, Documentation, and Review

  1. Reconcile door count, opening perimeter, room perimeter, and trim quantities. Compare the workbook totals against the door schedule and room schedule independently.
  2. Prepare the estimate by room, opening, profile, material, labor, finish, and total. Enter the completed estimate into the Project 01 workbook's cost-code sections.
  3. Document exclusions and unresolved opening, profile, hardware, finish, and responsibility questions. Record each in the assumptions, exclusions, and clarifications register linked to the affected line.
  4. Complete independent review of doors, stock lengths, specialty trim, and finish labor. Confirm the review is performed by someone other than the original estimator where company policy requires it.

Common Estimate Inspection

Inspect the finished estimate using the same checks regardless of which room, opening, or trim category is being reviewed.

  • Reconciliation check: confirm the door count matches the door schedule and every discrepancy is logged and resolved.
  • Formula check: confirm order-quantity, material-cost, labor-cost, and line-total formulas calculate correctly and match Section D.
  • Waste check: confirm waste is applied by profile, stock length, and stain grade rather than one blanket percentage.
  • Coordination check: confirm every shared boundary with flooring, cabinets, painting, hardware, and electrical is recorded in the trade-boundary log.
  • Pricing check: confirm every unit cost carries a quotation date, source, and confirmed scope.
  • Documentation check: confirm exclusions and unresolved questions are recorded and linked to the affected line.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Door Count Does Not Match the Door Schedule

Probable cause: a door was added, removed, or relabeled on a plan revision that was not reflected in the schedule, or the schedule and plan were reviewed against different revisions.
Confirmation: compare the revision date on the plan set against the revision date on the door schedule and recount doors by tag on the current revision only.
Correction: log the discrepancy as an unresolved clarification, obtain the correct current-revision count, and update every hardware, casing, and labor line dependent on the affected tag.
Reinspection: repeat the door-count reconciliation against the corrected schedule.

Casing Quantity Does Not Reconcile With Opening Count

Probable cause: an opening was measured twice, an opening was omitted, or the reveal allowance was applied inconsistently across openings.
Confirmation: recount openings from the plan independently and compare against the casing line count; check the reveal value used on each line.
Correction: correct the omitted or duplicated opening line and apply the approved reveal value consistently across all openings.
Reinspection: repeat the opening count and casing-quantity reconciliation.

Baseboard Total Does Not Match Room Perimeter

Probable cause: the opening-deduction rule was applied inconsistently, or a room perimeter was measured from an outdated wall dimension.
Confirmation: recompute the room perimeter from the current wall dimensions and reapply the approved opening-deduction rule.
Correction: correct the room perimeter and reapply the deduction rule consistently to that room's baseboard line.
Reinspection: repeat the room-perimeter reconciliation for the corrected room.

A Trade Boundary Is Priced by Two Trades or by Neither

Probable cause: the trade-boundary log was not updated when the finish-carpentry scope was finalized, or the boundary was assumed rather than confirmed.
Confirmation: compare the finish-carpentry estimate against the flooring, cabinet, painting, hardware, and electrical estimates for the same shared item.
Correction: confirm the correct responsible trade with the affected estimator and update the trade-boundary log and both estimates.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary comparison across all coordinated estimates.

A Unit Cost Has No Quotation Date or Confirmed Scope

Probable cause: a price was entered from memory, a prior project, or an unconfirmed verbal quotation.
Confirmation: the error-check sheet's missing-source flag or a manual scan of the pricing log identifies the affected line.
Correction: obtain a dated quotation confirming product, scope, freight, and expiration, or mark the value as unverified and exclude it from pricing until confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the missing-source check on the corrected line.

Stock-Length Waste Is Applied as One Blanket Percentage

Probable cause: the estimator applied a single waste percentage across all profiles instead of the profile-specific and stain-grade-specific percentages required by Section H, Step 18.
Confirmation: compare the waste percentage recorded on each line against the profile, stock length, and stain-grade basis required.
Correction: recalculate waste for each profile category using its specific basis and update the affected order quantities.
Reinspection: repeat the waste check across all profile categories.

Field Terms

The document listing every door tag with size, swing, core, rating, frame, hardware group, and finish.
Hardware Group
The set of hinges, locksets, closers, and stops assigned to a door tag or type.
Opening Perimeter
The head plus both side dimensions of a door or window opening, used to calculate casing.
Room Perimeter
The total wall length of a room measured at floor level, used to calculate baseboard before opening deductions.
Built-Up Profile
A trim assembly made from two or more individual components installed together.
Stock-Length Optimization
Assigning net linear quantities to available stock lengths to minimize waste while preserving pattern match.
Trade-Boundary Log
The record identifying which trade supplies and which trade installs each shared component.
Reveal
The recessed margin between the jamb edge and the inside edge of casing.
Stain-Grade Material
Material finished with a transparent coating that requires grain and pattern-match consistency.
Long-Lead Item
A custom door, profile, species, hardware, or factory finish with a lead time affecting the schedule assumption.

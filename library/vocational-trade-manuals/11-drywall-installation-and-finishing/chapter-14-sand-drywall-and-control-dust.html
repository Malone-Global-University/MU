Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to sand a finished drywall area with controlled dust collection and minimal material removal. The finished work product is a smooth, primer-ready surface: every flat joint, butt joint, and inside corner blended without cutting into face paper, every dust source contained by vacuum extraction or protected containment, and a completed surface-smoothness inspection recording the defects found, corrected, and reinspected.

You will confirm that all compound from Chapter 13 is fully dry before sanding begins; establish dust containment and verify that ventilation will not spread dust or backdraft combustion equipment; select the least aggressive abrasive that will correct each defect; sand flat joints, butt joints, and inside corners using controlled technique that protects corner-bead noses and trim reveals; stop sanding at the point of smoothness rather than polishing through compound to paper; and inspect frequently under raking light, correcting with touch-up compound rather than additional sanding wherever a defect calls for material rather than abrasion.

This chapter follows Chapter 13, which applied the final compound coat and issued a sanding-readiness decision. It feeds directly into Chapter 15, which primes and inspects the sanded surface. No area may be primed under Chapter 15 until this chapter's surface-smoothness inspection is complete and the area is clean of dust.

Where This Skill Fits

Sanding is the step in the finishing sequence most likely to undo good work from earlier chapters if it is done carelessly. A knife line left ridged by Chapter 13 is correctable here with light, controlled abrasion; the same ridge sanded aggressively enough to cut through the paper into the gypsum core creates a defect that no later coat of primer or paint can hide without additional compound and another full dry cycle. This chapter treats sanding as a finishing tool used sparingly, not as a substitute for controlled application in the coats that came before it.

The dust-containment methods you establish here — HEPA vacuum extraction, protected ventilation, and controlled containment — are the same methods Chapter 16's texture preparation and Chapter 17 through Chapter 19's repair sanding rely on. The raking-light inspection habit built in this chapter carries forward unchanged into Chapter 15's primer inspection and every later finish-quality check in the book.

Scope and Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming that all compound is fully dry before sanding begins
  • establishing dust containment and protecting adjacent occupied or finished areas
  • verifying that ventilation will not spread dust or backdraft combustion equipment
  • selecting sanding sponges, pole sanders, hand sanders, vacuum sanders, abrasive grades, and corner tools appropriate to the defect being corrected
  • inspecting HEPA vacuum equipment, hoses, filters, and power cords before use
  • sanding flat joints, butt joints, and inside corners using controlled, even technique
  • protecting corner-bead noses and trim reveals during sanding
  • stopping sanding at the point of smoothness rather than polishing through compound to face paper
  • inspecting frequently under raking light and marking defects that require compound rather than more sanding
  • vacuuming walls, floors, ledges, boxes, and equipment using HEPA filtration and completing the surface-smoothness inspection

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin sanding before the compound identified in Chapter 13 is confirmed fully dry
  • dry-sweep fine drywall dust in an occupied or finished area at any point during or after sanding
  • sand through face paper into the gypsum core in an attempt to remove a ridge, lap mark, or high spot; a defect that cannot be corrected by light sanding must receive touch-up compound instead
  • disable, bypass, or operate dust-control ventilation in a manner that could backdraft a fuel-burning appliance in or near the work area
  • claim the surface meets a Level 4 or Level 5 finish designation based on sanding alone; finish-level determination depends on the operations and inspection criteria specified across the applicable chapters, not on sanding effort

Any defect that a careful, light sanding pass cannot correct without removing face paper must be referred back to Chapter 13 or Chapter 12 for an additional compound coat rather than sanded further.

Trade Note

This chapter produces a smooth, dust-cleared surface ready for primer, not a finished paint-ready wall. Primer inspection and defect touch-up under Chapter 15 still follow this chapter before the surface is approved for paint or the texture sequence in Chapter 16.

Setup Standard

Do not begin sanding (Step 9) until dust containment is established, ventilation is verified safe, and the abrasive and tool selection in Steps 1–8 is complete. Sanding started before containment is established must stop immediately and the area must be cleaned before work resumes.

Shop Tip

Begin every pass with the least aggressive abrasive that could plausibly correct the defect. Moving up in grit is always available if the light pass does not clear the ridge; moving back down after cutting into paper with a coarse abrasive is not.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every sanding operation in this book, from this chapter's primary finishing pass to the repair sanding in Chapters 17 through 19, follows the same sequence: confirm the compound is dry, establish containment, select the least aggressive abrasive, sand with controlled technique, inspect under raking light, correct workable defects with compound rather than more abrasion, and vacuum the area completely before removing protection. Learning that discipline here, before primer and texture chapters depend on it, keeps sanding a light finishing step rather than a repeated source of rework.

Selecting the Least Aggressive Abrasive and Protecting Face Paper

Match the abrasive to the defect rather than defaulting to the coarsest tool on hand. A shallow ridge from Chapter 13 usually responds to a fine sanding sponge; a heavier lap mark may require a coarser sheet on the pole sander followed by a fine pass to blend the transition. Stop the instant the surface reads smooth by hand and under raking light. Sanding past that point removes compound the earlier coats were designed to leave in place and risks cutting into face paper or exposing tape.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Smoothness by Hand and Under Raking Light

Run a bare hand across every sanded joint and corner in addition to sighting it under raking light. A ridge that reads smooth by eye under overhead light is often still detectable by touch. Confirm both before moving to the next section.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Sand Through Face Paper or Exposed Tape

Stop sanding the moment face paper begins to fuzz, roughen, or expose fibers, or the moment tape becomes visible through the compound. Do not continue sanding in an attempt to blend a sanding-through defect. Apply touch-up compound to the affected area, allow it to dry, and resand lightly instead.

Field Safety Cue — Continuous Respiratory Protection and Contained Ventilation

Wear the specified respiratory protection continuously while sanding, not only during the heaviest passes. Confirm that any exhaust fan or negative-air equipment draws from the work area outward along a path that does not pass a water heater, furnace, or other fuel-burning appliance's combustion air intake.

Code Alert — Dust Control in Occupied Structures

Where the work area is inside or adjacent to an occupied structure, verify the containment method meets the adopted code and any permit condition for dust and airborne-particulate control before sanding begins. Do not rely on an open doorway or a single box fan as a substitute for the specified containment method.

F. Preparation and Containment Procedure (Steps 1–8)

  1. Confirm all compound is fully dry. Check representative joints, fasteners, and corner-bead runs from Chapter 13 for full drying before proceeding.
  2. Establish dust containment and protect adjacent occupied or finished areas. Seal doorways, vents, and openings leading to areas outside the work zone using the specified containment method.
  3. Verify ventilation will not spread dust or backdraft combustion equipment. Confirm exhaust and negative-air paths draw away from fuel-burning appliances and their combustion air intakes.
  4. Select sanding sponge, pole sander, hand sander, vacuum sander, abrasive grade, and corner tools. Match the tool and abrasive grade to the defect identified on each joint, corner, and fastener location.
  5. Inspect HEPA vacuum, hoses, filters, and power cords. Confirm filters are seated correctly and hoses and cords are free of damage before starting equipment.
  6. Wear the specified respiratory, eye, skin, and clothing protection. Put on all required PPE before beginning any sanding pass.
  7. Illuminate the wall with strong raking light. Position the work light nearly parallel to the surface to reveal ridges, tool lines, and defects before sanding begins.
  8. Mark ridges, tool lines, edge transitions, and defects. Note each identified defect location on the finishing log before starting the sanding pass.

G. Sanding Procedure (Steps 9–17)

  1. Begin with the least aggressive abrasive that will correct the defect. Start every pass with the finest grade that could plausibly clear the marked ridge or tool line.
  2. Sand flat joints using broad, even strokes. Move the pole sander or hand sander in long, consistent passes rather than short, concentrated scrubbing.
  3. Blend feathered edges without cutting into face paper. Lighten pressure as the pass reaches the tapered outer edge of the coat.
  4. Sand butt joints without creating a hollow center. Keep pressure even across the width of the wider coat rather than concentrating it at the center.
  5. Sand inside corners using a sponge or controlled corner tool. Preserve the defined corner line rather than rounding or flattening it.
  6. Protect corner-bead noses and trim reveals. Use light hand pressure only near bead and trim, checking frequently for exposed metal or worn facing.
  7. Stop when the surface is smooth; do not polish through compound to paper. End the pass at the point the joint reads smooth by hand and under raking light.
  8. Use vacuum extraction continuously where specified. Run the vacuum sander hose during sanding wherever the tool and specification call for continuous extraction.
  9. Hand-sand delicate areas and around electrical boxes. Use the hand sander or sponge for small areas where a pole sander cannot be controlled precisely.

H. Inspection, Correction, and Cleanup Procedure (Steps 18–23)

  1. Inspect frequently under raking light. Recheck each section as it is completed rather than waiting until the entire room is sanded.
  2. Mark defects that require compound rather than more sanding. Identify any hollow, exposed tape, or sanding-through condition and log it for touch-up rather than continuing to sand it.
  3. Vacuum walls, floors, ledges, boxes, and equipment using HEPA filtration. Remove all settled dust from every horizontal and vertical surface in the work area.
  4. Do not dry-sweep fine dust. Use HEPA vacuum extraction exclusively for all dust removal in the work area.
  5. Apply localized touch-up compound, allow it to dry, and resand. Correct every logged defect with a thin compound application, allow full drying, and lightly resand the corrected area.
  6. Complete the surface-smoothness inspection. Confirm every section of the sanded area meets the Acceptance Standards table before releasing the area to Chapter 15.

Common Sanding Inspection

Inspect every sanded area using the same checks regardless of room size or joint length.

  • Joint check: confirm every flat and butt joint is smooth by hand and under raking light.
  • Corner check: confirm every inside corner retains a defined line with no rounding or paper damage.
  • Bead and trim check: confirm no exposed metal, worn facing, or reveal damage from sanding.
  • Paper-integrity check: confirm no fuzzed, roughened, or worn-through face paper and no exposed tape anywhere in the area.
  • Dust-control check: confirm no dust migration to adjacent areas and no combustion-appliance backdraft occurred.
  • Cleanliness check: confirm all surfaces are HEPA-vacuumed with no dry-sweeping performed.

Complete the Sanding and Dust-Control Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 15.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Face Paper Is Fuzzed or Worn Through After Sanding

Probable cause: an abrasive grade coarser than needed was used, or sanding continued past the point the surface read smooth.
Confirmation: inspect the affected area under raking light and by hand to determine the extent of the paper damage.
Correction: apply a thin touch-up compound coat over the damaged area, feather the edges, and allow it to dry fully.
Reinspection: lightly resand the dried touch-up using the least aggressive abrasive and recheck under raking light.

A Butt Joint Shows a Hollow Center After Sanding

Probable cause: sanding pressure concentrated at the center of the wider coat rather than distributed evenly across its width.
Confirmation: sight the joint under raking light along its full length and compare the center to the edges.
Correction: apply a thin touch-up coat to the hollow, feathering it into the surrounding coat, and allow it to dry.
Reinspection: lightly resand the corrected area and recheck for a flat, even profile under raking light.

An Inside Corner Reads Rounded or Flattened After Sanding

Probable cause: a flat hand sander or pole sander was used on the corner instead of a sponge or corner sanding tool.
Confirmation: sight the corner line under raking light from both adjoining wall planes.
Correction: apply a thin touch-up coat along the corner line using the one-side or corner-tool method, and allow it to dry.
Reinspection: resand lightly with a corner tool or sponge and recheck the corner line for definition.

Dust Is Found in an Adjacent Occupied Area After Sanding

Probable cause: containment sheeting was incomplete, or negative-air equipment was not balanced correctly for the work-area opening.
Confirmation: inspect containment seals at doorways, vents, and other openings, and confirm airflow direction with a smoke pencil or tissue test.
Correction: reseal or reposition containment sheeting, rebalance negative-air equipment, and HEPA-vacuum the affected adjacent area.
Reinspection: recheck containment integrity and airflow direction before resuming sanding.

Corner-Bead Metal or Trim Facing Is Exposed After Sanding

Probable cause: sanding pressure near the bead or trim was too aggressive, or a coarse abrasive was used where only light hand sanding was appropriate.
Confirmation: inspect the bead or trim closely under raking light for exposed metal, worn facing, or damaged reveal edges.
Correction: apply a thin touch-up compound coat over the exposed area using the bead nose and panel plane as guides, and allow it to dry.
Reinspection: lightly resand the corrected area by hand and recheck for full coverage under raking light.

Cleanup

  • HEPA-vacuum all sanding equipment, sponges, and reusable corner tools before storage
  • Empty and seal HEPA vacuum bags or clean filters according to the manufacturer's instructions
  • Remove and dispose of containment sheeting without releasing collected dust into the work area
  • File the completed Sanding and Dust-Control Log with the room's project records
  • Retain the log with the Chapter 15 primer and inspection documentation for the same area

Field Terms

Raking Light
Light directed nearly parallel to the surface so shallow ridges and hollows cast a visible shadow.
Least Aggressive Abrasive
The lowest-cutting abrasive grade capable of correcting a given defect.
Feathered Edge
The tapered outer limit of a compound coat that must be blended rather than sanded flat.
Sanding-Through Defect
A condition where abrasion has removed face paper or exposed tape, requiring compound correction.
Dust Containment
Physical barriers, negative-air equipment, and vacuum extraction used to confine gypsum and compound dust to the work area.
Surface-Smoothness Inspection
The documented raking-light inspection confirming a sanded surface is ready for primer.
Negative Air
A ventilation condition in which air pressure inside the work area is kept lower than surrounding areas to prevent dust migration.
Backdraft
A reversal of a combustion appliance's exhaust flow caused by competing negative pressure elsewhere in the structure.
Vacuum Sander
A sanding tool connected by hose to a vacuum source that extracts dust at the point of contact.
Finishing Log
The document recording abrasive grades, dust-control method, application date, and inspection results for each finishing stage.

Performance Check

With sanding and dust cleanup complete, review the area against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that every flat and butt joint reads smooth by hand and under raking light, every inside corner retains a defined line, corner bead and trim show no exposed metal or damage, no face paper is fuzzed or worn through and no tape is exposed anywhere in the area, dust containment held throughout the work with no migration or appliance backdraft, and every surface has been HEPA-vacuumed with no dry-sweeping performed. Do not release the area to Chapter 15 until every logged defect is corrected, dried, and reinspected.

You have completed the sanding and dust-control stage of drywall finishing. This chapter required you to select sanding tools (DW-079), sand flat joints (DW-080), sand corners (DW-081), avoid paper damage (DW-082), control dust exposure (DW-083), and inspect surface smoothness (DW-084).

You now have a smooth, dust-cleared drywall surface with every defect corrected and reinspected, and a completed Sanding and Dust-Control Log.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to lay out and cut drywall openings for electrical boxes, windows, doors, and other identified access points without damaging the components behind the panel. The finished work product is one complete set of labeled, dry-fitted panel cutouts for one room or wall system, each documented against its opening record.

You will identify every box, window, door, access panel, pipe, and duct opening from the panel layout; verify that circuits are de-energized before cutting near boxes or wiring; transfer box and opening dimensions to the panel face from fixed references; cut with controlled blade or bit depth; dry-fit the cut panel; and inspect for overcuts, broken corners, and damaged box edges before the panel is accepted for hanging.

This chapter builds directly on the straight cuts produced in Chapter 3. Every panel entering this chapter has already been measured, scored, snapped, and labeled. Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 assume that every opening required for a panel has already been cut and dry-fitted under this chapter before the panel is lifted into place.

Where This Skill Fits

An opening cut in the wrong place, at the wrong size, or with an unsafe cutting depth can sever a conductor, puncture a pipe, or leave a box that a cover plate cannot seat against. Every measurement reference established in Chapter 1's readiness inspection — box position, framing spacing, backing location — becomes the reference this chapter uses to place a cutout accurately from outside the finished panel face.

The straight, clean panel edges produced in Chapter 3 remain intact only if this chapter's openings are cut with controlled depth and a supported blade path. A panel with a torn or overcut opening returns to Chapter 3's replacement criteria rather than moving forward to Chapter 5 or Chapter 6.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying every box, window, door, access panel, pipe, and duct opening from the approved panel layout
  • confirming that electrical circuits are de-energized before cutting near boxes or wiring
  • measuring and transferring box centerlines and opening dimensions from two fixed panel references
  • marking box outlines with the required clearance for pre-cut openings
  • cutting with a drywall saw or approved rotary tool at a controlled blade or bit depth
  • cutting window and door openings from framing or back-side references, including in-place trimming where the approved method requires it
  • dry-fitting the panel and verifying clearance to boxes, frames, and cover plates
  • inspecting for overcuts, broken corners, unsupported narrow sections, and damaged box edges
  • repairing minor permissible overcuts or replacing a panel when a defect exceeds the project limit

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut into a box, panel, or opening on a circuit that has not been confirmed de-energized with a non-contact voltage tester
  • relocate a box, window, or door opening from its approved position without direction from the responsible trade or the approved plans
  • perform electrical, plumbing, or HVAC repair on a component exposed or damaged during cutting; damaged conductors, pipes, or ducts must be referred to the responsible trade
  • accept a panel with an overcut, broken corner, or damaged box edge that exceeds the project's permissible-repair limit stated in Section F below

Any condition outside the learner's qualification must be referred to the responsible electrician, plumber, HVAC technician, or framing trade as identified in Steps 2 and 10 below.

Trade Note

This chapter produces dry-fitted, labeled cutout panels, not hung drywall. A panel that has not passed the Step 20 inspection and Step 21–22 correction or documentation is not ready for Chapter 5 or Chapter 6 installation.

Setup Standard

Do not begin marking a box outline (Step 5) until Step 2's circuit de-energization is confirmed with a non-contact voltage tester and Step 3's box and rough-opening security is verified. A cutout marked or cut before these checks must be discarded and the panel reinspected before reuse.

Shop Tip

Pierce the starter point for a box cutout inside the marked outline, never on the line itself. Starting inside the waste area gives the saw or rotary bit a controlled entry point and keeps the finished edge on the marked line rather than beyond it.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every cutout in this chapter follows the same sequence regardless of whether it serves a box, a window, or a door: identify the opening from the layout, confirm the area is safe to cut, measure from fixed references, mark the outline, pierce a starter point inside the waste area, cut with controlled depth toward the line, dry-fit, and inspect. Learning that sequence on a box cutout transfers directly to the larger, framing-referenced cuts required for windows and doors.

BoxesCenterline transfer, clearance, blade or bit depth control
WindowsBack-side or in-place reference, flashing protection, factory-edge preservation
DoorsFraming-edge reference, supported cut path, jamb clearance

Locating and Marking Openings

Take every opening measurement from two fixed panel references, not from a single edge or a visual estimate. A box centerline transferred from only one reference can rotate out of position even when the linear distance is correct. Confirm the transferred outline against the physical box or rough opening before cutting whenever the panel can be held or dry-fitted in place.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Circuits Are De-Energized Before Cutting

Do not cut, drill, or pierce a panel near an electrical box or cable route until the circuit has been confirmed de-energized with a non-contact voltage tester. Keep rotary tools clear of any wiring that has not been positively identified. Treat any wiring of uncertain status as energized until confirmed otherwise by the responsible electrician.

Measurement Cue — Transfer From Two Fixed References

Measure every box centerline and opening dimension from two fixed panel references before marking. Confirm the marked outline against the physical box, frame, or template before cutting whenever the panel can be checked in place.

Field Safety Cue — Blade and Bit Depth Control

Set drywall-saw and rotary-tool cutting depth so the blade or bit clears the panel thickness without extending far enough to reach conductors, pipes, boxes, insulation, or framing behind the panel. Reduce cutting depth further when the panel location has not been fully verified against the rough-in plan.

Code Alert — Device Ear and Cover-Plate Bearing

Confirm that the required clearance leaves device ears and cover plates bearing on the finished surface as the approved plans specify. Local inspection requirements for box-to-surface relationship vary; verify the applicable standard before accepting a cutout as final.

F. Layout and Safety Verification Procedure (Steps 1–6)

  1. Review the panel layout and identify every box, window, door, access panel, pipe, and duct opening. Confirm the opening count on each panel matches the cutout worksheet before marking begins.
  2. Verify that electrical circuits are de-energized where cutting may contact boxes or wiring. Test each affected circuit with a non-contact voltage tester and record the result on the cutout worksheet.
  3. Confirm that boxes and rough openings are secure and correctly located. Compare box and rough-opening position against the Chapter 1 readiness release before transferring measurements.
  4. Measure each opening from two fixed panel references. Use the same two references for every measurement on a given panel to avoid compounding error.
  5. Transfer box centerlines and edge dimensions to the panel face. Mark the centerline first, then the full outline with the required clearance.
  6. Confirm measurements before cutting. Recheck each transferred dimension against the panel layout and, where possible, against the physical box or rough opening.

G. Box Cutout Procedure (Steps 7–12)

  1. For pre-cut box openings, mark the exact box outline with the required clearance. Use the box locator or template to produce a square, accurately sized outline.
  2. Drill or pierce a starter point inside the waste area. Position the starter point away from the marked line so the saw or bit enters cleanly.
  3. Cut toward the marked line using a drywall saw or approved rotary tool. Follow the outline in a controlled, continuous motion rather than multiple disconnected passes.
  4. Control blade depth to prevent damage to conductors, pipes, boxes, insulation, and framing. Set the depth stop before cutting and verify it against the panel thickness.
  5. Keep rotary tools away from live or unidentified wiring. Stop immediately and refer the condition to the electrician if unidentified wiring is encountered.
  6. Cut to the edge of the box without removing excessive face paper. Trim the final edge with a utility knife where the saw or bit leaves ragged paper beyond the box edge.

H. Window and Door Opening Procedure (Steps 13–17)

  1. For window and door openings, hang or dry-fit the panel where the approved method uses in-place trimming. Confirm which method the panel layout specifies before cutting begins.
  2. Locate the rough-opening edges from the back or controlled reference points. Use the same fixed references established in Step 4 for consistency with adjoining panels.
  3. Cut along supported framing edges without damaging window or door flashing. Keep the blade path on the framing side of any flashing membrane.
  4. Preserve factory edges where required by the panel layout. Confirm which panel edges are factory edges before trimming begins.
  5. Remove waste pieces carefully. Support the waste piece during final separation to avoid tearing the retained panel's face paper.

I. Dry-Fit, Inspection, and Correction Procedure (Steps 18–22)

  1. Dry-fit the panel and verify that openings clear boxes and frames without large gaps. Hold or temporarily position the panel and check clearance at every cut opening.
  2. Confirm that device ears and cover plates will bear on the finished surface as designed. Check this against the Acceptance Standards table before accepting the panel.
  3. Inspect for overcuts, broken corners, unsupported narrow sections, and damaged box edges. Measure any suspected overcut against the project's permissible-repair limit.
  4. Repair minor permissible overcuts using the approved method or replace the panel when the defect exceeds the project limit. Do not conceal an out-of-limit overcut with joint compound at this stage.
  5. Label and document each completed opening. Record opening type, reference measurements, cutting method, and inspection result on the cutout worksheet before the panel moves to Chapter 5 or Chapter 6.

Common Cutout Inspection

Inspect every cutout using the same checks regardless of opening type or panel location.

  • Position check: confirm the cutout matches the recorded reference measurements within the stated tolerance.
  • Clearance check: confirm the panel edge clears the box, frame, or penetration without exceeding the cover-plate or trim tolerance.
  • Depth check: confirm no cutting exceeded the panel thickness plus the minimum required clearance behind the panel.
  • Edge condition check: confirm no broken corners, unsupported narrow sections, or torn face paper remain at any cutout edge.
  • Overcut check: confirm any overcut is within the project's permissible-repair limit or the panel has been replaced.
  • Documentation check: confirm every opening is labeled and recorded on the cutout worksheet.

Complete the Cutout Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before releasing panels to Chapter 5 or Chapter 6.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Box Opening Is Too Small and the Panel Will Not Sit Flat Against the Box

Probable cause: the required clearance was omitted when the outline was marked, or the centerline was transferred from only one reference and rotated out of position.
Confirmation: remeasure the box centerline from both fixed references and compare the cut outline against the recorded clearance.
Correction: enlarge the opening to the correct outline using a rasp or drywall saw, working from the existing cut toward the corrected line, or replace the panel if the correction would leave an unsupported edge.
Reinspection: dry-fit the panel again and confirm clearance meets the Acceptance Standards table.

The Cutout Shows an Overcut Beyond the Marked Line

Probable cause: the starter point was placed on or across the marked line rather than inside the waste area, or the saw or rotary tool wandered during the cut.
Confirmation: measure the overcut distance from the marked line and compare it to the project's permissible-repair limit.
Correction: repair the overcut using the approved method if it is within the permissible limit; replace the panel if the overcut exceeds the limit.
Reinspection: reinspect the repaired or replaced panel against the Acceptance Standards table before dry-fitting.

A Corner of the Cutout Is Broken or a Narrow Section Is Unsupported

Probable cause: excessive blade or bit pressure at a corner, or a narrow strip of panel left between two openings without adequate support.
Confirmation: inspect the corner and narrow section against the panel layout to determine whether the layout itself requires an unsupported strip.
Correction: replace the panel if the break compromises structural support at the opening; refer the layout to the responsible trade if the design itself leaves an unsupported strip.
Reinspection: reinspect the replacement panel's corners and narrow sections before dry-fitting.

The Rotary Tool Contacts an Unidentified Object Behind the Panel

Probable cause: a conductor, pipe, or duct is present at a location not shown on the rough-in plan, or the cutting depth exceeded the panel thickness by more than the approved margin.
Confirmation: stop cutting immediately, inspect the contacted object, and compare its location to the rough-in plan and the Chapter 1 readiness release.
Correction: refer any damaged conductor, pipe, or duct to the responsible electrician, plumber, or HVAC technician before continuing; do not resume cutting in the area until the condition is resolved.
Reinspection: reinspect the corrected condition and reverify blade or bit depth settings before cutting resumes.

A Window or Door Opening Does Not Preserve the Required Factory Edge

Probable cause: the panel was oriented incorrectly during layout, or the cut removed a factory edge that the panel layout required to remain intact.
Confirmation: compare the cut panel against the panel layout to confirm which edges were designated as factory edges.
Correction: replace the panel using the correct orientation if the factory edge was required for an adjoining joint; document the deviation if the layout permits a cut edge at that location.
Reinspection: reinspect the replacement panel's orientation and edge condition before dry-fitting.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with dry-fitted, labeled panels ready for Chapter 5 or Chapter 6, not a physical installation.

  • Vacuum gypsum dust and waste pieces from the cutting station and surrounding floor area
  • Return drywall saws, rotary tools, bits, and voltage testers to storage and inspect them for damage
  • Sort and label waste pieces and offcuts for reuse or disposal
  • File the completed cutout worksheets with the room's project records alongside the Chapter 3 cut records

Field Terms

Box Centerline
The vertical and horizontal centerline of an electrical box, used to transfer the box outline to the panel face.
Required Clearance
The distance added around a box or opening outline so the installed panel clears the box or frame within tolerance.
Starter Point
The pierced or drilled entry point inside the waste area of a marked cutout.
Back-Side Reference
A rough-opening edge measured or marked from the framing side of the panel.
Overcut
Removed material beyond the marked cutout line, measured against the permissible-repair limit.
Device Ear
The mounting tab on an electrical device that must bear on the finished drywall surface.
Non-Contact Voltage Tester
A tool used to confirm whether a circuit is energized without direct contact with conductors.
Rough Opening
The framed opening sized to receive a window or door unit before drywall is fitted around it.
Factory Edge
The tapered, manufactured long edge of a drywall panel, preserved where the panel layout requires it for an adjoining joint.
Cutout Worksheet
The document recording opening type, reference measurements, cutting method, and inspection result for each completed cutout.

Performance Check

With every opening cut and dry-fitted, review the panel set against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that box, window, and door cutouts sit within the recorded reference tolerance, panel edges clear boxes and frames without exceeding the cover-plate or trim tolerance, no cutting exceeded the controlled blade or bit depth, no overcut exceeds the project's permissible-repair limit, corners and narrow sections remain intact, factory edges and flashing are preserved where required, and every opening is labeled and recorded on the cutout worksheet. Accept the panel set for Chapter 5 or Chapter 6 only after every cutout meets these standards.

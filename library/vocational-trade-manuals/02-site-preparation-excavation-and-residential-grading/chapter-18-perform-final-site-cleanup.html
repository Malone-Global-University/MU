Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete final site housekeeping, material recovery, waste removal, equipment demobilization, and selective removal of temporary controls. The finished work product is a clean, organized, hazard-controlled site with required temporary systems preserved. This project begins only after the restoration work of Project 17 has stabilized disturbed areas, and it does not include the restoration of disturbed ground itself as covered in Project 17, does not include removing a temporary drainage or erosion-control system that must remain in service, and does not include the complete site-readiness inspection covered in Project 19.

You will define cleanup limits and identify temporary systems that must remain in service; walk the site and mark waste, surplus material, reusable material, hazardous material, and owner-retained items; separate waste streams according to project and disposal requirements; remove sharp objects, protrusions, loose wire, broken masonry, scrap metal, packaging, and debris; recover, clean, count, and store reusable stakes, forms, signs, barriers, tools, and surplus materials; remove unauthorized stockpiles and dispose of excess soil or aggregate through the approved route; clean hard surfaces, access routes, streets, sidewalks, and drainage inlets; remove mud and track-out without washing sediment into drainage systems; remove temporary fencing, signs, barriers, access stone, or erosion controls only when authorized; repair damage caused by removal of temporary installations; secure or remove fuel, chemicals, lubricants, batteries, and compressed-gas cylinders; inspect remaining materials for stable storage and weather protection; confirm utility covers, drains, wells, septic components, hydrants, and emergency access remain unobstructed; check the site for trip, puncture, collapse, fire, and environmental hazards; correct deficiencies and repeat the walkthrough; document waste disposal, recovered materials, remaining controls, and unresolved items; and complete the final housekeeping inspection record.

This is the eighteenth project in the site preparation sequence. It depends on the restoration record produced in Project 17 and the temporary drainage, erosion-control, and access systems installed in Projects 8 and 15, and it uses the stockpile and material-separation records from Projects 4 and 8. The housekeeping record produced here becomes part of the evidence reviewed during the complete site-readiness inspection of Project 19.

Where This Skill Fits

The restoration and stabilization completed in Project 17 must already be in place before general housekeeping begins across the restored areas; cleaning up before restoration is finished risks re-disturbing stabilized ground. This project removes waste, surplus material, and unauthorized stockpiles across the whole site, but it does not authorize removal of any temporary drainage, erosion-control, or access system that must remain in service until permanent stabilization or final grading acceptance is confirmed.

This project stands between the restoration work of Project 17 and the complete site-readiness inspection of Project 19. Its waste removal, material recovery, and hazard control protect workers, the public, and the site itself during the remaining construction period, and its housekeeping record becomes part of the evidence reviewed during the Project 19 readiness inspection. A cleanup that removes a still-required erosion control, blocks emergency access, or leaves hazardous material improperly stored can create a hazard or a compliance failure discovered only during the final inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining cleanup limits and identifying temporary systems that must remain in service
  • walking the site to mark waste, surplus material, reusable material, hazardous material, and owner-retained items
  • separating waste streams according to project and disposal requirements
  • removing sharp objects, protrusions, loose wire, broken masonry, scrap metal, packaging, and debris
  • recovering, cleaning, counting, and storing reusable stakes, forms, signs, barriers, tools, and surplus materials
  • removing unauthorized stockpiles and disposing of excess soil or aggregate through the approved route
  • cleaning hard surfaces, access routes, streets, sidewalks, and drainage inlets
  • removing mud and track-out without washing sediment into drainage systems
  • removing temporary fencing, signs, barriers, access stone, or erosion controls only when authorized
  • repairing damage caused by removal of temporary installations
  • securing or removing fuel, chemicals, lubricants, batteries, and compressed-gas cylinders
  • inspecting, correcting, and documenting the completed housekeeping condition

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • remove a temporary drainage, erosion-control, or access system that has not been confirmed as no longer required
  • restore disturbed ground; that work belongs to Project 17
  • dispose of contaminated soil, hazardous material, or regulated waste outside an approved disposal route
  • wash sediment, mud, or debris into a storm drain, ditch, or drainage inlet
  • discard an item marked for owner retention without owner or supervisor confirmation
  • relocate or remove a utility cover, well, septic component, or emergency-access feature

Any condition where a temporary system's continued need is unclear, or where a waste stream requires specialized handling, must be escalated before removal or disposal.

Trade Note

The cleanup limits, list of temporary systems required to remain in service, and disposal requirements referenced throughout this project are supplied by the project plan, erosion-control plan, or supervisor, and are not generated by this chapter. Where this chapter refers to "the approved disposal route" or "the required temporary systems," it means those supplied designations; do not assume a system is no longer needed without confirmation.

Common Field Fundamentals

Final site cleanup only serves its purpose if it removes hazards and surplus material without disturbing work that must remain, disposing of material through an unapproved route, or creating an environmental discharge violation. Removing a temporary drainage or erosion-control system before it is confirmed unnecessary can undo protection the site still depends on, and washing sediment into a storm drain during surface cleaning can create a discharge violation even when the intent was housekeeping.

Working Through the Cleanup Sequence

Each category of this sequence depends on the categories before it. Removing a temporary control before confirming it is no longer required, or disposing of a waste stream before it is properly separated, creates rework or a compliance problem. Work through Steps 3 through 14 of this project in the order given, and recheck an earlier category if a later step reveals a discrepancy. If a temporary system's continued need is unclear or a waste stream requires specialized handling, stop and escalate before continuing; do not remove or dispose of the item on assumption.

Measurement Cue — Confirming Systems Required to Remain in Service

Before removing any temporary fencing, sign, barrier, access stone, or erosion control, verify its status against the cleanup limits and the current project plan, drainage record, or supervisor confirmation. Do not rely on visual appearance alone to judge whether a system is still needed.

Code Alert — Disposal and Discharge Requirements

Confirm the approved disposal route for each waste stream and the discharge requirements governing surface-cleaning wastewater before beginning. Requirements vary by jurisdiction, material type, and disposal facility, and do not assume a method used on a prior site applies here.

Manufacturer Cue — Fuel, Chemicals, Batteries, and Cylinders

Follow manufacturer and supplier instructions for storing, handling, and transporting fuel, chemicals, lubricants, batteries, and compressed-gas cylinders. Improper storage or handling of these materials can create a fire, chemical, or pressure hazard even during routine cleanup.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Remove a Required Temporary System

Do not remove a temporary drainage, erosion-control, or access system without confirmed authorization. Stop work immediately if the status of a system is uncertain, and escalate to the responsible supervisor before removing it.

Field Safety Cue — Debris, Sediment, and Hazardous Materials

Wear cut-resistant gloves when handling scrap metal, broken masonry, and wire, and keep sediment out of storm drains and drainage inlets during surface cleaning. Secure fuel, chemicals, batteries, and cylinders in approved storage before leaving the work area unattended.

E. Scope Definition and Marking

  1. Define cleanup limits and identify temporary systems that must remain in service. Confirm both against the approved project plan and current drainage and erosion-control records before proceeding.
  2. Walk the site and mark waste, surplus material, reusable material, hazardous material, and owner-retained items. Tag each category so subsequent sorting and removal proceed without ambiguity.

F. Waste Separation and Debris Removal

  1. Separate waste streams according to project and disposal requirements. Sort trash, recyclable, vegetative, contaminated, and hazardous material into the designated containers.
  2. Remove sharp objects, protrusions, loose wire, broken masonry, scrap metal, packaging, and debris. Clear every hazard within the cleanup limits before continuing.

G. Material Recovery and Stockpile Removal

  1. Recover, clean, count, and store reusable stakes, forms, signs, barriers, tools, and surplus materials. Organize recovered items for reuse or return per the project's material-recovery requirements.
  2. Remove unauthorized stockpiles and dispose of excess soil or aggregate through the approved route. Confirm each stockpile is unauthorized before removal and use only the designated disposal route.

H. Surface Cleaning

  1. Clean hard surfaces, access routes, streets, sidewalks, and drainage inlets. Remove accumulated debris and mud from every surface within the cleanup limits.
  2. Remove mud and track-out without washing sediment into drainage systems. Use dry removal methods or approved capture methods that prevent sediment discharge.

I. Selective Removal of Temporary Systems

  1. Remove temporary fencing, signs, barriers, access stone, or erosion controls only when authorized. Confirm authorization for each system individually before removal.
  2. Repair damage caused by removal of temporary installations. Correct any disturbance to surrounding grade, surfaces, or materials caused by the removal.

J. Hazardous Materials and Final Inspection

  1. Secure or remove fuel, chemicals, lubricants, batteries, and compressed-gas cylinders. Follow manufacturer and supplier storage and handling instructions for each material.
  2. Inspect remaining materials for stable storage and weather protection. Confirm all remaining stored materials are stable and protected from weather exposure.
  3. Confirm utility covers, drains, wells, septic components, hydrants, and emergency access remain unobstructed. Verify each feature is visible, accessible, and free of stored material or debris.
  4. Check the site for trip, puncture, collapse, fire, and environmental hazards. Walk the full cleanup limits and record every hazard found.
  5. Correct deficiencies and repeat the walkthrough. Complete each correction and reinspect the corrected area before proceeding.
  6. Document waste disposal, recovered materials, remaining controls, and unresolved items, and complete the final housekeeping inspection record. Confirm every quantity, condition, and unresolved item is recorded before closeout.

Common Site Inspection

Inspect the completed cleanup using the same checks regardless of site size or waste volume.

  • Waste-stream check: confirm each waste stream is separated and removed through the approved disposal route.
  • Debris and hazard check: confirm sharp objects, scrap, and packaging are removed from the cleanup limits.
  • Material-recovery check: confirm reusable materials are cleaned, counted, and stored.
  • Surface-cleaning check: confirm hard surfaces and drainage inlets are free of mud and track-out with no sediment discharge.
  • Selective-removal check: confirm only authorized temporary systems were removed and removal damage is repaired.
  • Hazardous-material check: confirm fuel, chemicals, batteries, and cylinders are secured or removed per instructions.

Complete the Final Housekeeping Inspection Record Sheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before cleanup is presented for acceptance.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Still-Required Temporary System Was Removed

Probable cause: the system's status was not confirmed against the current drainage or erosion-control record before removal.
Confirmation: compare the removed system against the Project 8, 14, or 15 records to determine whether it was still required.
Correction: reinstall the system per its original installation requirements and confirm its condition matches the prior record.
Reinspection: reverify the reinstalled system's function before continuing cleanup elsewhere on site.

Sediment Is Discharged Into a Storm Drain or Drainage Inlet During Surface Cleaning

Probable cause: mud or debris was washed toward the inlet instead of removed by dry or approved capture methods.
Confirmation: inspect the inlet and downstream drainage path for sediment accumulation.
Correction: remove the discharged sediment from the drainage system using the approved method and repeat surface cleaning using dry removal or approved capture methods.
Reinspection: reinspect the inlet and drainage path after correction and before the next cleaning pass.

An Unauthorized Stockpile or Waste Item Was Disposed of Through the Wrong Route

Probable cause: the material was not identified or sorted correctly during the walkthrough, or the approved disposal route was not confirmed before disposal.
Confirmation: compare the disposed material against the project's waste-stream and disposal-route requirements.
Correction: document the discrepancy, notify the responsible party, and correct future sorting and disposal for the same material type.
Reinspection: reverify remaining stockpiles and waste sorting before completing cleanup.

A Utility Cover, Hydrant, or Emergency Access Point Remains Obstructed

Probable cause: stored material, debris, or a recovered stockpile was placed over or near the feature without checking its location.
Confirmation: compare the obstruction against the site plan or utility markings identifying the feature's location.
Correction: relocate the obstructing material and confirm the feature is visible and accessible.
Reinspection: reverify the feature remains unobstructed during the repeated walkthrough.

Fuel, Chemicals, Batteries, or Cylinders Are Found Improperly Stored

Probable cause: the material was not secured according to manufacturer or supplier storage instructions, or was left in an unprotected location.
Confirmation: compare the storage condition against manufacturer or supplier instructions for the specific material.
Correction: relocate and secure the material per instructions, or remove it from the site through the approved method.
Reinspection: reverify storage condition before the site is left unattended.

An Owner-Retained Item Was Discarded

Probable cause: the item was not tagged during the site walkthrough, or the tag was removed or overlooked during sorting.
Confirmation: compare the disposed item against the owner-retained items list recorded during the walkthrough.
Correction: notify the owner or supervisor, document the discrepancy, and recover or replace the item where possible.
Reinspection: reverify remaining owner-retained items are correctly tagged and separated before completing cleanup.

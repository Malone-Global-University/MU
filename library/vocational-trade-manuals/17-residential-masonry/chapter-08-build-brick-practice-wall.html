Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one nonstructural brick practice wall using the layout established in Chapter 4 and the mortar mixed in Chapter 3. The finished work product is one completed brick practice wall section with full, consistent joints, controlled running bond, level courses, an aligned face, a plumb condition, and uniformly tooled joints, ready for the inspection recorded on the Brick Practice Wall Inspection Record.

You will select brick, inspect it for defects, dry-lay the first course, build end leads or corner controls, spread and furrow mortar beds, butter and set brick to a raised mason's line course by course, maintain running bond, tool the joints at the correct firmness, and inspect the completed wall for bond, joint fullness, color distribution, line, level, plumb, tooling, and cleanliness.

This chapter builds directly on the Chapter 1 readiness plan, the Chapter 2 staged work area, the Chapter 3 mortar batch, and the Chapter 4 layout for the selected wall. It does not authorize cutting units — that work belongs to Chapter 10 — and it does not authorize brick corners and piers beyond the leads needed to control this wall, which are taught in Chapter 9.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-043 — Spread mortar for brick, producing a bed of the specified width and thickness and furrowing it to a consistent depth.
  • RM-044 — Butter brick ends, applying a full head-joint of mortar to the leading end of each unit before it is set.
  • RM-045 — Set brick courses, positioning each unit to line, level, and the story-pole course elevation with full bed bearing.
  • RM-046 — Maintain running bond, offsetting alternate courses by the specified half-unit or bond unit and preserving bond throughout the wall.
  • RM-047 — Tool joints, finishing bed and head joints to a consistent depth and profile at the correct firmness and brushing them clean.
  • RM-048 — Inspect brick-wall alignment, verifying bond, joint fullness, color distribution, line, level, plumb, tooling, and cleanliness against the Acceptance Standards table.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 established the wall baseline, story pole, and mason's-line reference for this wall. Chapter 3 produced the mortar batch this chapter consumes within its working time. This chapter turns those two prepared inputs into laid brick masonry, and its bond, joint, and plumb technique carries forward directly into Chapter 9 corners and piers, Chapter 11 brick veneer, and the Chapter 20 capstone.

The mortar-bed, buttering, and joint-tooling technique taught here is the same technique used for every later brick assembly in this book. Learning it correctly on a nonstructural practice wall, where a bond or plumb error can be corrected without structural consequence, protects every later chapter that depends on it.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • inspecting brick for cracks, chips, warpage, color range, and contamination before laying
  • blending brick from multiple pallets or cubes where required
  • dry-laying the first course and confirming modular wall length and joint width
  • building end leads or corner controls to establish line and course elevation
  • spreading and furrowing mortar beds for a manageable length of wall
  • buttering head ends and setting brick with filled head joints
  • maintaining joint width, running bond, and the story-pole course height
  • stretching and raising the mason's line by course and checking wall face and plumb
  • tooling bed and head joints, brushing diagonally after tooling, and protecting new work
  • inspecting bond, joint fullness, color distribution, line, level, plumb, tooling, and cleanliness

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut brick, block, or stone; all cutting is performed only under Chapter 10 procedures and controls
  • build a corner lead beyond the end control needed for this single wall, or build a freestanding pier; those tasks belong to Chapter 9
  • install veneer ties, WRB, flashing, or a drainage cavity; those tasks belong to Chapter 11
  • proceed on any load-bearing, retaining, or structural brick assembly without the exact engineered design and inspection sequence supplied by a qualified professional
  • lay brick on a base, footing, or support that has not passed the Chapter 1 or Chapter 4 inspection

Any wall, base, mortar, or layout condition that fails the inspection in this chapter must be documented, corrected, and reinspected before the wall proceeds to the next course or the next chapter.

Trade Note

This chapter builds a nonstructural practice wall. Do not exceed the practice-wall height limit stated in the book outline or approved plan without bracing or an engineered design. A practice wall is a legitimate reference fixture in its own right — record its dimensions and keep it available for later comparison against Chapter 9, Chapter 11, and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Setup Standard

Do not begin laying brick until the Chapter 4 layout has been approved, the Chapter 3 mortar batch has passed its workability check, and the brick has been inspected for cracks, chips, warpage, color range, and contamination. A wall started on an unapproved layout or unacceptable mortar must be taken down and rebuilt, not corrected in place after mortar has set.

Shop Tip

Lay only as much mortar bed as you can cover with brick before it begins to stiffen. A bed spread too far ahead of the work loses its bond potential, and units set into a stiffened bed will not achieve full bearing no matter how hard they are tapped.

Reference Conventions

Common Field Fundamentals

Every brick wall in this book, from this practice wall to the Chapter 20 capstone, is built with the same core technique: inspect and stage the brick, spread and furrow a workable mortar bed, butter and set each unit to a raised line at the story-pole course height, maintain running bond and full joints, and tool the joints at the correct firmness. Learning that technique here, on a nonstructural wall where a bond or plumb error carries no structural consequence, protects every later brick chapter that depends on it.

Bond, Joint, and Plumb Judgment

Running bond is not decorative; it distributes load and controls the visual and dimensional integrity of the wall. Do not shift the bond offset partway up a wall to correct an accumulated dimensional error. If the bond drifts from the specified offset, stop, identify the course where the drift began, and correct it before continuing rather than absorbing the error into later courses.

Measurement Cue — Verify Course Height at Every Course

Check the story pole against the top of every course before raising the mason's line. Do not wait several courses to check cumulative height; an uncorrected course-height error compounds and forces a visible joint-width change to correct at the top of the wall.

Critical Safety Cue — Mortar and Cement Exposure

Treat wet mortar as caustic. Wear waterproof gloves, eye protection, and long sleeves at all times while spreading, buttering, or tooling. Do not kneel in wet mortar without approved waterproof knee protection. Wash exposed skin immediately if wet mortar makes contact.

Field Safety Cue — Line Tension and Work Area

Apply only enough tension to the mason's line to keep it straight without pulling the line blocks or pins loose. Keep the mortar board and brick stock clear of the walking path along the wall face to prevent tripping while carrying units or tools.

Code Alert — Height and Bracing Limits

Do not exceed the freestanding height limit stated in the approved plan or book outline for this practice wall without the specified bracing or an engineered design. Confirm the limit before laying the final course.

E. Preparation — Inspection, Blending, and Dry-Lay

  1. Review brick dimensions, absorption, mortar, bond, joint profile, wall height, and color distribution. Confirm these against the Chapter 4 layout and approved project documents before laying begins.
  2. Inspect brick for cracks, chips, warpage, color range, and contamination. Set aside any unit that fails this inspection and do not lay it.
  3. Blend brick from multiple pallets or cubes where required. Draw units from at least three pallets or cubes in rotation when the specification calls for blending.
  4. Dry-lay the first course. Position units without mortar along the baseline established in Chapter 4.
  5. Confirm modular wall length and joint width. Measure the dry-laid course against the story pole and adjust before spreading mortar.

F. Leads, Mortar Bed, and First Course

  1. Build end leads or establish corner controls. Lay the leads ahead of the field of the wall using the Chapter 4 reference points, checking level, plumb, and bond at every course of the lead.
  2. Spread a mortar bed for a manageable length. Limit the spread to the length that can be covered with brick before the mortar begins to stiffen.
  3. Furrow the bed. Draw a shallow furrow down the center of the spread bed with the trowel point.
  4. Butter the head end of the first brick. Apply a full head joint of mortar to the leading end before setting the unit.
  5. Press and slide the brick into position. Seat the unit into the furrowed bed and against the buttered head joint with full bed bearing.
  6. Tap to line and course elevation. Use the trowel handle or a rubber mallet to bring the unit to the mason's line and the story-pole course height.

G. Field Coursing, Bond, and Line Control

  1. Set subsequent bricks with filled head joints. Butter each leading end before setting; do not leave head joints to be filled after the unit is in place.
  2. Maintain joint width without excessive squeezing. Verify joint width with the tape measure at intervals along each course.
  3. Stretch and raise the mason's line by course. Move the line to the next course height at the lead before laying each new course in the field.
  4. Begin alternate courses with the specified half-unit or bond unit. Confirm the correct starting unit at each lead before laying the field of that course.
  5. Maintain running bond. Check that head joints do not align vertically between adjacent courses.
  6. Use the story pole to control course height. Verify cumulative height at every course, not only at the top of the wall.
  7. Check wall face and plumb. Use the straightedge along the face and the level at the controlled intervals specified in Chapter 4.

H. Cutting, Tooling, and Protection

  1. Cut units only using Chapter 10 procedures. Do not hand-break or force-fit an oversized unit; stop and cut it under the Chapter 10 controls.
  2. Tool joints when thumbprint hard or at the specified condition. Test firmness before tooling; tooling too early smears the joint, and tooling too late will not compress it.
  3. Tool bed and head joints to consistent depth and profile. Use the same jointer and technique across the entire wall face.
  4. Brush diagonally after tooling. Remove tooling burrs and loose crumbs without smearing mortar across the unit faces.
  5. Protect new work from rain, freezing, impact, and rapid drying. Cover the wall as specified for the anticipated weather before leaving the work area.

I. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect bond, joint fullness, color distribution, line, level, plumb, tooling, and cleanliness. Record each result against the Acceptance Standards table on the Brick Practice Wall Inspection Record.
  2. Document the practice wall. Photograph the completed wall, file the inspection record with the Chapter 4 layout and Chapter 3 mortar batch record, and note any deficiency and its correction.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed practice wall using the same checks regardless of wall length or brick type.

  • Bond check: confirm the specified bond offset was maintained from the first course to the last with no unapproved vertical head-joint alignment.
  • Joint check: confirm bed and head joints are fully filled at the specified width and free of voids at not fewer than five measured locations.
  • Line, level, and plumb check: confirm each course was verified against the mason's line and story pole as it was laid, and the finished face was checked with a straightedge and level at the controlled intervals.
  • Tooling check: confirm joints were tooled at thumbprint-hard firmness to a consistent profile and brushed diagonally without smearing.
  • Cleanliness and protection check: confirm the finished wall face is free of mortar smears and debris and was protected from weather before leaving the work area.

Complete the Brick Practice Wall Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before proceeding to Chapter 9.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Head Joints Show Voids After Tooling

Probable cause: the head joint was not fully buttered before the unit was set, or the unit was slid into place without enough mortar at the leading end.
Confirmation: tap the joint lightly and check for a hollow sound, or probe the joint with a pointing tool before tooling.
Correction: remove the affected unit while the mortar is still workable, rebutter the head joint fully, and reset the unit to line and course elevation.
Reinspection: recheck joint fullness by tape measure and visual probe once the mortar has stiffened.

Running Bond Has Drifted From the Specified Offset

Probable cause: a course was started from the wrong bond unit at a lead, or an accumulated joint-width error was absorbed into the bond instead of being corrected at the point it occurred.
Confirmation: compare the head-joint alignment of each course against the course two below it; any unapproved vertical alignment indicates drift.
Correction: identify the course where the drift began and rebuild from that course forward using the correct bond unit at each lead.
Reinspection: recheck bond alignment across the full wall height before proceeding to tooling.

Course Height No Longer Matches the Story Pole

Probable cause: joint thickness varied course to course without being checked against the story pole at each course.
Confirmation: hold the story pole against the lead and compare marked course elevations to the actual top of each course.
Correction: adjust joint thickness gradually over the next several courses rather than making one large correction in a single joint, and verify the story pole at every course going forward.
Reinspection: recheck cumulative height at the top of the wall against the story pole.

Wall Face Is Out of Plumb

Probable cause: units were tapped to the line without checking plumb at controlled intervals, allowing a gradual lean to develop uncorrected.
Confirmation: check the level at the specified intervals along the full wall height and compare against the Chapter 4 tolerance.
Correction: correct plumb course by course while mortar remains workable; do not attempt to correct a set wall by tapping units after the mortar has hardened.
Reinspection: recheck plumb at all controlled intervals after correction.

Joints Are Smeared or Show Inconsistent Profile

Probable cause: joints were tooled before or after the correct thumbprint-hard firmness, or brushing was performed before tooling was complete.
Confirmation: compare joint profile and surface condition across several locations on the wall; inconsistency indicates the firmness was not checked before tooling each section.
Correction: where mortar remains workable, retool affected joints; where mortar has hardened past working time, document the condition and address it as a repointing item under Chapter 14.
Reinspection: recheck joint profile consistency and cleanliness across the full wall face.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a protected, cured, and documented practice wall, not an unrecorded pile of laid brick.

  • Confirm every deficiency identified during laying has a documented correction and reinspection result
  • Cover the finished wall as specified for the anticipated weather before leaving the work area
  • Return unused brick to staged storage and clean mortar boards, trowels, and jointers before mortar hardens on them
  • File the completed Brick Practice Wall Inspection Record with the Chapter 4 layout and Chapter 3 mortar batch record
  • Retain the wall, uncut, for reference in Chapter 9 corner and pier construction

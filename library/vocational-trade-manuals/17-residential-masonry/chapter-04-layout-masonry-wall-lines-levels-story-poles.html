Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to lay out one straight masonry wall or veneer section before any unit is set in mortar. The finished work product is a dimensionally verified layout: a marked baseline, corner or lead reference points, a story pole built from the actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness, course marks transferred to every control point, and a mason's line set and checked at the first course reference.

You will work from the wall dimensions, unit module, joint size, opening schedule, bond pattern, control-joint locations, reinforcement locations, flashing lines, and finished elevations already documented in the accepted Chapter 1 readiness plan. Nothing in this chapter authorizes you to spread mortar or set a unit permanently; this chapter produces the layout that Chapters 5 through 9 depend on for every course they lay.

Layout errors compound. A baseline off by a fraction of an inch, a story pole built from the wrong bed-joint thickness, or a mason's line set at the wrong offset will be repeated in every course above it. This chapter exists to catch those errors before the first unit is bedded in mortar, when correction costs nothing more than a pencil mark.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-019 — Establish the wall line, marking the baseline, ends, corners, intersections, openings, piers, control joints, and movement joints from approved references.
  • RM-020 — Set corner references, establishing lead points that control line and course elevation for the intervening wall.
  • RM-021 — Use mason's line, stretching, tensioning, and offsetting the line at each course reference without displacing its supports.
  • RM-022 — Create a story pole, building it from the actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness and marking every course, sill, lintel, flashing, tie, reinforcement, and cap elevation on it.
  • RM-023 — Verify course heights, checking cumulative course height against the wall and opening dimensions before laying begins.
  • RM-024 — Inspect layout accuracy, rechecking wall length, opening widths, corner positions, and course elevations and documenting the approved layout.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter uses the wall dimensions, support condition, and material identification recorded on the Chapter 1 Masonry-Readiness Worksheet and the work-area staging completed in Chapter 2. It assumes the mortar-mixing and unit-preparation skills from Chapter 3 will be applied once the layout in this chapter is approved.

Every following production chapter — the block practice wall in Chapter 5, the block corner and intersection work in Chapter 6, the brick practice wall in Chapter 8, brick corners and piers in Chapter 9, and both veneer chapters — begins by dry-laying to the baseline, corner references, and story pole this chapter produces. A layout error carried past this chapter is repeated in every course of the wall above it.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • establishing a wall baseline from approved references and marking ends, corners, intersections, openings, piers, control joints, and movement joints
  • dry-laying representative units without mortar and adjusting the layout to minimize small cuts while maintaining the required joint width
  • verifying opening dimensions and jamb modules against the approved schedule
  • establishing corner or lead reference points that control line and course elevation for the wall between them
  • building a story pole from the actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness, and marking every course, sill, lintel, flashing, tie, reinforcement, and cap elevation on it
  • verifying cumulative course height against the wall and opening dimensions, and preparing separate story poles when units or joint dimensions change
  • setting line blocks, pins, or leads, stretching mason's line at the first course reference, and checking line level and reference height
  • transferring course marks to every lead or control point and rechecking wall length, opening widths, corner positions, and course elevations before documenting the approved layout

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • spread mortar, set a unit permanently, cut units, install reinforcement, ties, or flashing, or begin production wall work; those tasks belong to later chapters
  • alter the wall dimensions, unit module, joint size, opening schedule, bond pattern, or elevation data supplied by the approved drawings or the Chapter 1 readiness plan
  • proceed on any retaining wall, load-bearing wall, foundation wall, reinforced masonry, tall freestanding wall, chimney, structural pier, or major veneer system without the exact engineered design and inspection sequence supplied by a qualified professional
  • begin layout on a base or support that has not passed the Chapter 1 inspection

Any dimension, module, or elevation conflict discovered during layout must be documented and resolved against the approved drawings before dry-laying continues.

Trade Note

A story pole is built once, from the actual units on hand, not from a nominal or catalog dimension. Two production runs of the same nominal unit can differ enough in actual height that a story pole built from a catalog figure produces a wall that drifts off its intended elevations by the top course. Always measure the units you will actually lay.

Setup Standard

Do not stretch mason's line or transfer course marks to any lead until the story pole in Step 9 has been built from actual unit measurements and the cumulative course height in Step 11 has been checked against the wall and opening dimensions. A layout that has not been rechecked in Step 19 must not be documented as approved.

Shop Tip

Dry-lay the full course length before marking a single permanent baseline point. Adjusting joint width across a dry-laid course to eliminate an unwanted small cut is far easier than discovering the same problem after the baseline and story pole are already fixed.

Module, Dry-Laying, and the Cost of an Uncorrected Layout Error

A wall's module is the combination of unit dimension and specified joint width that determines how many units fit a given wall length or opening width without an unplanned cut. Dry-laying a representative course before marking a permanent baseline is the only reliable way to confirm the module works for the actual wall length recorded in the approved drawings. Adjusting joint width slightly across a dry-laid course, within the tolerance stated in the approved drawings, is the correct response to an unwanted small cut. Changing the wall length, the opening width, or the specified joint-width tolerance to force a module to work is not.

Measurement Cue — Build the Story Pole From Actual Units

Measure the actual height of the units you will lay, not a nominal or catalog dimension, before marking the story pole. Add the specified bed-joint thickness to each unit height to establish the course mark. A story pole built from a catalog figure instead of measured units will drift off the intended elevations by the top of the wall.

Critical Safety Cue — Verified Base Required Before Layout

Do not begin baseline marking, corner establishment, or story-pole use on any base or support that has not passed the Chapter 1 inspection. If a base condition changes since that inspection — new cracking, settlement, contamination, or moisture — stop layout on that section and refer it back to the Chapter 1 acceptance standards before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Line and Lead Placement

Set line blocks, pins, or leads outside the active laying area so they do not create a tripping or falling-material hazard once production work begins. Keep dry-laid units stacked stably and clear of walkways while layout is in progress.

Code Alert — Opening and Movement-Joint Locations

Confirm opening locations, jamb modules, control joints, and movement joints against the approved drawings before marking the baseline. Movement-joint spacing and location are governed by the approved design; do not add, remove, or relocate a movement joint based on layout convenience.

E. Preparation — Data Review and Baseline Establishment

  1. Review wall dimensions, unit module, joint size, openings, bond pattern, control joints, movement joints, reinforcement, flashing, and finished elevations. Confirm every value is sourced from the approved drawings or the Chapter 1 readiness plan before marking begins.
  2. Verify support and base approval. Confirm the base or support carries an Approved or cleared-Conditional status from the Chapter 1 inspection before proceeding.
  3. Establish the wall baseline from approved references. Mark the baseline with a chalk line struck from the approved reference points, and record its location and length.
  4. Mark wall ends, corners, intersections, openings, piers, control joints, and movement joints. Transfer every location from the approved drawings to the baseline.

F. Dry-Laying and Module Verification

  1. Dry-lay representative units without mortar. Lay a full course along the baseline using the specified bond pattern and joint width.
  2. Adjust layout to minimize small cuts and maintain required joint width. Record any joint-width adjustment and confirm it remains within the tolerance stated in the approved drawings.
  3. Verify opening dimensions and jamb modules. Measure each opening in the dry-laid course and compare it against the approved opening schedule.

G. Corner References and Story-Pole Preparation

  1. Establish corner or lead reference points. Mark and square each corner or lead using the framing square and level, and record the location.
  2. Create a story pole using actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness. Measure the actual height of the units on hand and add the specified bed-joint thickness for each course mark.
  3. Mark each course, sill, lintel, flashing, tie, reinforcement, and cap elevation. Transfer every elevation from the approved drawings to the story pole in sequence.
  4. Verify cumulative course height against wall and opening dimensions. Compare the total marked height at the top of the pole against the approved wall or opening-head elevation.
  5. Prepare separate story poles when units or joint dimensions change. Build an additional story pole for any wall section using a different unit height or joint thickness.

H. Mason's-Line Setup and Course-Mark Transfer

  1. Set line blocks, pins, or leads outside the active laying area. Position supports so they do not obstruct movement once production laying begins.
  2. Stretch mason's line at the first course reference. Attach the line to the line blocks or pins at the marked first-course elevation.
  3. Apply only enough tension to maintain a straight line without moving supports. Check that the supports remain stationary under the applied tension.
  4. Offset the line from the unit face by the approved working distance. Confirm the offset matches the drawings or the established distance for the line-block system in use.
  5. Check line level and reference height. Verify the line is level using the 4-foot level and matches the marked first-course elevation.
  6. Transfer course marks to all leads or control points. Copy every story-pole mark to each corner, lead, or intermediate control point.
  7. Recheck wall length, opening widths, corner positions, and course elevations. Compare every dimension against the approved drawings before documenting the layout.
  8. Document the approved layout before laying units. Record the final approved status on the Masonry Layout Worksheet, supported by the recheck record and photographs.

Common Layout Inspection

Inspect every layout using the same checks regardless of the wall type or material.

  • Baseline check: confirm the baseline is straight within tolerance and every end, corner, opening, and joint location is marked and measured.
  • Module check: confirm the dry-laid course achieves the specified joint width within tolerance and every opening matches the approved schedule.
  • Corner check: confirm every corner or lead is square within tolerance and its location is recorded.
  • Story-pole check: confirm every course mark equals actual unit height plus specified bed-joint thickness and the cumulative height matches the approved elevation.
  • Line check: confirm the mason's line is level, correctly offset, and transferred to every lead or control point.
  • Recheck and approval: confirm every dimension was rechecked after the story pole and line were set, and that the layout is documented as approved only when every check passes.

Complete the Masonry Layout Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning production laying in Chapter 5 or later chapters.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Story Pole Was Built From a Nominal Unit Dimension

Probable cause: the pole was marked using a catalog or nominal unit height instead of measuring the actual units staged for the project.
Confirmation: measure several staged units directly and compare their actual height to the dimension used on the story pole.
Correction: rebuild the story pole using the measured actual unit height plus the specified bed-joint thickness.
Reinspection: recheck the cumulative course height against the wall and opening dimensions using the rebuilt pole.

Module Did Not Fit the Dry-Laid Wall Length

Probable cause: the wall length, unit dimension, and specified joint width were not checked together before the baseline was marked as final.
Confirmation: dry-lay the full course again and measure the resulting joint width at each head joint.
Correction: adjust joint width within the tolerance stated in the approved drawings to eliminate an unplanned small cut; if no adjustment within tolerance resolves the fit, refer the discrepancy to the responsible design authority rather than altering the wall length.
Reinspection: recheck the adjusted joint width across the full dry-laid course.

A Corner or Lead Was Out of Square

Probable cause: the corner was marked from the baseline without checking it against a framing square or diagonal measurement.
Confirmation: recheck the corner angle with a framing square or diagonal measurement against the approved angle.
Correction: re-mark the corner reference point and recheck square before building the story pole from it.
Reinspection: repeat the square check after correction and record the result.

Mason's Line Sagged or Moved Its Supports

Probable cause: excessive tension was applied to compensate for a long line run, or line blocks were not seated firmly before tensioning.
Confirmation: sight along the line for sag and check whether the line blocks or pins have shifted from their marked position.
Correction: reseat the line blocks or pins, reduce tension to the minimum needed for a straight line, and add an intermediate support if the run requires one.
Reinspection: recheck line level, offset, and support position after correction.

Course Marks Were Not Consistent Between Leads

Probable cause: course marks were transferred from the story pole to one lead but re-measured independently at another lead instead of being copied directly from the pole.
Confirmation: compare the course marks at every lead directly against the story pole rather than against each other.
Correction: re-transfer every course mark directly from the story pole to each lead or control point.
Reinspection: recheck course-mark alignment at every lead before stretching the mason's line for production laying.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, verified layout ready for production laying, not a baseline chalked from memory.

  • Confirm every photograph is labeled with the item, location, and date it documents
  • Store the story pole and mason's-line equipment in a protected location where they will not warp, bend, or be disturbed before production laying begins
  • File the completed Masonry Layout Worksheet with the Chapter 1 readiness plan and approved drawings it references
  • Leave the dry-laid units in place or restack them in sequence if the layout will be used immediately for production laying
  • Retain the worksheet, story pole, and photographs for use in Chapter 5 and every later production chapter for this wall

Field Terms

Baseline
The single straight reference line marked on the base or support from which the wall face and ends are measured.
Corner or Lead
A reference section at each wall end or intersection that controls line and course elevation for the wall between corners.
Story Pole
A length of stock marked with every course, sill, lintel, flashing, tie, reinforcement, and cap elevation, built from actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness.
Mason's Line
The tensioned line stretched between corners or leads at each course reference, offset from the unit face by the approved working distance.
Module
The combined unit dimension and joint width that determines how many units fit a given wall length or course height.
Dry-Lay
Positioning units without mortar to verify module, joint width, and fit before permanent laying begins.
Line Block
A device that anchors the mason's line at a corner or lead at the correct offset from the unit face.
Jamb Module
The unit and joint arrangement required to frame an opening at its specified width.
Movement Joint
A designed gap in a masonry wall that accommodates material expansion, contraction, or differential movement.
Masonry Layout Worksheet
The document recording every layout dimension, story-pole elevation, line setup, recheck result, and the final approved layout status.

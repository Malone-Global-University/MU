Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one small, nonretaining masonry garden-wall section from base preparation through capping and final inspection. The finished work product is one completed garden-wall section built to the wall dimensions, height, unit type, foundation or base, cap, reinforcement, drainage, and movement details supplied in the approved drawings for this project, together with a completed Garden-Wall Construction Record documenting base, course, drainage, and cap results.

You will excavate or prepare the approved base, lay out the wall line, lay the first course level and fully supported, build subsequent courses in the specified bond or stagger, install any specified reinforcement, grout, pins, adhesive, or connectors, provide the specified drainage and movement details, install cap units, tool and clean joints, cure and protect the wall, backfill only after cure, and inspect the completed wall against defined acceptance standards.

This chapter depends on the readiness plan from Chapter 1, the mortar and material preparation from Chapter 3, and the layout skills from Chapter 4. It does not authorize retaining-wall construction, load-bearing wall construction, or any wall exceeding the approved height and loading limits stated in the project drawings.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-091 — Prepare the wall base, including excavation or preparation of the approved base and installation of the specified aggregate or concrete footing.
  • RM-092 — Lay the first course, setting it level, straight, and fully supported on the prepared base.
  • RM-093 — Maintain level courses, checking wall plumb and course level at every course using the story pole and level.
  • RM-094 — Stagger joints, laying subsequent courses in the specified bond or stagger pattern.
  • RM-095 — Cap the wall properly, installing cap units with the specified slope, overhang, drips, joints, and adhesive or mortar.
  • RM-096 — Inspect the finished garden wall, verifying line, level, plumb, bond, cap, drainage, stability, and surrounding grade against the acceptance standards.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter combines the base inspection judgment from Chapter 1, the work-area staging from Chapter 2, the mortar preparation from Chapter 3, and the layout and story-pole skills from Chapter 4 into one complete, freestanding masonry structure built from the ground up. The bond-maintenance and plumb-checking skills used here are the same skills used in the Chapter 20 capstone, whether the capstone option selected is a garden wall or a veneer section.

The height, foundation, drainage, and stability limits enforced in this chapter exist because a garden wall that exceeds its approved scope becomes a retaining wall or a structural wall, which this book does not authorize without an engineered design and inspection sequence supplied separately.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing wall dimensions, height, unit type, foundation or base, cap, reinforcement, drainage, movement, and local permit limits from the approved drawings
  • confirming the wall is decorative and nonretaining, locating utilities, and verifying property limits
  • laying out the wall line, excavating or preparing the approved base, and installing the specified aggregate or concrete footing
  • dry-laying, adjusting module, and laying the first course level, straight, and fully supported
  • establishing end leads, stretching the mason's line, and laying subsequent courses in the specified bond or stagger while maintaining plumb and level
  • installing reinforcement, grout, pins, adhesive, or connectors only where specified, and providing movement and drainage details
  • installing cap units, tooling and cleaning joints, protecting and curing the wall, and backfilling only after the approved cure with specified material
  • inspecting line, level, plumb, bond, cap, drainage, stability, and surrounding grade, and documenting final wall dimensions and use limitation

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • build a retaining wall, load-bearing wall, or any wall used to hold back soil or slope without an engineered design and inspection sequence supplied separately
  • exceed the approved wall height, foundation dimensions, or loading limits stated in the project drawings
  • trap soil or water against an unapproved wall face, or omit drainage or movement details called for in the approved drawings
  • backfill against the wall before the specified cure period has elapsed or with material other than that specified

Any base, utility conflict, property-line question, or wall condition exceeding this scope must be documented and referred to the responsible design or permitting authority before work proceeds.

Trade Note

A garden wall fails most often not at the units but at the base and the cap: an under-prepared footing settles unevenly, and a poorly sloped or unbonded cap admits water into the wall core. Both defects are prevented, not repaired, so verify base compaction and cap slope before mortar sets rather than after.

Setup Standard

Do not proceed past Step 5 until utilities are located and property limits are verified. Do not proceed past Step 7 until the base has passed the level, width, and compaction check. A wall that fails the base check must be corrected and reinspected before the first course is laid.

Shop Tip

Check plumb and level after every course, not only at the end of the wall. A one-course drift caught immediately is corrected in minutes; the same drift discovered ten courses later requires disassembly.

Common Field Fundamentals

A small garden wall is a complete masonry system in miniature: it depends on a compacted, correctly sized base; a first course laid level and fully supported; consistent bond and plumb through every subsequent course; drainage and movement details appropriate to a freestanding wall; and a properly sloped cap. Skipping or shortcutting any one of these elements produces the same defects seen in larger masonry work — uneven settlement, water infiltration, and cracking — at a smaller and more visible scale.

Nonretaining Scope and Stop-Work Judgment

Before excavation begins, confirm in writing that the selected wall is decorative and nonretaining. A wall used to hold back soil, a slope, or lateral earth pressure is a retaining wall and is outside this chapter's scope unless an engineered retaining design and inspection sequence is supplied separately. Do not reclassify a retaining condition as decorative to avoid obtaining an engineered design.

Measurement Cue — Story Pole Controls Every Course

Mark the story pole with the actual unit height and specified bed-joint thickness before laying the first course, and check every course against it. Do not estimate course height by eye once several courses are complete; cumulative small errors compound quickly on a short wall.

Critical Safety Cue — Excavation and Utilities

Do not excavate for the wall base until buried utilities have been located and property limits verified. Stop excavation immediately if an unidentified utility, unstable soil, or unexpected buried obstruction is encountered, and do not resume until the condition is resolved by the responsible authority.

Field Safety Cue — Lifting and Unit Handling

Use mechanical assistance, carts, or team lifting for heavy block, stone, or cap units. Do not overload the base, adjacent trench edges, or staged material areas. Keep excavation spoil and staged units clear of the trench edge to prevent collapse or overload.

Code Alert — Height, Setback, and Permit Limits

Confirm the approved wall height, setback from property lines, and permit requirements before construction begins. Requirements vary by jurisdiction; verify locally rather than assuming a prior project's limits apply to this wall.

E. Site Review and Base Preparation

  1. Review wall dimensions, height, unit type, foundation or base, cap, reinforcement, drainage, movement, and local permit limits. Confirm every value against the approved drawings before excavation begins.
  2. Confirm the wall is decorative and nonretaining unless an engineered retaining design is provided. Record this confirmation on the Garden-Wall Construction Record.
  3. Locate utilities and verify property limits. Do not excavate until both are confirmed and documented.
  4. Lay out the wall line and ends. Mark the baseline, wall ends, and any corners using the chalk line and stakes referenced to the approved drawings.
  5. Excavate or prepare the approved base. Excavate to the depth and width specified, removing organic material and unsuitable soil.
  6. Compact subgrade and install the specified aggregate or concrete footing. Compact in the lift thickness specified and verify with the tamper or plate compactor.
  7. Verify base width, level, and elevation. Check with the level and tape measure against the story pole reference before proceeding; correct and recheck any deficiency before laying units.

F. First Course and Lead Construction

  1. Dry-lay the first course. Position units without mortar across the full wall length to verify fit.
  2. Adjust module and cuts. Adjust joint width within the specified range to minimize small cuts while preserving the wall's overall dimensions.
  3. Spread mortar or setting bed according to the wall system. Use the mortar or setting-bed material specified for this unit type and exposure.
  4. Set the first course level, straight, and fully supported. Verify full bearing on the prepared base with no unsupported unit corners or edges.
  5. Establish end leads. Build the wall ends first to control line and course elevation for the field of the wall.
  6. Stretch mason's line. Set the line at the course reference height between the completed leads before laying intervening units.

G. Course Construction, Reinforcement, and Drainage

  1. Lay subsequent courses with the specified bond or stagger. Maintain the pattern shown in the approved drawings without substituting a different bond.
  2. Maintain wall plumb and course level. Check both at every course using the level and the story pole reference.
  3. Install reinforcement, grout, pins, adhesive, or connectors only as specified. Do not add or omit reinforcement based on personal judgment; follow the approved drawings exactly.
  4. Provide movement and drainage details. Install movement joints and any drainage provisions at the locations shown in the approved drawings.
  5. Do not trap soil or water against an unapproved wall face. Verify that the finished grade and any drainage provisions direct water away from the wall base.

H. Capping, Cure, and Backfill

  1. Install cap units with slope, overhang, drips, joints, and adhesive or mortar as specified. Verify the cap sheds water away from the wall face per the approved detail.
  2. Tool and clean joints. Tool exposed joints at the correct firmness and brush away loose mortar without smearing the face.
  3. Protect and cure. Protect the wall from rain, freezing, rapid drying, and impact for the full specified cure period.
  4. Backfill only after the approved cure and only with specified material. Do not backfill early or substitute an unspecified backfill material.

I. Final Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect line, level, plumb, bond, cap, drainage, stability, and surrounding grade. Record each result against the Acceptance Standards table.
  2. Document final wall dimensions and use limitation. Record as-built length, height, base width, and cap dimensions, and restate the wall's decorative, nonretaining use limitation on the Garden-Wall Construction Record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed garden-wall section using the same checks regardless of unit type or bond pattern.

  • Base check: confirm the base was compacted, leveled, and verified before the first course was laid, with photographs and measurements on record.
  • Course check: confirm every course was checked for level and plumb against the story pole, not estimated by eye.
  • Reinforcement check: confirm any specified reinforcement, grout, pins, adhesive, or connectors were installed at the exact specified locations.
  • Drainage check: confirm no soil or water is trapped against the wall face and that specified movement and drainage details are present.
  • Cap and cure check: confirm the cap sheds water away from the wall, joints are fully tooled, and the wall was protected through the full specified cure period before backfilling.

Complete the Garden-Wall Construction Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before the wall is handed off for use.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Wall Rocks or Settles Unevenly After a Few Courses

Probable cause: the base was not compacted to the specified depth, or the aggregate or concrete footing was placed on uncompacted or unsuitable subgrade.
Confirmation: check the base preparation record and photographs against the approved drawings; probe the base at the affected location to check for soft spots.
Correction: where the wall is still uncured, remove the affected section, recompact or reconstruct the base to specification, and relay the removed units. Where the wall has cured, refer to Chapter 15 repair procedures rather than patching in place.
Reinspection: repeat the base level and compaction check and the affected course-level check before proceeding.

Course Height Drifts From the Story Pole Marks

Probable cause: joint thickness was not held consistent, or units of varying height were mixed without adjustment.
Confirmation: measure cumulative course height against the story pole at the affected location.
Correction: before mortar sets, adjust joint thickness within the specified range to return to the story pole marks. After mortar sets, do not force alignment; document the deviation and correct on subsequent courses within the tolerance stated in the approved drawings.
Reinspection: recheck course height at each subsequent course until the wall returns to the story pole reference.

Water Pools Against the Wall Base After Rain

Probable cause: surrounding grade was not verified to slope away from the wall, or a specified drainage detail was omitted.
Confirmation: inspect the surrounding grade slope and compare the drainage details actually built against the approved drawings.
Correction: regrade the surrounding soil to direct water away from the wall base, or install the omitted drainage detail as specified.
Reinspection: observe drainage during or immediately after a rain event and confirm no ponding remains at the wall base.

Cap Units Do Not Shed Water Away From the Wall Face

Probable cause: cap units were set without the specified slope or overhang, or bedding mortar was not fully compacted under the cap.
Confirmation: check cap slope with a level against the specified value and inspect for voids under the cap by sounding or visual inspection at joints.
Correction: reset the affected cap units with full bedding and the specified slope and overhang before the bedding material cures.
Reinspection: recheck cap slope and bedding at the corrected location and observe water shedding during the next rain event.

Backfill Was Placed Before the Wall Fully Cured

Probable cause: the cure-period log was not checked before backfilling began.
Confirmation: compare the backfill date against the cure-period log and the specified minimum cure period.
Correction: where backfilling before cure has caused visible displacement or cracking, remove the backfill, inspect the wall for damage, and refer to Chapter 15 repair procedures if damage is found.
Reinspection: confirm the wall shows no displacement or cracking before backfill is reapplied after the full cure period.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, capped, cured, and inspected wall, not a wall left unprotected or partially backfilled.

  • Remove excess mortar, mixing residue, and waste from the work area without discharging washout to drains or waterways
  • Return excavation, compaction, and lifting equipment to storage
  • Confirm every photograph is labeled with the item, location, and date it documents
  • File the completed Garden-Wall Construction Record with the approved drawings and Chapter 1 readiness plan it references
  • Retain the record for use in future maintenance, inspection, or Book 18 hardscaping coordination

Field Terms

Nonretaining Wall
A freestanding masonry wall not designed or used to hold back soil, slope, or lateral earth pressure.
Lead
An end section of wall built first and used to control line and course elevation for the wall between leads.
Story Pole
A marked reference pole showing actual unit height and specified joint thickness, used to control course elevation.
Bond or Stagger Pattern
The specified arrangement of unit overlap from course to course.
Cap Unit
The unit installed at the top of a wall to shed water and terminate the assembly.
Movement Joint
A designed gap or joint that allows a masonry assembly to accommodate expansion, contraction, or differential movement.
Cure Period
The specified time during which mortar or grout must be protected before it is loaded, backfilled, or exposed to freezing or rapid drying.
Garden-Wall Construction Record
The document recording base preparation, course construction, reinforcement, drainage, cap, cure, and final inspection results for the wall.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one brick corner lead and one small nonstructural pier, both governed by the readiness plan from Chapter 1, the work-area setup from Chapter 2, the mortar batch from Chapter 3, and the layout, story pole, and mason's-line references from Chapter 4. The finished work product is one completed corner lead built from the practice wall started in Chapter 8, and one freestanding brick pier built to its approved height limit and capped.

Chapter 8 taught running-bond brick laying along a straight wall face. This chapter extends that skill to the two conditions every brick project eventually requires: a corner, where two wall faces meet and both directions must read plumb, level, and square at once; and a pier, where every face of a single freestanding column must be controlled simultaneously. Both conditions concentrate more risk of drift, bond error, and instability into a smaller area than a straight wall run.

Nothing in this chapter authorizes work on a structural pier, a load-bearing corner, or any brick assembly identified in the Chapter 1 readiness plan as load-bearing, retaining, or requiring an engineered design. This chapter is limited to the nonstructural practice corner and the small pier described in the Project Specification Sheet.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-049 — Lay brick corners, establishing both wall directions from a single corner unit and maintaining bond across the turn.
  • RM-050 — Build masonry leads, racking back each course from the corner in the specified stepped pattern to control line and elevation for later infill work.
  • RM-051 — Construct a brick pier, maintaining square, bond, and interlocking coursing on all four faces within the approved freestanding height limit.
  • RM-052 — Maintain plumb on every corner face and every pier face throughout construction, not only at the top course.
  • RM-053 — Maintain consistent bed and head joints across the corner lead and the pier, matching the joint width and tooling profile established in Chapter 8.
  • RM-054 — Inspect the finished corner and pier for bond, square, plumb, stability, cap or termination, joint condition, and surface cleanliness.

Where This Skill Fits

Corner leads control line and course elevation for every wall section built between them; the mason's line used in Chapter 8 and future veneer chapters is stretched from lead to lead, not laid free-hand along the whole wall length. A corner lead built out of plumb or off bond will transfer that error into every course of infill work that follows.

The pier assembly in this chapter is a self-contained exercise in four-face control. The same discipline — checking every face, not just the one facing the mason's line — applies directly to Chapter 16's garden-wall piers, Chapter 11's veneer corner leads, and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the approved corner bond, pier dimensions, bond pattern, reinforcement or core details, cap, and freestanding height limit before laying begins
  • verifying that both bases are level, square, stable, and adequately sized for the corner lead and the pier
  • dry-laying and adjusting the unit and joint module at both first courses
  • building a stepped, racked-back corner lead with bond, level, square, and plumb maintained on both wall directions
  • building a small nonstructural pier with interlocking coursing, level, plumb on all four faces, and a specified cap or termination
  • tooling, cleaning, protecting, and inspecting both assemblies

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • build any pier, corner, or column identified as structural, load-bearing, or requiring an engineered design without that design and its inspection sequence
  • exceed the approved freestanding height for the pier without bracing or a design that specifically permits the additional height
  • install reinforcement, ties, or grout in the pier beyond what the chapter outline specifies; reinforced and grouted assemblies are governed by Chapter 7
  • use this corner lead or pier as a structural component of any occupied building; both assemblies remain nonstructural practice work

Any base, square, or stability condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected and reinspected before mortar is spread.

Trade Note

A corner or pier concentrates four directions of control — level, plumb, square, and bond — into a small footprint. Checking only the face nearest the mason's line, and assuming the other faces are correct because the first one looks right, is the single most common cause of an out-of-square corner or a leaning pier discovered only after several courses are laid.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the corner lead or the pier until the Chapter 1 base approval and the Chapter 4 layout record for this location are on hand, and until both bases have been reverified level, square, stable, and adequately sized under Step 2 of this chapter's procedure.

Shop Tip

Check plumb on both corner faces, and on all four pier faces, every single course — not only every few courses. A one-course lean is a five-minute correction. A five-course lean is a teardown.

Common Field Fundamentals

A corner and a pier both require simultaneous control of more directions than a straight wall run. Establishing that control at the base, and rechecking it every course, prevents the drift that a straight-wall mason may not notice until several courses have accumulated.

Base checkLevel, square, stable, and adequately sized before any unit is laid
Corner bondBoth wall directions read correctly from the same corner unit
Rack-backEach course stepped back the specified amount, never an unsupported tooth
Pier squarenessAll four faces checked, not only the line-facing face
PlumbChecked every course on every face of both assemblies
Course heightVerified against the story pole at both assemblies
Height limitPier does not exceed the approved freestanding height without bracing or design
Cap and inspectionSpecified cap installed; bond, square, plumb, stability, and joints inspected

Corners, Leads, and Piers Are Not Ordinary Wall Runs

A corner lead controls the line and elevation of every course laid between it and the next lead; an error at the lead is carried into all connecting work. A pier is checked on every face because no adjacent wall run exists to visually confirm alignment. Treat both assemblies with the same care given to the readiness inspection in Chapter 1 and the layout in Chapter 4 — verify before proceeding, and do not assume a condition is correct because it was correct on a previous project.

Measurement Cue — Check Every Face, Every Course

Check plumb on both corner faces and all four pier faces at every course, not at intervals. Check the corner angle with a square or diagonal measurement at the base and again after several courses. A single unchecked course can carry an error upward that is far harder to correct once additional courses are laid on top of it.

Critical Safety Cue — Freestanding Height Limit

Do not exceed the approved freestanding height for the pier without installing the specified bracing or without an engineered design that permits the additional height. An unbraced pier built beyond its approved freestanding height is unstable and can topple, causing serious injury. Stop construction at the approved height and install the specified cap or termination.

Field Safety Cue — Working Near a Freestanding Assembly

Do not lean on, brace against, or store materials against the pier or corner lead while mortar is uncured. Keep the work area around both assemblies clear of tripping hazards, and do not allow foot traffic to strike the corner arris or pier faces before the mortar has reached adequate strength.

Code Alert — Structural Use

This chapter's corner and pier are nonstructural practice assemblies. Do not incorporate either into an occupied structure, a load-bearing condition, or a retaining application without the exact engineered design, reinforcement schedule, and inspection sequence required for that use. Local requirements vary; verify before repurposing this practice work.

D. Preparation — Base Verification and Layout

  1. Review corner or intersection detail, bond pattern, reinforcement, anchors, wall thickness, and height. Confirm the pier detail separately, including its plan dimensions, bond or core arrangement, cap, and freestanding height limit.
  2. Verify base dimensions and square. Recheck both the corner base and the pier base for level, square, stability, and adequate size using the level, square, and tape measure; do not proceed on a base that fails this check.
  3. Mark both wall baselines. Use the Chapter 4 layout record to mark the corner baselines; mark the pier footprint separately using a chalk line and the approved pier plan dimensions.

E. Corner Lead Construction

  1. Dry-lay the first-course corner units. Place units without mortar along both wall directions from the corner point to confirm module and joint width.
  2. Adjust layout for correct overlap and modular dimensions. Shift units as needed to minimize cuts while preserving the specified bond and joint width.
  3. Spread mortar for the corner lead. Spread and furrow a mortar bed sized for the corner unit and the first few units in each direction.
  4. Set the first corner block to level, square, and elevation. Tap the corner unit to the story-pole elevation, check level in both directions, and check the corner angle with a square before proceeding.
  5. Lay units extending in both wall directions. Butter head joints and set units outward from the corner along each wall line, maintaining full bed and head joints.
  6. Check the corner angle with square or diagonal controls. Reverify the angle after the first course is complete in both directions.
  7. Build the lead one course at a time. Complete each course fully before beginning the next; do not build one direction significantly ahead of the other.
  8. Rack back each course using the approved stepped pattern. Step each course back by the specified amount on the racked side; do not leave an unsupported tooth.
  9. Maintain bond and overlap. Confirm the bond pattern reads correctly across the corner turn at every course.
  10. Use the story pole to control course elevation. Check every course against the story pole marks transferred from Chapter 4.
  11. Check plumb on both faces and the corner arris. Use the mason's level on each wall face and along the corner arris at every course.
  12. Install intersecting-wall ties, bond units, reinforcement, or anchors as specified. Install only where the approved corner detail requires them; do not add unspecified reinforcement.
  13. Do not create an unapproved tooth or unsupported intersection. Every racked-back course must remain fully bearing on the course below it.
  14. Stretch mason's lines from the lead for connecting wall work. Set line blocks at the lead and stretch the line toward the connecting wall run once the lead has reached its required course count.
  15. Keep the lead only the permitted number of courses above adjacent work. Confirm this limit against the approved corner detail; do not build the lead excessively far ahead of the infill work it will guide.
  16. Tool joints at the proper time. Tool bed and head joints when thumbprint hard, matching the profile established in Chapter 8.
  17. Inspect corner square, plumb, bond, rack-back, ties, and course height. Complete this inspection before considering the lead finished.
  18. Correct drift before mortar hardens. Adjust any unit found out of plumb, level, or bond while the mortar remains workable; do not leave a known drift condition for later correction.
  19. Document the completed intersection. Record final course count, height, angle measurement, and photographs on the Corner and Pier Work Record.

F. Pier Construction

  1. Review pier dimensions, bond pattern, reinforcement or core details, cap, and height limit. Confirm these match the Project Identification Record before laying begins.
  2. Verify the pier base is level, square, stable, and adequately sized. Recheck independently of the corner base verification in Step D.2.
  3. Dry-lay the pier first course to the approved bond. Confirm the plan dimensions and bond or core arrangement match the approved detail.
  4. Verify pier dimensions and square. Check all four sides and both diagonals of the first course before spreading mortar.
  5. Continue pier courses with interlocking bond or approved core arrangement. Maintain the specified bond so that each course interlocks with the course below on every face.
  6. Install reinforcement, ties, or grout only where specified. Do not add reinforcement, ties, or grout beyond what the approved pier detail requires; a fully grouted, reinforced pier is governed by Chapter 7, not this chapter.
  7. Check each course for level. Verify level across all faces of the pier at every course.
  8. Check all pier faces for plumb. Use the mason's level on every face, not only the face nearest the work area entrance, at every course.
  9. Rotate or blend brick for consistent appearance. Select units to maintain uniform color and texture across all visible faces.
  10. Maintain full bed and head joints. Confirm joint width and fullness match the standard established in Chapter 8.
  11. Do not exceed the approved freestanding height without bracing or design. Stop construction at the approved freestanding height limit unless the specified bracing is installed or an engineered design permits additional height.
  12. Install the specified cap or termination. Install the exact cap unit or capping detail specified in the approved pier detail; do not substitute a different termination.
  13. Tool and clean joints. Tool bed and head joints on all faces and remove excess mortar without smearing.
  14. Protect the assemblies through cure. Cover both the corner lead and the pier as required by the anticipated weather conditions identified in the Chapter 1 readiness plan.
  15. Inspect bond, square, plumb, stability, cap, joints, and surface condition. Complete this inspection on both assemblies before considering the chapter project finished.
  16. Document final measurements. Record final pier height, plan dimensions, and cap installation on the Corner and Pier Work Record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect both the corner lead and the pier using the same checks regardless of which assembly is reviewed first.

  • Base check: confirm both bases were verified level, square, stable, and adequately sized before mortar was spread.
  • Angle and square check: confirm the corner angle and the pier's four sides and diagonals were checked at the base and rechecked as courses were added.
  • Plumb check: confirm every face of both assemblies was checked for plumb at every course, not at intervals.
  • Bond and rack-back check: confirm the corner rack-back pattern and the pier's interlocking bond match the approved detail with no unapproved tooth or aligned vertical joints.
  • Height-limit check: confirm the pier does not exceed the approved freestanding height without the specified bracing or design.
  • Termination check: confirm the specified cap or termination is installed and matches the approved detail.
  • Stability check: confirm both assemblies show no rock, shift, or movement after adequate cure.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Corner Angle Drifts Out of Square After Several Courses

Probable cause: the corner angle was checked only at the first course and not rechecked as the lead rose, allowing small unit or joint variations to accumulate.
Confirmation: recheck the angle with a square or diagonal measurement at the current course and compare against the recorded first-course measurement.
Correction: identify the course where the drift began, remove units above that course if mortar has not fully set, and rebuild with the angle rechecked at every course.
Reinspection: repeat the angle check at every course going forward until the lead is complete.

One Pier Face Reads Plumb While an Adjacent Face Leans

Probable cause: plumb was checked only on the face nearest the work area or the mason's line, and the adjacent faces were assumed correct.
Confirmation: check all four pier faces individually with the mason's level at the current course and at several courses below it.
Correction: while mortar remains workable, adjust the leaning face back to plumb; if mortar has set, remove and relay the affected courses.
Reinspection: check all four faces at every subsequent course before continuing.

The Rack-Back Pattern Leaves an Unsupported Tooth

Probable cause: a course was stepped back by an amount different from the pattern specified in the approved corner detail, or units were laid with insufficient bearing on the course below.
Confirmation: compare the built rack-back pattern against the approved corner detail, course by course, and check bearing at each step.
Correction: remove and relay any course that does not match the specified pattern or that lacks full bearing.
Reinspection: verify the corrected rack-back matches the approved detail before continuing the lead.

The Pier Rocks or Shifts After Cure

Probable cause: the base was not adequately level or stable before laying began, or a lower course was disturbed before the mortar reached sufficient strength.
Confirmation: check the base level and condition beneath the pier, and inspect lower joints for cracking or separation.
Correction: stop use of the pier, document the condition, and do not proceed to further chapters using this assembly until the base and affected courses are corrected and reinspected; treat any condition suggesting base failure as a Chapter 1 stop-work referral.
Reinspection: recheck stability by hand after the correction has fully cured.

The Pier Exceeds Its Approved Freestanding Height Before the Cap Is Installed

Probable cause: course count was not checked against the approved freestanding height limit before additional courses were added.
Confirmation: measure the current pier height and compare it against the approved freestanding height limit in the Project Identification Record.
Correction: stop laying immediately; do not add further courses without installing the specified bracing or obtaining an engineered design permitting the additional height.
Reinspection: confirm the pier height at the point work stopped and document the corrective action taken before resuming.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with two protected, cured, documented masonry assemblies, not a verbal impression of their condition.

  • Remove excess mortar from the mortarboard and clean all tools before mortar sets
  • Brush loose mortar crumbs from both the corner lead and the pier after tooling
  • Cover both assemblies with the protection specified for the anticipated weather conditions
  • Store the mason's line, line blocks, story pole, and squaring tools for reuse in Chapter 10 and later chapters
  • File the completed Corner and Pier Work Record with the Chapter 1 readiness plan and the Chapter 4 layout record for this location

Field Terms

Corner Lead
Stepped masonry built up first at a wall corner, guiding the mason's line for infill wall courses.
Rack-Back
A stepped reduction in course length as a lead rises, used to control elevation without an unsupported tooth.
Corner Arris
The vertical edge where two wall faces of a corner meet.
Pier
A freestanding masonry column built with interlocking coursing on every face.
Freestanding Height Limit
The maximum unbraced height a pier may reach before bracing or an engineered design is required.
Cap or Termination
The specified finishing detail that completes the top of a pier or wall.
Interlocking Bond
A bond pattern in which units overlap across faces so no continuous vertical joint runs through the assembly.
Line Blocks
Devices that hold the mason's line at the corner lead while infill courses are laid.
Diagonal Check
A method of verifying square by comparing the two diagonal measurements of a rectangular layout.
Stop-Work Condition
A condition, such as pier instability or an unresolved base failure, that halts work until a qualified professional evaluates it.

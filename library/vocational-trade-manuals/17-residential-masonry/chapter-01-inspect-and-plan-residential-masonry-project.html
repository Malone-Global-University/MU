Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect a proposed masonry project and produce a documented masonry-readiness plan. The finished work product is one completed Masonry-Readiness Worksheet covering project classification, base and support condition, drainage and moisture exposure, wall condition, openings and reinforcement, access and staging, utility and flue conflicts, a stop-work determination where applicable, material identification, weather controls, a preliminary quantity and crew plan, a deficiency list, a reinspection record, and a final readiness decision.

You will select one proposed masonry project — block, brick, stone, veneer, repair, chimney-exterior-maintenance, or small nonretaining wall — and inspect it in the field before any masonry unit, mortar, or tool is staged. Nothing in this chapter authorizes you to lay masonry, mix mortar, cut units, or perform chimney, flue, or structural work; this chapter produces the planning document that later chapters and later projects depend on.

This is the first chapter in the book. Every later chapter in this book — mixing mortar, laying out a wall, building block or brick, installing veneer and flashing, repairing masonry, building a garden wall or steps, inspecting a chimney, cleaning and sealing, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on a completed, accurate readiness plan for the selected project.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-001 — Identify masonry project type, classifying it as load-bearing, nonload-bearing, veneer, freestanding, retaining, repair, chimney-related, step-related, or decorative.
  • RM-002 — Inspect substrate or base conditions, including footing, slab, shelf angle, ledge, framing, sheathing, or existing masonry support.
  • RM-003 — Evaluate drainage needs, including grade, roof runoff, splashback, cap details, flashing, weeps, foundation drainage, and freeze-thaw exposure.
  • RM-004 — Identify structural concerns, including wall displacement, bulging, cracking, failed joints, spalls, failed lintels, and loose chimney sections.
  • RM-005 — Select masonry materials, identifying units, mortar, grout, reinforcement, ties, flashing, weeps, sealants, cleaners, and caps specified for the project.
  • RM-006 — Document project readiness, issuing an approved, conditional, or rejected masonry-readiness decision supported by a written deficiency list and inspection record.

Where This Skill Fits

Every project in this book begins the same way: with an inspection of what already exists before a single masonry unit is staged. The readiness plan you produce here defines the base condition, drainage strategy, and material list that Chapter 2 uses to stage the work area, that Chapter 3 uses to select mortar, and that Chapter 4 uses to lay out the wall.

The stop-work determination you practice in this chapter — recognizing settlement, structural movement, unidentified flues, and unsafe access before work begins — is the same judgment used throughout the book, including in the Chapter 18 chimney inspection and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • classifying a proposed masonry project by structural role and work type
  • inspecting an existing base, footing, slab, shelf angle, ledge, or support for level, width, cracks, settlement, contamination, moisture, and bearing
  • inspecting surrounding grade, drainage, and moisture sources
  • inspecting existing masonry, where present, for alignment, bulging, displacement, cracking, failed joints, spalls, staining, and prior repairs
  • identifying openings, lintels, ties, anchors, reinforcement, movement joints, and service or roof interfaces from approved drawings and visual inspection
  • inspecting access, delivery routes, scaffold conditions, mixing area, washout, and weather exposure
  • identifying utility, flue, and structural conflicts
  • identifying masonry units, mortar, grout, reinforcement, ties, flashing, weeps, sealants, cleaners, and caps specified for the project
  • preparing a preliminary quantity, staging, crew, access, and protection plan
  • preparing a deficiency list, reinspecting corrected items, and issuing a readiness decision

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • lay masonry units, mix or place mortar or grout, cut masonry, or install veneer, flashing, or reinforcement; those tasks belong to later chapters
  • proceed on any retaining wall, load-bearing wall, foundation wall, reinforced masonry, tall freestanding wall, chimney, fireplace, structural pier, or major veneer system without the exact engineered design and inspection sequence supplied by a qualified professional
  • alter chimney flues, liners, smoke chambers, fireplaces, dampers, combustion-air systems, vent connectors, or gas appliances under this chapter
  • disturb, remove, or repair any masonry, flashing, or structural element discovered to be unstable, displaced, or actively moving; such conditions are documented and referred, not corrected, in this chapter

Any base, support, wall, or chimney condition that fails the inspection in this chapter must be documented, corrected by a qualified party where required, and reinspected before masonry work begins.

Trade Note

Every finding in this chapter is recorded on the Masonry-Readiness Worksheet at the point it is observed — never reconstructed from memory afterward. A finding written down later, away from the base or wall it describes, is the single most common source of the incomplete readiness plans diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not stage masonry units, mortar, mixing equipment, or cutting tools until the readiness decision in Step 19 has been issued as Approved or Conditional and every prerequisite in Step 17 has been reinspected and cleared. A project that receives a Rejected decision must not proceed to Chapter 2.

Shop Tip

Photograph every deficiency before you write its description on the worksheet. Deciding what a deficiency "probably means" before it is fully documented is how conditional approvals turn into rework once masonry is already staged.

Project Classification and Stop-Work Judgment

The project-type classification recorded in Step 2 controls how the rest of the inspection is read. A retaining wall, load-bearing wall, foundation wall, reinforced masonry, tall freestanding wall, chimney, fireplace, structural pier, or major veneer system is never treated as ordinary practice work in this book unless the exact engineered design and inspection sequence are supplied. Record the classification before evaluating any other item, and do not change it later to make a deficient condition appear less serious.

Measurement Cue — Record Findings at the Point of Observation

Record every dimension, moisture reading, and condition directly on the worksheet at the location it was observed. Do not wait until the end of the inspection to reconstruct measurements from memory; delayed recording is the leading cause of incomplete or contradictory readiness plans.

Critical Safety Cue — Unstable Conditions

Stop the inspection of any item showing unstable foundations, active settlement, major wall displacement, bulging walls, failed lintels, widespread cracking, loose chimney sections, fire damage, unidentified flue conditions, or movement requiring structural evaluation. Do not touch, tap, climb on, or apply load to a wall or chimney element showing these conditions. Photograph the condition from a safe distance and refer it to the responsible design or inspection authority identified in the Project Identification Record.

Field Safety Cue — Access and Overhead Hazards

Do not enter crawlspaces, climb on roofs, chimney tops, improvised platforms, or unprotected edges during this inspection. Inspect chimney tops, high walls, and roof-level flashing from the ground using binoculars. Keep clear of any wall or chimney section showing visible displacement while photographing it.

Code Alert — Permit and Inspection Authority

Confirm whether the selected project requires a permit, inspection, engineer, architect, surveyor, mason, chimney specialist, fireplace professional, scaffold specialist, or structural repair contractor before the readiness decision is issued. Requirements vary by jurisdiction and project type; verify locally rather than assuming the requirements of a prior project apply.

C. Documentation Requirements

  • Photographs: at least one photograph of the base or support, the surrounding grade and drainage, any existing masonry condition, and every recorded deficiency.
  • Drawing references: a citation to the specific drawing sheet, detail, or specification section supporting every material and dimension identified in Step 13 and Step 14.

E. Preparation — Documents and Project Selection

  1. Obtain approved drawings, site information, wall sections, foundation or ledge details, drainage details, material specifications, and permit requirements. Confirm every document is the current approved version before the inspection begins.
  2. Define whether the project is load-bearing, nonload-bearing, veneer, freestanding, retaining, repair, chimney-related, step-related, or decorative. Record this classification on the Project Identification Record before proceeding.
  3. Identify the responsible design and inspection authority. Record the name and contact information of the engineer, architect, building department, or other authority of record for the project.

F. Base, Support, and Drainage Inspection

  1. Inspect the base, footing, slab, shelf angle, ledge, framing, sheathing, or existing masonry support. Identify the exact support element the new or repaired work will bear on.
  2. Check support for level, width, cracks, settlement, contamination, moisture, and adequate bearing. Use the level, tape measure, and moisture meter and record every reading on the Inspection Item Schedule.
  3. Inspect surrounding grade and drainage. Confirm the grade slopes away from the work area and record any standing water, ponding, or reversed slope.
  4. Identify water sources, roof runoff, splashback, cap details, flashing, weeps, foundation drainage, and freeze-thaw exposure. Record the location of every identified water source relative to the proposed work.

G. Existing Masonry, Openings, and Conflict Inspection

  1. Inspect wall alignment, bulging, displacement, cracking, failed joints, spalls, staining, and previous repairs. Photograph every condition found and record it as Pass, Deficient, or Stop-Work.
  2. Identify openings, lintels, ties, anchors, reinforcement, movement joints, service penetrations, and roof or wall interfaces. Cross-reference each against the approved drawings.
  3. Inspect access, material-delivery routes, scaffold conditions, lifting needs, mixing area, washout, and weather exposure. Record measured clearances for delivery and staging areas.
  4. Identify electrical, gas, plumbing, drainage, flue, and structural conflicts. Mark the location of every identified conflict on the site sketch or drawing.
  5. Stop work for disputed support, unidentified flues, major structural cracking, active movement, loose chimneys, failed lintels, or unsafe access. Classify each stopped item per the Acceptance Standards table and record the required professional referral.

H. Materials, Weather Controls, and Preliminary Planning

  1. Identify masonry units, mortar, grout, reinforcement, ties, flashing, weeps, sealants, cleaners, and caps specified for the project. Cite the specific drawing, specification section, or manufacturer instruction for each.
  2. Verify unit dimensions, absorption, finish, color range, and compatibility. Record each value with its source document.
  3. Determine cold-, hot-, wet-, and windy-weather controls. Record the specific protection method required for the anticipated construction season.
  4. Prepare a preliminary quantity, staging, crew, access, and protection plan. Base every quantity on the component schedule in the approved drawings; do not estimate quantities without a source document.

I. Deficiency Resolution and Readiness Decision

  1. Prepare a deficiency and prerequisite list. Compile every item recorded as Deficient or Stop-Work into a single written list with location, description, and required correction or referral.
  2. Reinspect corrected items. Return to each corrected deficiency, verify the correction using the same tool or method used in the original inspection, and record the reinspection date and result.
  3. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected masonry-readiness decision. Base the decision solely on the Acceptance Standards table; a project with any unresolved stop-work condition must be classified Rejected.
  4. Document professional referrals and inspection hold points. Record every professional referral made during the inspection and every hold point that must be cleared before the corresponding later chapter begins.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every readiness plan using the same checks regardless of the selected project type.

  • Classification check: confirm the project-type classification was recorded before the base inspection began and was not changed afterward to minimize a deficient finding.
  • Base and drainage check: confirm level, width, cracking, settlement, contamination, moisture, bearing, grade, and water-source findings are recorded with measurements and photographs.
  • Structural check: confirm existing wall condition, openings, reinforcement, and any stop-work condition are recorded and, where required, referred to a qualified professional.
  • Material check: confirm every identified unit, mortar, grout, reinforcement, tie, flashing, weep, sealant, cleaner, and cap is traced to a specific source document.
  • Decision check: confirm the final readiness decision matches the Acceptance Standards table and that every deficiency has a documented correction or referral.

Complete the Masonry-Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Findings Were Reconstructed From Memory After the Site Visit

Probable cause: dimensions, moisture readings, or conditions were not recorded on the worksheet at the point of observation.
Confirmation: compare the worksheet entries against the site photographs; missing measurements or vague descriptions indicate reconstruction rather than direct recording.
Correction: return to the site and reinspect every item lacking a directly recorded measurement, photograph, or drawing citation.
Reinspection: repeat the affected inspection items and update the worksheet with directly recorded findings.

A Stop-Work Condition Was Recorded as an Ordinary Deficiency

Probable cause: the condition was not compared against the Acceptance Standards table's stop-work list before classification.
Confirmation: re-review the recorded condition against the stop-work list; unstable foundations, active settlement, major displacement, bulging, failed lintels, widespread cracking, loose chimney sections, fire damage, unidentified flues, or movement requiring structural evaluation must be classified Stop-Work, not Deficient.
Correction: reclassify the item as Stop-Work, halt further inspection of that item, and record the required professional referral.
Reinspection: confirm the referral was made and do not proceed to a readiness decision until the referral is resolved.

A Material Was Selected Without a Source Document

Probable cause: the inspector estimated a unit, mortar type, or fastener based on general trade practice rather than citing the approved drawings, specification, or manufacturer instruction.
Confirmation: check the Materials line of the worksheet for a specific drawing sheet, specification section, or manufacturer document reference for each item.
Correction: obtain the missing source document before finalizing the material list; if no document exists, record the material as undetermined and add it to the deficiency list.
Reinspection: repeat the material identification check once the source document is obtained.

The Readiness Decision Does Not Match the Recorded Findings

Probable cause: the decision was issued before all deficiencies were reinspected, or a Rejected-level condition was overridden to Conditional.
Confirmation: compare the final decision against the Acceptance Standards table's decision definitions and the completed deficiency and reinspection records.
Correction: reissue the decision using only the Acceptance Standards table's criteria; any unresolved stop-work condition requires a Rejected decision regardless of the size or cost of the project.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected decision is supported by a complete deficiency list and reinspection record before proceeding to Chapter 2.

Access or Drainage Conditions Were Overlooked

Probable cause: the inspection focused only on the wall or base and did not walk the full delivery, staging, and drainage path around the work area.
Confirmation: walk the site again and compare the Inspection Item Schedule entries for access, staging, and drainage against the actual site conditions.
Correction: record the overlooked conditions, add any resulting deficiencies to the deficiency list, and resolve them before the readiness decision is finalized.
Reinspection: repeat the access and drainage checks and update the worksheet.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, filed readiness plan, not a verbal impression of the site.

  • Confirm every photograph is labeled with the item, location, and date it documents
  • File the completed Masonry-Readiness Worksheet with the approved drawings and specifications it references
  • Store professional referral correspondence with the worksheet
  • Return borrowed tools (level, moisture meter, camera) to storage
  • Retain the worksheet and photographs for use in Chapter 2 staging and every later chapter for this project

Field Terms

Project Type
The single controlling classification of a masonry project by structural role and work category.
Base or Support
The footing, slab, shelf angle, ledge, framing, sheathing, or existing masonry that new or repaired work will bear on.
Deficiency
An inspected condition that does not meet the stated acceptance standard and requires correction or referral.
Stop-Work Condition
A deficiency serious enough to halt inspection of that item until a qualified professional evaluates it.
Readiness Decision
The final classification of a project as Approved, Conditional, or Rejected.
Freeze-Thaw Exposure
The degree to which a masonry assembly is subject to repeated freezing and thawing of retained moisture.
Shelf Angle
A structural steel angle that supports masonry veneer at floor lines or other interruptions in the backup wall.
Weep
An opening in a mortar joint or flashing system that allows drainage water to exit a masonry cavity.
Masonry-Readiness Worksheet
The document recording every inspection finding, deficiency, reinspection result, and the final readiness decision.
Professional Referral
The documented act of directing a stop-work condition to an engineer, architect, or other qualified professional rather than resolving it in this chapter.

Performance Check

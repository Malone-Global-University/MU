Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build a plumb, square, bonded concrete-masonry corner lead and a T-intersection practice assembly. The finished work product is one stepped corner lead and one intersecting-wall assembly, each built to the approved bond pattern, rack-back detail, course elevations, and tie or reinforcement schedule, and each inspected before mortar hardens.

Chapter 5 taught you to build a straight nonstructural block practice wall using bed joints, head joints, and a story pole. This chapter extends that skill to the point where two walls meet — a corner, where one wall turns a angle, and an intersection, where a second wall joins the first as a T. Corners and intersecting walls control the line and course elevation for every unit laid between them; a corner lead built out of square or out of level carries that error into every wall section that follows it.

Nothing in this chapter authorizes structural or load-bearing corner and intersection work beyond the approved practice-assembly detail supplied for this project. This is nonstructural practice work built to demonstrate bond, plumb, square, rack-back, and tie placement.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RM-031 — Lay block corners, setting the first-course corner unit to level, square, and elevation and building the lead one course at a time.
  • RM-032 — Rack back courses, stepping each successive course of the lead in the approved pattern while maintaining bond and overlap.
  • RM-033 — Tie intersecting walls, installing intersecting-wall ties, bond units, reinforcement, or anchors exactly as specified at the T-intersection.
  • RM-034 — Maintain plumb, checking both wall faces and the corner arris at each course using the level.
  • RM-035 — Maintain bond pattern, preserving the specified overlap and joint alignment through the corner and intersection.
  • RM-036 — Inspect corner strength and alignment, verifying square, plumb, bond, rack-back, ties, and course height before continuing adjacent wall work.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 established the layout, corner references, and story pole for a masonry wall. Chapter 5 applied bed joints, head joints, and course control to a straight run. This chapter applies that same course control to the two conditions every real block wall eventually reaches: a turned corner and an intersecting wall. The lead you build here is what a mason's line is stretched from in every subsequent wall section, so its square, plumb, and elevation control the accuracy of everything built between leads.

Chapter 7 depends on the reinforcement and grouting skills you demonstrate placing ties and anchors at the intersection in this chapter. Chapter 9 repeats this corner-and-lead logic in brick. The Chapter 20 capstone assumes the learner can build and check a corner lead without supervision.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • laying out and squaring both wall baselines for a corner or T-intersection
  • setting the first-course corner unit to level, square, and elevation
  • building a stepped corner lead one course at a time with the approved rack-back pattern
  • maintaining bond, overlap, and course height using the story pole through the lead
  • checking plumb on both wall faces and the corner arris at each course
  • installing intersecting-wall ties, bond units, reinforcement, or anchors exactly as specified at a T-intersection
  • stretching mason's lines from the completed lead for adjoining wall work
  • inspecting corner square, plumb, bond, rack-back, ties, and course height before mortar hardens

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • build a load-bearing corner, retaining-wall corner, foundation-wall corner, or any structural intersection without the exact engineered design and inspection sequence supplied by a qualified professional
  • create an unapproved tooth or unsupported intersection not shown in the approved corner or intersection detail
  • build the lead more than the permitted number of courses above adjacent wall work before that adjacent work is brought up to match
  • place grout, vertical reinforcement, or embedded anchors beyond what the approved detail for this practice assembly specifies; structural reinforcement and grouting are covered in Chapter 7

Any corner or intersection that fails square, plumb, bond, or tie inspection during this chapter must be corrected before the mortar hardens, or removed and rebuilt.

Trade Note

A corner lead is a reference, not just a piece of wall. Every unit laid between two leads is checked against the line stretched from them. An out-of-square or out-of-plumb lead does not stay a local error — it is transferred into every course of the wall built from it. Check square and plumb at the lead before stretching the line, not after the intervening wall is built.

Setup Standard

Do not begin laying the corner lead until the base has passed the Chapter 1 readiness inspection, both wall baselines have been verified square, and the story pole from Chapter 4 is on hand and matches this assembly's unit height and joint thickness. Do not stretch a mason's line from a lead that has not yet been checked for square, plumb, and course elevation.

Shop Tip

Check the corner angle with a diagonal measurement before laying the second course, not after the lead is several courses high. A square error caught at course one is corrected by resetting one unit; the same error caught at course six requires rebuilding the lead.

Common Field Fundamentals

A corner lead is built to establish two things that the rest of the wall depends on: the corner angle and the course elevation. Every unit laid in the intervening wall is checked against the line stretched from the lead, so an error in the lead is transferred into the entire wall run. An intersection adds a third requirement — a tie or anchor connection between two walls that must accommodate differential movement while transferring the specified load or restraint, exactly as the approved detail requires.

Corner and Intersection Judgment

Do not create an unapproved tooth or unsupported intersection. If the approved detail does not show a specific tie, anchor, bond-unit, or rack-back arrangement for a condition encountered in the field, stop and obtain the missing detail rather than improvising an interlock. Do not build the lead more than the number of courses above adjacent wall work permitted by the approved detail; an overextended lead is unsupported and unstable.

Measurement Cue — Check the Corner Angle Before the Second Course

Verify the corner angle with a framing square or diagonal measurement immediately after the first-course corner unit is set, before laying the second course. Recheck the diagonal at each lead course. A corner angle error compounds with height; catch it at the base.

Critical Safety Cue — Lead Height Above Adjacent Work

Do not build the corner lead more than the number of courses above adjacent wall work permitted by the approved detail. An overextended, unsupported lead can be struck or can shift under its own weight before adjoining courses are laid. Stop work and bring adjacent wall sections up to the lead before continuing higher.

Field Safety Cue — Working at Two Wall Faces

Checking plumb on both wall faces and the corner arris requires moving around the lead repeatedly. Keep the work area at the base of the lead clear of loose units, tools, and mortar-board debris so this movement does not create a trip hazard.

Code Alert — Intersecting-Wall Tie Product and Spacing

Confirm the specified intersecting-wall tie, bond unit, or anchor product and its spacing against the approved detail and manufacturer instructions before installation. Tie type and spacing requirements vary by wall system and jurisdiction; do not substitute a different product or spacing without written approval.

Manufacturer Cue — Tie and Anchor Installation

Install intersecting-wall ties, bond units, or anchors using the embedment depth, orientation, and fastening method stated in the manufacturer's instructions for the specified product. Do not bend, cut, or reposition a tie or anchor in a way the manufacturer's instructions do not permit.

F. Layout and Base Verification

  1. Review corner or intersection detail, bond pattern, reinforcement, anchors, wall thickness, and height. Confirm the detail is the current approved version and matches the assembly being built.
  2. Verify base dimensions and square. Measure both wall baselines and check the included angle with a framing square or diagonal measurement before laying any unit.
  3. Mark both wall baselines. Chalk-line both baselines from the Chapter 4 reference points.
  4. Dry-lay the first-course corner units. Position units without mortar to confirm module and joint width in both wall directions.
  5. Adjust layout for correct overlap and modular dimensions. Shift the dry-laid units as needed to minimize cuts while preserving the specified joint width.

G. Corner Lead Construction

  1. Spread mortar for the corner lead. Spread and furrow a bed sized for the first two or three units at the corner.
  2. Set the first corner block to level, square, and elevation. Tap the unit to the story-pole mark, check level in both directions, and verify square against the second baseline before continuing.
  3. Lay units extending in both wall directions. Butter and set units along each baseline from the corner unit.
  4. Check the corner angle with square or diagonal controls. Recheck the diagonal measurement before laying the second course.
  5. Build the lead one course at a time. Complete and check each course before beginning the next.
  6. Rack back each course using the approved stepped pattern. Step each successive course back from the one below per the approved bond and rack-back detail.
  7. Maintain bond and overlap. Confirm head-joint offset matches the specified bond pattern at every course.
  8. Use the story pole to control course elevation. Mark and verify each course against the story-pole reference.
  9. Check plumb on both faces and the corner arris. Use the 4-foot level on each wall face and along the corner arris at every course.

H. Intersection Construction and Tie Installation

  1. Install intersecting-wall ties, bond units, reinforcement, or anchors as specified. Place the specified tie or anchor at the exact course and spacing shown in the approved detail, following manufacturer embedment and orientation instructions.
  2. Do not create an unapproved tooth or unsupported intersection. Build only the interlock or tie condition shown in the approved detail; stop and obtain the missing detail if a field condition is not covered.
  3. Stretch mason's lines from the lead for connecting wall work. Set line blocks or pins at the completed lead and stretch the line to the corresponding point at the far end of the intervening wall.
  4. Keep the lead only the permitted number of courses above adjacent work. Confirm the course count above adjacent work against the approved detail before continuing the lead higher.
  5. Tool joints at the proper time. Tool exposed bed and head joints when the mortar is thumbprint-hard, matching the specified profile.

I. Inspection and Correction

  1. Inspect corner square, plumb, bond, rack-back, ties, and course height. Complete the Acceptance Standards checks for every course of the lead and the intersection.
  2. Correct drift before mortar hardens. Reset any unit that fails square, plumb, bond, or elevation checks while the mortar remains workable.
  3. Document the completed intersection. Record final measurements, tie locations, and inspection results on the work order.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every corner and intersection assembly using the same checks regardless of the specific bond pattern specified.

  • Square check: confirm the corner angle was verified before the second course and rechecked at intervals through the lead.
  • Plumb check: confirm both wall faces and the corner arris were checked with the level at every course, not only at the top of the completed lead.
  • Bond and rack-back check: confirm the bond pattern and rack-back step match the approved detail at every course, with no unapproved aligned vertical joints.
  • Tie check: confirm every intersecting-wall tie, bond unit, or anchor is installed at the specified course, spacing, and embedment, with no substituted product.
  • Course-height check: confirm every course matches the story-pole mark for that course.
  • Lead-height check: confirm the lead does not exceed the permitted number of courses above adjacent wall work.

Complete the Corner and Intersection Inspection Record below before stretching a mason's line to intervening wall sections.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Corner Angle Drifts Out of Square as the Lead Rises

Probable cause: the first-course corner unit was set without verifying square against both baselines, or the diagonal measurement was not rechecked at subsequent courses.
Confirmation: measure the diagonal at the current course and compare it against the approved tolerance; a growing deviation confirms an uncorrected first-course error.
Correction: if the mortar is still workable, reset the affected units to correct square; if hardened, remove and rebuild the lead from the first course showing the error.
Reinspection: recheck the diagonal at every course from the corrected point upward before continuing.

A Wall Face or the Corner Arris Is Out of Plumb

Probable cause: units were tapped to line without checking plumb at each course, allowing a small lean to accumulate.
Confirmation: check the level along the full height of the affected face and at the corner arris; compare the reading against the story pole and the acceptance tolerance.
Correction: reset the out-of-plumb units while mortar remains workable; do not attempt to correct accumulated lean by tapping only the top course.
Reinspection: recheck plumb on both faces and the arris at every course above the correction.

Bond Pattern or Rack-Back Does Not Match the Approved Detail

Probable cause: the rack-back step was estimated by eye rather than measured against the approved detail, or units were laid in running bond where the detail specified a different pattern.
Confirmation: compare the head-joint offset and rack-back step of each course against the approved detail's bond diagram.
Correction: remove and relay the courses that do not match the specified pattern.
Reinspection: recheck bond and rack-back at every course from the correction point upward.

An Intersecting-Wall Tie Is Missing, Substituted, or Improperly Embedded

Probable cause: the tie schedule was not checked against the approved detail before laying the course, or a different tie or anchor was used because the specified product was not on hand.
Confirmation: compare the installed tie type, course, spacing, and embedment against the approved detail and manufacturer instructions.
Correction: if mortar is still workable, remove the unit, install the correct tie, and relay the unit; if hardened, remove and rebuild the affected course with the specified tie.
Reinspection: verify tie type, course, spacing, and embedment at every intersection course before continuing.

The Lead Is Built Too Many Courses Above Adjacent Wall Work

Probable cause: the lead was continued upward without bringing the intervening wall sections up to match, exceeding the permitted course difference in the approved detail.
Confirmation: count the course difference between the lead and the highest adjacent wall section and compare it against the approved detail's limit.
Correction: stop building the lead higher and lay the intervening wall sections up to the permitted course difference before continuing.
Reinspection: recheck the course difference before resuming work on the lead.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with an inspected, cured corner lead and intersection, not an assembly left to cure unchecked.

  • Remove excess mortar from both wall faces and the corner arris without smearing
  • Confirm every tooled joint is clean and free of loose mortar crumbs
  • Protect the completed lead and intersection from rain, freezing, and impact during cure
  • Store mason's line, line blocks, level, and story pole for the next work session
  • File the completed inspection record with the Chapter 4 layout documentation for this project

Field Terms

Lead
The corner or end section of a wall built ahead of the intervening wall to establish line and course elevation.
Rack-Back
The stepped pattern in which each successive course of a lead is set back from the course below it.
Corner Arris
The vertical outside edge formed where two wall faces meet at a corner.
Tooth
An unapproved, unsupported step or interlock at an intersection not shown in the approved detail.
Intersecting-Wall Tie
A metal tie, bond unit, or anchor connecting an intersecting wall to a primary wall at a T-intersection.
Diagonal Check
A measurement of the diagonal distance across a laid-out rectangle or corner, used to verify square.
Story Pole
A reference pole marked with course heights, used to control elevation through a lead or wall section.
Mason's Line
A taut line stretched between leads or reference points to guide the alignment of intervening courses.

Field Cue System

✋

⛑

Job Overview

This project produces one controlled subcontractor, supplier, and vendor management system for a residential contracting company. The finished work product is a single Partner Qualification and Purchasing File, supported by a subcontract scope template, a purchase-order template, a vendor contact and pricing log, a performance scorecard, and a payment and closeout control sheet.

You will identify which services and products require outside partners, separate subcontractors from suppliers and vendors, build qualification criteria for each partner type, verify license, insurance, and bond status, create attorney-reviewed subcontract and purchase-order templates, build fraud and payment-verification controls, and set up performance tracking and requalification procedures.

This project depends on the business model in Chapter 1, the licensing matrix in Chapter 3, the insurance file in Chapter 4, the accounting controls in Chapter 5, the scope library in Chapter 6, and the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7. It feeds directly into the job-start packet in Chapter 13, the change-order system in Chapter 10, the job-cost review in Chapter 18, and the final closeout in Chapter 19.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the service menu and scope library in Chapter 6, the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7, and the banking and accounting controls in Chapter 5. A subcontract issued without a verified license and current insurance certificate, or a purchase order issued without a verified vendor and confirmed payment instructions, converts a controlled pricing and scope decision into an unqualified and unverified financial and legal exposure.

The qualification and documentation discipline built here is used again in the job-start packet in Chapter 13 to confirm subcontractor agreements before mobilization, in the change-order system in Chapter 10 to price and authorize subcontractor-performed changes, in the job-cost review in Chapter 18 to compare committed subcontractor cost against actual, and in the closeout process in Chapter 19 to confirm subcontractor and supplier payment and lien-release documentation before customer handoff.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying which services, trades, products, equipment, and professional functions require an outside partner
  • separating subcontractors, suppliers, manufacturers, fabricators, equipment-rental companies, consultants, and service vendors, and building qualification criteria specific to each
  • collecting and verifying legal name, ownership, license, registration, insurance, bond, and disciplinary information for each partner
  • creating an attorney-reviewed subcontract scope template and a purchase-order template linked to the company scope library
  • creating vendor contact, pricing-history, credit-application, and fraud-verification records
  • creating delivery, receiving, performance-scorecard, invoice, payment, retainage, lien-release, warranty, and requalification procedures

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • award a subcontract or purchase order to any partner whose license, registration, insurance, or bond status has not been verified through the procedure in Section G
  • approve a subcontract price, term, or clause that has not been reviewed under the applicable jurisdiction's attorney-reviewed subcontract template
  • rely on this chapter for legal, tax, insurance, or licensing advice regarding any specific partner or contract; every jurisdiction-specific requirement must be verified with the applicable authority or a qualified professional
  • provide a personal guarantee, indemnity, or credit application on the company's behalf without the review required in Section H

Any partner whose qualification cannot be verified, or whose insurance, license, or bond has lapsed, must be moved to suspended status and removed from active bidding until the deficiency is corrected and documented.

Trade Note

A certificate of insurance, a business card, an online listing, or a verbal assurance is never a complete qualification. Every partner record in this chapter is built from verified license status, verified insurance terms confirmed with the broker or carrier, and documented references — never from the partner's own unverified representation.

Setup Standard

Do not issue a subcontract or purchase order to any partner until its status in the Partner Qualification and Purchasing File is Approved. A partner in Conditional, Probationary, Suspended, or Rejected status must not be awarded work until the specific deficiency is corrected and re-verified.

Shop Tip

Build the vendor fraud-verification step — an independent callback to a known contact before changing any banking or payment instruction — into the purchasing procedure now, not after the first attempted diversion. This single control is the most common gap found in subcontractor and vendor fraud losses.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every subcontract, purchase order, and vendor account created after this project is built from the same four written controls defined here: who is qualified to do the work, what scope and price they are authorized to perform it for, how their insurance and license status is confirmed before and during the relationship, and how their performance and payment are tracked to closeout.

Qualification Discipline

A candidate subcontractor, supplier, or vendor is only moved to Approved status after its license, registration, insurance, and (where applicable) bond status have been independently verified — never accepted solely on the partner's certificate, business card, or verbal assurance. A partner that fails verification is moved to Rejected or Conditional status with the specific deficiency recorded, not silently added to the active list.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Verification, Not Just the Result

When a license, insurance certificate, or bond is verified in Section F, record the verification date, the method used, the specific policy or license number confirmed, and the name of the person who performed the verification. Do not simply mark the partner "verified" without this record; the record is what protects the company if the partner's status later lapses.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Award Without Verified Insurance and License

Do not issue a subcontract or purchase order to any partner whose license, insurance certificate, and required endorsements have not been independently verified through the broker or carrier and the licensing authority. A certificate presented by the partner is not, by itself, verification.

Operational Discipline Cue — One File, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled Partner Qualification and Purchasing File in the company record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow estimators, project managers, or field supervisors to maintain separate informal partner lists; every award must reference the single approved file.

Professional Advisor Cue — Contract Terms and Guarantees Require Review

Subcontract and purchase-order terms must use attorney-reviewed language for the applicable jurisdiction. Personal guarantees, indemnity clauses, and credit applications must be reviewed by counsel and, where financial exposure is involved, a CPA or financial advisor, before they are signed.

E. Partner Identification and Categorization

  1. Identify which services, trades, equipment, products, and professional functions require outside partners. Record each on the Partner Qualification and Purchasing File index, cross-referenced to the Chapter 6 scope library item it supports.
  2. Separate subcontractors, suppliers, manufacturers, fabricators, equipment rental companies, consultants, and service vendors. Assign each candidate partner to exactly one category on the index.
  3. Create qualification criteria by partner type. Write a specific checklist of required license, insurance, bond, reference, and capacity items for each category before evaluating any individual partner.

F. Due Diligence and Verification

  1. Collect legal name, trade name, address, contacts, ownership, taxpayer information, and authorized signer. Record each item in the partner's file before any verification step begins.
  2. Verify business registration, contractor license, trade license, local registration, and disciplinary status where applicable. Use the official licensing-authority resource identified in Chapter 3 and record the verification date and result.
  3. Verify insurance certificate and required endorsements through approved procedures. Contact the broker or carrier directly; do not rely on the certificate alone.
  4. Confirm policy dates, classifications, limits, exclusions, workers' compensation, auto, and completed operations with professional guidance. Record the confirming broker's name and the date of confirmation.
  5. Verify bond, permit, training, certification, and manufacturer status where required. Record the verifying source and date for each.
  6. Collect references, project history, capacity, workforce, equipment, service area, safety information, and quality history. Contact at least the number of references required by the company's qualification criteria for that partner type.
  7. Review worker-classification risk and operational independence with counsel or tax professionals. Record the reviewing professional's name, date, and conclusion.
  8. Do not approve a subcontractor solely because it offers the lowest price. Record the basis for approval — qualification results and references, not price alone.
  9. Create an approved, conditional, probationary, suspended, and rejected status. Assign one status to each partner and record the specific basis for that status.

G. Scope, Purchasing, and Vendor Controls

  1. Create a subcontract scope template linked to the project documents and Chapter 6 scope library. Reference the specific scope-library identifier for each standard subcontracted service.
  2. Define inclusions, exclusions, material, labor, equipment, permits, safety, quality, schedule, changes, billing, lien documents, warranty, and closeout. Complete each field in the template before it is used on any project.
  3. Use attorney-reviewed subcontract terms applicable to the jurisdiction. Do not insert unreviewed clauses copied from another state's form or a generic internet template.
  4. Create purchase-order templates for materials, equipment, rentals, and services. Include a distinct template section for each purchase type where terms differ.
  5. Define pricing, quantity, unit, freight, tax, delivery, unloading, return, damage, warranty, and lead time. Complete each field before the template is used.
  6. Create a vendor contact and account record. Record account number, primary contact, and authorized company purchasers for each vendor.
  7. Create a quotation and pricing-history log with effective and expiration dates. Update the log each time a new quotation is received.
  8. Create credit application and personal-guarantee review procedures. Route every credit application through the Section H professional-review requirement before signature.
  9. Do not provide personal guarantees without authorized legal and financial review. Record the reviewing attorney or CPA's name and date before any guarantee is signed.
  10. Create vendor fraud controls for banking and payment changes. Require a documented independent callback before any payment-instruction change is accepted.
  11. Independently verify payment instructions through a known contact. Use a phone number or contact on file before this request, never one supplied in the change request itself.
  12. Create purchase approval and commitment limits. State the specific dollar threshold requiring owner or manager approval before a purchase order is issued.
  13. Create delivery appointment, receiving, inspection, discrepancy, damage, return, and storage procedures. Assign a responsible person for receiving and inspection on each project.

H. Performance, Payment, and Requalification

  1. Create subcontractor startup and project-orientation requirements. Confirm each subcontractor completes orientation before site access, consistent with the Chapter 15 onboarding controls.
  2. Create performance scorecards for safety, quality, schedule, communication, changes, documentation, and closeout. Complete one scorecard per partner per completed project.
  3. Record deficiencies factually and provide correction opportunities according to contract. Avoid editorial or speculative language in the scorecard record.
  4. Create invoice, payment, retainage, tax, insurance, lien-release, and closeout controls. Confirm each required document is received before final payment is released.
  5. Do not pay invoices that cannot be matched to approved scope, delivery, progress, or documentation. Route unmatched invoices to the responsible project manager for resolution before payment.
  6. Create warranty and callback responsibility tracking. Record the subcontractor's or supplier's warranty term and contact information in the partner file.
  7. Create annual and project-specific requalification. Set a recurring requalification date for every Approved partner and an incident-triggered requalification review for safety, quality, or compliance events.
  8. Create suspension procedures for expired license, insurance, unsafe work, fraud, nonperformance, or unresolved claims. State the specific condition that triggers suspension and the person authorized to lift it.
  9. Review the system with attorney, insurer, accounting, procurement, safety, and operations. Record each reviewer's name, date, and comments.
  10. Approve the partner records and assign ownership for updates. Sign, date, and store the completed Partner Qualification and Purchasing File and its supporting templates in the Chapter 2 company record system, and name the person responsible for keeping it current.

Common Model Inspection

Inspect the completed partner file and its supporting templates using the same checks regardless of which trade, product, or service is involved.

  • Identification check: confirm every service, trade, product, and professional function requiring an outside partner is listed and correctly categorized.
  • Qualification check: confirm license, insurance, bond, and reference verification is independently documented with date, method, and verifying person for every Approved partner.
  • Template check: confirm the subcontract and purchase-order templates are attorney-reviewed for the applicable jurisdiction and include every required field.
  • Fraud-control check: confirm the payment-instruction verification procedure is documented and has been tested.
  • Performance check: confirm a scorecard exists for at least one completed project per active partner category.
  • Requalification check: confirm every Approved partner has a scheduled requalification date on the calendar.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Subcontractor's Insurance or License Has Lapsed Mid-Project

Probable cause: the requalification calendar did not include this partner, or a renewal reminder was not sent far enough in advance.
Confirmation: check the partner's file for the most recent verification date and compare it against the current license and policy expiration dates.
Correction: suspend the partner from new work, notify the project manager and insurer as applicable, and do not resume work until current license and insurance status is verified and documented.
Reinspection: repeat the qualification check on the corrected file and confirm the requalification calendar now includes an advance reminder.

A Purchase Was Made From an Unverified or Unapproved Vendor

Probable cause: a field employee or estimator issued a purchase order directly without checking partner status, or the approval-limit control was bypassed.
Confirmation: compare the purchase order against the Partner Qualification and Purchasing File and the approval-limit record.
Correction: route future purchases for that category through the approved vendor list and reinforce the purchase-approval limits with the responsible personnel.
Reinspection: repeat the purchasing-control check on the next several purchase orders in that category.

A Vendor Payment Was Sent to a Changed Bank Account Without Verification

Probable cause: the independent callback-verification step was skipped under time pressure, or the change request appeared to come from a trusted contact.
Confirmation: review the payment record and accounting fraud-control log for evidence the callback verification was completed and documented.
Correction: immediately notify the bank and the vendor through a known contact, escalate to counsel and insurer as applicable, and retrain accounting personnel on the mandatory callback procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the fraud-control check on the next several payment-instruction changes across all vendor categories.

A Subcontract Was Signed Using an Unreviewed Generic Template

Probable cause: a project manager used a template copied from another jurisdiction or found online instead of the attorney-reviewed company template.
Confirmation: compare the signed subcontract against the current attorney-reviewed subcontract scope template for the applicable jurisdiction.
Correction: route the discrepancy to counsel for review of the executed agreement and correct the field process to require use of the controlled template only.
Reinspection: repeat the template check on the next subcontract issued in that jurisdiction.

A Partner With a Documented Safety or Quality Deficiency Was Rehired Without Review

Probable cause: the performance scorecard was completed but not consulted before the next award, or the partner's status was not updated to Probationary.
Confirmation: review the partner's scorecard history and current status field against the award decision.
Correction: update the partner's status to reflect the documented deficiency and require the specific correction condition be met before further work is authorized.
Reinspection: repeat the qualification-status check before the next award to that partner.

Final Payment Was Released Without Required Lien-Release Documentation

Probable cause: the payment and closeout control sheet was not consulted, or the requirement was treated as optional under schedule pressure.
Confirmation: review the accounting record for the required lien-release, waiver, or affidavit before the final payment date.
Correction: obtain the missing documentation before any further payment to that partner and route the gap to counsel for jurisdiction-specific correction.
Reinspection: repeat the payment-control check on the next several final payments.

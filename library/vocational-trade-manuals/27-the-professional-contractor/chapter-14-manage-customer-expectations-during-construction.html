Job Overview

This project produces one operating customer-communication process for a single active residential project. The finished work product is a controlled communication calendar, a standard project-update format, a documented selections log, a complaint-intake and response procedure, and a preserved job-file record of every material conversation, decision, disruption notice, and change discussed with the customer while work is underway.

You will confirm the customer's authorized decision makers, build a communication calendar tied to the project schedule, report progress accurately without concealing delay, explain disruption before it happens, route every scope request through the Chapter 10 change system, document every owner decision in writing, handle complaints through a defined intake-to-closure procedure, and preserve every meaningful conversation in the job file.

This project runs from job start through final walkthrough. It depends on the signed contract and scope of work from Chapter 9, the change-order and allowance system from Chapter 10, and the job startup packet from Chapter 13. It feeds directly into the final walkthrough, punch list, invoice, and warranty handoff in Chapter 19.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-079 — Communicate schedule updates using baseline, forecast, and commitment language that does not conceal known delay.
  • PC-080 — Explain disruption points to the customer with reasonable advance notice before the disruption occurs.
  • PC-081 — Manage change requests by routing every scope request through the approved change-order procedure.
  • PC-082 — Document owner decisions, selections, refusals, and access restrictions in writing at the time they are made.
  • PC-083 — Handle complaints professionally through a defined intake, investigation, and closure procedure.
  • PC-084 — Preserve communication records in a complete, organized, retrievable job file.

Where This Skill Fits

This project begins the moment the job-start packet from Chapter 13 is approved and mobilization is authorized. It runs continuously through the active-job cost review in Chapter 18 and closes into the final walkthrough, punch list, and warranty handoff in Chapter 19. Every schedule statement, disruption notice, decision record, and complaint response produced here becomes part of the job file that Chapter 19 relies on to reconcile the final account and Chapter 18 relies on to investigate cost variance tied to customer-caused delay or scope change.

The same discipline used in Chapter 10 — writing a decision down before acting on it — controls this project. A verbal understanding with a customer is not a record. Only a written, dated entry in the communication log or job file counts as documentation for this project's acceptance standards.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the customer's authorized decision makers and preferred communication channels at project start
  • building a communication calendar for kickoff, weekly updates, selections, inspections, billing, walkthrough, and closeout
  • reporting completed work, current work, next work, decisions needed, deliveries, inspections, changes, risks, and access conditions in a standard format
  • explaining upcoming noise, dust, odor, utility shutdown, access, delivery, parking, security, and room-use disruptions with reasonable advance notice
  • documenting selections, owner decisions, refusals, deferrals, and access restrictions in writing
  • routing every scope request through the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance system
  • operating a complaint intake, investigation, and closure procedure
  • escalating claims, threats, injury, damage, discrimination, legal, insurance, or media matters to authorized professionals
  • preserving emails, texts, platform messages, meeting notes, photographs, approvals, and notices in the job file

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • promise free added work, schedule changes, or substitutions outside the Chapter 10 change-order procedure
  • present an optimistic target completion date as a guaranteed date, or conceal a known delay from the customer
  • threaten lien, collection, permit, utility, or abandonment action outside an attorney-approved procedure
  • condition a legally required correction or warranty response on a positive review
  • disclose private customer or project information while responding to a complaint or negative review
  • treat this chapter as legal advice for overdue-payment, lien, or dispute matters; every jurisdiction-specific procedure must be verified with counsel

Any communication that cannot be supported by the signed contract, the approved scope of work, or a properly authorized change order must be corrected before it is sent to the customer.

Trade Note

Every schedule update, disruption notice, decision, and complaint response in this chapter is recorded in the job file at the time it occurs — never reconstructed from memory afterward. An unrecorded conversation is the single most common source of the "he said, she said" disputes diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not allow field personnel, sales staff, or office staff to make a schedule, price, scope, warranty, or payment-term promise to the customer outside this chapter's process. A promise made outside the process must be corrected in writing with the customer before it is relied upon.

Shop Tip

Summarize every material verbal conversation with the customer in writing the same day it happens, and send that summary to the customer. A same-day written summary the customer does not dispute becomes strong documentation; a summary written weeks later from memory does not.

Communication Discipline

A schedule date, disruption notice, or decision is only considered communicated when it appears in the job file in writing. A conversation that happened only by phone or in person is documented the same day with a written summary sent to the customer. If the customer does not dispute the summary within a reasonable time stated in the summary itself, the summary stands as the record of that conversation.

Documentation Cue — Summarize Every Material Verbal Conversation

After any phone call, site conversation, or meeting that touches schedule, price, scope, selections, access, or a complaint, write a same-day summary and send it to the customer through the established communication channel. File a copy in the job file. Do not rely on memory of the conversation at a later date.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Field-Level Promises Outside the Change System

Do not allow field personnel to promise free added work, a schedule acceleration, a substitution, or a payment-term change during a site conversation. Any such request must be routed through the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance system before it is confirmed to the customer.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Job File, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled job file for this project's communications, consistent with the company record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow separate, informal message threads to substitute for the job file. Chapter 18 and Chapter 19 both depend on this single record.

Professional Advisor Cue — Overdue Payment and Dispute Language Requires Review

Overdue-payment, lien, work-stoppage, and dispute-escalation communications must use attorney-approved language for the applicable jurisdiction. Do not send an unreviewed threat of lien, collection, permit action, or abandonment.

E. Communication Setup

  1. Confirm the customer's authorized decision makers and preferred communication channels. Record names, roles, and contact methods on the communication calendar template before the first update is sent.
  2. Review the contract, scope, schedule, payment, allowance, change, warranty, and notice procedures. Confirm the communication process will not contradict any approved contract term.
  3. Create the customer communication calendar for kickoff, weekly updates, selections, inspections, billing, walkthrough, and closeout. Populate every planned contact point with a target date.
  4. Create a standard project-update format. Fix the fields used in every update so reports remain consistent and comparable over time.

F. Schedule and Disruption Reporting

  1. Report completed work, current work, next work, decisions needed, deliveries, inspections, changes, risks, and access conditions. Use the standard format for every update issued on the communication calendar.
  2. State schedule dates as baseline, current forecast, or commitment accurately. Label every date given to the customer with one of these three terms and do not blend them.
  3. Do not conceal known delay or present an optimistic target as a guaranteed completion date. Report a known delay in the next scheduled update, not after the date has already passed.
  4. Explain upcoming noise, dust, odor, utility shutdown, access, delivery, parking, security, and room-use disruptions. Identify the specific disruption, its expected duration, and the specific rooms or areas affected.
  5. Provide reasonable advance notice according to contract and circumstances. Send disruption notices far enough ahead that the customer can plan around them.
  6. Confirm customer responsibilities and decision deadlines in writing. State the specific action required of the customer and the date by which it is needed.

G. Decisions, Selections, and Change Routing

  1. Document selections with product, color, model, price effect, lead time, and approval. Record every selection on the selections and owner-decision log at the time it is made.
  2. Do not rely on memory or undocumented showroom discussions. Convert every showroom or supplier conversation into a written entry before it is treated as an approved selection.
  3. Route scope requests through the Chapter 10 change system. Do not price, schedule, or begin any requested change outside that system.
  4. Do not allow field personnel to promise free added work, schedule changes, or substitutions. Redirect any such customer request to the responsible project manager for change-order processing.
  5. Document owner decisions, refusals, deferrals, access restrictions, and separate-contractor coordination. Record each with date, decision maker, and specific content on the owner-decision log.
  6. Record customer-caused, contractor-caused, weather, supplier, inspection, utility, and design impacts factually. State the observed fact without assigning blame language that has not been established.

H. Complaint Handling and Escalation

  1. Create a complaint intake procedure that acknowledges the concern without premature admission or dismissal. Log the date, description, and source of every complaint on the complaint log.
  2. Inspect and document the reported condition. Record location, description, and photograph before forming a conclusion.
  3. Separate warranty, punch, damage, design preference, scope dispute, schedule concern, billing concern, and unrelated condition. Classify the complaint on the log using one of these categories.
  4. Assign one responsible manager and response deadline. Record the name and date on the complaint log at intake.
  5. Communicate investigation, interim protection, corrective plan, and closure. Update the customer at each stage and record the update in the job file.
  6. Escalate claims, threats, injury, damage, discrimination, legal, insurance, or media matters to authorized professionals. Route these items to the attorney, insurer, or other identified professional before any further customer response.
  7. Do not retaliate, argue publicly, disclose private information, or demand a review change as a condition of service. Respond factually and keep private information out of any public reply.
  8. Create an incident and property-damage communication procedure coordinated with the insurer. Confirm the incident-reporting steps from Chapter 4 are followed before customer communication continues.
  9. Create a missed-appointment, denied-access, unsafe-condition, and work-stoppage procedure. Record the specific condition, notice given, and resulting schedule effect.
  10. Create an overdue-payment communication procedure consistent with contract and law. Use only attorney-approved language for the applicable jurisdiction.
  11. Do not threaten lien, collection, permit, utility, or abandonment action outside attorney-approved procedures. Route any such communication through counsel before it is sent.
  12. Preserve emails, texts, platform messages, meeting notes, photographs, approvals, and notices in the job file. File every item at the time it is created or received.
  13. Summarize material verbal conversations in writing. Send the summary to the customer the same day and file a copy.
  14. Review open customer actions at project meetings. Confirm outstanding decisions, complaints, and change requests at each internal and customer meeting.
  15. Correct inconsistent messages among sales, office, project manager, and field team. Identify and resolve any conflicting statement before it reaches the customer again.
  16. Conduct a midpoint expectation review on longer projects. Confirm schedule, decisions, and satisfaction at the midpoint of extended projects.
  17. Conduct a pre-closeout review before final walkthrough. Confirm all open decisions, complaints, and changes are resolved before Chapter 19 begins.
  18. Measure communication performance through response time, unresolved decisions, complaint age, change authorization, and customer feedback. Record these measures for the project file and for the Chapter 18 review.
  19. Close communication items only after action and confirmation are documented. Do not mark an item closed on the log until the corresponding job-file record exists.

Common Process Inspection

Inspect the operating communication process using the same checks regardless of project size or type.

  • Schedule check: confirm every schedule statement in the job file is labeled baseline, current forecast, or commitment, and no known delay was concealed.
  • Disruption check: confirm every disruption point was explained with reasonable advance notice and is recorded in the job file.
  • Change-routing check: confirm no field-level promise of added work, schedule change, or substitution exists outside the Chapter 10 change-order log.
  • Decision check: confirm every selection, approval, refusal, deferral, and access restriction appears on the owner-decision log with date and decision maker.
  • Complaint check: confirm every logged complaint has a responsible manager, response deadline, investigation record, and closure date.
  • Record check: confirm the job file contains the emails, texts, platform messages, meeting notes, photographs, approvals, and notices for the current reporting period.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Customer Disputes a Schedule Date That Was Never Written Down

Probable cause: a schedule date was given verbally without a written label of baseline, forecast, or commitment, and was not entered in the job file.
Confirmation: search the job file and communication log for a written record of the disputed date.
Correction: send a written clarification restating the current forecast and its basis, and file the clarification in the job file.
Reinspection: repeat the schedule check on the next update and confirm every future date is labeled and recorded.

Field Personnel Promised Added Work Without a Change Order

Probable cause: a site conversation was treated as an approved change without routing it through the Chapter 10 system.
Confirmation: compare the field team's statement against the approved change-order log.
Correction: notify the customer in writing that the item requires a change order, process it through Chapter 10, and correct the field team's understanding of the routing requirement.
Reinspection: repeat the change-routing check on the next several field conversations.

A Selection Is Disputed Because It Was Never Documented

Probable cause: the selection was made verbally at a showroom or on site and was not entered on the owner-decision log.
Confirmation: check the owner-decision log and job file for a written record of the selection, its price effect, and its approval.
Correction: document the current understanding in writing, obtain the customer's confirmation, and enter it on the log with the actual decision date noted as best known.
Reinspection: repeat the decision check on all pending selections before further procurement.

A Complaint Was Never Closed or Assigned

Probable cause: the complaint was addressed informally in conversation without a corresponding log entry, responsible manager, or closure record.
Confirmation: compare the customer's account of the complaint against the complaint log.
Correction: open a log entry with the correct date, classify the complaint, assign a responsible manager and deadline, and document the investigation and closure going forward.
Reinspection: repeat the complaint check on all open items at the next project meeting.

An Overdue-Payment Message Was Sent Without Review

Probable cause: a payment reminder escalated into a lien or collection threat without attorney-approved language.
Confirmation: review the sent communication against the attorney-approved template for the applicable jurisdiction.
Correction: send a corrected communication using approved language, and route any further escalation through counsel.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before any further overdue-payment communication is sent.

Sales, Office, and Field Teams Give the Customer Conflicting Information

Probable cause: the standard update format was not used consistently, or one department communicated directly with the customer without checking the job file.
Confirmation: compare recent messages from each department against the job file and the standard update format.
Correction: issue a single corrected message reconciling the conflict, and route future customer communication through the responsible project manager.
Reinspection: repeat the record check at the next billing cycle and confirm one consistent message source.

Cleanup

This project remains active for the life of the job, but each reporting period ends with the record filed for reuse, not left as informal notes.

  • File the current period's standard update, decision-log entries, and complaint-log entries in the job file established under Chapter 2 controls
  • Confirm every disruption notice and schedule statement sent this period is present in the job file
  • Confirm every open complaint has a responsible manager, deadline, and current status
  • Confirm every pending change request has been routed to the Chapter 10 system
  • Carry forward unresolved decisions, complaints, and change requests to the next communication-calendar entry

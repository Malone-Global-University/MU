Job Overview

This project produces one operating job-cost and profit review process for a residential contracting business. The finished work product is a controlled job-cost record for at least one sample project, built and tested against the estimate, contract, changes, commitments, actual costs, revenue, and cash position for that job, together with a variance-investigation procedure, threshold alerts, a periodic review cycle, and a final reconciliation and lessons-learned procedure.

You will import the original estimate and contract value, create committed-cost and actual-cost records, reconcile labor hours and production, compare material and equipment cost and use, track subcontractor commitments and invoices, separate profit from cash flow, calculate forecast cost to complete and forecast gross profit, identify and investigate variance by cause, create corrective actions, complete a final job-cost reconciliation, and record lessons learned for pricing and scope.

This project depends directly on the Chapter 5 accounting system, the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet, and the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance system. It supplies the actual-cost feedback that Chapter 7 pricing and Chapter 6 scope assumptions require, and it supplies the financial reconciliation that Chapter 19 closeout and the Chapter 20 capstone depend on.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-103 — Track estimated versus actual cost by cost code for labor, material, equipment, and subcontractors on an active project.
  • PC-104 — Review labor hours by reconciling timekeeping to job, task, and cost code and comparing estimated to actual and remaining hours.
  • PC-105 — Review material costs by comparing estimated quantity and price to purchase, use, waste, return, and credit.
  • PC-106 — Identify overruns by cost category, calculate variance, and assign a probable cause from a defined list.
  • PC-107 — Calculate gross profit, gross margin, and forecast cost to complete using a consistent, documented formula.
  • PC-108 — Document lessons learned by scope, estimating, production, supplier, subcontractor, customer, schedule, quality, and warranty category, and route approved updates to Chapter 6 and Chapter 7.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the Chapter 5 banking and accounting system, the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet, and the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance system. Without a working chart of accounts and cost-code structure from Chapter 5, and without an accurate original estimate from Chapter 7, a job-cost comparison has nothing reliable to compare against.

The job-cost record built here is the financial spine that the Chapter 13 job-start packet initializes, the Chapter 14 customer-communication process references when discussing changes and delays, and the Chapter 19 final walkthrough and invoice depend on for a correct final account. The variance-investigation discipline used here — identify, confirm, correct, reinspect — is the same discipline used throughout the book's troubleshooting sections and is required again in the Chapter 20 capstone operating system.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • importing the original estimate, contract value, approved changes, allowances, and credits for one sample project
  • creating committed-cost records for purchase orders, subcontracts, and rentals
  • creating actual-cost records for payroll, materials, equipment, subcontractors, permits, fees, delivery, disposal, and expenses
  • reconciling employee time to job, cost code, date, task, and supervisor
  • comparing estimated and actual labor hours, material quantity and price, equipment duration, and subcontractor cost
  • tracking customer invoices, payments, retainage, credits, and receivable age
  • calculating forecast cost to complete, forecast final cost, forecast gross profit, and gross margin
  • identifying variance by a defined list of probable causes and creating corrective actions
  • completing periodic active-job reviews and one final job-cost reconciliation
  • documenting lessons learned and routing approved updates to Chapter 6 and Chapter 7

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • treat overhead recovery as net profit, or represent a forecast gross-profit figure as a guaranteed result, without full financial review
  • cut safety, quality, code compliance, wages, or required warranty work to protect a margin figure
  • update Chapter 7 pricing rates or Chapter 6 scope assumptions without management approval of the underlying lessons-learned finding
  • rely on this chapter for tax, accounting, or payroll advice; every accounting-method question must be confirmed with the company's CPA or bookkeeper

Any job-cost figure that has not been reconciled against the accounting system of record must be labeled as preliminary until it is confirmed.

Trade Note

A job-cost review that happens only after the project is finished tells the business what already went wrong; it cannot change the outcome. The process built in this chapter is designed to run every week or billing cycle while the job is still open, so that a labor overrun, a material-price increase, or an unbilled change is caught while there is still time to correct it.

Setup Standard

Do not wait until the job is complete to learn whether it lost money. Every active job must be reviewed on the periodic cycle defined in Section H, beginning at the first billing cycle after mobilization, not only at substantial completion.

Shop Tip

Reconcile committed cost — purchase orders and subcontracts issued but not yet invoiced — at the same time as actual cost. A job that looks profitable on posted invoices alone can be carrying a large unrecorded commitment that will erase the margin once it is billed.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every job-cost review compares the same four things, in the same order, regardless of trade or project size: what was estimated, what has been committed and spent, what remains to be spent, and what that leaves for profit. Skipping the committed-cost step, mixing cash received with profit earned, or comparing total hours instead of hours by task are the most common ways this comparison gives a false answer.

Variance Investigation Discipline

A variance is only recorded as explained after its probable cause has been confirmed against a specific source — a timekeeping record, a purchase-order price, a change-order log, or a field report. A variance may not be closed as "estimating error" merely because no other cause was checked first.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Variance With Its Cause

When a cost code shows a variance in Section H, record the cost code, the dollar amount, the probable cause from the approved list, the confirming source, and the corrective action. Do not simply note that the job is over or under budget without identifying which cost code and which cause.

Critical Compliance Cue — Do Not Treat Overhead Recovery as Net Profit

Overhead recovery embedded in the contract price covers the company's actual overhead cost; it is not additional net profit. Do not report forecast gross profit to management, ownership, or a lender as net profit without full financial review by the CPA.

Operational Discipline Cue — Review Before Substantial Completion, Not Only After

Complete the periodic active-job review defined in Section H beginning at the first billing cycle after mobilization. Do not wait until substantial completion or final invoice to run the first job-cost comparison.

Professional Advisor Cue — Confirm the Accounting Method With the CPA

Revenue-recognition method, retainage treatment, and cost-to-complete accounting affect how gross profit is calculated and reported. Confirm the approved method with the CPA before comparing job-cost figures across multiple projects or reporting them externally.

E. Import the Estimate, Contract, and Change Data

  1. Confirm the job number, customer, contract, estimate revision, budget, schedule, cost codes, and accounting records. Record each identifier on the Job-Cost Comparison Worksheet header before importing figures.
  2. Import original estimated quantity, labor hours, labor cost, material, equipment, subcontract, general conditions, overhead recovery, and profit. Enter each figure by cost code from the locked Chapter 7 pricing worksheet.
  3. Import approved changes, allowances, alternates, credits, and current contract value. Enter each approved amount from the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance log and calculate the current contract value.

F. Create Committed-Cost and Actual-Cost Records

  1. Create committed-cost records for purchase orders, subcontracts, rentals, and other obligations. List each open commitment by cost code with vendor, amount, and expected invoice date.
  2. Create actual-cost imports for payroll, materials, equipment, subcontractors, permits, fees, delivery, disposal, and expenses. Import posted costs by cost code from the Chapter 5 accounting system through the stated data date.
  3. Reconcile employee time to job, cost code, date, task, and supervisor. Match every timesheet entry to the job and cost code it was billed against.
  4. Identify missing, late, or miscoded time. List each unresolved timekeeping entry and assign a responsible person to correct it before the review is finalized.

G. Compare Labor, Material, Equipment, and Subcontract Performance

  1. Compare estimated labor hours with actual and remaining forecast. Enter estimated, actual-to-date, and forecast-remaining hours for each labor cost code.
  2. Compare labor production by task and quantity, not only total hours. State the production rate achieved (quantity installed per hour) against the estimated rate for each measured task.
  3. Compare estimated material quantity and price with purchase, use, waste, return, and credit. Enter estimated and actual quantity, unit price, waste, returns, and credits for each material cost code.
  4. Compare equipment duration and utilization with estimate. Enter estimated and actual equipment days or hours and note idle or extended-use periods.
  5. Compare subcontractor commitments, approved changes, invoices, retainage, and forecast. Enter the current subcontract value, invoiced amount, retainage withheld, and forecast final cost for each subcontract.
  6. Compare general-condition duration and cost with schedule. Enter estimated and actual general-condition cost against the current schedule duration.

H. Calculate Revenue, Cash, Forecast, Variance, and Lessons Learned

  1. Track customer invoices, payments, retainage, credits, disputed amounts, and receivable age. Enter each figure on the worksheet's revenue and cash section, separate from the cost section.
  2. Separate profit from cash flow. Present forecast gross profit and net cash position as two distinct lines; do not combine them into one figure.
  3. Calculate revenue earned, invoiced, collected, and remaining using the approved accounting method. Use the company's CPA-confirmed revenue-recognition method consistently across all four figures.
  4. Calculate forecast cost to complete. Estimate remaining cost by cost code for all unperformed scope, using current production rates and pricing.
  5. Calculate forecast final cost and forecast gross profit. Sum actual-to-date cost, committed cost, and forecast cost to complete for forecast final cost; subtract forecast final cost from contract value for forecast gross profit.
  6. Calculate gross margin using a consistent formula. Divide forecast gross profit by contract value using the company's approved, documented formula.
  7. Do not treat overhead recovery as net profit without full financial review. Label the overhead-recovery line separately from the profit line on the worksheet.
  8. Identify variance by estimating error, scope change, customer delay, supplier price, labor productivity, waste, rework, weather, inspection, schedule, damage, or accounting error. Record the probable cause for each cost code showing a variance beyond the company's approved threshold.
  9. Identify unapproved work and unbilled approved work. List any work performed without an approved change order and any approved change not yet invoiced.
  10. Identify allowance overrun, credit, and remaining exposure. Cross-check each allowance's committed amount against its original amount from the Chapter 10 log.
  11. Identify pending change exposure separately from approved revenue. List pending, unapproved changes and their potential value without including them in contract value.
  12. Create threshold alerts for labor, material, subcontract, schedule, receivable, and margin variance. State the specific dollar or percentage threshold that triggers an immediate review outside the normal cycle.
  13. Investigate variance promptly with field, estimator, procurement, accounting, and project management. Record who was consulted and what was confirmed for each variance investigated.
  14. Create corrective actions for purchasing, staffing, sequencing, billing, change control, scope, or customer decisions. State the specific action, responsible person, and target date for each variance requiring correction.
  15. Do not cut safety, quality, compliance, wages, or required warranty work to protect margin. Confirm no proposed corrective action reduces required safety, quality, compliance, wage, or warranty performance.
  16. Complete weekly or periodic active-job reviews. Run the comparison worksheet on the schedule stated in the Periodic-Review Schedule, beginning at the first billing cycle.
  17. Complete a substantial-completion review before final invoice. Run a full comparison at substantial completion and resolve open variances before the final invoice is prepared.
  18. Complete a final job-cost review after all costs and credits are posted. Run the comparison a final time after every cost, credit, and change has been posted to the accounting system.
  19. Reconcile final contract value, cost, gross profit, cash, receivable, payable, tax, and warranty reserve. Confirm every figure ties to the accounting system of record and record any remaining discrepancy with an explanation.
  20. Create lessons learned by scope, estimating, production, supplier, subcontractor, customer, schedule, quality, and warranty administration. Complete the Lessons-Learned Record with a specific finding in each applicable category.
  21. Update Chapter 7 pricing rates and Chapter 6 scope assumptions only after management approval. Route each proposed update through management review before it is applied to the controlled pricing worksheet or scope library.
  22. Archive the final job-cost report and present portfolio trends to management. Store the completed worksheet, variance log, and lessons-learned record in the job file and summarize trends across jobs for management review.

Common Model Inspection

Inspect the completed job-cost process using the same checks regardless of which job or trade is involved.

  • Comparison check: confirm every cost code shows estimate, committed, actual, forecast, and variance figures, not only a total.
  • Labor and material check: confirm hours and quantities are reconciled by task and cost code, not only in aggregate.
  • Variance check: confirm every variance beyond threshold has a recorded probable cause, confirming source, and corrective action.
  • Profit and cash check: confirm forecast gross profit and cash position are presented as two distinct figures.
  • Timing check: confirm at least one periodic review occurred before substantial completion, in addition to the final review.
  • Feedback check: confirm lessons learned are categorized and any proposed pricing or scope update is routed through management approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Job Looks Profitable Until the Final Invoice, Then Loses Money

Probable cause: committed cost from open purchase orders or subcontracts was not included in the periodic comparison, so only posted invoices were counted as cost.
Confirmation: compare the forecast final cost calculated with committed cost included against the actual final cost after all invoices post.
Correction: add committed-cost records to every future periodic review before calculating forecast final cost.
Reinspection: repeat the comparison check on the corrected worksheet for the next active job.

Labor Hours Show as Over Budget but the Cause Is Unclear

Probable cause: timekeeping entries were coded to the job but not to a specific task or cost code, so total hours cannot be compared by task.
Confirmation: review a sample of timesheet entries for the job and check whether each entry identifies a task-level cost code.
Correction: correct the timekeeping procedure to require task-level coding and recode the affected entries where possible.
Reinspection: repeat the labor-reconciliation check on the next periodic review.

A Variance Is Recorded as "Estimating Error" Without Investigation

Probable cause: the variance was closed without checking the change-order log, purchase-order price, or field report that might explain it.
Confirmation: check whether a confirming source is recorded for the variance entry in the Variance Log.
Correction: reopen the variance, check it against the change-order log, purchase-order price history, and field reports, and record the confirmed cause.
Reinspection: repeat the variance-documentation check before the entry is closed again.

Forecast Gross Profit Is Reported to Ownership as Cash Available

Probable cause: the worksheet combined the profit and cash-flow figures into a single reported number.
Confirmation: check whether the worksheet presents forecast gross profit and net cash position as two separate lines.
Correction: separate the two figures on the worksheet and correct any prior report that combined them.
Reinspection: repeat the profit-and-cash-separation check before the next report is issued.

An Allowance Overrun Is Not Discovered Until Final Invoice

Probable cause: the allowance register from Chapter 10 was not cross-checked against committed cost during the periodic review.
Confirmation: compare each allowance's committed amount on the Chapter 10 log to its original amount as of the last periodic review date.
Correction: add the allowance cross-check to every future periodic review and notify the customer of any overrun as soon as it is identified.
Reinspection: repeat the allowance-exposure check on the next periodic review.

A Pricing or Scope Update Is Applied Without Management Approval

Probable cause: a lessons-learned finding was entered directly into the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet or Chapter 6 scope library without routing it for review.
Confirmation: check the Lessons-Learned Record for a recorded management approval date corresponding to the applied change.
Reinspection: revert the unapproved change, route the finding through management review, and reapply it only if approved.
Reinspection: repeat the feedback check before the next pricing or scope revision cycle.

Job Overview

This project produces one controlled estimate and proposal template system for a residential contracting company. The finished work product is a master internal estimate template and a master customer-facing proposal template, each tested on one standard repair and one larger remodeling project, and both reviewed by the attorney and management personnel identified in Section H below.

You will separate internal estimates from customer-facing proposals, build a controlled header and identification section, build a scope section linked to the Chapter 6 scope library, build materials, responsibility, access, permit, schedule, pricing, exclusion, and assumption sections, insert attorney-reviewed placeholders for payment, warranty, and acceptance language, and test the finished templates against the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet before they are approved for use.

This project depends directly on the approved business model in Chapter 1, the service menu and scope library in Chapter 6, and the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7. It in turn controls the contract-ready scope of work in Chapter 9 and the change-order documentation in Chapter 10, both of which are built from the same template structure.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-043 — Write concise, project-specific summaries that identify the project, customer, property, and controlling documents.
  • PC-044 — List included work by linking each item to an approved Chapter 6 scope identifier.
  • PC-045 — List exclusions in clear, project-specific language that removes ambiguity about what is not included.
  • PC-046 — Organize line items by phase, room, system, or package in a way a customer can verify against the finished work.
  • PC-047 — State assumptions and concealed-condition language that has been reviewed for accuracy and completeness.
  • PC-048 — Produce one attorney-reviewed, tested, and approved professional proposal template ready for repeated use.

Where This Skill Fits

This project sits between pricing and contracting. It converts the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet's cost and price data into a document a customer can read, question, and sign, and it supplies the controlled structure that the Chapter 9 contract-ready scope of work and the Chapter 10 change-order system will reuse for every individual project.

The discipline used here — writing a controlled master template once, testing it against real projects, and revising only through a documented review process — is the same discipline used for the pricing worksheet in Chapter 7 and the contract-ready scope in Chapter 9. A proposal issued outside this controlled template is treated the same as an unreviewed contract clause: it must not be used until it has passed the same review.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • separating internal estimate documents from customer-facing proposal documents
  • building a controlled template header, project summary, scope, materials, responsibility, access, permit, demolition, schedule, pricing, exclusion, and assumption sections
  • inserting placeholders for change-order, allowance, payment, warranty, and acceptance language pending jurisdiction-specific attorney review
  • creating document-status, revision, and archive controls for every proposal issued
  • testing the templates on one standard repair and one larger remodeling proposal
  • obtaining attorney and management review and formally approving the master templates

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • treat a completed proposal as an executed contract; the proposal becomes a contract only when the jurisdiction-specific, attorney-reviewed contract language in Chapter 9 is executed by an authorized signer
  • insert payment, deposit, cancellation, financing, warranty, or lien language copied from the internet or another jurisdiction's form
  • issue a proposal that has not been checked against the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet for every priced commitment
  • finalize the master templates without the attorney and management review required in Section H below

Any legal placeholder that has not received jurisdiction-specific attorney review must remain marked as a placeholder and must not be issued to a customer.

Trade Note

A proposal template is a controlled document, not a starting point for freehand editing on every job. Every field left blank, every clause improvised on the spot, and every unreviewed legal sentence added under deadline pressure becomes a documented failure point in this chapter's troubleshooting section. Build the template once, correctly, and populate it project by project.

Setup Standard

Do not issue a proposal built on this template to a customer until the attorney-reviewed legal placeholders for payment, warranty, cancellation, and acceptance have been completed for the applicable jurisdiction. A proposal issued with an unreviewed placeholder still visible must be withdrawn and reissued.

Shop Tip

Draft the exclusions section at the same time as the included-work section, using the same scope identifiers. An exclusion written without reference to the matching included-work item is far more likely to leave a gap that surfaces as a dispute during construction.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every proposal this business issues, from a single-room repair to a full remodeling contract, is built from the same controlled sections defined here: identification, summary, scope, materials, responsibilities, access and permit conditions, schedule assumptions, pricing and validity, exclusions, and assumptions. Building this structure once, testing it, and locking it before individual proposals are drafted is what keeps every later proposal consistent, complete, and defensible.

Internal-Versus-Customer Discipline

The internal estimate carries confidential cost, labor-hour, and margin detail used for company decision-making. The customer-facing proposal carries only the scope, price, options, exclusions, and conditions the customer needs to make a decision. Confidential cost detail is never transferred into a customer-facing document, even when a customer requests a cost breakdown.

Documentation Cue — Link Every Line Item to a Scope Identifier

Every included-work line item on the proposal must reference its Chapter 6 scope identifier. A line item without a scope identifier has not been checked against its prerequisites, exclusions, and completion conditions and must not be issued.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Unreviewed Legal Language

Do not insert payment, deposit, cancellation, financing, retainage, lien, or warranty language into the master template without jurisdiction-specific attorney review. A generic clause copied from another state's form or a competitor's proposal is not a substitute for that review.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Master Template, Controlled Revisions

Maintain a single controlled master estimate template and a single controlled master proposal template in the company record system. Individual project proposals are generated from the locked master; the master itself is changed only through the revision procedure in Section H.

Professional Advisor Cue — Attorney Review Before Approval

The payment, warranty, cancellation, and acceptance sections of the proposal template must be reviewed by a qualified attorney licensed in the jurisdiction of use before the template is approved. Do not present these sections to a customer with placeholder language still in place.

E. Identification and Structure

  1. Identify the estimate and proposal types used by the business. Record whether the business issues fixed-price, unit-price, time-and-material, or service-call proposals, and note which template sections apply to each type.
  2. Separate internal estimates from customer-facing proposals. Build the internal estimate and customer proposal as two distinct documents from the outset; do not attempt to redact one into the other.
  3. Create a controlled template header with legal business name, trade name, address, contacts, license information, and insurance statements only as required and verified. Pull each value from the verified Chapter 3 licensing file and Chapter 4 insurance file; do not estimate or approximate any header value.
  4. Create project, customer, property, proposal number, date, revision, plan date, and validity fields. Assign a controlled numbering convention for proposal identifiers before the first test proposal is created.
  5. Create a concise project-summary section. Limit the summary to the project objective, location, and overall scope in plain, customer-readable language.

F. Scope, Materials, and Line-Item Organization

  1. Create a scope section linked to approved Chapter 6 scope identifiers. Insert the identifier and reusable description for every included service.
  2. Organize work by phase, room, system, service package, or line item appropriate to the project. Select one organizing structure per project type and apply it consistently.
  3. Define quantity and unit display rules without disclosing confidential cost unless intended. Show quantity and unit of measure on the customer proposal; withhold unit cost and margin unless the business has decided to disclose it.
  4. Create separate base, alternate, allowance, unit-price, credit, and optional-service sections. Do not combine these categories into one undifferentiated list.
  5. Create a materials and finishes section that identifies brand, model, grade, color, selection status, or allowance basis. Mark each material item as selected, pending, or allowance-based.

G. Responsibilities, Conditions, and Schedule

  1. Create a contractor-responsibility section. List labor, supervision, equipment, protection, cleanup, testing, and documentation the contractor provides.
  2. Create an owner-responsibility section. List access, decisions, selections, utilities, and payment responsibilities the owner provides.
  3. Create an access, occupancy, utility, protection, storage, and working-hours section. State the assumed access and occupancy conditions the price is based on.
  4. Create a permits, inspections, design, engineering, testing, and code-responsibility section. Identify who is responsible for obtaining and paying for each required permit or inspection.
  5. Create a demolition, salvage, disposal, cleanup, and restoration section. State what is removed, salvaged, disposed of, and restored as part of the proposed price.
  6. Create a schedule-assumption and lead-time section that avoids unsupported promises. State schedule assumptions as estimates dependent on material lead time, weather, and inspection scheduling, not as guaranteed dates.

H. Pricing, Legal Placeholders, Testing, and Approval

  1. Create a pricing, tax, freight, escalation, and expiration section. Populate this section from the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet and state the proposal's validity period.
  2. Create an exclusions section in clear project-specific language. Write each exclusion in terms specific to the project rather than a boilerplate disclaimer list.
  3. Create an assumptions and concealed-condition section. State the specific conditions assumed to exist and the process for pricing a condition that differs from the assumption.
  4. Create a change-order and allowance statement consistent with Chapter 10. Reference the Chapter 10 change-order and allowance procedures rather than restating them in different language.
  5. Create a payment, deposit, financing, retainage, cancellation, lien, and collection placeholder requiring jurisdiction-specific attorney-approved language. Mark this section clearly as a legal placeholder pending attorney review.
  6. Create a warranty and manufacturer-information placeholder requiring approved terms. Mark this section clearly as a legal placeholder pending attorney review.
  7. Create signature and acceptance placeholders appropriate to proposal status without treating the proposal as a contract unless legally intended. State explicitly whether signing this document constitutes contract execution or only proposal acceptance pending a separate contract.
  8. Create a document-reference section for drawings, specifications, photographs, site reports, and clarifications. List every attachment referenced elsewhere in the proposal.
  9. Create attachment lists for selections, schedules, allowances, notices, plans, and terms. Maintain a single attachment index per proposal.
  10. Create a proposal-status field such as draft, budget, issued, revised, accepted, declined, expired, or withdrawn. Update this field every time the proposal's status changes.
  11. Create a revision table identifying what changed and why. Record every revision with date, section changed, and reason.
  12. Create a customer-friendly summary page and a detailed scope attachment. Separate the short summary a customer reads first from the detailed scope they can review in full.
  13. Create accessibility, readability, font, page-number, footer, and print controls. Set a minimum font size, consistent footer with proposal number and page count, and print-ready margins.
  14. Create PDF, electronic-signature, email-delivery, and archive procedures with legal review. Confirm the delivery and signature method satisfies the jurisdiction's requirements for proposal and contract delivery.
  15. Test the template on one standard repair and one larger remodeling proposal. Populate both templates completely using real or representative project data.
  16. Compare proposal scope with the pricing worksheet and identify missing or unpriced commitments. Correct any scope statement that lacks a corresponding priced line item.
  17. Review the template with attorney, management, estimator, project manager, and customer-service personnel. Record each reviewer's name, date, and comments on the attorney review record.
  18. Remove unauthorized legal language and replace it with approved terms. Update the payment, warranty, cancellation, and acceptance sections to match the attorney's approved language exactly.
  19. Approve the master templates and lock controlled fields. Sign, date, and lock the master estimate and proposal templates against uncontrolled editing.
  20. Create a template revision and jurisdiction-selection procedure. Record how the template is updated when jurisdiction, license, or legal requirements change, and who is authorized to approve that update.

Common Template Inspection

Inspect the completed templates and test proposals using the same checks regardless of which project type or jurisdiction is involved.

  • Separation check: confirm no confidential cost, labor-hour, or margin data appears on the customer-facing proposal.
  • Scope-linkage check: confirm every included-work line item references its Chapter 6 scope identifier.
  • Pricing check: confirm every priced commitment on both test proposals matches the Chapter 7 pricing worksheet.
  • Legal-placeholder check: confirm payment, warranty, cancellation, and acceptance sections carry only attorney-approved language, with no placeholder text remaining.
  • Exclusion and assumption check: confirm every exclusion and assumption is written in project-specific, not generic, language.
  • Review check: confirm the attorney and management review comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Confidential Cost Data Appears on a Customer Proposal

Probable cause: the customer proposal was generated by editing the internal estimate document rather than a separate controlled template.
Confirmation: compare the issued proposal against the internal estimate template for unit cost, margin, or labor-hour fields.
Correction: rebuild the customer proposal from the approved master proposal template and withdraw the exposed document.
Reinspection: repeat the separation check on the corrected proposal.

A Proposal Line Item Has No Matching Scope Identifier

Probable cause: a line item was added directly to a proposal without first creating or selecting a Chapter 6 scope identifier.
Confirmation: review the scope section for any item lacking an identifier.
Correction: create the missing scope identifier in the Chapter 6 library or remove the item until it has one.
Reinspection: repeat the scope-linkage check on the corrected proposal.

Proposal Price Does Not Match the Pricing Worksheet

Probable cause: the proposal was populated from an outdated worksheet version, or a manual adjustment was made directly on the proposal without updating the worksheet.
Confirmation: compare every priced line item on the proposal against the current locked Chapter 7 worksheet for that project.
Correction: regenerate the proposal pricing from the current worksheet and document any approved adjustment on both documents.
Reinspection: repeat the pricing check on the corrected proposal.

A Legal Placeholder Was Issued to a Customer Without Attorney Review

Probable cause: the proposal was issued under schedule pressure before the attorney review record was completed.
Confirmation: check the attorney review record for a completed review date covering the payment, warranty, cancellation, and acceptance sections used on the issued proposal.
Correction: withdraw the proposal, complete the required attorney review, and reissue the proposal only with approved language.
Reinspection: repeat the legal-placeholder check before any further proposals are issued from the template.

An Exclusion Is Too General to Be Enforceable

Probable cause: the exclusion was copied from a generic boilerplate list instead of being written for the specific project scope.
Confirmation: read each exclusion against the matching included-work item and ask whether a customer could reasonably dispute its meaning.
Correction: rewrite the exclusion in project-specific language naming the excluded work, material, or condition.
Reinspection: repeat the exclusion and assumption check on the revised proposal.

The Master Template Was Edited Without a Recorded Revision

Probable cause: a field-level or office-level user edited the locked master template directly instead of proposing a revision through the controlled procedure.
Confirmation: compare the current master template against the last approved and archived version.
Correction: restore the approved master template and route the proposed change through the Section H revision procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the review check on the corrected master template before further use.

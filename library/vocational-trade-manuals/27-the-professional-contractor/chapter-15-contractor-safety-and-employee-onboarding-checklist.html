Job Overview

This project produces one written, professionally reviewed employee and worker onboarding system for a residential contracting company. The finished work product is a controlled onboarding packet — worker-category definitions, a lawful hiring and classification procedure, a restricted personnel-record system, a training matrix, company and jobsite safety rules, a PPE and tool-authorization schedule, and an onboarding checklist — completed on the templates supplied in this chapter.

You will identify every worker category the company uses, work with qualified professionals to set classification, payroll, and record requirements, build a lawful hiring and documentation process, verify credentials through official sources, create a task-based training matrix, define site rules, PPE, and tool authorization, assign supervision and stop-work authority, build emergency and incident procedures, and test the completed system with real onboarding records before it is approved.

This project depends on the Chapter 1 business model, the Chapter 2 company records and confidentiality controls, the Chapter 3 licensing file, and the Chapter 5 payroll and accounting system. It supplies the verified, insured, and supervised workforce required before the Chapter 16 subcontractor system, the Chapter 13 job-start packet, and the Chapter 20 capstone operating system can rely on field labor.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PC-085 — Establish site rules that control conduct, access, and hazard exposure on an active residential jobsite.
  • PC-086 — Document PPE requirements by hazard and task, and confirm PPE is provided, maintained, and used.
  • PC-087 — Explain tool safety expectations, including inspection, guarding, and authorization for controlled tasks and equipment.
  • PC-088 — Document training needs by task, tool, hazard, and supervisory level, and record completed training.
  • PC-089 — Assign supervision responsibilities, including competent-person and qualified-person roles and direct-supervision requirements for new or restricted workers.
  • PC-090 — Create onboarding records that verify classification, credentials, notices, training, and authorization before jobsite access.

Where This Skill Fits

This project builds directly on the Chapter 2 company records and confidentiality controls, the Chapter 3 licensing file that identifies which credentials a worker must hold, the Chapter 4 insurance and bonding file that assumes a properly classified and trained workforce, and the Chapter 5 payroll and tax record system. It sits ahead of the Chapter 16 subcontractor and vendor qualification system, the Chapter 13 job-start packet, and the Chapter 14 customer-management procedure, all of which assume that any worker on a customer's property has been lawfully classified, verified, trained, and supervised.

The restricted-record discipline used here — verifying before authorizing, and documenting the verification rather than relying on a worker's own statement — is the same discipline used for subcontractor qualification in Chapter 16 and for the capstone workforce review in Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying every worker category used by the company, including employees, temporary workers, apprentices, supervisors, operators, and subcontractor personnel
  • working with qualified professionals to determine classification, payroll, tax, workers' compensation, immigration, wage, overtime, and record requirements
  • building a lawful application, offer, hiring, background, drug-testing, medical, and accommodation process reviewed by HR or counsel
  • creating restricted-access employment and personnel records
  • verifying required licenses, certifications, apprenticeships, cards, medical clearances, fit tests, and training through official or approved sources
  • creating a training matrix by task, tool, equipment, hazard, and supervisory level
  • creating site rules, PPE requirements, tool-authorization lists, competent-person and qualified-person assignments, and stop-work authority
  • creating incident, emergency, and return-to-work procedures
  • producing an approved, dated onboarding system and a tested set of onboarding records

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • classify a worker as an employee or independent contractor without qualified legal and payroll review specific to the applicable federal, state, and local rules
  • substitute this checklist for required training, competent-person authority, medical evaluation, fit testing, certification, or site-specific hazard control
  • authorize any worker for a task, tool, or piece of equipment before the required credential, training, or competent-person determination is verified and recorded
  • treat a subcontractor qualification file as employee onboarding, or the reverse; the two records are separate and are not interchangeable
  • finalize the onboarding system without the professional review required in Section H below

Any worker category, task authorization, or training claim that cannot be supported by current credentials, verified training, or qualified professional sign-off must be removed from the system before it is approved.

Trade Note

Every classification, credential, and training decision in this chapter is recorded on the reference worksheet named in that step — never left as an unwritten understanding between the owner and a new worker. An unwritten onboarding decision is the single most common source of the misclassification, uninsured-injury, and unauthorized-task exposure diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not assign any worker to a jobsite task, tool, or piece of equipment based on a draft onboarding file. A worker placed on-site before classification, required notices, training, PPE, and supervision are verified and recorded must be treated as unauthorized and removed from the task until the file is complete.

Shop Tip

Draft the tool and task authorization list in Section F at the same time as the training matrix, not afterward. A worker who has completed general orientation is not automatically authorized for a ladder, a lift, a powder-actuated tool, or hot work; each of those requires its own recorded authorization.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every later system that places a worker on a customer's property, from the Chapter 13 job-start packet to the Chapter 20 capstone operating system, depends on the same four written boundaries defined here: who the worker legally is, what the worker is trained and authorized to do, who supervises the worker, and what record proves it. Getting this sequence right before any worker reaches a jobsite means every later system inherits a lawfully classified, verified, and supervised workforce.

Classification Discipline

A worker is only added to the payroll or task schedule after the worker's classification, payroll, tax, workers' compensation, immigration, wage, overtime, and record requirements are confirmed with qualified professionals. A worker whose classification cannot be confirmed is not scheduled for site work until the classification question is resolved and documented.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Classification Determination

When a worker's classification is determined in Section E, record the worker, the classification, the basis for it, and the reviewing professional's name and date. Do not rely on a signed subcontract or a tax form alone as proof of classification.

Critical Compliance Cue — No Task Authorization Without Verified Training

Do not authorize any worker for a ladder, scaffold, lift, forklift, vehicle, trailer, excavation equipment, saw, nailer, hot work, or other controlled task until the required training and competency check are verified and recorded on the task-authorization list. Prior stated experience alone does not authorize the task.

Operational Discipline Cue — One Onboarding File, One Copy of Record

Maintain a single controlled onboarding file for each worker in the restricted personnel-record system established in Chapter 2. Do not allow informal versions of training records, PPE assignments, or task authorizations to circulate outside that file. Every later system in this book references the single approved copy.

Professional Advisor Cue — Classification and Safety-Program Decisions Require Review

Worker classification, wage and overtime treatment, immigration documentation, and the governing safety program itself are set with HR, employment counsel, payroll, and safety-professional review. This chapter's checklist organizes those decisions; it does not replace the professional determination behind them.

E. Worker Category, Classification, and Hiring

  1. Identify every worker category used by the company, including employees, temporary workers, apprentices, supervisors, operators, and subcontractor personnel. Record each category as its own row on the worker-category and classification worksheet.
  2. Determine classification, payroll, tax, workers' compensation, immigration, wage, overtime, and record requirements with qualified professionals. Record the reviewing professional's name, the classification determination, and the basis for it next to each worker category.
  3. Do not treat a subcontractor checklist as employee onboarding or vice versa. Confirm which system — this chapter's onboarding file or the Chapter 16 subcontractor-qualification file — applies to each worker category before proceeding.
  4. Create a lawful application, offer, hiring, background, drug-testing, medical, and accommodation process with professional review. Document each stage of the process and the professional who reviewed it.
  5. Create employment and personnel records with restricted access. Establish the restricted personnel-record system and name the persons authorized to access it.
  6. Collect required identity, tax, payroll, emergency, benefits, and authorization documents securely. Record the document list and the secure storage location for each worker category.
  7. Provide required wage, policy, leave, workers' compensation, unemployment, harassment, discrimination, and other notices. Record which notices apply and confirm delivery for each new worker.
  8. Create an employee handbook or policy acknowledgement through attorney and HR review. Record the reviewing attorney or HR advisor's name and the review date.
  9. Define job title, duties, reporting relationship, work location, travel, tools, vehicle, and performance expectations. Record each element on the individual worker's onboarding checklist.

F. Credentials, Training, and Task Authorization

  1. Identify required licenses, certifications, apprenticeships, cards, medical clearances, fit tests, and training. Record each requirement against the worker's assigned duties on the training matrix.
  2. Verify credentials through official or approved sources. Record the verification source, date, and result for each credential; do not accept an unverified self-report.
  3. Create a training matrix by task, tool, equipment, hazard, and supervisory level. Complete the matrix begun in Section E, Step 9 with every task, tool, and hazard the worker's role may involve.
  4. Do not authorize work solely because the employee states prior experience. Require a demonstration, observation, or competency check before recording task authorization.
  5. Create company safety rules consistent with the governing safety program. Record the governing program reference and confirm the site rules do not conflict with it.
  6. Create site-access, attendance, conduct, substance, violence, harassment, privacy, photography, and customer-property rules. Record each rule on the site-rules and PPE schedule.
  7. Create PPE requirements by hazard and task. Record the specific PPE item required for each hazard and task identified in the training matrix.
  8. Provide, maintain, replace, and document PPE according to legal and company responsibilities. Record the responsible party and replacement procedure for each PPE item.
  9. Create tool inspection, guard, battery, cord, blade, accessory, lockout, storage, and reporting rules. Record each rule and the responsible inspector.
  10. Create an authorization list for ladders, scaffolds, lifts, forklifts, vehicles, trailers, excavation equipment, saws, nailers, hot work, and other controlled tasks. Record the named worker, verified training basis, and authorization date for each controlled task.

G. Supervision, Emergency Readiness, and Records

  1. Create competent-person and qualified-person assignments only where training and authority meet requirements. Record the named worker, the specific hazard or task, and the basis for the designation.
  2. Define direct supervision for new, inexperienced, or restricted workers. Record the supervising person and the specific tasks requiring direct supervision.
  3. Create stop-work authority and hazard-reporting procedures. Record who may exercise stop-work authority and the procedure for reporting a hazard.
  4. Create incident, near-miss, injury, property damage, and return-to-work procedures. Record each procedure and the responsible manager.
  5. Create emergency contacts, first aid, fire, evacuation, weather, utility, and customer-home procedures. Record the current contact list and post it with the site rules.
  6. Create customer privacy, keys, access codes, valuables, occupied-home, pets, children, and daily shutdown procedures. Record each rule on the site-rules schedule.
  7. Create housekeeping, material storage, waste, vehicle, parking, and public-protection rules. Record each rule and confirm consistency with the Chapter 6 scope library.
  8. Conduct company orientation before site assignment. Record the orientation date, topics covered, and instructor for each worker.
  9. Conduct project-specific orientation before jobsite access. Record the project, date, and topics covered for each worker's project-specific orientation.
  10. Use demonstrations, observation, and competency checks rather than signatures alone. Record the method used to verify each training item, not only a signed acknowledgment.
  11. Record training topic, instructor, date, method, employee, result, restriction, and retraining. Complete the training matrix with these fields for each worker.
  12. Create probation, coaching, discipline, retraining, and removal-from-task procedures with HR and legal review. Record the reviewing professional and the approved procedure.
  13. Review payroll setup and workers' compensation classification after onboarding. Confirm the Chapter 5 payroll and Chapter 4 insurance records match the completed classification.
  14. Audit records for missing documents and expired credentials. Record the audit date, findings, and corrective actions for each worker file.
  15. Review the checklist with safety, HR, counsel, insurer, and operations. Obtain and record specific comments from at least one qualified reviewer.
  16. Approve the onboarding system and establish annual and task-change updates. Sign and date the final system and record the next scheduled review date and the specific events — new task, new tool, incident, licensing change — that would trigger an earlier review.

Common System Inspection

Inspect the completed onboarding system and its per-worker records using the same checks regardless of which worker category or task is involved.

  • Classification check: confirm every worker category has a documented, professionally reviewed classification basis.
  • Credential check: confirm every required license, certification, and training item was verified through an official or approved source, not a self-report.
  • Task-authorization check: confirm every controlled tool or task has a named authorized worker with a recorded training basis and authorization date.
  • Supervision check: confirm competent-person and qualified-person assignments are supported by training and authority, and direct supervision is defined for restricted workers.
  • Record check: confirm personnel records are restricted-access, complete, and stored in the Chapter 2 company record system.
  • Professional-review check: confirm at least one qualified reviewer's comments are recorded and addressed before approval.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Worker Is Treated as an Independent Contractor Without Review

Probable cause: classification was assumed from a signed subcontract or a tax form rather than a professional determination.
Confirmation: check the worker-category and classification worksheet for a named reviewing professional and a documented basis.
Correction: suspend reliance on the assumed classification, obtain qualified professional review, and correct payroll, tax, and insurance treatment retroactively as advised.
Reinspection: repeat the classification check on the corrected worksheet.

A Worker Is Assigned a Controlled Task Without Recorded Authorization

Probable cause: the worker's stated prior experience was accepted in place of a demonstration, observation, or competency check.
Confirmation: check the tool and task authorization list for a recorded training basis and authorization date for the specific worker and task.
Correction: remove the worker from the task until the required training and competency check are completed and recorded.
Reinspection: repeat the task-authorization check before the worker resumes the task.

Credentials on File Have Expired

Probable cause: no renewal calendar or periodic audit was created for licenses, certifications, or medical clearances.
Confirmation: audit the personnel records for expiration dates against the current date.
Correction: remove the worker from any task requiring the expired credential until it is renewed and verified, and create or correct the renewal calendar.
Reinspection: repeat the credential check on the corrected records.

A Competent-Person Designation Is Not Supported

Probable cause: a worker was designated competent or qualified based on job title rather than verified training and demonstrated authority.
Confirmation: review the designation record for the specific training and authority basis.
Correction: remove the unsupported designation and reassign the responsibility to a worker whose training and authority are verified, or arrange the required training before redesignating.
Reinspection: repeat the supervision check on the corrected assignment.

Personnel Records Are Not Restricted-Access

Probable cause: personnel files were stored in a shared or general company file rather than the restricted-access system established in Chapter 2.
Confirmation: review file location and access permissions against the Chapter 2 confidentiality controls.
Correction: move the records to the restricted-access system and correct access permissions to named, authorized personnel only.
Reinspection: repeat the record check on the corrected file location.

The Onboarding System Was Approved Without Professional Review

Probable cause: a qualified reviewer was not identified early enough, or the review step was skipped to meet an internal deadline.
Confirmation: check the approval record for a named reviewer, review date, and recorded comments.
Correction: suspend reliance on the system for hiring, classification, or task-authorization decisions until a qualified reviewer's comments are obtained and addressed.
Reinspection: repeat the professional-review check before re-approving the system.

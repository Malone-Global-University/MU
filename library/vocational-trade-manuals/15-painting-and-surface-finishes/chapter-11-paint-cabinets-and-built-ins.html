Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to remove, label, degrease, sand, prime, and coat a small cabinet or built-in shelving assembly, then reassemble and adjust it to a smoothly operating, fully cured finish. The finished work product is one cabinet or built-in section with all doors, drawers, and hardware reinstalled, adjusted, and functioning, coated with a bonded primer and cabinet enamel system appropriate to the substrate.

You will identify the cabinet material and existing finish, confirm the coating system is compatible with that substrate, photograph and label every removable component before disassembly, degrease and repair the surfaces, sand or degloss them, apply bonding primer and cabinet enamel while keeping moving joints and hardware cavities clear, and reassemble and adjust the finished unit.

This chapter builds directly on the cleaning methods from Chapter 3, the patching methods from Chapter 4, and the primer-selection principles from Chapter 7. It applies those methods to cabinet-grade surfaces, which see far more handling, moisture, and grease exposure than a wall, and which require exact hardware tracking so the finished piece still opens, closes, and aligns correctly.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-061 — Remove doors and hardware and record their original position for accurate reassembly.
  • PF-062 — Clean cabinet surfaces to remove grease, wax, and contamination before sanding or coating.
  • PF-063 — Sand and degloss cabinet surfaces uniformly without sanding through veneer or thin laminate.
  • PF-064 — Apply bonding primer appropriate to the cabinet substrate and inspect adhesion after cure.
  • PF-065 — Apply cabinet enamel in thin, even coats without buildup in moving joints or hardware cavities.
  • PF-066 — Inspect the finished cabinet for adhesion, sheen, color, edge coverage, and correct door and drawer operation.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 1 through 10 prepared and coated walls, ceilings, and standard trim and door assemblies. Cabinets and built-ins carry a heavier handling and moisture load — hinge cups, drawer slides, and shelf-pin holes cannot tolerate the film buildup that a flat wall coat can. This chapter applies the compatibility and primer-selection discipline from Chapter 7 to laminate, veneer, solid wood, MDF, and thermofoil substrates specifically, and it adds the disassembly-and-labeling discipline that a multi-component cabinet project requires.

The bonding-primer and hardware-clearance methods taught here carry forward into Chapter 18, where cabinet coating failures are diagnosed and repaired using the same substrate categories, and into the Chapter 20 capstone, where a designated cabinet or built-in section is completed using the exact sequence taught here.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying cabinet material, existing finish, door and drawer system, hardware, and interior surfaces
  • confirming coating-system compatibility with laminate, veneer, solid wood, MDF, or thermofoil substrates
  • photographing and labeling every door, drawer, hinge, pull, and shelf before removal
  • degreasing, repairing, sanding, or deglossing cabinet surfaces
  • applying a compatible bonding primer and inspecting adhesion after cure
  • applying cabinet enamel by brush, roller, or controlled spray as specified, without buildup in moving joints
  • reassembling, adjusting, and inspecting the finished cabinet or built-in

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • coat failing thermofoil, contaminated silicone surfaces, or an unknown existing finish without first performing an approved compatibility test
  • remove a component that was not photographed and labeled beforehand
  • substitute a different primer or coating system than the one confirmed compatible with the identified substrate
  • reinstall hardware before the specified cure time has been observed

Any cabinet substrate or existing finish that cannot be confirmed compatible with the specified coating system must be tested on an inconspicuous or hidden surface before full application, per Chapter 7's compatibility method.

Trade Note

A cabinet project fails at reassembly more often than at coating. Every door, drawer, hinge, pull, and shelf must return to its original position and orientation, or reveals will be uneven and hardware will bind. Label before you remove, not after.

Common Field Fundamentals

Cabinet coating differs from wall or trim coating in three ways: the substrate is frequently glossy, laminated, or veneered and will not accept a standard primer without a bonding product; every door, drawer, and hardware item must return to its exact original position; and the finished coating must resist daily handling, moisture, and cleaning without blocking or chipping at edges and hinge points.

Compatibility Before Coating

Do not treat any cabinet substrate as automatically ready for a standard wall primer. Laminate, thermofoil, and existing glossy factory finishes require a bonding primer formulated for low-porosity surfaces. Confirm compatibility using the Chapter 7 test-patch method on an inconspicuous area — an interior shelf edge or the underside of a door — before committing to full application.

Measurement Cue — Photograph and Label Before Removal

Photograph every door, drawer, hinge, pull, and shelf in its installed position, then label each with a matching code, before removing anything. A component removed without a recorded position is a common source of misaligned reveals at reassembly.

Critical Safety Cue — Untested Thermofoil, Silicone Contamination, and Unknown Finishes

Do not coat failing or peeling thermofoil, a surface with silicone contamination, or an unknown existing finish without first performing an approved test-patch evaluation. Coating over an incompatible or contaminated surface produces adhesion failure that cannot be corrected without stripping the new coating.

Safety Cue — Degreasing and Neutralizing

Wear chemical-resistant gloves when handling degreasing products. Rinse or neutralize the cleaner exactly as the product instructions specify before sanding or priming; residual cleaner left on the surface can interfere with primer adhesion.

Field Safety Cue — Dust Control During Sanding

Control dust during sanding and degloss work with a vacuum-assisted sander or wet method where the product allows it, and wear the dust mask or respirator matched to the task. Do not dry-sweep fine sanding dust.

Manufacturer Cue — Bonding Primer and Cabinet Enamel Cure Times

Follow the bonding primer and cabinet enamel manufacturer's stated recoat, handling, and full-cure times exactly. Reinstalling hardware or closing doors before full cure is complete is a common cause of blocking and surface damage.

E. Substrate Identification and Compatibility Confirmation

  1. Identify cabinet material, existing finish, door and drawer system, hardware, interior surfaces, and required coating. Record the substrate category and existing finish condition for every component on the Cabinet Component Schedule.
  2. Confirm the coating system is compatible with laminate, veneer, solid wood, MDF, thermofoil, or existing paint. Cross-check the selected bonding primer and cabinet enamel against the identified substrate.
  3. Do not coat failing thermofoil, contaminated silicone surfaces, or unknown finishes without an approved test. Perform a test-patch application on an inconspicuous area and inspect adhesion after the specified cure time before proceeding to full application.

F. Disassembly and Labeling

  1. Photograph and label every door, drawer, hinge, pull, and shelf position. Assign a matching code to each door and its hinge locations, and to each drawer and its slide position, before removing anything.
  2. Remove doors, drawers, pulls, knobs, hinges, and removable shelves as permitted. Bag hardware from each component together with its label.
  3. Protect cabinet interiors and adjacent surfaces. Mask or cover interior surfaces, countertops, and floors not scheduled for this chapter's coating.

G. Cleaning and Repair

  1. Degrease all coating surfaces thoroughly. Apply the approved degreaser to every surface scheduled for primer, working from the least- to most-contaminated areas.
  2. Rinse or neutralize cleaner as required. Follow the product instructions for rinse or neutralization before proceeding.
  3. Repair minor damage with compatible filler. Fill dents, dings, and fastener holes, and allow the filler to cure fully before sanding.

H. Sanding and Dust Removal

  1. Sand or degloss sound surfaces uniformly. Use the least aggressive abrasive that produces a uniform scuff across the entire surface.
  2. Control dust and avoid sanding through thin veneer. Use light, even pressure and check veneer thickness at edges before sanding aggressively.
  3. Vacuum and wipe surfaces clean. Remove all sanding dust with a HEPA vacuum followed by the approved tack or wipe method.

I. Bonding Primer Application

  1. Apply the specified bonding primer. Apply in thin, even coats to every surface identified in Section E, maintaining wet edge and specified coverage.
  2. Inspect adhesion after cure. Perform the tape adhesion test on a representative area once the primer has reached its stated cure time.
  3. Sand primer lightly where required. Scuff-sand the cured primer only if the product instructions call for it before topcoat.

J. Cabinet Enamel Application, Cure, and Reassembly

  1. Apply cabinet enamel by brush, roller, or controlled spray as specified. Follow the application method identified in the finish schedule.
  2. Maintain thin, even coats on profiles and edges. Avoid overloading panel recesses, door edges, and drawer fronts.
  3. Prevent paint buildup in hinge cups, slide tracks, shelf-pin holes, and moving joints. Mask or shield these areas during application and remove any bridging film before it sets.
  4. Observe full recoat and cure requirements. Allow each coat to reach its stated recoat window before applying the next.
  5. Apply additional coats. Apply the number of coats specified in the finish schedule, maintaining consistent sheen and color across all components.
  6. Reinstall hardware only after sufficient cure. Confirm the manufacturer's full cure time has elapsed before installing hinges, pulls, and slides.
  7. Hang doors and install drawers. Match each labeled component to its recorded position and hardware set.
  8. Adjust reveals, hinges, and slides. Adjust hinge cups and slide mounting points until reveals match the pre-removal photographs and doors and drawers operate without binding.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every component regardless of substrate or coating method.

  • Labeling check: confirm every removed door, drawer, hinge, pull, and shelf is matched to a recorded position before reassembly begins.
  • Adhesion check: confirm the bonding primer passed the tape adhesion test on a representative area before enamel was applied.
  • Buildup check: confirm hinge cups, slide tracks, and shelf-pin holes are free of coating buildup.
  • Cure check: confirm hardware was not reinstalled before the manufacturer's stated full cure time had elapsed.
  • Operation check: confirm every door and drawer opens, closes, and aligns without binding, and that reveals match the pre-removal photographs.
  • Finish check: confirm consistent sheen, color, and edge coverage across all coated components.

Complete the Cabinet Coating Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the unit to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Enamel Lifts From a Laminate or Thermofoil Surface

Probable cause: a standard wall or trim primer was used instead of a bonding primer formulated for low-porosity surfaces, or the surface was not degreased before priming.
Confirmation: inspect the lifted area for a clean substrate break versus a residue layer, and review the worksheet for the primer product used.
Correction: remove the failed coating back to sound material, degrease the exposed surface, apply the specified bonding primer, and recoat.
Reinspection: perform the tape adhesion test on the corrected area before returning the component to service.

A Door or Drawer Does Not Align After Reassembly

Probable cause: the component was not labeled before removal, or it was reinstalled with a different hinge or slide set than the one it was removed with.
Confirmation: compare the current position with the pre-removal photograph and check the hardware label against the bagged set.
Correction: remove and reinstall the component with its correct labeled hardware, then adjust hinges or slides to match the recorded reveal.
Reinspection: confirm the reveal matches the pre-removal photograph and the component operates without binding.

A Door Sticks or Blocks Against the Frame After Closing

Probable cause: the door was closed or hardware was reinstalled before the cabinet enamel reached its full cure time.
Confirmation: check the worksheet for the recorded cure time against the manufacturer's stated requirement and inspect the contact area for a soft or tacky film.
Correction: separate the blocked surfaces carefully, allow the coating to reach full cure undisturbed, and touch up any transferred film.
Reinspection: confirm full cure has elapsed before closing the door again.

Coating Buildup Prevents a Hinge or Slide From Seating

Probable cause: the hinge cup, slide mounting point, or shelf-pin hole was not masked or shielded during enamel application, allowing film to bridge the cavity.
Confirmation: inspect the cavity for coating film blocking the fastener path or hardware seat.
Correction: carefully clear the cavity of cured film using the method appropriate to the substrate without damaging surrounding coating, then reinstall the hardware.
Reinspection: confirm the hardware seats fully and the door or drawer operates correctly.

Sheen or Color Differs Between Components

Probable cause: coats were not boxed or mixed consistently, or one component received a different number of coats than the others.
Confirmation: compare the affected component with the finish schedule sample and check the worksheet's coat-count record.
Correction: recoat the affected component using boxed material and the same coat count as the rest of the assembly.
Reinspection: compare sheen and color across all components under consistent lighting before final acceptance.

Veneer Shows Through or Delaminates After Sanding

Probable cause: sanding pressure or abrasive grit was too aggressive for the thin veneer layer.
Confirmation: inspect the affected edge for exposed substrate beneath the veneer.
Correction: stop sanding the affected area immediately; repair with compatible filler or veneer patch as the substrate allows, then reprime and recoat.
Reinspection: confirm no further veneer breakthrough occurs before continuing to adjacent areas.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a fully reassembled, adjusted, and inspected cabinet or built-in ready for normal use.

  • Clean brushes, rollers, or sprayer components using the approved method for the coating used
  • Dispose of degreaser residue, sanding dust, and coating waste without drain or soil disposal
  • Remove and store protection materials from interiors, countertops, and floors
  • Confirm all hardware bags are empty and all fasteners accounted for
  • File the completed Cabinet Coating Worksheet, photographs, and product records with the room's overall paint-project records

Field Terms

Cabinet Body
The fixed carcass of the cabinet or built-in, remaining in place through disassembly and coating of doors and drawers.
Bonding Primer
A primer formulated to adhere to glossy, laminate, veneer, or low-porosity cabinet substrates.
Cabinet Enamel
A finish coating formulated for the hardness, block resistance, and cleanability required on cabinet surfaces.
Blocking
Coated surfaces sticking together under pressure or heat before the coating has fully cured.
Hardware Cavity
Hinge cups, drawer-slide mounting points, and shelf-pin holes that must remain free of coating buildup.
Reveal
The visible gap between a door or drawer front and the surrounding cabinet frame, used to verify correct reassembly alignment.
Thermofoil
A vinyl film substrate laminated over MDF, requiring specific compatibility confirmation before coating.
Test Patch
A small application performed on an inconspicuous area to confirm coating compatibility before full application.
Full Cure
The manufacturer-stated time required before a coating reaches its final hardness and block resistance.
Cabinet Coating Worksheet
The document recording substrate, primer, enamel, adhesion-test, and reassembly findings for the project.

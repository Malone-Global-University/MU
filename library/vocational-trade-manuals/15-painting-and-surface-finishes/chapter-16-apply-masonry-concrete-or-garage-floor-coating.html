Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare and coat one controlled masonry wall, concrete panel, or garage-floor practice slab using a verified moisture, alkalinity, contamination, and surface-profile assessment followed by a compatible patch, primer or sealer, and floor-coating or masonry-coating system. The finished work product is one fully bonded, cured coating system on the selected practice substrate, documented from moisture testing through final inspection.

You will select an approved substrate option, test the surface for moisture and contamination, patch cracks and spalls, verify and where required create a surface profile, apply a compatible primer or sealer, mix and apply the coating at the specified rate, optionally broadcast decorative aggregate or flakes, and inspect the cured coating for adhesion, coverage, and slip condition.

Concrete and masonry coating failures are overwhelmingly caused by uncontrolled moisture, alkalinity, or contamination beneath the coating, not by the coating product itself. This chapter treats substrate verification as the controlling step, ahead of any application work. Chapter 15 taught exterior siding coating; Chapter 17 continues with controlled spray application, which may be used for the floor coating in this chapter where the approved system specifies it.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-091 — Clean masonry or concrete substrates and verify residue and contaminant removal.
  • PF-092 — Etch or mechanically profile the surface where the coating system requires it.
  • PF-093 — Patch cracks, spalls, and surface defects with a compatible repair product.
  • PF-094 — Apply a compatible primer or sealer at the specified coverage rate.
  • PF-095 — Apply the floor or masonry coating at the specified rate, including any decorative broadcast.
  • PF-096 — Inspect the cured coating for adhesion and coverage and document the result.

Where This Skill Fits

Masonry and concrete coating depends on the same substrate-diagnosis discipline taught in Chapter 1, applied to a substrate that can trap moisture and alkaline salts long after it appears dry. The moisture and contamination tests in this chapter are the concrete-specific counterpart to the hazard and moisture screening every chapter in this book requires before coating begins.

This chapter's patch-cure and primer-cure requirements follow the same rule established in Chapter 4 and Chapter 5: nothing is coated over an uncured repair or an unverified surface. The final adhesion and slip inspection in this chapter feeds directly into the Chapter 19 quality inspection, using the same documentation standard.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • selecting one approved substrate option: masonry wall, concrete panel, or garage-floor practice slab
  • performing the specified moisture and surface tests before any product is opened
  • testing for existing sealers, oils, curing compounds, and contaminants
  • degreasing, cleaning, rinsing, and drying the substrate
  • mechanically profiling, grinding, shot-blasting, or acid-etching only where the approved system requires it
  • repairing cracks, spalls, and surface defects with a compatible patch product while preserving active movement joints
  • applying primer or sealer, then the floor or masonry coating, at specified coverage rates
  • broadcasting decorative aggregate or flakes only where the approved system requires it
  • inspecting the cured coating for adhesion, pinholes, bubbles, fish-eyes, gloss, and slip texture

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • coat a substrate where moisture, alkalinity, or contamination testing exceeds the selected product's stated limits
  • perform mechanical profiling, grinding, shot-blasting, or acid-etching using silica, chemical, ventilation, or wastewater controls that do not match the requirements stated in this chapter
  • discharge acidic, contaminated, or slurry rinse water into a drain, storm system, or soil
  • seal or coat over an active movement joint, or bridge a joint the substrate specification identifies as a required expansion or control joint
  • apply a floor coating containing isocyanates or other high-hazard components without the supplied-air respiratory protection and professional controls identified in the product's safety data sheet

Any substrate that fails the moisture, alkalinity, or contamination screen in this chapter must be corrected, retested, or referred to a qualified professional before coating proceeds.

Trade Note

A concrete or masonry substrate can look, and feel, completely dry while still transmitting enough moisture vapor to blister or delaminate a coating weeks later. The moisture test in this chapter's Section H is not a formality; it is the single most reliable predictor of whether this chapter's coating will still be bonded a year from now.

Setup Standard

Do not open any patch, primer, or coating container until the moisture and surface tests in Section H have been performed and recorded (Step 3) and the substrate is confirmed within the product's stated limits (Step 5). Do not apply the floor coating (Step 21) until the primer or sealer has completed its specified induction and recoat window (Step 18).

Shop Tip

Run the moisture and alkalinity tests in more than one location on the same slab or wall, particularly near exterior walls, below-grade areas, and locations without a vapor barrier. A single test point near the center of a garage slab can pass while an edge near an uninsulated foundation wall fails.

Moisture, Alkalinity, and the Stop-Work Threshold

Concrete continues releasing moisture vapor for months or years after it appears dry, and that vapor pressure is enough to lift a coating from beneath even when the surface passes every visual check. The moisture and alkalinity limits belong to the coating manufacturer, not to this chapter; record the manufacturer's stated limit alongside the test result on the Substrate and Coating Record, and treat any result at or above that limit as a stop-work condition until the substrate is dried, sealed with a vapor-mitigation system rated for the condition, or referred to a qualified professional.

Critical Safety Cue — Silica Dust from Mechanical Profiling

Grinding, shot-blasting, or scarifying concrete or masonry generates respirable crystalline silica dust. Use HEPA-filtered local extraction at the tool, wear the respirator specified for silica exposure, and HEPA-vacuum residual dust. Do not dry-sweep profiling dust.

Critical Safety Cue — Acid-Etching and Rinse Water

Acid-etching solutions are corrosive and generate reactive rinse water. Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection, ventilate the work area, and neutralize the etched surface per the product instructions. Do not discharge acidic or contaminated rinse water into a drain, storm system, or soil; contain and dispose of it using the approved method.

Code Alert — Slip Resistance and Wastewater Discharge

Required slip-resistance ratings for coated floors, and permitted methods for disposing of rinse water, grinding slurry, and etching residue, vary by jurisdiction and facility type. Verify local wastewater discharge and slip-resistance requirements before selecting a coating gloss level or discharge method.

Field Safety Cue — Two-Part Product Handling and Ventilation

Mix two-part primers and coatings only in the ratio and quantity the manufacturer specifies, in a ventilated area away from ignition sources. Observe pot life; do not apply material past its working time, and dispose of unused mixed material using the approved fire-safe method.

C. Repair and Coating Takeoff

  • Patch material: quantity sufficient to fill every recorded crack and spall to the manufacturer's specified depth and cure profile.
  • Primer/sealer: quantity calculated from measured surface area and the product's stated coverage rate, with a minimum 10 percent waste allowance.
  • Floor/masonry coating: quantity calculated the same way, matched to the specified film thickness and number of coats.
  • Decorative aggregate/flake (if specified): quantity per the broadcast rate stated in the approved coating specification.

E. Substrate Selection and Document Review

  1. Select the approved substrate option: masonry wall, concrete panel, or garage-floor practice slab. Record the selected option on the Substrate and Coating Record.
  2. Review concrete age, cure, moisture, alkalinity, contamination, vapor transmission, existing sealer, profile, coating, and slip-resistance requirements. Record every value stated in the approved coating specification.

F. Moisture and Contamination Testing

  1. Perform the specified moisture and surface tests. Run the calcium chloride or in-situ test and the pH test at a minimum of two locations, including one near an exterior wall or below-grade area if present.
  2. Test for existing sealers, oils, curing compounds, and contaminants. Use the specified test method (water-droplet test or manufacturer-specified method) and record the result for each test location.
  3. Stop work when moisture or contamination exceeds the coating limits. Record the excess condition and the required correction, drying period, or professional referral before proceeding.

G. Cleaning and Profiling

  1. Protect adjacent walls, drains, doors, equipment, and landscaping. Mask or cover surfaces that must remain free of degreaser, etching solution, or coating overspray.
  2. Degrease and clean the substrate. Apply the specified degreaser and scrub with a stiff brush, working from the least to the most contaminated area.
  3. Rinse and dry according to the product. Confirm the substrate reaches the manufacturer's specified dry condition before profiling.
  4. Mechanically profile, grind, shot-blast, or acid-etch only where the approved system requires it. Do not profile a substrate the specification does not call for profiling.
  5. Use silica, chemical, ventilation, and wastewater controls. Operate HEPA-filtered extraction during grinding or shot-blasting and neutralize etching solution per the product instructions.
  6. Do not discharge acidic or contaminated rinse water improperly. Contain and dispose of rinse water and slurry using the approved method.
  7. Vacuum dust and neutralize residues where required. HEPA-vacuum all profiling dust and confirm neutralization with a pH check where acid-etching was used.

H. Repair and Cure

  1. Repair cracks, spalls, and surface defects with compatible products. Fill each recorded defect to the manufacturer's specified depth and finish.
  2. Preserve active movement joints. Do not fill, bridge, or coat over a joint identified as an expansion, control, or isolation joint.
  3. Allow repairs to cure. Observe the patch product's stated cure time before proceeding to profile verification.
  4. Verify the required surface profile. Confirm the cured, repaired surface still meets the manufacturer's stated profile requirement at each repair location.

I. Primer, Coating, and Broadcast Application

  1. Apply primer or sealer at the specified coverage rate. Mix per the manufacturer ratio and apply by roller, squeegee, or brush as specified.
  2. Observe induction, pot life, and recoat limits. Do not apply the floor coating before the primer's minimum recoat time or after its maximum recoat window.
  3. Mix the floor coating accurately. Combine components in the exact ratio and for the mixing time the manufacturer specifies.
  4. Cut edges and detail penetrations. Apply the coating along walls, drains, columns, and other penetrations before working the field area.
  5. Roll or squeegee field areas at the specified rate. Spread the coating using the notched squeegee, then back-roll with the phenolic roller to the specified film thickness.
  6. Broadcast aggregate or decorative flakes only where required. Broadcast into the wet coating at the specified rate using spiked shoes to access the field area.
  7. Back-roll for uniform film. Maintain a consistent wet film thickness across the entire coated area.

J. Cure, Additional Coats, and Inspection

  1. Protect the surface through cure. Block access and control dust, moisture, and foot traffic until the coating reaches its specified cure stage.
  2. Apply additional coats or clear topcoat within the recoat window. Recoat only within the manufacturer's stated window; extend surface profile by light sanding if the window is exceeded and the product requires it.
  3. Inspect adhesion, pinholes, bubbles, fish-eyes, thin spots, roller lines, gloss, slip texture, and movement joints. Perform the specified adhesion check and record the condition of every listed item.
  4. Document moisture, profile, batches, coverage, cure, and inspection. Complete the Substrate and Coating Record with every test result, batch number, and inspection finding.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every substrate option regardless of which one is selected.

  • Moisture check: confirm every recorded moisture and alkalinity result is at or below the coating manufacturer's stated limit.
  • Contamination check: confirm no oil, sealer, or curing-compound residue remains after degreasing.
  • Profile check: confirm the surface profile matches the manufacturer's requirement wherever profiling was specified, including at repaired areas.
  • Repair check: confirm every recorded crack and spall is patched, cured, and inspected, and that no movement joint is bridged.
  • Coverage check: confirm primer, sealer, and coating coverage falls within 10 percent of the specified rate with no thin spots.
  • Adhesion and slip check: confirm the cured coating passes the specified adhesion test and meets the required slip-resistance texture.

Complete the Substrate and Coating Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the practice area to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Coating Blisters or Delaminates Weeks After Application

Probable cause: moisture vapor emission exceeded the coating manufacturer's stated limit at the time of application, or the moisture test was skipped or performed at only one location.
Confirmation: re-test moisture at multiple locations beneath and around the failed area, and review the original recorded test result against the product's stated limit.
Correction: remove the failed coating down to sound substrate, correct the moisture source or install a rated vapor-mitigation system, retest, and recoat only after the substrate passes.
Reinspection: repeat moisture testing across the full coated area before recoating.

Coating Fails Adhesion Test in Isolated Spots

Probable cause: localized contamination, such as residual oil or an existing sealer, was not fully removed during degreasing.
Confirmation: inspect the failed spots for a shiny or oily residue and compare with the degreasing record for that location.
Correction: remove the coating in the affected area, redegrease and reprofile as required, and recoat within the specified system.
Reinspection: perform the specified adhesion check on the recoated area and adjacent sound coating.

Pinholes or Fish-Eyes Appear in the Cured Coating

Probable cause: outgassing from a porous, insufficiently sealed substrate, or contamination such as silicone or oil on the surface before coating.
Confirmation: inspect the substrate's porosity and profile record and review the degreasing and priming steps for the affected area.
Correction: sand or grind the defect area, reclean, apply an additional primer coat if the system allows outgassing control, and recoat within the recoat window.
Reinspection: inspect the corrected area under raking light after cure.

Patched Crack Reopens After Coating

Probable cause: the crack was an active movement joint that was patched and coated over instead of preserved.
Confirmation: inspect the reopened crack location and compare with the original substrate record for any noted movement-joint designation.
Correction: remove the patch and coating from the joint, install the correct joint detail (backer rod, sealant, or cover system) rather than a rigid patch, and coat around the joint.
Reinspection: confirm the joint moves freely and remains unbridged after correction.

Finished Floor Is Slipperier Than Specified

Probable cause: the specified aggregate or flake broadcast rate was not achieved, or a clear topcoat was applied at a gloss level heavier than specified.
Confirmation: review the broadcast-rate record and compare the topcoat film thickness against the specification.
Correction: abrade the surface and apply an additional broadcast or slip-resistant additive coat per the manufacturer's corrective procedure.
Reinspection: re-check slip texture against the specified requirement after the correction cures.

Rinse or Slurry Water Reaches a Floor Drain During Profiling

Probable cause: profiling or etching was performed without containment, or the floor drain was not protected before work began.
Confirmation: inspect the drain and surrounding area for slurry or acidic residue and review the wastewater-control step in the record.
Correction: stop work, contain and remove the discharged material using the approved method, and install drain protection before resuming.
Reinspection: confirm drain protection is in place and functioning before continuing profiling or etching.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a fully cured, inspected coating system and a documented record retained for the Chapter 19 quality inspection.

  • Contain and dispose of degreaser wash water, etching residue, and grinding slurry using the approved method — never into a drain, storm system, or soil
  • Clean mixing paddles, squeegees, rollers, and broadcast tools with the approved solvent or water method before the mixed coating cures in the tool
  • Dispose of unused mixed two-part product and solvent-soaked rags in approved fire-safe containers
  • Store partial containers of primer, sealer, and coating upright, closed, labeled, and within their temperature limits
  • File the completed Substrate and Coating Record with the approved coating specification and all test results

Field Terms

Moisture Vapor Emission
The rate at which moisture moves through a slab or wall to the surface, measured against the coating manufacturer's stated limit.
Alkalinity (pH)
The acidity or alkalinity of the substrate surface, measured against the coating manufacturer's stated acceptable range.
Surface Profile
The texture created on concrete or masonry, by mechanical or chemical means, to the degree of roughness the coating manufacturer specifies.
Spall
An area where the concrete or masonry surface has fractured, flaked, or broken away.
Movement Joint
An expansion, control, or isolation joint that must remain unbridged by patch, primer, or coating material.
Induction Time
The manufacturer-specified waiting period after mixing a two-part product and before it may be applied.
Broadcast
The application of decorative aggregate or flakes into a wet floor coating at a specified rate.
Back-Rolling
Rolling a coating a second time in a consistent direction to even the film thickness after initial spreading.
Vapor-Mitigation System
A product or system rated to reduce moisture vapor transmission through a slab to a level a coating can tolerate.
Substrate and Coating Record
The document recording every moisture, contamination, profile, repair, coverage, and inspection finding for the coated substrate.

Performance Check

With the coating fully cured, review the completed Substrate and Coating Record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every moisture and alkalinity result is within the coating manufacturer's stated limit, every contamination test shows a clean result, the surface profile matches the specification wherever profiling was required, every recorded crack and spall is patched and cured with all movement joints preserved, primer and coating coverage fall within 10 percent of the specified rate, and the final adhesion and slip-condition check both pass. Issue the final result only after every item is complete. If any item is missing or fails, return to the affected area, complete the correction, and record the result before treating the project as final.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to set up, operate, and clean a spray-finishing system on one controlled practice panel, using an approved low-hazard coating in a contained, ventilated, fire-controlled enclosure. The finished work product is a uniformly sprayed practice panel produced without dry spray, runs, sags, or uneven overlap, along with a completed spray-settings and inspection record.

You will establish a designated spray enclosure with exhaust and make-up air, exclude isocyanate and other professionally restricted coatings, inspect and set up the sprayer, wear the specified PPE and respirator, test and adjust the spray pattern on a test panel, apply a controlled coat to the practice surface, depressurize the equipment using the manufacturer procedure, clean the equipment, and dispose of waste safely.

This chapter depends on the masking method taught in Chapter 6 and the primer and coating-compatibility principles taught in Chapter 7. It stands apart from Chapters 8 through 16 because it is the only chapter in this book built around powered spray equipment rather than brush or roller application. Chapter 18 addresses repairing sprayed-finish defects, and Chapter 19 inspects sprayed work as part of the final quality inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-097 — Set up a contained, ventilated spray area appropriate to the coating and equipment.
  • PF-098 — Mask the surrounding overspray zone to protect adjacent surfaces and property.
  • PF-099 — Mix, strain, and thin a spray coating only within manufacturer-permitted limits.
  • PF-100 — Operate a sprayer using correct pressure, distance, and travel technique.
  • PF-101 — Maintain a consistent spray pattern and wet edge across the practice surface.
  • PF-102 — Inspect the sprayed finish for common spray defects and document settings and results.

Where This Skill Fits

Spray application is faster and produces a smoother film than brush or roller work, but it multiplies the consequences of poor setup: overspray travels farther, atomized coating and solvent vapor concentrate faster in an unventilated space, and a fire or explosion hazard exists with flammable coatings and solvents that brush work does not create in the same way. This chapter treats spray equipment as a system that must be authorized, contained, ventilated, and controlled before any trigger is pulled.

The masking and containment method taught here protects everything outside the designated spray zone the same way Chapter 6 protects a room from brush and roller work. The coating-authorization screen taught in Step 2 of this chapter's work order is the same screen that keeps isocyanate-containing and other professionally restricted products out of this training setting entirely.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • establishing a designated spray enclosure or contained practice zone with exhaust and make-up air
  • confirming that the coating selected for this training setup is authorized and excluding isocyanate, high-toxicity, explosive, or professionally restricted coatings
  • masking overspray zones and protecting adjacent property, vehicles, equipment, floors, and surfaces
  • inspecting the sprayer, hose, gun, guard, filter, tip, cup, pressure control, and electrical components
  • mixing, straining, and thinning the coating only within manufacturer limits
  • testing and adjusting the spray pattern, fan width, atomization, and flow on a test panel
  • operating the sprayer with correct gun angle, distance, travel speed, and overlap
  • applying an approved coat to the practice surface and inspecting for spray defects
  • depressurizing, cleaning, and safely disposing of spray waste

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • spray any coating containing isocyanates or other high-hazard components; that work requires supplied-air respiratory protection, containment, training, and professional controls outside this chapter's scope
  • spray inside an occupied room or a large uncontained interior or exterior area; that work may require professional handling under conditions this chapter does not establish
  • operate spray equipment without the specified PPE, respirator, exhaust, make-up air, and grounding or bonding where required
  • substitute an unapproved thinner, reducer, or additive, or thin beyond the manufacturer's stated limit

Any coating, enclosure condition, or equipment fault that fails the screening steps in this chapter must be corrected, substituted, or referred to a qualified professional before spraying begins or continues.

Trade Note

Every spray-pattern adjustment in this chapter is tested on a test panel first and recorded before the practice surface is coated. A setting change made directly on the finished practice panel, without a test-panel check, is the single most common source of dry spray and uneven overlap identified in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not connect or pressurize the sprayer (Step 9) until the enclosure, exhaust, make-up air, and coating authorization (Steps 1–8) are complete. Do not apply the approved coat to the practice surface (Step 23) until the test-panel pattern check (Steps 12–14) has produced a complete, adjusted pattern.

Shop Tip

Walk the spray-pattern test through slowly the first time: trigger on, sweep, trigger off, without worrying about coverage. Getting the release timing right on the test panel, before the practice panel is at stake, prevents the tapered "dumbbell" pattern that comes from triggering on and off while the gun is stationary.

Common Field Fundamentals

Spray application replaces brush and roller contact with atomized coating traveling through the air. That difference is the source of every added control in this chapter: overspray travels beyond the intended surface, atomized vapor and mist concentrate faster in an unventilated space than brushed or rolled solvent does, and static discharge during transfer of flammable coatings can ignite vapor that a brush or roller never disperses into the air. Every step in this chapter's work order exists to contain, ventilate, or control one of those three differences.

Coating Authorization for This Training Setting

Only an approved low-hazard coating identified in the finish specification for this chapter may be sprayed in this training setup. Isocyanate-containing coatings, other high-toxicity products, explosive-classification products, and any coating the manufacturer restricts to professional or industrial application are excluded from this chapter without exception. Spraying a restricted coating requires supplied-air respiratory protection, containment, and training controls this chapter does not establish.

Measurement Cue — Test-Panel Verification Before Committing

Verify fan pattern, atomization, and flow on a test panel, and record the pressure, tip, and distance settings that produced an acceptable pattern, before applying any coating to the practice surface. Adjust one variable at a time and retest.

Critical Safety Cue — Restricted Coatings and Respiratory Protection

Do not spray coatings containing isocyanates or other high-hazard components without the required supplied-air respiratory protection, containment, training, and professional controls. Confirm the selected coating is authorized for this training setup before opening the container.

Critical Safety Cue — Ignition Sources and Static Discharge

Remove all ignition sources from the spray enclosure before mixing or spraying a flammable coating. Ground or bond equipment where the coating or transfer method requires it. Do not spray in an enclosure containing an unshielded pilot light, spark source, or unrated electrical device.

Field Safety Cue — Ventilation and Backdraft

Confirm the exhaust and make-up air path removes overspray and vapor without drawing contaminants into occupied space and without backdrafting a fuel-burning appliance elsewhere in the structure. Do not begin spraying until this airflow path is confirmed.

Code Alert — Professional-Handling Thresholds

Requirements for permitted spray-finishing quantities, occupied-space spraying, and professional-applicator licensing vary by jurisdiction and by coating classification. Verify the applicable threshold before treating any project beyond this chapter's contained practice scope as authorized by this chapter.

E. Review, Authorization, and Enclosure Setup

  1. Review the coating, sprayer type, tip, pressure, filtration, ventilation, respiratory protection, fire risk, and containment requirements. Read the product data sheet and safety data sheet before opening the container.
  2. Confirm the coating is authorized for the training spray setup. Record the product name and classification on the worksheet.
  3. Exclude isocyanate, high-toxicity, explosive, or professionally restricted coatings. Do not proceed if the selected product falls into any excluded category.
  4. Establish a designated spray enclosure or contained practice zone. Mask the boundary and confirm no other work is scheduled to cross it during the session.
  5. Remove ignition sources. Walk the enclosure and adjacent area for pilot lights, sparking equipment, and unrated electrical devices.
  6. Provide approved exhaust and make-up air. Confirm the airflow direction removes overspray and vapor from the enclosure.
  7. Protect adjacent property, vehicles, equipment, floors, and surfaces from overspray. Extend masking beyond the immediate panel to the full overspray boundary identified in Step 4.
  8. Ground or bond equipment where required. Confirm continuity before introducing flammable coating.

F. Equipment Inspection and PPE

  1. Inspect the sprayer, hose, gun, guard, filter, tip, cup, pressure control, and electrical components. Record any worn, cracked, or damaged component and replace it before use.
  2. Wear the specified PPE and respirator. Confirm the respirator cartridge or filter matches the safety data sheet for the selected product.

G. Mixing and Pattern Testing

  1. Mix, strain, and thin the coating only within manufacturer limits. Record the thinning ratio used and confirm it does not exceed the product's stated limit.
  2. Prepare a test panel. Position it in the enclosure at the same working distance planned for the practice panel.
  3. Set the minimum pressure that produces a complete pattern. Start low and increase only as needed.
  4. Test spray pattern, fan width, atomization, and flow. Trigger briefly onto the test panel and inspect the result.

H. Spray Application

  1. Hold the gun perpendicular to the surface. Maintain this angle throughout each pass.
  2. Begin gun movement before triggering. Start the sweep off the edge of the panel.
  3. Maintain constant distance and travel speed. Use the distance and speed recorded during the test-panel check.
  4. Overlap passes by the specified amount. Follow the product's stated overlap percentage.
  5. Release the trigger before stopping movement. End each pass off the edge of the panel.
  6. Maintain wet edge without overbuilding corners. Slow travel speed slightly at corners rather than pausing.
  7. Inspect for dry spray, runs, sags, orange peel, tails, spits, and uneven overlap. Check the test-panel result before proceeding to the practice panel.
  8. Adjust one variable at a time. Change pressure, distance, or speed individually and retest before combining adjustments.
  9. Apply the approved coat to the practice surface. Use the confirmed settings from the test-panel check.
  10. Allow flash and recoat times. Observe the manufacturer's stated interval before any additional pass.

I. Depressurization, Cleanup, and Inspection

  1. Depressurize the system using the manufacturer procedure. Follow the exact sequence stated in the equipment manual before disassembly.
  2. Clean equipment with controlled solvent or water handling. Do not discharge cleaning waste to a drain, soil, or storm system.
  3. Dispose of waste safely. Place solvent-soaked rags and filters in the fire-safe waste container.
  4. Inspect the cured practice finish and document settings and defects. Record final pressure, tip, distance, and overlap settings, along with any defects found and their correction, on the Spray Settings and Inspection Worksheet.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks to every pass and to the finished practice panel regardless of coating or equipment type.

  • Authorization check: confirm the coating used is the approved low-hazard product recorded in Step 2, with no substitution.
  • Enclosure check: confirm the masked boundary, ignition-source removal, and exhaust/make-up air path were verified before spraying began.
  • Pattern check: confirm the test-panel pattern was complete, with recorded settings, before the practice panel was sprayed.
  • Technique check: confirm gun angle, distance, travel speed, and overlap were held constant across the practice panel.
  • Finish check: confirm the cured panel shows no dry spray, runs, sags, orange peel, tails, spits, or uneven overlap.
  • Cleanup check: confirm depressurization, cleaning, and waste disposal were completed and documented.

Complete the Spray Settings and Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before storing the equipment.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Dry, Rough Texture Appears on the Practice Panel

Probable cause: spray distance was too great, pressure was too high, or the coating flashed off in the air before reaching the surface.
Confirmation: compare the recorded distance and pressure against the test-panel settings and check ambient temperature and airflow in the enclosure.
Correction: reduce spray distance, reduce pressure, or adjust ventilation airflow, then re-verify the pattern on a test panel before recoating.
Reinspection: inspect the corrected area for wetted-out film and even sheen after cure.

Runs or Sags Develop on a Vertical Section

Probable cause: travel speed was too slow, distance was too close, or too much coating was applied in one pass.
Confirmation: review the recorded travel speed and distance and compare film thickness in the affected area against adjacent areas.
Correction: allow the coating to dry, sand or level the affected area only as the product permits, and recoat using increased travel speed or greater distance.
Reinspection: inspect the corrected pass for uniform film thickness before proceeding.

A Split or Tapered "Dumbbell" Pattern Appears on the Test Panel

Probable cause: a clogged or damaged tip, insufficient pressure, or coating viscosity outside the manufacturer's range.
Confirmation: inspect the tip for damage or buildup and recheck the mixed viscosity against the product's stated range.
Correction: clean or replace the tip, adjust pressure, or correct the thinning ratio, then retest the pattern.
Reinspection: confirm a complete, evenly tapered pattern on the test panel before spraying the practice surface.

Overspray Is Found Outside the Masked Enclosure Boundary

Probable cause: the masked boundary did not extend far enough for the fan width and distance used, or airflow carried overspray beyond the enclosure.
Confirmation: measure the actual overspray distance and compare it with the masked boundary and airflow path.
Correction: extend masking, reduce fan width or pressure, or reposition the enclosure exhaust before resuming.
Reinspection: confirm the extended boundary contains overspray on the next test pass before continuing.

Uneven Sheen or Color Banding Appears Across the Panel

Probable cause: inconsistent overlap between passes or a wet edge that was not maintained across the panel.
Confirmation: review the recorded overlap percentage and compare pass timing against the product's flash-time window.
Correction: recoat the full plane using consistent overlap and pass timing rather than touching up isolated bands.
Reinspection: inspect the recoated panel under raking light for uniform sheen before final documentation.

Equipment Will Not Depressurize Using the Standard Procedure

Probable cause: a clogged filter, tip, or line is trapping pressure, or the depressurization sequence was performed out of order.
Confirmation: review the manufacturer's depressurization sequence step by step and inspect the filter and tip for blockage.
Correction: follow the manufacturer's blockage-clearing procedure before attempting disassembly, and do not force open a pressurized component.
Reinspection: confirm zero pressure at the gauge or trigger before proceeding with cleaning or disassembly.

Cleanup

Complete equipment cleanup and waste handling before ending the session.

  • Confirm the sprayer has been fully depressurized before any disassembly
  • Clean the gun, hose, cup, and filter using the solvent or water method specified by the coating manufacturer
  • Do not discharge cleaning solvent, rinse water, or coating waste into a drain, soil, or storm system
  • Place solvent-soaked rags, filters, and other waste in the fire-safe waste container; do not leave them piled or folded
  • Remove masking from the enclosure boundary and inspect adjacent surfaces for overspray
  • Store the sprayer, tips, and accessories according to the manufacturer's storage instructions
  • File the completed Spray Settings and Inspection Worksheet with the coating's safety data sheet

Field Terms

The designated, contained, and ventilated zone authorized for spraying during this project.
Fan Pattern
The shape and width of atomized coating leaving the tip, verified and adjusted on a test panel.
Wet Edge (Spray)
The still-workable boundary of the most recent pass, maintained so the next pass blends without a visible line.
Flash Time

Dry Spray
A rough, poorly bonded texture caused by coating partially drying in the air before reaching the surface.
Orange Peel
A pebbled surface texture caused by improper atomization, distance, or viscosity.
Overlap
The percentage of each spray pass that covers the previous pass, controlling uniform film build.
Depressurization
The manufacturer-specified procedure for safely releasing stored pressure from a sprayer before disassembly.
Restricted Coating
A coating containing isocyanates or other components requiring supplied-air respiratory protection and professional controls.
Stop-Work Condition
Any finding that halts spraying until it is corrected or referred to a qualified professional.

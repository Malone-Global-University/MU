Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to prepare and coat an exterior siding practice wall using verified weather, surface, primer, and application controls. The finished work product is a uniformly coated siding practice wall with bonded primer and finish coats, complete edge and lap coverage, and preserved drainage and movement details, supported by a documented Exterior Siding Preparation and Coating Log.

You will identify the siding material, profile, existing coating, and moisture condition; confirm that flashing, caulk joints, drainage, and fasteners are sound; verify weather, surface-temperature, humidity, sunlight, and dew-point limits before opening any coating; wash the siding, scrape and feather loose paint, prime bare and stained areas, brush coating into laps and edges, roll or spray the field area with required back-brushing, and inspect the completed coating for coverage, adhesion, and preserved drainage.

This chapter follows Chapter 14, which coated an exterior door and trim assembly under the same weather and moisture controls. It applies those same environmental limits to a larger field area and introduces lap-and-profile technique specific to siding. Chapter 16 applies a related set of substrate-verification controls to masonry, concrete, and garage-floor coating.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PF-085 — Inspect siding condition, including material, profile, existing coating, and moisture indicators.
  • PF-086 — Scrape loose and peeling paint using controlled dust methods without damaging sound coating or the substrate.
  • PF-087 — Prime bare or stained siding with a compatible exterior primer at full coverage.
  • PF-088 — Brush coating into laps, edges, ends, and profiles to achieve complete edge coverage.
  • PF-089 — Roll or spray field areas with required back-brushing or back-rolling for a uniform film.
  • PF-090 — Inspect exterior coverage for laps, lower edges, end grain, fasteners, thin spots, overspray, and blocked drainage.

Where This Skill Fits

Exterior siding coating carries every control this book has built toward: the Chapter 1 substrate and moisture screening, the Chapter 3 cleaning method, the Chapter 4 patching approach, the Chapter 7 primer-selection rule, and the Chapter 14 exterior weather and dew-point limits. This chapter applies those same controls to a large, weather-exposed field surface where lap coverage and drainage preservation determine whether the coating survives its first season of exposure.

The weather-verification and moisture-screening method taught here recurs without exception in Chapter 16, where masonry and concrete substrates require their own moisture and profile checks before coating.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying siding material, profile, existing coating condition, exposure, and moisture condition
  • confirming that siding, flashing, caulk joints, drainage, fasteners, and moisture defects are corrected before coating
  • verifying weather forecast, wind, surface temperature, humidity, sunlight, and dew-point limits
  • screening existing coatings for lead where age and condition require it
  • washing, scraping, feathering, and priming a practice siding wall
  • brushing laps, edges, ends, and profiles and rolling or spraying field areas with required back-brushing
  • inspecting the completed coating for coverage, adhesion, and preserved drainage and movement details

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • scrape, sand, or disturb any coating suspected of containing lead without the testing and controls established in Chapter 1
  • caulk siding laps, weeps, drainage openings, or movement gaps unless the approved coating system explicitly requires it
  • apply coating outside the manufacturer's surface-temperature, air-temperature, humidity, or dew-point limits, regardless of schedule pressure
  • force wash water behind the siding or damage the water-resistive barrier during washing
  • perform full-building exterior spray application; this chapter's spray reference is limited to controlled back-brushed or back-rolled application on the practice wall, with large-scale professional spray work addressed as a referral condition

Any siding condition that fails the weather, moisture, or substrate screening in this chapter must be corrected or the application must be postponed before work proceeds.

Trade Note

Exterior coating failures are disproportionately weather failures, not product failures. A coating applied outside its surface-temperature or dew-point window can fail within one season even when every preparation step was performed correctly. Verify and record the readings in Section H before opening any container.

Setup Standard

Do not begin washing or scraping until siding, flashing, caulk joints, drainage, and fasteners have been confirmed sound (Step 2) and existing coatings have been screened for lead where required (Step 4). Do not open a coating container until weather, surface temperature, humidity, sunlight, and dew-point readings have been recorded and confirmed within the manufacturer's limits (Step 3).

Shop Tip

Check the surface temperature of the siding itself, not just the air temperature. A wall in direct morning sun can already exceed the product's maximum application temperature while the air temperature reading still looks acceptable.

Common Field Fundamentals

Exterior siding coating differs from interior wall coating in three ways this chapter addresses directly: the coating must survive continuous weather exposure, the lap-and-profile geometry of siding creates edges that are easy to under-coat, and the application itself is bounded by weather and dew-point conditions that can change during the work session, not just before it begins.

Weather and Dew-Point Limits

Confirm four readings before opening any coating container: air temperature, surface temperature of the siding itself, relative humidity, and dew point. The coated surface temperature must remain above the dew point throughout application and through the manufacturer's specified initial cure period. Do not begin application if the forecast shows rain, high wind, or a dew-point crossing before the specified cure period ends.

Measurement Cue — Record Weather Readings Before and During Application

Record air temperature, surface temperature, humidity, and dew point at the start of application and again at any point conditions appear to change. A reading taken once at the start of a multi-hour application does not confirm conditions held through the entire work session.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspected Lead-Based Paint on Exterior Siding

Test existing coatings for lead where the structure's age and condition require it, per the Chapter 1 hazard-screening method. Do not scrape, sand, or otherwise disturb suspect coatings until the required testing and controls are complete.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Extension Poles, and Weather Conditions

Use a stable, rated ladder or platform for upper-course work. Do not extend a ladder or work from a platform in high wind. Keep the base of the ladder on firm, level ground clear of the wash and scrape debris.

Code Alert — Wash-Water and Scrape-Debris Disposal

Requirements for containing and disposing of wash water and paint-scrape debris vary by jurisdiction, particularly where lead-based paint is suspected. Verify the applicable disposal requirements before washing or scraping begins.

E. Siding Identification and Substrate Verification

  1. Identify siding material, profile, existing coating, exposure, moisture condition, and product system. Record the siding material and profile, and select the exterior primer and finish system that matches it per the approved finish schedule.
  2. Confirm that siding, flashing, caulk joints, drainage, fasteners, and moisture defects are repaired. Inspect each element per the Substrate and Condition Schedule and correct any defect before proceeding.

F. Weather and Hazard Verification

  1. Review weather forecast, wind, surface temperature, humidity, sunlight, and dew-point limits. Record each reading and confirm the forecast covers the full application and specified cure window.
  2. Test existing coatings for lead where required. Apply the Chapter 1 hazard-screening method to the siding based on structure age, location, and coating condition, and stop disturbance until clearance is documented where required.

G. Protection, Washing, and Preparation

  1. Protect windows, doors, roofing, masonry, landscaping, vehicles, and adjacent property. Cover or mask every surface adjacent to the wash and coating area.
  2. Wash the siding using the approved low-pressure method. Work from the top of the wall downward, holding the wash equipment at the pressure and distance specified for the siding material, and direct spray to avoid forcing water behind the cladding.
  3. Allow full drying. Confirm the substrate moisture reading is within the coating manufacturer's limit before proceeding.
  4. Scrape loose and peeling coating. Remove only unsound material using the hand scraper under controlled dust methods.
  5. Feather sound edges using dust control. Sand the scraped edge transition smooth without cutting into sound coating.
  6. Do not force water behind siding or damage the WRB. Inspect the washed and scraped area for any sign of water intrusion or barrier damage before continuing.
  7. Repair minor substrate defects. Apply exterior-rated patching compound to minor dents, cracks, or gouges identified during inspection, and allow the repair to cure per the product instructions.

H. Priming and Joint Preservation

  1. Prime bare or stained siding with the specified exterior primer. Apply primer to every area of exposed bare substrate or stained surface at the coverage rate stated on the product data sheet.
  2. Seal only designated joints. Apply sealant only to the joints identified on the approved finish schedule.
  3. Do not caulk siding laps, weeps, drainage openings, or movement gaps unless the system requires it. Confirm every drainage and movement feature remains open and unobstructed after priming.

I. Lap, Edge, and Field Application

  1. Brush coating into laps, edges, ends, and profiles. Use the angled sash brush to work coating fully into every lap, cut end, and profile detail before rolling or spraying the adjacent field area.
  2. Roll field areas or use controlled spray with required back-brushing or back-rolling. Apply coating to the field area using the roller or sprayer specified for the siding texture, then back-brush or back-roll immediately to press the coating into the surface profile.
  3. Maintain wet edge and consistent film. Work in a planned sequence that keeps each section's edge wet until the adjoining section is coated.
  4. Observe sun and wind effects on drying. Adjust the work sequence to avoid coating a wall face in direct, high-heat sun exposure where the product's open time will be shortened beyond a workable range.
  5. Stop application when weather leaves product limits. Halt work immediately if surface temperature, humidity, wind, or dew-point conditions move outside the manufacturer's stated range, and record the stopping point on the log.
  6. Apply additional coats after the recoat interval. Confirm the manufacturer's recoat window has elapsed before applying a second coat.

J. Inspection, Cleanup, and Documentation

  1. Inspect laps, lower edges, end grain, fasteners, thin spots, overspray, and blocked drainage. Examine every lap and edge under raking light where practical, and confirm no drainage or movement feature was sealed or coated shut.
  2. Remove protection and clean the area. Remove masking and protective sheeting once the coating has reached the specified touch-dry condition.
  3. Document weather, preparation, primer, application, and coverage. Complete the Exterior Siding Preparation and Coating Log with every weather reading, preparation step, product used, and inspection result.

Common Inspection Points

Apply the same checks across the entire coated wall regardless of siding profile.

  • Weather check: confirm surface temperature, air temperature, humidity, and dew point were recorded and within limits at the start of application and at any point conditions changed.
  • Moisture check: confirm the substrate moisture reading was recorded and within the coating manufacturer's limit before coating began.
  • Lap check: confirm every lap and edge was brushed to complete coverage with no thin or missed sections.
  • Drainage check: confirm no weep, drainage opening, or movement joint was sealed or coated shut.
  • Film check: confirm the field coating shows no dry spray, runs, heavy edges, or roller-line inconsistency.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Exterior Siding Preparation and Coating Log is complete with no blank weather, preparation, or inspection entries.

Complete the Inspection Checklist section below before treating the coated wall as finished.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Coating Fails Along the Lower Edge of Siding Courses Within One Season

Probable cause: the lap and lower-edge area was rolled over without brushing coating fully into the lap before the field area was coated.
Confirmation: inspect the failed section for a visible coverage line at the lap consistent with a rolled-only application.
Correction: scrape and feather the failed section, prime the exposed substrate, and recoat with the brush-then-roll sequence specified in Section I.
Reinspection: confirm complete lap coverage on the corrected section and on adjoining courses before considering the repair complete.

Coating Shows Blistering or Poor Adhesion After a Recent Application

Probable cause: the siding was coated before it had fully dried after washing, or the substrate moisture reading exceeded the product's limit.
Confirmation: check the moisture reading recorded on the log against the coating manufacturer's stated limit and inspect the blistered area for trapped moisture.
Correction: remove the failed coating from the affected area, allow the substrate to dry fully, reverify the moisture reading, and recoat.
Reinspection: confirm the moisture reading is within limits before any recoat is applied.

Coating Develops a Flat, Chalky Film Shortly After Application

Probable cause: the surface temperature crossed below the dew point during the cure window, causing condensation on the fresh film.
Confirmation: review the weather log for the surface temperature and dew-point readings recorded during the application and cure period.
Correction: allow the coating to fully cure, then evaluate whether the affected area requires a full recoat once conditions return within limits.
Reinspection: confirm surface temperature remains above the dew point through the entire cure window on any subsequent application.

A Weep or Drainage Opening Is Found Painted Shut After Completion

Probable cause: the drainage feature was not identified before priming or field coating, and coating bridged the opening.
Confirmation: inspect the location against the original substrate verification record from Section E.
Correction: reopen the drainage feature using a method that does not damage the surrounding coating or the WRB, and touch up the disturbed edge.
Reinspection: confirm the drainage feature is clear and functioning before closing out the project.

Sprayed or Rolled Field Coating Shows Dry Spray or Orange Peel Texture

Probable cause: the field area was coated without the required back-brushing or back-rolling step, or conditions were too hot or too windy for the product's open time.
Confirmation: compare the affected area's texture with an adjoining properly back-brushed section, and review the recorded wind and surface-temperature readings.
Correction: back-brush or back-roll the affected area while the coating remains workable, or allow it to dry and apply a full recoat if it has already set.
Reinspection: confirm uniform film texture across the corrected section before proceeding to the next area.

Cleanup

Complete cleanup after the finish coat has reached its specified touch-dry condition.

  • Remove protective sheeting from windows, doors, roofing, masonry, landscaping, and vehicles
  • Clean brushes, rollers, and sprayer components using the method specified for the coating used
  • Dispose of scrape debris, wash water, and empty containers using the approved method identified in Section F
  • Store remaining primer and finish coating upright, sealed, and within the product's temperature limits
  • File the completed Exterior Siding Preparation and Coating Log with weather records and photographs

Field Terms

Lap
The overlapping edge where one siding board or course covers the course below it.
Field Area
The broad, flat exposed face of each siding board between laps, edges, and penetrations.
Back-Brushing / Back-Rolling
Working a brush or roller over just-applied coating to press it into surface texture and even the film.
Dew Point
The temperature at which airborne moisture condenses on a surface.
Weep / Drainage Opening
A manufactured gap or opening that allows moisture to exit the wall assembly.
Water-Resistive Barrier (WRB)
The building wrap or paper beneath the siding that prevents moisture intrusion.
Surface Temperature
The temperature of the siding substrate itself, measured directly rather than inferred from air temperature.
Feathering
Sanding a scraped edge transition smooth so it blends into the surrounding sound coating.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to create and operate a trade-coordination and handoff system for a residential project — the controlling process that defines which trade is responsible for which task, what condition an area must be in before the next trade takes over, and how that handoff is inspected, accepted, and documented. The finished work product is one approved trade-responsibility matrix, a set of measurable handoff conditions by area or system, an area-release and handoff form in active use, and a closed set of handoff records showing predecessor deficiencies, successor acceptance, and required documentation.

You will collect the approved subcontracts, purchase scopes, WBS, drawings, specifications, and schedule; build a trade-responsibility matrix and resolve scope gaps and overlaps in writing; identify every physical interface between trades and the dependencies at that interface; define measurable handoff conditions for each area or system; confirm each trade's mobilization readiness; coordinate work zones, penetrations, and routing to prevent damage and unsafe stacking; inspect predecessor work, record deficiencies, and accept or reject the handoff; protect accepted work; and close each handoff record only when documentation is complete.

This project depends on the scope baseline from Project 01, the WBS from Project 03, the sequence logic from Project 04, the baseline schedule from Project 05, the lookahead from Project 06, and the procurement plan from Project 07. It supplies the accepted, documented trade conditions that the coordination meetings in Project 09, the RFI process in Project 10, and the quality-control plan in Project 13 all depend on.

Where This Skill Fits

The trade-responsibility matrix built here is the direct successor to the responsibility matrix first created in Project 01 and refined in the Project 03 WBS dictionary — it takes those role assignments down to the level of individual physical interfaces between trades. The measurable handoff conditions defined here give the two-week lookahead from Project 06 a concrete readiness test, and they give the coordination meetings in Project 09 a specific agenda item: unresolved interface deficiencies.

The discipline of defining acceptance by measurable condition rather than an informal statement that an area is "ready enough" is the same discipline used later in the Project 13 quality-control plan and the Project 14 punch-list process. A handoff accepted without measurable conditions produces exactly the kind of disputed responsibility this project is built to prevent.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting approved subcontracts, purchase scopes, WBS packages, drawings, specifications, schedule, and quality requirements
  • building a trade-responsibility matrix and resolving scope gaps and overlaps in writing before mobilization
  • identifying every physical, information, material, access, utility, equipment, and inspection interface between trades
  • defining measurable handoff conditions by area or system, and defining who inspects and who accepts each handoff
  • confirming subcontractor licenses, insurance, permits, safety documentation, staffing, and understanding of scope before mobilization
  • coordinating work zones, penetrations, routing, and finish-trade sequencing to prevent unsafe stacking and rework
  • inspecting predecessor work, recording deficiencies, and recording acceptance, conditional acceptance, or rejection
  • protecting accepted work, tracking trade-caused damage, and closing the handoff record with complete documentation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • direct a subcontractor to perform work outside its license, permit, insurance, training, or contractual scope
  • transfer employer safety responsibility from the subcontractor, competent person, or permit holder to project coordination
  • accept a handoff on behalf of a party whose approval the subcontract or quality plan specifically requires
  • waive a required inspection, test, or authority approval merely to keep the schedule moving

Any conflict between a subcontract scope, the WBS package it is tied to, and the current approved drawings must be resolved in writing before that trade mobilizes.

Trade Note

A handoff accepted on an informal statement that an area is "ready enough" is not a handoff. It is an unrecorded assumption that becomes a dispute the first time a successor trade finds damaged, incomplete, or nonconforming predecessor work. Every handoff in this chapter is accepted against a written, measurable condition — never against a verbal impression.

Setup Standard

Do not mobilize a trade (Step 16) until the trade-responsibility matrix (Steps 2–4), the interface identification (Steps 5–6), and the measurable handoff conditions (Steps 7–10) for that trade's work area are complete. A trade mobilized before these steps are complete must have its handoff conditions confirmed before its predecessor area is released.

Shop Tip

Write every handoff condition as something you can check with a tape measure, a moisture meter, a level, a photograph, or a signed inspection record — not as a description of how the area should generally look or feel.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every trade handoff in this book, from a rough framing-to-MEP handoff to the final punch-list closure in Project 14, follows the same sequence: assign responsibility, identify the interface, define the measurable condition, confirm readiness, inspect, accept or reject, protect, and document. Learning that sequence here, across the full residential trade sequence, means every later coordination task starts from a method you already trust.

Interfaces, Handoff Conditions, and Acceptance

Every trade-coordination plan must identify the exact point at which one trade's work ends and the next trade's dependency begins, and must state the measurable condition that area must meet before release. Write the handoff condition immediately next to the interface it governs so a reader cannot mistake a general description of readiness for a specific, checkable requirement. An interface left ambiguous between predecessor and successor responsibility becomes a disputed deficiency, a damaged work claim, or an unsafe trade-stacking condition once construction proceeds.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Handoff on the Area-Release Form

Every area or system handoff must be recorded on the area-release and handoff form with date, predecessor, successor, inspection result, deficiencies, and acceptance status. Do not accept a verbal statement that an area is ready in place of the completed form. An unrecorded handoff leaves no evidence of who accepted the area or what condition it was in at acceptance.

Critical Coordination Cue — Do Not Declare an Area Ready With Unresolved Defects

Do not declare an area ready for handoff while material defects, incomplete work, or required approvals remain unresolved. Accepting a predecessor area under schedule pressure, without the measurable handoff condition being met, transfers an unrecorded risk to the successor trade and to the project.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Mobilization Readiness Before the Trade Arrives

Confirm mobilization date, working hours, crew size, delivery, parking, storage, waste, power, water, lighting, and sanitation with each trade before it arrives on site. A trade that mobilizes without confirmed access and utilities loses productive time and increases the chance of unsafe improvisation.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow the Subcontract's Scope and Notice Terms

Where the executed subcontract specifies a particular method, address, or timeframe for notice, inspection request, or handoff acceptance, follow that method exactly. Do not substitute an informal communication channel for the notice method the subcontract requires.

E. Preparation — Build the Responsibility Matrix and Identify Interfaces

  1. Collect approved subcontracts, purchase scopes, WBS packages, drawings, specifications, schedule, and quality requirements. Assemble every available document before building the matrix.
  2. Create a trade-responsibility matrix for labor, material, equipment, layout, backing, penetrations, protection, testing, cleanup, permits, and closeout. Assign one primary responsible party to each category.
  3. Identify gaps and overlaps among trade scopes. Compare each subcontract scope against the WBS package it is tied to.
  4. Resolve scope boundaries in writing before mobilization. Issue a written clarification for every gap or overlap found in Step 3.
  5. Identify every physical interface between trades. Walk the drawings and specifications by area and system to find each point of dependency.
  6. Identify information, material, access, utility, equipment, and inspection dependencies for each interface. Record what each successor trade needs from its predecessor at that interface.

F. Define Measurable Handoff Conditions

  1. Define measurable handoff conditions for each area or system. State the condition as a dimension, test result, or documented approval, not a general impression.
  2. Include dimensions, substrate, dryness, cleanliness, temperature, access, backing, rough-in, testing, approval, and protection where applicable. Cite the controlling drawing, specification, or quality-plan reference for each.
  3. Define which party inspects the predecessor work and who accepts the handoff. Name the inspecting party and the accepting party separately when they differ.
  4. Define exceptions that may be accepted without releasing the predecessor from correction responsibility. State the conditional-acceptance rule and its limits.
  5. Create an area-release and handoff form. Include area, predecessor, successor, inspection date, result, deficiencies, and acceptance status fields.
  6. Link handoffs to the master schedule and two-week lookahead. Confirm the planned handoff date supports the successor trade's scheduled start.

G. Confirm Mobilization Readiness

  1. Confirm subcontractor licenses, insurance, permits, safety documentation, staffing, and supervision as required. Verify each item against the current file before mobilization.
  2. Confirm current drawings, submittals, RFIs, selections, and approved changes have been distributed. Confirm the trade has the current version, not a superseded draft.
  3. Confirm the trade understands scope, exclusions, allowances, quality criteria, schedule, and closeout requirements. Review the subcontract scope and the responsibility matrix with the trade lead directly.
  4. Confirm mobilization date, working hours, crew size, delivery, parking, storage, waste, power, water, lighting, and sanitation. Record commitments in writing.
  5. Confirm ladders, scaffolds, lifts, cranes, temporary protection, and shared equipment responsibility. Identify which party provides and maintains shared equipment.
  6. Confirm required competent persons, inspections, tests, and trade permits. Verify these are assigned and scheduled before mobilization.

H. Coordinate Zones and Interfaces

  1. Coordinate work zones to avoid unsafe trade stacking. Assign exclusive or scheduled work zones where multiple trades would otherwise occupy the same area.
  2. Coordinate penetrations, sleeves, blocking, embeds, equipment pads, clearances, and access panels before closure. Confirm every required opening or embed is installed before the area is closed.
  3. Coordinate MEP routing with framing, cabinets, ceilings, structure, and finishes. Resolve routing conflicts before rough-in proceeds.
  4. Coordinate finish-trade sequencing to prevent damage and rework. Sequence finish trades to protect completed work from subsequent trade traffic.
  5. Hold a preinstallation meeting for high-risk or multi-trade work. Confirm attendance from every affected trade for complex interfaces.

I. Inspect, Accept, Protect, and Close the Handoff

  1. Inspect predecessor work before releasing the area. Check the completed work against the measurable handoff condition defined in Step 7.
  2. Record deficiencies with location, requirement, owner, and due date. Enter every deficiency in the interface deficiency log individually.
  3. Do not declare an area ready while material defects or required approvals remain unresolved. Withhold acceptance until the condition is met or a documented exception is recorded.
  4. Record handoff acceptance, conditional acceptance, or rejection. Complete the area-release and handoff form with the result and signature.
  5. Photograph concealed or high-risk interface conditions. Capture the condition before it is covered by successor work.
  6. Provide successor-trade protection requirements and limits on modifying accepted work. State what the successor trade may and may not alter without notice.
  7. Require notice before a trade cuts, drills, relocates, removes, or damages another trade's work. Route the notice through the responsible supervisor before the modification occurs.
  8. Track trade-caused damage and correction responsibility. Enter each incident in the trade-damage log with responsible party and correction status.
  9. Reinspect completed successor work before the next handoff. Confirm the successor's own work meets its handoff condition before it becomes the next predecessor.
  10. Update schedule, quality, cost, and change logs for handoff impacts. Reflect any delay, deficiency, or damage cost in the relevant project-control log.
  11. Review handoff performance in coordination meetings. Report unresolved interface deficiencies at the next Project 09 coordination meeting.
  12. Close the handoff record only when predecessor deficiencies, successor acceptance, and required documentation are complete. Do not close a record with an open deficiency or missing signature.
  13. Archive the trade matrix, meeting records, handoff forms, photographs, and unresolved responsibility decisions. File the complete record in the project job file established in Project 02.

Common Trade-Coordination Inspection

Inspect every trade handoff using the same checks regardless of the trades or interface involved.

  • Responsibility check: confirm exactly one primary responsible party is assigned to the task in dispute or under review.
  • Interface check: confirm every physical, information, material, access, utility, equipment, and inspection dependency at the interface is identified.
  • Handoff-condition check: confirm the release condition is measurable and checkable, not a general impression of readiness.
  • Mobilization check: confirm license, insurance, permits, safety documentation, staffing, and current documents were confirmed before the trade began.
  • Zone-conflict check: confirm no unsafe trade stacking, undocumented penetration, or unresolved routing conflict remains in the affected area.
  • Closure check: confirm the handoff record shows inspection, deficiencies, acceptance status, and required signatures before it is closed.

Complete the Trade-Coordination Inspection Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 09.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Successor Trade Finds the Area Not Ready After Acceptance

Probable cause: the handoff condition in Step 7 was written as a general impression rather than a measurable requirement, or the predecessor inspection in Step 24 was not performed against the stated condition.
Confirmation: compare the recorded handoff condition against the actual state of the area, and check whether the area-release form shows a completed inspection.
Correction: stop successor work in the affected area, correct the predecessor deficiency, reinspect, and reissue the handoff record.
Reinspection: repeat the handoff-condition check for the affected area and confirm the corrected condition against the written standard.

Two Trades Occupy the Same Work Zone at the Same Time

Probable cause: work-zone coordination in Step 19 was not completed, or the lookahead in Project 06 was published without checking the zone assignment against this project's coordination plan.
Confirmation: check the work-zone assignment for the area and date in question against both trades' scheduled tasks.
Correction: reassign work zones or reschedule one trade, and document the change in the lookahead and coordination-meeting record.
Reinspection: repeat the zone-conflict check for the affected area across the remainder of the current lookahead window.

A Trade Mobilizes Without Current Documents

Probable cause: Step 14 was not completed before the trade's mobilization date, or a document revision was issued after distribution without a follow-up transmittal.
Confirmation: check the document register for the revision the trade received against the current approved revision.
Correction: distribute the current documents immediately, confirm receipt, and verify that no work has proceeded from the superseded version.
Reinspection: repeat the mobilization-readiness check for the trade before further work in that area is accepted.

A Trade Cuts or Modifies Another Trade's Completed Work Without Notice

Probable cause: the notice requirement in Step 31 was not communicated to the trade, or the trade treated the completed work as unprotected because no protection requirement was recorded at handoff.
Confirmation: check the area-release form for a recorded protection requirement and check whether a notice was issued before the modification.
Correction: record the damage in the trade-damage log, assign correction responsibility, and reissue the protection requirement to all trades working in the area.
Reinspection: repeat the protection-requirement check on the corrected work and confirm the notice requirement is understood by all affected trades.

A Handoff Record Is Closed With an Open Deficiency

Probable cause: the closure check in Step 35 was skipped, or a deficiency was verbally reported as corrected without reinspection.
Confirmation: check the interface deficiency log for the item's status and compare it against the closed handoff record's signature and date.
Correction: reopen the handoff record, reinspect the deficiency, and close it only after verified correction.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check across the full deficiency log for the affected area, not only the reopened item.

Two Trades Each Assume the Other Installs a Required Component

Probable cause: the responsibility matrix in Step 2 was not resolved for that category of work, or an overlap identified in Step 3 was not corrected before mobilization.
Confirmation: check the responsibility matrix for the component in question and confirm whether exactly one primary party is assigned.
Correction: assign one primary responsible party, document the decision, and distribute the updated matrix to both trades.
Reinspection: repeat the responsibility check across the full matrix, not only the disputed item.

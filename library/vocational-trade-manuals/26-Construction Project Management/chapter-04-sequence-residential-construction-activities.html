Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build the sequencing logic that governs a residential construction project before any calendar date is assigned. The finished work product is one approved, version-controlled construction sequence logic chart that orders every activity in the approved work breakdown structure by physical construction logic, permit and inspection requirements, procurement and long-lead relationships, safety and quality-hold conditions, access and trade-stacking constraints, and cure or drying durations.

You will convert the approved WBS into measurable schedule activities, order preconstruction through closeout work by its true technical dependencies, identify concealment and inspection relationships that must remain visible, identify long-lead and owner-decision logic, identify parallel work opportunities and unsafe overlaps, assign predecessor-successor relationships, and test the resulting logic against a delayed inspection, a late material, and an owner-selection delay before it is approved.

This project uses the WBS from Project 03 as its only activity source. It produces the sequencing logic that Project 05 converts into a dated baseline schedule, that Project 06 converts into an executable two-week lookahead, and that Project 07 uses to calculate procurement release dates. A schedule built without this sequencing discipline is not a working schedule — it is a set of dates without logic behind them.

Where This Skill Fits

The scope baseline from Project 01, the job file from Project 02, and the work breakdown structure from Project 03 are the controlling inputs to this project. This project does not create new deliverables and does not revise the WBS; it orders the deliverables the WBS already defines.

The sequence logic produced here becomes the backbone of the Project 05 baseline schedule. Every duration, resource assignment, and milestone date in Project 05 is applied on top of the logic established here. The two-week lookahead in Project 06 draws its readiness checks directly from the predecessor conditions defined in this project, and the procurement plan in Project 07 calculates backward release dates from the installation logic established here.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • converting every lowest-level WBS work package into one or more measurable schedule activities with defined start and finish conditions
  • ordering preconstruction, mobilization, sitework, and foundation work according to approved plans and required inspections
  • ordering structural, dry-in, and envelope work according to cure, bracing, and weather-tight requirements
  • ordering MEP rough-in, insulation, and pre-drywall work according to inspection and concealment requirements
  • ordering finish, exterior, and closeout work according to protection, access, and owner-decision requirements
  • identifying long-lead, submittal, fabrication, and delivery logic for procurement-dependent activities
  • identifying parallel work opportunities, unsafe overlaps, and trade-stacking or access conflicts
  • assigning predecessor-successor relationships and testing the sequence against foreseeable delay

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • assign calendar dates, durations, or resource loading; those are established in Project 05 on top of the logic produced here
  • remove a required inspection, cure period, safety control, or approved design step to shorten the apparent sequence
  • invent a technical dependency that is not supported by the approved drawings, specifications, code, manufacturer instructions, or field condition
  • reorder or merge WBS work packages; the sequence must use the WBS exactly as approved in Project 03

Any technical logic that cannot be supported by an approved document, code requirement, manufacturer instruction, or verified field condition must be flagged as an assumption and confirmed before the sequence is approved.

Trade Note

A sequence built from habit rather than from the actual approved WBS, drawings, and inspection requirements will look reasonable and still be wrong. Every predecessor-successor relationship in this chapter must trace back to a physical, regulatory, procurement, safety, or quality reason recorded on the logic chart — not merely "that's how we usually do it."

Setup Standard

Do not begin building the visual logic chart (Step 31) until every activity has been converted from the WBS (Steps 1–3) and the physical, procurement, inspection, and access relationships (Steps 4–28) have been identified. A logic chart built before these relationships are identified must be revised before it is submitted for review.

Shop Tip

State every activity's finish condition as an observable result, not an elapsed time. "Rough plumbing complete and pressure-tested" is a finish condition. "Plumbing — three days" is a duration, not logic, and belongs in Project 05, not here.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every sequence logic chart in this book, from this chapter's residential sequence to the Chapter 20 capstone project, follows the same discipline: convert, order, relate, test, approve. Sequence logic always precedes calendar dates, and it is never adjusted merely to make a schedule appear shorter.

Concealment, Inspection, and the Weather-Tight Milestone

Work that will be concealed by a later trade — framing before drywall, rough plumbing before wall closure, waterproofing before backfill — must remain in the logic chart as visible until the governing inspection or internal quality check is recorded as passed. Do not model a concealment activity as complete merely because the trade reports the work finished; completion for sequencing purposes means the hold point has been satisfied.

Documentation Cue — Record the Logic Baseline Date, Revision, and Approver

Every version of the sequence logic chart must carry a baseline date, a revision number, and the name and role of the approver. Do not distribute an undated or unnumbered draft as though it were the approved baseline. A superseded logic chart left in active use can carry an outdated dependency into the Project 05 schedule.

Critical Logic Cue — Do Not Remove a Required Hold Point to Shorten the Sequence

Do not accelerate the sequence by removing a required inspection, cure period, safety control, or approved design step. If schedule pressure makes a hold point appear to be the obstacle, escalate the conflict through the project's decision authority rather than silently deleting the dependency from the logic chart.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Access and Crew Conflicts Before Modeling Parallel Work

Before marking two activities as capable of proceeding in parallel, confirm they do not require the same access, laydown area, lift, scaffold, or crane at the same time, and that the combined crews do not create unsafe congestion in the same work area.

Procurement Logic Cue — Model Fabrication and Delivery as Real Dependencies

Custom, engineered, imported, and finish-selection items must be modeled with their full submittal, approval, fabrication, shipping, and delivery logic as predecessors to installation. Do not model a long-lead item's installation as though the material will simply be available when the crew is ready.

E. Preparation — Convert the WBS and Identify Preconstruction Logic

  1. Use the approved WBS as the complete activity source. Do not add, remove, merge, or reorder WBS work packages while converting activities.
  2. Convert each lowest-level work package into one or more measurable schedule activities. Split a package into multiple activities only where the split reflects a genuine logic break, not merely convenience.
  3. Define the start condition and finish condition for each activity. State each condition as an observable result, not an elapsed duration.
  4. Identify mandatory preconstruction activities, including permits, design approvals, selections, submittals, contracts, insurance, and utility coordination. Record each as a predecessor to mobilization.
  5. Identify mobilization, site access, temporary utilities, fencing, protection, sanitation, and layout prerequisites. Record each as a predecessor to the first physical work activity.
  6. Order clearing, erosion control, excavation, utilities, drainage, grading, and foundation work according to approved plans and inspections. Record the governing inspection for each concealment-sensitive step.
  7. Order footing, foundation, waterproofing, drainage, backfill, slab, and structural activities according to cure and inspection requirements. Record the cure duration as a lag, not as part of the activity's own duration description.
  8. Order floor, wall, roof, stair, bracing, sheathing, and structural-connector work. Record bracing and connector requirements as finish conditions on the predecessor activity.
  9. Identify crane, lift, delivery, and temporary-bracing dependencies. Record each as a predecessor condition on the affected structural activity.

F. Structural, Envelope, and MEP Logic

  1. Order roofing, flashing, windows, doors, WRB, siding, and dry-in activities. Sequence each according to the manufacturer's required installation order.
  2. Define the project weather-tight milestone and the exact conditions required to achieve it. State the milestone as a list of completed envelope conditions, not as a date.
  3. Order plumbing, electrical, HVAC, gas, fire, low-voltage, and specialty rough-in work. Record each trade's rough-in as a successor to the weather-tight milestone where interior protection requires it.
  4. Identify framing, drilling, blocking, equipment, and access dependencies among MEP trades. Record cross-trade dependencies explicitly rather than assuming informal coordination.
  5. Identify rough-in inspections and work that must remain visible. Record each inspection as a hold point before the successor concealment activity may begin.
  6. Order insulation, air sealing, energy testing, fire blocking, and pre-drywall inspections. Record the pre-drywall inspection as the final hold point before drywall may begin.
  7. Order drywall, curing, sanding, primer, cabinets, trim, flooring, paint, fixtures, devices, and finish hardware. Record drywall cure and primer dry time as lags supported by manufacturer instructions.
  8. Identify finish-protection and humidity or temperature dependencies. Record any environmental condition required before a finish activity may begin.
  9. Order exterior flatwork, decks, fences, hardscape, landscaping, utility completion, and final grading based on access and weather. Record access conflicts with interior finish work explicitly.
  10. Identify permanent power, water, gas, HVAC, security, and utility-release milestones. Record each utility release as a predecessor to the activities that depend on it.

G. Procurement, Access, and Relationship Logic

  1. Identify owner-selection and material-release deadlines required before fabrication or installation. Record each deadline as a predecessor to the affected procurement activity.
  2. Identify design, submittal, fabrication, shipping, delivery, and installation logic for long-lead items. Record the complete chain as predecessors to installation, not as a single combined activity.
  3. Identify required samples, mockups, first-work inspections, and quality hold points. Record each as a predecessor to full-scale installation of the same assembly.
  4. Identify permit, special, manufacturer, utility, lender, and owner inspection relationships. Record the responsible party and required lead time for each.
  5. Identify curing, drying, acclimation, testing, startup, commissioning, and training durations as logic conditions. Record each as a lag supported by a specific document or manufacturer instruction.
  6. Identify access conflicts, shared equipment, limited laydown, occupied-area phasing, and trade-exclusion zones. Record each conflict and the activities it affects.
  7. Identify activities that cannot overlap safely or without quality loss. Record the reason for each exclusion.
  8. Identify activities that can proceed in parallel by area, phase, floor, or system. Confirm the parallel activities do not share the access, equipment, or crew conflicts identified in Step 25.
  9. Assign finish-to-start, start-to-start, finish-to-finish, or approved lag relationships. Assign the relationship type that matches the actual technical condition, not the type that is easiest to enter.
  10. Use lags only when they represent a real technical condition and not to hide an unmodeled activity. Support every lag with a cited document, code requirement, or manufacturer instruction.

H. Build, Test, and Approve the Logic Chart

  1. Identify potential rework loops caused by premature closure or incomplete handoff. Record each loop and the hold point that should prevent it.
  2. Create a visual logic chart from mobilization through owner handoff. Follow the document schedule in Section B.
  3. Walk the logic with field supervision and key trades. Record every comment received and how it was addressed.
  4. Test the sequence against one delayed inspection, one late material, and one owner-selection delay. Record the effect of each test scenario on the logic in the sequence-test record.
  5. Revise the logic to preserve safe alternate work paths where possible. Do not accept a sequence with no alternate path around a single point of failure without documenting the risk.
  6. Approve and version the sequence logic before building the baseline schedule. Record the approval on the sequence-approval record before Project 05 begins.

Common Sequence Logic Inspection

Inspect every sequence logic chart using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Activity-conversion check: confirm every lowest-level WBS work package appears as at least one activity with a defined start and finish condition.
  • Physical logic check: confirm sitework through closeout activities are ordered according to approved drawings, cure, and structural requirements without an invented dependency.
  • Concealment check: confirm every activity that will be concealed remains open in the logic until its governing inspection or hold point is satisfied.
  • Procurement check: confirm long-lead and owner-selected items are modeled with complete submittal-to-delivery logic as predecessors to installation.
  • Access and safety check: confirm parallel-work activities are free of unsafe overlap, access conflict, or unmanaged trade stacking.
  • Testing check: confirm the sequence has been tested against a delayed inspection, a late material, and an owner-selection delay, with results recorded.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Trade Arrives to Find the Predecessor Work Not Actually Complete

Probable cause: the finish condition for the predecessor activity was defined as a duration or a general statement rather than an observable result, so "complete" was judged inconsistently.
Confirmation: check the activity's finish condition in the logic chart against the Step 3 standard and compare it with the field condition found.
Correction: rewrite the finish condition as an observable, verifiable result and confirm the same standard is used by field supervision.
Reinspection: repeat the activity-conversion check on the corrected activity and any activity sharing the same predecessor.

Work Is Concealed Before the Required Inspection

Probable cause: the concealment-sensitive activity was not linked to its governing inspection as a hold point in Step 15, so the successor activity was allowed to proceed.
Confirmation: check the logic chart for the affected activity and confirm whether an inspection hold point was recorded as its finish condition.
Correction: stop the successor activity, document the concealment, coordinate reinspection or engineering review as required, and add the missing hold point to the logic chart.
Reinspection: repeat the regulatory logic check across every concealment-sensitive activity in the affected phase, not only the one found.

A Long-Lead Item Is Not Available When the Crew Is Ready to Install It

Probable cause: the item's submittal, approval, fabrication, or shipping logic from Step 21 was not modeled as a full predecessor chain, or was modeled as a single combined activity that hid the true lead time.
Confirmation: check the procurement logic for the item against the Step 21 standard and confirm each stage is a separate, dated predecessor.
Correction: rebuild the procurement chain with each stage as a separate activity, and identify an executable alternate work path for the affected crew.
Reinspection: repeat the procurement logic check for every long-lead item in the same trade or supplier group.

Two Trades Are Working the Same Area at the Same Time Unsafely

Probable cause: the activities were marked as capable of proceeding in parallel in Step 27 without confirming they were free of the access, equipment, or crew conflicts identified in Step 25.
Confirmation: check the access and parallel-work logic for both activities and compare it against the actual conditions found in the field.
Correction: separate the activities by area, phase, or time, or assign an exclusion zone, and update the logic chart with the corrected relationship.
Reinspection: repeat the access and safety check across all activities sharing the same work area or equipment.

A Lag Cannot Be Explained When Questioned

Probable cause: the lag was entered to compensate for an unmodeled activity or an assumed delay rather than a real technical condition, in violation of Step 29.
Confirmation: check the lag against its cited supporting document, code requirement, or manufacturer instruction in the relationship log.
Correction: remove the unsupported lag, model the missing activity explicitly if one exists, and cite the correct supporting document for any remaining lag.
Reinspection: repeat the relationship-assignment check across all lags in the same phase.

The Sequence Has No Alternate Path Around a Single Delayed Activity

Probable cause: the logic test in Step 33 was not performed, or was performed without revising the sequence to preserve an alternate work path as required by Step 34.
Confirmation: check the sequence-test record for the delayed-inspection, late-material, and owner-selection-delay scenarios and confirm each was tested against the current logic.
Correction: identify and document an executable alternate work path, or record the accepted single-point risk and escalate it to the decision authority.
Reinspection: repeat the testing check against all three required delay scenarios on the revised logic.

Field Staff Are Working From a Superseded Logic Chart

Probable cause: the superseded draft was not moved to the archive location, or the approved baseline was not distributed to field supervision and key trades.
Confirmation: check the document register for the baseline number and revision currently in the active location, and confirm distribution records against the review list from Step 32.
Correction: remove the superseded draft from active use, redistribute the current approved logic chart, and confirm receipt from affected parties.
Reinspection: repeat the approval and distribution check for the current baseline.

Field Terms

Sequence Logic Baseline
The one current, approved version of the sequence logic chart the Project 05 schedule is built from.
Predecessor
An activity that must reach its defined finish condition before a dependent activity may begin.
Successor
An activity that depends on a predecessor's defined finish condition before it may begin.
Finish-to-Start Relationship
A dependency in which the successor may not begin until the predecessor's finish condition is met.
Lag
A required waiting period between activities representing a real technical condition such as cure or drying time.
Weather-Tight Milestone
The defined point at which the envelope is sufficiently enclosed to permit interior work, stated by condition rather than date.
Hold Point
A required inspection or quality check that must be satisfied before a concealment-sensitive activity's successor may begin.
Long-Lead Item
A custom, engineered, imported, or limited-availability item whose submittal-to-delivery chain must be modeled as a full predecessor sequence.
Trade Stacking
Unsafe or unproductive congestion created when multiple trades share the same work area, access, or equipment at the same time.
Alternate Work Path
A safe, executable sequence of work that can proceed if a planned activity is delayed by inspection, material, or owner decision.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to define and gain approval for a residential project scope statement — the controlling document that establishes what the project includes, what it excludes, who has authority over what decisions, and how change will be handled. The finished work product is one approved, version-controlled scope statement supported by a stakeholder list, a responsibility matrix, documented assumptions and constraints, and a resolved scope-gap list.

You will collect and review the owner request and executed project documents, define measurable objectives and the completed physical outcome, identify mandatory authority conditions, list every deliverable required for owner acceptance, define inclusions and explicit exclusions, identify stakeholders and their decision authority, document assumptions and constraints, compare the draft scope against the estimate and permit plan, resolve gaps and ambiguities, and obtain written approval of the baseline.

This is the first project in the book. It establishes the single controlling scope baseline that every later project in this book depends on: the job file in Project 02, the work breakdown structure in Project 03, the schedule in Project 04 and Project 05, procurement in Project 06, trade coordination in Project 07, and every planning document through the Chapter 20 capstone all trace back to the scope statement produced here.

Where This Skill Fits

Every later document in this book depends on the scope baseline produced here. The job file in Project 02 is organized around the sections and references this scope statement establishes. The work breakdown structure in Project 03 converts this scope statement's deliverables into schedulable, cost-coded work packages. The change-control system in Project 11 exists specifically to prevent informal expansion of the baseline you approve in this project.

The discipline of separating included work, excluded work, allowances, alternates, and future phases — taught in this chapter — is the same discipline used later to separate original contract scope from approved and pending changes throughout the rest of the book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting and reviewing the owner request, executed agreement, estimate, drawings, specifications, addenda, permits, and prior correspondence
  • defining measurable business and owner objectives and the completed physical outcome
  • identifying mandatory permit, code, design, lender, insurer, utility, HOA, and authority conditions
  • listing every primary deliverable required for owner acceptance, including temporary work, demolition, and closeout deliverables
  • defining included design, procurement, and installation responsibilities, and identifying owner-furnished and third-party work
  • writing explicit exclusions and distinguishing them from deferred work, alternates, allowances, and future phases
  • identifying stakeholders, their authority, and a responsibility matrix
  • documenting existing-condition information, assumptions, and constraints
  • comparing the draft scope against the estimate and permit plan, resolving gaps, and obtaining approval of the baseline

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • interpret contract law, render a legal opinion, or resolve a contract dispute; the scope statement is a project-management coordination document, not a legal instrument
  • make design, engineering, or code-compliance decisions; those remain with the licensed design professional, engineer, or authority having jurisdiction
  • approve cost, schedule, design, or scope changes without the authority identified in Step 16 of the procedure below
  • treat a verbal owner conversation, text message, or field sketch as sufficient authorization to expand the approved scope baseline once it is frozen

Any conflict between the owner request, the executed agreement, the drawings, and prior correspondence must be identified and resolved in writing before the scope baseline is approved.

Trade Note

Every scope statement in this chapter is built from the executed agreement and approved project documents — never from an unconfirmed verbal understanding of what the owner wants. Treating a verbal or informal understanding as controlling, rather than the written and approved record, is the single most common source of the scope disputes diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin drafting the scope statement (Step 30) until the document collection and review (Steps 1–5) and the stakeholder and responsibility identification (Steps 15–17) are complete. A scope statement drafted before these steps are complete must be revised before it is submitted for review.

Shop Tip

Write every exclusion in language a non-tradesperson can understand without trade assumptions. "Excludes finish plumbing fixtures" is clear. "Excludes fixtures" is not, because it does not say whether that means faucets, light fixtures, or both.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every scope statement in this book, from this chapter's residential scope baseline to the Chapter 20 capstone project charter, follows the same sequence: collect, define, bound, assign, document, reconcile, approve. Learning that sequence here, on one residential project, means every later planning document starts from a method you already trust.

Included Work, Excluded Work, and Deferred Work

Every scope statement must state what the contractor will perform, furnish, or coordinate, and separately, what the contractor will not. Write exclusions immediately after the related inclusion so a reader cannot mistake a deferred item, an allowance, or an alternate for a firm inclusion or a firm exclusion. An item left ambiguous between included and excluded becomes an unpriced scope dispute once construction begins.

Documentation Cue — Record the Baseline Date, Revision, and Approver

Every version of the scope statement must carry a baseline date, a revision number, and the name and role of the approver. Do not distribute an undated or unnumbered draft as though it were the approved baseline. Recording only the current version, without the revision history, allows a superseded draft to be mistaken for the controlling document later in the project.

Critical Scope Cue — Written Authorization Before Expansion

Do not allow an informal request, text message, field sketch, or verbal conversation to change the approved scope baseline. Once the baseline is approved and distributed, any expansion, reduction, or substitution must go through the written change-control process established in Project 11. Treating a verbal understanding as authorization is a leading cause of unpriced and undocumented scope expansion.

Field Coordination Cue — Confirm Stakeholder Contact Information

Confirm the current name, role, organization, and preferred contact method for every stakeholder before the scope statement is distributed. A scope statement routed to an outdated contact, or missing a required approver, delays approval and creates confusion about who has authority over a given decision.

Contract Instruction Cue — Follow the Executed Agreement's Notice and Approval Terms

Where the executed agreement specifies a particular method, address, or timeframe for notices, approvals, or scope changes, follow that method exactly. Do not substitute an informal communication channel for the notice method the contract requires, even when it would be faster.

E. Preparation — Collect Documents and Identify Project Basics

  1. Collect the owner request, executed agreement or proposal, approved estimate, drawings, specifications, addenda, permits, clarifications, selections, and prior correspondence. Assemble every available document before drafting begins.
  2. Identify the project address, property boundaries, occupied conditions, project type, delivery method, and intended use. Record these facts from the agreement and permit application.
  3. Define the business and owner objectives in measurable terms. State the objective in terms that can later be checked as met or not met.
  4. Define the completed physical outcome by area, system, room, structure, and trade. Draw the outcome directly from the approved drawings and specifications.
  5. Identify mandatory permit, code, design, lender, insurer, utility, HOA, and authority conditions. Record each condition and the document or authority that establishes it.
  6. List every primary deliverable required for the owner to accept the work. Include each deliverable individually rather than as a general category.

F. Objectives, Deliverables, and Authority Conditions

  1. List temporary work, protection, access, utilities, storage, sanitation, security, and cleanup needed to deliver the project. Record these as deliverables even though they are not part of the permanent finished work.
  2. Identify demolition, salvage, disposal, testing, inspection, commissioning, training, warranty, and closeout deliverables. Record each as an individual item on the deliverable list.
  3. Define included design, engineering, surveying, selections, submittals, mockups, samples, and shop-drawing responsibilities. State which party is responsible for each.
  4. Define included procurement, delivery, unloading, staging, installation, startup, and warranty responsibilities. State which party is responsible for each.
  5. Identify owner-furnished, contractor-installed and owner-furnished, owner-installed items. List each item and its furnishing and installation responsibility separately.
  6. Identify work by utility, municipality, separate contractor, landlord, tenant, HOA, designer, or other third party. Record the party and the work they are responsible for.
  7. Define explicit exclusions in language that can be understood without trade assumptions. State each exclusion as a specific item, not a general category.
  8. Distinguish excluded work from deferred work, alternates, allowances, and future phases. Label each item with its correct category rather than leaving it ambiguous.
  9. Identify stakeholders by name or role, organization, interest, authority, contact method, and required communication. Build the stakeholder list from the agreement and project directory.
  10. Identify the person authorized to approve scope, cost, schedule, design, and final acceptance. Confirm this authority in writing before proceeding.
  11. Create a responsibility matrix for owner, contractor, subcontractors, suppliers, designer, inspectors, utilities, and other parties. Assign one primary responsible party to each category of work.

G. Exclusions, Assumptions, and Constraints

  1. Document existing-condition information and conditions that remain concealed or unverified. State clearly which existing conditions have not been confirmed by direct observation.
  2. Document assumptions concerning dimensions, access, working hours, utilities, occupancy, weather, soil, structure, and existing systems. Record each assumption individually in the assumptions log.
  3. Document constraints concerning permits, budget, financing, schedule, neighborhood, storage, noise, dust, parking, and seasonal work. Record each constraint individually in the constraints log.
  4. Define quality and performance requirements using approved documents, samples, manufacturer instructions, and acceptance criteria. Cite the controlling document for each requirement rather than describing it generally.
  5. Define milestone and completion conditions without assigning unsupported dates. State the condition that defines each milestone; leave date assignment to the Project 04 schedule.
  6. Define how owner selections and allowance decisions will be made and by what deadlines. Record the decision process and the consequence of a missed deadline.
  7. Define the process for RFIs, substitutions, hidden conditions, owner requests, delays, and change orders. Reference the change-control process established for the project.
  8. Define which work may not begin until approval, permit, inspection, selection, or payment condition is satisfied. List each hold condition and the work it affects.

H. Draft, Reconcile, and Approve the Scope Statement

  1. Compare the scope against the Book 24 estimate and identify priced items, unpriced items, gaps, and duplicated items. Record every discrepancy found.
  2. Compare the scope against the Book 25 permit plan and identify regulated work and permit-holder responsibilities. Confirm each regulated item is assigned to the correct responsible party.
  3. Create a scope-gap and ambiguity list. Record every unresolved item discovered during Steps 26–27 in the scope-gap log.
  4. Resolve gaps through written clarification, approved assumption, allowance, alternate, exclusion, or contract revision. Do not leave a gap-log item unresolved when the draft is submitted for review.
  5. Prepare the draft scope statement in logical project sequence. Follow the document schedule in Section B.
  6. Review the draft with the owner, project manager, estimator, field lead, designer, and key trades as applicable. Distribute the draft far enough in advance for informed review.
  7. Record comments and revise the statement without deleting unresolved risk. Preserve a record of every comment received and how it was addressed.
  8. Obtain approval from the authorized parties. Confirm the approval is from the person or role identified in Step 16.
  9. Assign the scope baseline number and date. Record both directly on the scope-approval record.
  10. Distribute the approved scope and archive the superseded drafts. Move superseded drafts to the archive location so they cannot be mistaken for the current baseline.
  11. Create a change-control rule preventing informal expansion of the approved baseline. State the rule in the scope statement itself and confirm it matches the process defined in Step 24.

Common Scope-Statement Inspection

Inspect every scope statement using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Objective check: confirm every objective is stated in measurable terms that can later be checked as met or not met.
  • Deliverable check: confirm every item required for owner acceptance is listed individually, including temporary work, demolition, and closeout deliverables.
  • Inclusion/exclusion check: confirm every exclusion is stated in plain language and correctly distinguished from deferred work, allowances, and alternates.
  • Stakeholder and authority check: confirm every stakeholder is identified with contact information and that decision authority is explicitly named for scope, cost, schedule, design, and acceptance.
  • Reconciliation check: confirm the scope has been compared against the estimate and permit plan and that every identified gap has been resolved.
  • Approval check: confirm written approval was obtained from the authorized party and that the baseline number and date are recorded before distribution.

Complete the Scope Statement Inspection Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 02.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Exclusion Is Disputed as an Inclusion During Construction

Probable cause: the exclusion was written using a general category rather than a specific item, or it was not distinguished from a deferred item or allowance.
Confirmation: re-read the exclusion language against the plain-language standard in Step 14 and check whether the item appears anywhere else in the document as included, deferred, or allowance work.
Correction: issue a written clarification identifying the item specifically, route it through the change-control process, and update the scope statement's next controlled revision.
Reinspection: repeat the inclusion/exclusion check on the revised document.

Two Parties Each Assume the Other Owns a Task

Probable cause: the responsibility matrix in Step 17 was not completed for that category of work, or the matrix lists a general party such as "trades" instead of one primary responsible party.
Confirmation: check the responsibility matrix for the task in question and confirm whether exactly one primary party is assigned.
Correction: assign one primary responsible party to the task, document the decision, and distribute the updated matrix to all affected parties.
Reinspection: repeat the responsibility check across the full matrix, not only the disputed item.

Work Proceeds Based on a Verbal Owner Request

Probable cause: the change-control rule from Step 36 was not communicated to field staff, or a verbal conversation was treated as equivalent to written authorization.
Confirmation: check whether a written change record exists for the work performed and whether the person who authorized it holds the decision authority identified in Step 16.
Correction: stop work pending written authorization where safely possible, document the conversation, and route it through the change-control process before continuing.
Reinspection: confirm the change-control rule has been reissued to the field team and acknowledged.

An Item Appears in the Estimate but Not in the Scope Statement, or the Reverse

Probable cause: the reconciliation in Step 26 was not completed, or a gap identified during reconciliation was not resolved before approval.
Confirmation: re-run the comparison between the current scope statement and the current estimate and check the scope-gap log for the item.
Correction: resolve the discrepancy through written clarification, allowance, alternate, exclusion, or contract revision, and update both documents.
Reinspection: repeat the full reconciliation check between the scope statement and the estimate.

Field Staff Are Working From a Superseded Draft

Probable cause: the superseded draft was not moved to the archive location, or the approved baseline was not distributed to every stakeholder identified in Step 15.
Confirmation: check the document register for the baseline number and revision currently in the active location, and confirm distribution records against the stakeholder list.
Correction: remove the superseded draft from active use, redistribute the current approved baseline, and confirm receipt from affected parties.
Reinspection: repeat the approval and distribution check for the current baseline.

The Scope Statement Cannot Be Approved Because the Approver Is Unclear

Probable cause: the authorized approver from Step 16 was not confirmed before drafting began, or more than one party believes they hold approval authority.
Confirmation: check the executed agreement and project directory for the named decision authority, and confirm this matches the person the draft was routed to for approval.
Correction: resolve the authority question in writing with the owner and project management before requesting approval again.
Reinspection: repeat the approval check once a single confirmed approver has signed the baseline.

An Assumption Later Proves False

Probable cause: the assumption was not documented individually in the assumptions log at Step 19, so its effect on scope was not visible when conditions changed.
Confirmation: check the assumptions log for the specific assumption and confirm whether it was recorded and linked to the affected scope items.
Correction: document the actual condition, evaluate its effect on scope, cost, and schedule, and route any resulting change through the change-control process.
Reinspection: repeat the assumptions and constraints check for the full log, not only the affected item.

Cleanup

This project ends with the approved scope statement filed and distributed, not left as an open draft.

  • Confirm the scope-approval record is signed, dated, and filed with the project documents
  • Confirm the scope-gap log shows every item resolved or explicitly carried forward as a documented open risk
  • Move every superseded draft to the archive location and confirm it is clearly marked as superseded
  • Distribute the approved baseline to every stakeholder identified in the stakeholder list
  • Provide the approved scope statement, responsibility matrix, and reconciliation records to Project 02 for inclusion in the project job file

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to plan, conduct, document, and close one residential jobsite coordination meeting. The finished work product is one completed meeting record: a distributed agenda, verified attendee list, reviewed schedule and safety status, recorded decisions, assigned action items with owners and due dates, and published minutes filed in the project job file.

You will define the meeting purpose and required attendees, review prior action items before building the agenda, collect current project-control information, run the meeting starting with safety, walk the schedule, procurement, trade handoffs, quality, inspections, RFIs, changes, and owner decisions, assign every action item to one named person with one due date, publish minutes promptly, and update every project-control log the meeting affected.

This project depends on the scope baseline from Project 01, the job file from Project 02, the schedule from Project 05, the lookahead from Project 06, the procurement plan from Project 07, and the trade-coordination system from Project 08. A coordination meeting that is not built on current versions of those documents produces decisions based on stale information. The coordination-meeting discipline built here also directly supports Project 10 through Project 17, because RFIs, changes, safety issues, quality deficiencies, inspections, and owner communication are all reviewed and actioned through this same meeting structure.

Where This Skill Fits

The coordination meeting is the recurring control point where every other project-management system in this book is checked, updated, and advanced. The schedule from Project 05 and the lookahead from Project 06 are reviewed here for actual progress and near-term commitments. The procurement log from Project 07 is reviewed here for items threatening installation dates. The trade-handoff system from Project 08 is reviewed here for unresolved interface deficiencies. The RFI process in Project 10, the change-control system in Project 11, the safety plan in Project 12, the quality plan in Project 13, and the inspection system in Project 16 all rely on this meeting to surface issues, assign owners, and record decisions.

The discipline of distinguishing discussion from decision, and assigning one named owner and one due date to every action, taught in this chapter, is the same discipline used later throughout the book whenever an issue must be tracked from identification to verified closure.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • defining the meeting purpose, frequency, location, platform, duration, required attendees, and decision authority
  • reviewing prior minutes and open action items before building the agenda
  • collecting current schedule, lookahead, procurement, RFI, change, safety, quality, inspection, and cost information
  • preparing and distributing an agenda that opens with safety and covers status, schedule, procurement, inspections, RFIs, changes, owner decisions, trade interfaces, and closeout
  • conducting the meeting, recording actual commitments, and distinguishing discussion, recommendation, decision, instruction, approval, and pending information
  • assigning each action item to one responsible person with one due date and one closure criterion
  • publishing minutes promptly and updating every project-control log the meeting affected
  • following up on overdue items before the next meeting

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • approve scope, cost, schedule, or design changes in the meeting unless the meeting attendee holds the decision authority identified in the Project 01 scope statement or the executed agreement
  • direct unsafe work, bypass a safety control, or override a required inspection to satisfy a schedule commitment made in the meeting
  • record “team” or an unnamed group as the responsible party for an action item
  • treat verbal discussion in the meeting as a substitute for the written notice method the executed agreement requires for formal decisions

Any conflict between what is discussed in the meeting and the current approved scope, schedule, or budget baseline must be identified and routed through the appropriate change-control, RFI, or schedule-update process rather than treated as an informal revision.

Trade Note

A coordination meeting that only repeats status that is already visible in the schedule and logs has failed its purpose. Every meeting in this chapter must produce at least one recorded decision or one assigned action item, or the meeting record must state explicitly that no decision or action was required.

Setup Standard

Do not begin conducting the meeting (Step 10) until the agenda has been prepared and distributed with sufficient advance notice (Steps 1–9) and every trade lead has updated staffing, material, constraints, and commitments as required by Step 9. A meeting conducted without a distributed agenda must be treated as a status update, not a coordination meeting, and rescheduled if decisions are required.

Shop Tip

Read back every critical decision, date, and commitment before adjournment, in the exact words that will appear in the minutes. This single habit resolves more disputed commitments than any amount of after-the-fact clarification.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every coordination meeting in this book, from a routine weekly jobsite meeting to a Chapter 20 capstone project meeting, follows the same sequence: prepare, open with safety, review status, review open items, decide, assign, publish, follow up. Learning that sequence here, on one residential project, means every later coordination meeting starts from a method you already trust.

Discussion, Recommendation, Decision, Instruction, and Approval

A coordination meeting record must distinguish these five categories every time information is exchanged. A comment made in discussion is not a decision until the authorized decision maker states it as one, and the minutes must record it that way. Treating discussion as decision, or a recommendation as an instruction, is a leading cause of work performed on an unauthorized basis.

Documentation Cue — Record One Owner and One Due Date Per Action

Every action item must carry exactly one responsible person and one due date. Do not record "team," "trades," or "all subs" as the responsible party. An action item without one named owner cannot be tracked to closure and will not appear as overdue on any individual's list.

Critical Coordination Cue — Confirm Decision Authority Before Recording a Decision

Before recording any item as a decision, confirm the attendee who stated it holds the decision authority for that subject under the Project 01 scope statement, the executed agreement, or company policy. Recording a comment from an unauthorized attendee as a decision creates a false record that field staff may rely on to proceed without proper authorization.

Field Safety Cue — Open With Emergency, Safety, Access, and Weather Issues

Every coordination meeting must begin with emergency, safety, access, weather, and public-protection issues before status topics are discussed. Do not defer a safety item to the end of the agenda to save time; an unresolved hazard discussed last is a hazard that remains uncorrected for the length of the meeting.

Contract Instruction Cue — Confirm Written Notice Where the Agreement Requires It

Where the executed agreement requires a specific method for formal notice of a schedule change, delay claim, or contractual decision, a verbal statement in the meeting does not satisfy that requirement. Confirm the required written notice is issued separately according to the agreement's terms.

E. Preparation — Define the Meeting and Build the Agenda

  1. Define the meeting purpose, frequency, location, platform, duration, required attendees, and decision authority. Record these facts before the first meeting and confirm they remain current for each recurring meeting.
  2. Review prior minutes and open action items before creating the agenda. Identify every item that remains open, overdue, or newly closed since the last meeting.
  3. Collect current schedule, lookahead, procurement, RFI, change, safety, quality, inspection, and cost information. Pull the current version of each log rather than relying on memory of the last meeting.
  4. Identify decisions required during the meeting and who must attend to make them. Confirm the person holding decision authority for each anticipated decision is invited.
  5. Prepare an agenda with project status, safety, quality, schedule, procurement, inspections, RFIs, changes, owner decisions, trade interfaces, and closeout. Order the agenda so safety appears first.
  6. List open action items with owner, due date, and status. Attach the current action-item log to the agenda.
  7. Attach or link only the documents needed for informed discussion. Avoid distributing unrelated documents that dilute preparation.
  8. Distribute the agenda far enough in advance for preparation. Confirm the distribution list matches the required-attendee list.
  9. Require trade leads to update staffing, material, constraints, and commitments before the meeting. Confirm each trade lead's update is received before the meeting begins.

F. Open the Meeting and Review Safety and Schedule Status

  1. Start with emergency, safety, access, weather, and public-protection issues. Address these before any status topic, regardless of agenda length.
  2. Review incidents, near misses, observations, corrective actions, and upcoming high-risk work through the approved safety process. Confirm each item has a responsible party and status.
  3. Review the baseline milestone status and current forecast. Compare actual progress against the Project 05 baseline schedule.
  4. Review the two-week lookahead by area, trade, crew, material, inspection, and constraint. Use the current Project 06 lookahead as the reference.
  5. Record actual commitments rather than general intentions. Do not record a trade's statement of intent as a committed date unless it is stated as a commitment.
  6. Review procurement items that threaten installation dates. Use the current Project 07 procurement log.
  7. Review deliveries, storage, lifting, access, and shared equipment. Confirm logistics conflicts are identified and assigned for resolution.
  8. Review trade handoffs and unresolved interface deficiencies. Use the current Project 08 trade-handoff records.

G. Review Quality, Inspections, Open Questions, and Owner Decisions

  1. Review quality checkpoints, mockups, first-work inspections, deficiencies, and punch status. Use the current quality plan and deficiency log.
  2. Review inspections scheduled, passed, failed, corrected, and pending. Use the current inspection register.
  3. Review RFIs and field clarifications by age, impact, and required response date. Identify any RFI at risk of affecting the schedule.
  4. Review pending owner decisions, selections, allowances, and approvals. Confirm each pending decision has a required-by date visible to the owner.
  5. Review change events, pricing, authorization, schedule effect, and work status. Use the current change log.
  6. Review cost or billing issues only with the appropriate attendees and confidentiality. Exclude attendees who do not require this information.

H. Record Decisions, Assign Actions, Publish, and Follow Up

  1. Distinguish discussion from decision and record the authorized decision maker. Use the reference conventions above for every recorded item.
  2. Assign each action item to one responsible person. Do not defer this assignment to "after the meeting."
  3. Assign one due date and one closure criterion to each action. State the observable condition that will close the item.
  4. Do not record "team" as the responsible party. Name the individual, not the group they belong to.
  5. Read back critical decisions, dates, and commitments before adjournment. Confirm agreement from the relevant attendees before closing the meeting.
  6. Record unresolved disagreements and required escalation. State who is responsible for escalating and by what date.
  7. Publish draft or final minutes promptly according to project rules. Do not allow minutes to lag so far behind the meeting that commitments are forgotten.
  8. Include attendees, absentees, documents reviewed, decisions, actions, due dates, and next meeting. Complete every field on the minutes template.
  9. Distribute minutes to attendees and affected parties. Confirm the distribution list matches Step 8 plus any party affected by a recorded decision.
  10. Provide a defined correction period for factual errors. State the correction deadline in the distributed minutes.
  11. Update the action log, schedule, procurement log, RFI log, change log, inspection log, and decision register. Carry every meeting outcome into the affected project-control log before the next meeting.
  12. Follow up on overdue items before the next meeting. Contact the responsible party for any item past its due date.
  13. Archive the agenda, attendance, attachments, notes, issued minutes, and corrections. File the complete meeting record in the project job file established in Project 02.

Common Meeting-Record Inspection

Inspect every coordination meeting record using the same checks regardless of project size or meeting frequency.

  • Agenda check: confirm the agenda was distributed with adequate advance notice and covers every required topic in the standard order, with safety first.
  • Safety check: confirm emergency, safety, access, weather, and public-protection issues were reviewed before status topics.
  • Status check: confirm schedule, procurement, quality, and inspection status were reviewed against current logs, not general impressions.
  • Decision check: confirm every recorded decision names the authorized decision maker and is distinguished from discussion or recommendation.
  • Action-item check: confirm every action item has exactly one named owner, one due date, and one closure criterion.
  • Publication check: confirm minutes were published promptly, distributed to attendees and affected parties, and that every affected project-control log was updated.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Action Item Is Never Completed

Probable cause: the item was assigned to "team" or a general group instead of one named person, or no due date was recorded at Step 26.
Confirmation: check the action-item log for the item and confirm whether exactly one named person and one due date are recorded.
Correction: reassign the item to one named responsible person with a new due date and closure criterion, and record the reassignment in the next meeting's minutes.
Reinspection: repeat the action-item check across the full log, not only the corrected item.

Field Staff Proceed on a Decision That Was Never Actually Authorized

Probable cause: a comment made in discussion was recorded as a decision, or the person who made the statement did not hold decision authority for that subject.
Confirmation: check the decision register for the item and confirm whether the recorded decision maker matches the authority identified in the scope statement or executed agreement.
Correction: issue a written correction identifying the item as discussion rather than decision, and route the item to the party holding actual authority before work proceeds.
Reinspection: repeat the decision check on the corrected minutes and confirm distribution of the correction.

A Safety Issue Raised in the Meeting Is Not Corrected Before the Next Meeting

Probable cause: the safety item was discussed but not assigned as an action item with an owner and due date, or it was placed later in the agenda and lost time before adjournment.
Confirmation: check the action-item log and the safety records for the item and confirm whether it was assigned, and whether it was addressed through the approved safety process rather than only discussed.
Correction: assign the item immediately to the responsible competent person or employer with a due date, and escalate through the approved safety process if the hazard remains active.
Reinspection: confirm correction at the next meeting's safety review before proceeding to other topics.

Minutes Are Published Too Late to Be Useful

Probable cause: minutes were not drafted during or immediately after the meeting, or the responsible recorder was not clearly assigned at Step 1.
Confirmation: check the meeting record for the time between the meeting date and the publication date, and confirm who was assigned as recorder.
Correction: publish the minutes immediately in draft form if final review is delayed, and confirm the recorder assignment for future meetings.
Reinspection: repeat the publication-timing check for the next two meetings.

A Trade Denies Making a Commitment Recorded in the Minutes

Probable cause: the commitment was recorded from a general statement of intent rather than a confirmed commitment, or it was not read back before adjournment as required by Step 28.
Confirmation: check whether the read-back occurred and whether the trade representative acknowledged the commitment at the meeting.
Correction: clarify the actual current commitment with the trade in writing and correct the record for the next meeting.
Reinspection: confirm the read-back step is performed at every subsequent meeting before adjournment.

The Same Constraint Reappears Unresolved at Consecutive Meetings

Probable cause: the constraint was discussed but never assigned as an action item with an owner and due date, or escalation required by Step 30 did not occur.
Confirmation: check the action-item log across the last several meetings for the constraint and confirm whether it was assigned and escalated.
Correction: assign the constraint to one owner with a firm due date, and record the required escalation if the due date is not met.
Reinspection: track the item at each subsequent meeting until it is verified closed.

A Project-Control Log Does Not Reflect a Decision Made in the Meeting

Probable cause: the log update required by Step 34 was not completed before the next meeting, or responsibility for the update was not clearly assigned.
Confirmation: compare the current schedule, procurement log, RFI log, change log, inspection log, or decision register against the minutes for the item.
Correction: update the affected log immediately and assign responsibility for future log updates to a specific person after each meeting.
Reinspection: confirm all affected logs are current at the start of the next meeting.

Field Terms

Coordination Meeting
A recurring project meeting used to make decisions, assign actions, remove constraints, and confirm commitments.
Discussion
An exchange of information or opinion that does not by itself authorize or direct any action.
Decision
An authorized determination made by the party holding decision authority for that subject.
Action Item
A task assigned to one named responsible person with one due date and one stated closure criterion.
Decision Register
A record distinguishing authorized decisions from discussion and recommendation, naming the decision maker.
Action-Item Log
A log tracking each assigned action to owner, due date, closure criterion, and status.
Read-Back
Restating a critical decision, date, or commitment aloud for confirmation before adjournment.
Constraint
An unresolved condition, such as missing information, labor, material, or access, that could prevent a scheduled task from proceeding.
Escalation
The defined process for raising an unresolved disagreement or overdue item to a higher decision authority.
Correction Period
The defined window after minutes are distributed during which attendees may report factual errors.

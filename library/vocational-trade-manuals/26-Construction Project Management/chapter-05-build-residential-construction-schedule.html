Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and gain approval for a baseline residential construction schedule — the controlling time document that converts the approved work breakdown structure and sequence logic into estimated durations, calendar dates, milestones, resource checks, and a defensible critical path. The finished work product is one approved, version-controlled baseline schedule supported by a schedule narrative, a risk review, and a defined update procedure.

You will confirm the schedule purpose and calendars, import the approved WBS activities and sequence logic, estimate durations from production data rather than guesswork, enter fixed contractual dates and milestones, calculate the initial schedule, identify the critical path and float, check the schedule against inspections, procurement, and owner decisions, check resource demand for trade stacking and impossible crew loads, run a schedule-risk review, and obtain authorized approval of the frozen baseline.

This project depends directly on the work already completed in Project 01 through Project 04. The approved scope statement, the job file, the work breakdown structure, and the sequence logic chart are the controlling inputs here — this project does not redefine scope, invent a new WBS, or re-derive sequence logic. It converts approved logic into approved dates. Every later project in this book depends on the baseline produced here: the two-week lookahead in Project 06, the procurement plan in Project 07, trade coordination in Project 08, and every schedule status reviewed in coordination meetings, RFIs, changes, and closeout through the Chapter 20 capstone.

Where This Skill Fits

The baseline schedule produced here is the time backbone of every remaining project-management document in this book. The two-week lookahead in Project 06 exists to convert this baseline into executable field commitments; it cannot function without an approved baseline behind it. The procurement plan in Project 07 calculates release dates backward from the installation dates fixed in this schedule. Trade coordination in Project 08 links handoff readiness to schedule activities created here. Job-cost reporting in Project 15 compares progress against this same baseline. The punch process in Project 14 and the final walkthrough in Project 19 both trace back to the milestone conditions defined here.

The discipline of separating the frozen original baseline from forecast dates — taught in this chapter — is the same discipline used later to preserve schedule history through delay, recovery, and approved changes without ever concealing or resetting a missed commitment.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming schedule purpose, level of detail, reporting cycle, calendars, and contractual milestones before entering activities
  • importing the approved WBS activities and sequence-logic relationships without altering their technical order
  • estimating durations from quantity, production rate, crew size, subcontractor commitment, access, and complexity
  • including submittal, review, fabrication, shipping, permit, inspection, curing, drying, and commissioning durations
  • entering fixed external dates only where their authority is confirmed, and entering required milestones
  • calculating the initial schedule, identifying critical path, near-critical paths, and float
  • checking inspection, concealment, delivery, owner-decision, and resource logic for completeness
  • leveling resources without destroying required milestones or concealing delay
  • running a schedule-risk review and developing mitigation for the highest risks
  • obtaining authorized approval, freezing the baseline, and defining the update and rebaseline rules

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent milestone dates, contractual deadlines, or production rates not supported by the executed agreement, approved estimate, subcontractor commitments, or field data
  • remove required inspections, cure periods, safety controls, or approved design steps to compress the schedule
  • reset the original baseline merely to hide delay or a missed commitment
  • redesign the work breakdown structure or the sequence logic while building the schedule; both remain the controlling inputs from Project 03 and Project 04

Any conflict between the sequence logic, the estimate's duration assumptions, subcontractor proposals, and contractual milestones must be identified and resolved in writing before the baseline is approved.

Trade Note

Every duration in this chapter is built from an approved production rate, a confirmed subcontractor commitment, or documented field history — never from an arbitrary guess intended to make the finish date look acceptable. Treating a schedule as a negotiating document instead of a resource-informed forecast is the single most common source of the schedule failures diagnosed in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin calculating the initial schedule (Step 15) until the calendars, durations, and fixed-date confirmation (Steps 3–11) are complete. A schedule calculated before fixed dates are confirmed and duration assumptions are recorded must be revised before it is submitted for review.

Shop Tip

Record the source of every duration next to the duration itself — "framing: 6 crew-days, per subcontractor proposal dated [date]" rather than a bare number. When the schedule is questioned later, the source record settles the question in minutes instead of days.

Duration, Logic, and Milestone Integrity

Every schedule activity must trace to an approved WBS work package and preserve the predecessor, successor, and lag relationships approved in the Project 04 sequence-logic chart. Do not create a new logic relationship while building the schedule; if the logic appears wrong or incomplete, return to Project 04 and revise the logic chart through its own approval process before continuing. A schedule that quietly changes logic to fit a desired finish date is not a baseline — it is an unrecorded redesign of the project sequence.

Documentation Cue — Preserve the Original Baseline and Record Why Forecast Dates Moved

Once the baseline is approved and frozen, do not overwrite it with updated dates. Record actual starts, actual finishes, remaining duration, and forecast dates as a separate layer, and record the reason for every forecast-date change. A schedule with no preserved baseline cannot demonstrate whether the project is ahead, on time, or behind.

Critical Schedule Cue — Do Not Reset a Baseline to Hide Delay

Do not reset or replace the approved baseline merely because the project has fallen behind or a milestone was missed. A rebaseline requires an authorized approval process of its own, with a documented reason. Resetting a baseline without that approval conceals delay from the owner, the estimator, and the cost-control system, and it removes the record needed to evaluate a future change or claim.

Field Coordination Cue — Check Resource Demand Before Freezing the Baseline

Before the baseline is approved, check resource demand by trade, crew, and equipment against the calculated schedule. A logically correct schedule that assumes three trades can simultaneously use one lift, one crane, or one limited work area is not executable. Identify trade stacking and impossible crew loads and resolve them before approval, not after mobilization.

Contract Instruction Cue — Confirm the Authority Behind Every Fixed Date

Enter a fixed external date only when its contractual, regulatory, or lender authority is confirmed. Do not use an arbitrary date constraint merely to make the schedule appear compliant with an assumed deadline. An unconfirmed fixed date can hide float, distort the critical path, and mislead every party who relies on the schedule.

E. Preparation — Confirm Purpose, Calendars, and Fixed Dates

  1. Confirm the schedule purpose, required level of detail, reporting cycle, calendar, and contractual milestones. Record these before entering activities.
  2. Import or enter the approved WBS activities and sequence relationships. Do not alter the technical order approved in Project 04.
  3. Create working calendars for standard workdays, holidays, shutdowns, seasonal restrictions, and authorized overtime. Base every calendar on company policy and the executed agreement.
  4. Identify weather-sensitive activities and define reasonable weather allowances or calendars. Record the basis for each allowance.
  5. Estimate each activity duration from quantity, production rate, crew size, subcontractor commitment, access, and complexity. Record the source of every estimate.
  6. Distinguish effort hours, crew-days, and calendar duration. Do not present labor hours as though they were calendar duration.
  7. Include mobilization, setup, layout, protection, cleanup, testing, inspection, and correction time. Add this time to the relevant activities rather than omitting it.
  8. Include submittal preparation, design review, owner review, resubmission, fabrication, shipping, and delivery durations. Coordinate these durations with the Project 07 procurement plan once it exists.
  9. Include permit review, utility coordination, special inspection, testing, curing, drying, and commissioning durations. Confirm each duration against the Book 25 permit-and-inspection plan.
  10. Identify fixed external dates and confirm their authority. Record the document or authority that establishes each fixed date.
  11. Do not use arbitrary date constraints merely to make the schedule appear compliant. Remove any constraint whose authority cannot be confirmed.

F. Milestones and Initial Calculation

  1. Enter required milestones for permit issuance, mobilization, foundation, dry-in, rough inspections, permanent utilities, substantial completion, final inspection, and handoff as applicable. State the measurable condition that defines each milestone.
  2. Enter owner-selection and allowance-decision milestones. Coordinate these dates with the process defined in the Project 01 scope statement.
  3. Enter long-lead release and required-on-site milestones. Use preliminary procurement information where the Project 07 plan is not yet complete.
  4. Calculate the initial schedule using approved logic. Do not calculate the schedule before Steps 3–11 are complete.
  5. Identify the critical path and near-critical paths. Record which activities control the finish date.
  6. Identify total float and free float without treating float as belonging exclusively to one trade. Record float for every activity, not only critical-path activities.
  7. Inspect negative float, open ends, excessive lags, missing predecessors, and date constraints. Correct each condition found before continuing.

G. Verification, Resource Check, and Risk Review

  1. Check that every inspection has predecessor completion and successor-release logic. Confirm no inspection is scheduled before its predecessor work is logically complete.
  2. Check that every concealed-work activity is linked to quality and inspection approval. Confirm no concealment activity precedes its required approval in the logic.
  3. Check that material delivery precedes installation with inspection and staging time. Add staging and inspection time to the relevant activities where missing.
  4. Check that owner decisions precede procurement and field need dates. Confirm each decision milestone allows adequate lead time.
  5. Check resource demand by trade, crew, and equipment, and work area. Record demand by period for comparison against available resources.
  6. Identify trade stacking, impossible crew loads, and competing use of cranes, lifts, scaffolds, or access. Record each conflict found.
  7. Level resources without destroying required milestones or concealing delay. Adjust non-critical activities within their float before adjusting milestone dates.
  8. Run a schedule-risk review for long-lead items, weather, inspections, utilities, owner decisions, and hidden conditions. Record each risk in the schedule-risk register.
  9. Develop mitigation for the highest schedule risks. Assign an owner and a response to each significant risk.

H. Review, Approve, and Freeze the Baseline

  1. Compare planned duration with contract requirements, estimate assumptions, and subcontractor proposals. Record any discrepancy found.
  2. Review the draft with owner, field lead, key trades, procurement, design team, and inspectors or utilities where coordination is appropriate. Distribute the draft far enough in advance for informed review.
  3. Record commitments and unresolved schedule assumptions. Preserve a record of every comment received and how it was addressed.
  4. Add schedule narrative explaining calendars, milestones, critical path, major constraints, and risks. Write the narrative in plain language a non-scheduler can follow.
  5. Obtain authorized approval of the baseline. Confirm the approval is from the person or role identified during Step 1 coordination.
  6. Freeze and archive the approved baseline without overwriting it. Assign the baseline number and date directly on the schedule-approval record.
  7. Distribute the baseline and define the update cutoff and reporting process. State the frequency and cutoff for progress updates.
  8. Create rules for actual dates, progress, remaining duration, forecast, recovery, and approved rebaseline. State the exact condition under which a rebaseline may occur and who must approve it.
  9. Link the schedule to the procurement, inspection, cost, and communication systems. Confirm the identifiers used here will be recognized by the Project 07, Project 15, Project 16, and Project 17 systems.

Common Schedule Inspection

Inspect every baseline schedule using the same checks regardless of project size or complexity.

  • Logic check: confirm every activity traces to an approved WBS package and preserves the approved sequence-logic relationships without unrecorded changes.
  • Duration check: confirm every duration has a recorded source and that effort hours, crew-days, and calendar duration are distinguished.
  • Fixed-date check: confirm every fixed date has confirmed authority and that no arbitrary constraint remains in the schedule.
  • Critical-path and float check: confirm the critical path has been calculated and reviewed, and that float is recorded without being assigned exclusively to one trade.
  • Resource check: confirm trade stacking and impossible crew loads have been identified and resolved through leveling rather than milestone erosion.
  • Approval check: confirm written approval was obtained from the authorized party and that the baseline number and date are recorded before distribution.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Finish Date Slips Immediately After the Baseline Is Approved

Probable cause: a duration was estimated without a recorded source, or a fixed date was entered without confirmed authority, producing a schedule that was never realistic.
Confirmation: review the duration source for the affected activities and check whether the fixed date's authority was confirmed in Step 10.
Correction: re-estimate the duration from a confirmed production rate or commitment, remove any unconfirmed fixed date, and route the correction through the schedule-update process.
Reinspection: repeat the duration and fixed-date check across the full schedule, not only the affected activity.

Two Trades Are Committed to the Same Crane, Lift, or Work Area on the Same Days

Probable cause: the resource-demand check in Steps 23–24 was skipped or was performed only for labor crews and not for shared equipment or access.
Confirmation: pull the resource-demand record for the affected period and confirm whether the equipment or access conflict was identified.
Correction: level the conflicting activities within their available float, or negotiate additional equipment or access; do not resolve the conflict by informally telling one trade to "work around" the other.
Reinspection: repeat the full resource-demand check for the revised schedule.

An Inspection Is Scheduled Before the Work It Depends On Is Logically Complete

Probable cause: the inspection-logic check in Step 19 was not completed, or the inspection activity was entered without a predecessor link to the work it inspects.
Confirmation: trace the inspection activity's predecessor in the schedule and confirm it matches the actual work the inspection covers.
Correction: add or correct the predecessor relationship and recalculate the schedule.
Reinspection: repeat the inspection-logic check for every inspection activity in the schedule.

The Project Appears On Schedule Even Though Field Reports Show Delay

Probable cause: the original baseline was overwritten with updated dates instead of being preserved as a separate layer, or a rebaseline occurred without the required approval and documented reason.
Confirmation: check the schedule archive for a preserved original baseline and confirm whether any rebaseline has a recorded approval and reason.
Correction: restore or reconstruct the original baseline from the archived approval record, and require future updates to preserve it as a separate layer.
Reinspection: confirm the baseline-preservation rule from Step 34 has been reissued to the scheduling team and acknowledged.

Float Is Being Treated as Belonging to One Trade

Probable cause: the float check in Step 18 recorded float only in relation to a single trade's activities rather than as a project resource shared by all activities on that path.
Confirmation: review the float value for the disputed activities and confirm whether it was communicated as exclusive to one trade.
Correction: clarify in writing that float belongs to the project, not to any single trade, and document how float will be allocated when more than one activity could use it.
Reinspection: repeat the float check across the full schedule and confirm the clarification has been distributed.

A Long-Lead Item's Late Delivery Was Not Anticipated in the Schedule

Probable cause: the schedule-risk review in Step 26 did not identify the item as high-risk, or the review was performed without confirmed procurement lead times.
Confirmation: check the schedule-risk register for the item and compare the recorded lead time against the actual supplier commitment.
Correction: add or correct the risk entry, develop mitigation, and coordinate the correction with the Project 07 procurement plan.
Reinspection: repeat the schedule-risk review for all long-lead, custom, and engineered items.

The Schedule Cannot Be Approved Because the Approver Is Unclear

Probable cause: the authorized approver was not confirmed before calculation began, or more than one party believes they hold approval authority for the baseline.
Confirmation: check the executed agreement and project directory for the named decision authority, and confirm this matches the person the draft was routed to for approval.
Correction: resolve the authority question in writing with the owner and project management before requesting approval again.
Reinspection: repeat the approval check once a single confirmed approver has signed the baseline.

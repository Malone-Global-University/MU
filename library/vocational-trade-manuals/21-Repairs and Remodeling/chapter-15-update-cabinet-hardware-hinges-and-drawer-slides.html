Job Overview

This project qualifies you to update the hardware on an existing set of cabinets — installing new pulls or knobs, replacing at least one hinge, and replacing at least one drawer-slide set — while keeping every door and drawer square, aligned, and smoothly operating when the work is finished. This is a finish-hardware and mechanical-adjustment project, not a cabinet-building project: the cabinet boxes, doors, and drawer boxes already exist, and your job is to remove old hardware without damaging the substrate, lay out and drill new hardware locations accurately, and install compatible replacement hardware that matches the cabinet's construction and the load it carries.

You will inventory the cabinets and their existing hardware, confirm that replacement hardware is compatible with the existing construction, protect the room and finishes, remove old hardware without enlarging or tearing the substrate, build and use a drilling template so every pull or knob lands in the same location, machine or dry-fit new hinges and slides to the verified cabinet geometry, install and adjust every updated door and drawer, and inspect alignment, fastening, and operation before calling the work complete.

This chapter follows Chapter 14's backsplash-replacement project and precedes Chapter 16's non-structural wall opening. In the Chapter 20 capstone, this chapter's hardware-update procedure is reused directly to install or update cabinet pulls, hinges, drawer slides, and door hardware in the selected laundry, utility, or powder-room remodel.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-085 — Remove old cabinet hardware, including pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, and catches, without enlarging or tearing the substrate.
  • RR-086 — Mark a new hardware layout using a template or jig referenced from door and drawer edges rather than from distorted existing holes.
  • RR-087 — Install pulls and knobs with compatible fasteners that seat fully without bottoming out, protruding, or crushing the panel.
  • RR-088 — Replace a cabinet door hinge and mounting plate, matching overlay, reveal, and opening geometry, and adjust the hung door.
  • RR-089 — Replace a drawer-slide set matched for handedness, length, setback, and mounting geometry, and adjust the reinstalled drawer.
  • RR-090 — Inspect cabinet hardware alignment, fastener security, hole quality, closing action, slide travel, and finish condition.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 11 through 14 replaced or reconnected plumbing fixtures and finish surfaces at cabinets, vanities, and backsplashes. This chapter completes the cabinet system by updating the hardware that controls how doors and drawers actually operate every day. The layout discipline taught here — referencing a template from a verified edge rather than from a possibly distorted existing hole — is the same discipline Chapter 16 uses when it requires opening layouts to be referenced from common level and plumb lines rather than from irregular existing surfaces.

The Chapter 20 capstone reuses this chapter's hardware procedures directly for whichever cabinets, drawers, and doors are part of the selected small-room remodel.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • inventorying existing cabinet boxes, doors, drawers, pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, catches, overlays, insets, reveals, and visible damage
  • confirming replacement hardware compatibility with existing cabinet material, thickness, edge construction, door weight, and drawer load
  • removing old pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, and catches without enlarging or tearing the substrate
  • building and using a full-size template or commercial jig to lay out new pull and knob locations from verified reference edges
  • installing pulls and knobs with compatible, correctly sized fasteners
  • replacing at least one hinge and mounting plate, matching overlay, reveal, opening angle, and hole pattern, and adjusting the hung door
  • replacing at least one drawer-slide set matched for handedness, length, setback, and mounting geometry, and adjusting the reinstalled drawer
  • cycling every updated door and drawer under normal approved load and inspecting alignment, fastening, and finish condition

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • drill any new hardware hole before handle centers, hinge geometry, and slide clearance have been verified against the specific cabinet and checked for conflict with existing holes
  • machine new hinge recesses in cabinet material that is too thin for the approved jig and hinge system, or without the approved jig
  • use fasteners that bottom out, protrude through a finished face, or split rails, stiles, or drawer-box sides
  • load a hinge, slide, or fastener beyond the door weight or drawer load the selected hardware is rated for
  • correct a structural cabinet defect — a cracked rail, a split face frame, a failed drawer-box joint — by installing hardware over it; such defects must be repaired first or referred, using an approved detail

Trade Note

Every hardware layout in this chapter is established from a door or drawer edge, never copied from an existing hole. Existing holes can be off-center, elongated from years of use, or positioned for hardware that no longer matches the replacement. A layout copied from a distorted hole reproduces the distortion in the new installation.

Setup Standard

Do not drill any new hardware hole (Step 13 forward) until the template has been marked and verified against door and drawer edges (Steps 11–13) and a single test installation has been completed and checked (Step 15). Drilling every door from an unverified template repeats any layout error across the whole cabinet run.

Shop Tip

Install and check one pull or knob completely — drilled, fastened, and inspected — before drilling the remaining doors and drawers. A five-minute test on one door catches a template error before it is repeated across an entire kitchen or bathroom.

Concealed Hinges vs. Surface and Semi-Concealed Hinges

A concealed (European-style) hinge is machined into a round cup recess bored into the back of the door and is not visible when the door is closed; it uses a mounting plate fastened to the cabinet interior. A surface or semi-concealed hinge is screwed directly to the face of the door and face frame or cabinet edge without a bored cup. These two hinge families use different mounting methods, different adjustment mechanisms, and different tools, and this chapter treats them as separate procedures rather than a single generic hinge-replacement method.

Measurement Cue — Reference Every Layout From a Verified Edge

Establish a consistent reference from door and drawer edges — not from an existing hole — before marking any new pull, knob, hinge, or slide location. Verify every location with the template before drilling. A layout copied from a distorted or off-center existing hole reproduces that error in the replacement hardware.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Handedness and Load Rating Before Installing Slides

Left and right drawer-slide members are not interchangeable, and a slide rated for a lighter load than the loaded drawer will actually carry can fail under use. Confirm handedness, length, setback, and load rating against the specific drawer before installing any slide member.

Field Safety Cue — Support Removed Doors and Drawers

Support every removed door and drawer on a padded, stable work surface. Do not lean a removed door against an unsecured surface, and do not leave a partially fastened hinge or slide bearing the weight of a door or drawer while unattended.

Code Alert — No Code Applies to This Chapter's Cosmetic Hardware Scope

Standard cabinet pull, hinge, and drawer-slide replacement is cosmetic and mechanical hardware work and does not typically require a permit or inspection. If the cabinet run includes an accessibility-mandated pull, reach range, or clearance requirement, verify that requirement locally before selecting replacement hardware.

E. Preparation — Inventory and Compatibility (Steps 1–6)

  1. Inventory cabinet boxes, doors, drawers, existing pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, catches, overlays, insets, reveals, and damage. Record each item on the hardware inventory, noting the cabinet run or room, door and drawer count, and any cracked rail, split face frame, or failed drawer-box joint observed.
  2. Confirm the cabinet material, thickness, edge construction, door weight, drawer load, hinge type, cup or mortise pattern, slide type, length, travel, and mounting clearances. Measure and record each value directly on the hardware inventory before selecting final replacement hardware.
  3. Select replacement hardware compatible with existing construction or an approved retrofit system. Confirm the selected pulls, knobs, hinges, and slides match the recorded overlay/inset, cup size, hole pattern, length, and load rating; if no compatible match exists, identify the required retrofit plate or adapter before proceeding.
  4. Do not drill new holes until handle centers, hinge geometry, slide clearance, and conflict with existing holes are verified. Compare the new hardware's hole-center spacing and mounting geometry against the existing panel and mark any conflict with an existing hole for resolution before drilling.
  5. Photograph and label every door, drawer, hinge, slide, and hardware location. Assign a matching label to each door and its corresponding cabinet opening so that every component is reinstalled in its original location.
  6. Protect countertops, floors, cabinet finishes, appliances, and adjacent walls. Establish a padded, stable work surface for removed doors and drawers before beginning removal.

F. Removal and Inspection (Steps 7–10)

  1. Remove doors and drawers as required and support them on padded stable work surfaces. Remove doors and drawers only for the components included in this update, keeping labeled hardware and fasteners with their matching component.
  2. Remove old pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, catches, and fasteners without enlarging or tearing the substrate. Back out fasteners fully before removing hardware; do not pry hardware loose while fasteners remain engaged.
  3. Inspect for stripped holes, cracked rails, loose face frames, split doors, damaged drawer boxes, and prior misalignment. Record every defect found and compare it against the approved scope's repair limits.
  4. Repair only minor accepted substrate damage using a method compatible with the new hardware load. Stop and refer any defect that exceeds the approved repair limit rather than installing new hardware over an unsound substrate.

G. Pull and Knob Layout and Installation (Steps 11–16)

  1. Prepare a full-size template or commercial jig for pull and knob locations. Set the jig or template to the exact hole-center spacing of the selected hardware.
  2. Establish a consistent reference from door and drawer edges rather than transferring from distorted existing holes. Reference the template from the same edge — top, bottom, or side — on every door and drawer in the run.
  3. Mark and verify every location before drilling. Mark each location with the template clamped in place and verify the mark against the recorded reference dimension before removing the clamp.
  4. Clamp sacrificial backing to reduce breakout and drill with the correct bit and controlled depth. Clamp backing material flush against the exit face of the panel at every marked location before drilling.
  5. Test one pull or knob installation before drilling the remaining components. Drill and install the first pull or knob completely, verify fit, hole quality, and fastener seating, and correct the template before continuing if any defect is found.
  6. Install pulls and knobs with compatible screw length that does not bottom out, protrude, or crush the panel. Hand-tighten each fastener to full seating and verify the pull or knob sits flat without rocking.

H. Hinge Replacement and Door Adjustment (Steps 17–21)

  1. Identify hinge overlay, inset, reveal, opening angle, closing action, cup size, plate height, and screw pattern. Confirm each value matches the replacement hinge's manufacturer specification before dry-fitting.
  2. Dry-fit the replacement hinge and mounting plate. Verify cup engagement, plate alignment, and door swing clearance before machining any new recess.
  3. Machine new hinge recesses only with the approved jig and only where cabinet thickness permits. Confirm the door material is thick enough for the specified cup depth before boring; do not bore a cup that would break through the opposite face.
  4. Install hinges and plates without stripping screws or splitting door edges. Pilot the mounting-plate screw locations where the cabinet material requires it, and seat every screw fully without overdriving.
  5. Hang the door and adjust height, side, depth, reveal, and closing action progressively. Make one adjustment at a time and recheck the reveal at each edge before making the next adjustment.

I. Drawer-Slide Replacement and Adjustment (Steps 22–25)

  1. Identify drawer-slide handedness, length, setback, side or under-mount geometry, rear support, and required clearances. Confirm the replacement slide set matches every recorded value before installation.
  2. Install cabinet-side slide members from level and parallel reference lines. Mark the reference line at the recorded setback and verify it with a level before fastening either slide member.
  3. Install drawer-side members square, aligned, and at matching setbacks. Verify the drawer-side member sits square to the drawer-box front before fastening.
  4. Reinstall the drawer and adjust engagement, reveal, travel, and soft-close or self-close action. Engage the drawer-side member with the cabinet-side member and verify full, smooth travel before final adjustment.

J. Testing and Documentation (Steps 26–29)

  1. Verify doors and drawers do not collide with walls, appliances, adjacent fronts, plumbing, or each other. Open every updated door and drawer to its full travel and check for collision at every adjacent surface.
  2. Cycle every updated door and drawer repeatedly under normal approved load. Operate each door and drawer through at least ten full cycles and note any binding, rubbing, or hardware loosening.
  3. Inspect hardware alignment, fastener security, hole quality, reveals, closing action, slide support, travel, pinch points, and finish damage. Record the result of each check against the Acceptance Standards table.
  4. Document hardware models, drilling template, adjustment settings, and any cabinet defects outside the repair scope. File the completed hardware inventory, adjustment record, and any deferred defect for the owner's records.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed cabinet using the same checks regardless of hardware type.

  • Compatibility check: confirm the installed hardware matches the recorded cabinet material, thickness, overlay/inset, load rating, and hole pattern.
  • Hole-quality check: confirm no hole is enlarged, torn, or blown out, and that sacrificial backing was used where breakout risk existed.
  • Layout-consistency check: confirm every pull or knob is installed at the same reference-edge location across the full cabinet run.
  • Hinge-fit check: confirm overlay, reveal, opening angle, and closing action are correct and the door hangs plumb with consistent reveals.
  • Slide-fit check: confirm handedness, length, setback, and load rating are correct and the drawer travels smoothly without binding or racking.
  • Operation check: confirm every updated door and drawer cycles repeatedly under normal approved load without collision, binding, or hardware loosening.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Pull or Knob Sits Crooked or Rocks on the Panel

Probable cause: the template was not clamped tightly during drilling, the panel is not flat at the hole location, or the fastener was overtightened before the pull seated fully.
Confirmation: check the hole spacing against the template and check the panel for flatness at the hole location.
Correction: loosen the fastener, verify seating, and re-tighten evenly; if the hole itself is out of position, correct the template before drilling any remaining doors.
Reinspection: repeat the layout-consistency check across the affected door and any doors drilled after it.

A Hinge-Mounted Door Does Not Sit Plumb or Shows an Uneven Reveal

Probable cause: the mounting plate was installed off the reference line, the hinge cup was bored off-center, or the door adjustment screws were not adjusted progressively.
Confirmation: measure the reveal at the hinge jamb, head, latch side, and bottom, and compare each to the approved reveal.
Correction: adjust the hinge's height, side, and depth screws one at a time, rechecking the reveal after each adjustment; if the cup or plate itself is mispositioned, remove and reposition it before adjusting further.
Reinspection: repeat the hinge-fit check on the corrected door.

A Reinstalled Drawer Binds, Racks, or Will Not Travel Fully

Probable cause: the cabinet-side and drawer-side slide members were not installed level and parallel to each other, the wrong handedness was installed, or the setback does not match the recorded dimension.
Confirmation: check both slide members with a level and compare the recorded setback against the actual installed position.
Correction: loosen and realign the affected slide member to the reference line, or replace a slide member installed with the wrong handedness.
Reinspection: repeat the slide-fit check and cycle the drawer through its full travel again.

A Door or Drawer Collides With an Adjacent Front, Appliance, or Wall

Probable cause: the replacement hardware's overlay, opening angle, or travel differs from the original hardware and was not checked against adjacent components before installation.
Confirmation: open the affected door or drawer through its full travel and identify the exact point of collision.
Correction: select a hardware variant with a compatible opening angle or travel, or adjust the mounting position within the hardware's approved adjustment range.
Reinspection: repeat the operation check on the corrected component and on any adjacent component it affects.

A Fastener Strips, Bottoms Out, or Protrudes Through a Finished Face

Probable cause: the fastener length was not verified against the panel thickness before installation, or the fastener was overdriven.
Confirmation: measure the panel thickness at the fastener location and compare it to the installed fastener length.
Correction: replace the fastener with the correct length and re-seat it without overdriving; repair any protrusion or crushing damage using a method compatible with the panel material before reinstalling hardware.
Reinspection: repeat the hole-quality and compatibility checks on the corrected component.

Cleanup

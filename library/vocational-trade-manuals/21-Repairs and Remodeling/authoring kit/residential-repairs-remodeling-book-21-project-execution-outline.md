# Book-Level Authoring Rule

This file controls the actual project sequence for Book 21.

For every chapter, Claude must preserve the approved project title, filename, competency range, main-project outcome, and numbered sequence; expand every numbered step into complete field-manual prose; add point-of-use safety cues, occupied-home controls, dust and debris controls, concealed-condition controls, hazardous-material alerts, manufacturer-instruction cues, code and permit alerts, inspection checkpoints, troubleshooting, competency assessment, visual briefs, documentation, and project closeout; not redesign the project while drafting; not omit or merge required steps; and not invent structural details, plumbing arrangements, electrical modifications, waterproofing assemblies, fire-resistance requirements, fastening schedules, product cure times, material compatibility, repair limits, or code provisions that are not supplied by approved plans, adopted code, manufacturer instructions, qualified trade direction, or the chapter project specification.

Repair and remodeling work differs from new construction because the existing building is both the work environment and part of the evidence. Finishes may conceal structural damage, wiring, plumbing, ducts, fire blocking, insulation, moisture, mold, pests, hazardous materials, noncompliant alterations, or previous repairs. The chapter author must therefore treat every project as a controlled sequence of investigation, protection, selective exposure, cause correction, repair, finish restoration, testing, documentation, and handoff.

The following controls apply throughout the book:

- Use the approved project scope, existing-condition report, photographs, measurements, drawings, product data, repair details, finish schedule, trade requirements, permit conditions, inspection requirements, and adopted building, plumbing, electrical, mechanical, fire, accessibility, environmental, and occupational-safety requirements.
- Confirm ownership authorization, occupant needs, work hours, access, security, pets, children, vulnerable occupants, temporary loss of utilities, sanitation, noise, dust, odor, and daily cleanup expectations before work begins.
- Distinguish cosmetic work from work affecting structure, fire resistance, egress, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, waterproofing, ventilation, accessibility, or health.
- Confirm whether the work requires a permit, inspection, engineer, architect, building official, environmental consultant, lead-safe or asbestos-qualified firm, mold-remediation professional, licensed electrician, licensed plumber, HVAC technician, flooring specialist, countertop fabricator, cabinet installer, or other qualified person.
- Stop work when the actual condition differs materially from the approved scope or when hidden damage exceeds the chapter’s defined repair limits.
- Do not cut, drill, pry, fasten, or demolish until concealed electrical, plumbing, gas, HVAC, fire-suppression, communications, and structural conditions have been evaluated.
- De-energize, lock out, cap, isolate, or disconnect utilities only through the approved procedure and by qualified persons where required.
- Treat every unidentified wire, pipe, duct, cable, valve, device, or line as active until verified otherwise.
- Do not modify load-bearing walls, shear walls, braced wall panels, posts, beams, joists, rafters, trusses, stair framing, guards, handrails, or foundation elements without an approved design.
- Do not cut or notch structural members to make a repair fit.
- Do not modify prefabricated trusses or engineered wood products without written authorization from the responsible design authority.
- Preserve required fire blocking, draft stopping, fire-rated assemblies, garage separation, smoke barriers, and penetration protection.
- Restore any required rated assembly with an approved complete system rather than matching appearance alone.
- Identify potential lead-containing coatings, asbestos-containing materials, silica-producing materials, mold, sewage contamination, animal waste, pesticides, and other environmental hazards before disturbance.
- Do not sand, scrape, grind, drill, saw, heat, or demolish suspect hazardous material until the required assessment and controls are in place.
- Stop work for suspect friable material, widespread mold, sewage, chemical contamination, uncontrolled dust migration, or hazardous-material conditions outside the approved scope.
- Correct the source of water, movement, impact, corrosion, settlement, or wear before restoring the damaged surface.
- Do not close walls, floors, ceilings, cabinets, or chases over active leaks, wet materials, unsafe wiring, damaged piping, structural decay, pests, or unresolved inspection items.
- Use moisture measurements, drying criteria, leak tests, operational tests, and inspection records where the repair depends on concealed conditions being dry and serviceable.
- Protect occupied areas with containment, floor and surface protection, controlled airflow, sealed waste handling, clean access routes, and daily housekeeping.
- Do not use a building’s central HVAC system as a construction dust-control system.
- Coordinate containment and ventilation so that dust, fumes, and odors are not driven into occupied rooms, return-air paths, attached garages, neighboring units, or concealed cavities.
- Use tools with guards, dust collection, stable work support, and the correct blade, bit, or accessory for the material.
- Establish safe cutting, fastening, lifting, ladder, scaffold, and material-staging practices before work begins.
- Use team lifting, carts, dollies, suction devices, temporary supports, and manufacturer lifting points for cabinets, countertops, vanities, toilets, sinks, doors, panels, and appliances.
- Do not leave unsupported cabinets, fixtures, doors, countertops, mirrors, glass, or plumbing components in an unstable condition.
- Verify substrate condition before attaching trim, cabinets, fixtures, flooring, wall panels, or accessories.
- Use fasteners appropriate for the substrate, required load, material, exposure, and manufacturer instructions.
- Do not substitute drywall screws, trim nails, adhesive, caulk, or filler for structural attachment or required mechanical fastening.
- Match drywall type, thickness, edge support, fire rating, moisture resistance, fastener pattern, tape, compound, primer, and finish to the approved assembly.
- Match flooring repair material for type, thickness, locking profile, attachment method, expansion, moisture limits, and substrate requirements.
- Do not trap moisture beneath flooring, cabinets, trim, paint, sealant, or patching compounds.
- Confirm finish compatibility before applying primer, paint, adhesive, grout, sealant, caulk, filler, stain blocker, or cleaning product.
- Observe required surface preparation, ventilation, temperature, humidity, cure, and recoat conditions from product instructions.
- Maintain plumbing fixture access, shutoffs, traps, cleanouts, supply connections, drain slope, support, venting, clearances, and leak-testing requirements.
- Do not conceal a plumbing connection until it has passed the required inspection and test.
- Maintain electrical box access, device clearances, grounding and bonding, cable protection, fire stopping, and required working space.
- Do not bury junction boxes, cover service equipment, relocate devices, or alter circuits outside the approved electrical scope.
- Wet-area walls, floors, backsplashes, tub or shower surrounds, and countertop transitions require the approved water-management detail; tile, grout, caulk, and paint alone are not universal waterproofing systems.
- Verify that toilets, vanities, sinks, faucets, drains, hinges, slides, hardware, flooring, trim, coatings, and repair products are compatible with the existing installation before demolition.
- Use a change-control process for concealed conditions, owner-requested additions, unavailable materials, dimensional conflicts, and work outside the approved scope.
- Record pre-work conditions, protection, utility status, demolition limits, concealed conditions, moisture results, structural observations, product labels, repair details, inspections, tests, corrections, finish information, and owner acceptance.
- Maintain daily cleanup and remove protruding fasteners, sharp debris, trip hazards, wet compounds, open penetrations, exposed utilities, and unsecured materials before leaving the work area.
- Restore smoke alarms, carbon-monoxide alarms, fire doors, locks, handrails, lighting, plumbing fixtures, appliances, ventilation, and other life-safety or essential functions before occupancy resumes where they were affected.
- Complete the project only after the repaired cause, concealed work, surface finish, fixture operation, cleanup, documentation, and owner handoff have all been accepted.

The numbered sequences below are the minimum required repair-and-remodeling workflow. Claude may divide a step into smaller substeps but may not change the technical order without documenting the controlling reason.

## System-Sequence Control

The chapter order is instructional. A real repair or remodeling project must follow the approved investigation-to-closeout sequence even when that sequence crosses chapter boundaries.

In particular:

- Existing conditions, owner objectives, occupancy, hazardous-material risks, permits, utilities, and repair limits are resolved before demolition.
- Containment, floor protection, debris routes, tool staging, and utility controls are complete before disturbance begins.
- Demolition remains selective and stops when concealed conditions differ from the approved assumptions.
- The cause of moisture, movement, damage, binding, leakage, corrosion, or failure is corrected before cosmetic restoration.
- Structural, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, fire-rated, waterproofing, and environmental work is inspected before concealment where required.
- Drywall, trim, flooring, paint, cabinets, fixtures, and hardware are installed only over accepted substrates.
- Plumbing fixtures are supported, connected, and tested before cabinets, trim, sealant, or finishes restrict access.
- Flooring patches are matched for system, thickness, movement, and substrate rather than appearance alone.
- Paint blending follows accepted patch preparation, priming, sheen, texture, and full-plane transition strategy.
- Chapter 16 proceeds only after the wall is positively verified as non-structural and free of conflicting utilities and protected systems.
- Chapter 17 proceeds only after the water source is corrected and the affected assembly has met the approved drying and contamination criteria.
- Chapter 18 coordinates room-level work in an approved sequence so that later tasks do not damage completed finishes.
- Chapter 19 verifies the entire approved scope, not only visible appearance.
- Chapter 20 must pass readiness, protection, demolition, concealed-condition, substrate, installation, operational, finish, cleanup, documentation, and owner-acceptance inspections.

## Capstone Design Control

Chapter 20 uses one approved small-room remodeling plan selected before procurement or demolition.

The preferred capstone is a **small laundry or utility room remodel** that may include limited selective demolition, wall repair, localized flooring replacement, trim replacement, paint, storage installation, cabinet hardware, and reconnection of existing appliances or fixtures by qualified persons where required.

A **small powder-room refresh** may be used as an alternate only when the vanity, faucet, toilet, flooring, wall repair, trim, paint, ventilation, electrical, and plumbing scopes are fully documented and the project does not require structural alteration, shower or tub waterproofing, major drain relocation, service upgrades, or uncontrolled hazardous-material disturbance.

A kitchen gut remodel, full bathroom remodel, load-bearing wall removal, structural opening, major water-damage restoration, whole-room rewiring, service or panel work, plumbing-stack relocation, gas work, HVAC redesign, window replacement affecting the exterior envelope, or work involving uncontrolled lead, asbestos, mold, sewage, or fire damage is not a default capstone. Such work requires the exact approved design, permits, inspections, environmental controls, and qualified trade participation.

## Capstone Continuity Rule

The capstone may use a room previously assessed or repaired during Chapters 1 through 19, but it must begin from a documented existing condition and approved final design. Earlier work may be credited only when its measurements, photographs, materials, tests, inspections, and acceptance records are available.

The capstone is handed off as a complete, clean, operational, documented small-room improvement ready for the property-maintenance and building-inspection practices introduced in Book 22.

# Chapter 1

## Project Title
Inspect and Scope a Residential Repair or Remodeling Project

## Filename
`chapter-01-inspect-and-scope-repair-remodel-project.html`

## Competencies
**RR-001 through RR-006:** Inspect existing conditions, identify repair goals, distinguish repair from remodel, identify hidden-risk areas, document project scope, and prepare a repair-readiness checklist.

## Main Project
Inspect one defined residential repair or small remodeling area and produce an existing-condition report, approved scope, risk register, prerequisite list, and readiness decision.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Obtain owner authorization, project objectives, budget constraints, occupancy requirements, desired completion condition, and known building history.
2. Collect available plans, permits, inspection reports, repair records, product information, warranties, photographs, and prior contractor documentation.
3. Define the inspection boundary and identify adjacent rooms, floors, ceilings, exterior surfaces, attics, crawlspaces, basements, garages, and utility areas that may affect the work.
4. Record building age, construction type, finish materials, prior alterations, and visible evidence of unpermitted or incomplete work without assuming concealed compliance.
5. Interview the owner or occupant about leaks, odors, noise, movement, intermittent failures, previous repairs, seasonal conditions, and when the problem occurs.
6. Photograph the room and each proposed work area before moving contents or disturbing finishes.
7. Measure room dimensions, openings, fixture locations, floor elevations, wall planes, ceiling condition, and access constraints.
8. Identify the stated repair goal and distinguish restoration of a failed component from an elective change in layout, material, fixture, or appearance.
9. Inspect walls, floors, ceilings, trim, doors, windows, cabinets, fixtures, finishes, and visible structure for damage, movement, wear, staining, corrosion, and poor attachment.
10. Inspect for active or historic moisture using visual evidence, odor, approved moisture testing, and access to adjacent assemblies.
11. Identify likely routes of plumbing, wiring, gas, ducts, vents, drains, and structural framing before any cutting or drilling is planned.
12. Identify possible lead-containing coatings, asbestos-containing materials, mold, silica-producing materials, or other environmental hazards that require assessment before disturbance.
13. Identify load-bearing, braced, shear, fire-rated, garage-separation, wet-area, egress, accessibility, and life-safety conditions.
14. Operate doors, drawers, latches, shutoffs, faucets, drains, toilets, lights, fans, and appliances only when safe and within the inspection scope.
15. Separate observed facts from suspected concealed conditions and from owner-reported symptoms.
16. Define the approved repair limits, finish limits, access limits, protection requirements, and excluded work.
17. Identify permits, inspections, utility shutdowns, specialty testing, licensed trades, design professionals, and manufacturer consultation required before work.
18. Prepare a concealed-condition allowance and a stop-work list for conditions that cannot be resolved during the initial inspection.
19. Prepare an ordered scope of demolition, investigation, repair, installation, finishing, testing, cleanup, and handoff.
20. Prepare a materials and product-compatibility research list without selecting products whose suitability has not been verified.
21. Prepare a repair-readiness checklist covering authorization, design, hazards, utilities, access, protection, materials, trades, permits, and inspections.
22. Review the scope, exclusions, assumptions, change-control process, and expected disruption with the owner or responsible party.
23. Classify the project as approved, conditionally approved, or rejected pending additional investigation.
24. File the signed scope, photographs, measurements, risk register, prerequisites, and readiness decision.

## Required Outcome
A documented and approved repair or remodeling scope with measured existing conditions, defined limits, concealed-risk controls, required referrals, ordered work, and a formal readiness decision.

# Chapter 2

## Project Title
Set Up a Remodeling Work Area and Dust-Control Plan

## Filename
`chapter-02-set-up-remodeling-work-area-and-dust-control.html`

## Competencies
**RR-007 through RR-012:** Protect occupied spaces, establish containment, stage tools and materials, create debris paths, set up dust control, and complete the work-area safety inspection.

## Main Project
Prepare one occupied-home remodeling area with contents protection, containment, controlled access, dust collection, debris handling, tool staging, and daily shutdown procedures.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Review the approved scope, hazardous-material assessment, demolition limits, occupant plan, work hours, utility interruptions, and emergency contacts.
2. Confirm whether occupants, children, pets, tenants, neighbors, or vulnerable persons require relocation or special protection.
3. Walk the path from exterior access to the work area and identify floors, walls, doors, stairs, elevators, landscaping, and common areas requiring protection.
4. Remove authorized contents from the work zone and document items that must remain.
5. Establish clean, transition, work, cutting, material, waste, and restricted zones.
6. Install durable floor protection appropriate to the finished floor and expected traffic without creating a trip or moisture trap.
7. Protect walls, corners, doors, windows, cabinets, countertops, fixtures, appliances, and finished surfaces that remain.
8. Establish the approved containment boundary using compatible barriers, sealed transitions, and closable entries.
9. Protect or isolate HVAC supplies and returns only through the approved building and indoor-air-quality plan.
10. Set up local tool dust collection and approved air-filtration or pressure-control equipment where required.
11. Confirm that airflow does not distribute dust or fumes into occupied rooms, neighboring units, attached garages, or concealed cavities.
12. Establish makeup-air, exhaust, temperature, humidity, and weather controls compatible with the products and containment plan.
13. Create a protected debris path and define container, bagging, loading, and disposal procedures.
14. Locate waste containers so that sharp, heavy, dusty, wet, or regulated debris cannot injure occupants or contaminate clean areas.
15. Stage tools on stable surfaces with cords, hoses, chargers, batteries, blades, fasteners, and accessories organized by task.
16. Establish a separate cutting station with support for long or sheet materials, blade-clearance control, dust collection, lighting, and weather protection.
17. Stage materials flat, dry, secure, acclimated where required, and separated from waste or damp surfaces.
18. Locate fire extinguisher, first-aid supplies, utility shutoffs, communication device, lighting, and emergency exit route.
19. Provide safe temporary lighting without overloading circuits or placing hot lamps against combustible materials.
20. Establish worker sanitation, handwashing, drinking water, and clean-break procedures appropriate to the hazard assessment.
21. Install signs, barriers, door controls, and access rules for occupants and visitors.
22. Test containment, dust collection, filtration, lighting, access, and debris movement before demolition begins.
23. Complete a documented work-area safety and protection inspection.
24. Correct every failed protection, airflow, access, electrical, lighting, storage, or public-control item before authorizing work.
25. Define the daily cleanup, waste removal, tool security, utility restoration, and containment inspection procedure.
26. Photograph the completed setup and record filter, barrier, floor-protection, and access conditions.

## Required Outcome
A protected and controlled remodeling work area with functioning containment, safe access, organized staging, managed debris, tested dust controls, and a documented daily shutdown plan.

# Chapter 3

## Project Title
Perform Selective Interior Demolition

## Filename
`chapter-03-perform-selective-interior-demolition.html`

## Competencies
**RR-013 through RR-018:** Remove trim, remove drywall sections, remove cabinets or fixtures, protect hidden utilities, separate debris, and inspect exposed conditions.

## Main Project
Perform a controlled selective-demolition project that removes only approved finishes, trim, cabinet, fixture, or drywall components while preserving the building and exposing concealed conditions for inspection.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Review the demolition drawing, limits, salvage list, hazardous-material clearance, utility plan, structural restrictions, and required inspections.
2. Verify containment, floor protection, debris path, lighting, fire protection, and occupant exclusion.
3. Confirm required plumbing, electrical, gas, HVAC, alarm, or appliance shutdowns and document who performed them.
4. Scan, inspect, and otherwise evaluate the work area for concealed utilities and framing using approved methods.
5. Mark removal boundaries, cut lines, fastener locations, salvage items, protected items, and stop-work limits.
6. Remove doors, drawers, shelves, hardware, accessories, and other loose components that obstruct controlled demolition.
7. Label and store reusable hardware and components in protected containers.
8. Score paint, caulk, sealant, paper facing, and finish transitions at the removal boundary to reduce unintended damage.
9. Remove approved trim using thin prying tools, backing blocks, progressive release, and nail control.
10. Pull or cut exposed fasteners and remove sharp projections immediately.
11. Support cabinets, countertops, mirrors, sinks, fixtures, panels, and heavy components before releasing fasteners.
12. Disconnect and remove cabinets or fixtures only after all utilities and hidden attachments are verified.
13. Use team lifting or mechanical assistance for heavy, fragile, or awkward components.
14. Create small exploratory openings at approved locations before enlarging drywall or panel removal.
15. Stop work immediately for unverified wires, pipes, ducts, structural members, wet materials, mold, pests, hazardous material, fire damage, or unexpected movement.
16. Cut drywall or panel material to controlled lines without plunging blades beyond the known safe depth.
17. Remove material in manageable sections while protecting framing, insulation, utilities, and adjacent finishes.
18. Preserve required fire blocking, draft stopping, rated layers, backing, blocking, and structural sheathing unless the approved scope includes their replacement.
19. Separate salvage, clean recyclable material, ordinary debris, sharp metal, regulated waste, and contaminated material.
20. Bag or containerize dusty debris before moving it through clean or occupied areas.
21. Vacuum framing, cavities, and work surfaces using the approved equipment rather than dry sweeping hazardous dust.
22. Inspect exposed framing, subfloor, wall backing, insulation, utilities, fasteners, moisture, corrosion, decay, and previous alterations.
23. Photograph and measure concealed conditions before any repair or closure.
24. Compare actual conditions with the approved scope and issue a change notice for every material difference.
25. Stabilize open assemblies, cap or guard openings, and maintain utility safety until follow-on work begins.
26. Complete the selective-demolition inspection and authorize repair work only after concealed conditions are accepted.

## Required Outcome
A selectively opened work area with approved components removed, reusable materials protected, hidden systems undamaged, debris controlled, concealed conditions documented, and repair authorization established.

# Chapter 4

## Project Title
Remove and Replace Damaged Interior Trim

## Filename
`chapter-04-remove-and-replace-damaged-interior-trim.html`

## Competencies
**RR-019 through RR-024:** Remove damaged trim, preserve reusable pieces, match trim profiles, cut replacement trim, install new trim, and inspect the finished repair.

## Main Project
Remove and replace one defined section of damaged baseboard, casing, shoe molding, or similar non-structural interior trim while matching the existing profile, alignment, attachment, and finish.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Identify the trim type, material, profile, dimensions, joint style, finish, fasteners, backing, and adjacent surface conditions.
2. Determine whether the damage was caused by moisture, impact, movement, pests, poor attachment, or an unresolved substrate defect.
3. Correct or refer the underlying cause before replacing the trim.
4. Check for lead-containing coating or other hazardous finish before sanding, cutting, or disturbing older painted trim.
5. Photograph the existing joint layout, reveals, returns, copes, miters, transitions, and finish.
6. Select removal limits at natural joints or locations that can be blended cleanly.
7. Score paint and caulk lines along all retained surfaces.
8. Use protective blocks and progressive prying to release the damaged trim without crushing drywall, plaster, flooring, jambs, or adjacent trim.
9. Remove or cut fasteners and control sharp points.
10. Preserve an undamaged sample long enough to verify profile, thickness, species, and finish match.
11. Inspect the wall base, jamb, framing, backing, floor edge, moisture condition, and prior fastener damage.
12. Repair minor accepted substrate defects and install approved backing where the original attachment was inadequate.
13. Select replacement trim that matches the approved profile and dimensional relationship.
14. Condition or acclimate wood or composite trim as required by the product.
15. Measure the replacement from verified reference points rather than copying a distorted damaged piece.
16. Plan the sequence of square cuts, scarf joints, miters, copes, returns, and end conditions.
17. Cut a test piece and verify profile alignment before cutting the final material.
18. Cut replacement pieces with supported stock, controlled blade clearance, and clean face orientation.
19. Dry-fit the complete repair and verify length, joint closure, reveals, floor clearance, and transition to existing trim.
20. Apply primer or finish to concealed faces, cut ends, or difficult areas where the approved finish system requires it.
21. Fasten into verified framing or backing with the approved fastener length and placement.
22. Set fasteners without splitting, crushing, or distorting the trim.
23. Fill fastener holes and approved gaps with compatible material.
24. Caulk only joints intended to be flexible or painted; do not use caulk to conceal poor fit.
25. Sand and prepare the repair using the applicable dust and finish controls.
26. Prime and finish the replacement to match the approved color, sheen, and texture.
27. Inspect profile match, joint quality, reveal, alignment, attachment, finish, floor movement clearance, and damage to adjacent surfaces.
28. Document the trim material, finish product, repaired cause, and final acceptance.

## Required Outcome
A securely attached replacement trim section that matches the existing profile, joint layout, alignment, and finish over an accepted dry substrate.

# Chapter 5

## Project Title
Repair Damaged Drywall and Prepare for Finish

## Filename
`chapter-05-repair-damaged-drywall-and-prepare-for-finish.html`

## Competencies
**RR-025 through RR-030:** Cut out damaged drywall, install backing, install a patch panel, tape seams, apply compound, and inspect a paint-ready surface.

## Main Project
Remove one limited damaged drywall area and install, tape, coat, sand, and inspect a supported patch that restores the approved wall or ceiling assembly and is ready for primer.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Confirm the source of impact, moisture, movement, fastener failure, or surface damage has been corrected.
2. Identify the existing board type, thickness, number of layers, edge support, texture, finish, moisture classification, and fire-resistance requirements.
3. Confirm that the repair is not within an unapproved fire-rated, shaft, garage-separation, wet-area, structural, or specialty assembly.
4. Complete required hazardous-material assessment before disturbing joint compound, texture, coating, or board.
5. Locate framing, backing, wiring, plumbing, ducts, and other concealed systems.
6. Mark a controlled rectangular or framing-centered removal boundary beyond unsound material.
7. Score the finish and cut to a known safe depth without damaging concealed systems.
8. Remove damaged board in manageable pieces and retain evidence of the failure where useful.
9. Inspect the cavity, framing, insulation, utilities, fasteners, moisture, mold, pests, and adjacent board edges.
10. Stop and revise the scope if concealed damage, contamination, or assembly requirements exceed the approved repair.
11. Dry, clean, repair, or refer concealed conditions before closure.
12. Square and stabilize retained board edges.
13. Install approved wood or metal backing where seams do not land on existing framing.
14. Verify backing width, attachment, plane, and clearance from utilities.
15. Cut a matching patch panel with controlled gaps and correct face orientation.
16. Dry-fit the patch and verify that it sits flush without forcing or rocking.
17. Fasten the patch to framing and backing using the approved fastener type, spacing, edge distance, and depth.
18. Do not break the face paper or leave proud fasteners.
19. Pre-fill deep gaps only with the approved material and allow required set or cure.
20. Apply the specified tape centered over each seam.
21. Apply the first compound coat while maintaining a flat supported tape bed.
22. Allow the coat to dry or set under approved temperature, humidity, and ventilation conditions.
23. Apply successive wider coats, feathering edges and controlling buildup.
24. Match corner, bead, butt-joint, tapered-joint, or ceiling conditions to the existing assembly.
25. Use dust-controlled sanding or approved wet-finishing methods without exposing tape or damaging face paper.
26. Inspect the patch under raking light and from normal viewing positions.
27. Apply compatible texture only after the flatness and joint work are accepted where texture is part of the project.
28. Verify the surface is dry, clean, smooth, properly feathered, and free from ridges, pinholes, scratches, loose paper, and visible fastener defects.
29. Complete the paint-readiness inspection and document board, tape, compound, drying conditions, and concealed photographs.

## Required Outcome
A supported drywall repair that restores the approved assembly and presents a dry, smooth, properly feathered, paint-ready surface.

# Chapter 6

## Project Title
Repair Squeaky Floors and Loose Subflooring

## Filename
`chapter-06-repair-squeaky-floors-and-loose-subflooring.html`

## Competencies
**RR-031 through RR-036:** Locate floor squeaks, identify loose subfloor areas, secure subfloor panels, correct raised fasteners, patch access points, and inspect floor stability.

## Main Project
Diagnose and correct one limited floor squeak or loose-subfloor condition without damaging concealed utilities, weakening framing, or creating a visible flooring defect.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Interview the occupant about the squeak location, seasonality, loading condition, and prior repairs.
2. Identify the finish-floor type, underlayment, subfloor, joist direction, framing access, and room below.
3. Confirm the symptom is not caused by structural movement, damaged joists, inadequate bearing, moisture swelling, plumbing contact, duct movement, or unsafe flooring.
4. Stop and refer structural sag, rot, broken framing, widespread movement, or active moisture.
5. Remove loose rugs, furniture, or approved finish components needed for safe access.
6. Map the squeak by walking the floor in a controlled grid while a second person observes from below where access exists.
7. Mark sound locations, movement locations, joist lines, panel seams, fastener lines, and suspected pipe or duct routes.
8. Use approved locating methods to verify framing and concealed utilities before fastening or drilling.
9. Inspect from the basement, crawlspace, or ceiling access where available.
10. Identify whether the movement occurs between subfloor and joist, at a panel seam, within underlayment, at a raised fastener, or within the finish floor.
11. Select the least destructive approved repair method for the verified condition.
12. Remove or protect finish flooring only as required by the selected method.
13. Correct accessible raised, backed-out, or rubbing fasteners without driving them into utilities or below required support.
14. Install approved subfloor fasteners into verified joist locations using controlled length, spacing, and depth.
15. Draw the panel into contact without crushing the finish, stripping the framing, or dimpling the surface.
16. Where access from below is used, remove debris from the joint and install approved blocking, cleats, shims, or adhesive only as specified.
17. Do not force shims that lift the floor, separate assemblies, or transfer load improperly.
18. Do not notch, drill, or cut joists to reach the squeak.
19. Test the repair after each limited fastening or support step rather than installing excessive fasteners.
20. Repeat the walking and observation test across the repaired area.
21. Check adjacent flooring for new movement, fastener telegraphing, finish damage, or changes in plane.
22. Patch approved access holes or reinstall removed finish components using the matching system.
23. Clean the floor and remove all sharp fasteners, dust, and debris.
24. Inspect stability, sound reduction, floor plane, finish condition, and concealed-work documentation.
25. Record the repair locations and any residual noise that originates outside the approved repair limit.

## Required Outcome
A documented localized floor repair with verified subfloor attachment or support, reduced movement and noise, intact framing and utilities, and restored access points.

# Chapter 7

## Project Title
Replace Damaged Flooring in a Small Area

## Filename
`chapter-07-replace-damaged-flooring-small-area.html`

## Competencies
**RR-037 through RR-042:** Remove damaged flooring, prepare the substrate, match replacement material, install a repair section, blend transitions, and inspect repair integration.

## Main Project
Replace one limited damaged section of approved resilient, laminate, engineered, tile, or wood flooring using a system-specific repair method and a verified dry, flat substrate.

## Required Project Sequence

1. Identify the flooring product, manufacturer, material, thickness, wear layer, locking profile, attachment method, underlayment, pattern, dye lot, and expansion requirements.
2. Confirm the chapter repair method is approved for the specific flooring system.
3. Determine and correct the cause of damage, including water, impact, movement, heat, sunlight, appliance leakage, substrate failure, or installation error.
4. Complete hazardous-material assessment before disturbing older flooring, backing, adhesive, patching compound, or underlayment.
5. Measure moisture conditions and verify that the substrate and room meet product requirements.
6. Stop for widespread moisture, mold, substrate decay, structural movement, or material that cannot be safely disturbed.
7. Photograph the pattern, seams, transitions, base, door clearances, and furniture or appliance locations.
8. Select removal limits at natural seams, board joints, grout joints, pattern breaks, or transitions.
9. Remove base, shoe molding, transitions, fixtures, or approved adjacent pieces needed for access while preserving reusable components.

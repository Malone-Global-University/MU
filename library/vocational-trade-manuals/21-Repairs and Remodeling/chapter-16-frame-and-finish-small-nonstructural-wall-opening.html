Job Overview

This project qualifies you to create one small opening in an interior partition that has already been positively verified as non-load-bearing, and to frame, restore, and finish that opening so it is structurally sound, free of utility conflicts, and integrated with the surrounding wall.

You will confirm the opening dimensions and approved design, verify non-structural status and utility routes through plans and exploratory access, protect the room, lay out and cut the wall finish on both faces, expose and inspect the framing bay before cutting any stud, cut only the studs identified in the approved detail, install king studs, trimmers, header, and sill framing for the opening, restore fire blocking and insulation affected by the work, close the wall with matching drywall or panel material, and finish the opening with corner bead, jamb material, tape, compound, primer, and paint to match the room.

This chapter depends entirely on the readiness process from Chapter 1 and the demolition controls from Chapter 3. It does not authorize the learner to determine whether a wall is load-bearing; that determination must already be positively verified by plans, field investigation, and qualified evaluation before Step 1 of this chapter begins.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-091 — Verify non-structural conditions using plans, field investigation, and qualified evaluation before laying out or cutting a wall opening.
  • RR-092 — Mark the opening on both wall faces from common level and plumb reference points, accounting for framing, drywall, and trim thickness.
  • RR-093 — Cut drywall or panel material to controlled lines and depths without damaging concealed utilities or retained framing.
  • RR-094 — Frame the opening with king studs, trimmers, header, sill, and blocking specified by the approved non-structural framing detail.
  • RR-095 — Finish the opening's edges with matching wall material, corner bead, jamb material, tape, compound, primer, and paint.
  • RR-096 — Inspect the completed opening for dimension, square, plumb, framing attachment, utility clearance, and finish integration.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter builds directly on the readiness process from Chapter 1, the containment and dust-control setup from Chapter 2, the selective-demolition controls from Chapter 3, the drywall-repair methods from Chapter 5, and the casing and paint-integration procedures from Chapters 9 and 10. Chapter 16 is the first chapter in this book to authorize cutting a framing member, and it does so only within the narrow, positively verified non-structural condition this chapter defines.

The verification records, framing photographs, and inspection approvals produced here are the documents Chapter 19's punch-list inspection and Chapter 20's capstone remodel review when a wall opening is part of the approved project.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • obtaining the approved opening dimensions, location, purpose, and framing detail from the book outline or an approved design
  • verifying non-load-bearing status through plans, field investigation, and qualified evaluation
  • locating wiring, plumbing, gas, ducts, vents, communications, alarms, and fire blocking before any cutting
  • laying out and cutting drywall or panel material on both faces to a controlled depth
  • exposing the full opening area for inspection before any stud is cut
  • cutting only the studs identified in the approved framing detail and installing king studs, trimmers, header, and sill framing for the opening
  • restoring insulation, fire blocking, and other wall components required by the approved assembly
  • installing matching drywall, corner bead, jamb material, tape, compound, primer, and paint
  • inspecting the completed opening for dimension, square, plumb, attachment, and finish integration

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • determine, independently and without plans or qualified evaluation, that a wall is non-load-bearing; this chapter begins only after that determination has already been positively verified
  • cut, modify, or open a shear wall, braced wall panel, party wall, garage-separation wall, smoke barrier, fire-rated assembly, shaft wall, plumbing wall, or duct chase
  • cut top plates, bottom plates, adjacent studs outside the approved detail, fire blocking, or any concealed structural member not expressly identified in the approved framing detail
  • relocate wiring, plumbing, gas lines, ducts, or other utilities encountered in the opening area without an approved design and a qualified trade
  • close the opening over an unresolved utility conflict, an unverified structural condition, or a concealed condition that differs from the approved scope

Any opening located in a wall that has not been positively verified as non-load-bearing, or that involves a fire-rated, shear, braced, or utility-bearing wall, is outside this chapter's scope and requires the design, permits, and qualified-trade review those conditions demand.

Trade Note

Positive verification of a non-load-bearing wall is not a visual guess. It comes from approved plans, an attic or floor-framing investigation that shows the wall does not carry joists, trusses, or a beam, and, where the condition is uncertain, a qualified evaluation. If any one of those sources is unavailable or contradicts another, this chapter does not proceed until the conflict is resolved.

Setup Standard

Do not mark or cut any wall face (Step 9 forward) until the non-load-bearing verification (Step 2), the utility and fire-blocking location (Step 4), and the approved opening dimensions (Step 1) are all recorded in writing. Do not cut any stud until the full opening area has been exposed and inspected (Step 16).

Shop Tip

Cut small exploratory openings before enlarging either face. An exploratory opening that reveals an unexpected duct, a plumbing vent, or a second layer of blocking is far easier to work around when it is found early than after the finished opening's layout has already been cut to full size.

Common Field Fundamentals

This chapter is the book's first authorization to cut a framing member, and it grants that authorization only within a narrow, already-verified condition. The discipline this chapter teaches — confirm before cutting, expose before committing, and stop the moment a concealed condition contradicts the approved detail — carries forward into every later chapter that touches structure, plumbing, or electrical work.

Why This Chapter Does Not Determine Load-Bearing Status

Determining whether a wall is load-bearing requires tracing the framing above the ceiling and below the floor, identifying whether joists, trusses, or a beam bear on the wall, and, in an uncertain case, a qualified evaluation. This chapter assumes that work is already complete and documented before Step 1. If the learner arrives at this chapter without that documentation, the correct action is to stop and obtain it, not to infer load-bearing status from wall thickness, stud spacing, or the presence or absence of a header at a nearby existing opening.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Cut Until the Opening Is Fully Exposed

Do not cut any stud until both wall faces have been opened across the full approved opening width and the entire cavity has been inspected for wiring, plumbing, gas, ducts, vents, fire blocking, and unexpected framing. A stud cut before this inspection can sever a live circuit, an active pipe, or a required fire-blocking member before it has been identified.

Code Alert — Verify Permit and Inspection Requirements Locally

Even a non-structural wall opening may require a permit and a framing inspection in some jurisdictions, particularly where the wall separates dwelling units, encloses a required fire-rated corridor, or is part of a garage separation. Confirm the local building department's requirement before framing begins, and do not close the wall until any required framing inspection has been completed.

Measurement Cue — Account for Every Layer Before Marking the Opening

The finished opening size the owner expects to see is not the same as the framed rough-opening size. Before marking either wall face, add the thickness of drywall or panel material, corner bead, jamb material, and any casing to the approved finished dimension to determine the actual framed opening size the trimmers and header must produce.

Field Safety Cue — Support Cut Framing and Loose Panels

A cut stud section or a loosened drywall panel can shift or fall as adjacent material is removed. Support cut framing members before their last fastener is released, and do not leave a partially cut panel resting unsupported against the opening while other steps are performed.

E. Preparation — Verification and Layout (Steps 1–8)

  1. Obtain the approved opening dimensions, location, purpose, finish detail, framing detail, and required permits or inspections. Record the finished-opening width and height, the framing detail reference, and any permit or inspection requirement on the framing record before proceeding.
  2. Verify through plans, field investigation, and qualified evaluation that the wall is non-load-bearing and is not a shear wall, braced wall panel, fire-rated assembly, smoke barrier, garage separation, shaft, party wall, plumbing wall, duct chase, or protected egress element. Record the verification source, verifier, and date on the Verification and Boundary Record. Stop if any source is unavailable or if two sources conflict.
  3. Verify ceiling, floor, roof, joist, truss, beam, post, and foundation relationships above and below the opening. Record the framing direction and bearing condition observed above and below the wall from the field investigation.
  4. Locate and identify wiring, plumbing, gas, ducts, vents, communications, alarms, and fire blocking using approved methods and exploratory access. Mark each identified route on the wall face and record it as a suspected or confirmed condition on the risk register.
  5. Stop if any structural or utility condition is uncertain or conflicts with the approved opening. Do not proceed to Step 6 until the conflict is resolved through the approved change-control process.
  6. Complete hazardous-material assessment before cutting the wall finish. Confirm the assessment result is recorded and any required abatement is complete before layout begins.
  7. Coordinate relocation of any permitted utilities through approved designs and qualified trades before framing the opening. Record the qualified trade, scope, and completion date for any utility relocation required by the approved design.
  8. Protect the room and establish containment, lighting, access, and debris controls. Confirm the Chapter 2 containment and dust-control setup is in place and functioning before marking either wall face.

F. Layout and Exploratory Access (Steps 9–13)

  1. Lay out both faces of the opening from common level and plumb references. Establish one level line and one plumb line common to both wall faces and mark the opening from those references, not from independent measurements on each face.
  2. Verify the finished opening size accounts for framing, drywall or panel thickness, corner bead, jamb, trim, and required clearances. Add each layer's thickness to the approved finished dimension per the Measurement Cue above and record the resulting framed rough-opening size.
  3. Mark cut lines and exploratory openings. Mark the full opening boundary and at least one smaller exploratory cut location within that boundary before committing to the full-size cut.
  4. Create controlled exploratory openings to confirm stud locations, utilities, blocking, and wall thickness. Cut the exploratory opening to a controlled depth and inspect it before enlarging the cut.
  5. Revise the layout only through the approved change-control process. Document any layout change and the reason before marking the revised cut lines.

G. Wall-Face Removal and Concealed-Condition Inspection (Steps 14–17)

  1. Score finish layers and cut the first face to a controlled depth. Score paint, texture, and joint compound along the cut line before cutting through the drywall or panel to the marked boundary.
  2. Remove drywall or panel sections in manageable pieces while preserving retained edges. Support each removed section as it is released and set it aside without damaging the retained wall edge.
  3. Inspect the full opening area before cutting any stud. Confirm every identified utility, fire-blocking member, and stud location visible in the exposed cavity matches the Step 4 record before proceeding.
  4. Install temporary protection or bracing where the approved plan requires it, even in a non-load-bearing partition. Install any temporary support the approved framing detail specifies before removing a stud that provides lateral bracing to adjacent finishes.

H. Framing (Steps 18–23)

  1. Cut only the studs identified in the approved framing detail. Confirm each stud against the approved detail before cutting; do not cut a stud that is not specifically identified.
  2. Do not cut top plates, bottom plates, adjacent studs, fire blocking, or concealed structural members unless expressly detailed. Stop and revise the scope through change control if the approved detail did not anticipate a member that must be cut.
  3. Install approved king studs, trimmers, sill, head framing, blocking, and perimeter members for the non-structural opening. Install each member in the sequence specified by the approved detail, verifying plumb and level before fastening.
  4. Fasten framing using the specified method without splitting members or contacting utilities. Use the fastener type, size, and spacing from Section C, and verify clearance from every utility identified in Step 4.
  5. Verify opening width, height, square, plumb, framing plane, and attachment. Measure the framed opening against the Section D acceptance standards before covering any framing member.
  6. Complete required framing and utility inspection before closure. Do not proceed to Section I until any required inspection has been scheduled or completed per the Code Alert above.

I. Wall Restoration and Finish (Steps 24–29)

  1. Restore insulation, fire blocking, acoustic treatment, vapor control, or other wall components required by the approved assembly. Install each required component per the approved assembly before closing the wall.
  2. Install matching drywall or finish panels with supported edges. Cut and dry-fit the patch panel per the Chapter 5 drywall-repair standard before fastening.
  3. Install corner bead, jamb material, returns, casing, or edge trim according to the approved finish design. Fasten or embed each component per the manufacturer's instructions and the approved finish design.
  4. Tape, coat, sand, prime, and paint repaired wall surfaces in the correct sequence. Follow the Chapter 5 taping and Chapter 10 paint-integration sequence for every restored surface.
  5. Finish exposed framing or jamb surfaces and protect all cut edges. Apply the specified finish to any exposed framing or jamb edge before the opening is placed into use.
  6. Inspect structural verification records, framing, opening dimensions, wall restoration, corners, trim, finish, utility clearances, and adjacent damage. Complete this inspection using the Section D acceptance standards before Section J.

J. Documentation and Closure (Step 30)

  1. Document photographs before closure, inspection approvals, framing details, products, and final acceptance. File the completed verification record, framing photographs taken before drywall closure, inspection approvals, and final acceptance together with the room's Chapter 1 documentation package.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed opening using the same checks regardless of its size or location within the approved room.

  • Verification check: confirm the non-load-bearing determination is documented from plans, field investigation, and qualified evaluation where required, and was completed before layout began.
  • Dimension check: confirm the framed opening matches the approved width and height within the stated tolerance.
  • Square and plumb check: confirm king studs are plumb, the header is level, and the opening corners are square within the stated tolerance.
  • Utility check: confirm no wiring, plumbing, gas line, or duct contacts or was damaged by any framing member or fastener.
  • Concealed-condition check: confirm every concealed condition that differed from the approved scope was resolved through a documented change order before closure.
  • Finish check: confirm the restored surface matches surrounding texture, sheen, and color under normal and raking light.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Header Does Not Bear Fully on Both Trimmers

Probable cause: one trimmer was cut slightly long or short, or the header was installed before both trimmers were verified plumb and at equal height.
Confirmation: measure both trimmer heights against the approved detail and check for a gap between the header and either trimmer.
Correction: remove and re-cut the incorrect trimmer to the specified height and reinstall the header with full bearing on both sides.
Reinspection: repeat the square, plumb, and level check across the full opening.

An Unidentified Utility Was Found After the Wall Face Was Opened

Probable cause: the Step 4 utility location did not identify a route present in the cavity, or the route was mislabeled on the wall face.
Confirmation: compare the exposed condition against the Step 4 record and confirm whether the utility is active.
Correction: stop framing at that location, treat the utility as active until verified otherwise, and process a change order identifying the required relocation, protection, or design revision before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the concealed-condition inspection after the change order is resolved.

The Framed Opening Is Smaller Than the Approved Finished Dimension

Probable cause: the Step 10 layer-thickness allowance for drywall, corner bead, jamb, or trim was not added to the approved finished dimension before marking the framed opening.
Confirmation: measure the actual framed opening and compare it against the approved finished dimension plus the required layer allowances.
Correction: reframe the opening to the correct rough dimension before installing any finish layer; do not compensate by trimming the finish material undersized.
Reinspection: repeat the dimension check before proceeding to Section I.

The Restored Wall Surface Shows a Visible Patch Line After Painting

Probable cause: the compound coats were not feathered wide enough, or the paint was not extended to a natural break per the Chapter 10 standard.
Confirmation: inspect the surface under raking light and compare the patch boundary to the Chapter 10 finish-integration standard.
Correction: reapply and feather an additional compound coat over a wider area, sand, prime, and repaint to a natural break or the full approved wall plane.
Reinspection: repeat the finish check under normal and raking light.

Fire Blocking or Insulation Was Not Restored Before Closure

Probable cause: Step 24 was skipped or the required component was not identified before the drywall patch was installed.
Confirmation: review the pre-closure photographs and the approved assembly requirements against what was actually installed.
Correction: remove the drywall patch, install the required fire blocking or insulation, and document the correction before reclosing the wall.
Reinspection: repeat the pre-closure inspection before installing the drywall patch a second time.

Cleanup

This project ends with a fully closed, finished, and inspected wall opening, not with an open framing cavity.

  • Remove all cut drywall, framing offcuts, insulation debris, and fasteners from the work area
  • Vacuum framing cavities and surrounding surfaces before closing the wall, and clean sanding dust after finishing
  • Confirm every temporary support or brace has been removed once the approved framing is complete and inspected
  • Store photographs, verification records, framing details, and inspection approvals together as a single dated package for the opening
  • Restore containment removal in the controlled clean-to-dirty sequence established in Chapter 2

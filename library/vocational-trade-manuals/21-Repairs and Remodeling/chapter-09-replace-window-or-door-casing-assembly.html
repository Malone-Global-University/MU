Job Overview

This project qualifies you to remove one damaged interior window or non-rated door casing assembly and install a complete matching replacement while preserving the jamb behind it. You will identify the existing casing profile and joint system, confirm the project does not touch the window installation, exterior water-management system, a rated door assembly, an egress opening, or a structural header, remove the casing without damaging the wall or jamb, inspect what the casing was covering, cut and dry-fit a replacement using the same reveal and joint system, fasten it into verified backing, and finish it to match the room.

The finished work is one opening with new casing installed at a consistent reveal, sound backing and attachment, matched profile and finish, clean joints, and unaffected window or door operation. This chapter builds directly on the trim-removal and trim-matching skills from Chapter 4 and the readiness process from Chapter 1, and it feeds directly into the wall-preparation and paint-integration work in Chapter 10.

This is a casing chapter, not a window or door chapter. The window sash, glazing, weatherstripping, exterior flashing, and any rated door core, frame, or hardware are outside this chapter's scope and are addressed, where applicable, through the approved plans, manufacturer instructions, and qualified trades identified in Chapter 1.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-049 — Remove an existing casing assembly cleanly, preserving the jamb, wall finish, and a representative sample piece for matching.
  • RR-050 — Inspect jamb, framing, backing, and wall-edge condition exposed by casing removal, and identify conditions that stop or limit the repair.
  • RR-051 — Cut new casing to match the existing profile, joint style, and dimensional relationship using verified reference measurements.
  • RR-052 — Establish and maintain a consistent reveal around the jamb throughout layout, dry-fit, and installation.
  • RR-053 — Fasten casing into verified jamb and framing backing using the specified fastener and pattern, without contacting glass, hardware, or wiring.
  • RR-054 — Inspect the finished trim for reveal consistency, joint quality, attachment, finish, and unaffected window or door operation.

Where This Skill Fits

Casing is the last layer covering the gap between a jamb and the surrounding wall finish. Removing it exposes shims, fasteners, insulation, and sometimes evidence of a leaking window or a settled door frame — conditions Chapter 1's inspection process may have already flagged as suspected concealed conditions. This chapter is where that exposure happens under controlled conditions, and where the underlying opening problem, if one exists, must be corrected or referred before new casing is installed.

The completed casing assembly becomes part of the finish integration that Chapter 10 blends into the surrounding wall paint, and the same reveal, joint-matching, and inspection disciplines taught here are reused directly in the Chapter 20 capstone whenever the selected capstone room includes a window or door casing repair.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the existing casing profile, material, joint system, reveal, and finish before removal
  • confirming the project does not alter the window installation, exterior water-management system, a rated door assembly, an egress opening, or a structural header
  • removing the existing casing without damaging the jamb, wall finish, or adjacent trim
  • inspecting the exposed jamb, framing, shims, and backing for moisture, decay, movement, and prior repairs
  • cutting and dry-fitting replacement casing that matches the existing profile, joint style, and reveal
  • fastening the new casing into verified backing and finishing it to match the approved room finish
  • inspecting the completed casing for reveal consistency, joint quality, attachment, finish, and unaffected window or door operation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • remove, adjust, reinstall, reglaze, or reflash a window sash, frame, or exterior water-management assembly
  • alter a fire-rated, smoke-rated, or otherwise rated door frame, core, or hardware
  • widen, narrow, or otherwise resize an egress opening or its clear width or height
  • cut, notch, or modify a structural header, king stud, trimmer, or jamb extension beyond the approved casing-removal limit
  • proceed with casing installation over a jamb the inspection finds loose, leaking, decayed, structurally distorted, or part of an unresolved rated assembly; such a condition must be referred and resolved first

Any window installation defect, structural distortion, or rated-assembly condition identified during this chapter's jamb inspection must be referred to the qualified trade or design authority identified in the risk register before new casing is installed.

Trade Note

Casing damage is frequently a symptom, not the problem. A cracked miter, a stained corner, or repeated finish failure at the same casing joint is often evidence of window leakage or door-frame movement behind it. Treat casing damage as a reason to inspect the jamb before assuming the casing itself is simply worn out.

Common Field Fundamentals

A casing assembly does two jobs: it covers the gap between the jamb and the wall finish, and it presents a consistent, level, plumb, and evenly reveal-set frame around the opening. Every step in this chapter protects one of those two jobs — protecting the jamb and wall during removal, and protecting the reveal and joint alignment during installation.

Casing Systems: Mitered, Coped, Square, and Rosette

A mitered system joins head and side casing at a 45-degree angle at each top corner. A coped system uses a square-cut head casing with the side casing's inside profile cut, or "coped," to fit against it. A square-cut system uses simple butt joints, often at a plinth block or rosette. Identify which system the existing casing uses before cutting any replacement piece, and use that same system throughout the repair; do not substitute a mitered corner for an existing coped or rosette detail.

Measurement Cue — Set the Reveal Before Marking Any Casing

Establish the reveal, typically 3/16 inch to 1/4 inch as specified by the approved trim system, using a reveal gauge or combination square set to that dimension, and mark it at multiple points around the jamb before laying out any casing piece. A reveal that is not verified at the head and at both side jambs before cutting will not read as consistent once the casing is installed.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Alter a Rated or Egress Assembly

Stop immediately if the opening is found to be part of a fire-rated or smoke-rated door assembly, an egress window or door, or a structural header, or if removal reveals framing distortion, active leakage, or decay. Do not continue casing removal past the point needed to expose the condition. Refer the condition to the qualified trade or design authority identified in the Chapter 1 risk register before proceeding.

Field Safety Cue — Protect Glass and Hardware During Removal and Fastening

Keep pry bars, saws, and fasteners clear of window glass, sash balances, weatherstripping, and door hardware at all times. Support any loose or unsecured sash component you encounter rather than forcing it back into place, and stop if a window balance or hardware component appears damaged.

Code Alert — Verify Egress and Rated-Assembly Requirements Locally

Minimum egress opening dimensions and fire- or smoke-rated door assembly requirements vary by jurisdiction and by the specific listed assembly. This chapter does not authorize any change to an egress opening or a rated assembly; verify the exact requirement with the local building department or the assembly's listing documentation before any such condition is disturbed.

E. Preparation — Identification and Documentation (Steps 1–4)

  1. Identify the opening type, casing profile, material, width, thickness, joint style, stool or apron condition, jamb extension, reveal, finish, and adjacent wall texture. Record each characteristic on the project record, measuring the existing reveal at the head and both side jambs with a reveal gauge before any removal begins.
  2. Confirm that the project does not alter the window installation, exterior water-management system, rated door assembly, egress opening, or structural header. Verify this against the Chapter 1 readiness decision and risk register; do not proceed until every exclusion is confirmed in writing.
  3. Check for hazardous coatings before disturbing painted casing or adjacent wall finish. Complete the required assessment and apply the corresponding containment and work-method controls before any sanding, scraping, or cutting of painted material.
  4. Photograph all joints, reveals, returns, transitions, and defects. Photograph the full opening and close-up views of each corner joint, the stool or apron, and any visible damage before touching the casing.

F. Cause Identification and Removal (Steps 5–10)

  1. Determine whether casing damage was caused by moisture, jamb movement, window leakage, door-frame movement, impact, or finish failure. Record the suspected cause as an observed fact or a suspected concealed condition, consistent with the Chapter 1 fact-labeling standard.
  2. Correct or refer the underlying opening or moisture problem before installing new casing. Do not install replacement casing over an unresolved leak, movement, or moisture condition; stop and refer it to the qualified trade identified in the risk register.
  3. Score paint and caulk lines along the wall and jamb. Run a utility knife along every painted or caulked seam between the casing and the wall or jamb to reduce tear-out during removal.
  4. Remove hardware, blinds, curtains, stops, or accessories only where authorized. Label and store each removed item, and leave any item not authorized for removal in place and protected.
  5. Release casing progressively with protective blocks and remove fasteners safely. Work from one end of each piece to the other, using a protective block behind the pry bar at every contact point with the wall or jamb, and set aside removed fasteners for controlled disposal.
  6. Preserve one representative piece for profile and finish matching. Select the least-damaged full-length piece removed and store it flat until the replacement casing has been finished and accepted.

G. Jamb Inspection (Steps 11–13)

  1. Inspect jambs, jamb extensions, framing edges, shims, backing, drywall, plaster, moisture, and fastener damage. Record each finding as an observed fact, including shim condition, gaps, and any staining or softness detected by probing with a screwdriver or moisture meter.
  2. Stop if the opening is loose, leaking, decayed, structurally distorted, or part of an unresolved rated assembly. Halt work immediately, protect the open jamb from further disturbance and weather, and refer the condition per the risk register before continuing.
  3. Repair minor accepted wall-edge, jamb, backing, or shim conditions. Complete only the minor repairs the approved scope allows, using compatible materials, and re-inspect the repaired area before proceeding to layout.

H. Layout, Cutting, and Dry-Fit (Steps 14–20)

  1. Select replacement casing matching the approved profile and thickness. Confirm the selected stock matches the preserved sample piece in profile, width, and thickness before cutting any piece to length.
  2. Condition material as required and prefinish concealed or difficult surfaces where specified. Allow wood or composite stock to acclimate to the room per the product's requirements, and prefinish any back or edge surface that will be difficult to coat after installation.
  3. Establish the reveal around the jamb with a gauge or consistent measurement. Mark the reveal line at the head and both side jambs using the same reveal gauge setting recorded in Step 1.
  4. Mark head and side casing intersections using the approved mitered, square, rosette, or other joint system. Lay out each joint location directly from the reveal marks, matching the joint system identified in Step 1.
  5. Cut test joints and verify profile alignment. Cut a scrap test joint at each corner angle required and confirm the profile lines match cleanly before cutting the final material.
  6. Cut the final casing with supported stock and clean face orientation. Support long pieces fully during the cut, keep the finished face oriented consistently, and label each piece by location immediately after cutting.
  7. Dry-fit the full assembly and verify reveal, joint closure, head alignment, floor or stool termination, and wall contact. Hold every piece in place without fasteners and confirm the reveal is consistent at all three jambs, the corner joints close tightly, and the casing sits flat against the wall along its full length.

I. Installation and Fastening (Steps 21–24)

  1. Make controlled adjustments without pulling a distorted jamb into position with trim fasteners. Correct any gap by adjusting the casing cut or shimming behind it; do not force a jamb into alignment using nailing pressure.
  2. Fasten casing into verified jamb edges, framing, or backing with appropriate fasteners. Confirm framing and backing locations with the stud or utility locator before driving each fastener listed in the Fastener and Hardware Schedule.
  3. Keep fasteners clear of glass, window balances, wiring, and door hardware. Verify clearance at every fastener location before driving, and stop if a fastener path would intersect glass, hardware, or a concealed utility.
  4. Set and fill fasteners with compatible material. Set every fastener head below the surface with a nail set and fill each hole with the compatible filler identified in the Material Takeoff.

J. Finish and Documentation (Steps 25–29)

  1. Caulk approved wall-to-casing joints and finish joints without masking poor fit. Apply the paintable caulk only at the joints the approved finish system specifies; do not use caulk to close a gap that should be corrected by refitting the casing.
  2. Prime and finish to the approved color, sheen, and coating system. Sand, prime, and apply the finish coats matching the color formula, sheen, and coating system recorded in Step 1, allowing the required dry time between coats.
  3. Reinstall authorized accessories without compromising casing or opening operation. Reinstall any hardware, blinds, curtains, or stops removed in Step 8 and confirm they operate without binding on the new casing.
  4. Inspect reveal consistency, miters or square joints, alignment, attachment, finish, jamb condition, and opening operation. Complete the full inspection using the Acceptance Standards below before closing out the project.
  5. Document material, finish, repaired cause, and final measurements. Record the casing product, finish product, the cause identified in Step 5 and its correction, and the final reveal measurements on the project record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Reveal Is Inconsistent Around the Opening

Probable cause: the reveal was marked at only one point rather than at the head and both side jambs, or the casing shifted during fastening.
Confirmation: measure the reveal at the head and both side jambs with a reveal gauge and compare each reading to the recorded target.
Correction: remove and reset the affected piece using the marked reveal line, or shim behind the casing to restore a consistent gap.
Reinspection: repeat the reveal-consistency check at all three jambs.

A Mitered or Coped Corner Joint Has a Visible Gap

Probable cause: the test-joint cut in Step 18 was skipped or the final cut angle drifted from the marked layout.
Confirmation: hold the joint together without fasteners and check for light passing through the gap.
Correction: recut the affected piece using a new test joint to verify the angle before the final cut.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-closure check on the corrected piece.

A Fastener Was Driven Into the Window Frame, Glass Stop, or Concealed Wiring

Probable cause: the fastener location was not verified with the stud or utility locator before driving, or the fastener length was not confirmed against the jamb and wall thickness.
Confirmation: inspect the fastener location from both faces where accessible and check for resistance, deflection, or damage at the suspected contact point.
Correction: remove the fastener immediately, inspect the affected component, and refer any damaged window, hardware, or wiring condition to the qualified trade identified in the risk register before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the fastener-clearance check on every remaining fastener location before finishing the installation.

The Finish Does Not Match the Surrounding Room Under Raking Light

Probable cause: the color formula, sheen, or coating system was not verified against the approved sample before application, or an insufficient number of coats was applied.
Confirmation: compare the finished casing to the recorded color formula and sheen under both normal and raking light.
Correction: apply an additional coat using the verified product, or correct the product selection and refinish the casing.
Reinspection: repeat the finish-match check under both lighting conditions.

The Window or Door Binds Against the New Casing

Probable cause: the casing was installed with insufficient reveal, or the reveal line was marked before an unresolved jamb-movement condition was corrected.
Confirmation: operate the window or door through its full range and identify the exact point of contact against the casing.
Correction: remove and reset the affected casing piece with additional reveal, or refer the underlying jamb condition if the contact point corresponds to a movement or distortion issue identified in Section G.
Reinspection: repeat the opening-operation check through the full range of motion.

Cleanup

  • Reinstall or return any authorized accessory removed during the project

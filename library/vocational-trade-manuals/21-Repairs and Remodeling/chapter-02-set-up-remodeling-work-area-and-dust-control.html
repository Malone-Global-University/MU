Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert the readiness decision produced in Chapter 1 into a physically protected, contained, and controlled occupied-home work area. You will protect the contents and finishes that remain in place, establish a containment boundary that keeps dust and debris out of occupied rooms, set up and test dust-collection and airflow equipment, create a safe debris path from the work area to a waste container, stage tools and materials for efficient and safe use, and complete a documented work-area safety inspection before any demolition begins.

You will review the approved scope, confirm occupant and vulnerable-person protections, protect floors and remaining finishes, establish clean and dirty zones with a sealed containment boundary, set up local dust collection and air-filtration equipment, verify that airflow does not migrate into occupied spaces, create a controlled debris path and waste-handling plan, stage tools and materials at a dedicated cutting station, confirm emergency egress and life-safety items, and test every protection and airflow control before authorizing work.

This is the second chapter in the book. It begins from the existing-condition report, approved scope, risk register, and readiness decision produced in Chapter 1, and it produces the protected, tested work area that Chapter 3's selective demolition depends on. No demolition may begin until this chapter's work-area safety inspection is complete and every failed item has been corrected.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-007 — Protect occupied spaces by installing floor protection, wall and fixture protection, and contents relocation appropriate to the approved scope.
  • RR-008 — Establish a containment boundary using compatible barriers, sealed transitions, and closable entries that separate the work zone from occupied areas.
  • RR-009 — Stage tools and materials, including a dedicated cutting station, in an organized and safe configuration appropriate to the project.
  • RR-010 — Create a debris path and waste-handling plan that prevents injury and contamination in occupied and clean areas.
  • RR-011 — Set up and verify dust collection, air filtration, and airflow controls so dust and fumes do not migrate into occupied rooms or concealed cavities.
  • RR-012 — Complete a documented work-area safety inspection and correct every failed protection, access, or control item before authorizing work.

Where This Skill Fits

Every demolition, repair, and installation chapter in this book takes place inside this protected work area. The containment boundary, dust controls, and debris path built here are what keep Chapter 3's selective demolition, Chapter 5's drywall repair, and every later cutting, sanding, or finishing task from contaminating occupied rooms.

This chapter reviews the approved scope, hazardous-material risk flags, and occupant protections recorded in the Chapter 1 readiness decision. The completed and tested work area produced here is the documented starting condition that Chapter 3 verifies before any tool is used to open a wall, floor, or cabinet.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the approved scope, hazardous-material assessment, demolition limits, occupant plan, work hours, and emergency contacts from Chapter 1
  • confirming relocation or protection needs for occupants, children, pets, tenants, neighbors, or vulnerable persons
  • protecting floors, walls, corners, doors, windows, cabinets, countertops, fixtures, and finishes that remain
  • establishing a sealed containment boundary with closable entries between clean and work zones
  • setting up and testing local dust collection and approved air-filtration or pressure-control equipment
  • establishing a protected debris path, waste containers, and disposal procedure
  • staging tools and a dedicated cutting station with dust collection, lighting, and weather protection
  • confirming fire extinguisher, first-aid, utility shutoff, communication, and emergency-exit access
  • completing a documented work-area safety and protection inspection and correcting every failed item
  • defining the daily cleanup, waste removal, tool security, and containment-inspection procedure

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin any demolition, cutting, prying, or fastening; this chapter builds and tests the protected work area only
  • disconnect, cap, or isolate any utility beyond the shutdowns already confirmed and documented in the approved Chapter 1 scope
  • route containment airflow through a building's central HVAC system, or direct dust, fumes, or odors into occupied rooms, return-air paths, attached garages, neighboring units, or concealed cavities
  • authorize work to proceed while any protection, airflow, access, electrical, lighting, storage, or public-control item on the safety inspection remains failed
  • relocate an occupant, child, pet, or vulnerable person without the owner's or responsible party's agreement

Any containment, dust-control, or life-safety condition this chapter cannot resolve must remain unauthorized until the correction is verified and the inspection is repeated.

Trade Note

A containment boundary is only as good as its weakest transition. A sealed zipper door with an unsealed gap at the floor or ceiling, or a barrier that stops at a doorway but leaves an adjacent duct register uncovered, will not control dust. Inspect every edge of the boundary, not only the door.

Setup Standard

Do not begin protecting floors and finishes (Step 5 forward) until the Chapter 1 scope, hazardous-material assessment, and occupant relocation plan (Steps 1–2) are reviewed and confirmed in writing. Shop Tip

Build the containment boundary from the occupied side first, sealing every transition before you cut the zipper door opening. It is far easier to seal a continuous sheet of poly to the wall, ceiling, and floor and then cut a controlled entry than to chase gaps around an entry that was framed first.

Negative Pressure vs. Simple Filtration

A negative-pressure setup uses a sealed containment boundary and an exhausting air scrubber or fan to pull air out of the work zone faster than air can leak in, so dust migrates into the work zone rather than out of it. Simple filtration recirculates work-zone air through a filter without controlling the pressure difference across the boundary. Use the negative-pressure method whenever the approved scope includes drywall demolition, sanding, or any other dust-generating work; simple filtration alone does not prevent dust migration through an imperfect boundary.

Measurement Cue — Size the Exhaust Opening to the Equipment

Cut the exhaust opening in the containment boundary to match the diameter of the air scrubber's exhaust duct or the box fan's frame, sealed with tape at every edge. An oversized or unsealed exhaust opening allows unfiltered air to bypass the equipment and defeats the negative-pressure setup.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Block Egress or Life-Safety Access

Do not position containment, staged materials, or waste containers across a required emergency exit, in front of an electrical panel, or over a smoke or carbon-monoxide alarm. Confirm the fire extinguisher, first-aid kit, and emergency exit route are accessible from inside the work zone before any dust-generating work begins in a later chapter.

Field Safety Cue — Cords, Hoses, and Footing in the Debris Path

Route extension cords and vacuum hoses along a wall rather than across the debris path or a doorway threshold. Confirm floor protection lies flat without curling edges or gaps that create a trip hazard, and correct any loose or lifted seam before continuing.

Code Alert — Verify Local Requirements for Occupied-Home Containment

Requirements for negative-pressure containment, HEPA filtration, and hazardous-material work-area controls vary by jurisdiction and by the hazardous-material assessment completed in Chapter 1. Where that assessment identified suspect lead, asbestos, or mold, confirm the qualified professional's containment requirement before proceeding; this chapter's general containment method does not substitute for that requirement.

E. Preparation — Scope Review and Contents/Finish Protection (Steps 1–7)

  1. Review the approved scope, hazardous-material assessment, demolition limits, occupant plan, work hours, utility interruptions, and emergency contacts. Confirm each item against the Chapter 1 readiness decision and record any change directly on the Zone and Access Record before proceeding.
  2. Confirm whether occupants, children, pets, tenants, neighbors, or vulnerable persons require relocation or special protection. Record the owner's decision and the confirmed relocation or protection plan for each identified person or animal.
  3. Walk the path from exterior access to the work area and identify floors, walls, doors, stairs, elevators, landscaping, and common areas requiring protection. Record the full debris-path route on the Zone and Access Record.
  4. Remove authorized contents from the work zone and document items that must remain. List every remaining item and its planned protection method.
  5. Establish clean, transition, work, cutting, material, waste, and restricted zones. Mark each zone boundary using the Reference Conventions defined above.
  6. Install durable floor protection appropriate to the finished floor and expected traffic without creating a trip or moisture trap. Seal every seam with tape and confirm no lifted edge remains along the debris path or work zone.
  7. Protect walls, corners, doors, windows, cabinets, countertops, fixtures, and finished surfaces that remain. Install corner protectors and coverings at every location identified in Step 4.

F. Containment Boundary (Steps 8–9)

  1. Establish the approved containment boundary using compatible barriers, sealed transitions, and closable entries. Build the poly-sheeting barrier from the occupied side, sealing the ceiling, wall, and floor transitions before cutting the entry opening.
  2. Protect or isolate HVAC supplies and returns only through the approved building and indoor-air-quality plan. Cover or seal every supply and return register inside the work zone according to the plan confirmed in Step 1; do not rely on an uncovered register remaining closed on its own.

G. Dust Control and Airflow (Steps 10–12)

  1. Set up local tool dust collection and approved air-filtration or pressure-control equipment where required. Position the air scrubber or fan at the exhaust opening sized and sealed per the Measurement Cue above.
  2. Confirm that airflow does not distribute dust or fumes into occupied rooms, neighboring units, attached garages, or concealed cavities. Test the containment boundary with the equipment running and confirm air is drawn into the work zone at the entry and any gap, rather than pushed outward.
  3. Establish makeup-air, exhaust, temperature, humidity, and weather controls compatible with the products and containment plan. Confirm the makeup-air path does not draw unfiltered outdoor air across a product that requires a controlled temperature or humidity range.

H. Debris Path, Staging, and Life-Safety Access (Steps 13–21)

  1. Create a protected debris path and define container, bagging, loading, and disposal procedures. Confirm the path matches the route recorded in Step 3 and remains free of obstructions.
  2. Locate waste containers so that sharp, heavy, dusty, wet, or regulated debris cannot injure occupants or contaminate clean areas. Position containers within the waste zone, clear of occupant traffic and the transition zone.
  3. Stage tools on stable surfaces with cords, hoses, chargers, batteries, blades, fasteners, and accessories organized by task. Group tools by the task they support and confirm each is on a stable, dry surface.
  4. Establish a separate cutting station with support for long or sheet materials, blade-clearance control, dust collection, lighting, and weather protection. Position the cutting station within the cutting zone, away from the primary work zone and debris path.
  5. Stage materials flat, dry, secure, acclimated where required, and separated from waste or damp surfaces. Confirm the material zone is dry and clear of the debris path.
  6. Locate fire extinguisher, first-aid supplies, utility shutoffs, communication device, lighting, and emergency exit route. Confirm each is accessible from inside the work zone and not obstructed by containment, staged materials, or waste containers.
  7. Provide safe temporary lighting without overloading circuits or placing hot lamps against combustible materials. Confirm the circuit load and lamp placement before energizing temporary lighting.
  8. Establish worker sanitation, handwashing, drinking water, and clean-break procedures appropriate to the hazard assessment. Confirm the procedure matches the hazard level identified in the Chapter 1 risk register.
  9. Install signs, barriers, door controls, and access rules for occupants and visitors. Post signage at every entry to the restricted zone and confirm door controls function as intended.

I. Testing, Inspection, and Daily Procedure (Steps 22–26)

  1. Test containment, dust collection, filtration, lighting, access, and debris movement before demolition begins. Run every system under working conditions and record the result of each test on the work-area safety inspection form.
  2. Complete a documented work-area safety and protection inspection. Walk every zone and confirm each item against the Acceptance Standards table above.
  3. Correct every failed protection, airflow, access, electrical, lighting, storage, or public-control item before authorizing work. Repeat the specific failed test after each correction until it passes.
  4. Define the daily cleanup, waste removal, tool security, utility restoration, and containment inspection procedure. Write the procedure directly on the work-area safety inspection form for use at the end of each future work day.
  5. Photograph the completed setup and record filter, barrier, floor-protection, and access conditions. Store the photographs with the Chapter 1 package for reference by Chapter 3.

Common Inspection Review

Review the completed work area using the same checks regardless of which room was protected.

  • Scope check: confirm the approved scope, hazardous-material assessment, and occupant plan were reviewed and confirmed before protection began.
  • Protection check: confirm floor protection is sealed and flat, and every remaining wall, corner, door, and fixture is protected.
  • Containment check: confirm every transition of the containment boundary is sealed and the entry closes fully.
  • Airflow check: confirm the dust-control equipment draws air into the work zone under test conditions and does not migrate dust into clean zones.
  • Staging and access check: confirm the debris path, waste containers, cutting station, and life-safety items are positioned per the Acceptance Standards table.
  • Inspection check: confirm every item on the work-area safety inspection form is resolved, with corrections retested and passed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Dust Escapes at a Containment Transition Despite a Closed Entry

Probable cause: the ceiling, wall, or floor transition of the barrier was not sealed continuously, or a gap remains at a corner, outlet, or duct penetration.
Confirmation: run the air-scrubber test again and check each transition individually with a hand or a smoke pencil for inward airflow.
Correction: reseal the specific failed transition with tape or additional sheeting and retest.
Reinspection: repeat the airflow check across the entire boundary, not only the corrected transition.

Floor Protection Lifts or Curls Along the Debris Path

Probable cause: seams were not fully taped, or the protection material was not rated for the expected traffic and rolling loads.
Confirmation: walk the debris path and check every seam and edge for lifting, curling, or movement underfoot.
Correction: retape the affected seam or replace the section of protection with a product rated for the actual traffic.
Reinspection: repeat the protection check along the full debris path.

The Air Scrubber or Fan Pushes Air Outward Instead of Drawing It In

Probable cause: the equipment is oriented backward, the exhaust opening is oversized or unsealed, or makeup air is entering faster than the equipment can exhaust.
Confirmation: check the equipment's airflow direction against its manufacturer instructions and inspect the exhaust-opening seal.
Correction: correct the equipment orientation, reseal the exhaust opening to the equipment's exact dimension, or reduce the makeup-air opening.
Reinspection: repeat the airflow test at the entry and at every boundary transition.

A Life-Safety Item Is Blocked by Containment, Materials, or Waste Containers

Probable cause: the fire extinguisher, first-aid kit, utility shutoff, or emergency exit route was not identified before staging materials or building the containment boundary.
Confirmation: walk the work zone and confirm each life-safety item is visible and reachable without moving containment, materials, or waste containers.
Correction: relocate the obstructing material, container, or containment element and confirm clear access.
Reinspection: repeat the life-safety access check as part of the full work-area safety inspection.

The Work Area Was Authorized Despite a Failed Inspection Item

Probable cause: the work-area safety inspection in Step 23 was signed before every failed protection, airflow, access, electrical, lighting, storage, or public-control item was corrected and retested.
Confirmation: compare the inspection form against the Acceptance Standards table to confirm every check has a passing result and a retest record where a correction was required.
Correction: withhold authorization, correct the specific failed item, and repeat only that test.
Reinspection: repeat the full work-area safety inspection before authorizing any demolition work in Chapter 3.

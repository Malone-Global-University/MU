Job Overview

This project qualifies you to perform controlled selective demolition inside an occupied home: removing only the trim, drywall, cabinet, or fixture components that the Chapter 1 approved scope and demolition drawing identify, while protecting reusable materials, hidden utilities, and the surrounding building.

You will verify the demolition drawing, limits, salvage list, hazardous-material clearance, and required utility shutdowns; confirm containment and occupant exclusion; scan for concealed utilities and framing; mark removal boundaries; remove loose components and trim; support and disconnect cabinets or fixtures; create exploratory openings; remove drywall or panel material to controlled lines; separate and containerize debris; and inspect and document the exposed concealed conditions before authorizing repair work.

This chapter builds directly on the existing-condition report, risk register, approved scope, and readiness decision from Chapter 1, and the containment, dust-control, and staging setup from Chapter 2. No demolition may begin until that scope is approved and the work area is protected. Every later repair chapter in this book — trim, drywall, subfloor, flooring, doors, casing, paint, fixtures, and the Chapter 20 capstone — begins from the concealed conditions this chapter exposes and documents.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • RR-013 — Remove approved interior trim using controlled prying and fastener-release methods that preserve reusable material.
  • RR-014 — Remove approved drywall or panel sections to controlled cut lines without damaging concealed systems.
  • RR-015 — Remove approved cabinets or fixtures only after verifying and disconnecting all attached utilities.
  • RR-016 — Protect hidden electrical, plumbing, gas, HVAC, and structural systems throughout the demolition sequence.
  • RR-017 — Separate salvage, recyclable, ordinary, sharp, and regulated debris and containerize it for safe removal.
  • RR-018 — Inspect and document exposed concealed conditions and authorize repair work only after they are accepted.

Where This Skill Fits

Selective demolition is the point in every repair or remodeling project where finishes stop hiding the building's real condition. Every chapter that follows — trim replacement, drywall repair, subfloor repair, flooring replacement, door and casing work, painting, fixture replacement, wall openings, and water-damage repair — depends on the concealed conditions this chapter exposes, photographs, measures, and either accepts or refers.

This chapter uses the demolition drawing, salvage list, and hazardous-material clearance produced from the Chapter 1 approved scope, and the containment, dust control, and staging established in Chapter 2. A repair authorized from this chapter's concealed-condition inspection is the starting point for Chapters 4 through 18, and the capstone in Chapter 20 repeats this exact selective-demolition discipline for its selected room.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the demolition drawing, removal limits, salvage list, hazardous-material clearance, and utility plan before any tool touches the building
  • verifying required plumbing, electrical, gas, HVAC, alarm, or appliance shutdowns and documenting who performed them
  • scanning and evaluating the work area for concealed utilities and framing using approved locating methods
  • marking removal boundaries, cut lines, fastener locations, salvage items, protected items, and stop-work limits
  • removing loose components, trim, and approved cabinet or fixture assemblies using controlled, non-destructive methods
  • creating small exploratory openings before enlarging drywall or panel removal
  • cutting and removing drywall or panel material to controlled lines and depths
  • separating salvage, clean recyclable material, ordinary debris, sharp metal, and regulated waste
  • inspecting, photographing, and measuring exposed concealed conditions before any repair or closure
  • comparing actual conditions to the approved scope and issuing a change notice for every material difference

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin demolition before the Chapter 1 readiness decision is approved, the Chapter 2 work-area protection is tested, and required utility shutdowns are confirmed
  • demolish, cut, or disturb any load-bearing, braced, shear, fire-rated, or structural element beyond the approved demolition drawing
  • disturb, sand, or disrupt any suspect lead-containing, asbestos-containing, mold, or other hazardous material without the required assessment and controls already in place
  • continue demolition past an unverified wire, pipe, duct, structural member, wet material, mold, pest, hazardous material, fire damage, or unexpected movement
  • close, patch, or authorize repair over any exposed concealed condition that has not been accepted through this chapter's inspection

Any concealed condition this chapter exposes that exceeds the Chapter 1 risk register or approved scope must be documented as a change notice and referred before repair work continues.

Trade Note

Selective demolition is controlled exposure, not general destruction. Every cut, pry, and removal in this chapter targets a specific approved component. Treat every unidentified wire, pipe, duct, cable, valve, device, or line as active until it has been verified otherwise by an approved method or a qualified person.

Setup Standard

Do not begin any prying, cutting, or fastener removal (Step 5 forward) until containment, floor protection, debris path, lighting, fire protection, and occupant exclusion from Chapter 2 are verified (Step 2), and required utility shutdowns are confirmed and documented (Step 3). Demolition performed before these confirmations must stop and be revalidated before continuing.

Shop Tip

Create the first exploratory opening at the smallest size that still answers the concealed-condition question. A small opening that reveals a route or hazard can be repaired far more easily than a full-panel removal made before the concealed condition was known.

Common Field Fundamentals

Selective demolition depends on the same discipline every chapter in this book applies to concealed conditions: expose only what the approved scope authorizes, verify before you cut, and stop the moment the actual condition differs from what was assumed. This chapter is where that discipline is applied with tools in hand for the first time.

Progressive Release vs. Forced Removal

Progressive release means inserting a thin prying tool at a joint, applying light and even pressure, and moving the release point along the length of the component rather than forcing a single point past its resistance. Forced removal at a single point crushes adjacent drywall, splits trim, and damages salvage items. Every trim and panel removal step in this chapter specifies progressive release; do not substitute a single forceful pull for the specified sequence.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop at Every Unverified Condition

Stop work immediately upon encountering any unverified wire, pipe, duct, structural member, wet material, suspect mold, pests, suspect hazardous material, fire damage, or unexpected movement. Do not continue cutting or prying past the point of discovery. Photograph the condition, mark the stop-work limit, and follow the referral procedure identified in the Chapter 1 risk register before resuming work in that area.

Safety Cue — Control Sharp Projections Immediately

Pull or cut every exposed fastener and remove sharp projections as soon as they are created, not at the end of the work session. An exposed nail, screw, or metal edge left in an active work area is a puncture and laceration hazard to every person who enters afterward.

Field Safety Cue — Support Before You Release

Support cabinets, countertops, mirrors, sinks, fixtures, and panels before releasing their final fasteners. A component that is fully supported by hand, a temporary brace, or a second worker before the last screw is removed cannot fall, shift, or strike a worker or a protected surface.

Code Alert — Utility Disconnection Requires Verification

Confirming that a shutoff, breaker, or valve has been operated is not the same as confirming that the utility is de-energized, depressurized, or isolated. Test that flow, voltage, or pressure has actually stopped using an approved method before disconnecting any plumbing, electrical, or gas component, and document who performed the shutdown.

E. Preparation — Verification and Utility Control (Steps 1–4)

  1. Review the demolition drawing, limits, salvage list, hazardous-material clearance, utility plan, structural restrictions, and required inspections. Confirm the drawing and Chapter 1 approved scope agree on every component to be removed, and record the drawing reference on the Demolition Boundary and Utility Record.
  2. Verify containment, floor protection, debris path, lighting, fire protection, and occupant exclusion. Walk the Chapter 2 protected area and confirm every barrier, floor covering, and access control is in place and undamaged before proceeding.
  3. Confirm required plumbing, electrical, gas, HVAC, alarm, or appliance shutdowns and document who performed them. Test that flow, voltage, or pressure has actually stopped using an approved method, and record the name of the person who performed each shutdown.
  4. Scan, inspect, and otherwise evaluate the work area for concealed utilities and framing using approved methods. Use the stud, utility, or voltage locator across the full removal boundary and record every detected route on the Removal and Boundary Log as a suspected condition until confirmed by exploratory opening.

F. Marking, Loose-Component Removal, and Trim (Steps 5–10)

  1. Mark removal boundaries, cut lines, fastener locations, salvage items, protected items, and stop-work limits. Use painter's tape and a marker to label every boundary and item directly on the surface before any tool is used.
  2. Remove doors, drawers, shelves, hardware, accessories, and other loose components that obstruct controlled demolition. Set each item aside on a protected surface without forcing hinges or fasteners.
  3. Label and store reusable hardware and components in protected containers. Match each item to its Salvage Item entry and store it where it cannot be lost, damaged, or mixed with debris.
  4. Score paint, caulk, sealant, paper facing, and finish transitions at the removal boundary to reduce unintended damage. Use a utility knife along the marked boundary before any prying begins.
  5. Remove approved trim using thin prying tools, backing blocks, progressive release, and nail control. Work from one end of the piece to the other, inserting the pry bar behind a backing block at each release point.
  6. Pull or cut exposed fasteners and remove sharp projections immediately. Do not leave a fastener or sharp edge exposed in the active work area between steps.

G. Cabinet or Fixture Removal and Controlled Panel Demolition (Steps 11–26)

  1. Support cabinets, countertops, mirrors, sinks, fixtures, panels, and heavy components before releasing fasteners. Position hand support, a temporary brace, or a second worker under or against the component before the final fastener is removed.
  2. Disconnect and remove cabinets or fixtures only after all utilities and hidden attachments are verified. Confirm every supply, drain, gas, or electrical connection is disconnected and capped or isolated using the approved procedure before lifting the component.
  3. Use team lifting or mechanical assistance for heavy, fragile, or awkward components. Do not lift a countertop, cabinet, or fixture alone if its weight, size, or fragility requires a second worker or a lifting device.
  4. Create small exploratory openings at approved locations before enlarging drywall or panel removal. Cut the smallest opening that answers the concealed-condition question at that location before proceeding to a larger removal.
  5. Stop work immediately for unverified wires, pipes, ducts, structural members, wet materials, mold, pests, hazardous material, fire damage, or unexpected movement. Mark the stop-work limit, photograph the condition, and do not proceed in that area until it is referred and resolved.
  6. Cut drywall or panel material to controlled lines without plunging blades beyond the known safe depth. Set the blade depth to the confirmed board thickness plus a small margin before cutting.
  7. Remove material in manageable sections while protecting framing, insulation, utilities, and adjacent finishes. Work in sections no larger than can be safely lifted and controlled by the worker performing the removal.
  8. Preserve required fire blocking, draft stopping, rated layers, backing, blocking, and structural sheathing unless the approved scope includes their replacement. Confirm each of these components against the demolition drawing before removing any material near it.
  9. Separate salvage, clean recyclable material, ordinary debris, sharp metal, regulated waste, and contaminated material. Sort each removed item into its designated container as it is removed, rather than sorting a mixed pile afterward.
  10. Bag or containerize dusty debris before moving it through clean or occupied areas. Seal each bag or container at the point of removal before it enters the debris path established in Chapter 2.
  11. Vacuum framing, cavities, and work surfaces using the approved equipment rather than dry sweeping hazardous dust. Use the HEPA-filtered shop vacuum on every exposed cavity and surface before inspection.
  12. Inspect exposed framing, subfloor, wall backing, insulation, utilities, fasteners, moisture, corrosion, decay, and previous alterations. Record each observed condition on the Concealed-Condition Report with its location.
  13. Photograph and measure concealed conditions before any repair or closure. Photograph from a fixed reference point and record dimensions for every condition entered in Step 22.
  14. Compare actual conditions with the approved scope and issue a change notice for every material difference. Complete a Change Notice Log entry for each condition that was not anticipated in the Chapter 1 risk register or approved scope.
  15. Stabilize open assemblies, cap or guard openings, and maintain utility safety until follow-on work begins. Install temporary covers or guards over any exposed opening or utility before leaving the area unattended.
  16. Complete the selective-demolition inspection and authorize repair work only after concealed conditions are accepted. Review every entry in the Concealed-Condition Report and Change Notice Log and confirm each is resolved, scheduled, or explicitly deferred before signing the authorization.

Common Inspection Review

Review every completed record using the same checks regardless of which components were removed.

  • Verification check: confirm the demolition drawing, salvage list, hazardous-material clearance, and utility shutdowns were verified and documented before any prying or cutting began.
  • Boundary check: confirm every removal stayed within the marked removal boundary, with a change notice for any expansion.
  • Utility protection check: confirm no wiring, plumbing, duct, or structural member outside the approved demolition drawing was cut, damaged, or disturbed.
  • Salvage check: confirm every salvage-list item is labeled, undamaged, and stored in a protected container.
  • Debris check: confirm salvage, recyclable material, ordinary debris, sharp metal, and regulated waste were separated and containerized.
  • Concealed-condition check: confirm every exposed concealed condition is photographed, measured, recorded, and either accepted or entered as a change notice.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Drywall or Panel Cracked Beyond the Marked Removal Boundary

Probable cause: the finish was not scored along the boundary before prying began, or blade depth exceeded the confirmed board thickness.
Confirmation: inspect the damaged edge and compare it against the marked boundary and the blade-depth setting used.
Correction: extend the removal boundary only as far as the actual damage requires, document the expansion as a change notice, and score the new boundary before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check on the revised removal area.

A Concealed Wire, Pipe, or Duct Was Struck During Cutting

Probable cause: the locating scan in Step 4 did not detect the route, or cutting proceeded past the confirmed safe depth without a fresh exploratory opening.
Confirmation: stop work immediately, inspect the struck utility without touching it if electrical, and confirm whether it is still active.
Correction: follow the stop-work procedure, contact the qualified trade required for the affected utility, and do not resume demolition in that area until the utility is repaired or confirmed safe.
Reinspection: repeat the utility-protection check across the full removal boundary before continuing.

A Salvage Item Was Damaged or Mixed Into Debris

Probable cause: the item was not labeled and set aside immediately after removal, or debris sorting was performed on a mixed pile rather than at the point of removal.
Confirmation: compare the salvage list against the labeled containers and identify the missing or damaged item.
Correction: document the loss or damage, identify whether a replacement or substitute is required, and adjust the repair scope accordingly.
Reinspection: repeat the salvage check for every remaining removal step.

A Concealed Condition Was Exposed but Not Documented

Probable cause: the photograph or measurement in Step 23 was skipped, or the condition was mentally noted rather than recorded on the Concealed-Condition Report.
Confirmation: compare the physical work area against the completed Concealed-Condition Report and Change Notice Log for missing entries.
Correction: photograph and measure the condition before any further work covers it, and add the entry before authorizing repair.
Reinspection: repeat the concealed-condition check on the full report.

Repair Work Was Authorized With an Open Change Notice

Probable cause: the Step 26 authorization was completed before every change notice from Step 24 was resolved, scheduled, or explicitly deferred with a stated condition.
Confirmation: compare the Change Notice Log against the authorization signature to confirm every item has a resolution.
Correction: withdraw the authorization, resolve or schedule the outstanding change notice, and reissue the authorization once complete.
Reinspection: repeat the concealed-condition and change-notice checks before reauthorizing repair work.

Cleanup

This project ends with a stabilized, documented, and contained work area ready for repair — not with an open or unprotected opening.

  • Confirm every exposed opening is temporarily covered or guarded and every disturbed utility remains capped, isolated, or otherwise safe
  • Remove all protruding fasteners, sharp debris, and trip hazards from the work area before leaving
  • Seal and remove all bagged or containerized debris through the approved debris path
  • Store labeled salvage containers where they will not be damaged or lost before the repair chapter begins
  • File the Demolition Verification Record, Removal and Boundary Log, Concealed-Condition Report, and Change Notice Log together where they will be available to the learner performing the next repair chapter

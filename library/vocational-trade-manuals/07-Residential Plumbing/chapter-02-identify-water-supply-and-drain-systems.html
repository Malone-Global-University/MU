Job Overview

This project qualifies you to trace and document the visible residential water-supply and drain-waste-vent (DWV) systems serving a selected bathroom, kitchen, or utility area. The finished work product is one completed System-Layout Record: a sketch showing every traced fixture-to-branch relationship, a table of accessible pipe markings, sizes, materials, and valve types, a list of unresolved or concealed piping, and a proceed or stop-work decision for any project that depends on the mapped system.

You will locate the building water entry, main shutoff, and water heater; identify hot and cold distribution piping by material, size, and direction; trace fixture supply branches without opening concealed walls; identify drain, waste, and vent piping by material, orientation, and termination; distinguish sanitary drainage from other piping systems; and record every observation, discrepancy, and unresolved condition on the System-Layout Worksheet.

This project builds directly on Project 01. The work area you isolated, drained, and protected there is the same area you will now map. Every later chapter — PEX and copper installation, DWV assembly, fixture replacement, appliance connections, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on knowing exactly which pipe is which before it is cut, joined, or disturbed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-007 — Identify hot and cold water-supply lines by material, size marking, direction, and connected fixture.
  • PL-008 — Identify visible drain, waste, and vent (DWV) piping by material, fitting orientation, and direction of flow.
  • PL-009 — Trace fixture supply branches from the fixture back to the identifiable source without opening concealed walls.
  • PL-010 — Identify vent piping and distinguish it from supply and drain piping by location, orientation, and termination.
  • PL-011 — Identify shutoffs, cleanouts, and valve types serving the mapped area.
  • PL-012 — Document the system layout in a verified, labeled record distinguishing verified, inferred, concealed, and unresolved piping.

Where This Skill Fits

Every later project in this book depends on correctly identifying which piping is supply, which is drain, and which is vent before any work begins. Chapters 4 through 7 install and replace supply piping; Chapters 8 and 9 install DWV piping and traps; Chapters 10 through 18 connect fixtures and appliances to existing systems. A misidentified pipe — mistaking a vent for a drain, or a cold branch for a hot branch — is a direct path to a cross-connection, a failed test, or a disturbed system that was never meant to be opened.

The system-mapping discipline built here — recording verified, inferred, concealed, and unresolved piping separately, rather than guessing at a single answer — is the same documentation standard used for concealed-work records throughout the book, including the rough-in inspection record required in the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • locating the building water entry, meter where accessible, main shutoff, pressure-control equipment, treatment equipment, and water heater
  • identifying visible hot- and cold-water distribution piping by material, size marking, direction, and connected fixtures
  • locating fixture stops, branch valves, appliance valves, hose connections, and drain-down points
  • tracing each selected fixture supply branch using only accessible, non-destructive observation
  • identifying visible drain, waste, and vent piping by material, fitting orientation, direction of flow, cleanouts, traps, and vent termination
  • distinguishing sanitary drainage from storm drainage, condensate, sump, irrigation, fuel-gas, hydronic, and other systems
  • recording a verified system-layout record identifying verified, inferred, concealed, and unresolved portions

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • open a concealed wall, ceiling, or floor cavity to trace piping — trace only what is visible and accessible without demolition
  • disconnect, cut, join, or replace any pipe, fitting, fixture, or appliance connection — that work is covered in later chapters
  • assign a guessed function to any unidentified or conflicting piping — mark it unresolved instead
  • proceed with any project that depends on the mapped system when the map contains an unresolved condition that affects that project's scope

Any piping condition that cannot be confidently identified using approved non-destructive methods must be marked unresolved and escalated to a qualified professional before a dependent project proceeds.

Trade Note

A system-layout record is only as reliable as the distinction it draws between what was actually observed and what was assumed. Marking a section "inferred" rather than "verified" when the route could not be directly traced is not a weakness in the record — it is the information a later chapter author or installer needs to avoid trusting a guess as if it were a confirmed fact.

Setup Standard

Do not assign a function to any pipe, fitting, or valve based on assumption, appearance alone, or similarity to a nearby identified pipe. Confirm uncertain piping using approved non-destructive observation methods, and mark any piping that cannot be confirmed as unresolved rather than guessing.

Shop Tip

Sketch each fixture-to-branch relationship in the field, at the point of observation, rather than trying to reconstruct the route from memory later. A route that seems obvious while standing in front of the fixture is easy to misremember once several fixtures have been traced.

Verified, Inferred, Concealed, and Unresolved Piping

Every pipe recorded in this project's sketch and worksheet must carry one of four confidence markings: verified, inferred, concealed, or unresolved. A pipe is verified only when its material, size, direction, and connected fixture have been directly observed. A pipe is inferred when its likely route is judged from visible entry and exit points but was not directly observed along its full accessible length. Concealed piping is piping enclosed by a finish that cannot be observed without demolition. Unresolved piping is piping whose function cannot be confidently identified using approved non-destructive methods.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Uncertain Piping Non-Destructively

Confirm uncertain piping using approved non-destructive observation methods only, such as tracing an accessible run to its termination, comparing pipe markings, or observing flow response at a fixture. Do not open a concealed wall, ceiling, or floor cavity to confirm a route in this project.

Critical Safety Cue — Unidentified or Conflicting Piping

Treat any piping that cannot be confidently identified, or that conflicts with available drawings, as unresolved rather than assigning it a guessed function. Do not test, tap into, or disturb unidentified piping to determine its function. A pipe assumed to be a drain that is actually a pressurized or gas-carrying line is a serious hazard.

Field Safety Cue — Access and Housekeeping During Tracing

Use adequate lighting when tracing piping in cabinets, crawlspace edges, and attics accessible from the controlled area. Do not enter a confined space, crawlspace, or excavation to trace piping without the controls and qualified supervision required by the book-level rules. Keep cabinet contents, access panels, and cleanout covers organized and returned to place after inspection.

Code Alert — System Identification Varies by Jurisdiction and Installation

Pipe color coding, size marking conventions, and vent-termination requirements vary by material, manufacturer, and jurisdiction. Verify local markings and adopted code references rather than assuming a marking convention observed in one location applies everywhere in the controlled area.

E. Preparation and Prior Records Review

  1. Obtain available plumbing drawings, fixture schedules, inspection records, and owner information. Note any information that identifies pipe routes or flags a prior issue in the controlled area.
  2. Select the controlled area and define which fixtures, appliances, and visible piping will be mapped. Record this scope in writing before tracing begins.

F. Entry-Point and Supply Identification

  1. Locate the building water entry, meter where accessible, main shutoff, pressure-control equipment, treatment equipment, and water heater. Photograph each control point.
  2. Identify visible cold-water distribution piping by material, size marking, direction, and connected fixtures. Record each observation on the worksheet.
  3. Identify visible hot-water distribution piping from the water heater to served fixtures. Record the route and connected fixtures.
  4. Locate fixture stops, branch valves, appliance valves, hose connections, and drain-down points. Mark each on the field sketch.

G. Branch Tracing

  1. Trace each selected fixture supply branch without opening concealed walls or assuming the route through inaccessible spaces. Mark any portion that cannot be directly observed as inferred or concealed rather than assumed verified.

H. DWV Identification and System Separation

  1. Identify visible drain, waste, and vent piping by material, fitting orientation, direction of flow, cleanouts, traps, and roof or wall vent termination. Record each observation on the worksheet.
  2. Distinguish sanitary drainage from storm drainage, condensate, sump, irrigation, fuel-gas, hydronic, and other systems. Note the basis for each distinction, such as pipe material, slope direction, or connection point.
  3. Confirm uncertain piping using non-destructive observations and approved test methods. Do not disturb or tap into piping to confirm its function.
  4. Mark unidentified or conflicting piping as unresolved rather than assigning a guessed function. Record what prevents confirmation for each unresolved item.

I. Recording, Sketching, and Labeling

  1. Record accessible pipe markings, nominal sizes, material types, valve types, and fitting transitions. Complete the pipe schedule on the worksheet.
  2. Sketch the fixture-to-branch relationships using distinct supply, drain, and vent symbols. Use a consistent symbol key across the entire sketch.
  3. Label shutoffs and cleanouts using durable, non-damaging identification. Confirm each label is legible and correctly placed.
  4. Note corrosion, unsupported piping, improper transitions, leakage, cross-connection concerns, inaccessible valves, or missing cleanouts. Record each condition with its location.
  5. Photograph representative piping routes and control points. Confirm photographs correspond to worksheet entries.

J. Comparison and Final Documentation

  1. Compare the field map with available drawings and note discrepancies. Record each discrepancy and its likely cause.
  2. Prepare a final system-layout record identifying verified, inferred, concealed, and unresolved portions. Confirm every recorded pipe carries exactly one confidence marking.
  3. Issue a proceed or stop-work decision for projects that depend on the mapped system. State the decision in writing and identify any unresolved condition that must be corrected or escalated first.

Common Documentation Inspection

Inspect every completed system-layout record using the same checks regardless of which controlled area the project involves.

  • Entry-point check: confirm the building water entry, main shutoff, treatment equipment, and water heater are correctly located and recorded.
  • Supply check: confirm every accessible hot and cold run is recorded with material, size, direction, and connected fixture.
  • Branch-tracing check: confirm every selected fixture branch was traced using only non-destructive observation.
  • DWV check: confirm every accessible drain, waste, and vent run is recorded with material, orientation, flow direction, and termination.
  • Separation check: confirm sanitary drainage is not confused with storm, condensate, sump, irrigation, gas, or hydronic piping.
  • Confidence-marking check: confirm every recorded pipe carries a verified, inferred, concealed, or unresolved marking, with no guessed function assigned.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Traced Branch Disappears Into a Concealed Space

Probable cause: the branch route continues behind a wall, floor, or ceiling finish beyond the accessible portion.
Confirmation: confirm the point where the pipe becomes inaccessible and verify no additional access point, such as a panel or cabinet back, is available nearby.
Correction: mark the inaccessible portion as concealed on the sketch and worksheet; do not extend the traced line as verified or inferred beyond the last observed point.
Reinspection: confirm the sketch correctly shows the transition from verified or inferred to concealed at the correct location.

Two Pipes Have Similar Markings but Serve Different Functions

Probable cause: similar pipe material or color was used for more than one system, such as a supply line and a hydronic line, in the same accessible area.
Confirmation: trace each pipe independently to its connected fixture or equipment rather than relying on the marking alone.
Correction: record the confirmed function of each pipe separately on the worksheet; mark any pipe that cannot be confirmed as unresolved.
Reinspection: re-check the sketch to confirm no two piping systems are shown as a single traced line.

The Field Map Conflicts With an Available Drawing

Probable cause: the drawing reflects an earlier design that was changed during construction, or the field observation is incomplete.
Confirmation: re-observe the conflicting section directly and compare the specific discrepancy against the drawing.
Correction: record the discrepancy and the confirmed field condition; treat the direct field observation as controlling for any dependent project, and note the conflict for the qualified professional's review.
Reinspection: confirm the final record clearly documents both the drawing and the observed condition where they differ.

A Pipe's Function Cannot Be Confidently Identified

Probable cause: the pipe lacks legible markings, does not match any expected system, or its connections are not accessible for observation.
Confirmation: attempt non-destructive confirmation methods only, such as tracing to a termination or comparing against available drawings.
Correction: mark the pipe as unresolved rather than assigning a guessed function; do not test, tap into, or disturb the pipe to determine its function.
Reinspection: confirm the unresolved item is recorded with a clear description of what prevented identification and an assigned escalation.

A Shutoff or Cleanout Cannot Be Located

Probable cause: the control point is concealed by finish materials, obstructed by stored items, or was never installed at an accessible location.
Confirmation: search adjacent accessible areas and compare against available drawings for the expected location.
Correction: record the control point as inaccessible or concealed on the worksheet; note the condition as an item requiring correction before a dependent project that assumes access to that control point proceeds.
Reinspection: confirm the record clearly flags the missing or inaccessible control point for the proceed/stop-work decision.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build and test two independent water-supply specimens: one assembled with an approved push-fit fitting and one assembled with an approved compression fitting. Push-fit and compression connections are separate joining methods with separate pipe-preparation, insertion or seating, and torque procedures. They are not interchangeable, and a technique that works for one method will not produce a reliable joint with the other.

You will prepare pipe for each method, mark the required insertion or engagement reference, assemble each fitting according to its own procedure, support and align each connection, pressurize both specimens with an approved water test, and correct any leak using the method-specific correction procedure rather than a generic fix.

This project builds directly on the pipe-cutting and deburring skills from Chapter 3 and the shutoff and isolation skills from Chapter 1. It stands alongside the PEX crimp/clamp system in Chapter 4 and the soldered copper system in Chapter 5 as one of the approved water-supply joining methods used throughout the rest of the book, including the fixture-stop replacement in Chapter 7 and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-031 — Prepare pipe ends for push-fit and compression connections without damaging the sealing surface.
  • PL-032 — Mark the manufacturer-required insertion depth for a push-fit connection.
  • PL-033 — Install a push-fit fitting correctly, including insertion, engagement confirmation, and support.
  • PL-034 — Install a compression fitting correctly, including ferrule orientation, backup-wrench technique, and controlled tightening.
  • PL-035 — Check alignment and support on both assemblies so neither connection carries bending load.
  • PL-036 — Test both assemblies for leaks under static pressure and controlled operation, and correct any failure using the method-specific procedure.

Where This Skill Fits

Push-fit and compression fittings appear throughout residential plumbing wherever a fast, mechanically sealed connection is preferred over soldering or a permanent crimp — at fixture stops in Chapter 7, at appliance and hose-bibb connections in Chapters 14 through 16, and as a correction method when replacing a failed joint diagnosed in Chapter 19. Learning both methods as distinct, non-interchangeable procedures here means you will select and execute the correct one later without guessing.

The pipe-preparation discipline from Chapter 3 — square cuts, clean deburred ends, and no scratches on the sealing surface — is directly responsible for whether either fitting in this chapter seals on the first attempt.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • preparing pipe ends for one push-fit specimen and one compression specimen
  • marking and confirming the manufacturer-required push-fit insertion depth
  • assembling and supporting a push-fit connection
  • assembling and supporting a compression connection using correct ferrule orientation and backup-wrench technique
  • testing both assemblies under an approved water-pressure test
  • diagnosing and correcting a leak at either fitting using the method specific to that connection

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • treat push-fit and compression methods as interchangeable, or mix their preparation, insertion, or tightening procedures
  • use thread sealant on a compression sealing surface unless the manufacturer's instructions explicitly require it
  • overtighten a compression nut or force a push-fit fitting past its designed engagement to correct a leak
  • conceal either connection before the approved water-pressure test has passed

Any pipe or fitting condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected using the method-specific procedure or escalated to a qualified professional before work continues.

Trade Note

A push-fit fitting seals on an internal O-ring and grips the pipe with an internal collet; a compression fitting seals by compressing a ferrule against the pipe as the nut is tightened. Because the sealing mechanisms are different, the pipe-preparation requirement, the insertion or seating action, and the correction procedure for a leak are different for each method. This chapter documents the two methods separately for that reason.

Setup Standard

Do not assemble either fitting until the work area is isolated, drained, and protected using the Chapter 1 sequence, the specific push-fit or compression fitting has been confirmed compatible with the specimen pipe material and size, and the pipe end has been cut, deburred, and inspected free of scratches, paint, corrosion, and contamination.

Shop Tip

Mark the push-fit insertion depth on the pipe before you push the fitting on, not after. A depth mark added after insertion is unreliable, because you cannot confirm how far the fitting actually traveled onto the pipe.

Common Field Fundamentals

Push-fit and compression fittings both create a mechanical, non-soldered, non-crimped seal, but they achieve it through different means, and each has its own failure pattern. Learning the two methods side by side, without blending their procedures, is what allows you to select and execute the correct one under real field conditions in later chapters.

Method Separation and Sealing-Surface Protection

Do not treat push-fit and compression fittings as interchangeable or attempt to combine their procedures in one assessed connection. Each method receives its own pipe preparation, insertion or seating procedure, and testing record. Protect the sealing surface of both specimens — the outer pipe wall the O-ring or ferrule will contact — from scratches, paint, corrosion, and contamination throughout cutting, deburring, and assembly.

Measurement Cue — Mark Insertion Depth Before Assembly

Use the manufacturer's depth-marking gauge or a measured reference to mark the full push-fit insertion depth on the pipe before inserting it into the fitting. After insertion, confirm the depth mark is flush with the fitting body. A depth mark that has not reached the fitting body indicates incomplete insertion, not a passed connection.

Critical Safety Cue — Pressurized Water and Uncontrolled Release

Confirm the work area is isolated and drained per Chapter 1 before assembling either specimen, and do not pressurize a specimen until both connections have been visually inspected for insertion, alignment, and support. An uninspected connection can release pressurized water suddenly during the test.

Field Safety Cue — Protect the Sealing Surface During Prep

Handle prepared pipe ends by the body of the pipe, not by dragging them across a bench edge or tool tray. A single scratch on the sealing surface, invisible without close inspection, is enough to prevent either fitting from sealing.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Torque and Tightening Limits

Tighten the compression nut according to the fitting manufacturer's controlled procedure — typically hand-tight plus a specified additional turn, or a stated torque value. Do not tighten by feel alone or continue tightening past the specified point to stop a leak; overtightening deforms the ferrule and can crack the fitting body or pipe.

F. Work-Area Preparation and Pipe Preparation

  1. Review the approved pipe materials, fitting sizes, manufacturer limits, insertion depths, support requirements, reuse restrictions, and test method. Confirm both the push-fit and compression fittings are compatible with their specimen pipe before cutting.
  2. Separate the project into Method A—push-fit and Method B—compression; do not treat the methods as interchangeable. Record each method boundary on the worksheet before proceeding.
  3. Isolate, drain, and clean the controlled work area. Apply the Chapter 1 shutoff and protection sequence to the area where testing will occur.
  4. Inspect pipe for roundness, scratches, paint, corrosion, burrs, contamination, and incompatible dimensions. Reject any specimen pipe that fails this inspection before cutting.

G. Method A — Push-Fit Preparation and Assembly

  1. Cut the push-fit specimen square and deburr or chamfer it as required without scratching the sealing surface. Confirm the cut end is round and free of burrs before marking.
  2. Use the manufacturer's depth gauge to mark full insertion depth. Mark the line clearly before inserting the pipe into the fitting.
  3. Install any required tube liner or stiffener in the correct orientation. Confirm the liner seats fully before insertion where the pipe material requires one.
  4. Push the fitting straight onto the pipe until the depth mark reaches the fitting body. Apply steady, even pressure without rocking the fitting side to side.
  5. Pull back lightly to confirm engagement without partially removing the fitting. Confirm the collet has gripped the pipe and the depth mark has not moved.
  6. Support and align the push-fit assembly so the connection is not carrying bending load. Confirm the pipe enters the fitting square before proceeding to the next specimen.

H. Method B — Compression Preparation and Assembly

  1. For the compression specimen, cut and prepare the compatible rigid tube. Confirm the cut end is square and free of burrs before proceeding.
  2. Slide the compression nut and ferrule onto the tube in the required orientation. Confirm the ferrule's tapered or beveled side faces the correct direction per the manufacturer's instructions.
  3. Insert the tube fully into the valve or fitting body. Confirm the tube is seated against the internal stop before tightening begins.
  4. Bring the ferrule and nut into position without cross-threading. Start the nut by hand and confirm smooth thread engagement before using a wrench.
  5. Hold the fitting body with a backup wrench and tighten the nut according to the manufacturer's controlled procedure. Do not tighten by feel alone; follow the specified turn count or torque value.
  6. Do not use thread sealant on compression sealing surfaces unless the manufacturer explicitly requires it. Confirm the manufacturer's instructions before applying any sealant.

I. Combined Inspection and Pressure Testing

  1. Check both assemblies for insertion, alignment, support, and visible damage. Complete this inspection before either specimen is pressurized.
  2. Fill and pressurize the specimens using the approved water test. Apply the test pressure gradually and observe both connections throughout.
  3. Inspect under static pressure and during controlled operation. Use dry tissue at each connection to confirm the absence of moisture.

J. Correction and Retest

  1. If a push-fit connection leaks, depressurize and follow the manufacturer's removal, inspection, recut, and replacement procedure. Do not attempt to reseat a removed push-fit fitting on the same unmodified pipe end without following the manufacturer's reuse instructions.
  2. If a compression fitting leaks, depressurize, confirm alignment, and make only the permitted incremental adjustment. Do not exceed the manufacturer's specified additional tightening beyond the original controlled procedure.
  3. Replace damaged ferrules, nuts, pipe, or fitting bodies rather than overtightening. Confirm the replacement component matches the original specification before reassembly.
  4. Retest and document each method separately. Record the final pass result for Method A and Method B independently on the worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect both completed specimens using the checks appropriate to each method before recording a pass result.

  • Push-fit depth check: confirm the insertion depth mark is flush with the fitting body and light pull-back shows full engagement.
  • Push-fit alignment check: confirm the connection is supported and carries no bending load.
  • Compression tightening check: confirm the nut was tightened to the manufacturer's specified point using a backup wrench.
  • Compression alignment check: confirm the tube enters the fitting body straight, without side load on the ferrule.
  • Sealing-surface check: confirm both specimens are free of scratches, paint, corrosion, and contamination at the point of seal.
  • Pressure-test check: confirm both specimens held the approved test pressure for the required duration with no visible leakage or dry-tissue moisture.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Push-Fit Connection Leaks Under Pressure

Probable cause: incomplete insertion, a damaged O-ring, a scratched sealing surface, or an out-of-round pipe end.
Confirmation: depressurize and inspect the depth mark against the fitting body; inspect the pipe end for roundness and surface damage.
Correction: follow the manufacturer's removal procedure, recut and deburr the pipe end if damaged, and reassemble with a new fitting if the O-ring is suspect.
Reinspection: repeat the insertion depth check and pressure test.

Compression Fitting Leaks Under Pressure

Probable cause: insufficient tightening, ferrule misalignment, a scratched or out-of-round tube, or cross-threading.
Confirmation: depressurize and inspect the nut for full thread engagement, the ferrule for even compression, and the tube for surface damage.
Correction: make only the permitted incremental tightening; if the ferrule or tube is damaged, replace the affected components rather than continuing to tighten.
Reinspection: repeat the alignment check and pressure test.

Push-Fit Fitting Was Reused on the Same Pipe End

Probable cause: a fitting removed for inspection or correction was reinserted onto the same, unmodified pipe end without following the manufacturer's reuse procedure.
Confirmation: compare the removal and reinsertion steps taken against the manufacturer's stated reuse instructions.
Correction: recut the pipe end to remove the collet-marked section, or replace the fitting, per the manufacturer's instructions.
Reinspection: repeat the insertion depth check and pressure test on the corrected specimen.

Either Connection Shows Movement or Bending Load

Probable cause: the specimen was not supported before pressurizing, allowing the pipe or tube to flex at the fitting.
Confirmation: inspect the support at both ends of the specimen and observe the connection during controlled operation.
Correction: add support so the connection carries no bending load, and depressurize before making any adjustment.
Reinspection: repeat the alignment and support check before retesting.

Compression Nut Was Overtightened to Stop a Leak

Probable cause: tightening continued past the manufacturer's specified point in an attempt to resolve a leak by feel.
Confirmation: inspect the ferrule for deformation and the fitting body or tube for cracking.
Correction: replace the deformed ferrule and any damaged component; reassemble following the manufacturer's specified tightening procedure exactly.
Reinspection: repeat the full assembly and pressure-test sequence for the corrected connection.

Method A and Method B Procedures Were Blended

Probable cause: a push-fit technique or component was applied to the compression specimen, or vice versa.
Confirmation: compare the completed specimen against its recorded method boundary on the worksheet.
Correction: disassemble the affected specimen and rebuild it using only the procedure and components specified for its assigned method.
Reinspection: repeat the full inspection and pressure test for the corrected specimen.

Field Terms

Method B — Compression
The specimen joined using an approved compression fitting, nut, and ferrule, seated with a backup wrench and controlled tightening.
Insertion Depth Mark
The line marked on push-fit pipe at the manufacturer's specified distance from the pipe end, confirming full engagement.
Sealing Surface
The outer pipe surface contacted by a push-fit O-ring or a compression ferrule; it must be free of scratches, paint, and corrosion.
Backup Wrench
The wrench holding the fitting body stationary while a second wrench tightens the compression nut.
Ferrule
The compressible ring that seals a compression connection as the nut is tightened onto the fitting body.
Collet
The internal gripping mechanism inside a push-fit fitting that engages the pipe once fully inserted.

Any observed hazard or fitting failure that must halt the project until corrected or escalated.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to measure, cut, ream, deburr, and label copper, PEX, and PVC pipe so each specimen is ready for its compatible joining method. The finished work product is one labeled, dimensionally controlled set of copper, PEX, and PVC specimens: each cut square, each end reamed or deburred as its material requires, and each specimen dry-fit checked against a compatible test fitting.

You will review the controlled cut schedule and accepted tolerances; inspect your cutting and deburring tools; establish a reference end for each specimen; measure, mark, and cut each material using its correct tool and technique; ream or deburr and chamfer each cut end; label every specimen; dry-check fittings; and record any dimensional error before replacing failed specimens.

This project builds directly on the shutoff and system-identification skills from Projects 01 and 02, and it is the direct foundation for every joining method taught in Chapters 4 through 8: PEX crimp and clamp connections, soldered copper, push-fit and compression fittings, and PVC DWV assembly all begin with a correctly cut, reamed or deburred, and dimensionally verified pipe end.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-013 — Measure pipe accurately using a defined reference end and target-length convention.
  • PL-014 — Cut copper pipe square using an approved tubing cutter without deforming the tube.
  • PL-015 — Cut PEX tubing square using a compatible tubing shear in one controlled action.
  • PL-016 — Cut PVC pipe square using an approved ratcheting cutter or fine-tooth saw procedure.
  • PL-017 — Deburr pipe ends without thinning, gouging, or damaging the sealing surface.
  • PL-018 — Prepare pipe for fittings by reaming, chamfering, cleaning, labeling, and dry-fit checking each specimen.

Where This Skill Fits

Every joining method in this book depends on a correctly prepared pipe end. A crimp or clamp connection in Chapter 4, a soldered joint in Chapter 5, a push-fit or compression connection in Chapter 6, and a solvent-welded PVC joint in Chapter 8 will all leak, resist proper seating, or fail inspection if the pipe entering the fitting is not cut square, is not the correct length, retains a burr, or is contaminated.

The labeling and dimensional-control discipline established here — a defined reference end, a stated target length, and a written record of any dimensional error — is the same discipline carried into the branch layout work of Chapter 4 and the DWV assembly work of Chapter 8.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the controlled cut schedule, pipe materials, nominal sizes, fitting allowances, and accepted tolerances
  • confirming that every pipe specimen is new, clean, compatible with the selected fittings, and free of concealed pressure
  • inspecting pipe cutters, tubing shears, saws, reamers, deburring tools, chamfer tools, and work-holding devices
  • establishing a reference end and target-length convention for each specimen
  • measuring, marking, cutting, reaming or deburring, and chamfering copper, PEX, and PVC specimens using the tool and technique specific to each material
  • labeling every specimen and dry-checking it against a compatible fitting
  • recording dimensional errors and replacing failed specimens

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • join, assemble, solder, solvent-weld, crimp, or clamp any prepared specimen into a pressurized or tested branch — that work is covered in Chapters 4, 5, 6, and 8
  • cut, prepare, or reuse pipe that has been in prior pressurized service unless the assigned chapter project explicitly authorizes reclaimed material
  • substitute a cutting or deburring method for a material it is not designed for
  • proceed with any specimen when a stop-work condition identified in this chapter is present and uncorrected

Any specimen, tool, or dimensional result that fails verification in this chapter must be corrected, replaced, or escalated to a qualified professional before the specimen is used in a later project.

Trade Note

Copper, PEX, and PVC each require a different cutting and preparation technique. A tubing cutter that produces a clean, square copper cut will crush and flatten PEX. A saw blade suited to PVC will leave copper rough and out-of-round. This chapter treats each material as a separate, non-interchangeable procedure, and mixing techniques between materials is the most common cause of a wasted specimen documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not cut any pipe specimen until the controlled cut schedule has been reviewed, the correct tool for that material has been inspected, and a reference end and target length have been marked. Do not join, solder, crimp, clamp, or solvent-weld any specimen prepared in this project; that work belongs to the joining-method chapters that follow.

Shop Tip

Cut and label one specimen at a time rather than cutting an entire batch before deburring any of them. A batch of unlabeled, undeburred cuts is easily mixed up by material and size, and re-sorting them after the fact wastes more time than labeling as you go.

Reference End, Target Length, and Fitting Allowance

Before cutting any specimen, decide whether the stated target length is a pipe-only length or a finished assembly length that includes a fitting allowance. Mark a fine, square reference line at the reference end of the pipe, then measure and mark the target length from that same reference line. Do not shift the reference end partway through marking a specimen.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Pipe-Only Versus Assembly Length Before Marking

Confirm whether the controlled cut schedule states a pipe-only length or a finished assembly length that already includes the fitting allowance. Do not add a fitting allowance to a length that already includes it, and do not omit the allowance from a length that requires it.

Critical Safety Cue — Cutting Tool Control and Freshly Cut Edges

Support pipe securely before cutting and keep hands clear of the cutting wheel, shear blade, saw blade, or reamer flute during operation. Treat every freshly cut copper or PVC edge as sharp until deburred. Do not run a bare hand along an unfinished cut edge.

Field Safety Cue — Work Holding and Housekeeping

Support each pipe specimen on both sides of the cut location so it cannot roll, drop, or bind the cutting tool. Clear cut-offs, shavings, and PVC chamfer waste from the work surface between specimens so they do not create a slip hazard or contaminate the next dry-fit check.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Cutter and Shear Operation

Tighten a tubing cutter incrementally with partial rotations between tightening steps rather than clamping it down in one motion, which reduces tube diameter and causes an out-of-round cut. Operate a PEX shear in one complete, controlled squeeze rather than a sawing or rocking motion. Follow the specific manufacturer procedure for each tool.

E. Cut Schedule Review and Tool Inspection

  1. Review the controlled cut schedule, pipe materials, nominal sizes, fitting allowance rules, and accepted tolerances. Record every specimen's material, size, and target length on the Pipe Preparation Worksheet before cutting begins.
  2. Confirm that all pipe specimens are new, clean, compatible with the selected fittings, and free of concealed pressure. Reject any specimen stock that does not meet this condition.
  3. Inspect each pipe cutter, tubing shear, saw, reamer, deburring tool, chamfer tool, and work-holding device. Confirm each tool operates smoothly and that cutting wheels, blades, and reamer flutes are sharp and undamaged.

F. Reference Marking

  1. Establish a reference end and identify whether each target length is pipe-only length or finished assembly length. Record this decision on the worksheet before marking any specimen.

G. Copper Specimen Preparation

  1. Measure and mark each copper specimen using a fine, square reference line. Confirm the mark is square to the tube using a square or wraparound layout method.
  2. Support the copper tube without crushing or flattening it. Use a work-holding clamp or vise sized for the tube diameter.
  3. Cut copper with the approved tubing cutter, tightening incrementally to avoid reducing the tube diameter. Rotate the cutter fully around the tube between each incremental tightening.
  4. Ream the copper interior and remove the exterior burr without thinning or gouging the tube wall. Use the reamer and deburring tool specified for the tube size.
  5. Check copper roundness, square cut, clean surface, and fitting insertion. Confirm the specimen meets the Acceptance Standards table before labeling.

H. PEX Specimen Preparation

  1. Measure and mark each PEX specimen using the required insertion or cut reference. Confirm the mark matches the reference convention established in Step 4.
  2. Cut PEX square with the compatible tubing shear in one controlled action. Do not saw or rock the shear through the tubing.
  3. Reject PEX with angled cuts, flattened ends, scratches, kinks, contamination, or tool damage. Set aside any rejected specimen and record the defect on the worksheet.

I. PVC Specimen Preparation

  1. Measure and mark each PVC specimen using a square wraparound or approved layout method. Confirm the mark is square around the full pipe circumference.
  2. Support PVC on both sides of the cut and use the approved ratcheting cutter or fine-tooth saw procedure. Keep the cut aligned with the marked reference line throughout the cut.
  3. Remove interior and exterior burrs and form the chamfer required by the selected fitting system. Use the specified chamfer tool and depth.
  4. Clean PVC ends without using incompatible chemicals or contaminating the prepared surface. Use only a cleaner approved for the pipe material.

J. Labeling, Dry-Fit Checking, and Error Correction

  1. Label every specimen by material, size, target length, actual length, and intended joining method. Complete the label before the specimen leaves the work surface.
  2. Dry-check compatible fittings without damaging prepared surfaces. Insert each specimen fully into its test fitting and remove it without forcing or scoring the surface.
  3. Record dimensional errors and identify whether they resulted from measurement, fitting allowance, cut angle, tool control, or deburring. Write the identified cause on the worksheet for every specimen outside tolerance.
  4. Replace failed specimens and complete the preparation inspection. Confirm every item on the Pipe Preparation Schedule meets its target condition before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed specimen set using the same checks regardless of which material or joining method it is intended for.

  • Reference check: confirm the reference end and target-length convention were recorded before any cutting began.
  • Tool check: confirm every cutter, shear, saw, reamer, deburring tool, and chamfer tool was inspected before use.
  • Cut-quality check: confirm every specimen is square, round, and free of flattening, angled cuts, or tool damage appropriate to its material.
  • Deburring check: confirm interior and exterior burrs are removed without thinning, gouging, or scratching the sealing surface.
  • Dimensional check: confirm every actual length is recorded and compared against its target length and tolerance.
  • Labeling check: confirm every specimen carries a complete label before dry-fit checking.
  • Dry-fit check: confirm every specimen was checked against a compatible fitting without damage to the prepared surface.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Copper Tube Is Out-of-Round or Flattened After Cutting

Probable cause: the tubing cutter was tightened in one motion instead of incrementally, or excessive force was applied while rotating the cutter.
Confirmation: check the cut end against the original tube diameter using calipers or a fitting insertion check.
Correction: cut off the deformed section and recut using incremental tightening between partial rotations.
Reinspection: repeat the roundness and fitting-insertion check on the new cut.

Copper Fitting Insertion Is Tight or Will Not Seat Fully

Probable cause: an incomplete reaming pass left a partial interior burr, or the exterior burr was not fully removed.
Confirmation: inspect the interior and exterior of the cut end visually and by feel for remaining burr material.
Correction: ream and deburr the end again until the fitting seats fully without forcing.
Reinspection: repeat the dry-fit check.

PEX Cut Is Angled or the Tubing Is Flattened at the Cut

Probable cause: the shear was operated with a sawing or rocking motion instead of one controlled squeeze, or the shear blade is dull or damaged.
Confirmation: inspect the cut end for angle and cross-section against a new, undamaged specimen.
Correction: cut off the damaged section, inspect or replace the shear blade, and recut in a single controlled action.
Reinspection: repeat the visual cut-quality check.

PVC Cut Drifts Off the Marked Line or Chamfer Is Uneven

Probable cause: the pipe was not supported on both sides of the cut, or the chamfer tool was not held square to the pipe end.
Confirmation: compare the cut against the marked wraparound line and inspect the chamfer depth around the full circumference.
Correction: cut off the affected section, re-support the pipe on both sides of the new cut location, and reapply the chamfer tool squarely.
Reinspection: repeat the cut-quality and chamfer checks.

Actual Length Does Not Match the Target Length

Probable cause: the reference end was not held consistent during marking, the fitting allowance was applied incorrectly, or the measuring tool was misread.
Confirmation: remeasure the specimen from the defined reference end and compare against the recorded target length and fitting-allowance rule.
Correction: identify whether the error occurred in measurement, fitting allowance, cut angle, tool control, or deburring, then recut or replace the specimen.
Reinspection: repeat the dimensional check and record the corrected actual length on the worksheet.

A Specimen Is Unlabeled or Mixed Up With Another Material or Size

Probable cause: the specimen was cut and set aside before labeling, or multiple specimens were cut in a batch before any were labeled.
Confirmation: compare the unidentified specimen's material, diameter, and length against the controlled cut schedule.
Correction: identify the specimen conclusively before use; if it cannot be conclusively identified, discard it and cut a replacement.
Reinspection: confirm every remaining specimen is labeled before proceeding to the dry-fit check.

Cleanup

This project ends with a labeled, verified specimen set ready for the joining-method chapters that follow, not with any specimen permanently joined or installed.

  • Collect and dispose of copper shavings, PEX offcuts, and PVC cuttings and chamfer waste
  • Wipe down and store the tubing cutter, reamer, deburring tool, shear, PVC cutter or saw, and chamfer tool
  • Store labeled specimens by material and size in a manner that protects the prepared ends from damage
  • Return test fittings used for dry-fit checking to storage without altering them for permanent use
  • Store the Pipe Preparation Worksheet with the specimen set for reference by the assigned joining-method project

Performance Check

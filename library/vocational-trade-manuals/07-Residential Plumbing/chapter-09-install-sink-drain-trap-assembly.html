Job Overview

This project qualifies you to remove and replace one accessible sink P-trap assembly while preserving the trap, vent, slope, cleanout, and wall-adapter configuration already approved for the fixture. The finished work product is one inspected, leak-free P-trap assembly connecting the sink tailpiece to the wall drain, correctly aligned, correctly sloped, and holding its trap seal through repeated drainage tests.

You will identify the sink type, drain size, tailpiece, trap adapter, and wall connection; protect the cabinet and floor; remove the old trap without damaging the wall adapter or tailpiece; inspect the exposed connections for defects that are outside this project's scope; dry-fit and align the new trap bend and trap arm; assemble the slip-joint connections to the correct tightness; and verify the finished assembly under a full-basin drainage test.

This project uses the isolation, protection, and documentation habits established in Chapter 1 and the system-identification skills from Chapter 2. It applies the pipe-cutting and preparation skills from Chapter 3 to a slip-joint trap arm, and it prepares you for Chapter 10, where the same sink receives a new faucet and pop-up drain that connect into the trap assembly built here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • PL-049 — Remove the old trap while supporting the assembly and controlling retained water.
  • PL-050 — Align the tailpiece and trap arm so the assembly reaches the wall adapter without side load.
  • PL-051 — Install slip-joint fittings with correctly oriented washers and the correct tightening method.
  • PL-052 — Maintain the required trap seal depth and trap-arm slope.
  • PL-053 — Connect the trap to the wall drain without stressing the tailpiece or wall adapter.
  • PL-054 — Leak-test the completed trap assembly under static and full-flow drainage conditions.

Where This Skill Fits

A P-trap holds a water seal that blocks sewer gas from entering the building through the fixture drain. Every fixture-replacement project later in this book — the bathroom faucet and pop-up drain in Chapter 10, the toilet in Chapter 11, and the capstone bathroom in Chapter 20 — depends on a correctly aligned, correctly sloped trap assembly that holds its seal under repeated use.

The trap-arm cutting and slip-joint assembly skills built here reuse the pipe-preparation discipline from Chapter 3 and prepare you for the DWV assembly work in Chapter 8, where the same slope and alignment principles apply to glued PVC branch piping instead of slip-joint fittings.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • removing an existing accessible sink P-trap assembly, including the trap bend and trap arm
  • inspecting the exposed tailpiece and wall adapter for damage or an unapproved configuration
  • cutting a new trap arm to the controlled length required to reach the existing wall adapter
  • dry-fitting and aligning a new trap bend, trap arm, slip-joint nuts, and washers
  • assembling the slip-joint connections to the manufacturer-controlled tightness
  • leak-testing the completed assembly under static and full-basin drainage conditions

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • relocate the wall adapter, change the vent connection, or alter concealed DWV piping — that work belongs to a licensed plumber and is outside this project's boundary
  • correct a double-trapped, S-trap, or unvented condition discovered during inspection; any such condition is a stop-work condition requiring escalation
  • replace a cracked, corroded, or unsupported wall adapter without first stopping work and obtaining the required professional correction
  • use chemical drain cleaner or any hazardous liquid in a fixture being serviced under this project

Any wall-adapter, tailpiece, or venting condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected, repaired, or escalated to a qualified professional before the new trap assembly is installed.

Trade Note

A P-trap holds water by design; it is not simply a bend in the pipe. Every alignment, slope, and tightening decision in this chapter exists to protect that water seal. A trap that leaks air even without leaking water has already failed its intended purpose and must be corrected.

Setup Standard

Do not loosen any slip-joint nut until the cabinet and floor are protected, a container is positioned beneath the trap, and any chemical drain cleaner or hazardous liquid has been ruled out. Work started before the assembly is confirmed free of hazardous liquid must stop immediately.

Shop Tip

Photograph and measure the existing assembly before loosening the first nut. A photograph of the original tailpiece length, trap-arm slope, and wall-adapter height is the fastest way to confirm the new assembly matches the approved configuration once it is reassembled.

Trap Seal, Alignment, and Slip-Joint Orientation

The trap bend must retain the manufacturer-designed water depth after drainage, and the trap arm must maintain the slope observed in the original installation. Every slip-joint washer has a required orientation; installing one backward is a common cause of a slow, hard-to-diagnose leak that appears only under flowing conditions.

Measurement Cue — Cut the Trap Arm to the Controlled Length

Cut the trap arm square to the length that allows the tailpiece to enter the trap bend vertically and the trap arm to reach the wall adapter without side load, while preserving the required slip-joint insertion depth at both ends. Do not force a trap arm that is too short or too long into alignment by bending the tailpiece or the wall adapter.

Critical Safety Cue — Retained Water and Sewer-Gas Exposure

Support the trap and control retained water before loosening the final slip-joint nut. Treat a double-trapped, S-trap, or unvented configuration discovered during inspection as a stop-work condition requiring professional correction before a new trap is installed on an unapproved configuration.

Field Safety Cue — Cabinet and Floor Protection

Protect the cabinet floor and surrounding surfaces and position a container beneath the trap before loosening any nut. Confirm no chemical drain cleaner or hazardous liquid is present in the fixture before beginning removal.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Slip-Joint Tightening

Hand-tighten every slip-joint nut first, then apply only the permitted additional tightening stated by the fitting manufacturer — typically a partial turn with pliers beyond hand-tight. Do not use thread tape or pipe compound on a slip-joint sealing surface unless the product instructions require it.

F. Identification and Documentation Review

  1. Identify the sink type, drain size, tailpiece, trap adapter, wall connection, vent relationship, and approved replacement configuration. Record each item on the Sink Trap Assembly Worksheet before touching the existing assembly.
  2. Protect the cabinet and floor and place a container beneath the trap. Confirm protection is in place before loosening any nut.
  3. Confirm that no chemical drain cleaner or hazardous liquid is present. Treat any uncertainty as a stop-work condition until confirmed safe.
  4. Photograph and measure the existing assembly before removal. Record tailpiece length, trap-arm length, wall-adapter height, and observed slope.

G. Removal and Inspection

  1. Loosen the trap nuts while supporting the trap and controlling retained water. Direct retained water into the staged container.
  2. Remove the trap bend and trap arm without damaging the wall adapter or tailpiece. Set removed components aside on protected material.
  3. Clean the tailpiece and trap adapter sealing surfaces. Remove old washer residue, corrosion, and debris without damaging the sealing surface.
  4. Inspect for corrosion, cracks, incorrect slope, double trapping, S-trap configuration, excessive extension, missing venting, or inaccessible wall damage. Record every condition observed, whether or not it stops work.
  5. Stop work if the system requires concealed DWV correction or contains an unapproved configuration outside the chapter scope. Escalate to a qualified professional before installing a new trap on an unapproved configuration.

H. Dry-Fit and Alignment

  1. Dry-fit the new trap bend, trap arm, nuts, and washers. Do not apply final tightening during the dry-fit.
  2. Cut the trap arm square to the controlled length while preserving required insertion. Measure against the recorded original trap-arm length and adjust only as needed to reach the wall adapter correctly.
  3. Orient each slip-joint washer correctly. Confirm the beveled or tapered face is positioned per the manufacturer instruction before final assembly.
  4. Align the trap so the tailpiece enters vertically and the trap arm reaches the wall adapter without side load. Confirm no component is bent or forced to achieve alignment.
  5. Maintain the approved trap-seal depth and trap-arm slope. Compare the dry-fit configuration against the original measurements recorded before removal.

I. Assembly and Tightening

  1. Hand-tighten slip-joint nuts, then apply only the permitted additional tightening. Use slip-joint pliers for the final partial turn only, not for hand-tightening.
  2. Do not use thread tape or pipe compound on slip-joint sealing surfaces unless the product instructions require it. Confirm the manufacturer instruction before applying any sealant.

J. Testing and Verification

  1. Fill the sink and inspect the stopper or drain-body connection. Confirm no leakage at the drain body before proceeding to the trap test.
  2. Release the full basin and observe the trap under high-flow drainage. Watch every joint during the full-flow release, not only after it completes.
  3. Inspect every joint for leakage, movement, slow drainage, siphoning, and cabinet contact. Use dry tissue at each joint to confirm a dry condition.
  4. Correct alignment or washer seating after draining the assembly. Depressurize by draining before loosening any joint for correction.
  5. Repeat the fill-and-release test and perform a delayed dry-tissue reinspection. Allow the assembly to stand undisturbed before the delayed check.
  6. Clean the work area and document the completed trap configuration. Complete every field on the Sink Trap Assembly Worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed trap assembly using the same checks regardless of which sink or cabinet the project involves.

  • Alignment check: confirm the tailpiece enters vertically and the trap arm reaches the wall adapter without side load.
  • Trap-seal check: confirm the trap-seal depth and trap-arm slope match the recorded original configuration.
  • Tightness check: confirm every slip-joint nut is hand-tightened plus only the permitted additional turn.
  • Static leak check: confirm no leakage or weeping after the basin is filled and observed.
  • Full-flow leak check: confirm no leakage, siphoning, slow drainage, or cabinet contact during a full-basin release.
  • Delayed reinspection: confirm a dry-tissue check remains dry after the assembly has stood undisturbed.

Complete the Sink Trap Assembly Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 10.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Slip-Joint Connection Weeps Under Static Pressure

Probable cause: a washer installed backward, an under-tightened nut, a cracked washer, or a sealing surface with old debris or a nick.
Confirmation: drain the assembly, loosen the affected nut, and inspect washer orientation and condition and the sealing surface.
Correction: clean the sealing surface, replace a damaged washer, reorient it correctly, and hand-tighten plus the permitted additional turn; do not over-tighten to compensate for a damaged washer.
Reinspection: refill the basin and repeat the static leak test at the corrected joint.

The Trap Leaks Only During Full-Flow Drainage

Probable cause: misalignment placing side load on a joint, an incorrect trap-arm slope, or a connection tightened without full engagement.
Confirmation: observe the joint directly during a full-basin release and compare the trap-arm slope and alignment against the recorded original configuration.
Correction: drain the assembly, realign the trap arm and tailpiece without side load, correct the slope, and retighten to the permitted standard.
Reinspection: repeat the full-flow drainage test and observe every joint again.

Drainage Is Slow or the Trap Siphons Dry

Probable cause: an incorrect trap-arm length or slope, a missing or restricted vent connection, or a trap installed with insufficient seal depth.
Confirmation: inspect the trap-arm length and slope against the recorded original configuration and confirm the vent connection observed during removal is unaffected by this project.
Correction: correct the trap-arm length or slope within this project's scope; if the cause is a missing or restricted vent, stop work and escalate to a qualified professional, since venting correction is outside this project's boundary.
Reinspection: repeat the full-flow drainage test and confirm the trap retains its water seal afterward.

The Trap Arm Contacts the Cabinet or Adjacent Piping

Probable cause: the trap arm was cut too long, the tailpiece was extended more than required, or the assembly was forced into position rather than cut to the correct length.
Confirmation: measure the installed trap-arm length against the wall-adapter distance and inspect for contact points.
Correction: drain and disassemble the affected joint, recut the trap arm to the correct length, and reassemble without forcing contact.
Reinspection: repeat the alignment check and confirm no cabinet contact during the full-flow drainage test.

An Unapproved Configuration Is Discovered During Inspection

Probable cause: a prior repair created a double trap, an S-trap configuration, or removed required venting without correction.
Confirmation: compare the existing configuration against the approved replacement configuration identified in Step 1.
Correction: stop work; do not install a new trap on an unapproved configuration. Escalate the concealed DWV correction to a qualified professional.
Reinspection: resume this project only after the professional correction is complete and the configuration is reconfirmed as approved.

Field Terms

Tailpiece
The vertical pipe extending down from the sink drain body to the trap bend.
Trap Bend
The curved fitting that holds the water seal between the tailpiece and the trap arm.
Trap Arm
The horizontal pipe section connecting the trap bend to the wall adapter.
Wall Adapter
The fixed fitting in the wall or floor that receives the trap arm and connects to the building DWV system.
Trap Seal Depth
The vertical depth of standing water retained in the trap bend after drainage, as designed into the trap bend geometry.
Trap-Arm Slope
The controlled downward pitch of the trap arm from the trap bend to the wall adapter.
Double Trapping
An unapproved condition in which two trap seals exist in series on the same fixture drain, restricting flow and venting.
S-Trap Configuration
An unapproved trap arrangement that can siphon its water seal due to the absence of a properly vented trap arm.

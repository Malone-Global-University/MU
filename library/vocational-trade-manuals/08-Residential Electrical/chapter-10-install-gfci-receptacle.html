Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install and commission one listed GFCI receptacle on a de-energized, inspected branch circuit. The finished work product is one correctly wired GFCI receptacle — with line and load conductors identified and connected exactly as the approved design requires, terminals tightened to the manufacturer-controlled method, a cover plate installed, and the device verified by its own test button, a listed external tester, and, where downstream protection is part of the design, confirmed loss of power at every protected outlet.

You will review the circuit design, required GFCI location, and whether the project calls for a line-only device or a device that also protects downstream outlets; verify the circuit de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1; separately identify and label the incoming line cable and any downstream load cable before disconnecting anything; connect conductors to the correct line or load terminals without interchanging them; tighten every terminal to the manufacturer-controlled method; mount the device and cover plate; complete the required inspection; restore power; and test the device's reset, trip, and downstream-protection function before applying the required labels and closing out the documentation.

This project builds directly on the box preparation of Chapters 3 and 4, the cable and connector work of Chapters 5 and 6, the splicing and pigtailing of Chapter 7, the grounding and bonding of Chapter 8, and the receptacle-wiring fundamentals of Chapter 9. It feeds directly into the switching and fixture work of Chapters 11 through 15, the panel-level AFCI/GFCI breaker work of Chapter 16, and the receptacle troubleshooting of Chapter 18, and it is one of the required device installations in the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-055 — Identify the line and load terminals on a listed GFCI receptacle and correctly distinguish them from each other before making any connection.
  • EL-056 — Connect the GFCI device according to the approved line-only or line-and-load design without interchanging line and load conductors.
  • EL-057 — Protect downstream devices where the approved design includes a load connection, keeping line and load conductor groups identifiable throughout the box.
  • EL-058 — Reset and test the device using its own test button and a listed external tester, confirming correct trip and reset behavior.
  • EL-059 — Label protected outlets and any no-equipment-ground condition using the required GFCI labeling.
  • EL-060 — Inspect the completed installation for terminal use, box fill, labeling, and downstream protection before and after energization.

Where This Skill Fits

A GFCI receptacle is a life-safety device, not an ordinary receptacle with an extra button. Interchanging line and load conductors does not merely fail to protect downstream outlets — it can prevent the device from resetting at all, or create a condition where the device appears to work but does not trip correctly. The line-load distinction taught here is the same distinction used at the panel level for GFCI and dual-function breakers in Chapter 16, and the reset-and-test sequence taught here is the same verification pattern used throughout the book whenever protective devices are commissioned.

The intact-cover, plug-in-tester diagnostic habit built in Chapter 2, and the de-energized live-dead-live verification built in Chapter 1, both apply directly here: this project never allows exposed-conductor diagnosis, and every conductor is treated as energized until proven otherwise by direct test.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the circuit, required GFCI location, device rating, line-and-load design, downstream outlets, box fill, and labeling requirements from the approved plan
  • de-energizing and verifying the circuit using the live-dead-live procedure before disconnecting or connecting any conductor
  • separately identifying and labeling the incoming line cable and any downstream load cable before disconnection
  • connecting the equipment-grounding conductor, bonding the box where required, and connecting line and, where the design requires it, load conductors to their correct terminals
  • mounting the device, installing the cover plate, and completing the required inspection before energization
  • restoring power, resetting the device, testing with the device test button and a listed external tester, and testing each approved downstream receptacle
  • applying the required GFCI-protected and no-equipment-ground labels and documenting the installation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on the circuit while energized, or disconnect line and load conductors before the circuit has been verified de-energized by the live-dead-live procedure
  • connect a downstream load connection that is not part of the approved design, or protect outlets not identified in the circuit plan
  • interchange line and load conductors, bypass the device's terminal markings, or use an unlisted or damaged GFCI device
  • energize the completed installation before required inspection is complete, where inspection is required by the project or jurisdiction
  • continue past nuisance tripping, inability to reset, line-load reversal, open grounding concerns, or incompatible downstream wiring without stopping work and correcting the condition

Any GFCI installation that fails reset, trip, or downstream-protection testing must be corrected and fully retested before the project is closed out.

Trade Note

A GFCI receptacle protects people, not just equipment. It compares the current going out on the ungrounded conductor to the current returning on the grounded conductor and interrupts the circuit when a small difference indicates current is escaping through an unintended path — potentially through a person. Reversing line and load conductors, or wiring downstream outlets to the line terminals by mistake, can leave those outlets completely unprotected while appearing to function normally on an ordinary plug-in tester.

Setup Standard

Do not disconnect or connect any conductor until the circuit has been verified de-energized by the live-dead-live procedure and the incoming line cable and any downstream load cable have been separately identified and labeled. Do not connect a downstream load connection unless it is part of the approved design.

Shop Tip

Label the line cable and the load cable the moment you identify them, before disconnecting either one. Once both cables are out of the box and lying loose, it is easy to reconnect them backward — and a GFCI wired backward will often still pass a casual glance while leaving the downstream outlets completely unprotected.

Line-Only Versus Line-and-Load Design

Confirm from the approved circuit plan whether this installation is line-only or line-and-load before opening the device packaging. A line-only GFCI protects only the receptacle itself; the factory load-terminal covering stays in place and no load conductors are ever connected. A line-and-load GFCI additionally protects every downstream outlet wired to its load terminals. Do not create a downstream load connection unless the approved design specifically calls for one, and do not leave a required downstream connection unmade.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify De-Energized Before Touching Any Conductor

Do not disconnect or connect any line or load conductor until the circuit has been verified de-energized by the complete live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1. Treat every conductor in the box as energized until that test proves otherwise.

Measurement Cue — Identify Line and Load Before Disconnecting Anything

Confirm which cable is the incoming line cable and which, if any, is the downstream load cable using the approved circuit records and a controlled testing method, and label both cables before disconnecting either one. Do not rely on physical position, box order, or memory once conductors have been separated.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Terminal Markings and Torque

Use only the terminals the device manufacturer marks for line and, where applicable, load conductors. Tighten every terminal using the manufacturer-controlled method and torque. A device with damaged, corroded, or unmarked terminals is a stop-work condition, not a device to be wired by guesswork.

Code Alert — Downstream Protection and Labeling Requirements

Whether downstream GFCI protection is required at this location, and what labeling must be applied to protected outlets or ungrounded circuits, depends on the approved plan and adopted code. Verify the exact labeling requirement before completing the installation rather than assuming a generic label is sufficient.

E. Design Review and Circuit Verification

  1. Review the circuit, required GFCI location, device rating, line and load design, downstream outlets, box fill, and labeling requirements. Confirm whether this installation is line-only or line-and-load before opening the device packaging.
  2. Verify the circuit is de-energized using the approved live-dead-live procedure. Complete the full test at the box before disconnecting or connecting any conductor.

F. Line and Load Identification

  1. Identify and separately label the incoming line cable and any intended downstream load cable before disconnecting conductors. Mark each cable clearly at the box.
  2. Confirm line identity using the approved circuit records and controlled testing method. Do not rely on physical position or memory once conductors are separated.
  3. Keep the factory load-terminal covering in place when no downstream protection is intended. Do not remove it unless the approved design requires a load connection.

G. Box Inspection and Conductor Preparation

  1. Inspect the box, conductors, grounding path, and device instructions. Confirm box fill, conductor length, and grounding provisions are adequate before proceeding.
  2. Prepare pigtails and conductors to the listed strip length. Strip conductors without nicking, scoring, or cutting strands.

H. Termination

  1. Connect the equipment-grounding conductor and bond the box where required. Verify continuity per Chapter 8 before closing the box.
  2. Connect line neutral and line ungrounded conductors to the identified line terminals. Confirm each conductor lands on its marked line terminal.
  3. Connect load neutral and load ungrounded conductors only when downstream GFCI protection is part of the approved design. Do not create a load connection that is not called for in the plan.
  4. Do not interchange line and load conductors. Recheck the labeled cables against the terminal markings before tightening.
  5. Tighten all terminals using the manufacturer-controlled method and torque. Use a calibrated torque method where the device instructions specify one.
  6. Inspect for exposed copper, damaged insulation, incorrect terminal use, and box overfill. Correct any deficiency before folding conductors into the box.
  7. Fold conductors into the box while keeping line and load groups identifiable. Arrange conductors so the line and load groups remain distinguishable on inspection.

I. Mounting and Pre-Energization Inspection

  1. Mount the device and install the cover plate. Confirm the device sits square to the box with no gap exposing energized parts.
  2. Complete the required inspection before energization. Do not restore power until the required inspection has been completed and passed.

J. Commissioning and Downstream Testing

  1. Restore power and press the reset button. Confirm the device indicates a reset condition per its instructions.
  2. Use the device test button and verify that protected power is interrupted. Confirm the device trips and indicates the tripped condition.
  3. Reset the device and test the receptacle with the listed external tester. Confirm correct wiring and reset function.
  4. Test each approved downstream receptacle for loss of power when the GFCI trips. Trip the device again and confirm every intended downstream outlet loses power.

K. Labeling, Failure Response, and Documentation

  1. Apply the required GFCI-protected and no-equipment-ground labels where applicable. Label every downstream outlet protected by this device.
  2. Stop work for nuisance tripping, inability to reset, line-load reversal, open grounding concerns, or incompatible downstream wiring. Do not close out the project with any of these conditions unresolved.
  3. De-energize before correction. Repeat the live-dead-live procedure before touching any conductor to correct a failed condition.
  4. Document the protected locations, labels, test results, and device identification. Complete every field on the GFCI Installation and Test Worksheet.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed GFCI installation using the same checks regardless of whether the design is line-only or line-and-load.

  • Line/load check: confirm line and load conductors were correctly identified before disconnection and were never interchanged during termination.
  • Terminal check: confirm every conductor lands on its correctly marked terminal, tightened to the manufacturer-controlled method.
  • Grounding check: confirm the equipment-grounding conductor is connected and the box is bonded where required, with no neutral-to-ground connection.
  • Box-fill check: confirm box fill remains within the approved allowance after all connectors, pigtails, and the device are accounted for.
  • Mounting check: confirm the device is square to the box with an intact cover plate.
  • Function check: confirm the device resets and trips correctly using its own test button and a listed external tester.
  • Downstream check: confirm every approved downstream outlet loses power when the device trips.
  • Labeling check: confirm every required GFCI-protected and no-equipment-ground label is applied.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Device Will Not Reset

Probable cause: line and load conductors are reversed, a downstream connection was made on a line-only design, or a wiring fault exists on the load side.
Confirmation: de-energize and verify absence of voltage, then recheck the labeled line and load cables against the terminal markings.
Correction: correct any line-load reversal or unintended downstream connection, and retighten terminals to the manufacturer-controlled method.
Reinspection: restore power and repeat the reset, test-button trip, and external-tester sequence.

The Device Trips Immediately or Repeatedly Without a Test Button Press

Probable cause: a ground fault on the load side, moisture in a downstream box, or a downstream neutral shared with another circuit.
Confirmation: de-energize and isolate the load conductors, then inspect downstream boxes for moisture, damage, or unintended neutral connections.
Correction: correct the identified fault, moisture condition, or shared-neutral wiring before restoring power.
Reinspection: restore power and repeat the full reset and trip test sequence, including downstream outlets.

A Downstream Outlet Remains Powered When the GFCI Trips

Probable cause: the downstream outlet was wired to the line terminals instead of the load terminals, or it is fed from a different circuit entirely.
Confirmation: de-energize and verify absence of voltage, then trace the downstream outlet's supply cable back to the GFCI box.
Correction: reconnect the downstream cable to the load terminals if it belongs on this circuit, or remove it from the labeled protected-outlet list if it is supplied by another circuit.
Reinspection: repeat the downstream protection test for every outlet on the corrected labeled list.

The External Tester Shows an Open Ground or Reversed Polarity

Probable cause: the equipment-grounding conductor is not connected, the box bonding is incomplete, or the ungrounded and grounded conductors are reversed at the terminals.
Confirmation: de-energize and verify absence of voltage, then inspect the grounding connection and retrace each conductor to its terminal.
Correction: correct the grounding connection or conductor assignment per Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 procedures.
Reinspection: restore power and repeat the external-tester check before continuing to the downstream test.

Box Fill Is Exceeded After All Pigtails and the Device Are Accounted For

Probable cause: the box was undersized for the conductor count, or additional pigtails were added without recalculating fill.
Confirmation: recalculate box fill using the approved conductor, clamp, device, and grounding allowances.
Correction: replace the box with one of adequate listed volume per Chapter 3 or Chapter 4 procedures before completing termination.
Reinspection: repeat the box-fill calculation and the full termination and test sequence in the corrected box.

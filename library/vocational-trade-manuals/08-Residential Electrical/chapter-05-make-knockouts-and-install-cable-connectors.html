Prepare a de-energized metal box or training enclosure with correctly selected knockouts and listed cable connectors for the approved wiring method, so that every cable entering the enclosure is sized to its opening, seated in a connector listed for the exact cable and enclosure condition, and supported without crushing the sheath or losing grounding continuity.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to remove correctly sized knockouts from a metal enclosure and install listed cable connectors for the approved wiring method. The finished work product is one de-energized enclosure with each intended knockout removed cleanly, deburred, and fitted with a connector listed for the exact cable, raceway, conductor material, size range, and enclosure condition; each cable seated and supported at the connector without a nicked conductor or a crushed sheath; and every unused opening closed with a listed closure.

You will review the enclosure type, wiring method, and connector listing; verify the enclosure is disconnected from all power sources; plan cable-entry locations that preserve bending space, box fill, grounding hardware, and cover installation; select and remove the correct knockout for each connector; deburr the opening; install and tighten each connector; prepare and insert the cable; secure the cable outside the box; close unused openings; and inspect the finished assembly.

This project builds directly on the box installation skills from Chapters 3 and 4 and prepares the enclosure for the NM cable routing taught in Chapter 6 and the splicing and grounding work taught in Chapters 7 and 8. A correctly sized knockout and a correctly seated connector are what keep a cable's sheath and grounding path intact from the moment it enters the box through every later termination.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-025 — Remove box knockouts of the correct size using the approved punch, knockout tool, or controlled hand-tool procedure without enlarging or deforming the opening.
  • EL-026 — Select cable connectors listed for the exact cable, raceway, conductor material, size range, and enclosure condition.
  • EL-027 — Install connectors through the knockout and tighten the locknut, bushing, or threaded body using the approved tool and seating method.
  • EL-028 — Protect cable sheathing while preparing and inserting the cable end through the connector.
  • EL-029 — Secure cables at boxes with the required cable support outside the box at the code- and product-controlled location.
  • EL-030 — Inspect connector fit, seating, sheath protection, cable support, and grounding continuity, and correct any distorted knockout, damaged connector, or crushed cable.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 installed and supported the box itself. This project prepares that box to receive cable: every knockout removed here is a permanent opening in the enclosure, and every connector installed here is the only protection the cable sheath has as it passes through the metal edge. Chapter 6 assumes an enclosure already fitted with correctly sized, correctly seated connectors before any cable is pulled through framing. Chapter 8 assumes the metal connectors and raceway fittings installed here provide bonding continuity as part of the equipment-grounding path.

An oversized or torn-out knockout cannot be restored to a listed condition; it must be treated as a defect and corrected before proceeding, which is why this chapter separates knockout removal from connector installation as distinct, inspected steps.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the enclosure type, wiring method, cable or raceway type, conductor count, connector listing, environmental rating, and grounding requirement
  • verifying the enclosure is disconnected from all power sources before any knockout or connector work begins
  • planning cable-entry locations that preserve bending space, box fill, grounding hardware, device clearance, and cover installation
  • selecting and removing the correct knockout size for each listed connector without enlarging or deforming the opening
  • selecting, installing, and tightening a connector listed for the exact cable, raceway, conductor material, size range, and enclosure condition
  • preparing and inserting the cable end without nicking conductors or removing excessive sheath, then securing the required cable support outside the box
  • closing unused openings with listed closures and inspecting the finished assembly

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on an enclosure that has not been verified disconnected from all power sources
  • enlarge, redrill, or force open a knockout beyond its manufactured size
  • substitute an unlisted connector, an incompatible conductor-material connector, or a connector not rated for the enclosure's environmental condition
  • leave a cable connector loose, an unused opening uncovered, or a cable unsupported at the required location

Any enclosure, knockout, or connector condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected before the cable routing project in Chapter 6 begins.

Trade Note

A knockout removed with the wrong tool or excessive force often tears past its scored edge, leaving an oversized or misshapen opening that no listed connector can seat correctly. Once a knockout is torn out, the enclosure has a permanent defect at that location; treat an enlarged or deformed knockout as a failed component, not a condition to work around with tape, oversized connectors, or field-improvised bushings.

Setup Standard

Do not remove a knockout or install a connector until the enclosure has been verified disconnected from all power sources and the cable-entry plan has been established. A knockout removed at the wrong location cannot be relocated; verify the plan against box fill, bending space, grounding hardware, and cover clearance before removing any knockout.

Shop Tip

Remove only the knockout you are ready to fit with a connector in the same work session. An open, unfitted knockout left in an enclosure is an unclosed opening and a stop-work condition if the enclosure is set aside before the connector is installed.

Common Field Fundamentals

A knockout and its connector are a matched pair: the connector is listed for a specific knockout size, and a connector installed in the wrong size opening cannot seat, tighten, or protect the cable sheath as intended. Confirm the connector before removing the knockout, not after.

Knockout Sizing and Connector Compatibility

Every knockout removed in this chapter is selected to match one specific listed connector. Concentric or eccentric knockouts allow more than one size at a single location; remove only the ring that matches the intended connector, working from the innermost ring outward if a smaller size is later found to be incorrect. A connector must never be forced into an opening larger than its listed size, and a knockout must never be enlarged by filing, drilling, or cutting to fit an oversized connector.

Measurement Cue — Match the Knockout to the Listed Connector Before Removal

Confirm the connector's listed knockout size against the enclosure's knockout markings before punching or knocking out any opening. Removing the wrong size cannot be corrected by adjustment; the enclosure must be replaced or the location changed if the wrong knockout is removed.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify the Enclosure Is Disconnected Before Any Knockout or Connector Work

Confirm that the enclosure is disconnected from all power sources using the Chapter 1 lockout and verified de-energization procedure before removing a knockout, installing a connector, or handling any cable end. Treat any enclosure of unknown status as energized until verified otherwise.

Field Safety Cue — Sharp Edges at Knockouts and Connectors

Freshly removed knockouts and unfiled openings have sharp edges capable of cutting skin or cable sheath. Wear work gloves when handling removed knockouts, deburr every opening before inserting a connector, and inspect the connector for a smooth, unbroken bushing or throat before pulling cable through it.

Code Alert — Connector Listing Must Match Enclosure Environment

A connector listed for a dry-location interior enclosure is not automatically suitable for a damp, wet, or corrosive location. Confirm the connector's listing against the enclosure's environmental condition, and verify local permit and inspection requirements before selecting a connector for anything other than a standard dry interior enclosure.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Connector Assembly and Tightening Method

Follow the connector manufacturer's instructions for disassembly, insertion direction, locknut or bushing tightening method, and required tool. A connector tightened with the wrong method or tool may not seat fully, leaving the cable unsupported and the grounding path uncertain.

F. Enclosure Review and De-Energized Verification

  1. Review the enclosure type, wiring method, cable or raceway type, conductor count, connector listing, environmental rating, and grounding requirement. Record these on the Knockout and Connector Verification Worksheet before proceeding.
  2. Verify that the enclosure is disconnected from all power sources. Apply the Chapter 1 lockout and verified de-energization procedure if the enclosure is or could become part of an energized system.
  3. Inspect the enclosure for sharp edges, corrosion, missing parts, damaged threads, and prohibited openings. Correct or replace the enclosure before proceeding if any defect is found.

G. Cable-Entry Planning

  1. Plan cable-entry locations to preserve conductor bending space, box fill, grounding hardware, device clearance, and cover installation. Mark each planned entry location on the enclosure before removing any knockout.
  2. Select the correct knockout size for each listed connector. Confirm the connector's listed knockout size against the enclosure's knockout markings before proceeding.

H. Knockout Removal

  1. Support the enclosure on a stable work surface. Confirm the enclosure cannot shift or roll during knockout removal.
  2. Remove the selected knockout using the approved punch, knockout tool, or controlled hand-tool procedure. Follow the tool manufacturer's instructions for setup and operation.
  3. Remove only the intended knockout and protect adjacent concentric rings. Stop immediately if an adjacent ring begins to loosen or deform.
  4. Deburr the opening without enlarging or deforming it. Inspect the opening under adequate light for remaining sharp edges before proceeding.

I. Connector Selection and Installation

  1. Select a connector listed for the exact cable, raceway, conductor material, size range, and enclosure condition. Confirm the connector's marked listing against the project's wiring method and environmental requirement.
  2. Disassemble the connector only as required by its instructions. Keep all removed parts identified and available for reassembly.
  3. Insert the connector through the opening in the correct direction. Confirm the connector's orientation matches its listed installation method.
  4. Install and tighten the locknut, bushing, or threaded body using the approved tool and seating method. Confirm full engagement before proceeding.
  5. Orient connector screws or clamps for access. Position the connector so its clamp screws remain reachable after the cable is installed.

J. Cable Preparation and Insertion

  1. Prepare the cable end without nicking conductors or removing excessive sheath. Inspect the prepared end under adequate light before inserting it into the connector.
  2. Insert enough sheath through the connector to satisfy the listed installation and box requirements. Confirm the sheath insertion length against the connector manufacturer's instructions.
  3. Tighten the connector clamp evenly without crushing the cable or damaging insulation. Inspect the sheath immediately after tightening for deformation.
  4. Verify that the cable cannot be withdrawn under the approved hand check. Apply a firm, steady hand-pull without force sufficient to damage the cable.

K. Cable Support and Enclosure Closure

  1. Install the required cable support outside the box at the code- and product-controlled location. Confirm the support point matches the connector and cable manufacturer's stated distance.
  2. Close unused openings with listed closures. Confirm each closure plug is fully seated and cannot be dislodged by hand.

L. Inspection and Correction

  1. Inspect connector seating, locknut engagement, sheath protection, cable support, grounding continuity, and environmental rating. Complete every item on the Acceptance Standards table before concealment.
  2. Replace any distorted knockout, damaged connector, or crushed cable. Do not conceal or proceed past a failed item; correct it and repeat the affected inspection step.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed enclosure using the same checks regardless of which cable, raceway, or connector type the project involves.

  • Enclosure check: confirm the enclosure was verified disconnected from all power sources before any knockout or connector work began.
  • Knockout check: confirm each removed knockout matches its intended connector size without enlargement or deformation.
  • Connector check: confirm each connector is listed for the exact cable, raceway, conductor material, size range, and enclosure condition.
  • Seating check: confirm each locknut, bushing, or threaded body is fully tightened by the approved method.
  • Sheath check: confirm no nicked conductors, no excessive sheath removal, and correct sheath insertion length.
  • Support check: confirm required cable support is installed at the correct distance outside the box.
  • Closure check: confirm every unused opening is closed with a listed closure plug.

Complete the Knockout and Connector Verification Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 06.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Knockout Tears Past Its Scored Line During Removal

Probable cause: excessive force, an incorrect tool, or striking the knockout off-center.
Confirmation: inspect the opening for an irregular edge, a torn tab, or damage extending into the enclosure wall beyond the scored line.
Correction: do not attempt to fit a connector into the damaged opening; treat the enclosure as defective at that location and replace the enclosure or select an alternate approved location.
Reinspection: repeat knockout selection and removal on the replacement enclosure or alternate location, then reinspect for a clean, correctly sized opening.

The Connector Will Not Seat Fully in the Knockout Opening

Probable cause: a mismatch between the connector's listed size and the actual knockout removed, or a deformed opening.
Confirmation: compare the connector's marked size against the knockout diameter and inspect the opening for deformation.
Correction: install a connector of the correct listed size for the actual opening, or correct the enclosure defect before proceeding; do not force an oversized or undersized connector into the opening.
Reinspection: repeat connector installation and tightening with the correctly matched connector.

The Cable Sheath Is Crushed or Cut After Connector Tightening

Probable cause: overtightening the connector clamp, a sharp or undeburred connector throat, or insufficient sheath thickness for the connector's clamping range.
Confirmation: inspect the cable sheath immediately after tightening and compare against an unused length of the same cable.
Correction: replace the damaged cable section; do not cover a crushed or cut sheath. Reinstall the connector with correct tightening torque and confirm the connector is listed for the cable's sheath thickness.
Reinspection: repeat cable preparation, insertion, and tightening with a new cable section, then repeat the hand-pull withdrawal check.

The Cable Can Be Withdrawn from the Connector Under the Hand Check

Probable cause: an undertightened clamp, insufficient sheath inserted through the connector, or a connector not matched to the cable's outside diameter.
Confirmation: inspect the clamp tightness and measure the inserted sheath length against the connector manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: retighten the connector clamp to the approved method, or reinstall with a connector matched to the cable's outside diameter.
Reinspection: repeat the hand-pull withdrawal check after correction.

An Unused Opening Is Discovered Without a Closure Plug at Final Inspection

Probable cause: a knockout removed for a planned cable entry that was not used, or a closure step skipped during assembly.
Confirmation: compare the number of installed connectors against the number of removed knockouts.
Correction: install a listed closure plug sized to the unused opening before concealment or further work.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check across the entire enclosure.

Grounding Continuity Cannot Be Confirmed at a Metal Connector

Probable cause: a painted or corroded enclosure surface at the connector location, a loose locknut, or a connector not listed for bonding continuity in the wiring method used.
Confirmation: inspect the connector seating and the enclosure surface at the connector location for paint, corrosion, or looseness.
Correction: remove paint or corrosion only from the designated bonding location, retighten the connector, or install a bonding jumper if required by the wiring method; escalate to a qualified electrical professional if continuity cannot be confirmed after correction.
Reinspection: repeat the grounding continuity check specified in Chapter 8 before proceeding.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to route and secure one approved nonmetallic-sheathed (NM) cable branch through open wood framing, connecting the boxes installed in Chapters 3 and 4 along a route defined by the approved circuit plan. The finished work product is one protected, supported, labeled cable run — drilled through the permitted zone of each framing member, free of damage, inserted into each box with the correct free conductor length, stapled or supported at the required intervals, protected with steel plates where the route is too close to the framing face, and fully documented before it is concealed.

You will confirm the circuit is de-energized where required, mark the route and each framing penetration, drill only within the permitted zone using a controlled technique, pull cable without kinks or crushing, insert the cable into each box connector without damaging the sheath, staple and support the cable to the manufacturer's and code-controlled spacing, install protection plates and fireblocking where required, and photograph and document the completed run before any wall finish conceals it.

This project builds directly on the boxes installed in Chapters 3 and 4 and the connectors and knockouts prepared in Chapter 5. It is performed only on a de-energized circuit, and the completed, protected, and documented cable run becomes the foundation for the splicing, pigtailing, and grounding work in Chapters 7 and 8.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-031 — Drill framing members within the permitted zone using a controlled technique that avoids structural damage and hidden utilities.
  • EL-032 — Route NM cable through framing between designated boxes according to the approved circuit plan.
  • EL-033 — Protect cable from physical damage using bushings, protection plates, and correct routing distance from the framing face.
  • EL-034 — Staple and support cable at the correct spacing without flattening, cutting, or otherwise damaging the sheath.
  • EL-035 — Maintain the minimum bend radius and required free conductor length at each box.
  • EL-036 — Inspect the completed rough wiring for route, support, protection, bend radius, and sheath condition, and document the run before concealment.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 established the boxes; Chapter 5 prepared the knockouts and connectors that receive the cable. This project is the physical connection between those boxes — the run that will carry every ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductor to the splices in Chapter 7, the grounding and bonding work in Chapter 8, and every device and fixture installed in Chapters 9 through 15. A cable run that is damaged, undersupported, over-bent, or undocumented cannot be corrected later without opening finished walls, so this chapter's inspection and photography steps are treated as a required, non-optional part of the work rather than an afterthought.

The de-energized work condition and live-dead-live discipline established in Chapter 1 governs this entire chapter. Every associated circuit and nearby conductor must be verified de-energized before drilling or routing begins, exactly as in the earlier projects in this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the approved circuit plan, cable type and size, box locations, route, conductor count, derating conditions, fireblocking, and inspection requirements
  • verifying that all associated circuits and nearby conductors are de-energized before drilling or routing
  • inspecting framing for structural restrictions, engineered members, plumbing, ducts, gas piping, blocking, and previous wiring
  • marking the cable route and each framing penetration, and confirming that holes are within the permitted drilling zone
  • drilling straight, smooth holes of the correct size, removing splinters, and installing bushings or edge protection where required
  • pulling, routing, and securing NM cable between the designated boxes without exceeding minimum bend radius, damaging sheath, or leaving inadequate free conductor length
  • installing steel protection plates and approved fireblocking materials where required
  • labeling both cable ends and photographing and documenting the completed route before concealment

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • drill engineered lumber, trusses, headers, beams, or other restricted structural members without engineering or manufacturer direction obtained first
  • route or terminate cable on an energized circuit, or assume de-energization without the live-dead-live verification established in Chapter 1
  • conceal, fireblock, or cover any cable run before required rough inspection is complete
  • continue routing cable through framing showing structural damage, unidentified conductors, moisture, or previous wiring not accounted for in the circuit plan

Any framing condition, drilling restriction, or cable damage identified during this project must be corrected, repaired, or escalated to a qualified professional before the run is concealed.

Trade Note

The permitted drilling zone, minimum bend radius, cable-support spacing, and box-entry free-length requirement are set by the adopted electrical code and the cable manufacturer's instructions, not by convenience of the route. A route that is faster to run but crosses an unapproved drilling zone or violates the minimum bend radius is not an acceptable substitute, even if the cable appears undamaged after installation.

Setup Standard

Do not drill any framing member or route any cable until the associated circuit has been verified de-energized, the route and every framing penetration has been marked, and each hole location has been confirmed within the permitted drilling zone for that framing member. Work started before these confirmations are complete must stop immediately.

Shop Tip

Control the opposite side of every drilling location before the bit breaks through. A hidden pipe, duct, or occupant on the far side of a stud or joist is invisible from the drilling side until the bit has already caused damage or contact.

Drilling Zones, Engineered Members, and Utility Control

Every hole drilled in this chapter must fall within the permitted zone for the framing member type being drilled. Engineered lumber, trusses, headers, beams, or other restricted structural members require engineering or manufacturer direction before any hole is drilled; do not assume an engineered member may be drilled using the same zone as dimensional lumber. Before drilling, control the opposite side of every location to prevent contact with hidden utilities or people on the far side of the framing member.

Measurement Cue — Confirm the Permitted Drilling Zone Before Every Hole

Confirm that each marked hole location is within the permitted drilling zone for that specific framing member before drilling. Do not drill a location that is faster or more convenient if it falls outside the permitted zone, and do not estimate the zone from memory when the framing member type has not been confirmed.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify De-Energization Before Drilling or Routing Cable

Verify that all associated circuits and nearby conductors are de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1 before drilling any framing member or routing any cable. Do not assume a circuit is de-energized because a related breaker was opened for a different project. Treat every existing cable encountered during drilling as energized until it has been positively identified and verified.

Field Safety Cue — Control the Far Side of Every Drilled Hole

Control the opposite side of each drilling location before the bit breaks through, to prevent contact with hidden utilities or people on the far side. Stop drilling immediately if the bit encounters unexpected resistance, metal, or a change in material that does not match the expected framing member.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Engineered Lumber and Restricted Structural Members

Obtain engineering or manufacturer direction before drilling engineered lumber, trusses, headers, beams, or other restricted structural members. A permitted zone for dimensional lumber does not apply to an engineered member, and drilling an engineered member outside its approved zone is a stop-work condition requiring engineering review before continuing.

E. Plan Review and De-Energization

  1. Review the approved circuit plan, cable type and size, box locations, route, conductor count, derating conditions, fireblocking, and inspection requirements. Confirm the route matches the boxes installed in Chapters 3 and 4 and the connectors prepared in Chapter 5.
  2. Verify that all associated circuits and nearby conductors are de-energized where required. Use the live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1 at every box and panel location affected by the route.

F. Framing Inspection and Route Marking

  1. Inspect framing for structural restrictions, engineered members, plumbing, ducts, gas piping, blocking, and previous wiring. Record every restriction or existing utility found along the planned route.
  2. Mark the cable route and each framing penetration. Use the approved circuit plan as the controlling reference for every mark.
  3. Confirm that holes are within the permitted drilling zone for each framing member. Reject and re-mark any location that falls outside the permitted zone before drilling begins.
  4. Obtain engineering or manufacturer direction before drilling engineered lumber, trusses, headers, beams, or restricted structural members. Do not drill a restricted member without this direction, regardless of route convenience.

G. Controlled Drilling

  1. Select the correct drill bit and inspect the drill. Confirm the bit size matches the approved cable count without excessive clearance.
  2. Control the opposite side of each drilling location to prevent contact with hidden utilities or people. Do not proceed until the far side is confirmed clear.
  3. Drill straight, smooth holes large enough for the approved cable count without excessive clearance. Stop immediately if the bit encounters unexpected resistance or material.
  4. Remove splinters and inspect holes for sharp edges. Correct any hole with a sharp or damaged edge before routing cable through it.
  5. Install bushings or edge protection where required. Confirm the bushing or protection is seated and will not be displaced during cable pulling.

H. Cable Pulling and Routing

  1. Pull cable from the reel in a manner that prevents twists, kinks, crushing, and abrasion. Inspect the cable continuously during the pull for any developing damage.
  2. Route the cable through framing without exceeding the minimum bend radius. Confirm every bend against the manufacturer's and code-specified minimum before proceeding to the next section.
  3. Leave the required free conductor length at each box. Measure and mark the required length before cutting the cable at each box location.

I. Box Entry and Support

  1. Insert the cable through the listed box connector or integral clamp without damaging the sheath. Confirm the connector installed in Chapter 5 accepts the cable without forcing.
  2. Maintain sheath inside the box as required. Confirm the sheath extends to the point required by the connector and box instructions.
  3. Secure cable with listed staples or supports without flattening or cutting the sheath. Inspect each staple immediately after driving it for correct seating and sheath condition.
  4. Maintain required spacing from box entries and along the cable route. Measure staple spacing against the code- and product-controlled interval rather than estimating by eye.

J. Protection, Fireblocking, and Documentation

  1. Install steel protection plates where cable is too close to the framing face. Confirm plate placement covers the full width of the shallow run.
  2. Maintain separation from heat sources, sharp edges, access openings, and incompatible systems. Correct any route segment that fails this separation before continuing.
  3. Seal fireblocking penetrations only with the approved system and after required inspection stages. Do not fireblock a penetration before the associated rough inspection has passed.
  4. Label both cable ends by circuit and destination. Confirm labels remain legible and attached.
  5. Inspect cable type, route, support, bend radius, protection plates, box entry, free length, and sheath condition. Correct any item that fails inspection before proceeding to photography.
  6. Replace damaged cable rather than covering cuts, crushed sections, or severe kinks. Do not attempt a field repair on damaged NM cable insulation or sheath.
  7. Photograph and document the route before concealment. Complete every field on the Rough-Wiring Route Record before the run is fireblocked, closed, or covered.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed cable run using the same checks regardless of which circuit or route the project involves.

  • De-energization check: confirm all associated circuits and nearby conductors were verified de-energized before drilling or routing began.
  • Drilling-zone check: confirm every hole was located within the permitted drilling zone for its framing member type, with engineered members drilled only under engineering direction.
  • Cable-condition check: confirm the cable is free of twists, kinks, crushing, and abrasion along the entire route.
  • Bend-radius check: confirm no bend along the route is smaller than the specified minimum.
  • Protection check: confirm bushings and steel plates are installed wherever required by clearance or edge condition.
  • Support check: confirm staples are installed at the required spacing without flattening or cutting the sheath.
  • Box-entry check: confirm the correct free conductor length and undamaged sheath at every box.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Rough-Wiring Route Record and photographs are complete before concealment.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Drilled Hole Is Found Outside the Permitted Zone

Probable cause: the route was marked without confirming the permitted zone for the specific framing member type, or an engineered member was treated as dimensional lumber.
Confirmation: measure the hole location against the permitted zone for the actual framing member type and identify whether the member is engineered.
Correction: stop routing cable through the affected hole; obtain engineering or manufacturer direction for an engineered member, or reroute through a correctly located hole for dimensional lumber; repair the framing member if required by that direction.
Reinspection: confirm the corrected or relocated hole against the permitted zone before routing cable through it.

The Cable Shows a Kink, Crush, or Abrasion During or After Pulling

Probable cause: the cable was pulled too quickly, dragged around a sharp edge, or crushed against framing during routing.
Confirmation: inspect the damaged section closely under adequate light and compare it against the acceptance standard for sheath and insulation condition.
Correction: cut out and replace the damaged section rather than covering it; do not conceal a kinked, crushed, or abraded section under the assumption that the conductors inside remain undamaged.
Reinspection: repeat the cable-condition check along the full replaced section before proceeding.

A Bend Along the Route Is Smaller Than the Minimum Bend Radius

Probable cause: the route was pulled tightly around a framing corner or box entry without accounting for the minimum bend radius.
Confirmation: measure the bend radius at the identified location against the manufacturer's and code-specified minimum.
Correction: loosen and reroute the cable through the location to achieve the minimum bend radius, adding a support point if needed to maintain the corrected path.
Reinspection: re-measure the bend radius at the corrected location and recheck adjacent support points.

A Staple Is Found Flattening or Cutting the Sheath

Probable cause: the staple was overdriven, the wrong staple size was used, or the cable was not seated flat before stapling.
Confirmation: inspect the sheath directly beneath the staple for compression, cutting, or discoloration.
Correction: remove the staple, inspect the underlying cable for damage, replace the section if the sheath or conductors are damaged, and reinstall a correctly sized staple seated without over-driving.
Reinspection: repeat the support check at the corrected location and inspect adjacent staples for the same error.

Insufficient Free Conductor Length Is Found at a Box

Probable cause: the cable was cut before the required free length was measured and marked, or the connector consumed more cable than expected.
Confirmation: measure the free conductor length actually present against the required length for the box and connector type.
Correction: if sufficient slack exists along the route, pull additional cable through to the box; if not, replace the affected cable segment back to a point where the required free length can be achieved.
Reinspection: re-measure free conductor length at the corrected box before proceeding to splicing.

An Unidentified Existing Cable or Utility Is Encountered While Drilling or Routing

Probable cause: the framing inspection in Step F did not identify an existing cable, pipe, or duct concealed within the framing bay.
Confirmation: stop work immediately and inspect the encountered utility directly without assuming its condition or function.
Correction: treat the unidentified utility as energized or active until positively identified and controlled; escalate to a qualified professional if it cannot be safely identified.
Reinspection: repeat the framing inspection for the full remaining route before resuming drilling.

Cleanup

This project ends with the cable run protected, supported, and documented, and the circuit remaining de-energized and controlled until the assigned splicing project begins.

  • Confirm all cable ends remain labeled by circuit and destination
  • Confirm all staples, protection plates, and bushings remain correctly seated along the route
  • Remove drilling debris and splinters from the work area and dispose of them appropriately
  • Store the drill, cutting tools, and cable reel in a protected, undamaged condition
  • Store the Rough-Wiring Route Record and photographs with the project records for reference by later chapters

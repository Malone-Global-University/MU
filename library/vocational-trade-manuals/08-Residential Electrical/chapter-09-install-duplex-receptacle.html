Install a Duplex Receptacle

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to terminate, mount, and test one listed duplex receptacle on a branch circuit that has already been isolated, spliced, pigtailed, bonded, and grounded in earlier chapters. The finished work product is one installed, covered, and successfully tested duplex receptacle, plus a completed installation and test record.

You will verify the circuit is de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1, inspect the box and conductors prepared in Chapters 3 through 8, identify the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors, terminate each conductor at the correct receptacle terminal using a listed method and the manufacturer-specified torque, mount the device and install an intact cover plate, restore power using the approved sequence, and test the finished outlet with a listed plug-in tester to confirm polarity and grounding.

This project depends directly on Chapter 1's verified de-energization procedure, Chapter 7's splicing and pigtailing, and Chapter 8's grounding and bonding work. It is the first device-termination project in the book and establishes the terminal-identification and torque discipline used again in Chapters 10 through 15.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-049 — Identify the line, or ungrounded, conductor at a receptacle installation from approved circuit documentation.
  • EL-050 — Prepare receptacle terminals and conductors to the device-controlled strip length without damaging copper.
  • EL-051 — Connect the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors to the correct receptacle terminals using a listed termination method.
  • EL-052 — Install the receptacle in the box in correct vertical alignment and fasten the yoke without stressing terminals.
  • EL-053 — Install an intact cover plate with no gap exposing energized parts.
  • EL-054 — Test receptacle operation, polarity, and grounding using a listed plug-in tester after restoration.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior chapter in this book prepared the conditions this project depends on: Chapter 1 established the verified de-energization procedure used again here; Chapters 3 and 4 installed the box; Chapters 5 and 6 brought cable into the box with correct connectors and support; Chapter 7 stripped, spliced, and pigtailed the conductors; and Chapter 8 bonded the box and verified the grounding path. This project is the first point in the book where a finished device is terminated, mounted, covered, and placed into service.

The terminal-identification, torque, and test discipline established here is reused directly in Chapter 10 for the GFCI receptacle, Chapter 11 for the switch and fixture, and Chapter 12 for the three-way switching circuit.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • verifying de-energization of the assigned branch circuit using the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure
  • identifying the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors from approved circuit documentation
  • preparing device pigtails where the project requires pass-through continuity independent of the receptacle
  • terminating conductors at the correct receptacle terminals using a listed method and manufacturer-specified torque
  • mounting the receptacle and installing an intact cover plate
  • restoring power using the approved sequence and testing the finished receptacle with a listed plug-in tester

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • work on the receptacle while the circuit is energized, except for the final restoration and testing steps performed after all terminations and the cover plate are complete
  • substitute an unlisted receptacle, connector, or terminal method not specified by the device manufacturer
  • place more conductors under a terminal than the terminal is listed to accept
  • energize the circuit before the required inspection, where an inspection is required by the assigned project or jurisdiction

Any box, conductor, or grounding condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected before the receptacle is energized and tested.

Trade Note

A receptacle installed with reversed line and neutral conductors, or with an open equipment-grounding path, can appear to work normally when a lamp or simple appliance is plugged in. Only a listed plug-in tester reliably detects these conditions. Never rely on the receptacle "working" as proof that it is correctly wired.

Setup Standard

Do not begin terminating conductors until the circuit has been verified de-energized using the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure, the box and conductors have passed the inspections from Chapters 3 through 8, and the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors have been positively identified from approved circuit documentation.

Shop Tip

Form each terminal loop so the hook closes in the same direction the screw tightens. A loop formed the wrong way pushes the conductor out from under the screw head as it is tightened, producing a connection that looks secure but is not.

Common Field Fundamentals

Correct receptacle installation depends on positively identifying each conductor before it reaches a terminal, using the terminal method the device is listed for, and never leaving a connection to chance because the receptacle "will probably work anyway."

Terminal Identification and Torque

Identify the line, neutral, and grounding terminals by their markings and color before connecting any conductor, and do not rely on the physical position of the terminal alone. Use only terminal methods listed for the conductor material and size, and tighten every terminal to the manufacturer-specified torque using a calibrated screwdriver or an approved torque method. A terminal tightened by feel alone is not a verified connection.

Measurement Cue — Verify Conductor Identity Before Every Termination

Confirm the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductor identity from the approved circuit documentation before connecting any conductor to a terminal. Do not assume conductor function from cable color alone where the documentation indicates otherwise.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify De-Energization Before Terminating

Do not begin stripping or terminating conductors until the circuit has been verified de-energized using the complete live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1. Do not restore power until all terminations, the cover plate, and the required inspection are complete.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Terminal Method and Torque

Use only the terminal methods listed for the receptacle and the conductor material and size, and tighten every terminal to the value specified in the receptacle's listed installation instructions. Do not place more than one conductor under a terminal unless the terminal is specifically listed for that number and conductor type.

Field Safety Cue — Fold Conductors Without Stress

Fold conductors into the box using controlled bends rather than force, keeping splices and pigtails organized behind the device. A conductor forced or crimped into the box can loosen a terminal or damage insulation without visible signs until the circuit is energized.

E. Pre-Termination Review

  1. Review the circuit voltage, conductor size and material, receptacle rating, grounding method, box fill, device location, and protection requirements. Confirm the receptacle rating matches the branch circuit before opening the package.
  2. Verify the circuit is de-energized using the approved live-dead-live procedure. Do not proceed until this verification is complete and documented.

F. Box and Conductor Inspection

  1. Inspect the box, conductors, grounding path, cable entries, and required free conductor length. Confirm the box and grounding path passed inspection in Chapters 3 through 8 before proceeding.
  2. Identify the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors from the approved circuit documentation. Label any conductor whose function is not obvious.

G. Conductor Preparation

  1. Prepare device pigtails where the project requires pass-through continuity independent of the receptacle. Use listed connectors sized for the conductor combination.
  2. Strip conductors to the device-controlled length without damaging copper. Inspect each stripped end under adequate light before terminating.
  3. Inspect the receptacle for broken parts, incorrect rating, altered tabs, or damaged terminals. Do not install a damaged or incorrectly rated device.

H. Termination

  1. Use only terminal methods listed for the conductor material and size. Confirm the method against the receptacle's listed instructions before connecting any conductor.
  2. Connect the equipment-grounding conductor to the green grounding terminal. Confirm this connection before proceeding to the neutral and line terminals.
  3. Connect the grounded conductor to the identified neutral terminal. Confirm terminal identity by its marking and color, not by physical position alone.
  4. Connect the ungrounded conductor to the identified line terminal. Confirm terminal identity by its marking and color before tightening.
  5. Orient terminal loops in the tightening direction when side-screw connections are used. Form each loop so it closes rather than opens as the screw is tightened.
  6. Tighten terminals using the manufacturer-specified torque or approved torque method. Record the torque method used on the worksheet.

I. Termination Inspection and Box Assembly

  1. Inspect for exposed copper, loose strands, insulation under terminals, or multiple conductors under unlisted terminals. Correct any deficiency before folding conductors into the box.
  2. Fold conductors into the box without stressing terminals or damaging insulation. Arrange splices and pigtails behind the device in an organized, non-crowded pattern.

J. Mounting and Cover Installation

  1. Align the receptacle vertically and fasten the yoke to the box. Confirm alignment before final tightening of the mounting screws.
  2. Do not use the cover plate to pull a misaligned device into position. Correct alignment at the yoke, not at the cover plate.
  3. Install an intact cover plate with no gap exposing energized parts. Confirm the cover seats fully against the wall or box surface.

K. Inspection, Restoration, and Functional Test

  1. Complete the required inspection before energization. Do not restore power to an uninspected installation where inspection is required.
  2. Remove tools and restore power using the approved restoration sequence. Confirm the work area is clear before re-closing the breaker.
  3. Test the receptacle with a listed plug-in tester. Confirm the tester indicates correct wiring with no fault.
  4. Verify voltage and polarity using the approved method where required. Record the measured result on the worksheet.
  5. Operate any upstream protection and confirm restoration. Confirm the receptacle remains powered after the upstream device is reset.
  6. De-energize and correct any failed test before retesting. Do not leave a failed receptacle energized while troubleshooting.
  7. Label the circuit and document installation and test results. Complete every field on the Installation and Test Worksheet.

Common Receptacle Inspection

Inspect every completed receptacle installation using the same checks regardless of location.

  • Conductor identity check: confirm the ungrounded, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors were correctly identified before termination.
  • Termination check: confirm no exposed copper, loose strands, or insulation trapped under a terminal.
  • Torque check: confirm every terminal was tightened to the manufacturer-specified torque.
  • Mounting check: confirm the device is vertically aligned and fastened without terminal stress.
  • Cover check: confirm the cover plate is intact and fully seated with no exposed energized parts.
  • Functional test check: confirm the listed plug-in tester shows correct polarity and grounding.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Installation and Test Worksheet is fully completed.

Complete the Installation and Test Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 10.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Plug-In Tester Indicates Reversed Polarity

Probable cause: the ungrounded and grounded conductors were connected to the wrong terminals.
Confirmation: de-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, and trace each conductor from the terminal back to its identified function in the circuit documentation.
Correction: disconnect and reterminate the conductors at the correct line and neutral terminals using the manufacturer-specified torque.
Reinspection: restore power and retest with the listed plug-in tester.

The Plug-In Tester Indicates an Open Ground

Probable cause: the equipment-grounding conductor was not connected, the grounding pigtail was omitted, or the grounding path from Chapter 8 was disturbed during termination.
Confirmation: de-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, and inspect the grounding terminal, pigtail, and box-bonding connection.
Correction: reconnect or replace the equipment-grounding conductor and confirm continuity using the de-energized continuity test from Chapter 8.
Reinspection: restore power and retest with the listed plug-in tester.

The Receptacle Has No Power After Restoration

Probable cause: a loose or incomplete termination, an upstream splice that opened during box assembly, or the upstream protective device did not reset.
Confirmation: de-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, and inspect every termination and splice in the circuit path back to the panel.
Correction: reterminate any loose or incomplete connection and confirm the upstream protective device is fully reset.
Reinspection: restore power and retest the receptacle with the listed plug-in tester.

A Terminal Is Loose or a Conductor Pulls Free Under Light Tension

Probable cause: the terminal was not tightened to the manufacturer-specified torque, the terminal loop was formed in the wrong direction, or insulation was trapped under the screw.
Confirmation: de-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, and inspect the terminal and conductor end directly.
Correction: reform the terminal loop in the correct tightening direction, remove any trapped insulation, and retighten to the manufacturer-specified torque.
Reinspection: repeat the conductor pull check and retest after restoration.

The Cover Plate Does Not Seat Flush or Leaves a Gap

Probable cause: the receptacle is misaligned in the box, the box is set at the wrong depth, or the wrong cover plate was selected.
Confirmation: de-energize the circuit, remove the cover plate, and inspect the device alignment and box depth against the finished wall surface.
Correction: realign the device at the yoke, correct the box depth if required, and install the correct cover plate. Do not force a gapped cover plate into place.
Reinspection: reinstall the cover plate and confirm a flush, gap-free fit before restoring power.

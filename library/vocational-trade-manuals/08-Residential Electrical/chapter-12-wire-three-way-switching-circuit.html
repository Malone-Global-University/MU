Job Overview

This project qualifies you to wire a complete three-way switching circuit: two three-way switches controlling one light fixture from two separate locations. The finished work product is one tested three-way circuit in which the light can be turned on and off from either switch location in every switch-position combination, with grounding continuous throughout and every conductor correctly identified by terminal marking rather than by color or handle position alone.

You will review the approved three-way diagram and power-entry point; verify the circuit is de-energized using the live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1; inspect both switch boxes and the fixture box; label the supply conductor, switched leg, two travelers, grounded conductors, and equipment-grounding conductors; identify each switch's common terminal by its marking rather than by physical position; prepare and bond grounding conductors; make the required terminations at both switches and the fixture; and test all four switch-position combinations after restoration.

This project depends directly on the isolation and verification procedure from Chapter 1, the circuit-mapping and labeling discipline from Chapter 2, the splicing and pigtailing methods from Chapter 7, and the grounding and bonding procedure from Chapter 8. It extends the single-pole switching skill from Chapter 11 to a two-location circuit where a common terminal and two traveler terminals must be correctly distinguished at each switch. Chapter 20's capstone practice wall includes this exact three-way circuit as one of its required subsystems.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • EL-067 — Identify the traveler conductors and distinguish them from the common and switched-leg conductors using the approved circuit diagram.
  • EL-068 — Wire two three-way switches according to the approved diagram, including common, traveler, and grounding terminations.
  • EL-069 — Connect each switch's common terminal correctly, identifying it by its marking or screw color rather than by physical position alone.
  • EL-070 — Route cable between the two switch boxes and the fixture box while maintaining conductor identification.
  • EL-071 — Test all four switch-position combinations and confirm the light operates correctly from either location.
  • EL-072 — Troubleshoot incorrect switching by de-energizing the circuit and re-verifying common and traveler identification against the diagram.

Where This Skill Fits

Three-way switching is one of the most commonly misdiagnosed residential circuits because the common terminal on a three-way switch is not always in the same physical position from one manufacturer to the next, and because two traveler conductors that are correctly connected but swapped between boxes will still allow the switches to operate — just not correctly from every position. Identifying the common terminal by its marking, and keeping traveler identification consistent at both boxes, is the skill that prevents the "sometimes works, sometimes doesn't" symptom that this chapter's troubleshooting section addresses directly.

This project reuses the de-energized live-dead-live procedure from Chapter 1, the splicing and pigtailing methods from Chapter 7, and the grounding and bonding procedure from Chapter 8. It extends the single-pole switch loop taught in Chapter 11 to a two-switch, two-traveler circuit and prepares the learner for the capstone practice wall in Chapter 20, which includes a required three-way switching subsystem wired to this same standard.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the approved three-way diagram, power-entry location, fixture connection, cable types, conductor identification, grounding, and box-fill calculations
  • de-energizing and verifying the circuit using the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure
  • inspecting both switch boxes, the fixture box, cable routes, and conductor lengths
  • labeling the supply ungrounded conductor, switched leg, two travelers, grounded conductors, and equipment-grounding conductors
  • identifying each switch's common terminal by its marking or screw color rather than by physical position alone
  • preparing and bonding grounding conductors and metal boxes according to Chapter 8
  • splicing grounded conductors according to the approved diagram without connecting them to ordinary three-way switch terminals
  • connecting the supply and switched-leg conductors to the correct common terminals, and the traveler conductors to the traveler terminals on both switches
  • terminating switch grounding terminals and the fixture's switched, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors
  • testing all four switch-position combinations after restoration and troubleshooting incorrect switching through de-energized reverification

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • skip the Chapter 1 live-dead-live verification before opening either switch box or the fixture box
  • assume switch handle position indicates on or off, or assume common-terminal position from memory rather than from the marking on the specific switch in hand
  • connect a grounded conductor to an ordinary three-way switch terminal under any circumstance
  • energize the circuit for testing before grounding continuity, box fill, and all required terminations are inspected and complete
  • proceed to Chapter 13 or later chapters with an unresolved switching fault left undocumented

Any conductor identification that cannot be confirmed against the approved diagram, or any switch, box, or cable condition outside this scope, is a stop-work condition requiring correction or escalation to a qualified electrical professional before work continues.

Trade Note

A three-way switch has three terminals: one common terminal, usually marked "COM" or a darker screw color, and two traveler terminals, usually brass-colored and interchangeable with each other but never with the common. Color alone is not sufficient proof of function — the manufacturer's marking on the specific switch in hand controls identification, and that marking must be verified at every switch in this project, even if a prior project used a different manufacturer's switch.

Setup Standard

Do not remove either switch cover, the fixture cover, or touch any conductor until the circuit has been de-energized and verified dead by the Chapter 1 live-dead-live procedure at both switch boxes and the fixture box. Do not terminate a single conductor until the supply, switched leg, both travelers, grounded, and equipment-grounding conductors have been identified and labeled according to the approved diagram.

Shop Tip

Label both traveler conductors with the same identification at each end of their run before making any termination. A traveler labeled "T1" at one switch box must be traced and labeled "T1" at the other switch box. Guessing traveler identity from color alone, especially when the two travelers are the same cable's red and black conductors, is the single most common cause of a three-way circuit that switches the light on and off from the wrong combination of positions.

Common Field Fundamentals

A three-way switching circuit uses two switches, each with one common terminal and two traveler terminals, to control one fixture from two locations. Power enters at the line-side switch's common terminal. The two traveler conductors run between the traveler terminals of both switches. The load-side switch's common terminal carries the switched leg to the fixture. Because either traveler can be connected to either traveler terminal at both switches without affecting operation — the circuit uses two travelers that are functionally interchangeable with each other, only not with the common — a swapped traveler at one end does not necessarily prevent the light from turning on and off, but it can produce a circuit where the switching sequence at each location does not follow the pattern the occupant expects.

Common-Terminal Identification and Traveler Discipline

Identify each switch's common terminal from its manufacturer marking before making any connection, even if a previous project used a switch from a different manufacturer with the common terminal in a different physical position. Keep the two traveler conductors identified consistently at both boxes using the same label at each end of each traveler's run. Do not rely on handle position to determine whether a three-way switch is in the "on" or "off" state, since a three-way switch's handle position has no fixed relationship to fixture state — only the combination of both switches' positions determines whether the fixture is lit.

Measurement Cue — Identify the Common Terminal by Its Marking, Not by Memory

Before terminating any conductor at a three-way switch, locate and read the manufacturer's marking identifying the common terminal on that specific switch. Do not assume the common terminal is in the same physical position as on a switch used in a prior project.

Critical Safety Cue — Verify De-Energization at Both Switch Boxes and the Fixture Box

Perform the Chapter 1 live-dead-live test at both switch boxes and at the fixture box before touching any conductor. A three-way circuit has conductors entering and leaving at three separate enclosures, and a test performed at only one box does not confirm the condition of conductors at the other two.

Field Safety Cue — Two-Box Coordination

Confirm that any second worker at the other switch box or the fixture box has been briefed on the controlled circuit and lockout before either box is opened. Do not allow independent testing at a second location that has not confirmed the lockout is still in place.

Code Alert — Grounded Conductor Never on an Ordinary Three-Way Switch Terminal

Splice grounded conductors continuously through the switch boxes to the fixture. Do not land a grounded conductor on an ordinary single-pole or three-way switch terminal under any circumstance. Verify the approved diagram before making any grounded-conductor connection.

E. Diagram Review and De-Energization

  1. Review the approved three-way diagram, power-entry location, fixture connection, cable types, conductor identification, grounding, and box-fill calculations. Confirm the diagram identifies the line-side switch, load-side switch, and fixture unambiguously before continuing.
  2. Verify the circuit is de-energized using the approved live-dead-live procedure. Perform the complete Chapter 1 sequence at both switch boxes and the fixture box before opening any cover.

F. Box Inspection and Conductor Labeling

  1. Inspect both switch boxes, fixture box, cable routes, and conductor lengths. Confirm sufficient free conductor length is present at each box for the required terminations.
  2. Label the supply ungrounded conductor, switched leg, two travelers, grounded conductors, and equipment-grounding conductors. Apply the same traveler label at both ends of each traveler run before making any connection.
  3. Identify each switch common terminal by its marking or screw color rather than by physical position alone. Confirm the marking on the specific switch in hand before proceeding, regardless of prior experience with a different manufacturer's device.

G. Grounding Preparation

  1. Prepare and bond grounding conductors and metal boxes. Follow the Chapter 8 bonding procedure at both switch boxes and the fixture box.
  2. Splice grounded conductors according to the approved diagram without connecting them to ordinary three-way switch terminals. Confirm the splice provides continuous neutral to the fixture independent of either switch.

H. Switch and Traveler Termination

  1. Connect the supply ungrounded conductor to the common terminal of the line-side switch. Confirm the terminal identity against the manufacturer's marking immediately before terminating.
  2. Connect the switched-leg conductor to the common terminal of the load-side switch. Confirm the terminal identity against the manufacturer's marking immediately before terminating.
  3. Connect the two traveler conductors to the traveler terminals on both switches. Confirm each traveler's label matches at both ends before terminating.
  4. Maintain traveler identification at both boxes. Do not proceed to the next box until both travelers are confirmed correctly labeled and landed at the first box.
  5. Connect switch grounding terminals. Terminate the device grounding pigtail at each switch's green grounding terminal.

I. Fixture Termination

  1. At the fixture, connect the switched ungrounded conductor, grounded conductor, and equipment grounding. Confirm the switched-leg conductor is landed at the fixture's ungrounded lead per the diagram.
  2. Tighten all terminals using the manufacturer-controlled method. Use the torque or seating method specified in the switch and fixture instructions.

J. Inspection Before Concealment

  1. Inspect for line-load confusion, incorrect common use, exposed copper, damaged insulation, and box overfill. Correct any deficiency before folding conductors into either box.
  2. Fold conductors and mount both switches in a consistent physical orientation without assuming handle position indicates on or off. Confirm both switches are mounted square to their boxes before installing cover plates.
  3. Install cover plates and the fixture. Confirm no gap exposes energized parts once covers are installed.
  4. Complete required inspection before energization. Do not restore power until the rough and device inspection required by the project scope is complete.

K. Restoration and Position Testing

  1. Restore power and test all four combinations of switch positions. Operate both switches through every combination and observe the fixture at each combination.
  2. Confirm the light can be turned on and off from either location. Confirm the fixture responds correctly to a change at either switch regardless of the other switch's position.
  3. If operation is incorrect, de-energize and verify common and traveler identification against the diagram. Do not attempt a correction before de-energizing and reverifying with the live-dead-live procedure.
  4. Correct only after repeating absence-of-voltage verification. Reopen only the box containing the suspected misidentified conductor.
  5. Retest all positions and document the final circuit. Confirm all four combinations operate correctly before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect the completed three-way circuit using the same checks regardless of switch manufacturer or box type.

  • Common-terminal check: confirm each switch's common terminal was identified by its manufacturer marking before termination.
  • Traveler check: confirm both traveler conductors are labeled identically at each end and correctly landed at both switches.
  • Grounded-conductor check: confirm no grounded conductor is landed on an ordinary three-way switch terminal.
  • Grounding continuity check: confirm continuity from both switch yokes and metal boxes to the branch equipment-grounding path.
  • Termination quality check: confirm no exposed copper, loose strands, or under-torqued terminals at either switch or the fixture.
  • Functional check: confirm all four switch-position combinations operate the fixture correctly.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Three-Way Circuit Worksheet is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Light Only Turns On or Off from One Location

Probable cause: a traveler conductor is landed on a common terminal at one switch, or the supply or switched-leg conductor is landed on a traveler terminal.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify absence of voltage, and trace each conductor at both switches against the approved diagram, checking each termination against the switch's marked common and traveler terminals.
Correction: relocate the misidentified conductor to the terminal required by the diagram and re-tighten to the manufacturer-specified method.
Reinspection: repeat the live-dead-live test, restore power, and retest all four switch-position combinations.

The Switching Sequence Is Reversed at One Location

Probable cause: the two traveler conductors are swapped between the traveler terminals at one switch relative to the other.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify absence of voltage, and check that the traveler labeled at one switch matches the same physical conductor at the other switch's corresponding traveler terminal.
Correction: swap the two traveler conductors at one switch's traveler terminals so labeling and function are consistent at both boxes.
Reinspection: repeat the live-dead-live test, restore power, and retest all four switch-position combinations.

The Fixture Does Not Operate from Either Location

Probable cause: an open supply conductor, a failed splice in the grounded-conductor path, or a common terminal that was not actually terminated at one switch.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify absence of voltage, and use the Chapter 17 continuity-testing method to trace the supply and grounded-conductor paths from source to fixture.
Correction: repair or re-terminate the open connection identified by the continuity test.
Reinspection: repeat the live-dead-live test, restore power, and retest all four switch-position combinations.

A Switch Feels Loose or the Fixture Flickers Under Load

Probable cause: an under-torqued terminal, a conductor not fully seated under a screw terminal, or a loose grounding connection at one box.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify absence of voltage, and inspect each terminal at both switches and the fixture for seating and torque.
Correction: re-terminate the loose connection using the manufacturer-specified torque or seating method.
Reinspection: repeat the live-dead-live test, restore power, and retest all four switch-position combinations.

Continuity Testing Shows Unexpected Continuity Between Conductors

Probable cause: a grounded conductor incorrectly bonded to the equipment-grounding path at a point other than the approved bonding location, or a pinched conductor contacting a metal box.
Confirmation: de-energize, verify absence of voltage, and inspect both boxes for pinched insulation or an unintended bonding point.
Correction: correct the pinch point or remove the unauthorized bonding connection, restoring isolation between the grounded and equipment-grounding conductors as required outside the service bonding point.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity test, the live-dead-live test, restore power, and retest all four switch-position combinations.

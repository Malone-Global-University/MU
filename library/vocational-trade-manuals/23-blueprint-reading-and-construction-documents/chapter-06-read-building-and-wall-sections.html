Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to interpret building sections and wall sections for one residential project and convert them into a controlled assembly interpretation sheet before any layer, member, or transition is treated as field-ready information. The finished work product is one assembly interpretation table, one set of authorized annotated excerpts, and one transition checklist covering foundation-to-wall, floor-line, opening, roof-edge, roof-wall, and penetration conditions, all built from the current approved building and wall sections received for one project.

You will locate each section cut on the plan, verify the exact path it crosses, trace the assembly from foundation through roof, identify structural members and their governing schedules, record wall and roof and floor layers in the documented order, trace continuity of water, air, thermal, and structural control layers, and follow every applicable detail reference before recording a layer sequence, function, or transition as confirmed.

This chapter builds directly on the navigation package from Chapter 1, the site-control sheet from Chapter 2, the foundation checklist from Chapter 3, the room-by-room layout from Chapter 4, and the exterior-elevation checklist from Chapter 5. The elevations located in Chapter 5 provide the heights and roof geometry that the sections in this chapter must match. The assembly interpretation produced here is then used in Chapter 7 to convert structural framing plans into a framing-scope checklist, and again in Chapter 15 when envelope and flashing details are read in full.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-031 — Interpret section cuts by locating each cut on the plan and verifying the exact path and conditions it crosses.
  • BP-032 — Identify floor-to-roof assemblies by tracing the foundation, floor, wall, ceiling, attic, and roof assemblies shown in a section.
  • BP-033 — Read wall layers by recording exterior wall layers in documented order from interior finish to exterior finish.
  • BP-034 — Identify structural members shown in a section and match their tags to framing plans, schedules, and details.
  • BP-035 — Locate insulation and air barriers and trace the continuity of water, air, thermal, and vapor control layers through the section.
  • BP-036 — Create an assembly interpretation sheet documenting layer order, material, function, support, attachment reference, continuity requirement, and governing source.

Where This Skill Fits

A building section or wall section is where the architectural, structural, and envelope information from every other sheet in the package comes together in one vertical view. Chapter 3 established the foundation systems; Chapter 4 established the floor plan and room layout; Chapter 5 established exterior heights, roof forms, and grade relationships. This chapter is the point where those separate readings are confirmed against a single coordinated vertical cut through the building.

The assembly interpretation produced here becomes the reference document Chapter 7 uses to convert structural framing information into a scope checklist, and the document Chapter 15 expands into a full envelope-detail checklist. Any layer, member, or transition left unresolved in this chapter carries forward as an open item into both of those later chapters.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • locating each section cut on the plan and verifying the exact path it crosses
  • distinguishing building sections, wall sections, enlarged sections, and construction details
  • tracing the foundation, floor, wall, ceiling, attic, and roof assemblies shown
  • identifying structural members and matching their tags to framing plans, schedules, and details
  • recording exterior wall, roof, and floor layers in documented order
  • locating water-resistive barriers, air barriers, vapor retarders, flashings, and transition membranes
  • tracing continuity of water, air, thermal, and structural control layers at transitions
  • producing an assembly interpretation table and a transition checklist

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • assume that one membrane performs every control function unless the documents state it
  • infer a layer, thickness, or continuity condition that is not shown in the section or a referenced detail
  • apply a typical detail to a location or condition the documents do not identify as typical for that condition
  • resolve a discontinuous membrane, unsupported component, or conflicting elevation through field judgment rather than the RFI process

Any layer, member, transition, or reference that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the missing-information and document-request list rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A section is a single vertical slice through the building at one exact location. A detail labeled "typical" describes a repeated condition, not every condition. Confirm the section location and the detail's stated area of application before treating either as governing a specific point in the building.

Setup Standard

Do not begin tracing an assembly (Step 6 of the Assembly Tracing phase) until the section cut has been located on the plan and its exact path confirmed in Steps 2 and 3. An assembly traced from an unverified section location must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

When a wall section and a building section both show the same wall, compare them before recording layers. The building section may simplify a condition that the wall section shows in full detail, or the reverse; use the more detailed and more specific view as the governing source and record which one you used.

Reference Conventions

Establish these definitions before beginning the assembly interpretation table, and apply them consistently across every section in the package.

TermDefinition

Common Field Fundamentals

Every section in this chapter is read the same way regardless of which wall, floor line, or roof condition it shows: locate the cut, confirm what it crosses, trace the assembly from foundation to roof, identify the members and layers in the order the documents show them, and trace continuity only where the documents show it.

Reading a Building Section Versus a Wall Section

A building section shows the overall relationship between floors, the roof, and the foundation but may not show every layer at legible scale. A wall section is drawn at a larger scale specifically to show layer detail. When a wall section exists for a condition also shown in a building section, use the wall section as the governing source for layer order and thickness, and use the building section to confirm overall heights and the wall section's location within the building.

Scale Warning — Verify Reproduction Size Before Scaling Any Layer Thickness

Layer thicknesses shown in a wall section are frequently close together and easy to misread at an incorrect reproduction size. Confirm the sheet's actual reproduction size against its title block and check the graphic scale before scaling any layer thickness, and rely on a written dimension or specification value wherever one is provided instead of a scaled measurement.

Critical Cue — Do Not Assume One Membrane Performs Every Function

A single membrane or component is sometimes capable of serving as a water-resistive barrier, air barrier, or vapor retarder, but only when the documents or an approved product evaluation state that it does. Do not assume multi-function performance from the layer's position in the assembly. Record only the function stated by the documents, and log an RFI if the assembly's air, water, or vapor strategy is not clearly stated.

Cross-Reference Cue — Confirm a Detail's Area of Application

Before applying any detail, including a detail labeled typical, confirm the exact location, assembly, material, exposure, and rating condition it is meant to serve. A typical eave detail does not automatically apply to a modified eave condition shown elsewhere in the same set. Follow the reference to the detail and back to the section that called it out to confirm the match.

RFI Threshold — Discontinuous or Unsupported Assemblies

Record an impossible or unclear layer sequence, a discontinuous control layer at a transition, an unsupported component, or a conflicting elevation as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not resolve the discontinuity by assuming a connection the documents do not show.

E. Preparation — Confirm Section Identity and Location

  1. Confirm section-sheet numbers, revisions, scales, section identifiers, viewing direction, and source-plan references. Enter these directly on the Section Identity Record for every section that will be used.
  2. Locate each section cut on the plan and verify the exact path through the building. Trace the cut line symbol on the referencing plan sheet and confirm it matches the section sheet's own callout.
  3. Identify which walls, openings, stairs, floors, roofs, and site conditions the cut crosses. Record the list of crossed elements next to the corresponding Section Identity Record.
  4. Distinguish building sections, wall sections, enlarged sections, and construction details. Classify each section sheet by type before beginning the tracing work.

F. Assembly Tracing

  1. Read vertical datums, floor elevations, ceiling heights, plate heights, roof heights, and grade relationships. Compare each value against the Chapter 5 exterior-elevation checklist for the same locations.
  2. Trace the foundation, footing, slab, crawlspace, basement, wall, floor, ceiling, attic, and roof assemblies shown. Work from the base of the section upward, recording each assembly in the order it appears.
  3. Identify structural members including footings, walls, posts, beams, joists, rim members, headers, rafters, trusses, blocking, and sheathing. List every structural member shown in the section.
  4. Match structural tags to framing plans, schedules, and details. Confirm each member tag against the structural documents; log any tag that cannot be matched.

G. Wall, Roof, and Floor Layer Recording

  1. Identify exterior wall layers from interior finish to exterior finish in order. Record each layer as a separate row in the assembly interpretation table.
  2. Record framing, sheathing, membranes, insulation, air space, drainage plane, cladding, and interior finish where shown. Enter the material and thickness for each documented layer.
  3. Identify roof layers including structure, deck, underlayment, insulation, ventilation, roofing, and interior finish where shown. Record each roof layer in the order shown.
  4. Identify floor layers including finish, underlayment, subfloor, framing, insulation, ceiling, and fire or acoustic components where shown. Record each floor layer in the order shown.

H. Control-Layer Continuity

  1. Locate water-resistive barriers, air barriers, vapor retarders, flashings, drainage mats, capillary breaks, sealants, and transition membranes. Record each in the assembly interpretation table with its stated function.
  2. Do not assume that one membrane performs every control function unless the documents state it. Record only the function stated; log an RFI where the function is unclear.
  3. Trace continuity of water, air, thermal, and structural control layers at foundation, floor lines, windows, doors, roof edges, and penetrations. Mark each transition as continuous, discontinuous, or unresolved.
  4. Identify insulation type, location, thickness, and performance references where documented. Record insulation data alongside the corresponding layer entry.
  5. Identify ventilation paths at attics, roofs, rainscreens, crawlspaces, and enclosed cavities. Record each documented ventilation path and its termination.
  6. Locate fire blocking, draft stopping, fire-rated layers, smoke barriers, acoustic seals, and protected penetrations where shown. Record each occurrence with its governing detail reference.

I. Transition and Detail Verification

  1. Identify interior and exterior finish transitions, trim, bases, soffits, fascias, and closure details. Record each transition location in the transition checklist.
  2. Locate window, door, deck, porch, balcony, stair, chimney, and roof-wall intersection details. Cross-reference each to its governing detail sheet.
  3. Follow detail references to enlarged views and specifications. Confirm the enlarged detail matches the section condition it was called from.
  4. Compare sections with floor plans, elevations, foundation plans, roof plans, and structural plans. Verify heights, materials, and member locations are consistent across all compared sheets.
  5. Identify impossible or unclear layer sequences, discontinuous membranes, missing support, or conflicting elevations. Log each on the missing-information and document-request list.
  6. Identify details labeled typical and verify the locations where they are intended to apply. Record the confirmed area of application for each typical detail used.
  7. Mark conditions requiring manufacturer instructions, approved submittals, delegated design, or field verification. Flag each such condition in the transition checklist.

J. Package Closeout

  1. Create an assembly interpretation table listing layer order, material, function, support, attachment reference, continuity requirement, and governing source. Finalize the table built across Sections F through H.
  2. Create enlarged sketches or annotated excerpts only as authorized, preserving original references and revision status. Label each excerpt with its source sheet and revision.
  3. Create a transition checklist for foundation-to-wall, floor-line, opening, roof-edge, roof-wall, and penetration conditions. Consolidate the transition findings from Section I.
  4. Prepare RFI-style questions for missing layers, unclear continuity, conflicting thickness, unsupported components, or unresolved transitions. Draft one neutral question per unresolved item.
  5. Review the assembly sheet for current documents, exact section applicability, and separation of documented facts from interpretation. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete before closing the chapter.

Common Assembly Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which section or wall type is involved.

  • Location check: confirm every section's cut line is verified on its source plan before its assembly is recorded.
  • Completeness check: confirm every assembly layer shown in the section appears in the assembly interpretation table with no omission or merge.
  • Structural check: confirm every structural member shown is matched to a framing-plan tag, schedule, or detail, or logged as unmatched.
  • Continuity check: confirm every water, air, thermal, and structural control layer is marked continuous, discontinuous, or unresolved at each transition.
  • Reference check: confirm every typical detail's area of application has been confirmed for the specific condition it was applied to.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Traced Assembly Does Not Match the Section Cut Line

Probable cause: the assembly was traced from the wrong section, or the cut-line location on the plan was not verified before tracing began.
Confirmation: compare the section identifier and viewing-direction arrows on the plan against the section sheet actually used.
Correction: re-locate the correct cut line on the plan and rebuild the traced assembly from that verified location.
Reinspection: repeat the location check and the completeness check on the corrected assembly.

A Structural Member Tag Cannot Be Matched

Probable cause: the framing schedule for that member is on a different sheet than expected, or the tag was misread from the section.
Confirmation: recheck the tag directly against the section sheet and search the full structural package for a matching schedule entry.
Correction: log the unmatched tag on the missing-information and document-request list rather than assuming the member's size or material.
Reinspection: repeat the structural check once the schedule entry is received or the tag is corrected.

A Control Layer Appears Discontinuous at a Transition

Probable cause: the transition detail was not located, or the documents genuinely do not show how the layer continues across that transition.
Confirmation: follow every available detail reference for the transition and confirm whether a governing detail exists.
Correction: if no governing detail exists, mark the transition unresolved and log it on the missing-information list; do not assume a connection the documents do not show.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity check once an approved detail or clarification is received.

A Typical Detail Is Applied to a Condition It May Not Cover

Probable cause: the detail's stated area of application was not confirmed before it was used at a specific location.
Confirmation: compare the detail's callout and any stated limitations against the exact condition in question.
Correction: if the condition is not clearly covered, log an RFI rather than applying the typical detail by default.
Reinspection: repeat the reference check for that location once a clarification confirms or replaces the applicable detail.

Layer Thicknesses Scaled From the Drawing Do Not Match the Specification

Probable cause: the layer thickness was scaled without verifying reproduction size, or a written specification value exists but was not checked.
Confirmation: check the sheet's reproduction size and search the specifications for a stated thickness or performance value for that layer.
Correction: discard the scaled measurement and use the specification or schedule value; mark the sheet's scale as unverified until reproduction size is confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the layer-order accuracy check on the corrected table entry.

Field Terms

Building Section
A full vertical cut through the building typically showing multiple floors, the roof, and the foundation together.
Wall Section
An enlarged vertical cut through a specific wall assembly showing layer detail.
Construction Detail
An enlarged view of one specific condition referenced from a section or plan.
Control Layer
A material or component whose documented function is to manage water, air, thermal, vapor, fire, or acoustic performance.
Continuity
The uninterrupted connection of a control layer from one building element to the next across a transition.
Typical Detail
A detail intended to apply to more than one location; its area of application must be confirmed before use.
Transition Condition
A location where two different assemblies meet, such as a foundation-to-wall or roof-wall intersection.
Water-Resistive Barrier
A documented layer whose function is to manage bulk water at the exterior wall assembly.
Air Barrier
A documented layer whose function is to control air leakage through the assembly.
Vapor Retarder
A documented layer whose function is to control vapor diffusion through the assembly.

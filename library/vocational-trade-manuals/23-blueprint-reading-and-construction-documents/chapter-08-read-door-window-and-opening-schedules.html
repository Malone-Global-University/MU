Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to take every tagged door, window, and specialty opening in a residential project and coordinate it across every document that governs it: the floor plans and elevations where the opening is located, the schedules that define its size and type, the details that define its installation, the hardware information that defines its function, and the manufacturer information that defines its rough-opening and clearance requirements. The finished work product is one opening register and one opening summary that a fabricator, supplier, or installer can use without guessing.

You will build an opening register from tags found on plans and elevations, confirm every tag against a schedule entry, distinguish nominal, unit, frame, masonry-opening, rough-opening, and clear-opening dimensions, record handing and operation, trace head, jamb, sill, and threshold details, follow hardware references, verify performance ratings, compare opening positions across related drawings, and record every conflict or missing item as a clarification rather than resolving it yourself.

This chapter depends directly on the navigation package from Chapter 1 and the room-by-room layout summary from Chapter 4. It also feeds directly into the structural framing checklist from Chapter 7, where rough framing must match the opening information developed here, and into the envelope-detail checklist in Chapter 15, where head, jamb, and sill flashing continuity is verified.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-043 — Locate schedule references and confirm which schedules govern doors, windows, and specialty openings in the current document set.
  • BP-044 — Match every opening tag found on plans, enlarged plans, and elevations to its correct schedule entry.
  • BP-045 — Read scheduled sizes and types and distinguish nominal, unit, frame, masonry-opening, rough-opening, and clear-opening dimensions.
  • BP-046 — Identify hardware notes and trace them to hardware schedules, specifications, or approved submittals.
  • BP-047 — Verify rough-opening requirements against manufacturer instructions, approved details, and approved submittals rather than assuming them from unit size.
  • BP-048 — Create a complete opening schedule summary coordinating tag, size basis, wall type, detail, hardware, rating, finish, field verification, and unresolved questions.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 located every door and window tag on the floor plan and recorded door swings, window operation, and clearances at a room level. This chapter takes those same tags and coordinates them fully against the schedules, hardware, details, and manufacturer information that control fabrication and installation. Chapter 7 uses the opening register produced here to verify that structural headers, trimmers, and rough framing match the documented opening requirements. Chapter 15 uses it again to verify head, jamb, and sill flashing continuity at every opening type. Chapter 20 rebuilds this same coordination discipline at full scale across the entire capstone document set.

The habit built here — never assuming a rough opening from a unit dimension, and never assigning a meaning to a handing or rating abbreviation without the project legend — is used without exception everywhere openings appear later in this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming which door, window, louver, skylight, storefront, garage-door, and specialty-opening schedules are current for the project
  • locating every opening tag on floor plans, enlarged plans, elevations, and interior elevations
  • building an opening register and checking it for duplicated, missing, or unmatched tags
  • distinguishing nominal, unit, frame, masonry-opening, rough-opening, and clear-opening dimensions
  • recording handing, swing, operation, and performance requirements as documented
  • tracing head, jamb, sill, threshold, and anchorage details for each opening type
  • following hardware notes to schedules, specifications, or approved submittals
  • comparing opening positions and dimensions across plans, elevations, and sections
  • producing a complete opening schedule summary with field-verification needs and unresolved questions

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • derive a rough-opening dimension from a unit dimension without the approved manufacturer or detail information required in Step 7
  • assign a fire, smoke, acoustic, security, accessibility, weather, impact, energy, or egress rating that is not stated in the schedule, specification, or approved submittal
  • select, substitute, or resize an opening, frame, or hardware set to resolve a coordination conflict found while building the register
  • treat a conceptual, preliminary, bid-only, or unapproved shop-drawing schedule as construction authorization

Any tag, dimension, rating, or hardware reference that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the opening summary's unresolved-questions column rather than assumed.

Trade Note

An opening schedule tells you what the design team intends the opening to be. The rough opening that a framer actually builds is a separate, derived fact controlled by the frame manufacturer, the approved detail, or the approved submittal. This chapter treats those as two separate facts that must be checked against each other, not one fact assumed from the other.

Setup Standard

Do not begin recording opening dimensions (Step 6) until the current schedules have been confirmed and every opening tag has been located on plans and elevations in Steps 1 through 5. An opening register built before tags are fully located must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Locate every tag on the drawings first, before reading schedule remarks closely. A tag that appears on a plan but not in any schedule, or a schedule entry with no matching tag on any plan, is a coordination problem you need to find early, not late.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every opening in a residential project is governed by at least three separate documents: the plan or elevation that shows where it is, the schedule that defines what it is, and one or more details that define how it is installed. A tag that appears in only one of these places is not yet a coordinated opening. This chapter treats coordination across all three, plus hardware and manufacturer information, as the minimum standard before any opening is released for framing or procurement.

Reading a Unit Dimension Versus a Rough-Opening Dimension

A door or window schedule most often lists a unit or nominal size. That size describes the manufactured product, not the framed opening that will receive it. The rough opening is a separate, derived value controlled by the frame manufacturer's instructions, an approved installation detail, or an approved submittal. Do not calculate a rough opening from a unit size using a rule of thumb; record the rough-opening source and value only when the documents state it, and log the item as unresolved when they do not.

Scale Warning — Do Not Scale an Opening Size

Opening width, height, and rough-opening dimensions must come from the written schedule, detail, or manufacturer data, never from scaling a plan or elevation. A scaled opening dimension can be wrong even on a properly reproduced sheet because frame and trim thicknesses are rarely drawn to a scale that supports accurate measurement.

Critical Cue — Do Not Assume a Rough Opening From a Unit Size

Framing an opening from an assumed rough-opening allowance instead of the manufacturer's or detail's stated value can produce an opening that is too tight, too loose, or misaligned with required flashing and shimming. Record the rough opening only from the approved source, and log it as unresolved until that source is available.

Cross-Reference Cue — Follow the Full Chain for Every Opening

Every opening tag should lead to a schedule entry, and every schedule entry should lead to a head, jamb, and sill detail and, where hardware is involved, a hardware set. Follow this full chain for each opening type rather than stopping at the schedule. A chain that breaks at any link is a coordination problem to record, not resolve on your own.

RFI Threshold — Tag, Rating, and Handing Conflicts

Record any opening tag that appears on a plan but not in a schedule, any schedule entry with no matching plan tag, any unclear handing convention, and any rating that cannot be verified against the wall, frame, glazing, and hardware as a clarification item. Do not guess a rating or handing from general trade familiarity; use only the project's own schedule, legend, and specifications.

E. Preparation — Confirm Schedules and Locate Tags

  1. Confirm current door, window, louver, skylight, storefront, garage-door, and specialty-opening schedules included in the project. Verify each schedule's revision against the Chapter 1 navigation package before proceeding.
  2. Identify schedule columns, abbreviations, type designations, size conventions, handing conventions, and notes. Record the project's own column definitions and abbreviation meanings on the document-control log.
  3. Locate every opening tag on floor plans, enlarged plans, elevations, and interior elevations. Work drawing by drawing and mark each tag as located.
  4. Create an opening register with tag, room or elevation, wall type, schedule reference, and status. Populate one row per located tag in the document-control log.
  5. Check for duplicated tags, missing tags, tags not in schedules, and scheduled items not located on plans. Cross-check the register against every schedule line item.

F. Size-Basis Verification

  1. Identify whether schedule dimensions are nominal, unit, frame, masonry opening, rough opening, or clear opening. Label each schedule's size column using the Reference Conventions table before recording any value.
  2. Do not assume rough openings from unit dimensions without the approved manufacturer or detail information. Locate the manufacturer instruction, approved detail, or approved submittal that states the rough-opening allowance before recording it.
  3. Record opening width, height, thickness, type, material, finish, glazing, rating, operation, and remarks as documented. Transfer these fields directly from the schedule into the size-basis record without inference.
  4. Identify door handing, swing, pair, inactive leaf, pocket, bypass, sliding, folding, overhead, or specialty operation. Use the project's stated handing convention recorded in Step 2.
  5. Identify window operation, fixed units, mullions, transoms, emergency escape, tempered glazing, and safety glazing notes. Record each as a separate field rather than folding it into a general remarks column.

G. Detail and Hardware Trace

  1. Locate head, jamb, sill, threshold, pan, flashing, trim, and anchorage details for each opening type. Record the detail sheet and callout number for each in the detail and hardware trace.
  2. Match each opening to the correct wall assembly and exterior material condition. Confirm the wall type referenced by the schedule matches the wall type shown at the opening's actual location.
  3. Identify hardware sets, lock functions, hinges, closers, stops, seals, thresholds, panic devices, access control, and specialty hardware references. Record the hardware set number or description linked to each opening tag.
  4. Follow hardware references to specifications or hardware schedules. Confirm the hardware set referenced by the door schedule matches an actual entry in the hardware schedule or specification section.
  5. Identify fire, smoke, acoustic, security, accessibility, weather, impact, energy, and egress performance requirements. Record each documented rating in the detail and hardware trace.
  6. Verify that the scheduled rating coordinates with the wall, frame, glazing, hardware, and opening detail. Compare the rating against the wall type, frame type, glazing type, and hardware set assigned to the same opening.

H. Cross-Drawing Coordination

  1. Locate sill heights, head heights, alignment dimensions, and centerlines on plans and elevations. Record these values in the opening register where dimensioned.
  2. Compare opening dimensions and positions across plans, elevations, and sections. Confirm the same opening reads consistently on every drawing where it appears.
  3. Check door swings and window operation against cabinets, fixtures, appliances, walls, stairs, guards, and circulation. Cross-check against the Chapter 4 room-by-room layout summary.
  4. Check threshold, landing, floor-finish, and accessibility relationships. Record any threshold or landing condition that requires coordination with finish or accessibility information.
  5. Identify blocking, framing, lintel, header, hold-down, and structural-opening requirements. Record structural references without altering or assuming member sizes.
  6. Compare structural rough framing with architectural opening information. Flag any opening where the structural and architectural rough-opening information does not match.
  7. Identify electrical, security, shade, sensor, motor, and low-voltage coordination requirements. Record any device or control shown at or near the opening.
  8. Identify owner-furnished, contractor-furnished, factory-finished, field-finished, or salvaged openings. Record the furnished-by and finished-by status for each opening.
  9. Identify required approved submittals, samples, shop drawings, mockups, testing, and field measurements. Record each hold point required before fabrication or installation.
  10. Record manufacturer rough-opening, clearance, fastening, flashing, and warranty requirements only from approved information. Do not substitute a general allowance for a stated manufacturer value.

I. Opening Summary Closeout

  1. Create an opening summary grouped by exterior doors, interior doors, windows, and specialty openings. Organize the consolidated summary by these four groups.
  2. Include tag, size basis, rough-opening source, wall type, detail, hardware, rating, finish, field verification, and unresolved questions. Confirm every field required by Section B is populated for each opening.
  3. Prepare RFI-style questions for tag conflicts, missing sizes, unclear handing, rough-opening ambiguity, rating conflicts, or hardware omissions. Draft one neutral question per unresolved issue, referencing the exact tag and source document.
  4. Review the final summary against current schedules, approved submittals, and all opening-related revisions. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete before closing the chapter.

Common Opening Coordination Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of opening type or discipline.

  • Completeness check: confirm every located tag appears in the register and every schedule entry has a matching tag or logged mismatch.
  • Size-basis check: confirm every recorded dimension is labeled by type and sourced correctly, with no rough opening assumed from a unit size.
  • Chain check: confirm every opening type has a linked head, jamb, sill/threshold detail and, where applicable, a linked hardware set.
  • Rating check: confirm every recorded rating coordinates with the wall, frame, glazing, and hardware, with conflicts logged.
  • Position check: confirm opening dimensions and positions are consistent across plans, elevations, and sections.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Opening Tag on the Plan Has No Matching Schedule Entry

Probable cause: the schedule was not updated after a plan revision, or the tag was misread from the plan.
Confirmation: recheck the tag against the actual plan symbol and compare it letter by letter and number by number against every schedule entry.
Correction: log the unmatched tag on the opening summary's unresolved-questions column and draft an RFI-style question identifying the exact tag and sheet.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check once a schedule update or clarification is received.

A Rough-Opening Value Was Recorded Without a Governing Source

Probable cause: a unit or nominal size was mistaken for a rough-opening size, or a general allowance was applied instead of the manufacturer's stated value.
Confirmation: check the size-basis record for a cited manufacturer instruction, approved detail, or approved submittal supporting the recorded rough-opening value.
Correction: remove the unsupported value, mark the rough opening as unresolved, and request the manufacturer or detail information through the document-control process.
Reinspection: repeat the size-basis check once the supporting document is received.

A Scheduled Rating Does Not Match the Wall, Frame, or Hardware

Probable cause: the schedule, wall type, and hardware set were developed on different revisions, or a rating requirement changed without updating every related document.
Confirmation: compare the rating against the wall assembly, frame type, glazing type, and hardware set assigned to the same opening tag.
Correction: log the rating conflict on the opening summary and draft a neutral RFI-style question identifying the conflicting documents.
Reinspection: repeat the rating check once the approved response is incorporated.

A Hardware Reference Does Not Lead to a Real Hardware Set

Probable cause: the hardware set number on the door schedule was not updated to match the current hardware schedule or specification section.
Confirmation: recheck the hardware schedule and specification section for the exact set number referenced by the door schedule.
Correction: log the broken hardware reference on the opening summary rather than assuming a hardware set.
Reinspection: repeat the detail and hardware trace once the missing hardware set or corrected reference is received.

An Opening Position Disagrees Between the Plan and the Elevation

Probable cause: the plan and elevation were revised separately, or a dimension was transcribed incorrectly onto one of the two drawings.
Confirmation: compare the dimensioned position of the opening on the plan against the dimensioned position on the elevation using the same reference point.
Correction: log the position conflict on the opening summary and draft an RFI-style question identifying both source drawings and the exact discrepancy.
Reinspection: repeat the cross-drawing coordination check once the approved response is incorporated.

Field Terms

Opening Tag
The symbol or alphanumeric mark linking a plan or elevation location to a schedule entry.
Unit Dimension
The manufactured size of the door, window, or frame unit, exclusive of rough-opening allowance.
Rough Opening
The framed opening size required to receive a unit and frame, per manufacturer or approved detail.
Clear Opening
The unobstructed passage dimension remaining after frame, trim, and hardware are installed.
Handing
The documented orientation of a door's swing or a window's operation.
Hardware Set
The defined group of hinges, locks, closers, stops, and accessories assigned to a specific opening.
Head Detail
The detail showing the top condition of an opening, including flashing and structural support.
Jamb Detail
The detail showing the side condition of an opening, including frame anchorage.
Sill Detail
The detail showing the bottom condition of an opening, including pan, slope, and drainage.
Opening Register
The document-control table listing every located opening by tag, room or elevation, wall type, schedule reference, and status.

Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to review one residential project's general notes and project manual and convert them into a concise, referenced requirement summary that a later trade can use without rereading every section of the specifications. The finished work product is one specification register, one requirement table, a set of trade-specific extracts, and one specification summary, all built from the current approved general notes, discipline notes, and specification sections received for one project.

You will confirm the current issue and hierarchy of the project manual, build a specification register by division and section, extract product, performance, submittal, execution, testing, and closeout requirements, compare general notes across disciplines, verify code references against the approved project basis, compare manufacturer instructions with the drawings and approved submittals, and record every conflict, missing section, or undefined standard as a clarification item rather than resolving it yourself.

Specifications and general notes routinely contain requirements that never appear on the drawings at all — submittal procedures, testing standards, warranty terms, delivery and storage rules, and performance criteria that a reader who only studies plans will never see. This chapter builds the habit of treating the written word as equally authoritative to the drawing, and of following the project's own stated order of precedence rather than assuming one.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-091 — Locate general notes, discipline notes, and the current project manual and confirm their issue, revision, and completeness.
  • BP-092 — Interpret material specifications and distinguish named products, acceptable manufacturers, performance specifications, delegated design, allowances, and alternates.
  • BP-093 — Identify manufacturer requirements controlling installation, compatibility, warranty, service access, or testing and compare them with the drawings.
  • BP-094 — Read performance standards, reference standards, and testing, inspection, and commissioning requirements without inventing a value not documented.
  • BP-095 — Identify code references, occupancy, construction type, energy criteria, fire ratings, and design loads and verify them against the approved project basis.
  • BP-096 — Create a specification summary that links product, performance, submittal, execution, testing, code, warranty, and closeout requirements to the applicable drawings and trades.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter from Chapter 3 through Chapter 15 asked you to follow representative specification and keynote references without fully building the specification register itself. This chapter completes that habit by requiring the full project manual to be registered, cross-checked against every discipline's general notes, and reduced to a requirement table that later chapters can use directly.

Chapter 17 uses this specification summary, together with every discipline checklist built in Chapters 3 through 15, to compare plans across trades and identify conflicts. Chapter 19 uses this same summary to extract build-ready trade scope. Chapter 20 rebuilds this discipline at full scale as one of the required capstone reviews.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • collecting and confirming the current general notes, discipline notes, and complete project manual
  • identifying the project's stated document hierarchy or order-of-precedence provision
  • building a specification register by division, section number, title, revision, and related trade
  • extracting product, submittal, execution, testing, warranty, and closeout requirements
  • comparing general notes across architectural, structural, plumbing, mechanical, and electrical disciplines
  • verifying code references, occupancy, construction type, energy criteria, and design loads against the approved project basis
  • comparing manufacturer instructions with the drawings, specifications, and approved submittals
  • producing a requirement table, trade-specific extracts, and a specification summary

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent a document hierarchy or order of precedence when the project documents do not state one
  • convert a referenced standard into a field requirement without confirming the applicable edition and scope
  • use a code reference to fill a missing design detail without obtaining clarification
  • resolve a conflict between a general note, a specific note, a drawing, and a specification by choosing a preferred answer

Any section, note, or standard that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded as an RFI-style question rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A specification section and a drawing note can each seem complete on their own and still disagree with each other. Treat that disagreement as a document-control finding to record, not a puzzle to solve by picking the version you find more convincing.

Setup Standard

Field Tip

Read general notes on every discipline sheet, not only the architectural general notes. A structural, plumbing, mechanical, or electrical sheet frequently carries its own general notes that a specification section will assume the reader has already found.

Common Field Fundamentals

Specifications, drawings, schedules, details, approved submittals, and manufacturer instructions may each control a different aspect of the same work. This chapter does not ask you to decide which document wins in general; it asks you to locate the project's own stated order of precedence and apply it, and to record every unresolved conflict as a clarification item rather than choosing an answer.

General Notes Versus Specifications

A general note on a drawing is controlling only within its stated discipline and scope. A specification section is controlling for the category of work it describes, regardless of which discipline's drawings reference it. When a general note and a specification section address the same requirement differently, compare them directly rather than assuming the more specific-sounding statement governs; record the conflict if the project's order of precedence does not resolve it.

Code Alert — Verify the Approved Project Basis Before Relying on a Code Citation

A code reference found in the general notes or specifications must be verified against the adopted jurisdiction, permit conditions, and current approved project basis before it is treated as governing. Do not interpret a code citation as permission to redesign the work or substitute an assembly, and do not use a code reference to fill in a design detail the documents do not otherwise provide.

Critical Cue — Do Not Convert a Referenced Standard Into an Invented Field Value

Specifications frequently reference outside standards for testing, tolerances, or installation methods. Confirm the applicable edition and scope of the referenced standard before treating it as a field requirement. Do not invent a tolerance, load value, or test condition because a standard is cited but its specific value is not reproduced in the project documents.

Cross-Reference Cue — Follow Sections and Drawing Notes in Both Directions

When a drawing note references a specification section, or a specification section references a drawing detail, follow the reference to its destination and confirm the destination correctly identifies the originating condition. A specification section that is never referenced by any drawing note is still binding; record it in the register regardless of whether a drawing points to it.

RFI Threshold — Missing Sections, Undefined Standards, and Conflicting Notes

Record any missing specification section, incomplete product criterion, undefined referenced standard, conflicting general note, or unapproved substitution as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not resolve the conflict by selecting the requirement that seems more reasonable or more common in the trade.

E. Preparation — Confirm Document Basis

  1. Collect current general notes, discipline notes, project manual, specification sections, addenda, substitutions, approved submittals, and product information. Gather everything provided for the project, not only the sections that appear most relevant to a single trade.
  2. Confirm issue dates, revisions, specification volume completeness, and relationship to the current drawing set. Cross-check the specification issue against the Chapter 1 navigation package's document-control register.
  3. Identify the project's stated document hierarchy or order-of-precedence provision without inventing one. Locate this statement in the general conditions or administrative sections; if none exists, record that fact explicitly.

F. Specification Register Construction

  1. Create a specification register by division, section number, title, revision, and related trade or assembly. Populate one row per received specification section in the document-control log.
  2. Identify sections referenced on drawings and drawing notes referenced in specifications. Record each cross-reference in the register alongside the section it connects to.
  3. Locate administrative requirements for submittals, substitutions, RFIs, meetings, schedules, quality control, testing, inspections, and closeout. Add these administrative sections to the register with their own entries, since they govern procedure rather than a specific assembly.

G. Product and Submittal Requirements

  1. Identify product requirements for manufacturers, basis of design, approved equals, performance criteria, materials, grades, finishes, and accessories. Record each requirement in the requirement table with its governing section.
  2. Distinguish named products, acceptable manufacturers, performance specifications, delegated design, allowances, and alternates. Tag each requirement-table entry with the applicable category.
  3. Identify required product data, shop drawings, samples, mockups, certificates, calculations, and engineering seals. Add each required submittal to the requirement table's submittal column.
  4. Identify review and approval hold points before procurement, fabrication, delivery, or installation. Record each hold point against the requirement it controls.

H. Execution, Testing, and Closeout Requirements

  1. Locate delivery, storage, handling, acclimation, environmental, substrate, and protection requirements. Add these to the requirement table under the applicable trade.
  2. Identify installation standards, manufacturer instructions, reference standards, tolerances, and quality requirements. Record the specific standard, edition, and scope where documented.
  3. Do not convert a referenced standard into a field requirement without confirming the applicable edition and scope. Flag any reference lacking a confirmable edition or scope as a clarification item instead of assigning a value.
  4. Identify testing, inspection, observation, special inspection, commissioning, startup, balancing, cleaning, and demonstration requirements. Add each to the requirement table's test column with its pass/fail source.
  5. Identify warranties, maintenance data, spare materials, attic stock, record documents, training, and closeout submittals. Record each in the requirement table's closeout column.

I. General Notes Review and Coordination

  1. Identify general-note requirements for dimensions, field verification, coordination, penetrations, fire stopping, blocking, sealants, finishes, and protection. Record each general note in the requirement table with its governing sheet.
  2. Compare general notes across architectural, structural, plumbing, mechanical, and electrical disciplines. Identify overlapping or duplicated notes addressing the same requirement.
  3. Identify notes that conflict, overlap, or assign unclear responsibility. Log each on the missing-information and RFI log rather than assigning responsibility yourself.

J. Code References and Manufacturer Instructions

  1. Locate code references, occupancy, construction type, energy criteria, fire ratings, design loads, and jurisdictional notes. Record each in the requirement table with its exact source citation.
  2. Verify that code references correspond to the approved project basis and permit documents. Cross-check each citation against the permit set identified in the Chapter 1 navigation package.
  3. Do not use a code reference to fill a missing design detail without clarification. Log any location where a code citation appears to substitute for missing design information as an RFI-style question.
  4. Identify manufacturer instructions that control installation, compatibility, warranty, service access, or testing. Record each manufacturer requirement in the requirement table with the governing product.
  5. Compare manufacturer information with drawings, specifications, and approved submittals. Confirm the manufacturer instruction does not conflict with the approved design.
  6. Identify discrepancies between product data and the project details. Log each discrepancy on the missing-information and RFI log.

K. Summary Assembly and Closeout

  1. Create a requirement table with topic, requirement, source, affected location, responsible party if documented, submittal, hold point, test, and closeout item. Consolidate every entry recorded in Sections G through J into one finished table.
  2. Create trade-specific extracts for site, concrete, framing, envelope, roofing, openings, finishes, cabinets, plumbing, electrical, and HVAC. Sort the requirement table by trade and produce one extract per trade.
  3. Identify missing sections, incomplete product criteria, undefined standards, conflicting notes, and unapproved substitutions. Finalize the missing-information and RFI log with every item found throughout this chapter.
  4. Prepare RFI-style questions for ambiguous requirements, document conflicts, missing performance criteria, or incompatible manufacturer instructions. State the document references, the observed issue, and the requested clarification without proposing an unauthorized answer.
  5. Create a specification summary that emphasizes requirements not obvious from the drawings. Prioritize submittal procedures, testing standards, warranty terms, and performance criteria a drawing-only reader would not otherwise find.
  6. Review the summary against all active addenda, clarifications, and approved submittals before issue. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete and current before closing the chapter.

Common Specification Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which division or trade is involved.

  • Completeness check: confirm every specification section actually received appears in the register or is logged as missing.
  • Hierarchy check: confirm the project's stated order of precedence, if any, is recorded and applied consistently.
  • Traceability check: confirm every requirement-table entry cites its source section, note, or standard.
  • Code check: confirm every code citation is verified against the approved project basis and permit documents.
  • Conflict check: confirm every conflicting note, undefined standard, or unapproved substitution is logged rather than resolved unilaterally.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Specification Register Omits Sections That Govern Work Already Underway

Probable cause: the register was built only from sections referenced on the drawings instead of from the complete project manual as received.
Confirmation: compare the table of contents of the received project manual line by line against the register.
Correction: add every missing section to the register, including sections not referenced by any drawing note.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check on the corrected register.

A Requirement Is Applied From a Superseded Specification Section

Probable cause: the section's revision was not checked against the document-control register before extraction.
Confirmation: compare the section's issue date and revision against the current addenda and substitution log.
Correction: remove the outdated entry from the requirement table and replace it with the current section's requirement.
Reinspection: repeat the register-completeness and hierarchy checks across the full requirement table.

A Referenced Standard Is Treated as a Specific Field Value

Probable cause: a cited standard was assumed to supply a tolerance, rating, or test value that the project documents never actually reproduced.
Confirmation: locate the exact edition and section of the referenced standard and confirm whether the value in question is actually stated in the project documents.
Correction: remove the invented value from the requirement table and record the missing value as an RFI-style question.
Reinspection: repeat the standard-verification check on every entry citing an outside reference standard.

A Code Citation Is Used to Fill a Missing Design Detail

Probable cause: a general note cited a code section, and the reader treated the citation as sufficient design information without confirming the approved project basis addressed the condition.
Confirmation: compare the code citation against the permit documents and confirm whether a design detail actually exists elsewhere in the current set.
Correction: remove the assumed design detail and record the gap as an RFI-style question rather than a code-derived answer.
Reinspection: repeat the code-cross-check on every code reference used in the summary.

A Manufacturer Instruction Conflicts With the Approved Drawing Detail

Probable cause: the manufacturer instruction sheet was not compared against the current wall section, detail, or approved submittal before being recorded as governing.
Confirmation: place the manufacturer instruction sheet and the governing detail side by side and identify the exact point of conflict.
Correction: record the discrepancy on the missing-information and RFI log; do not select one document over the other without an approved response.
Reinspection: repeat the conflict check once the approved clarification is received and incorporated.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a controlled, referenced requirement summary ready for use, not a project manual that must be reread from cover to cover for every later chapter.

  • Store the completed specification register, requirement table, trade-specific extracts, and specification summary together as the specification package
  • Archive any superseded specification section separately from the active register
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route the missing-information and RFI log through the project's document-control process
  • Retain the specification package for direct reuse in Chapter 17

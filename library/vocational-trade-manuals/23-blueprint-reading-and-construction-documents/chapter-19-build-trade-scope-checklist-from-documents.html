Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to convert a coordinated construction-document package into one build-ready trade-scope checklist for a single, clearly bounded work package. The finished work product is one source register, one chronological checklist organized from preconstruction through closeout, and one open-item list, all built from the current approved documents, the Chapter 17 conflict register, and the Chapter 18 field-markup set for one project.

You will select and bound one trade or work package, collect every governing reference, extract explicit scope items from plans, notes, schedules, details, and specifications, search related disciplines for supporting or interfering work, identify materials and prerequisites only as documented, identify testing and inspection checkpoints, and record every scope gap, unclear responsibility, or missing acceptance criterion as an unresolved question rather than an assumption.

This is the nineteenth chapter in the book. It depends directly on the conflict register produced in Chapter 17 and the field-markup set produced in Chapter 18, and it converts nineteen chapters of document interpretation into the single deliverable that Book 24 uses to begin quantity takeoff and estimating.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-109 — Extract explicit scope items for a defined trade or work package from plans, notes, schedules, details, and specifications.
  • BP-110 — Organize extracted scope items by trade and by chronological field sequence from preconstruction through closeout.
  • BP-111 — Identify required materials, products, and accessories for the work package only as documented, distinguishing them from quantities reserved for takeoff.
  • BP-112 — Identify testing, inspection, special-inspection, and manufacturer-review checkpoints that control the work package.
  • BP-113 — Identify unanswered questions, scope gaps, and responsibility ambiguities and convert them into neutral RFI-style questions.
  • BP-114 — Create a build-ready scope checklist with complete source references, prerequisites, interfaces, inspections, closeout requirements, and visible open items.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior chapter in this book produced a discipline-specific interpretation: a foundation checklist, an opening schedule summary, a finish matrix, a fixture rough-in checklist, a device checklist, an HVAC layout checklist, a roof-scope checklist, an envelope-detail checklist, a specification summary, a cross-trade conflict register, and a field-markup set. This chapter is where those separate interpretations are pulled into one execution-ready document for a single work package.

Chapter 20 reuses this exact procedure, at full project scale, to produce the capstone's build-ready trade-scope package. Book 24 begins its quantity takeoff and estimating work directly from the checklist produced here, using the materials identified in this chapter as the starting point for quantities.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • selecting and bounding one approved trade or work package
  • collecting every governing sheet, detail, schedule, specification, approved submittal, approved RFI, and addendum affecting the package
  • extracting explicit scope items and searching related disciplines for supporting or interfering work
  • identifying materials, prerequisites, interfaces, sequencing, testing, and inspection checkpoints only as documented
  • incorporating the Chapter 17 conflict register and the Chapter 18 field-markup set into the checklist
  • organizing the checklist in chronological field sequence with source references and open items
  • drafting RFI-style questions for scope gaps, missing materials, unclear responsibility, or incomplete acceptance criteria

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • convert contractor means and methods into design requirements unless the documents themselves require them
  • assign a trade based solely on drawing convention when the scope documents are silent
  • calculate final material quantities — this chapter identifies required materials only as documented; quantities are verified through the estimating and takeoff process in Book 24
  • revise the checklist from an RFI response until that response is approved and distributed through document control

Any scope item, material, prerequisite, or acceptance criterion that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the open-item list rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A trade-scope checklist is not a summary of one discipline's sheets. It is every governing requirement — from every discipline — that touches the physical and contractual boundary of the selected work package. A framing checklist built only from the structural sheets will miss the plumbing sleeve, the electrical panel clearance, and the roof-truss bearing note that all constrain the same wall.

Setup Standard

Do not begin extracting scope items (Section F) until the work package's physical, contractual, and document boundaries are defined and the source register is started in Sections D and E. A checklist built before the boundary is defined will mix scope from adjacent work packages and must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Search every related discipline, not only the discipline that matches the work package's name. A plumbing scope checklist still needs the structural penetration note, the cabinet backing requirement, and the electrical device clearance that shares the same wall or floor.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every checklist in this chapter is built by finding explicit scope, searching related disciplines for supporting or interfering work, separating documented fact from means-and-methods judgment, and recording anything that cannot be resolved as an open item. Applying that discipline consistently across procurement, pre-installation, installation, inspection, correction, and closeout is what makes the checklist build-ready rather than a partial summary.

Explicit Scope Versus Means and Methods

Documents define required results: a wall type, a rated assembly, a rough-in location, a finish, a slope, a clearance. They do not always specify the contractor's complete method of achieving that result. This chapter extracts the required results and the explicitly stated execution requirements. It does not convert the contractor's chosen method into a design requirement unless the documents themselves require that method.

Critical Cue — Do Not Assign Trade Responsibility from Drawing Convention Alone

A plumbing fixture shown on an architectural plan does not by itself establish which trade installs it, which trade furnishes it, or which trade connects it. Use the project scope documents, specifications, division responsibilities, contracts, and the approved coordination process to assign responsibility. Where the scope documents are silent, record the ambiguity as an open item rather than assigning responsibility from the sheet it happens to appear on.

RFI Threshold — Scope Gaps and Missing Acceptance Criteria

Record any scope item with no stated material, no stated acceptance criterion, no stated responsible party where the documents should establish one, or no stated inspection requirement as an open item. Do not fill the gap with typical practice, and do not assume a value used on a different project.

Cross-Reference Cue — Search Every Related Discipline

A work package's scope is rarely confined to one discipline's sheets. Before closing the extraction step for any area, confirm that architectural, structural, plumbing, mechanical, electrical, and specialty documents have all been checked for requirements that touch the same location, assembly, or component.

Document-Control Cue — Incorporate Only Approved Current Information

Pull open items from the Chapter 17 conflict register and current field information from the Chapter 18 markup set, but incorporate only items that are approved and distributed through document control. An unanswered RFI stays an open item on this checklist; it does not become an assumed field direction.

E. Preparation — Select and Bound the Work Package

  1. Select the approved trade or work package and define its physical, contractual, and document boundaries. Record the boundary directly on the Work Package Identity Record before any extraction begins.
  2. Collect all current sheets, details, schedules, specifications, approved submittals, approved RFIs, addenda, and scope documents affecting the package. Gather every document type that could govern the boundary defined in Step 1.
  3. Create a source register listing every governing reference and revision. Populate one row per document in the document-control log.
  4. Identify the work locations, phases, alternates, options, allowances, unit-price items, and owner decisions affecting the scope. Record each against the work-package boundary statement.

F. Scope Extraction

  1. Extract explicit scope items from plans, notes, schedules, details, and specifications. Work sheet by sheet and section by section within the defined boundary.
  2. Search related disciplines for work that affects or supports the selected trade. Check every discipline listed in the source register, not only the one matching the package's name.
  3. Identify required demolition, protection, temporary work, layout, field verification, substrate preparation, and access. Record each as a discrete checklist item with its source reference.
  4. Identify owner-furnished, contractor-furnished, trade-furnished, installed-by-others, and delegated-design components. Tag each extracted item with its furnishing and installation responsibility as documented.

G. Materials, Submittals, and Prerequisites

  1. Identify materials, products, grades, sizes, finishes, accessories, fasteners, connectors, sealants, flashings, and consumables only as documented. Do not add a material not stated by the drawings, specifications, or approved submittals.
  2. Distinguish required materials from quantities to be determined in Book 24. Record material identity and specification reference here; leave quantity fields for the takeoff process.
  3. Identify approved submittals, samples, mockups, engineering, shop drawings, manufacturer instructions, and procurement hold points. Attach each to the checklist item it governs.
  4. Identify long-lead items, field-measured items, custom fabrication, and release dependencies. Flag these items in the procurement section of the checklist.
  5. Identify prerequisites that must be complete and accepted before the trade begins. Record each prerequisite with its source reference and required acceptance status.
  6. Identify substrate, framing, backing, utilities, environmental, weather, moisture, temperature, and access requirements. Attach these to the pre-installation section.

H. Layout, Interfaces, and Sequencing

  1. Identify layout controls, dimensions, elevations, grids, benchmarks, centerlines, tolerances, and field-verification points. Record each with its governing sheet or detail reference.
  2. Identify interfaces with adjacent trades, finishes, equipment, penetrations, and owner operations. Populate the interface and prerequisite map with each identified dependency.
  3. Identify sequencing requirements and work that must remain exposed for coordination or inspection. Record sequencing notes directly against the affected checklist items.

I. Testing, Inspection, and Closeout Requirements

  1. Identify testing, inspection, special inspection, manufacturer review, mockup acceptance, and quality-control checkpoints. Attach each hold point to the checklist item it controls.
  2. Identify protection, cleaning, touch-up, adjustment, startup, demonstration, and temporary-service requirements. Record these in the closeout section of the checklist.
  3. Identify warranties, record documents, maintenance data, spare materials, attic stock, labels, training, and closeout submittals. Attach each to the closeout section with its source reference.
  4. Identify exclusions, assumptions, scope gaps, duplication, and responsibility ambiguities. Log each on the open-item list rather than resolving it in the checklist.

J. Coordination Incorporation and Assembly

  1. Review the Chapter 17 conflict register and include every open item affecting the work package. Cross-check each conflict number against the work-package boundary.
  2. Review the Chapter 18 field markup and incorporate only approved and current information. Confirm the markup revision matches the source register before incorporating any item.
  3. Organize the checklist in chronological field sequence from preconstruction through closeout. Arrange all extracted items into the sequence used in Section B.
  4. Assign each item a source reference, responsible party where established, prerequisite, acceptance criterion, and status. Complete every required field before marking an item ready.
  5. Create separate sections for procurement, pre-installation, installation, inspection, correction, and closeout. Confirm every extracted item is filed under the correct section.
  6. Add stop-work or hold-point language where unresolved information controls safety, structure, water, utilities, code, or fit. State the exact condition that must be resolved before the affected item may proceed.
  7. Do not convert contractor means and methods into design requirements unless the documents require them. Review each item and remove any method-level assumption not stated by the documents.
  8. Do not assign a trade based solely on drawing convention when the scope documents are silent. Flag any such item as an open responsibility question instead.

K. Review and Issue

  1. Prepare RFI-style questions for scope gaps, missing materials, unclear responsibility, or incomplete acceptance criteria. Draft one neutral question per open item or logically related group.
  2. Review the checklist with related trades or project leadership through the approved coordination process. Route the draft checklist and open-item list for review before treating it as issued.
  3. Revise the checklist only from approved clarifications and maintain revision history. Record each revision's date, source approval, and affected checklist items.
  4. Perform a build-readiness review confirming documents, submittals, materials, access, prerequisites, layout, inspections, and unanswered questions. Complete the Build-Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below.
  5. Issue the checklist with a current source register and open-item list. Distribute the completed package through document control.
  6. Define how completed items will be verified and archived for project closeout. Record the verification and archiving method directly on the checklist.

Common Scope-Extraction Inspection

Inspect the finished checklist using the same checks regardless of which trade or work package is involved.

  • Boundary check: confirm no checklist item falls outside the approved physical or contractual boundary.
  • Source check: confirm every item carries a specific, current source reference.
  • Coverage check: confirm every related discipline was searched for supporting or interfering scope.
  • Sequence check: confirm the checklist is organized chronologically from preconstruction through closeout.
  • Coordination check: confirm every applicable Chapter 17 conflict and approved Chapter 18 markup item has been incorporated or logged.
  • Open-item check: confirm every gap, missing material, or ambiguity is recorded rather than assumed.

Complete the Build-Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before issuing the checklist through document control.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Checklist Items Fall Outside the Approved Work Package

Probable cause: the physical or contractual boundary was not clearly defined before extraction began, or extraction drifted into an adjacent trade's scope.
Confirmation: compare every checklist item against the Work Package Identity Record's stated boundary.
Correction: remove out-of-boundary items and, if they represent a genuine interface, move them to the interface and prerequisite map instead of the checklist body.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check across the full checklist.

A Checklist Item Has No Source Reference

Probable cause: the item was extracted from memory of a similar project, or from a discussion that was never incorporated into an approved document.
Confirmation: search the source register for a matching sheet, detail, schedule, specification, submittal, or RFI.
Correction: if no source exists, remove the item from the checklist body and record it on the open-item list as a missing requirement requiring clarification.
Reinspection: repeat the source check on the corrected checklist.

A Related Discipline's Requirement Was Missed

Probable cause: extraction was limited to the discipline matching the work package's name rather than searching every discipline touching the same location.
Confirmation: reopen the architectural, structural, and every MEP or specialty sheet covering the work-package boundary and check for unextracted requirements.
Correction: add the missed requirement to the checklist with its source reference, or to the open-item list if it conflicts with an existing item.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage check across every discipline in the source register.

An Approved Chapter 17 Conflict Was Not Reflected in the Checklist

Probable cause: the conflict register was reviewed before its most recent update, or the conflict's affected location was not cross-checked against the work-package boundary.
Confirmation: compare the current conflict register against every checklist item touching the same location, assembly, or trade.
Correction: incorporate the approved response into the checklist, or add the open conflict to the open-item list if it remains unresolved.
Reinspection: repeat the coordination check once the conflict register is current.

A Means-and-Methods Assumption Was Written as a Requirement

Probable cause: a typical or convenient installation method was recorded as if the documents required it, when the documents actually left the method to the contractor.
Confirmation: trace the item back to its source reference and confirm whether the document states a required result or a required method.
Correction: revise the item to state only the documented required result; remove the assumed method.
Reinspection: repeat the source check on the corrected item.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with one issued, source-referenced checklist ready for build use, not a partial extraction.

  • Archive prior checklist drafts separately from the issued build-ready checklist
  • Store the completed source register, checklist, interface map, and open-item list together as the work-package package
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route the open-item list through the project's document-control and RFI process
  • Retain the build-ready checklist for direct reuse in Chapter 20 and handoff into Book 24

Field Terms

Work Package
The approved trade or defined scope of work with established physical, contractual, and document boundaries.
Explicit Scope Item
A requirement stated directly by a plan note, schedule entry, detail, or specification section.
Supporting Scope
Related-discipline work required for the work package to proceed or be accepted.
Prerequisite
A condition or completed item of work that must exist before the work package may begin.
Hold Point
A stage requiring inspection, testing, or approval before the next stage may proceed.
Build-Ready Checklist
The finished, source-referenced, chronologically organized checklist for one work package.
Open Item
An unresolved scope gap, missing material, unclear responsibility, or incomplete acceptance criterion.
Source Register
The document-control table listing every governing reference and revision used to build the checklist.
Interface
A documented point of dependency or contact between the work package and an adjacent trade or system.
Means and Methods
The contractor's chosen execution method, distinct from the documented required result.

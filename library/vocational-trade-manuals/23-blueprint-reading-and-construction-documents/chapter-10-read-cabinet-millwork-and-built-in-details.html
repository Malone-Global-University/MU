Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to interpret a residential cabinet, millwork, and built-in package and convert it into an installation-readiness checklist before fabrication or field installation begins. The finished work product is one cabinet and built-in register, one dimension and control-point summary, one backing and utility coordination record, and one installation-readiness checklist, all built from the current approved cabinet plans, interior elevations, sections, details, schedules, appliance information, specifications, and approved shop drawings received for one project.

You will identify every cabinet, vanity, island, pantry, bench, shelving unit, closet system, and built-in by room and tag; read overall run dimensions before individual cabinet widths; distinguish face, box, door, filler, finished-panel, scriber, reveal, and countertop dimensions; locate countertop, sink, cooktop, and appliance cutout information; identify required wall backing and compare it with framing and MEP plans; identify hardware, finish, and field-measurement requirements; and record every conflict, missing filler, unsupported overhang, or unclear finish as a clarification item rather than resolving it independently.

This chapter depends directly on the navigation package built in Chapter 1 and on the room-by-room layout summary built in Chapter 4. It also depends on the finish matrix from Chapter 9, since cabinet, counter, and adjacent finishes must be read together. The output of this chapter is used again in Chapter 17, when cabinet and appliance drawings are cross-checked against electrical, plumbing, HVAC, and structural backing information.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-055 — Identify cabinet elevations by room, tag, and cabinet type from the received cabinet and millwork package.
  • BP-056 — Read cabinet dimensions, distinguishing overall run, individual cabinet, face, box, filler, panel, and countertop dimensions.
  • BP-057 — Locate fillers, end panels, finished backs, scribes, toe kicks, and applied trim and match them to their governing elevation or detail.
  • BP-058 — Interpret countertop details, including thickness, edge, overhang, backsplash, seam, support, and appliance cutout information.
  • BP-059 — Identify hardware notes and match hardware, backing, and utility coordination requirements to the correct cabinet or built-in.
  • BP-060 — Create a cabinet-installation checklist coordinating layout, dimensions, backing, counters, hardware, appliances, utilities, and field measurements.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 9 established the room finish matrix, including the substrate, transition, and wet-area information adjacent to cabinets. This chapter uses that matrix directly when comparing countertop, toe-kick, and finished-panel conditions with the surrounding floor, wall, and ceiling finishes. Chapter 11 through Chapter 13 will later require you to compare plumbing, electrical, and HVAC device locations against the cabinet interiors documented here. Chapter 17 formally cross-checks cabinet and appliance drawings against every other discipline, and Chapter 20 repeats this entire discipline at full capstone scale.

The distinction between architectural cabinet information and approved shop-drawing information, established in this chapter, is used without exception in every remaining chapter that touches finish carpentry, casework, or built-in coordination.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying every cabinet, vanity, island, pantry, bench, shelving unit, closet system, and built-in by room and tag
  • reading overall run dimensions before individual cabinet widths and distinguishing dimension types
  • locating fillers, end panels, finished backs, skins, scribes, valances, toe kicks, light rails, and applied trim
  • interpreting countertop type, thickness, edge, overhang, backsplash, seam, support, and cutout information
  • identifying required wall backing and comparing it with wall framing and MEP plans
  • identifying hardware, finish, and field-measurement, templating, and approval hold points
  • producing a cabinet-installation checklist organized from document control through inspection

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • derive specialty countertop cutouts, sink templates, or appliance clearances from architectural drawings when an approved template or shop drawing governs
  • select or substitute a cabinet, countertop, hardware, or appliance product based on apparent similarity to the documented item
  • invent a filler width, panel dimension, backing location, or utility clearance not supplied by the current approved documents
  • proceed with fabrication or installation before required field measurements, templating, and shop-drawing approvals are complete

Any dimension, tag, or note that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded as an RFI-style question rather than assumed.

Trade Note

Architectural cabinet drawings establish design intent. Approved shop drawings, once issued, refine or supersede specific architectural dimensions for fabrication. This chapter treats those as two related but distinct sources and requires you to record which one currently governs each dimension, rather than assuming the architectural drawing remains correct after a shop drawing is approved.

Setup Standard

Do not begin the cabinet register (Step 2) until the current cabinet plans, interior elevations, sections, details, schedules, appliance information, specifications, and approved shop drawings have been confirmed in Step 1. A register built from an unverified document set must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Read overall run dimensions before reading individual cabinet widths. A run that adds correctly to its overall dimension confirms the dimension chain; a run that does not is a coordination problem to record, not a rounding error to absorb into the nearest filler.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every cabinet interpretation in this chapter follows the same discipline: confirm the tag, confirm which dimension type is being read, confirm the governing source between architectural and approved shop drawing, and record what still requires field verification. Learning that discipline here, before fabrication drawings are released, prevents a filler, backing location, or cutout from being built to an assumed rather than a documented value.

Architectural Intent Versus Approved Shop Drawing

An architectural cabinet elevation establishes design intent, room relationships, and coordination with adjacent trades. An approved shop drawing, once issued, is the fabricator's confirmed dimension set for the exact cabinets being built. When the two disagree on a dimension, the approved shop drawing governs for fabrication, but the disagreement must still be recorded so that architectural coordination items — backing, utilities, finish transitions — are checked against the current dimension.

Scale Warning — Do Not Derive Cutouts From Architectural Drawings

Sink, cooktop, faucet, and appliance cutout locations and sizes must come from the approved manufacturer template or approved shop drawing, not from scaling an architectural interior elevation. An architectural drawing establishes intended location only; the exact cutout dimension is controlled by the specific product template once it is approved.

Critical Cue — Confirm Backing Before Closure

Wall backing, blocking, cleats, and structural support for cabinets, grab bars, and heavy wall-mounted equipment must be confirmed against the framing and MEP plans before the wall is closed. A backing location assumed from typical practice instead of the documented location is a coordination failure to record, not a condition to install around.

Cross-Reference Cue — Follow Every Cabinet Tag to Its Full Source Set

A cabinet tag on a floor plan typically connects to an interior elevation, a schedule entry, and often a section or detail. Follow the tag through every connected document before recording a dimension, and confirm the destination documents agree with each other before treating the dimension as resolved.

RFI Threshold — Missing Fillers, Utility Interference, Unsupported Overhangs

Record any dimension conflict, missing filler, utility interference within a cabinet cavity, unsupported countertop overhang, or incomplete appliance data as a clarification item. Do not select a filler width, reroute a utility, or add unspecified support to resolve the condition independently.

E. Preparation — Confirm Documents and Identify Cabinets

  1. Confirm current cabinet plans, interior elevations, sections, details, schedules, appliance information, specifications, and approved shop drawings. Record each source on the Cabinet Document Identity Record before proceeding.
  2. Identify each cabinet, vanity, island, pantry, bench, shelving unit, closet system, and built-in by room and tag. Populate one row per item in the cabinet and built-in register.
  3. Compare cabinet tags and room names across floor plans, elevations, schedules, and shop drawings. Confirm every tag appears consistently across all connected documents.
  4. Identify base, wall, tall, corner, vanity, appliance, sink, drawer, open-shelf, and specialty cabinet types. Add a type column to the register and classify every item.

F. Dimension and Control-Point Reading

  1. Read overall run dimensions before individual cabinet widths. Record the overall run dimension for each cabinet grouping before recording individual cabinet widths within it.
  2. Identify control points at walls, corners, centerlines, appliances, sinks, windows, doors, and islands. Mark each control point on the dimension and control-point summary.
  3. Distinguish face, box, door, filler, finished panel, scriber, reveal, and countertop dimensions. Label every recorded dimension with its type using the Reference Conventions table.
  4. Locate fillers, end panels, finished backs, skins, scribes, valances, toe kicks, light rails, and applied trim. Record the location, width, and governing elevation or detail for each.
  5. Identify ceiling, soffit, crown, wall, floor, and adjacent-finish relationships. Cross-check each against the Chapter 9 finish-scope matrix for the same room.

G. Countertop and Cutout Coordination

  1. Locate countertop type, thickness, edge, overhang, backsplash, seam, support, and waterfall details. Record each in the dimension and control-point summary with its governing detail reference.
  2. Identify sink, cooktop, faucet, outlet, dispenser, appliance, and accessory cutout information. Record only the cutout information the current documents provide.
  3. Do not derive specialty countertop cutouts from architectural drawings when approved templates or shop drawings govern. Flag any cutout without an approved template or shop drawing as an open item rather than estimating its size.
  4. Identify cabinet mounting heights, countertop height, upper-cabinet height, shelf heights, and accessibility requirements. Record each height against the applicable accessibility standard identified in the documents.

H. Backing, Utility, and Appliance Coordination

  1. Locate wall backing, blocking, studs, cleats, anchors, and structural support references. Record each backing location in the backing and utility coordination record.
  2. Compare required backing with wall framing and MEP plans before closure. Confirm each backing location is achievable within the documented wall framing and does not conflict with MEP routing.
  3. Identify appliance model, width, height, depth, door swing, ventilation, clearance, utility, anti-tip, and service-access requirements. Record each from the approved appliance data sheet.
  4. Compare appliance information with approved manufacturer data and current selections. Confirm the scheduled model matches the manufacturer data sheet on file.
  5. Locate plumbing supplies, drains, cleanouts, gas, electrical devices, lighting, low voltage, and HVAC components within cabinet areas. Record each utility location within the affected cabinet on the coordination record.
  6. Identify conflicts between utilities and drawers, shelves, cabinet backs, fasteners, or appliance cavities. Log each conflict with its exact location and affected cabinet tag.

I. Hardware and Finish Coordination

  1. Identify hardware including pulls, knobs, hinges, slides, catches, locks, stays, support hardware, and accessory systems. Record each hardware item and its governing schedule or specification reference.
  2. Follow hardware notes to schedules, specifications, and approved submittals. Confirm every hardware tag resolves to a specific product or complete performance specification.
  3. Identify factory finish, field finish, edge treatment, interior finish, exposed surfaces, and matching requirements. Record each finish requirement and cross-check it against the Chapter 9 finish-scope matrix.

J. Field Verification and Sequencing

  1. Identify field-measurement, templating, mockup, sample, shop-drawing, and approval hold points. Record each hold point and its status in the installation-readiness checklist.
  2. Verify openings and room dimensions that must be field checked before fabrication. Cross-check against the Chapter 4 room-by-room layout summary and flag any unresolved discrepancy.
  3. Identify installation sequence involving flooring, walls, paint, cabinets, counters, backsplash, appliances, plumbing, and electrical finish work. Record the documented sequence, or record it as undocumented if the current set does not state one.
  4. Identify protection, adjustment, cleaning, touch-up, and punch-list requirements. Record each from the specifications or general notes.

K. Checklist Assembly and Closeout

  1. Create a cabinet-installation checklist organized by document control, field measurement, backing, room readiness, cabinet layout, fastening, counters, hardware, appliances, utilities, finish, and inspection. Assemble the findings from Sections E through J into this structure.
  2. Attach sheet, elevation, section, detail, shop-drawing, specification, and manufacturer references to each item. Confirm every checklist line cites its governing source.
  3. Prepare RFI-style questions for dimension conflicts, missing fillers, utility interference, unsupported overhangs, incomplete appliance data, or unclear finish. State the document references, the conflict or missing information, and the field impact for each question without proposing an unapproved answer.
  4. Review the checklist against the current approved shop drawings and record which architectural information has been superseded or refined. Confirm the register reflects the current governing dimension for every item before closing the chapter.

Common Cabinet Coordination Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which room or cabinet run is involved.

  • Completeness check: confirm every cabinet and built-in shown on plans, elevations, or schedules appears in the register with a resolved or logged status.
  • Dimension-type check: confirm every recorded dimension is labeled as face, box, filler, panel, or countertop and cites its governing source.
  • Governing-source check: confirm the register identifies the current governing document for every dimension where an approved shop drawing exists.
  • Backing and utility check: confirm every required backing location and cabinet-area utility has been compared against structural and MEP documents.
  • Cutout-source check: confirm no cutout dimension was recorded from a scaled architectural drawing where a template or shop drawing is required.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Individual Cabinet Run Does Not Add Up to the Overall Dimension

Probable cause: individual cabinet widths were read before the overall run dimension was confirmed, or a filler width was assumed instead of documented.
Confirmation: re-add the individual cabinet and filler widths against the overall run dimension shown on the elevation.
Correction: record the discrepancy on the RFI-style question log rather than adjusting a filler width to force the total to match.
Reinspection: repeat the dimension-type check once a response is received and incorporated.

A Cabinet Dimension Disagrees Between the Architectural Elevation and the Shop Drawing

Probable cause: the shop drawing was approved after the architectural elevation was issued and refined a dimension without a corresponding architectural revision.
Confirmation: compare the approval date and status of the shop drawing against the current architectural revision.
Correction: record the approved shop drawing as the current governing dimension for fabrication and note which architectural coordination items must be re-checked against it.
Reinspection: repeat the governing-source check across the affected room.

A Required Backing Location Is Missing From the Framing Plan

Probable cause: the cabinet or accessory requiring backing was added or revised after the framing plan was issued, or the backing note exists only on the cabinet detail and was not carried to the structural sheet.
Confirmation: compare the backing and utility coordination record against the current structural framing plan for the same wall.
Correction: record the missing backing as a conflict on the coordination record and submit an RFI-style question identifying the exact location and required backing type.
Reinspection: repeat the backing and utility check once a response is received and the framing plan is updated.

A Utility Location Falls Inside a Drawer or Shelf Cavity

Probable cause: the plumbing, electrical, or HVAC plan was not compared against the cabinet interior elevation before the conflict was discovered.
Confirmation: overlay the affected cabinet elevation with the plumbing, electrical, or HVAC plan at the same location.
Correction: record the conflict on the coordination record with the exact cabinet tag, drawer or shelf affected, and utility involved; do not relocate the utility or modify the cabinet independently.
Reinspection: repeat the utility check once an approved resolution is incorporated.

An Appliance Cutout Cannot Be Confirmed

Probable cause: the appliance model has not yet been confirmed through an approved submittal, or the manufacturer template has not been received.
Confirmation: check the appliance data sheet on file against the scheduled model and confirm whether a manufacturer template or approved shop drawing has been issued.
Correction: record the cutout as an open item on the installation-readiness checklist and hold fabrication of the affected component until the template or shop drawing is approved.
Reinspection: repeat the cutout-source check once the template or shop drawing is received.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a controlled, referenced cabinet package ready for installation planning, not a set of unresolved elevations.

  • Archive superseded architectural cabinet information once an approved shop drawing supersedes it, while preserving the archive record
  • Store the completed cabinet register, dimension and control-point summary, backing and utility record, and installation-readiness checklist together as the cabinet coordination package
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route every RFI-style question through the project's document-control process
  • Retain the cabinet coordination package for direct reuse in Chapters 11 through 13 and in Chapter 17

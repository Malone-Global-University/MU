Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to compare one residential plan set across every discipline that contributed to it and to produce a single controlled conflict register and RFI-style question list. Every prior chapter in this book taught you to read one discipline's documents in isolation: the site plan, the foundation plan, the floor plan, the elevations, the sections, the structural framing, the opening schedules, the finish plans, the cabinet package, the plumbing plans, the electrical plans, the HVAC plans, the roof plans, the envelope details, and the specifications. None of those disciplines actually works in isolation on a real project. A beam pocket has to match a structural detail. A plumbing vent has to miss a rafter. A switch leg has to miss a cabinet. This chapter is where those separate readings are brought together and checked against each other.

The finished work product is one coordination matrix, one documented set of cross-discipline comparisons, one conflict register with a unique identifier and full references for every issue found, and one neutral RFI-style question list. You do not resolve a structural, life-safety, system, accessibility, or waterproofing conflict yourself. You identify it, document it precisely, and route it through the approved review and document-control process.

This chapter depends on every navigation, interpretation, and checklist product built in Chapters 1 through 16. It is not a fresh read of the drawings; it is a structured comparison of what those earlier chapters already found.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-097 — Cross-check architectural and structural plans for grid, dimension, bearing, and opening coordination.
  • BP-098 — Compare plumbing and framing information to identify routing and structural-penetration conflicts.
  • BP-099 — Compare electrical and cabinet layouts to identify device, fixture, and clearance conflicts.
  • BP-100 — Identify missing dimensions, incomplete dimension chains, and undefined reference points across disciplines.
  • BP-101 — Flag plan conflicts by classification, location, source documents, and consequence.
  • BP-102 — Create an RFI-style question list that states required information without proposing an unauthorized design answer.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 16 gave you a specification and general-notes summary that stands beside the drawings as a second controlling source. This chapter uses that summary, together with every discipline checklist from Chapters 2 through 15, as the raw material for cross-trade comparison. The conflict register and RFI list produced here feed directly into Chapter 18, where selected issues are marked directly onto the field-use plan set, and into Chapter 19, where an approved trade scope is only finalized after this chapter's open items have been reviewed. Chapter 20 repeats this exact comparison process at full scale across an entire residential package as the coordination core of the capstone.

The RFI discipline built here — asking a neutral, referenced, single-issue question instead of proposing your own design fix — is the same discipline used for the rest of the book. It does not change in the capstone; it is only applied more broadly.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • building a coordination matrix identifying which sheets and document types must be compared for each building area
  • overlaying or comparing architectural and structural plans at a verified common scale or digital alignment
  • comparing plumbing, HVAC, electrical, and cabinet information against framing, ceilings, and clearances
  • comparing roof, elevation, envelope, and finish information against structure, MEP, and specifications
  • classifying every discrepancy found and assigning it a unique conflict identifier
  • drafting one neutral RFI-style question per issue or logically related group of issues
  • identifying work that must stop, may proceed elsewhere, or requires temporary protection pending a response
  • routing the conflict list through the approved review and document-control process and closing items only after an approved response is incorporated

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • resolve a structural, life-safety, system, accessibility, or waterproofing conflict through an undocumented field choice
  • propose an unapproved design as the only acceptable answer within an RFI
  • combine information from different document revisions unless the project's document-control process authorizes the combination
  • close a conflict item before an approved response has been incorporated into the active field documents and checklists

Any conflict that cannot be resolved through comparison of current approved documents must be recorded on the conflict register and carried forward as an open RFI rather than assumed or guessed.

Trade Note

A conflict register is not a list of complaints about the drawings. It is a controlled record that lets a designer, engineer, or owner make one informed decision instead of ten uncoordinated field decisions made by ten different trades. Precision in the register is what makes that possible.

Setup Standard

Common Field Fundamentals

Every comparison in this chapter follows the same underlying discipline: confirm the current revision of every document involved, compare the specific locations that interact, record the exact mismatch with its sources, classify it, and draft one neutral question. Learning this discipline once, and applying it consistently across every discipline pair, is what keeps a large coordination effort from becoming an unsorted pile of notes.

Comparing at a Verified Common Scale

Architectural and structural plans are not necessarily printed at the same scale, and a reproduced or resized sheet may not be to its stated scale at all. Before overlaying or visually comparing two plans, verify that both are being compared at a common, verified scale or through a digital alignment method that does not depend on an assumed print scale. A comparison performed against unverified scales can manufacture a conflict that does not exist, or hide one that does.

Scale Warning — Verify Alignment Before Overlaying Plans

Do not overlay or visually compare an architectural sheet and a structural sheet unless both have been confirmed to represent the same area at the same verified scale, or the comparison is performed through a digital method that aligns on gridlines or coordinates rather than printed scale. An unverified overlay can produce a false match or a false conflict.

Critical Cue — Never Resolve a Structural or Life-Safety Conflict in the Field

A conflict involving bearing, load path, egress, fire rating, accessibility, or waterproofing continuity must never be resolved through an undocumented field choice, regardless of how minor the apparent fix seems. Record the conflict, stop or protect the affected work as required, and route the issue through the approved RFI process.

Cross-Reference Cue — Confirm Every Comparison Uses the Current Revision

Before recording a conflict, confirm that every document involved in the comparison is the current approved revision, not a superseded sheet or an unincorporated bulletin. A conflict recorded against a superseded document is not a real conflict and wastes the document-control process's time.

RFI Threshold — State the Conflict, Not the Fix

An RFI-style question states the document references, the observed conflict or missing information, the field impact, and the required-by date, and asks for clarification. It does not propose an unauthorized design as the only acceptable answer. If more than one reasonable resolution exists, the question must leave that decision to the designer or engineer of record.

E. Preparation — Confirm Documents and Build the Coordination Matrix

  1. Confirm the current approved revision of every architectural, structural, civil, plumbing, mechanical, electrical, specialty, schedule, detail, specification, and approved submittal used. Check each against the Chapter 1 sheet register and revision summary before proceeding.
  2. Create a coordination matrix listing the sheets and document types that must be compared for each building area. Organize the matrix by room, zone, or system so no area is left uncompared.
  3. Select a consistent room-by-room, grid-by-grid, or system-by-system coordination route. Record the selected route on the Project and Document Identity Record and follow it without switching methods mid-chapter.

F. Architectural and Structural Coordination

  1. Overlay or compare architectural and structural plans at a verified common scale or digital alignment. Confirm the alignment method before recording any finding.
  2. Check grids, exterior dimensions, bearing walls, columns, beams, headers, openings, stairs, floor levels, roof geometry, and foundation supports. Work through the coordination matrix systematically rather than spot-checking.
  3. Record every mismatch by exact location and source references. Cite the sheet number, detail number, and revision for every document involved in each mismatch.
  4. Compare foundation penetrations, slab recesses, equipment pads, drains, and utilities with structural and architectural information. Note any penetration shown on one discipline's plan but absent from another's.

G. Systems Coordination

  1. Compare plumbing fixtures and pipe routes with joists, beams, trusses, shear walls, footings, cabinets, ceilings, and access panels. Flag any route crossing a structural member without a documented penetration detail.
  2. Compare HVAC equipment and ducts with framing, ceiling heights, soffits, roof penetrations, access, and service clearances. Confirm stated ceiling heights accommodate the documented duct and equipment dimensions.
  3. Compare electrical panels, devices, fixtures, and controls with cabinets, mirrors, doors, windows, plumbing, structure, ceilings, and equipment. Identify devices that fall behind cabinetry, appliances, or other obstructions as documented.
  4. Compare reflected ceiling plans with lighting, registers, fans, sprinklers, alarms, beams, soffits, access panels, and finish patterns. Identify overlapping or conflicting ceiling-mounted elements at the same location.
  5. Compare cabinet and appliance drawings with electrical, plumbing, HVAC, wall backing, openings, and clearances. Confirm required utility connections fall within the documented cabinet or appliance envelope.

H. Envelope and Finish Coordination

  1. Compare door and window schedules with plans, elevations, structural openings, flashing details, hardware, and finish thicknesses. Confirm rough-opening sources are consistent across all referencing documents.
  2. Compare roof plans with structural framing, plumbing vents, HVAC exhausts, electrical equipment, solar or specialty components, and drainage. Identify penetrations shown on one discipline's roof information but missing from another's.
  3. Compare exterior elevations with wall types, openings, MEP penetrations, grade, roof geometry, and material limits. Confirm elevation heights are consistent with the building and wall sections.
  4. Compare wall sections and envelope details with structural support, opening products, roof transitions, and specifications. Identify any assembly layer named in one document but absent from a related detail.
  5. Compare room finish schedules with plans, interior elevations, flooring transitions, cabinets, fixtures, and ceiling information. Confirm finish transitions are documented at every location where materials change.

I. Notes, Specifications, and Submittal Coordination

  1. Compare general notes and specifications with discipline-specific details and schedules. Use the Chapter 16 specification summary as the comparison baseline.
  2. Identify missing overall dimensions, incomplete dimension chains, ambiguous reference points, and field-verification notes without defined responsibility. Record each as missing information rather than estimating a value.
  3. Identify broken detail references, unmatched tags, undefined abbreviations, inconsistent room names, and missing schedule entries. Cross-check against the Chapter 1 reference map.
  4. Identify conflicts between approved submittals and construction drawings. Confirm the submittal's approval status and date before recording a conflict against it.
  5. Identify code, permit, inspection, manufacturer, and delegated-design information requiring confirmation. Record the specific citation or requirement and the document in which it appears.

J. Conflict Register Construction

  1. Classify each issue as missing information, direct conflict, coordination conflict, constructability concern, field discrepancy, scope ambiguity, or potential change. Use the classification key from the Reference Conventions table.
  2. Assign a unique conflict number and record location, description, source documents, revision, affected trades, consequence, and required-by date. Populate one complete row per issue in the conflict-log template.
  3. Attach clear annotated excerpts or overlays where authorized. Preserve original linework and text in any attached excerpt.
  4. Do not solve a structural, life-safety, system, accessibility, waterproofing, or design conflict through an undocumented field choice. Record the issue and its consequence instead of a proposed fix.

K. RFI Drafting and Routing

  1. Draft one neutral RFI-style question for each issue or logically related group. State the document references, the conflict or missing information, and the field impact.
  2. State what information is needed rather than directing the designer toward an unapproved answer. Review each draft question to confirm it does not embed a preferred solution.
  3. Identify work that must stop, may proceed elsewhere, or requires temporary protection pending response. Record this determination on the conflict register entry.
  4. Route the conflict list through the approved review and document-control process. Submit the RFI-style question list using the project's stated procedure.
  5. Update the register with responses, approvals, affected documents, and downstream impacts. Record the responder, date, and every document the response affects.
  6. Close an item only after the approved response has been incorporated into active field documents and checklists. Do not mark an item closed based on a verbal or informal answer.

Common Coordination Inspection

Inspect the finished conflict register using the same checks regardless of which discipline pair or building area is involved.

  • Coverage check: confirm every building area or system in the coordination matrix has a recorded comparison status.
  • Currency check: confirm every document used in a comparison was the current approved revision at the time of comparison.
  • Traceability check: confirm every conflict register entry has a unique identifier and complete source references.
  • Neutrality check: confirm every RFI-style question requests information rather than proposing an unauthorized design.
  • Closure check: confirm no entry is marked closed without an incorporated approved response.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Recorded Conflict Turns Out Not to Be Real

Probable cause: the comparison was performed against a superseded document, an unincorporated addendum, or an unverified overlay scale.
Confirmation: recheck the revision block and issue date of every document involved in the comparison against the current document-control register.
Correction: remove or revise the conflict register entry, note the correction and its cause, and rebuild the comparison using the current approved documents.
Reinspection: repeat the currency check on the corrected entry and confirm no other entries share the same underlying error.

An RFI-Style Question Embeds an Unauthorized Design Answer

Probable cause: the question was drafted around a preferred field solution rather than a neutral description of the conflict.
Confirmation: reread the draft question and identify any language that directs the designer toward one specific resolution.
Correction: rewrite the question to state only the document references, the observed conflict, and the field impact, and remove any proposed fix.
Reinspection: repeat the neutrality check on the revised question before it is routed for review.

Two Trades Independently Report the Same Conflict Differently

Probable cause: the conflict was recorded twice under different identifiers instead of being consolidated into one entry.
Confirmation: compare the location, source documents, and description of both entries against the coordination matrix.
Correction: merge the entries under a single conflict identifier, retain both descriptions in the record, and issue one consolidated RFI-style question.
Reinspection: repeat the traceability check to confirm the register no longer contains duplicate identifiers for the same issue.

A Conflict Is Closed Before a Response Is Incorporated

Probable cause: a verbal or informal answer was treated as sufficient to close the item without updating the active documents and checklists.
Confirmation: check the conflict register entry for a documented approved response and confirm whether the affected documents and checklists were actually updated.
Correction: reopen the entry, obtain or confirm the documented approved response, and incorporate it into every affected document and checklist before closing again.
Reinspection: repeat the closure check across the full register to confirm no other entry was closed prematurely.

A Coordination Area Was Skipped Entirely

Probable cause: the coordination route was not followed consistently, or an area was assumed complete because a related area had already been checked.
Confirmation: compare the coordination matrix against the completed comparison findings and identify any area without a recorded status.
Correction: perform the missed comparison using the same discipline of Sections F through I before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage check on the completed matrix.

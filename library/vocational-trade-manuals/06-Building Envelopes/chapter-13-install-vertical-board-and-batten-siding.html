Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install an approved vertical board-and-batten siding system on one prepared wall elevation, or on a separate practice elevation identified by the project specification, using the rainscreen, WRB, flashing, corner, and opening preparation completed in earlier projects. The finished work product is a plumb, balanced, ventilated, and correctly fastened vertical siding elevation ready for the exterior trim installed in Project 14.

You will review the selected board-and-batten system, confirm that the rainscreen and structural fastening support suit vertical cladding, verify WRB, flashing, trim, corners, openings, and base drainage, develop a balanced layout from a centerline or corner reference, transfer vertical control lines, stage and condition the siding, cut and install the first board and every successive board while maintaining the approved joint relationship, fit boards around openings and penetrations, install battens over the joints, install top and bottom closures, and inspect and document the completed elevation.

This project follows Project 11, which established the siding layout and starter and corner components, and Project 10, which installed the rainscreen furring and drainage mat this siding is fastened through. Chapter 12 covers horizontal lap siding as a separate approved cladding system; this project and Chapter 12 install on separate walls or practice elevations unless the approved design specifically includes a transition between the two.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BE-073 — Lay out a balanced vertical board-and-batten module from a defined centerline or corner reference and transfer vertical control lines across the elevation.
  • BE-074 — Measure, cut, and install vertical siding boards plumb and in the approved starting sequence and reference.
  • BE-075 — Maintain the approved open joint, shiplap engagement, or edge relationship and module width across the elevation, including at openings and penetrations.
  • BE-076 — Measure, cut, and install battens straight and centered over board joints using the specified independent fastening method.
  • BE-077 — Fasten boards and battens without distortion, splitting, or restriction of required movement, while maintaining clearances at roofs, decks, grade, and paving.
  • BE-078 — Inspect the completed elevation for plumb, batten centering, spacing, fastening, movement gaps, clearances, and surface condition, and correct deficiencies before closeout.

Where This Skill Fits

The rainscreen fastening support confirmed in Project 10 and the layout and corner components installed in Project 11 only function correctly if the siding installed here maintains their designed drainage gap, ventilation path, and module spacing. The plumb boards and centered battens installed in this project establish the reference surface that Project 14's exterior trim must align with, and any cumulative drift left uncorrected here becomes a visible and functional defect at every window, door, and corner trim installed afterward.

This project uses the same balanced-layout and control-line method taught for horizontal lap siding in Chapter 12, adapted to a vertical orientation. Keeping the two cladding systems on separate elevations, as required by the System-Sequence Control section of this book, allows you to compare their layout, fastening, and joint-maintenance methods without mixing their component schedules.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the selected board-and-batten system's board widths, batten widths, joint coverage, fastening pattern, furring orientation, expansion requirements, and top or bottom ventilation details
  • confirming that the rainscreen and structural fastening support are suitable for vertical cladding
  • verifying WRB, flashing, trim, corners, openings, and base drainage before siding begins
  • developing a balanced board-and-batten layout from a defined centerline or corner reference
  • transferring vertical control lines across the elevation
  • staging, conditioning, cutting, and installing boards plumb in the approved starting sequence
  • fitting boards around windows, doors, vents, utility blocks, and flashings without restricting drainage
  • measuring, cutting, and installing battens over board joints with the required coverage and independent fastening
  • installing top and bottom closures, screens, trim, or ventilation components required by the system
  • inspecting the completed elevation and correcting deficiencies before documentation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • select the board width, batten width, joint coverage, or fastening pattern independently of the approved board-and-batten system
  • begin siding installation before the rainscreen, WRB, flashing, trim, corner, and opening preparation from Projects 2 through 11 have passed inspection
  • mix components, fasteners, or details from the Chapter 12 horizontal lap siding system with this vertical board-and-batten system unless the approved design includes a documented transition
  • block window head flashing, sill-pan outlets, weep paths, base drainage, or rainscreen ventilation with boards, battens, or fasteners

Any condition where the rainscreen fastening support, WRB, flashing, or opening preparation has not passed inspection, or where the approved module cannot be achieved within the product's permitted range, must stop work in that area until direction is obtained.

Trade Note

A vertical board-and-batten elevation makes cumulative layout drift visible immediately, because every board and batten runs the full height of the wall in view of the eye at once. Confirming plumb and module width at the intervals specified in this project, rather than only at the start and end of the elevation, is the most reliable way to prevent a visibly uneven result that would otherwise only be caught during the Project 19 full exterior inspection.

Setup Standard

Shop Tip

Dry-lay the first several boards and one batten across the ground or a flat surface before fastening anything to the wall. Confirming the module width and joint relationship on the ground catches a miscalculated layout before it becomes a fastened, visible defect on the elevation.

Common Field Fundamentals

Vertical board-and-batten siding depends on the same drainage-plane principles as every other cladding system in this book: water sheds down the face of the boards and battens, exits at the base without obstruction, and the rainscreen cavity behind the cladding remains open for ventilation and drying. The batten's role is decorative and joint-covering only; it is not a substitute for the open joint, shiplap engagement, or drainage gap that the approved system relies on for water management.

Balancing the Module Before Cutting Begins

Measure the wall width and calculate the board-and-batten module before making the first cut. If the calculated layout produces a narrow terminal piece at a corner, opening, or termination, adjust the module within the product's permitted range rather than installing an undersized piece. Do this calculation once, record it on the siding installation record, and use it for every board on the elevation; do not recalculate the module partway across the wall.

Measurement Cue — Recheck Plumb and Module at Every Control-Line Interval

Recheck board plumb and module width at the control-line interval specified in Step 13, not only at the start of the elevation. Vertical siding makes cumulative drift visible over the full wall height, and a small module error repeated across many boards becomes a large, uncorrectable gap by the far corner if it is not caught early.

Critical Safety Cue — Power Tool Setup at Height

Do not operate a circular saw, track saw, or jigsaw from an unsecured ladder or unsupported platform. Cut boards and battens at a supported cutting station at grade or on a secured platform, then carry finished pieces to the installation location. Disconnect power and engage the blade guard before adjusting or clearing any saw.

Field Safety Cue — Long Board Handling

Vertical boards run the full wall height and are longer and more awkward to handle than horizontal courses. Use the number of workers required to control the board without contacting powerlines, scaffolding edges, or ladder positions, and lower or lay down boards fully before repositioning ladders or platforms.

Manufacturer Cue — Furring Orientation and Fastening Backing

Confirm that the rainscreen furring installed in Project 10 provides the horizontal backing required for vertical board-and-batten siding before beginning layout. If the furring was installed in a vertical-only orientation for a different cladding system, do not fasten boards into unsupported drainage mat or WRB; stop work and obtain the cross-furring or support pattern specified for this system.

Code Alert — Clearance Requirements Vary by Jurisdiction

Minimum siding clearances above grade, roofs, decks, and paving vary by adopted code and jurisdiction. Verify the current clearance requirement with the authority having jurisdiction before installing the bottom course or closure.

F. Document Review and Prior Inspection Confirmation (Steps 1–3)

  1. Review the selected board-and-batten system, board widths, batten widths, joint coverage, fastening pattern, furring orientation, expansion requirements, and top or bottom ventilation details. Record each value on the siding installation record before layout begins.
  2. Confirm that the rainscreen and structural fastening support are suitable for vertical cladding. Verify the furring orientation installed in Project 10 provides horizontal backing at the spacing this system requires.
  3. Verify WRB, flashing, trim, corners, openings, and base drainage before siding begins. Confirm each prior project's inspection record shows a passed status for the elevation.

G. Layout and Control Lines (Steps 4–7)

  1. Measure the wall width and develop a balanced board-and-batten layout from a defined centerline or corner reference. Calculate the module using the board and batten widths recorded in Step 1.
  2. Identify narrow terminal pieces and adjust the module within the product's permitted range. Do not install a terminal board or batten narrower than the manufacturer's minimum without adjusting the overall module.
  3. Transfer vertical control lines across the elevation. Use a chalk line and level referenced from the layout established in Step 4.
  4. Inspect, stage, and condition siding according to manufacturer requirements. Store boards and battens flat, dry, supported, and protected from soil and weather before cutting.

H. Board Installation (Steps 8–15)

  1. Measure and cut the first board to the required length and termination clearances. Use the reference face and end specified by the manufacturer for the cut.
  2. Seal or prime field-cut ends and edges where required. Follow the manufacturer's specified sealing or priming method before installation.
  3. Position the first board plumb at the approved starting reference. Align it to the centerline or corner reference and control line established in Step 6.
  4. Fasten the board into the required backing using the specified fastener location, spacing, and seating condition. Confirm fastener embedment into the furring, not only into the board.
  5. Install successive boards while maintaining the approved open joint, shiplap engagement, or edge relationship. Check module width against the calculated layout before fastening each board.
  6. Recheck plumb and module width at controlled intervals. Use the control-line interval established in Step 6 to catch drift before it accumulates.
  7. Fit boards around windows, doors, vents, utility blocks, and flashings without restricting drainage. Cut boards to preserve the drainage and clearance dimensions established in Projects 6, 9, 16, and 17.
  8. Maintain required clearances at roofs, decks, grade, paving, and horizontal surfaces. Verify each clearance against the Acceptance Standards table before continuing to the next board.

I. Batten Installation and Closures (Steps 16–19)

  1. Measure and cut battens to cover joints by the required amount. Confirm the batten length matches the adjoining board length and required clearances.
  2. Install battens straight, centered, and independently fastened where the system requires it. Verify centering over the joint before driving fasteners.
  3. Allow movement at joints and terminations without splitting or buckling components. Maintain the movement gap specified by the manufacturer at every termination.
  4. Install top and bottom closures, screens, trim, or ventilation components required by the system. Keep the base drainage outlet and top ventilation path open during installation.

J. Inspection, Correction, and Documentation (Steps 20–22)

  1. Inspect board plumb, batten centering, spacing, fastening, movement gaps, clearances, and surface condition. Complete the Inspection Checklist below before considering the elevation finished.
  2. Correct cumulative drift, split boards, tight joints, missed backing, blocked drainage, and distorted battens. Remove and replace any component that cannot be corrected within tolerance.
  3. Clean and document the finished elevation. Photograph the completed installation and file the siding installation record before Project 14 trim work begins.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the completed elevation using the same checks regardless of which wall it covers.

  • Layout check: confirm the module width matches the calculated layout and no terminal piece falls below the manufacturer's permitted minimum.
  • Plumb check: confirm every board is plumb within tolerance at each control-line interval.
  • Joint check: confirm the approved open joint, shiplap engagement, or edge relationship is maintained across the elevation, including at openings.
  • Batten check: confirm every batten is centered over its joint with the required coverage and independently fastened where specified.
  • Fastening check: confirm fasteners are seated per the manufacturer's schedule without overdriving, splitting, or restricting movement.
  • Drainage and clearance check: confirm base venting, top closures, and required clearances remain open and correct.

Complete the Siding Installation Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 14.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Module Width Drifts Across the Elevation

Probable cause: the layout calculation in Step 4 was not followed consistently, or a board was installed slightly wider or narrower than specified without rechecking against the control lines.
Confirmation: measure the module width at each control-line interval and compare against the calculated layout recorded on the siding installation record.
Correction: remove and reset boards back to the last correct control line, then reinstall using the specified module width.
Reinspection: recheck module width at every control line across the corrected section before continuing.

A Board or Batten Splits During Fastening

Probable cause: a fastener was driven too close to the board or batten edge, or without a required pilot hole, or the material was not conditioned per manufacturer requirements before installation.
Confirmation: inspect the split location and compare fastener placement against the approved fastening schedule.
Correction: remove and replace the split component; do not fasten through or patch a split board or batten.
Reinspection: confirm the replacement component is fastened per the schedule and remains free of new splits.

A Batten Does Not Fully Cover Its Joint

Probable cause: the batten was not centered before fastening, or the batten width does not match the specified coverage for the installed module width.
Confirmation: measure the coverage beyond each edge of the joint and compare against the manufacturer's required minimum.
Correction: remove and reposition the batten, or replace it with the correct width specified by the manufacturer.
Reinspection: recheck coverage at both edges of the joint after correction.

Boards Are Fastened Directly Into Unsupported Drainage Mat

Probable cause: the rainscreen furring installed in Project 10 does not provide the horizontal backing this vertical system requires at the fastened location.
Confirmation: probe the fastening location and confirm whether the fastener reaches solid furring or only drainage mat and WRB.
Correction: stop work, remove the affected board, and install the cross-furring or support pattern specified for this system before refastening.
Reinspection: confirm fastener embedment into solid backing at every location across the corrected section.

Base Drainage or Top Ventilation Is Blocked

Probable cause: the bottom closure, insect screen, or top venting component was installed without maintaining the open path established in Project 10.
Confirmation: inspect the base and top terminations for continuous, unobstructed openings.
Correction: remove and correct the closure installation to restore the required open path.
Reinspection: confirm the drainage and ventilation path remains open along the full width of the elevation.

Field Terms

Module Width
The combined width of one board plus one adjoining joint, as specified by the approved board-and-batten system.
Control Line
A vertical reference line transferred across the elevation to check plumb and module drift during installation.
Centerline or Corner Reference
The starting reference point from which the board-and-batten module is developed across the elevation.
Batten Coverage
The required minimum distance a batten extends beyond each edge of the joint it covers.
Independent Fastening
Fastening a batten directly into the required backing without relying on the boards beneath it for support.
Movement Gap
The clearance maintained at joints and terminations that allows movement without splitting or buckling.
Rainscreen Cavity
The drainage and ventilation gap behind the siding, established by the furring installed in Project 10.
Base Venting
The opening at the bottom termination that allows drainage and airflow into the rainscreen cavity.
Top Closure
The component installed at the top termination that ventilates or seals the rainscreen cavity, per the approved system.
Terminal Piece
A board or batten located at a corner, opening, or termination whose width may require module adjustment.

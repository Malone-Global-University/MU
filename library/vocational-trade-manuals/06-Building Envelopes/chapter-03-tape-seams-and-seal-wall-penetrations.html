Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete the continuity of the water-resistive barrier (WRB) installed in Project 02 by treating every seam, fastener tear, and approved service penetration with manufacturer-compatible tapes, primers, patches, boots, gaskets, and liquid flashing. The finished work product is a continuous WRB drainage plane in which every seam is taped or otherwise correctly lapped, every fastener tear and area of damage is repaired, and every approved pipe, duct, cable, conduit, or electrical-box penetration is flashed to shed water outward and downward without trapping it.

You will review the WRB manufacturer's approved accessories, confirm compatibility among the WRB, flashing products, penetration materials, sheathing, and planned cladding, inspect and treat every seam, repair fastener tears and damage, verify and flash every approved penetration in shingle-lap order, preserve required drainage and vent openings, inspect the completed work, correct failures, and photograph and document every detail before it is concealed by rainscreen, flashing, and siding work in later projects.

This project follows directly from Project 02, which installed the continuous WRB layer, and it sits immediately before Project 04, which flashes the first window rough opening. The seam and penetration treatment completed here must be finished and inspected before rough-opening flashing begins, because window and door flashing in later projects depends on an already-continuous WRB field.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BE-013 — Tape WRB seams using the manufacturer-approved tape, primer, and tooling method while preserving shingle-lap drainage geometry.
  • BE-014 — Seal fastener tears, fishmouths, punctures, and other WRB damage using an approved patch method extending beyond the defect.
  • BE-015 — Seal approved pipe penetrations with a compatible boot, gasket, or flashing membrane installed in bottom-to-top shedding order.
  • BE-016 — Seal approved wire and cable penetrations using the manufacturer-approved flexible method without trapping water.
  • BE-017 — Maintain the drainage plane by preserving designed drainage space, weeps, vents, and movement-dependent components while sealing.
  • BE-018 — Inspect WRB continuity at seams and penetrations, identify failures, and correct them using the approved removal, cleaning, patching, and reinspection procedure.

Where This Skill Fits

The WRB installed in Project 02 sheds water at its laps, but the field seams, fastener locations, and service penetrations are still open paths for water unless treated here. Every rough-opening flashing detail beginning in Project 04 assumes an already-continuous WRB field around the opening; a missed seam or an unflashed penetration left from this project becomes a hidden water path that the Project 19 full exterior water-control inspection must trace back to its source.

The penetration-flashing method used here — sill layer first, side layers overlapping the sill, top layer integrated beneath the WRB above — is the same shingle-lap method used for window and door flashing in Projects 4 through 9 and for exterior wall penetrations in Project 16. Learning it correctly here protects every later penetration-flashing project in this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing the WRB manufacturer's approved tapes, liquid flashings, boots, gaskets, patches, primers, rollers, and temperature or substrate limits
  • confirming compatibility among the WRB, flashing products, pipe or cable materials, sheathing, and planned cladding
  • inspecting and taping horizontal and vertical seams while preserving shingle-lap geometry
  • repairing fastener tears, fishmouths, punctures, and damaged areas with approved patches
  • verifying and flashing every approved pipe, duct, cable, conduit, and electrical-box penetration
  • preserving drainage space, weeps, vents, combustion-air paths, and components requiring movement
  • inspecting the completed seams and penetrations and correcting failures
  • photographing and documenting concealed penetration details before later work covers them

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • substitute a tape, primer, patch, boot, gasket, or liquid flashing not approved by the WRB and penetration manufacturers
  • enlarge a penetration opening beyond what the approved component requires, or relocate a planned penetration without approval
  • seal active vents, drainage openings, weeps, combustion-air paths, or components requiring movement
  • proceed to window or door rough-opening flashing in Project 4 or 7 before this project's seam and penetration treatment has passed inspection

Any seam, tear, or penetration condition that cannot be corrected with an approved product or method must stop work in that area until direction is obtained.

Trade Note

A taped seam or flashed penetration is only as reliable as the surface it is bonded to. Treating cleaning, drying, and priming as optional steps, rather than as the conditions that make adhesion possible, is the most common cause of seam and penetration failures traced back to this project during the Project 19 full exterior inspection.

Setup Standard

Do not begin seam or penetration treatment until the WRB manufacturer's approved tapes, primers, liquid flashings, boots, gaskets, and patches are confirmed on site and compatible with the sheathing, pipe or cable materials, and planned cladding (Steps 1–2). Do not tape or patch a seam that has not first been inspected for required overlap, cleanliness, flatness, and fastening (Step 3).

Shop Tip

Tape vertical seams from bottom to top in the same shingle-lap direction as the WRB courses themselves. Taping from the top down, or in short overlapping sections worked in mixed directions, is a common cause of trapped wrinkles and incomplete adhesion that only becomes visible once the tape has already been rolled.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every seam and penetration treated in this project follows the same drainage logic used throughout the book: the layer above always sheds onto the layer below, drainage outlets remain open, and nothing is sealed shut simply because it is convenient to seal. Learning to recognize which conditions require sealing and which must remain open protects every later flashing detail in this book, from window sill pans in Project 4 to exterior wall penetrations in Project 16.

Recognizing What Must Stay Open

Every wall has designed openings that must never be sealed: active vents, drainage openings, weeps, combustion-air paths, and components that rely on movement to function. Before treating any seam or penetration, identify these openings on the wall and mark them so they are not mistakenly taped, patched, or sealed during the work that follows.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Required Overlap Before Taping

Measure the overlap at each seam and the extension of each patch beyond its defect before applying tape, and compare the field measurement against the WRB manufacturer's minimum overlap and patch-extension requirements. Do not tape a seam or patch a tear based on appearance alone; confirm the measurement first.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Seal Designed Drainage or Vent Openings

Do not tape, patch, or seal across active vents, drainage openings, weeps, combustion-air paths, or any component that depends on movement to function. Sealing a designed opening to make a wall look complete creates a concealed condition that traps water or blocks required airflow, and it will not be visible again until damage appears. Identify every such opening before starting seam or penetration work in that area.

Field Safety Cue — Elevated Access During Seam and Penetration Work

Use only approved ladders, scaffolds, lifts, or work platforms to reach every seam and penetration location. Do not stand on window or door rough openings, unsecured platforms, makeshift supports, or siding materials while taping or flashing at height.

Manufacturer Cue — Primer, Temperature, and Substrate Limits

Confirm the WRB manufacturer's primer requirement, open time, minimum and maximum application temperature, and substrate compatibility before taping, patching, or flashing any seam or penetration. Do not substitute a tape, primer, boot, or liquid flashing not identified as compatible by the WRB and penetration manufacturers.

Code Alert — Concealed Water-Control Documentation Varies by Jurisdiction

Required photographic documentation, inspection hold-points, and permit requirements before concealed seams and penetration flashing are covered by rainscreen or siding vary by adopted code and jurisdiction. Verify the current requirement with the authority having jurisdiction and the general contractor before treating this project's documentation as complete.

E. Product Review and Compatibility (Steps 1–2)

  1. Review the WRB manufacturer's approved tapes, liquid flashings, boots, gaskets, patches, primers, rollers, and temperature or substrate limits. Confirm the current product data sheet against the project's document-control log before using any product.
  2. Confirm compatibility among the WRB, flashing products, pipe or cable materials, sheathing, and planned cladding. Do not proceed with a product combination that the manufacturers have not identified as compatible.

F. Seam Inspection and Taping (Steps 3–10)

  1. Inspect all horizontal and vertical seams for required overlap, cleanliness, flatness, and fastening. Measure overlap at intervals sufficient to detect drift along the full seam length.
  2. Clean and dry seam surfaces using the manufacturer-approved method. Remove dust, moisture, and residue before priming or taping.
  3. Apply required primer and allow it to reach the specified condition. Observe the manufacturer's stated open time before applying tape.
  4. Tape vertical seams from bottom to top while maintaining full contact and avoiding wrinkles. Work in the same shingle-lap direction as the WRB courses.
  5. Tape horizontal seams only where required, preserving the underlying shingle-lap geometry. Do not tape over a horizontal lap in a way that reverses its drainage direction.
  6. Roll pressure-sensitive tape with the specified tool to activate adhesion. Roll the full width and length of every taped seam, not only its edges.
  7. Repair fastener tears, fishmouths, punctures, and damaged areas with patches extending the required distance beyond the defect. Center each patch over its defect and confirm the extension measurement before rolling.
  8. Remove and patch unauthorized fasteners or holes according to the approved method. Treat every unauthorized penetration the same as accidental damage.

G. Penetration Flashing (Steps 11–18)

  1. Verify the final size and location of each pipe, duct, cable, conduit, and electrical-box penetration. Compare each location against the approved penetration schedule before cutting the WRB.
  2. Cut the WRB as tightly as practical around each approved penetration. Do not oversize a cut beyond what the approved flashing component requires.
  3. Install a compatible boot, gasket, flashing membrane, or liquid flashing around round and irregular penetrations. Select the product matched to the penetration's shape and diameter.
  4. Form bottom, side, and top layers so water from above sheds over the penetration flashing. Install the bottom layer first, the side layers overlapping it, and the top layer last, integrated beneath the WRB above.
  5. Install backing or mounting blocks where the approved penetration system requires them. Confirm the block provides flat, supported bearing for the fixture or cover installed in a later project.
  6. Preserve drainage space below horizontal projections and blocks. Do not create a back-pitched shelf that traps water against the wall.
  7. Seal small wire or cable penetrations with the approved flexible method without trapping water. Confirm the sealing method allows incidental moisture to drain rather than pool.
  8. Do not seal active vents, drainage openings, weeps, combustion-air paths, or components requiring movement. Confirm every marked opening from the Common Field Fundamentals review remains open before continuing.

H. Inspection and Documentation (Steps 19–21)

  1. Inspect all seams and penetrations for adhesion, full contact, reverse laps, voids, incompatible materials, and blocked drainage. Check every seam and penetration individually rather than sampling a portion of the wall.
  2. Correct failed areas using the approved removal, cleaning, patching, and reinspection procedure. Do not layer new tape or sealant over a failed seam or penetration without first removing the failed material.
  3. Photograph concealed penetration details and update the penetration record. Label each photograph with its elevation and location before rainscreen or flashing work in later projects conceals it.

Common Verification Inspection

Inspect the treated wall system using the same checks regardless of which elevation it covers.

  • Product check: confirm every tape, primer, patch, boot, gasket, and liquid flashing used matches the approved manufacturer instructions and compatibility requirements.
  • Seam check: confirm every horizontal and vertical seam has the required overlap, full adhesion, and no wrinkles, voids, or reverse laps.
  • Patch check: confirm every repaired tear, fishmouth, or puncture has a patch extending the required distance beyond the defect.
  • Penetration check: confirm every approved penetration has sill, side, and top flashing layers installed in shingle-lap order with preserved drainage.
  • Open-opening check: confirm every active vent, drainage opening, weep, combustion-air path, and movement-dependent component remains unsealed.
  • Documentation check: confirm every seam, patch, and penetration location, product, and inspection result is recorded on the penetration record.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Tape Fails to Adhere or Lifts at the Edge

Probable cause: insufficient cleaning or drying before taping, missing or expired primer, or application outside the manufacturer's temperature range.
Confirmation: inspect the substrate condition and compare the application temperature at the time of work against the product data sheet.
Correction: remove the failed tape, reclean and dry the surface, reprime if required, and reapply tape within the specified temperature range.
Reinspection: repeat the adhesion and full-contact check across the entire repaired seam.

A Patch Does Not Fully Cover a Fastener Tear

Probable cause: the patch was cut to the size of the visible defect rather than the manufacturer's required extension distance.
Confirmation: measure the patch extension in every direction from the edge of the defect and compare against the manufacturer's minimum requirement.
Correction: remove the undersized patch and install a correctly sized patch centered over the defect.
Reinspection: repeat the extension measurement and adhesion check after correction.

A Penetration Flashing Layer Is Installed Out of Sequence

Probable cause: the top or side layer was installed before the sill layer, or the layers were installed without regard to shingle-lap order.
Confirmation: inspect the layer sequence at the penetration and trace whether water from above would shed over or behind each layer.
Correction: remove and reinstall the affected layers in bottom-to-top shingle-lap order.
Reinspection: repeat the sequence check and confirm the top layer is integrated beneath the WRB above.

A Drainage Opening or Vent Was Accidentally Sealed

Probable cause: the opening was not marked before seam or penetration work began, or it was mistaken for a defect requiring a patch.
Confirmation: compare the sealed location against the approved plans and the marked drainage-and-vent locations from the Common Field Fundamentals review.
Correction: remove the sealant, tape, or patch material and restore the opening to its designed condition.
Reinspection: confirm the opening drains or vents freely after correction.

A Horizontal Projection at a Penetration Traps Water

Probable cause: the flashing or mounting block was installed level or back-pitched instead of sloped outward.
Confirmation: check the slope of the horizontal projection with a level and compare against the approved detail.
Correction: remove and reinstall the projection with the required outward slope.
Reinspection: repeat the slope check and confirm water sheds away from the wall.

An Incompatible Product Was Used at a Seam or Penetration

Probable cause: a substitute tape, primer, boot, or liquid flashing was used without confirming manufacturer compatibility.
Confirmation: compare the product actually installed against the approved compatibility list for the WRB, flashing, and penetration materials.
Correction: remove the incompatible product and reinstall the approved product using the correct preparation method.
Reinspection: repeat the adhesion and continuity check once the approved product is installed.

Field Terms

Shingle-Lap Order
The bottom-to-top, side-then-top installation sequence that keeps upper layers discharging onto lower layers.
Reverse Lap
A seam or flashing condition that directs water behind, rather than over, the layer below; never acceptable.
Fishmouth
An open gap at a seam or patch edge where the material does not lie flat against the surface below it.
Back Dam
A raised edge that prevents water from moving inward past a horizontal projection or penetration flashing.
Approved Penetration
A pipe, duct, cable, conduit, or electrical-box opening identified on the approved plans and penetration schedule.
Mounting Block
A supported backing component that provides flat bearing for a fixture, vent, or cover installed in a later project.
Open Time
The manufacturer-specified interval after priming during which tape or flashing must be applied to achieve proper adhesion.
Penetration Record
The documented log of each treated penetration's location, product, installation condition, and inspection result.
Drainage Plane
The continuous WRB surface that directs incidental water downward and outward across the wall.
Combustion-Air Path
An opening required to supply air to a fuel-burning appliance; never sealed during seam or penetration work.

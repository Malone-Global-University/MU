Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build one wood privacy-fence section from a verified property line through a finished, inspected run of posts, rails, and pickets. The finished work product is a straight, plumb, adequately anchored fence section that stays within documented property limits, resists the wind exposure of its location, and presents uniform height, spacing, and ground clearance along its full length.

You will confirm the fence line against the Project 01 readiness package, locate utilities before any post hole is dug, excavate and set posts on approved bearing, install rails to a consistent elevation and grade strategy, lay out and fasten pickets or panels with corrosion-compatible hardware, and inspect the finished section against line, plumb, height, spacing, and clearance standards.

This project assumes Project 01's property-limits and utility-locate findings are complete and current for the fence line being built. It does not cover pool barriers, security fencing, retaining structures, or other specialty barrier types, which carry their own height, opening, self-closing, and hardware rules and are outside this project's scope.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • OS-055 — Lay out the fence line from verified property references, marking post centers, corners, and gate openings within approved limits.
  • OS-056 — Set fence posts on approved bearing, plumb and aligned, using the specified footing, gravel, concrete, or post-base detail.
  • OS-057 — Install rails at consistent elevations using approved brackets, fasteners, or joinery, maintaining continuity across grade changes.
  • OS-058 — Install pickets or panels at uniform spacing and ground clearance using corrosion-compatible exterior fasteners.
  • OS-059 — Maintain fence height and alignment along the full run, correcting drift before it accumulates over multiple posts.
  • OS-060 — Inspect the completed fence section for property position, plumb, line, attachment, spacing, wind bracing, and hazard-free edges.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on the property-limits and utility-locate findings recorded in Project 01. A fence line laid out without those confirmed references risks an encroachment or a utility strike that no amount of careful carpentry can correct after the fact.

The gate work in Project 11 depends on the hinge and latch posts set here being stable, plumb, and adequately supported. A fence section built out of line, out of plumb, or on undersized post support will carry that error directly into the gate project and into every year of service the fence is expected to provide.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming the fence line against verified property, setback, and easement references
  • requesting or confirming a utility locate for the fence line before any post hole is dug
  • excavating and setting posts on approved bearing, footing, or post-base hardware
  • installing rails at consistent elevations using approved brackets, fasteners, or joinery
  • laying out and fastening pickets, boards, or panels at uniform spacing and ground clearance
  • inspecting the completed section for line, plumb, height, spacing, attachment, and hazard-free edges

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • dig any post hole before utility locating is confirmed for the fence line
  • build a pool, security, retaining, or other specialty barrier without the exact specialized design, height, opening, and hardware rules for that barrier type
  • set a post, rail, or picket outside the verified property line, setback, or easement limits recorded in Project 01
  • proceed to gate installation in Project 11 with hinge or latch posts that have not reached the approved footing condition

Any conflict between the planned fence line and a documented property limit, setback, or easement must be resolved before post holes are excavated.

Trade Note

A fence section is judged over its full run, not at any single post. A post that is plumb and on line by itself can still put the whole section out of true if the string reference it was set from has drifted. Recheck the string line and elevation reference at regular intervals while setting posts, not only at the two end posts.

Setup Standard

Do not excavate any post hole until the utility locate for the fence line is confirmed and marked, and the fence baseline has been checked against the property-limits sketch from Project 01. A post hole dug before the locate is confirmed must be treated as an unresolved condition and reported before work continues.

Shop Tip

Set and brace the two end or corner posts first, then stretch a string between them at both a top and a bottom reference height before setting any intermediate post. Checking each intermediate post against two string heights, not one, catches a post that is plumb but leaning the string out of a true vertical plane.

Common Field Fundamentals

Fence work follows the same sequence as every other post-supported structure in this book: confirm references, excavate and set posts on approved bearing, build the framing the posts support, then finish and inspect. A fence differs mainly in scale and repetition — the same plumb, line, and connection standard has to hold true across every post in a long run, not just one.

Fence Line, Property Limits, and Utility Locating

Every post center is checked against the property-limits sketch produced in Project 01, not against a neighbor's existing fence or an assumed line. A fence built from an assumed line risks an encroachment claim that can force the fence to be moved after it is fully built.

Code Alert — Setbacks, Height, and HOA Restrictions

Fence height, setback distance, and style restrictions vary by jurisdiction and by neighborhood. Verify the current requirements for the specific fence line before setting any post, and do not treat a neighboring property's fence as evidence of what is allowed on this site.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm the Utility Locate Before Excavation

Do not excavate any post hole until the utility locate for the fence line is confirmed and marked on site. Never dig or drive a post-hole digger deeper than flag depth in an unmarked area. Contact with a buried utility line can cause serious injury, service outages, or death.

Field Safety Cue — Excavation and Post Setting

Keep hands and feet clear of a power auger's rotating flighting, and keep other workers clear of the swing radius while excavating. Watch footing near open post holes and concrete mixing areas, and keep the work area free of trip hazards from stacked lumber and tools.

Measurement Cue — Recheck Line and Spacing Along the Full Run

Check post spacing, plumb, and string-line position at every post, not only at the two end posts. A slight drift at each intermediate post can accumulate into a visible bow or an out-of-spacing final bay by the far end of a long run.

E. Preparation — Document Review and Layout Planning

  1. Review the approved fence plan. Confirm fence line, setbacks, height, style, wind exposure, post spacing, material, gate locations, and permit requirements.
  2. Confirm the fence is a standard privacy design. Verify it is not a pool, security, retaining, or other specialty barrier unless the exact specialized design has been provided separately.
  3. Confirm or request the utility locate for the fence line. Do not proceed to layout until the locate is scheduled or confirmed.
  4. Establish the fence baseline and end points. Reference the confirmed property corners and setback distances from Project 01.
  5. Mark post centers along the baseline. Space centers per the approved plan and flag each one distinctly from property and utility markings.
  6. Verify gates, corners, grade changes, sight lines, and easements. Confirm the marked layout accounts for every opening and grade transition before excavation begins.

F. Post Excavation and Setting Procedure

  1. Excavate post holes to the approved dimensions and bearing. Confirm depth and diameter at each marked center before placing any footing material.
  2. Install gravel, concrete, sleeves, or post bases according to the approved footing detail. Do not substitute a footing method that was not specified for this fence line.
  3. Set the end and corner posts first. These posts establish the reference for every intermediate post in the run.
  4. Brace each set post plumb in two directions. Check plumb on two adjacent faces before the footing material is placed or allowed to set.
  5. Stretch alignment lines between the set end and corner posts. Establish both a top and a bottom reference height for intermediate post setting.
  6. Set intermediate posts to spacing and line. Check each intermediate post against both string references, not only the top line.
  7. Allow footings to reach the approved condition before loading the posts. Do not attach rails or remove bracing before the footing material has set as specified.

G. Rail Installation

  1. Mark rail elevations on each post from the confirmed reference. Carry the same elevation reference across the full run unless the approved grade strategy calls for a stepped or racked transition.
  2. Install rails using the approved brackets, fasteners, or joinery. Do not substitute a generic screw or bracket for a specified structural fastener.
  3. Maintain rail continuity and the approved grade strategy across slope changes. Confirm each rail run stays consistent with its neighbors before pickets are installed.

H. Picket or Panel Installation and Finish

  1. Lay out pickets, boards, or panels along the rail run. Dry-lay or mark spacing before fastening the first piece.
  2. Maintain the approved ground clearance and spacing along the full run. Recheck clearance and spacing at intervals, not only at the starting point.
  3. Fasten pickets or panels with corrosion-compatible exterior fasteners. Confirm fastener material matches the lumber treatment before driving the first fastener.
  4. Cut and treat exposed ends as required. Apply the specified field-cut preservative to any cut end of treated lumber.
  5. Install caps where specified. Confirm cap attachment does not compromise ground clearance or rail continuity.

I. Inspection and Closeout

  1. Inspect property position, height, line, post plumb, rail attachment, picket spacing, wind bracing, ground clearance, and sharp hazards. Check these items along the full run, not only at representative points.
  2. Document the final fence section. Record post layout, footing details, fastener types, and inspection results for the project file.

Common Inspection Review

Inspect the completed fence section using the same checks regardless of style or picket pattern.

  • Property-position check: confirm every post falls within the verified property line, setback, and easement limits.
  • Plumb and line check: confirm every post is plumb on two faces and the run holds a true line along its full length.
  • Rail check: confirm rails are attached with approved fasteners at a consistent elevation and grade strategy.
  • Spacing and clearance check: confirm pickets or panels are uniformly spaced with the approved ground clearance maintained.
  • Hardware check: confirm all fasteners and connectors are corrosion-compatible with the lumber treatment used.
  • Hazard check: confirm no sharp edges, protruding fasteners, or unstable posts remain.

Complete the Fence Section Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 11.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Fence Post Encroaches on a Setback or Property Line

Probable cause: the fence baseline was established from an assumed reference, such as an existing fence or hedge line, rather than from the confirmed property corners in Project 01.
Confirmation: re-measure the post position from the confirmed property corner and compare it to the current jurisdictional setback.
Correction: remove and reset the affected post from the confirmed corner and setback distance, adjusting spacing on adjacent posts as needed.
Reinspection: repeat the property-position check on the corrected post.

Post Hole Was Dug Before the Utility Locate Was Confirmed

Probable cause: excavation began before the locate request was submitted or before the marked results were confirmed on site.
Confirmation: check the locate service's confirmation date and time against the date excavation began.
Correction: stop excavation immediately, report the condition, and do not resume digging until the locate is confirmed and marked.
Reinspection: repeat the utility-locate confirmation check before continuing excavation at any remaining post.

Fence Run Bows or Drifts Off Line Over Its Length

Probable cause: intermediate posts were checked against a single string height, allowing a lean to accumulate without being caught early in the run.
Confirmation: sight down the completed run and measure post-face position against both the top and bottom string references at several points.
Correction: loosen and reset any post found out of line, rechecking plumb and both string references before re-securing the footing or bracing.
Reinspection: repeat the plumb and line check along the corrected run.

Picket Spacing or Ground Clearance Varies Along the Run

Probable cause: spacing was set by eye rather than with a consistent gauge, or ground clearance was not rechecked as grade elevation changed along the run.
Confirmation: measure spacing and clearance at multiple points and compare against the approved plan.
Correction: remove and reset the affected pickets using a spacing gauge, and adjust clearance to the approved dimension at each corrected location.
Reinspection: repeat the spacing and clearance check on the corrected section.

Incompatible Fasteners Used with Treated Lumber

Probable cause: generic or uncoated fasteners were substituted for the specified corrosion-compatible hardware during rail or picket installation.
Confirmation: inspect fastener heads and connectors for material type against the approved fastener schedule.
Correction: remove incompatible fasteners and replace them with the specified corrosion-compatible hardware at every affected connection.
Reinspection: repeat the hardware check on the corrected connections.

Post Was Loaded Before the Footing Reached the Approved Condition

Probable cause: rails or bracing were removed before the concrete or approved footing material had set as specified.
Confirmation: check placement records against the required cure or set time for the footing material used.
Correction: re-brace the affected post and do not resume rail or picket loading until the footing has reached the approved condition.
Reinspection: repeat the plumb and post-stability check before reloading the post.

Cleanup

  • Remove excavation spoil, gravel bags, and concrete residue from the work area, keeping drainage paths and adjacent hardscape clear

  • Photograph the completed section along its full run for the project records before removing any remaining layout stakes
  • Label and file the fence-section worksheet, layout records, and inspection results together
  • Leave hinge and latch post locations clearly marked and undisturbed for Project 11

Field Terms

Fence Line
The confirmed baseline the fence follows, established from verified property references.
Post Spacing
The on-center distance between fence posts, set by the approved rail and picket design.
Rail Continuity
Consistent rail elevation and connection along the fence run.
Ground Clearance
The gap maintained between the bottom of pickets or panels and finished grade.
Corrosion Compatibility
Matching fastener and connector material to the lumber treatment to prevent galvanic corrosion.
Wind Bracing
The post support, spacing, and connection detail sized to resist the wind exposure of the location.
Neighbor-Facing Side
The finished or "good" face of the fence, oriented as required by the approved design.
End Post
A post at the termination of a fence run, typically requiring stronger bracing than an intermediate post.
Corner Post
A post at a change of fence direction, carrying loads from two rail directions.
Racked Rail
A rail installed at an angle to follow a sloped grade while keeping pickets plumb.

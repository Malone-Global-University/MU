Job Overview

This project qualifies you to build, brace, hang, adjust, and inspect one wood fence gate matched to an accepted opening in a fence section built or verified in Project 10. The finished work product is a square, diagonally braced gate frame hung on stable hinge and latch posts, swinging without dragging or sagging, closing fully against its stop, and latching securely through repeated open-and-close cycles.

You will measure the opening at top, middle, and bottom; size the gate frame to the approved operating clearances; build and square the frame on a flat surface; install the diagonal brace or anti-sag system in the correct load direction; hang the gate on its hinge post; install and adjust the latch, strike, stop, and any self-closing or self-latching hardware the design requires; and inspect clearances, swing, and hardware before the gate is put into service.

This project assumes the fence section and hinge/latch posts from Project 10 are complete, plumb, and stable. A gate hung from a post that has not been verified to carry gate load and leverage will sag or pull loose regardless of how well the gate itself is built. No gate is hung, and no hinge is loaded, until the supporting posts are confirmed adequate for the gate's weight and swing.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • OS-061 — Measure the gate opening at top, middle, and bottom and determine gate-frame dimensions from approved operating clearances.
  • OS-062 — Build a square gate frame from straight, exterior-rated material.
  • OS-063 — Install diagonal bracing, cable, or an anti-sag system in the correct load direction.
  • OS-064 — Hang gate hinges using approved through-bolts or structural fasteners and support the gate through the hanging sequence.
  • OS-065 — Install latch, strike, stop, handle, drop rod, and any required self-closing or self-latching hardware.
  • OS-066 — Inspect gate operation, clearances, latch engagement, post movement, and hazards through repeated cycling.

Where This Skill Fits

This project depends directly on Project 10. The hinge post and latch post, their footing depth, bracing, and plumb condition were established there, and this project loads those posts for the first time. Any post movement discovered while hanging the gate must be traced back to the Project 10 installation before the gate is finished, not worked around with extra hardware.

The habit built here — measuring the actual opening rather than trusting the plan dimension, squaring the frame before it ever touches a hinge, and cycling the finished gate repeatedly before calling it complete — carries forward into every hinged, latched, or moving component built later in this book, including shed doors in Project 17 and enclosure lids and doors in Project 18.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • measuring an existing fence opening and confirming hinge- and latch-post stability
  • determining gate-frame dimensions from approved operating clearances
  • building and squaring a rectangular gate frame from exterior-rated material
  • installing infill matched to the fence section it serves
  • installing a diagonal brace, cable, or anti-sag system in the correct load direction
  • hanging the gate with approved hinges and structural fasteners
  • installing and adjusting latch, strike, stop, handle, drop rod, and self-closing or self-latching hardware where required
  • cycling and inspecting the finished gate

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • hang a gate on a hinge post that has not been confirmed stable, plumb, and adequately supported
  • substitute surface screws or undersized fasteners for the specified through-bolts or structural hinge fasteners
  • design or install the specialized height, opening, self-closing, self-latching, or climb-resistance hardware required for pool, spa, child-care, animal, or security gates without the exact approved specialized design
  • leave the gate in service with an unresolved sag, drag, latch failure, or pinch hazard

Any post movement, frame twist, hardware failure, or unresolved sag discovered during hanging or cycling must be corrected and reinspected before this project is declared complete.

Trade Note

A gate is the only part of a fence section that is designed to move, and every force that a fixed picket or rail never experiences is concentrated onto its two hinges and one latch. Frame square, correct brace direction, and hinge-post integrity are not optional refinements on a gate — they are the entire reason a gate stays square and functional for years instead of sagging within a season.

Setup Standard

Do not begin building the gate frame until the hinge and latch posts have been confirmed stable, plumb, and adequately supported for gate load and leverage. A gate built to fit an opening whose posts later prove unstable will require rework at the frame, not just the hinges.

Shop Tip

Measure the opening at top, middle, and bottom before cutting any frame material. An opening that looks parallel at the top can taper toward the bottom, and a frame cut to only the top measurement can bind or gap along the swing.

Reference Conventions

Frame Square and Anti-Sag Direction

A rectangular frame with no diagonal bracing will rack out of square under its own hinge-side weight over time. The diagonal brace or cable must run from the bottom hinge-side corner to the top latch-side corner, so that the brace is in compression or tension in the direction that resists the frame's natural sag rather than reinforcing it.

Measurement Cue — Measure the Opening at Three Heights

Measure the clear opening at the top, middle, and bottom before sizing the frame. Use the narrowest of the three measurements, minus the required operating clearance on each side, to set the frame width so the gate does not bind anywhere in its swing.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Hinge-Post Stability Before Loading It

Do not hang a gate on a hinge post that has not been confirmed plumb, adequately supported, and connected as specified in Project 10. A post that appears solid by hand-push can still fail under the sustained leverage of a hung and cycled gate, dropping the gate suddenly and creating a crush or impact hazard.

Field Safety Cue — Supporting the Gate While Hanging

Support the gate at its finished elevation with shims, blocks, or a jack while hinges are marked, drilled, and fastened. Do not rely on a second person's grip alone to hold gate weight during drilling, and keep hands clear of the hinge-side gap as temporary support is removed.

Code Alert — Specialized Barrier Gates

Pool, spa, child-care, animal, and security gates are governed by specialized height, opening, self-closing, and self-latching requirements that vary by jurisdiction. Do not install a standard privacy-fence gate as a substitute for a required specialized barrier gate; obtain the exact approved design before proceeding.

E. Preparation — Measurement and Design Review

  1. Review the approved gate width, height, weight, swing, slope, hinges, latch, stop, drop rod, and self-closing requirements. Confirm whether the gate is a standard privacy-fence gate or a specialized barrier gate requiring exact approved hardware.
  2. Confirm hinge and latch posts are stable and adequately supported. Recheck plumb and push-test each post; stop and correct the post condition before proceeding if either post moves.
  3. Measure the opening at top, middle, and bottom. Record all three measurements on the worksheet.
  4. Measure post plumb and grade clearance. Note the ground slope across the opening and any paving or grade change that will affect bottom clearance.
  5. Determine gate-frame dimensions from approved operating clearances. Use the narrowest recorded opening width, minus the required side clearances, to set the frame width.

F. Frame Construction Procedure

  1. Select straight exterior-rated frame material. Reject any piece with warp, splits, or structural defects before cutting.
  2. Cut frame rails and stiles square. Verify each cut with a square before assembly.
  3. Assemble the rectangular frame on a flat surface. Use clamps to hold joints while fastening.
  4. Check diagonals for square. Adjust the frame until both diagonal measurements are equal within the approved tolerance before proceeding.
  5. Install the approved diagonal brace, cable, or anti-sag system in the correct load direction. Confirm the brace runs from the bottom hinge-side corner to the top latch-side corner.
  6. Install pickets, panels, or infill. Match the spacing and pattern of the adjoining fence section.
  7. Maintain edge and ground clearances. Confirm the assembled gate still fits within the frame dimensions set in Section E before proceeding to hanging.

G. Hanging Procedure

  1. Mark hinge locations on gate and post. Reference the finished elevation and required ground clearance.
  2. Install hinges using approved through-bolts or structural fasteners. Do not substitute surface screws for the specified through-bolted connection.
  3. Support the gate at finished elevation. Use shims, blocks, or a jack rather than relying on manual support alone.
  4. Attach the gate to the hinge post. Fasten and tighten each hinge fully before releasing support.
  5. Remove temporary support gradually. Watch for sag or hinge movement as weight transfers onto the hinges.
  6. Check swing and sag. Cycle the gate through its full range and confirm it swings freely without dragging or binding.

H. Hardware and Cycling Procedure

  1. Install latch, strike, stop, handle, and drop rod where specified. Position the stop to establish the correct closed position without over-swing.
  2. Install self-closing or self-latching hardware where required. Confirm the hardware matches the approved specialized-barrier specification where applicable.
  3. Adjust hinges and anti-sag components. Fine-tune hinge tension or brace adjustment until the gate hangs level and closes consistently.
  4. Cycle the gate repeatedly. Open and close the gate through multiple full cycles, checking for consistent latch engagement each time.
  5. Perform the approved firm-hand or load check on the frame and hinge connections. Confirm no movement or loosening develops under normal operating force.

I. Recheck and Closeout Procedure

  1. Inspect clearances, swing, latch engagement, post movement, fasteners, pinch points, sharp edges, and barrier compliance. Classify any deficiency as a frame issue, a hinge-post issue, or a hardware-adjustment issue before correcting it.
  2. Document gate hardware and adjustment. Record hinge and latch hardware, adjustment notes, and final cycling results for the project file.

Common Inspection Review

Inspect the completed gate using the same checks regardless of gate size or hardware type.

  • Opening and clearance check: confirm the frame was sized from the narrowest of the three recorded opening measurements and maintains approved clearance at posts, ground, and paving through full swing.
  • Frame square check: confirm diagonals were equal within tolerance before hardware installation and the anti-sag brace runs in the correct load direction.
  • Hinge check: confirm through-bolted or structural fasteners were used into a verified, stable hinge post.
  • Latch check: confirm positive latch engagement, correct stop position, and proper function of any self-closing or self-latching hardware across repeated cycles.
  • Post-stability check: confirm no hinge- or latch-post movement developed during hanging or cycling.

Complete the Gate Installation Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before opening the gate for regular use.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Gate Drags or Binds on One Post

Probable cause: the frame was sized from a single opening measurement rather than the narrowest of the top, middle, and bottom readings, or the hinge post has moved out of plumb.
Confirmation: re-measure the opening at all three heights and recheck hinge-post plumb against the Project 10 record.
Correction: trim the frame within approved clearance limits or correct the hinge-post plumb before rehanging.
Reinspection: repeat the clearance check through a full swing cycle.

Gate Sags at the Latch Corner Over Time

Probable cause: the anti-sag brace or cable was installed in the wrong direction, or the diagonal was not fully engaged before hardware was fastened.
Confirmation: inspect the brace direction against the required bottom-hinge-to-top-latch orientation.
Correction: remove and reinstall the brace or cable in the correct direction and re-tension.
Reinspection: recheck frame square and cycle the gate to confirm the sag does not return.

Hinge Pulls Loose from the Post

Probable cause: surface screws or undersized fasteners were substituted for the specified through-bolted hinge connection, or the hinge post was not adequately supported.
Confirmation: inspect the hinge fasteners against the approved hardware specification and recheck hinge-post condition.
Correction: reinstall hinges with the specified through-bolts or structural fasteners into sound post material, correcting the post support first if it is inadequate.
Reinspection: repeat the hinge check and cycle the gate under load.

Latch Fails to Engage Consistently

Probable cause: the stop or strike was positioned before the gate's final hung position was confirmed, or hinge adjustment shifted the gate's resting position after latch installation.
Confirmation: cycle the gate multiple times and observe where the latch meets or misses the strike.
Correction: adjust the strike or stop position to match the gate's actual closed position after final hinge adjustment.
Reinspection: repeat the latch check through a full set of open-close cycles.

Post Movement Detected After Hanging

Probable cause: the hinge or latch post was not adequately supported or had not fully reached the accepted footing condition established in Project 10 before the gate was hung.
Confirmation: recheck the post against the Project 10 footing and bracing record and observe movement during a firm-hand cycling test.
Correction: remove the gate, correct the post support or footing condition, and confirm stability before rehanging.
Reinspection: repeat the post-stability check after the gate is rehung.

Self-Closing or Self-Latching Hardware Fails to Function

Probable cause: standard hinge or latch hardware was substituted for the required specialized self-closing or self-latching components, or the hardware was installed without following the manufacturer's adjustment sequence.
Confirmation: compare the installed hardware against the approved specialized-barrier specification and review the manufacturer instructions.
Correction: install the specified hardware and complete the manufacturer's adjustment procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the latch check with specific attention to unassisted self-closing and self-latching performance.

Field Terms

Clear Opening
The measured distance between the hinge post and the latch post.
Operating Clearance
The gap left between the gate and its posts, and the gate and the ground, for free swing.
Anti-Sag System
A diagonal brace, turnbuckle cable, or other component that resists frame racking over time.
Hinge Post
The post the gate is hung from, carrying its full weight and leverage.
Latch Post
The post the gate closes against, carrying the latch, strike, and stop.
Stop
The fixed component that establishes the gate's closed position.
Strike
The hardware component the latch engages to secure the closed gate.
Self-Closing Hardware
Spring hinges or closers that return the gate to closed position without manual action.
Self-Latching Hardware
Latch mechanisms that engage automatically as the gate closes.
Firm-Hand Check
A manual force test used to confirm hinge, hardware, and post stability under normal operating load.

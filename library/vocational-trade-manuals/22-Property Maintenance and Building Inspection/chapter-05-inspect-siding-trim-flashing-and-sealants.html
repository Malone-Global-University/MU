Job Overview

This project qualifies you to perform one complete, non-destructive, elevation-by-elevation inspection of a residential building exterior. The finished work product is a single written record: a location-by-location inventory of siding, trim, flashing, penetration, coating, and sealant conditions, supported by photographs, that identifies drainage-system concerns without overstating what is visible from safe observation points.

You will review known exterior history, confirm weather and surface conditions, walk a fixed clockwise route around the building, photograph each elevation, inspect siding and trim materials for damage and deterioration, inspect visible flashing at roof, deck, window, door, and penetration transitions, inspect coatings for failure, and inspect sealant joints for adhesion and correct placement. You will correlate exterior defects with interior evidence identified elsewhere in the property record, but you will label suspected relationships as unconfirmed until verified.

This project builds on the exterior segment of the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough and the roof-drainage findings from Chapter 3. It produces the envelope-condition record used later in Chapter 6, when foundation, grading, and crawlspace conditions are correlated with the same exterior evidence, and again in Chapter 20, when the capstone project repeats this discipline at full property scale.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-025 — Inspect siding condition for damage, deterioration, and improper exposure across every accessible elevation.
  • MI-026 — Inspect exterior trim, including corner boards, fascia, soffits, and window and door trim, for movement, decay, and finish failure.
  • MI-027 — Identify failed caulk and sealant joints, and distinguish sealant joints from intentional drainage openings that must remain open.
  • MI-028 — Inspect visible flashing details at roof-wall, deck-wall, window, door, and penetration transitions.
  • MI-029 — Identify rot, moisture staining, and biological growth on exterior materials and correlate findings with interior and drainage evidence.
  • MI-030 — Document envelope repair needs by elevation, separating cleaning, coating maintenance, sealant replacement, flashing repair, material replacement, and invasive-investigation referrals.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 identified the exterior in general terms as part of the whole-property baseline walkthrough. Chapter 3 inspected the roof-drainage system down through the gutters and downspouts. This project continues the water-management inspection down the building envelope: siding, trim, flashing, penetrations, coatings, and sealant joints. Chapter 6 continues the same water-management inspection from the building envelope down through the foundation, grading, and crawlspace.

Treat this project as one segment of a single continuous drainage and drying system that runs from the roofline to the foundation. Do not record siding, trim, flashing, or sealant conditions as isolated cosmetic items. A defect identified here may explain, or be explained by, a finding recorded in Chapter 3, Chapter 6, or the interior finish inspection in Chapter 9.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing known exterior material types, prior repairs, paint history, and recent storm or irrigation exposure
  • establishing a safe clockwise inspection route around the accessible exterior
  • photographing each elevation and major wall transition before close observation
  • inspecting siding, corner boards, fascia, soffits, and trim for damage, deterioration, and improper exposure
  • inspecting visible flashing at roof-to-wall, deck-to-wall, window, door, masonry, and penetration transitions
  • inspecting penetrations, sealant joints, and intentional drainage openings and distinguishing one from the other
  • identifying moisture staining, decay, biological growth, and repeated patching
  • producing one elevation-based envelope repair list with priority, photograph reference, and recommended next action for every finding

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • probe, cut into, or remove siding, trim, flashing, or sealant beyond an approved non-destructive or limited method with owner authorization
  • infer that concealed flashing is properly installed merely because sealant is present at the surface
  • seal, cover, or otherwise block a siding weep, masonry weep, window drainage path, roof ventilation opening, or other intentional drainage or ventilation feature
  • diagnose structural cladding failure, masonry veneer failure, stucco or EIFS system failure, or window installation defects; these are referred to the appropriate qualified professional

Any condition involving loose overhead material, an open wall cavity, active leakage, major decay, unsafe cladding, or exposed electrical components must stop the routine inspection and be handled per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue before the inspection continues.

Trade Note

Sealant is a maintenance material for joints designed to hold it, not a substitute for flashing, drainage, or a failed material. Every sealant joint you find in this inspection must first be identified as a designed sealant joint or an intentional drainage opening. Recording a weep hole as a "gap that needs caulk" is a documentation error this chapter's troubleshooting section corrects directly.

Setup Standard

Do not begin close inspection until the full clockwise route has been photographed elevation by elevation (Step 4). A finding recorded before the elevation overview photograph is taken must be re-verified against that photograph before it is entered on the envelope repair list.

Shop Tip

Walk the full clockwise route once without stopping to photograph every elevation and transition. Then walk it a second time for close observations. Working wide-to-close in two passes prevents missed elevations and keeps every finding tied to a photographed context.

Common Field Fundamentals

This project treats the exterior envelope as a drainage and drying system, not as an appearance-only surface. Every material, joint, and coating you inspect either sheds water, drains water, or is expected to remain dry because something upstream is doing its job. Learning to read the envelope this way here carries directly into Chapter 6, where the same drainage logic continues down to the foundation.

Sealant Joints Versus Drainage Openings

Before recording any gap or opening as a sealant defect, determine whether it is a designed sealant joint or an intentional drainage or ventilation opening. Siding laps, masonry weeps, window sill weeps, and some vent and movement joints are designed to remain open. Sealing one of these openings does not repair a defect; it creates one. Record the distinction on the worksheet for every opening inspected.

Documentation Cue — Elevation, Component, One Photo Pair

Assign every finding a unique identifier tied to its elevation and component before photographing. Take one overview photograph of the elevation and one close photograph of the specific finding, using a scale reference where useful. Do not batch findings from different elevations under one identifier.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Envelope Hazards Stop the Routine Inspection

If you encounter loose overhead siding, trim, or flashing material; an open wall cavity; active water entry; major structural decay; unsafe cladding; or exposed energized electrical components at a penetration, stop the routine inspection immediately. Secure or mark the area if it is safe to do so, and notify the owner or responsible party before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Route, Footing, and Weather

Walk the exterior route on stable, dry ground and avoid unstable footing near foundations, window wells, or landscaping. Do not climb siding, trim, or overhead features to gain a closer view; use binoculars, a camera-pole, or another approved remote-observation method instead. Stop exterior observation in unsafe wind, lightning, or precipitation.

Code Alert — Concealed Flashing Cannot Be Confirmed From the Surface

Do not represent concealed flashing as properly installed based on the presence of surface sealant alone. Structural cladding, masonry veneer, stucco, EIFS, high-access, window installation, and other concealed-envelope conditions require referral to the appropriate qualified professional; this project does not authorize the learner to certify any of those conditions.

E. Preparation — Records, Weather, and Route Planning

  1. Review exterior material types and history. Record known repairs, paint history, prior leaks, manufacturer information, and recent storm or irrigation exposure.
  2. Record weather and surface conditions. Note sun exposure and whether surfaces are wet, shaded, frozen, or obscured before beginning.
  3. Establish the safe clockwise inspection route. Fix the elevation labeling convention and the direction of travel before beginning, and do not change it mid-inspection.

F. Elevation Overview and Siding Inspection

  1. Photograph each elevation and major wall transition before making any close observations.
  2. Identify siding type, orientation, panel or board pattern, and intentional drainage or ventilation features for each elevation.
  3. Inspect siding for damage and deterioration. Look for cracks, holes, buckling, warping, loose pieces, impact damage, open joints, corrosion, delamination, swelling, decay, and biological growth.
  4. Inspect clearances above grade, roofs, decks, slabs, hardscape, and vegetation where visible.
  5. Identify improper material exposure. Note soil, mulch, snow, irrigation, or debris contacting materials not intended for that exposure.

G. Trim, Coatings, and Flashing

  1. Inspect exterior trim components. Cover corner boards, fascia, soffits, frieze, band boards, water tables, door trim, window trim, and other exterior wood or composite components.
  2. Inspect paint or coating condition. Note peeling, blistering, chalking, bare substrate, rust staining, and failed end-grain protection.
  3. Inspect visible flashing at every relevant transition. Cover roof-to-wall, deck-to-wall, window, door, horizontal trim, masonry, and material-transition flashing where visible.
  4. Do not infer proper concealed flashing merely because sealant is present at the surface.
  5. Inspect window and door drainage details. Cover head details, sills, thresholds, pans, weeps, and drainage paths that are visible.

H. Penetrations and Sealant Joints

  1. Inspect penetrations. Cover pipes, ducts, cables, lights, receptacles, hose bibbs, meters, vents, and fasteners.
  2. Identify penetration defects. Note missing escutcheons, open holes, unsupported penetrations, failed boots, and sealant separated from one or both surfaces.
  3. Distinguish designed sealant joints from intentional drainage and ventilation openings before recording any repair recommendation.
  4. Inspect sealant condition. Note cracking, hardening, softening, tearing, adhesion loss, staining, incompatible paint, excessive width, and three-sided adhesion evidence.

I. Moisture Evidence, Correlation, and Closeout

  1. Inspect for moisture and decay evidence. Note staining, soft material, fungal growth, insect damage, rusted fasteners, and repeated patching.
  2. Probe suspect material only through an approved non-destructive or limited method with owner authorization.
  3. Correlate exterior defects with interior and drainage evidence, labeling the suspected source as unconfirmed until verified.
  4. Identify immediate conditions requiring prompt action. Note loose overhead material, open wall cavities, active leakage, major decay, unsafe cladding, or electrical exposure.
  5. Refer concealed or specialized envelope concerns. Send structural cladding, masonry veneer, stucco, EIFS, high-access, window installation, or concealed-envelope conditions to qualified professionals.
  6. Record every finding by elevation, location, material, condition, priority, photograph, and recommended next action.
  7. Identify concealed or inaccessible areas. Note areas hidden by vegetation, decks, additions, stored materials, snow, or inaccessible height.
  8. Create the envelope repair list, separating cleaning, vegetation control, coating maintenance, sealant replacement, flashing repair, material replacement, and invasive investigation.
  9. Update the property maintenance log and schedule reinspection after repair or the next wet-weather condition.

Common Envelope Inspection Checks

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which elevation it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding has a unique identifier tied to a specific elevation and component.
  • Opening-type check: confirm every inspected gap or opening is labeled as a designed sealant joint or an intentional drainage/ventilation opening.
  • Concealed-flashing check: confirm no finding states or implies that concealed flashing is properly installed based on surface sealant alone.
  • Correlation check: confirm any suspected relationship to an interior, roof, or foundation finding is labeled "unconfirmed" rather than stated as fact.
  • Photograph check: confirm every significant finding has an elevation overview photograph and a close photograph.
  • Limitation check: confirm every concealed or inaccessible area is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Drainage Opening Was Recorded as a Sealant Defect

Probable cause: a weep, siding lap, vent, or movement joint was mistaken for a gap requiring sealant.
Confirmation: compare the opening's location and pattern against known siding and window drainage design, and check whether sealing it would block intended water or moisture exit.
Correction: relabel the finding as an intentional drainage or ventilation opening and remove any recommendation to seal it.
Reinspection: review every recorded sealant finding on the elevation for the same error before filing the record.

Concealed Flashing Was Assumed Satisfactory From Surface Sealant

Probable cause: the inspector observed intact surface sealant and concluded the underlying flashing was properly installed.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any statement that the flashing "is fine" or "is properly installed" without a visible flashing observation supporting it.
Correction: rewrite the finding to describe only the visible sealant condition, and note that concealed flashing was not observed.
Reinspection: review the full repair list for the same assumption at every roof-wall, deck-wall, window, and penetration transition.

An Elevation Was Skipped or Combined With Another

Probable cause: the fixed clockwise route was not followed, or two elevations were assumed identical.
Confirmation: compare the recorded elevation overview photographs against the building's actual number of exterior faces.
Correction: return to the skipped elevation and complete the same overview-then-close inspection sequence used elsewhere.
Reinspection: confirm the finished repair list includes a photographed entry for every elevation in Section B of the work order.

An Exterior Finding Was Stated as a Confirmed Interior Cause

Probable cause: the inspector linked an exterior sealant, siding, or flashing defect directly to an interior stain without confirmation.
Confirmation: review the finding for a stated cause-and-effect relationship that has not been verified by direct observation of the connecting path.
Correction: relabel the relationship as a suspected, unconfirmed connection, and record both findings separately with their own identifiers.
Reinspection: confirm every cross-referenced finding in the repair list uses the same "unconfirmed" labeling standard.

A Priority Concern Was Buried in the General Repair List

Probable cause: a finding involving loose overhead material, an open wall cavity, active leakage, major decay, or electrical exposure was recorded in sequence with routine cosmetic items.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding matching the Critical Safety Cue conditions in this chapter.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate condition, notify the responsible party, and record the notification separately from the routine repair list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate condition is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the repair list is closed out.

Field Terms

Elevation
One exterior face of the building, identified by a fixed labeling convention established before the route begins.
Designed Sealant Joint
A joint where sealant is intended to provide the primary seal against water and air movement.
Intentional Drainage Opening
A weep, siding lap, vent, or movement joint designed to remain open so water or moisture can exit the assembly.
Concealed Flashing
Flashing installed behind a visible surface that cannot be confirmed by surface observation alone.
Three-Sided Adhesion
A sealant-joint condition where sealant bonds to the back of the joint in addition to the two side surfaces, restricting normal joint movement.
Suspected Relationship (Unconfirmed)
A noted but unverified connection between an exterior finding and an interior, roof, or foundation observation.
Envelope Repair List
The single dated document recording every siding, trim, flashing, coating, penetration, and sealant finding from this inspection, organized by elevation.
Overview Photograph
A wide photograph establishing the elevation and context of a finding before a close photograph is taken.
Close Photograph
A detailed photograph of a specific finding, including a scale reference where useful.
Immediate Envelope Condition
A condition such as loose overhead material, an open wall cavity, active leakage, major decay, unsafe cladding, or exposed electrical components that stops the routine inspection for review and notification.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect the interior finish surfaces of an accessible residence, room by room, and produce a dated interior finish condition record. The finished work product is a single written record: a room-by-room inventory of ceiling, wall, trim, and floor conditions, supported by photographs, that separates active safety and water issues from routine wear, repairable finish damage, and cosmetic preference.

You will review owner-reported concerns, record room conditions, establish a consistent top-to-bottom and clockwise route through every room, inspect ceilings, walls, trim, and flooring for damage, movement indicators, and moisture evidence, use an approved moisture-screening tool where authorized, correlate findings across rooms and with prior exterior and foundation observations, and record every finding with a location, a description, a priority, and a photograph reference.

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough and depends on the exterior water-management findings from Chapters 3 through 6. It supplies the interior evidence that Chapter 19 uses to prioritize repairs and that the Chapter 20 capstone repeats at full property scale.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-049 — Inspect wall damage, including cracks, impact damage, staining, and loose coverings, using a consistent room route.
  • MI-050 — Inspect ceiling stains, cracks, sag, and other finish conditions without assigning an unconfirmed source.
  • MI-051 — Inspect flooring condition, including loose pieces, gaps, buckling, soft spots, and trip hazards.
  • MI-052 — Identify settlement and movement signs in finish surfaces without assigning structural significance.
  • MI-053 — Identify moisture indicators using visual observation and approved non-invasive moisture-screening tools.
  • MI-054 — Document interior finish repair needs in a prioritized, room-by-room record with photographs and observation limitations.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 established the baseline route and the observation-versus-interpretation discipline this chapter depends on. Chapters 3 through 6 documented exterior roof, envelope, and foundation water-management conditions that must be correlated with any interior staining or moisture evidence found here. Chapter 7 covers window and door inspection in greater depth; this chapter records only the interior finish evidence around those openings.

The interior finish repair list produced here feeds directly into Chapter 10, which begins plumbing-fixture inspection in areas where this chapter identifies water-using rooms, and into Chapter 19, which sorts every open finding from this book into a prioritized 30-60-90 day plan. Nothing in this project authorizes drywall removal, invasive moisture testing, or structural conclusions.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing owner-reported cracks, stains, odors, floor movement, prior repairs, leaks, and seasonal changes before entering each room
  • establishing a single consistent top-to-bottom and clockwise route through every accessible room
  • inspecting ceilings, walls, trim, and flooring for damage, movement indicators, moisture evidence, wear, and trip hazards
  • using an approved non-invasive moisture-screening tool to compare suspect and reference areas, where authorized
  • correlating interior findings with roof, envelope, plumbing, and foundation observations from other chapters
  • producing one room-by-room interior finish condition record with location, observation, priority, photograph, and recommended next action for every finding

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • diagnose structural adequacy, environmental contamination, or code compliance from a finish crack, stain, or reading alone
  • remove drywall, flooring, trim, or other finishes to inspect concealed conditions
  • interpret a non-invasive moisture-screening reading as proof of concealed moisture or as proof that no moisture is present
  • repair paint, drywall, flooring, or trim before the source of any related water or moisture is corrected

Any active dripping, bulging, wet material, loose plaster, or falling finish must stop the routine inspection of that surface and be handled per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before the inspection continues.

Trade Note

A finish crack, stain, or moisture-meter reading is evidence, not a verdict. Writing "moisture-screening reading elevated at baseboard, northeast corner of Bedroom 2, compared with a dry reference reading at the same wall" is correct. Writing "there is a plumbing leak in the wall" is not, unless a qualified professional has confirmed it. This chapter's troubleshooting section corrects this error pattern directly.

Common Field Fundamentals

This chapter uses the same five-part sequence established in Chapter 1: confirm scope, observe, document, separate hazard from routine, and record limitations. Here, that sequence is applied surface by surface, room by room, with special attention to distinguishing observed finish symptoms from unconfirmed structural or moisture conclusions.

CeilingsStains, cracks, sag, joint lines, fastener pops, peeling, texture changes, and penetrations
WallsCracks, displacement, impact damage, peeling, staining, mold-like growth, and patching
Trim and built-insBaseboards, casing, crown, paneling for movement, separation, swelling, and finish failure
FlooringLoose pieces, gaps, buckling, cupping, soft spots, squeaks, stains, wear, and transition defects

Observation Versus Interpretation

Record what is visible, audible, or measurable. Do not record a diagnosis, a cause, or a structural conclusion unless it comes from an identified prior report or a qualified professional. "Crack visible at the drywall seam above the bedroom door, approximately 14 inches long" is an observation. "The header above the door has failed" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact unless confirmed.

Documentation Cue — Crack and Stain Description Without Structural Conclusion

Record every crack by location, direction, length, visible width, displacement, staining, and relation to nearby openings or structural elements. Do not assign structural significance from a finish crack alone. Do not convert a generic width threshold into a pass/fail structural conclusion; that determination belongs to a qualified professional.

Critical Safety Cue — Active Finish Failure Stops the Routine Inspection

If you encounter active dripping, a bulging or wet ceiling, loose plaster, or falling finish material, stop the routine inspection of that surface immediately. Do not stand or work beneath the affected area. Notify the owner or responsible party, and treat the area as a priority water-source investigation before continuing the routine room-by-room sequence.

Field Safety Cue — Furniture, Storage, and Occupied Rooms

Do not force access, move heavy or unstable furniture, or disturb fixed finishes to inspect a concealed surface. Record furniture-obstructed and storage-obstructed areas as limitations. Confirm lighting is adequate before close inspection, and respect occupant privacy and belongings throughout the room-by-room route.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Moisture-Screening Tool Use

Use the moisture-screening tool only as described in its manufacturer instructions, and take a reference reading at a known-dry area of the same material before recording a suspect-area reading. Do not interpret a single reading as proof of concealed moisture or as proof that a surface is dry; record it as a comparative screening result only.

Code Alert — This Inspection Is Not a Structural or Environmental Determination

Do not represent any crack observation, moisture-screening reading, or finish condition recorded in this chapter as a structural evaluation, environmental assessment, or code-compliance certification unless the person performing it and the defined scope are qualified and authorized for that specific service.

E. Preparation — Review, Conditions, and Route Setup

  1. Review owner-reported concerns and prior records. Record owner-reported cracks, stains, odors, floor movement, prior repairs, leaks, and seasonal changes.
  2. Record room and environmental conditions. Record room use, occupancy, recent cleaning or painting, furniture obstructions, lighting, and temperature or humidity conditions.
  3. Establish the room route. Confirm a consistent top-to-bottom and clockwise route through each room, and do not change this sequence mid-inspection.
  4. Photograph the room overview. Take one overview photograph of each room before close inspection begins.

F. Ceiling and Wall Inspection

  1. Inspect ceilings. Record stains, cracks, sag, joint lines, fastener pops, peeling, texture changes, patching, and penetrations.
  2. Identify immediate ceiling conditions. Treat active dripping, bulging, wet material, loose plaster, or falling finish as immediate control conditions per the Critical Safety Cue.
  3. Inspect walls. Record cracks, displacement, impact damage, peeling, blistering, staining, mold-like growth, loose coverings, and visible patching.
  4. Inspect corners and transitions. Cover corners, wall-ceiling joints, openings, columns, beams, stair walls, and transitions between old and new construction.
  5. Record crack characteristics. Note crack location, direction, length, visible width, displacement, staining, and relation to openings or structural elements, without assigning structural significance from the finish crack alone.

G. Trim, Built-Ins, and Flooring Inspection

  1. Inspect trim and built-ins. Check baseboards, casing, trim joints, crown, paneling, and built-ins for movement, separation, swelling, decay, and finish failure.
  2. Inspect flooring. Record loose pieces, gaps, buckling, cupping, lippage, soft spots, squeaks, stains, wear, burns, cuts, and transition defects.
  3. Inspect stairs and level changes. Cover stairs, landings, thresholds, rugs, transitions, and changes in level for trip or fall hazards.
  4. Observe floor slope or movement. Note floor slope or movement qualitatively, and measure only through an approved repeatable method.

H. Moisture Correlation and Classification

  1. Inspect moisture-prone locations. Check around plumbing fixtures, exterior openings, chimneys, roof intersections, HVAC equipment, and appliances for moisture evidence.
  2. Use the moisture-screening tool where authorized. Compare suspect and reference areas using the approved tool and manufacturer procedure, without interpreting a reading as proof of concealed moisture or its absence.
  3. Inspect for condensation. Check windows, exterior corners, closets, supply registers, cold surfaces, and poorly ventilated rooms.
  4. Identify related moisture indicators. Record surface corrosion, efflorescence, musty odor, damaged flooring, and swollen trim as related moisture indicators.
  5. Identify pest evidence. Note pest damage, droppings, nesting, gnawing, insect holes, or damaged finishes without disturbing suspect contamination.
  6. Correlate patterns across the property. Compare findings across rooms, floors, exterior elevations, roof drainage, plumbing, and foundation observations from other chapters.
  7. Classify each finding. Separate active safety or water issues from monitoring items, routine wear, repairable finish damage, and cosmetic preference.

I. Documentation and Closeout

  1. Record every finding completely. Include room, surface, exact location, condition, priority, photograph, suspected relationship if any, and recommended next action.
  2. Record hidden areas. Note areas hidden by furniture, storage, fixed finishes, or inaccessible rooms as limitations.
  3. Create the interior-finish repair list. Organize entries by cause investigation, drying, stabilization, repair, refinishing, and monitoring.
  4. Update the maintenance log. Compare findings with the Chapter 1 baseline condition list and file the completed record.

Common Interior Finish Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which room or surface it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding has a unique identifier tied to a specific room, surface, and location.
  • Observation check: confirm the recorded text states what was seen, heard, or measured, without an unsupported structural or moisture-source conclusion.
  • Reference-reading check: confirm every moisture-screening result includes a comparison reading from a known-dry area of the same material.
  • Priority check: confirm every finding is classified as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  • Photograph check: confirm every room has an overview photograph and every significant finding has a close photograph.
  • Limitation check: confirm every furniture-obstructed, storage-obstructed, or inaccessible area is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Finding States a Structural or Moisture-Source Conclusion Instead of an Observation

Probable cause: the inspector recorded a suspected cause, such as "the header failed" or "there is a plumbing leak in the wall," instead of the visible condition.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any word implying a structural, moisture-source, or code-compliance conclusion not supported by a prior report or qualified professional.
Correction: rewrite the finding to state only what was seen, heard, or measured, and move any suspected cause to a clearly labeled "suspected cause, unconfirmed" note.
Reinspection: review the full interior finish condition record for the same wording pattern before filing it.

A Moisture-Screening Reading Was Recorded Without a Reference Reading

Probable cause: the inspector took a single reading at the suspect area and treated it as conclusive without a comparison reading at a known-dry area of the same material.
Confirmation: check each recorded screening entry for a paired reference reading and note whether the tool's manufacturer procedure was followed.
Correction: return to the location, if still accessible, and take a proper reference reading at an unaffected area of the same material, and record both readings together.
Reinspection: confirm every screening entry in the record includes both a suspect reading and a reference reading before filing.

A Room Was Skipped or Inspected Out of Sequence

Probable cause: the fixed top-to-bottom, clockwise route was not followed, or a room was assumed to be identical to a similar room.
Confirmation: compare the recorded room segments against the property's actual room count and the Chapter 1 baseline condition list.
Correction: return to the skipped or out-of-sequence room and complete the same ceiling-to-floor inspection sequence used elsewhere.
Reinspection: confirm the finished record includes every room listed in Section B of the work order.

A Furniture- or Storage-Obstructed Area Was Recorded as Satisfactory

Probable cause: the inspector assumed a partially blocked wall or floor area was in acceptable condition rather than recording it as a limitation.
Confirmation: review the record for areas marked "OK" or omitted entirely that were not actually visible.
Correction: change the entry to a recorded limitation stating the area was hidden by furniture, storage, fixed finishes, or was otherwise inaccessible.
Reinspection: confirm no entry states or implies that an unobserved area is satisfactory.

An Active Water or Falling-Finish Condition Was Recorded as a Routine Cosmetic Item

Probable cause: active dripping, a bulging or wet ceiling, or loose plaster was logged in sequence with routine wear findings instead of being flagged and escalated at the time it was found.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving active water, falling material, or loose plaster.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard, notify the responsible party per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate hazard is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the inspection is closed out.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to perform one systematic, non-destructive inspection of a residence's accessible foundation, site grading, drainage paths, and basement or crawlspace indicators. The finished work product is a single written record: a location-by-location inventory of foundation and drainage conditions, supported by photographs and measurements, that separates observed conditions from structural interpretation and identifies items requiring monitoring, maintenance, or a qualified professional.

You will review prior records and weather conditions, walk the accessible exterior foundation perimeter, observe and record cracks and displacement by consistent characteristics, trace site drainage from roof discharge through grading to the foundation, inspect basement or crawlspace access from approved entry points, and document every finding, limitation, and referral in the Foundation and Drainage Inspection Record.

This project builds directly on the site-drainage observations from Chapter 3 (roof, gutters, and downspouts) and Chapter 5 (siding, trim, and flashing). It sets up Chapter 7, where window and door operation is inspected with foundation movement already documented as context, and it feeds directly into Chapter 19's prioritized repair plan and the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-031 — Inspect accessible foundation walls, piers, posts, beams, slabs, and retaining walls for visible condition.
  • MI-032 — Identify and record cracks and movement signs by consistent, observable characteristics.
  • MI-033 — Verify soil slope and grading immediately adjacent to the building.
  • MI-034 — Inspect site and roof-water drainage paths in relation to the foundation.
  • MI-035 — Inspect crawlspace or basement moisture indicators from approved access points.
  • MI-036 — Document foundation and drainage concerns with measurements, photographs, priority, and recommended action.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 identified the foundation, grading, and drainage relationships as part of the whole-property baseline walkthrough. Chapter 3 traced roof discharge to grade, and Chapter 5 documented siding, trim, and flashing as a drainage system rather than an appearance issue. This project completes the water-management sequence by following that same water path from the ground surface into the foundation, basement, or crawlspace.

The crack-observation method and drainage-tracing discipline established here carry forward into Chapter 9 (interior finish surfaces, where related cracks and stains are cross-referenced), Chapter 19 (where movement and drainage findings are converted into monitoring items or urgent repairs), and the Chapter 20 capstone, which repeats this foundation and crawlspace sequence at full scale.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing foundation type, prior reports, repair history, and recent weather before the inspection begins
  • inspecting accessible foundation walls, piers, posts, beams, slabs, and retaining walls from safe observation points
  • recording cracks and displacement by location, orientation, length, visible width, and related staining or moisture, without assigning structural significance
  • observing soil slope, drainage paths, and site-water discharge in relation to the foundation
  • inspecting basement or crawlspace access and, where safe, dampness, staining, and pest evidence from approved entry points
  • producing one Foundation and Drainage Inspection Record with measurements, photographs, priority, and recommended action for every finding

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • assign structural significance to a crack, displacement, or movement indicator beyond the inspector's qualifications
  • enter a basement or crawlspace with unsafe access, standing water, exposed wiring, suspected contamination, structural instability, fuel-gas odor, or uncontrolled pests
  • excavate beside the foundation, alter grading, or redirect runoff in a way that could affect neighboring property, septic systems, wells, or retaining walls
  • defeat sump-pit covers or electrical safeguards, or test a sump pump outside the approved external-observation procedure

Any active flooding, failed structural support, severe displacement, damaged retaining wall, unsafe access, sewage, or energized wet equipment must stop the routine inspection and be handled per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before the inspection continues.

Trade Note

A crack observed at the foundation is recorded by its location, orientation, length, and visible width — never by a conclusion about cause or severity. Writing "vertical crack, 14 inches long, up to 1/16 inch wide, at the northeast foundation wall below the kitchen window" is correct. Writing "the foundation has a structural crack" is not, unless a qualified professional has made that determination. This chapter's troubleshooting section addresses this distinction directly.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every foundation and drainage inspection in this book follows the same water-first logic established in Chapter 3: trace water from its source to its effect, and separate what is observed from what is concluded. Here, that means walking the foundation from the outside in, then tracing site and roof drainage toward the foundation, and only then entering an approved basement or crawlspace to look for the interior evidence of the same water path.

Observation Versus Structural Interpretation

Record crack and movement characteristics exactly as observed. Do not record a structural conclusion, a cause, or a repair method unless it comes from an identified prior report or a qualified professional. "Diagonal crack, approximately 18 inches, up to 1/8 inch wide, at the southwest foundation corner, with efflorescence below" is an observation. "The foundation is settling" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact unless confirmed by a qualified professional.

Documentation Cue — Consistent Crack Characteristics, Every Time

Record the same characteristic set for every crack: location, orientation, length, visible width, displacement, staining or moisture, and prior repair. Reference monitored cracks using a non-destructive marking or measurement method so future width and length can be compared without damaging the structure.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Hazards Stop the Routine Inspection

If you encounter active flooding, failed structural support, severe displacement, a damaged retaining wall, unsafe crawlspace or basement access, sewage, or energized wet equipment, stop the routine inspection immediately. Secure or isolate the area if it is safe to do so, and notify the owner, building management, or the appropriate qualified professional before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Crawlspace and Basement Entry

Do not enter a basement or crawlspace with unsafe access, standing water, exposed wiring, suspected asbestos, vermiculite, or mold-like growth, structural instability, fuel-gas odor, low clearance beyond your approved limits, or uncontrolled pests. Confirm safe entry, adequate clearance, and a clear exit path before proceeding.

Code Alert — This Inspection Does Not Certify Structural Adequacy

Do not represent this inspection as a structural evaluation, engineering assessment, or code-compliance certification. Refer active movement, significant displacement, bowing, leaning, failed retaining walls, damaged beams or posts, widespread floor slope, or other structural concerns to the appropriate qualified professional.

E. Preparation — Records, Weather, and Route Planning

  1. Review foundation and site history. Record foundation type, prior structural or drainage reports, repair history, known settlement, waterproofing, sump, crawlspace, basement, and recent rainfall.
  2. Record current conditions. Note weather, recent precipitation, soil moisture, snow cover, irrigation status, and site conditions that limit observation.
  3. Establish the exterior route. Identify inaccessible or hazardous areas around the foundation before beginning.
  4. Photograph each foundation elevation. Capture each elevation and its site-drainage relationship before close inspection.

F. Foundation Perimeter and Crack Observation

  1. Inspect visible foundation components. Observe foundation walls, piers, posts, beams, slabs, retaining walls, and transitions where safely accessible.
  2. Record cracks by consistent characteristics. Note location, orientation, length, visible width, displacement, moisture, staining, efflorescence, and prior repair for every crack.
  3. Do not assign structural significance. Record characteristics only; do not conclude structural significance from crack appearance or a generic width threshold.
  4. Reference monitored cracks. Mark or reference cracks selected for monitoring without damaging the structure, and record the measurement method used.
  5. Inspect for displacement indicators. Note bowing, leaning, rotation, displaced masonry, spalling, exposed reinforcement, damaged parging, erosion, undermining, and open penetrations.
  6. Inspect related exterior elements. Check siding, trim, steps, porches, decks, chimneys, windows, doors, and interior finishes for related movement indicators.

G. Site Grading and Drainage Tracing

  1. Observe soil slope adjacent to the building. Identify low areas, reverse slope, settlement, erosion, and exposed footings.
  2. Inspect roof and surface-water discharge. Relate discharge points to the foundation walls.
  3. Trace site drainage features. Follow swales, drains, catch basins, area drains, sump discharge, retaining-wall drainage, and visible site outlets.
  4. Identify drainage deficiencies. Note ponding, saturated soil, blocked drains, icing paths, erosion channels, and runoff crossing walks or entering neighboring property.
  5. Inspect obstructions to drainage and access. Note vegetation, roots, irrigation, mulch, stored materials, and hardscape that may trap water or restrict access.

H. Crawlspace or Basement Access and Interior Inspection

  1. Inspect the access point before entry. Check structural condition, size, security, electrical hazards, pests, water, and safe entry.
  2. Confirm entry is not authorized where unsafe. Do not enter an area with unsafe access, standing water, exposed wiring, suspected contamination, structural instability, fuel-gas odor, low clearance beyond approved limits, or uncontrolled pests.
  3. Inspect interior moisture indicators from approved access. Note dampness, staining, condensation, standing water, damaged vapor retarder, efflorescence, mold-like growth, decay, corrosion, and pest evidence.
  4. Inspect visible structural and utility components without disturbing them. Observe piers, posts, beams, joists, subfloor, connections, insulation, ducts, plumbing, wiring, vents, and ground cover.
  5. Identify moisture contributors. Note plumbing leaks, condensate, groundwater, surface-water entry, or ventilation conditions that may contribute to moisture.
  6. Inspect sump pit and pump externally within the approved scope. Do not defeat covers or electrical safeguards, and test pumps only through the approved procedure when discharge, power, and backup conditions are safe.

I. Documentation and Closeout

  1. Identify immediate concerns. Note active flooding, failed support, severe displacement, damaged retaining wall, unsafe access, sewage, or energized wet equipment, and refer as required.
  2. Record exterior and interior findings. Include location, measurements, photographs, weather context, and observation limitations.
  3. Create separate actions by category. Distinguish water-source correction, drainage maintenance, crack monitoring, material repair, pest response, and structural evaluation.
  4. Update the property maintenance log. Define the condition or date for follow-up measurement and reinspection.

Common Foundation Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which segment of the route it came from.

  • Crack characteristic check: confirm every recorded crack includes location, orientation, length, visible width, displacement, staining, and prior repair.
  • Interpretation check: confirm no finding assigns structural significance beyond the inspector's qualifications.
  • Drainage-tracing check: confirm site slope, drains, and roof-water discharge are traced in relation to the foundation.
  • Access-safety check: confirm no crawlspace or basement entry occurred where access, structure, electrical, pest, or water conditions were unsafe.
  • Limitation check: confirm every inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, or unsafe area is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Crack Finding States a Structural Conclusion

Probable cause: the inspector recorded a diagnosis such as "settling" or "structural failure" instead of the observed characteristics.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any word implying cause, severity, or an engineering conclusion not supported by a qualified professional's report.
Correction: rewrite the finding using only location, orientation, length, visible width, displacement, staining, and prior repair.
Reinspection: review the full crack record for the same wording pattern before filing it.

Grading Was Assessed Without Reference to Roof Discharge

Probable cause: soil slope was recorded in isolation without tracing it back to gutter and downspout discharge points from Chapter 3.
Confirmation: compare the recorded grading findings with the discharge points documented in the Chapter 3 record for the same elevations.
Correction: revisit each discharge point and record whether the adjacent grading directs that water away from or toward the foundation.
Reinspection: confirm every downspout discharge point has a corresponding grading observation in this record.

A Crawlspace or Basement Was Entered Before Access Was Confirmed Safe

Probable cause: the inspector proceeded with entry before completing the access check for structure, electrical hazards, pests, and water.
Confirmation: review the sequence of steps recorded for the entry and confirm whether the access check preceded interior observation.
Correction: exit immediately if any unsafe condition is present, record the area as a limitation, and refer the condition instead of continuing the interior inspection.
Reinspection: confirm every access point has a documented safety check completed before any interior finding is recorded.

A Monitored Crack Has No Repeatable Measurement Method

Probable cause: the crack was identified for monitoring, but the measurement method or reference point was not recorded, making future comparison unreliable.
Confirmation: review the monitored-crack entries for a stated measurement method, reference point, and date.
Correction: return to the crack and record a specific, non-destructive measurement method and reference point that can be repeated at the next inspection.
Reinspection: confirm every monitored crack has a defined, repeatable measurement method before filing the record.

An Immediate Hazard Was Recorded as a Routine Monitoring Item

Probable cause: active flooding, severe displacement, or a damaged retaining wall was logged alongside routine findings instead of being escalated at the time it was found.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving active water entry, structural displacement, or retaining-wall failure.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard, notify the responsible party or qualified professional per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate hazard is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the inspection is closed out.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to perform one safe, non-destructive attic inspection and produce a documented record of insulation distribution, ventilation paths, moisture evidence, visible roof-structure conditions, electrical hazards, and pest indicators. The finished work product is one Attic Inspection Worksheet, supported by photographs, that identifies conditions requiring maintenance, monitoring, or referral to a qualified professional.

You will confirm access is safe before entering, use only verified structural framing or approved walk surfaces, observe insulation depth and distribution without disturbing it unnecessarily, trace ventilation paths from soffit to ridge or other mechanical outlet, inspect the underside of the roof sheathing and framing for moisture and daylight, identify ducts terminating improperly in the attic, and record pest evidence without disturbing contamination.

This project builds directly on the baseline walkthrough (Chapter 1), the property record system (Chapter 2), the roof-drainage inspection (Chapter 3), the exterior envelope inspection (Chapter 5), and the interior-finish inspection (Chapter 9). It feeds directly into the HVAC filter and duct observations already made in Chapter 14 and into the prioritized repair plan built in Chapter 19.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-085 — Inspect insulation depth and distribution using an approved representative method without disturbing air- or vapor-control layers.
  • MI-086 — Inspect ventilation paths from soffit, eave, gable, ridge, and mechanical outlets for blockage or short-circuiting.
  • MI-087 — Identify roof-leak indicators visible from the attic, including staining, daylight, rust, decay, and frost.
  • MI-088 — Identify condensation signs at nails, sheathing, ducts, and cold surfaces, and correlate them with ventilation and exhaust conditions.
  • MI-089 — Identify pest evidence, including droppings, nesting, gnawing, and entry points, without disturbing suspected contamination.
  • MI-090 — Document attic deficiencies by zone, component, condition, priority, photograph, and observation limitation.

Where This Skill Fits

The attic is the point where roof, insulation, ventilation, ducts, electrical wiring, and interior ceiling conditions all meet in one accessible space. Chapter 3 documented the roof from safe exterior observation points; this chapter documents the same roof structure from underneath. Chapter 9 documented ceiling stains from the room side; this chapter traces those stains to a source, or documents that the source remains unconfirmed. Chapter 14 identified dryer, bath, and kitchen exhaust ducts at their room-side termination; this chapter verifies where those ducts actually discharge.

Nothing in this project authorizes cutting, drilling, or notching framing, disturbing insulation beyond an approved representative check, handling pest waste, or performing electrical repair. Conditions beyond this scope are referred to the appropriate qualified professional and carried forward into the Chapter 19 prioritized repair plan.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming safe attic access, temperature, structural, electrical, and contamination conditions before entry
  • observing insulation type, distribution, depth, gaps, compression, and contamination from approved walk surfaces
  • tracing ventilation paths and identifying blocked baffles, insulation-covered vents, and short-circuited airflow
  • inspecting the underside of roof sheathing, rafters, trusses, and penetrations for staining, daylight, decay, rust, and frost
  • identifying condensation patterns and correlating them with ducts, exhaust terminations, and cold surfaces
  • identifying visible pest evidence without disturbing suspected contamination
  • documenting every finding by attic zone, component, condition, priority, photograph, and limitation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • enter an attic with unsafe heat, suspected asbestos or vermiculite insulation, mold, sewage, animal waste, aggressive pests, exposed energized wiring, structural damage, fuel-gas odor, inadequate access, or lack of safe footing
  • cut, drill, notch, or otherwise alter trusses, rafters, or sheathing
  • compress, sweep, relocate, or contaminate insulation beyond an approved representative measurement
  • handle pest waste, nests, traps, or dead animals without the approved biological-hazard procedure
  • diagnose structural adequacy, insulation thermal performance, or electrical safety from appearance alone

Any condition that appears to be an immediate hazard must stop the routine attic inspection and be handled per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue before the inspection continues.

Trade Note

Insulation depth is a physical measurement, not a performance rating. Recording "approximately 6 inches of blown-in fiberglass observed at three representative points" is correct. Recording "the attic meets the current insulation code" is not, unless the material, installation, and applicable requirement have been independently verified. Confusing a depth observation with a performance or code conclusion is corrected in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not enter the attic until access, temperature, structural, electrical, and contamination conditions have been accepted and recorded (Step 5 of the Preparation phase). An attic that fails any acceptance condition must be documented as an inaccessible area and referred rather than entered.

Shop Tip

Photograph the attic from the access opening before moving across the space or disturbing insulation. A single wide photograph taken from the hatch, before any weight is placed on framing, establishes the undisturbed condition and is often the most useful reference photograph in the entire finding register.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every attic inspection in this book follows the same sequence: confirm access is safe, photograph the undisturbed condition, move only on verified walk surfaces, observe insulation and ventilation without disturbing them beyond an approved representative check, trace roof and moisture evidence to the framing and sheathing, identify pest evidence without contact, and record every finding by zone before exiting.

Observation Versus Interpretation

Record what is visible, measurable, or photographed. Do not record a diagnosis, a cause, a thermal-performance rating, or a code-compliance conclusion unless it comes from an identified prior report or a qualified professional. "Approximately 4 inches of compressed fiberglass batt observed at the northeast corner" is an observation. "The attic is under-insulated" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact unless confirmed.

Documentation Cue — One Finding, One Zone Identifier, One Photo Pair

Assign each finding to a defined attic zone and component before photographing. Take one overview photograph and one close photograph for every significant finding, using a scale reference where useful. Do not batch multiple unrelated findings under a single zone entry.

Critical Safety Cue — Conditions That Stop Attic Entry

Do not enter, and exit immediately if already inside, for unsafe heat, suspected asbestos or vermiculite insulation, mold, sewage, animal waste, aggressive pests, exposed energized wiring, structural damage, fuel-gas odor, inadequate access, or lack of safe footing. Secure the access point, notify the owner, and refer the condition to the appropriate qualified professional before any further attic work is attempted.

Field Safety Cue — Footing, Heat, and Work Duration

Step only on verified structural framing, permanent decking, or an approved walk board or knee board. Do not step between framing members. Set a work-duration limit appropriate to the attic temperature and exit if heat, dust, or fatigue become excessive. Maintain awareness of the return path to the access opening at all times.

Code Alert — Insulation and Ventilation Requirements Vary

Insulation depth requirements, ventilation ratio requirements, and vapor-control layer requirements vary by climate zone and adopted requirement. Do not represent an observed depth or ventilation path as meeting a specific code value unless that value has been verified against the applicable adopted requirement for this property.

E. Preparation — Access, Conditions, and Safety Acceptance

  1. Review prior records. Review attic type, access, roof history, insulation records, ventilation design, prior pest activity, moisture concerns, and recent weather before scheduling entry.
  2. Check environmental conditions. Record exterior temperature, attic temperature, time of day, lighting, and the work-duration limit appropriate to those conditions.
  3. Inspect the access point before entry. Inspect the access ladder, hatch, stairs, framing, platform, guard, and surrounding ceiling before placing any weight on attic framing.
  4. Prepare protective equipment. Stage approved respiratory, eye, head, hand, and clothing protection, along with lighting, communication, and fall-through protection.
  5. Accept or reject entry conditions. Do not enter for unsafe heat, suspected asbestos or vermiculite, mold, sewage, animal waste, aggressive pests, exposed energized wiring, structural damage, fuel-gas odor, inadequate access, or lack of safe footing. Record the decision on the worksheet before proceeding.

F. Entry, Insulation, and Ventilation Observation

  1. Photograph the undisturbed attic. Take one overview photograph from the access point before disturbing insulation or moving through the space.
  2. Identify safe walk surfaces. Identify verified structural framing, platforms, low-clearance hazards, protruding fasteners, ducts, wiring, and fragile ceiling areas before moving further into the attic.
  3. Step only on verified structural framing or approved walk surfaces. Do not step between framing members at any point during the inspection.
  4. Observe insulation without unnecessary disturbance. Do not compress, sweep, relocate, or contaminate insulation beyond the approved representative measurement in the next step.
  5. Record insulation type and distribution. Identify insulation type, visible depth or thickness, distribution, gaps, compression, displacement, wind washing, contamination, and contact with heat-producing equipment.
  6. Take representative depth measurements. Measure insulation only through an approved representative method without damaging vapor- or air-control layers, and record each measurement location.
  7. Trace ventilation paths. Inspect eave, soffit, ridge, gable, and mechanical ventilation paths visible from the attic, identifying blocked baffles, insulation-covered vents, disconnected ducts, damaged screens, and short-circuited airflow paths.

G. Roof Structure, Ducts, and Penetration Observation

  1. Inspect the underside of the roof structure. Inspect sheathing, rafters, trusses, fasteners, penetrations, chimneys, valleys, and roof-wall intersections for staining, rust, daylight, decay, frost, and prior repairs.
  2. Identify condensation patterns. Inspect for condensation at nails, sheathing, ducts, pipes, hatches, bath-fan ducts, and other cold surfaces.
  3. Trace bath, kitchen, and dryer exhaust ducts. Inspect visible ducts for connection, support, insulation, damage, and confirmed exterior termination.
  4. Identify improperly discharging ducts. Record any duct discharging into the attic itself as a moisture and air-quality deficiency.
  5. Inspect remaining penetrations from a safe distance. Inspect visible plumbing, HVAC, flues, chimneys, electrical cables, junction boxes, recessed fixtures, and other penetrations without touching electrical components.

H. Pest Evidence, Correlation, and Referral

  1. Identify pest evidence without contact. Note droppings, nesting, gnawing, tracks, insect damage, carcasses, odor, damaged screens, and entry points without disturbing suspected contamination.
  2. Correlate findings across the property. Compare attic findings with exterior roof, siding, soffit, gutter, and interior ceiling observations already on file.
  3. Identify conditions requiring prompt referral. Flag active leakage, structural damage, widespread fungal growth, severe pest contamination, damaged flues, or electrical exposure for immediate referral.

I. Closeout and Recordkeeping

  1. Record every finding by zone. Complete each entry with attic zone, component, condition, photograph, priority, and observation limitation.
  2. Exit using the same controlled route. Retrace the entry path and account for all tools and equipment before leaving the attic.
  3. Restore the access point. Close and secure the access hatch, restore insulation or weatherstripping at the hatch where disturbed, and clean the surrounding room.
  4. Update the property maintenance log. Enter insulation, ventilation, moisture, roof, duct, electrical, and pest referrals into the Chapter 2 property record.

Common Attic Inspection Review

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which zone it came from.

  • Zone check: confirm every finding is tied to a defined attic zone and component.
  • Observation check: confirm the recorded text states what was seen, heard, or measured, without an unsupported cause, performance rating, or diagnosis.
  • Priority check: confirm every finding is classified as an immediate hazard, active failure, maintenance need, monitoring item, capital replacement, or cosmetic condition.
  • Photograph check: confirm every significant finding has an overview photograph and a close photograph.
  • Limitation check: confirm every inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, or excluded zone is listed rather than omitted or assumed satisfactory.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Insulation Depth Was Recorded as a Code or Performance Conclusion

Probable cause: the inspector converted a depth measurement directly into a statement about code compliance or thermal performance.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any statement asserting compliance, adequacy, or R-value that is not supported by verified material and installation information.
Correction: rewrite the finding to state only the observed type, distribution, and representative depth measurements, with the applicable requirement identified separately as something to be verified.
Reinspection: review the full worksheet for the same wording pattern before filing it.

A Duct Was Assumed to Terminate Outdoors Without Being Traced

Probable cause: the duct disappeared behind insulation or framing and the inspector assumed a proper termination instead of confirming it.
Confirmation: follow the duct route recorded on the worksheet and compare it against the exterior termination points identified in Chapter 3 and Chapter 14.
Correction: return to the attic, if safely accessible, and trace the duct to its actual termination point, or record the termination as unconfirmed and refer it.
Reinspection: confirm every duct entry on the worksheet states a confirmed termination or a recorded limitation.

A Ventilation Path Was Recorded as Open Without Being Traced End to End

Probable cause: the inspector observed an opening at one end of the path, such as a ridge vent, without confirming the corresponding soffit or eave intake was not blocked by insulation.
Confirmation: review the recorded ventilation finding and compare it against the visible baffle and intake condition at the corresponding eave or soffit.
Correction: reinspect the full path from intake to exhaust and correct the finding to reflect the actual airflow condition, including any short-circuiting.
Reinspection: confirm every ventilation finding identifies both the intake and the exhaust condition, not just one end of the path.

Pest Evidence Was Disturbed During Documentation

Probable cause: the inspector moved, swept, or handled droppings, nesting material, or a suspected carcass to get a clearer photograph.
Confirmation: compare the photograph and the field notes against the approved procedure for pest-evidence documentation.
Correction: record the disturbance on the worksheet, do not repeat the handling, and refer the area for an approved biological-hazard assessment before further disturbance occurs.
Reinspection: confirm no further pest evidence is contacted or relocated for the remainder of the inspection.

An Immediate Hazard Was Buried in the General Zone List

Probable cause: a finding involving exposed energized wiring, active leakage, structural damage, or severe pest contamination was recorded in sequence with routine insulation or ventilation items instead of being flagged and escalated.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving electrical exposure, active leakage, structural damage, or heavy contamination.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard, notify the responsible party per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every immediate hazard is visibly distinguished from routine findings before the worksheet is closed out.

Cleanup

This project ends with a filed record and a secured attic, not a set of loose notes and an open hatch.

  • Confirm the attic access hatch, ladder, and any moved insulation or weatherstripping at the hatch are restored to their original position
  • Confirm all tools, forms, and protective equipment are accounted for and removed from the attic
  • Clean the room surrounding the access point of any insulation fibers, dust, or debris
  • Back up or print all photographs and match them to their finding identifiers

Field Terms

Attic Zone
A defined portion of the attic used to organize findings consistently across the inspection.
Approved Walk Surface
Verified structural framing, permanent decking, or an approved walk board or knee board capable of carrying the inspector's weight.
Representative Measurement
An insulation-depth or moisture reading taken at a limited number of defined points, used to describe general conditions.
Short-Circuited Airflow
A ventilation condition where intake and exhaust air mix locally instead of moving along the intended path.
Baffle
A rigid or semi-rigid channel that keeps insulation clear of the soffit or eave vent opening to preserve airflow.
Wind Washing
Displacement or thinning of insulation caused by uncontrolled airflow at the eave, reducing effective coverage.
Observed Condition
A statement of what was seen, heard, measured, or photographed, without a stated or implied cause.
Limitation
An area or component that was inaccessible, concealed, obstructed, unsafe, not operating, or outside the approved scope.
Attic Inspection Worksheet
The single dated document recording every finding, limitation, and referral from this attic inspection.

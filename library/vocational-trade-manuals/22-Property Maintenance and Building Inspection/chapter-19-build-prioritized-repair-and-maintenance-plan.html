Job Overview

This project qualifies you to convert the accumulated findings from one property's baseline walkthrough (Chapter 1) and chapter-by-chapter inspections and maintenance cycles (Chapters 3 through 18) into a single accountable action plan. The finished work product is one written plan that sorts every open finding by urgency, assigns a responsible party and a target window, and sequences the work across a 30-day, 60-day, 90-day, annual, and multiyear horizon.

You will collect and de-duplicate the property's open findings, verify that every item contains the information needed to act on it, separate immediate hazards from routine maintenance, assign priority categories, identify dependencies between tasks, assign responsible parties and qualifications, and build the sequenced plan. You will then review the plan with the owner or responsible party and enter it into the property calendar and binder.

This project does not add new field observations. It organizes and sequences the findings already produced by Chapters 1 through 18. It also does not perform any repair, inspection, or maintenance task itself; it is a planning and documentation project. Chapter 20 uses the method built here to plan and execute the book's full capstone inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-109 — Sort accumulated property findings by urgency using a consistent priority system.
  • MI-110 — Distinguish life-safety and active-failure conditions from cosmetic or preference-based conditions.
  • MI-111 — Estimate maintenance frequency for recurring tasks from manufacturer instructions, adopted requirements, climate, and observed condition rather than a generic interval.
  • MI-112 — Assign a repair priority, responsible party, required qualification, and target window to each open finding.
  • MI-113 — Create a sequenced 30-60-90 day plan that reflects dependencies between tasks.
  • MI-114 — Document long-term monitoring and capital-replacement needs without assigning unsupported life-expectancy claims.

Where This Skill Fits

Every prior chapter in this book produced findings: the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough, the Chapter 3 through 18 system-specific inspections and preventive-maintenance cycles, and any referrals issued along the way. Those findings have accumulated in the Chapter 2 property binder as separate forms, logs, and photograph indexes. This project is the point in the book where all of that material becomes one accountable plan instead of a stack of separate records.

The plan you build here is not a final report. It is a working document the owner or responsible party uses to authorize, schedule, and verify work over the following year and beyond. Chapter 20 repeats this same classification-and-sequencing method at full property scale, immediately after completing a fresh full-property inspection and preventive-maintenance cycle, and delivers the resulting plan as part of the book's final owner handoff package.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • collecting and de-duplicating findings from the baseline walkthrough, chapter inspection forms, photographs, maintenance logs, service records, owner concerns, warranties, permits, and open referrals
  • separating observed conditions from suspected causes and unverified owner reports
  • classifying every open finding into immediate hazard, urgent water or system failure, short-term repair, recurring maintenance, monitoring item, planned capital replacement, or cosmetic preference
  • assigning a priority category, target window, responsible party, required qualification, permit or inspection need, and verification method to each finding
  • identifying dependencies between findings and grouping related items into work packages
  • building a 30-day, 31-60 day, 61-90 day, annual, and multiyear plan
  • reviewing the finished plan with the owner or responsible party and entering it into the property calendar and binder

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform any new field inspection, repair, or maintenance task; all findings used here must already exist in the property record from a prior chapter
  • assign a fixed cost, warranty claim, structural conclusion, code-compliance conclusion, or remaining-life estimate not supported by an identified source
  • place an immediate life-safety, fire, carbon-monoxide, electrical, fuel, structural, sewage, active-leakage, or blocked-egress finding into an ordinary future-dated plan instead of escalating it through the approved emergency or urgent-service process
  • represent budgetary figures gathered for planning purposes as fixed bids

Any immediate hazard discovered while reviewing prior records but not yet escalated must stop the planning session and be handled per the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter before planning continues.

Trade Note

A plan is only as reliable as the findings behind it. If a finding in the property record does not already state a location, an observed condition, a consequence, and supporting evidence, this chapter's first task is to correct that record — not to plan around an incomplete finding. A plan built on vague entries produces vague schedules that no one can verify as complete.

Setup Standard

Do not begin classification until every source record listed in Step 1 has been collected in one place. A plan built while records are still scattered across separate chapter folders will miss dependencies and duplicate findings.

Shop Tip

Work through the open-item list twice: once to classify and once to sequence. Classifying and sequencing in the same pass causes urgency judgments to be distorted by scheduling convenience — an item gets marked "short-term" because it fits an open week, not because that is its true priority.

Common Field Fundamentals

This chapter follows the same five-part sequence used throughout the book — confirm scope, observe, document, separate hazard from routine, and record limitations — applied to a collection of prior findings rather than to a fresh walkthrough. The scope here is the complete set of open findings; the "observation" is each finding as already recorded; the documentation is the sequenced plan itself; and hazard separation means immediate items are escalated, not scheduled.

Priority Categories at a Glance

Immediate HazardEscalated through the emergency or urgent-service process; never placed in a future-dated plan
Urgent Water/System FailureCan cause accelerating damage; scheduled into the 30-day plan
Short-Term RepairPrevents further deterioration; typically 30–90 day window
Recurring MaintenanceControlled by season, use, or manufacturer instruction; entered on the annual calendar
Monitoring ItemRequires repeat photographs, measurements, or operation checks over time
Capital ReplacementPlanned for aging or repeatedly repaired systems; multiyear horizon

A seventh category, cosmetic preference, is tracked separately from the six categories above and is never substituted for a safety, water, structural, or operational finding.

Documentation Cue — One Finding, One Classification, One Assignment

Carry the original finding identifier from its source chapter forward into the plan unchanged. Assign exactly one priority category per finding. If a finding could plausibly fit two categories, assign the more urgent one and note the reasoning in the plan.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Hazards Are Escalated, Not Scheduled

If collection or review of prior records surfaces a fire, carbon-monoxide, electrical, fuel-gas, structural, sewage, active-leakage, or blocked-egress finding that was not already escalated, stop the planning session. Notify the owner, building management, the utility, or emergency services as the situation requires, and confirm the condition is addressed or under qualified control before returning this item to the ordinary planning process.

Field Safety Cue — Coordinating Scheduled Work

When sequencing tasks that will interrupt water, power, heat, cooling, ventilation, or other essential services, coordinate the target date with the occupant and note the coordination requirement directly on the plan. Do not assign a target date that assumes uninterrupted access without confirming it.

Code Alert — Qualification and Authorization Boundaries

Assign each task to the correct category of responsible person: owner maintenance, general repair, licensed trade, specialty contractor, environmental professional, engineer, or other qualified person. Do not assign a task requiring a permit, license, or specialized inspection to owner maintenance merely because it fits the current schedule.

E. Collection and Verification of Findings

  1. Collect the current baseline walkthrough and every chapter inspection form. Gather the Chapter 1 baseline condition list, Chapter 3–18 inspection and maintenance forms, photographs, maintenance logs, service records, owner concerns, warranties, permits, and open referrals in one location.
  2. Remove duplicate findings. Merge repeated entries for the same location and component while preserving the original identifiers and dates.
  3. Verify completeness. Confirm every open item identifies location, component, observed condition, consequence, recommendation, and evidence before it enters the plan.
  4. Separate observation from interpretation. Confirm each finding states an observed condition rather than an unverified owner report or an unsupported suspected cause; label any unconfirmed cause clearly.

F. Priority Classification

  1. Identify immediate hazards. Flag every fire, carbon-monoxide, electrical, fuel, structural, sewage, active-leakage, or blocked-egress condition still shown as open.
  2. Escalate immediate hazards. Route each one through the approved emergency or urgent-service process rather than placing it in an ordinary future-dated plan.
  3. Identify urgent water-control and system-failure items. Flag roof, drainage, plumbing, condensate, and envelope findings that can cause accelerating damage.
  4. Identify operational failures. Flag findings affecting heat, cooling, water, sanitation, security, alarms, pumps, drainage, or essential appliances.
  5. Identify short-term repairs. Flag items needed to prevent further deterioration that are not immediate or urgent.
  6. Identify recurring preventive maintenance tasks. Flag items controlled by season, use, manufacturer instructions, or observed condition.
  7. Identify monitoring items. Flag items requiring repeat photographs, measurements, or operation checks.
  8. Identify planned capital replacements. Flag aging or repeatedly repaired systems without assigning an unsupported remaining-life estimate.
  9. Identify cosmetic preferences separately. Keep preference-based items distinct from safety, water, structure, operation, and preservation needs.

G. Assignment and Dependency Mapping

  1. Assign priority category, target window, responsible party, qualification, permit need, and verification method. Complete this assignment for each finding on the worksheet.
  2. Identify dependencies. Note required order, such as stopping a leak before drywall repair, correcting drainage before foundation finish work, or completing electrical repair before closing a wall.
  3. Group related items into work packages. Combine items for efficient scheduling without hiding individual acceptance criteria.
  4. Identify the correct category of responsible person. Assign each task to owner maintenance, general repair, licensed trade, specialty contractor, environmental professional, engineer, or other qualified person as appropriate.
  5. Gather budgetary information from appropriate sources. Use current qualified providers or documented references only, and label each estimate by confidence and date.
  6. Assign cost classes or allowances. Present preliminary figures as ranges or allowances, not as fixed bids.

H. Building the 30-60-90 Day and Long-Term Plan

  1. Create the first 30-day plan. Include hazards already escalated for tracking purposes, active failures, water entry, required testing, and high-consequence maintenance.
  2. Create the 31-60 day plan. Include stabilized repairs, service work, access improvements, and recurring tasks.
  3. Create the 61-90 day plan. Include lower-risk repairs, efficiency improvements, finish restoration, and documentation completion.
  4. Create the annual and multiyear plan. Include seasonal cycles, professional service, monitoring, and capital projects.
  5. Integrate manufacturer and warranty maintenance. Confirm required service intervals are represented so they are not missed.
  6. Identify scheduling constraints. Note permits, design, procurement lead times, shutdowns, occupant relocation, weather, and access that may affect timing.
  7. Assign a status to every item. Use open, scheduled, in progress, waiting, referred, completed, verified, or deferred by owner.
  8. Define closure evidence. State the invoice, photograph, test result, inspection approval, or reinspection record required to close each item.
  9. Create a risk statement for any owner-deferred safety, water, structural, or essential-system work. Document the deferral decision and its known consequence.

I. Review, Delivery, and Recordkeeping

  1. Review the plan for conflicts and gaps. Check for unrealistic timing, missing dependencies, unassigned items, and overloaded periods.
  2. Present the plan to the owner or responsible party. Document decisions, deferrals, budget constraints, and approved next actions.
  3. Enter tasks and reminders into the property calendar and maintenance binder. Confirm every target date and recurring task has a corresponding entry.
  4. Schedule the next plan review. Set the review date for after urgent work is completed and at the next seasonal cycle.

Common Plan Review

Review every entry using the same checks regardless of which chapter it originated from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every finding retains its original identifier and source chapter reference.
  • Classification check: confirm every finding is assigned exactly one of the seven priority categories.
  • Escalation check: confirm no immediate hazard appears only in a future-dated section of the plan.
  • Assignment check: confirm every finding has a responsible party, qualification, target window, and verification method.
  • Dependency check: confirm items with a required order are sequenced correctly relative to one another.
  • Cost-labeling check: confirm every budget figure is labeled by source, date, and confidence rather than presented as fixed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Immediate Hazard Was Left in the 30-Day Plan Instead of Escalated

Probable cause: the finding was classified correctly in its source chapter but was carried into this plan as an ordinary short-term item rather than flagged for escalation.
Confirmation: review every finding tagged fire, carbon monoxide, electrical, fuel, structural, sewage, active leakage, or blocked egress in the source records against its treatment in this plan.
Correction: remove the item from the ordinary 30-day schedule, escalate it through the approved emergency or urgent-service process, and record the escalation and its outcome separately.
Reinspection: confirm no remaining immediate-hazard finding appears solely in a future-dated section.

A Finding Entered the Plan Without Enough Information to Act On

Probable cause: the source record was incomplete, missing a location, observed condition, consequence, or evidence reference.
Confirmation: compare the finding against the Acceptance Standards for finding completeness.
Correction: return to the source chapter record and complete the missing field before the item is scheduled, or mark it as requiring reinspection before scheduling.
Reinspection: confirm every scheduled item meets the finding-completeness standard before the plan is presented to the owner.

A Dependency Was Missed and Two Tasks Were Scheduled Out of Order

Probable cause: related findings from different chapters (such as a roof leak and an interior ceiling stain) were classified independently without cross-referencing.
Confirmation: review findings by location across all chapters and identify any pair where one item's cause must be corrected before the other can be properly repaired.
Correction: reorder the dependent task to follow its source-correction task and note the dependency directly on the plan.
Reinspection: confirm the finished plan lists no finish, cosmetic, or downstream repair scheduled before its identified source correction.

A Preliminary Cost Estimate Was Presented as a Fixed Bid

Probable cause: a budgetary figure gathered for planning purposes was entered on the plan without a source, date, or confidence label.
Confirmation: review every cost figure on the plan for a labeled source and confidence level.
Correction: add the source, date, and confidence label to the figure, or remove it and replace it with a cost class or allowance until a qualified provider's estimate is obtained.
Reinspection: confirm no cost figure on the finished plan is presented without a source and confidence label.

A Deferred Safety or Water-Control Item Has No Risk Statement

Probable cause: the owner declined or postponed a safety, water, structural, or essential-system item, but the deferral was recorded only as a status change without documenting the known consequence.
Confirmation: review every item marked "deferred by owner" for an accompanying risk statement.
Correction: add a risk statement describing the known consequence of the deferral and confirm the owner's acknowledgment is recorded.
Reinspection: confirm every deferred safety, water, structural, or essential-system item carries a risk statement before the plan is filed.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect the accessible plumbing fixtures and visible supply connections of a residence and produce one prioritized plumbing maintenance list. The finished work product is a single written record: a fixture-by-fixture inventory of leakage, operation, drainage, shutoff serviceability, and visible pipe condition, supported by photographs, that separates hazards requiring immediate attention from routine maintenance and monitoring items.

You will locate the main water shutoff and every fixture shutoff, inspect under and around water-using fixtures and equipment for leakage evidence, operate faucets and drains within a safe scope, inspect traps and visible supply piping, and gently exercise selected shutoff valves to confirm whether they actually control flow. You will not dismantle traps, use chemical drain cleaner, operate a relief valve, or perform any water-heater combustion or electrical work.

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough, which first located the main shutoff and identified visible plumbing concerns at a lower level of detail. It produces the fixture-level record that Chapter 11 uses to select approved basic maintenance tasks, such as exercising valves, cleaning aerators, and clearing minor accessible drain buildup. It also feeds directly into the Chapter 19 prioritized repair plan and the Chapter 20 capstone inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-055 — Inspect fixture leaks at every accessible water-using fixture and appliance connection.
  • MI-056 — Test shutoff valves gently and verify whether each tested valve actually stops or reduces flow.
  • MI-057 — Inspect trap assemblies for material, alignment, support, corrosion, and leakage evidence without dismantling them.
  • MI-058 — Identify slow drains by observation without assigning a vent or sewer diagnosis from a single fixture.
  • MI-059 — Inspect visible supply lines and connectors for corrosion, unsupported runs, abrasion, kinks, bulges, and leakage.
  • MI-060 — Document plumbing maintenance needs in a prioritized, fixture-by-fixture plumbing maintenance list.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 9 inspected interior finish surfaces and identified moisture indicators around plumbing fixtures as part of a room-by-room finish review. This project returns to those same locations and inspects the plumbing system itself: fixtures, shutoffs, traps, drains, and visible supply piping.

Chapter 11 uses this project's findings to select a defined subset of items for approved basic maintenance, such as exercising a serviceable valve or cleaning an aerator. Findings that exceed the basic-maintenance scope, such as active leakage, corroded supply piping, or an unresponsive shutoff, are carried into the Chapter 19 prioritized repair plan and, where applicable, referred to a licensed plumber.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • locating the main water shutoff, fixture shutoffs, cleanouts, sump systems, water heater, and other visible service points
  • inspecting under and around sinks, toilets, tubs, showers, laundry connections, water heater, refrigerator, dishwasher, and other water-using equipment for leakage evidence
  • operating faucets, drains, and toilets through normal use only when it is safe to do so
  • inspecting traps, visible supply piping, and connectors without dismantling them
  • exercising only selected shutoff valves gently, with immediate leak observation, to confirm serviceability
  • inspecting water-heater connections, drain pan, relief-discharge path, venting, and clearances from safe external observation points only
  • producing one fixture-by-fixture plumbing maintenance list with observation, priority, and recommendation

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • dismantle a trap, use chemical drain cleaner, or perform any invasive drain investigation
  • operate a relief valve, remove a water-heater cover, adjust a control, or perform combustion or electrical work on the water heater
  • force a valve that binds, leaks, corrodes, lacks support, or appears likely to fail
  • diagnose vent or sewer conditions, water-heater safety, or piping-material adequacy from a single fixture observation

Any active uncontrolled leakage, sewage, fuel-gas odor, unsafe water-heater venting, damaged piping, or electrical exposure encountered during this project must be treated as a priority referral condition per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue.

Trade Note

A shutoff valve that has never been tested is an unverified control, not a working one. Recording "shutoff present" without confirming that it actually stops flow is the most common documentation gap corrected in this chapter's troubleshooting section. If a valve cannot be safely tested within this project's scope, record it as untested rather than assuming it functions.

Setup Standard

Do not exercise any shutoff valve until the main water shutoff is confirmed accessible and a leak-response plan, towels, and a catch pan are staged (Step 2). A valve tested before the leak-response plan is ready must be re-verified before it is entered as serviceable on the plumbing maintenance list.

Shop Tip

Inspect and photograph each fixture's undisturbed condition before operating any valve, faucet, or drain at that location. A pre-operation photograph protects you from mistaking a pre-existing stain or drip for one caused by your own testing.

Common Field Fundamentals

Plumbing fixture inspection follows the same five-part sequence used throughout this book: confirm scope, observe, document, separate hazard from routine, and record limitations. At the fixture level, this means locating shutoffs first, observing before operating, and testing valves only after a leak-response plan is in place.

Observation Versus Interpretation

Record what is visible, audible, or measurable at the fixture. "Water drains slowly from the hall bathroom sink" is an observation. "The vent stack is blocked" is an interpretation and must not be recorded as fact from a single fixture. Slow drainage at one fixture is recorded as an observed condition with a recommendation for further evaluation, not as a vent or sewer diagnosis.

Documentation Cue — Record Every Valve's Tested Status

For every shutoff valve encountered, record whether it was tested and, if tested, whether it fully stopped flow, partially reduced flow, leaked, bound, or could not be safely tested. Do not record a valve as functional based on appearance alone.

Critical Safety Cue — Active Leakage, Sewage, and Fuel-Gas Odor Stop Routine Inspection

If you encounter active uncontrolled leakage, sewage, fuel-gas odor, unsafe water-heater venting, damaged piping presenting an immediate hazard, or energized wet equipment, stop the routine inspection immediately. Secure or isolate the area if safe to do so, and notify the owner, building management, the utility, or emergency services as the situation requires before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Valve Testing Limits

Exercise shutoff valves gently and stop immediately if a valve leaks, binds, corrodes, lacks support, or appears likely to fail. Do not force a valve. Do not rely on a valve that has not been proven to stop flow; document inaccessible, inoperable, or missing shutoffs instead of assuming them functional.

Code Alert — This Inspection Is Not a Licensed Plumbing Evaluation

Do not represent this inspection as a licensed plumbing evaluation, code-compliance certification, or authorization for water-heater combustion, gas, or electrical service. Piping-material adequacy, water-heater safety, and vent or sewer diagnosis remain outside this project's scope and require a qualified professional.

E. Preparation — Records, Access, and Leak-Response Setup

  1. Review known plumbing history. Review known leaks, water-pressure concerns, slow drains, backups, fixture age, prior plumbing work, and water-damage history.
  2. Locate the main water shutoff. Confirm access to the main shutoff without operating it unless the approved scope includes a safe test.
  3. Identify all visible service points. Locate fixture shutoffs, cleanouts, sump systems, water heater, pressure equipment, and other visible service points.
  4. Stage leak-response supplies. Prepare towels, a catch pan, flashlight, inspection mirror, moisture-screening tool where authorized, and leak-response supplies.

F. Fixture-by-Fixture Leakage and Operation Inspection

  1. Inspect under and around every water-using fixture and appliance. Cover sinks, toilets, tubs, showers, laundry connections, water heater, refrigerator, dishwasher, and other water-using equipment.
  2. Look for leakage and related evidence. Note active droplets, mineral deposits, corrosion, staining, swelling, soft cabinet material, damaged flooring, and prior patching.
  3. Inspect faucet bodies and connections. Cover handles, spouts, sprayers, supply connectors, shutoffs, escutcheons, and mounting security.
  4. Operate each faucet through normal flow when safe. Run hot, cold, and mixed flow and observe flow, temperature response, sputtering, vibration, leakage, drainage, and fixture stability.
  5. Fill and drain approved sink or basin fixtures. Observe drain bodies, overflow paths, traps, joints, and cabinet bases during the fill-and-drain cycle.

G. Trap, Drain, and Fixture-Specific Inspection

  1. Inspect traps without dismantling them. Check material, alignment, support, corrosion, leakage evidence, improper strain, and access.
  2. Observe drain speed without assigning a system-wide diagnosis. Note drain speed and sounds without concluding a vent or sewer defect from one slow fixture.
  3. Inspect toilets. Check base movement, staining, supply leakage, tank leakage, continuous running, fill operation, flush performance, and secure seat, without aggressively rocking the toilet or concealing base leakage with caulk.
  4. Inspect tub and shower components. Cover controls, spouts, heads, drains, accessible panels, enclosure joints, and adjacent finishes.

H. Visible Supply Line and Shutoff Valve Testing

  1. Inspect visible supply piping and connectors. Check for corrosion, unsupported runs, abrasion, kinks, bulges, leakage, incompatible repairs, and freeze exposure, and identify recalled or inappropriate connector materials only through documented product information.
  2. Inspect each fixture shutoff visually before operation. Confirm accessibility and visible condition before any testing.
  3. Exercise only valves selected for safe testing. Use gentle, controlled movement and observe for leakage immediately, stopping if a valve binds, leaks, lacks support, corrodes, or appears likely to fail.
  4. Verify that each tested valve actually stops or reduces flow. Confirm the result through the approved downstream fixture.
  5. Return every tested valve to its required operating position. Recheck for leakage after the valve is restored.

I. Water Heater Exterior, Hazard Review, and Closeout

  1. Inspect water-heater connections from safe external observation points only. Cover the drain pan, relief-discharge path, venting, combustion area, and clearances without operating relief valves, removing covers, adjusting controls, or performing combustion work.
  2. Identify priority referral conditions. Flag active uncontrolled leakage, sewage, fuel-gas odor, unsafe water-heater venting, damaged piping, or electrical exposure for immediate referral.
  3. Record every fixture and visible connection. Include location, observation, operation result, shutoff status, drainage result, photograph, and recommendation for each item.
  4. Create the maintenance action list. Separate cleaning, aerator service, valve service, connector replacement, trap repair, fixture repair, drainage evaluation, and water-damage investigation.

Common Plumbing Fixture Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which fixture or segment of the route it came from.

  • Leakage check: confirm every water-using fixture and appliance connection was inspected for active leakage and related evidence.
  • Valve check: confirm every encountered shutoff is recorded as tested-serviceable, tested-defective, or untested.
  • Trap check: confirm every accessible trap was inspected without dismantling and recorded for material, alignment, support, and corrosion.
  • Observation check: confirm no finding states a vent, sewer, or water-heater-safety diagnosis unsupported by a prior report or qualified professional.
  • Photograph check: confirm every significant finding has an overview photograph and a close photograph.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Shutoff Valve Was Recorded as Functional Without Being Tested

Probable cause: the inspector assumed the valve worked because it looked intact, without gently exercising it.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for any valve entry marked "OK" or "functional" with no corresponding test result.
Correction: return to the valve, exercise it gently within the approved scope, and record the actual tested result, or mark it as untested if testing is not safely possible.
Reinspection: confirm every valve entry states tested-serviceable, tested-defective, or untested before filing the record.

A Slow Drain Was Recorded as a Vent or Sewer Diagnosis

Probable cause: the inspector concluded a system-wide cause from a single fixture's drainage speed.
Confirmation: reread the finding and identify any word implying a vent, sewer, or main-line cause not supported by a prior report or qualified professional.
Correction: rewrite the finding to state the observed drain speed only, and add a recommendation for further evaluation if the condition is significant.
Reinspection: review all drainage findings for the same wording pattern before filing the record.

A Trap Was Disassembled During the Inspection

Probable cause: the inspector attempted to open or loosen the trap to view internal condition instead of inspecting it externally.
Confirmation: check whether the trap was reassembled correctly and tested for leakage after the inspection.
Correction: reassemble the trap according to its original configuration, fill and drain the fixture, and inspect the joint for leakage before continuing.
Reinspection: confirm no further trap disassembly occurs during the remainder of the inspection.

A Valve Was Forced After Binding

Probable cause: the inspector continued turning a valve handle after resistance was felt, risking stem or body damage.
Confirmation: inspect the valve body, stem, and downstream connection for new leakage or damage.
Correction: stop further operation of the valve, record it as tested-defective, and refer it for qualified plumbing service.
Reinspection: confirm no other valve in the inspection was forced past initial binding resistance.

An Active Leak Was Recorded as a Routine Maintenance Item

Probable cause: the finding was placed in sequence with routine items instead of being flagged and escalated at the time it was found.
Confirmation: review the priority category assigned to every finding involving active dripping, uncontrolled leakage, or sewage.
Correction: reclassify the finding as an immediate hazard or active failure as appropriate, notify the responsible party per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue, and record the notification separately from the routine list.
Reinspection: confirm every active leak is visibly distinguished from routine maintenance findings before the inspection is closed out.

Cleanup

This project ends with a filed fixture-by-fixture record, not a set of loose notes.

  • Confirm every valve exercised during the inspection is returned to its required operating position and rechecked for leakage
  • Confirm every access panel, cabinet door, and moved item is restored to its original position
  • Dry and clean all work areas so any subsequent leakage can be detected
  • Transfer handwritten notes to the Plumbing Fixture Inspection worksheet if a digital form was not used in the field
  • Store the completed plumbing maintenance list, photographs, and any immediate-hazard notifications in the property record established in Chapter 2

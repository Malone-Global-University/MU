Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inventory, test, and service every accessible smoke alarm, carbon-monoxide alarm, and combination alarm in one residence, and to visually inspect approved emergency equipment and exit routes. The finished work product is a single dated device-by-device test record identifying manufacturer, model, power source, interconnection, test result, battery service, deficiencies, and replacement actions, supported by a walked and documented exit-route review.

You will notify occupants and any monitoring service before testing, locate and record every accessible alarm and emergency device, test each alarm using its built-in test control and manufacturer procedure, replace only the specified battery type where applicable, visually inspect fire extinguishers and escape equipment without discharging or deploying them, walk primary and secondary exit routes, and restore monitoring status before closing out the project.

This project builds directly on the Chapter 1 baseline walkthrough and the Chapter 12 electrical device review, both of which located alarms, exits, and panel information without testing them. It feeds directly into Chapter 18, which repeats alarm testing on a seasonal cycle, and into Chapter 19 and Chapter 20, where any failed alarm or blocked exit becomes an immediate-hazard item in the property's prioritized plan.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-073 — Test smoke alarms using their built-in test controls and manufacturer procedures.
  • MI-074 — Test carbon-monoxide alarms using their built-in test controls and manufacturer procedures.
  • MI-075 — Replace alarm batteries with the specified battery type, orientation, and date-marking procedure.
  • MI-076 — Inspect fire extinguishers visually for accessibility, damage, pressure indication, and service status.
  • MI-077 — Check emergency exit routes for obstruction and safe operation.
  • MI-078 — Document a complete life-safety readiness record with device inventory, test results, and replacement needs.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 located alarms, exits, and emergency equipment as part of the baseline walkthrough, and Chapter 12 inspected accessible electrical devices without testing life-safety equipment. This project completes the life-safety picture by testing every device on record and confirming exit routes are usable, without opening any panel, dismantling any alarm, or performing electrical repair.

The device inventory and test record produced here become the life-safety section of the Chapter 2 property binder, are repeated on the seasonal cycle in Chapter 18, and are escalated directly into the Chapter 19 30-60-90 day plan whenever a device fails, is beyond its documented service life, or an exit route is found blocked. The Chapter 20 capstone repeats this exact test-and-document procedure at full property scale.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • notifying occupants and any monitoring service before testing interconnected or monitored devices
  • inventorying every accessible smoke alarm, carbon-monoxide alarm, combination alarm, hearing-impaired alert device, fire extinguisher, escape ladder, emergency light, and designated exit
  • testing each alarm using its built-in test control and manufacturer procedure only
  • replacing removable batteries only with the manufacturer-specified type, orientation, and date-marking procedure
  • visually inspecting fire extinguishers and approved emergency escape equipment without discharging, deploying, or servicing them
  • walking primary and secondary exit routes and identifying obstructions
  • producing one device-by-device life-safety readiness record

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • use flame, candles, matches, smoke, vehicle exhaust, canned gas not approved for the device, or any other improvised test source
  • remove, silence, relocate, paint, cover, or disconnect a required alarm without an approved replacement and authorization
  • discharge a fire extinguisher as a routine test, or service, recharge, or repair a fire extinguisher, alarm, or escape device
  • service devices connected to a managed security, fire, sprinkler, or elevator system except through the approved authorized-service procedure

Any alarm that fails, chirps, shows a fault, is beyond its documented service life, lacks required power, or cannot be safely accessed must be identified for prompt replacement rather than reset and left in place. Any blocked or unsafe exit route must be reported to the responsible party before the walkthrough continues.

Trade Note

An alarm's service life, battery type, and interconnected behavior come from its product label, its printed manufacturer instructions, the property's existing records, and adopted requirements — never from a generic interval remembered from a different property or device. A ten-year sealed-battery smoke alarm, a replaceable-battery smoke alarm, and a plug-in carbon-monoxide alarm with battery backup are different products with different test and replacement procedures, and this chapter treats each one according to its own label.

Common Field Fundamentals

Life-safety testing in this book follows the same discipline established in Chapter 1: confirm scope, observe and test, document, separate hazard from routine, and record limitations. Here that discipline is applied specifically to devices whose entire purpose is to alert occupants to fire, carbon monoxide, and blocked escape, so a missed or mishandled test carries more consequence than a missed cosmetic finding elsewhere in the property.

Testing Versus Servicing

This project tests and performs only the battery service described in the outline. It does not authorize disassembly, recalibration, sensor cleaning beyond the manufacturer's basic user-level instruction, or any repair of an alarm, extinguisher, or escape device. A device that fails its built-in test, shows a fault, or is beyond its documented service life is a replacement item, not a repair item, within this chapter's scope.

Documentation Cue — One Device, One Identifier, One Test Result

Assign a unique device identifier tied to location before testing. Record manufacturer, model, power source, interconnection, manufacture date, replacement date if known, test method, and result for every device. Do not batch multiple devices under a single identifier or test entry.

Critical Safety Cue — Failed Devices and Blocked Exits Are Immediate Items

A smoke alarm or carbon-monoxide alarm that fails its test, a required alarm that is missing, or a primary or secondary exit route that is blocked or inoperable is an immediate life-safety finding. Notify the responsible party before the routine testing continues, and arrange prompt replacement or clearance rather than recording the condition as a routine maintenance item.

Field Safety Cue — Ladders, Reach Tools, and Occupant Coordination

Use a step stool or approved reach tool with stable footing and three-point contact when reaching ceiling-mounted alarms; do not stand on furniture. Coordinate testing times with occupants, especially in sleeping areas, and notify any monitoring service before and after testing so a test activation is not mistaken for a real event.

Code Alert — Device Placement, Interconnection, and Replacement Vary

The number, placement, interconnection, and required replacement schedule for smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms are controlled by adopted requirements and the specific product installed, not by a generic rule. Verify device type, location, and required quantity against the product label, manufacturer instructions, and applicable adopted requirements before representing any device as compliant or non-compliant.

E. Preparation — Notification, Inventory, and Setup

  1. Review property and device background. Review adopted requirements, property type, sleeping areas, fuel-burning equipment, attached-garage conditions, prior alarm records, and manufacturer instructions.
  2. Notify occupants and the monitoring service. Notify occupants and monitoring services as required before testing interconnected or monitored devices, and record the notification on the worksheet.
  3. Identify every device and exit. Locate every accessible smoke alarm, carbon-monoxide alarm, combination alarm, hearing-impaired alert device, fire extinguisher, escape ladder, emergency light, and designated exit.
  4. Record device identity from each label. Record manufacturer, model, power source, interconnection, location, manufacture date, replacement date if known, and visible condition for each device.
  5. Confirm no device will be altered without authorization. Do not remove, silence, relocate, paint, cover, or disconnect a required device without an approved replacement and authorization.

F. Pre-Test Visual Inspection

  1. Inspect each alarm before testing. Check for damage, obstruction, paint, contamination, loose mounting, a missing battery door, a removed battery, and end-of-life indication.
  2. Verify device type and location against available information. Compare the device and its location to product instructions and adopted requirements without representing the review as a code certification.

G. Alarm Testing

  1. Test using the built-in control only. Use the built-in test control and manufacturer procedure for each alarm.
  2. Do not use an improvised test source. Do not use flame, candles, matches, smoke, vehicle exhaust, canned gas not approved for the device, or any other improvised test source.
  3. Confirm output at the device. Confirm audible and visible output at the device being tested.
  4. Confirm interconnection through the approved procedure. Confirm interconnected activation only through the approved system procedure, coordinated with occupants.
  5. Verify audibility where included in scope. Verify occupants can hear or receive the required notification in sleeping and occupied areas where the project scope includes this assessment.
  6. Stop for managed systems. Stop and refer alarms connected to security, fire, sprinkler, elevator, or other managed systems when testing requires authorized service outside this project's scope.

H. Battery Service and Retesting

  1. Replace only the specified battery. Replace removable batteries only with the specified battery type, orientation, and date-marking procedure.
  2. Do not substitute a battery for required replacement. Do not install a battery in a damaged, contaminated, recalled, or end-of-life alarm as a substitute for replacing the device.
  3. Restore the device after service. Restore battery doors, mounting, power, and interconnection after service.
  4. Retest after service. Retest every alarm after battery replacement or remounting.
  5. Flag devices requiring replacement. Identify alarms that fail, chirp, show a fault, are beyond documented service life, lack required power, or cannot be accessed safely, and arrange prompt replacement with compatible approved equipment.

I. Emergency Equipment and Exit-Route Inspection

  1. Inspect fire extinguishers visually. Check accessibility, correct location, visible damage, corrosion, leakage, broken seal, missing pin, hose condition, pressure indication where present, service tag, and labeling.
  2. Do not discharge or service an extinguisher. Do not discharge an extinguisher as a routine test, and refer damaged, discharged, recalled, improperly rated, unserviceable, or questionable extinguishers for approved service or replacement.
  3. Inspect escape ladders and other approved equipment without deploying them. Check access, packaging, instructions, compatibility, and expiration or service information.
  4. Walk primary and secondary exit routes. Walk routes from every sleeping and occupied area.
  5. Verify exit operation. Confirm doors, windows, halls, stairs, landings, locks, and exterior paths are not blocked and can be operated as required.
  6. Identify obstructions. Note security devices, bars, storage, furniture, damaged hardware, snow, or landscaping that may obstruct emergency escape.
  7. Inspect supporting emergency information. Check posted emergency contacts, flashlights, first-aid supplies, and utility-shutoff information where included in the property plan.

J. Closeout and Recordkeeping

  1. Restore monitoring status. Restore monitoring status and notify occupants and the monitoring service that testing is complete.
  2. Complete the device-by-device test record. Record date, test method, result, battery, age information, deficiencies, replacement action, and next required condition for every device.
  3. Update the property log. File the readiness record in the property binder and issue immediate action for any failed required alarm or blocked exit.

Common Life-Safety Inspection

Review every device and route using the same checks regardless of which segment of the property it came from.

  • Identifier check: confirm every device and exit route has a unique identifier tied to a specific location.
  • Test-method check: confirm every alarm was tested only with its built-in control and manufacturer procedure.
  • Result check: confirm every test result is recorded as pass, fail, or fault, with no device left untested.
  • Battery check: confirm the correct manufacturer-specified battery type was installed and dated where applicable.
  • Replacement check: confirm every failed, faulted, or end-of-life device is flagged for prompt replacement rather than reset and left in service.
  • Exit-route check: confirm every primary and secondary route was walked and either confirmed clear or reported as an immediate finding.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Alarm Was Tested With an Improvised Source

Probable cause: the inspector used smoke, flame, or vehicle exhaust instead of the built-in test control.
Confirmation: compare the recorded test method against the manufacturer instructions for that device.
Correction: retest the device using only its built-in test control, and record the corrected test method.
Reinspection: review the full test record for the same improvised-method entry before filing it.

A Failed Alarm Was Reset Instead of Flagged for Replacement

Probable cause: the inspector replaced the battery and moved on without confirming a passing retest, or ignored a fault indication.
Confirmation: retest the specific device and check for chirping, fault lights, or failure to sound after battery service.
Correction: reclassify the device as a replacement item, arrange a compatible replacement alarm, and record the deficiency rather than marking the device as passed.
Reinspection: confirm every device on the record shows either a clear passing test or an open replacement action, with no ambiguous entries.

An Alarm Was Silenced Without Authorized Replacement

Probable cause: a nuisance alarm was disconnected, covered, or had its battery removed to stop it from sounding.
Confirmation: check every device location for a missing battery, a covered sensor, or a disconnected power source.
Correction: restore the device to service immediately if safe to do so, or arrange prompt authorized replacement, and document the interim condition as an immediate finding.
Reinspection: confirm the device is retested and passes before the project is closed out.

An Exit Route Obstruction Was Recorded as Routine Maintenance

Probable cause: stored furniture, security bars, or damaged hardware blocking an exit was logged with the same priority as a cosmetic finding.
Confirmation: review the priority assigned to every exit-route finding against the Critical Safety Cue in this chapter.
Correction: reclassify the obstruction as an immediate finding and notify the responsible party before the project continues.
Reinspection: confirm every exit route is either clear or listed as an immediate finding, never as an unflagged routine item.

The Monitoring Service Was Not Notified Before Testing

Probable cause: testing began before confirming whether a device was connected to a monitored security or fire system.
Confirmation: check whether any tested device is listed as monitored or interconnected in the property records.
Correction: notify the monitoring service immediately, confirm no false dispatch occurred, and document the notification gap on the worksheet.
Reinspection: confirm every remaining monitored device is notified before testing and that monitoring status is restored at closeout.

Field Terms

Built-In Test Control
The manufacturer-provided test button or control used to verify alarm operation; the only approved test method in this project.
Interconnected Activation
Confirmation that one alarm's activation triggers connected alarms elsewhere in the property, verified only through the approved system procedure.
Service Life
The manufacturer-documented usable life of an alarm, determined from the product label or instructions.
Fault Indication
A chirp, flashing fault light, or displayed error indicating a device requires service, distinct from a routine low-battery signal.
End-of-Life Indication
A manufacturer-defined signal or elapsed service period indicating a device must be replaced rather than serviced.
Device Identifier
A unique code tied to a specific alarm, extinguisher, or emergency device and its location.
Life-Safety Readiness Record
The single dated document recording every alarm test, battery service, emergency-equipment inspection, and exit-route finding from this project.
Primary Exit Route
The main path from a sleeping or occupied area to a safe exterior location.
Secondary Exit Route
An alternate escape path used when the primary route is blocked.
Immediate Finding
A failed required alarm, missing required alarm, or blocked exit route that must be reported to the responsible party before routine testing continues.

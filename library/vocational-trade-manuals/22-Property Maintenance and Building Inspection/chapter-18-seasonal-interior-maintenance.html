Job Overview

This project qualifies you to complete one coordinated seasonal interior maintenance cycle for an accessible residence. The finished work product is a single dated seasonal maintenance record that documents filter replacement, life-safety device testing, plumbing leak checks at every water-using fixture, interior humidity observations, opening and equipment inspection, and an updated recurring maintenance calendar.

You will identify the seasonal interior risks that apply to the property, replace approved filters, retest smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms using their manufacturer procedures, inspect under every accessible sink and water-using appliance for moisture, take representative interior humidity readings, inspect windows and doors for seasonal condensation and drafts, check exhaust fans and seasonal equipment, and record every result before restoring the property to normal use.

This project builds directly on Chapter 2 (the property maintenance log), Chapter 10 and 11 (plumbing fixture inspection and basic maintenance), Chapter 12 (electrical device inspection), Chapter 13 (alarm testing), Chapter 14 (HVAC filters), and Chapter 17 (seasonal exterior maintenance). It feeds directly into Chapter 19, where this cycle's findings are folded into the property's prioritized repair plan, and into Chapter 20, the capstone inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • MI-103 — Replace approved HVAC, air-cleaner, range-hood, appliance, or water filters with the correct product and installation procedure.
  • MI-104 — Test smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms and other life-safety devices using manufacturer test procedures.
  • MI-105 — Inspect under every accessible sink, appliance, and water-using fixture for leakage, corrosion, and moisture evidence.
  • MI-106 — Check interior relative humidity at representative locations using a consistent method.
  • MI-107 — Inspect door and window seals for seasonal condensation, frost, drafts, and weatherstripping condition.
  • MI-108 — Update the property maintenance records and recurring seasonal calendar with every test, reading, and finding from this cycle.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 17 addressed the exterior side of seasonal preparation: debris, drainage, hose bibbs, and exterior sealant. This chapter addresses the interior side of the same seasonal cycle. Together, Chapters 17 and 18 close out one full seasonal maintenance pass on the property before the next season begins.

This chapter reuses the alarm-testing procedure from Chapter 13, the plumbing-fixture inspection method from Chapter 10, the filter-replacement procedure from Chapter 14, and the window and door inspection method from Chapter 7. It does not reteach those procedures from scratch; it applies them on a recurring seasonal schedule and requires the results to be logged in the Chapter 2 property binder. Chapter 19 will use this seasonal record, along with every other chapter's findings, to build the property's 30-60-90 day and long-term maintenance plan.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying seasonal interior risks specific to the property and the coming season
  • replacing approved HVAC, air-cleaner, range-hood, appliance, and water filters with the correct product and procedure
  • testing smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms using their built-in test controls and manufacturer procedures, and replacing specified batteries
  • inspecting under every accessible sink, vanity, and water-using appliance connection for leakage, corrosion, and moisture evidence
  • checking representative interior relative humidity using a consistent method
  • inspecting windows and exterior doors for seasonal condensation, frost, drafts, weatherstripping, and minor approved adjustment
  • inspecting exhaust fans, ceiling fans, portable heaters, humidifiers, dehumidifiers, and other seasonal equipment for safe operation
  • updating the property maintenance log and seasonal calendar with every test, filter, reading, finding, and referral from this cycle

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • diagnose indoor-air-quality or mold conditions from a single humidity reading
  • operate damaged, recalled, or unsafe portable heating, humidifying, or dehumidifying equipment
  • perform internal HVAC service, combustion analysis, refrigerant work, gas work, or electrical repair
  • silence, disconnect, relocate, or paint over a required smoke or carbon-monoxide alarm without approved replacement and authorization

Any immediate hazard encountered during this cycle must stop the routine seasonal maintenance and be handled per this chapter's Critical Safety Cue before work continues.

Trade Note

Seasonal maintenance is a recurring inspection, not a one-time event. The value of this chapter's record comes from comparing this cycle's readings and findings against the prior seasonal cycle logged in the Chapter 2 binder. A single humidity reading of 55% means little on its own; a pattern of readings rising each winter at the same location is the kind of evidence that supports a maintenance decision.

Setup Standard

Do not begin this seasonal cycle until occupant access, utility operation, monitoring-service notifications, pets, children, and vulnerable occupants have been coordinated (Step 1). Interconnected or monitored alarms must not be tested until the monitoring service has been notified.

Shop Tip

Complete the filter and alarm tasks first, then move to the wet-area inspection, then to openings and equipment. Working in that order keeps dry hands on batteries and filters and keeps wet-area findings grouped together for the record.

Comparing Cycles, Not Just Recording One

Every reading and finding in this chapter is recorded against the prior seasonal cycle in the Chapter 2 binder, not in isolation. A humidity reading, a filter condition, or a window condensation note is most useful when it is compared with the same location's reading from the previous cycle. Do not interpret a single cycle's reading as a diagnosis of indoor air quality or mold; record it as an observation and compare trends over time.

Documentation Cue — Log Every Reading Against the Prior Cycle

Record each humidity reading, filter condition, and wet-area finding with its location and date, and compare it against the same location's entry from the previous seasonal cycle in the property binder. Do not discard the prior cycle's entries.

Critical Safety Cue — Immediate Hazards Stop the Routine Cycle

If you encounter active leakage, a failed or discharged alarm on a required device, fuel-gas odor, burning odor, exposed energized wiring, or damaged equipment with heat or water exposure, stop the routine seasonal maintenance immediately. Secure or isolate the area if safe to do so, and notify the owner, monitoring service, or emergency services as the situation requires.

Field Safety Cue — Occupants, Storage, and Access

Coordinate access, pets, children, and vulnerable occupants before beginning. Remove obstructing stored items carefully when accessing under-sink and utility areas, and restore them only after the area is dry and the finding is accepted.

Code Alert — Alarm and Monitoring Coordination

Notify occupants and monitoring services as required before testing interconnected or monitored smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms. Do not disconnect, silence, or bypass a required device to avoid this coordination step.

E. Preparation — Coordination, Records, and Staging

  1. Identify seasonal interior risks for the property. Record heating startup, cooling startup, dry air, high humidity, freezing, storms, holiday loads, or extended vacancy as applicable to the coming season.
  2. Review open interior findings and prior records. Check open interior findings, equipment manuals, prior maintenance dates, alarm records, the filter schedule, and occupant concerns in the Chapter 2 binder.
  3. Coordinate access and notifications. Confirm access, utility operation, monitoring-service notifications, pets, children, and vulnerable-occupant arrangements before beginning.
  4. Prepare supplies and instruments. Stage replacement filters, specified batteries, cleaning tools, leak-response supplies, a hygrometer or approved humidity instrument, labels, and forms.

F. Filter Replacement and Life-Safety Testing

  1. Inspect and replace approved filters. Service HVAC, air-cleaner, range-hood, appliance, or water filters only with the correct product and procedure identified in the equipment inventory.
  2. Record filter details. Record size, type, airflow direction, installation date, condition observed, and disposal method for each serviced filter.
  3. Test smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms. Use the Chapter 13 test controls and manufacturer procedures for every accessible alarm.
  4. Replace specified batteries. Install only the specified battery type and arrange replacement of any failed or end-of-life device.

G. Emergency Equipment and Wet-Area Inspection

  1. Inspect fire extinguishers and emergency equipment. Check emergency lighting, flashlights, first-aid supplies, and exit routes within the approved scope.
  2. Inspect under every accessible sink and connection. Check every sink, vanity, toilet connection, and water-using fixture connection for moisture, corrosion, and staining.
  3. Restore stored items carefully. Remove obstructing stored items to access the area, and restore them only after the area is dry and accepted.
  4. Operate approved fixtures and inspect connections. Check supply, drain, shutoff, trap, and cabinet-base conditions at each operated fixture.
  5. Inspect appliance and utility-room areas. Check washer hoses, the dishwasher area, the refrigerator line, the water-heater area, the sump area, and the utility-room floor for leakage indicators.

H. Humidity, Openings, and Seasonal Equipment

  1. Check interior relative humidity. Use a consistent method at representative locations and compare readings with outdoor conditions, season, equipment operation, condensation, and occupant activities.
  2. Inspect windows and exterior doors. Check for condensation, frost, drafts, failed weatherstripping, locks, and seasonal movement.
  3. Clean tracks, sills, and weeps. Use non-damaging methods without blocking drainage.
  4. Complete minor approved adjustment. Adjust weatherstripping within the approved minor-maintenance scope, or refer broader frame and water issues per Chapter 7.
  5. Inspect exhaust fans. Check bath, kitchen, laundry, and other exhaust fans for normal operation from user controls, and verify exhaust outlets are not blocked where safely observable.
  6. Inspect ceiling fans and seasonal equipment. Check ceiling fans, portable heaters, humidifiers, dehumidifiers, and seasonal equipment for cords, clearance, cleanliness, leakage, and safe placement.

I. Closeout and Recordkeeping

  1. Inspect interior circulation paths. Check stairs, handrails, rugs, mats, night lighting, and circulation paths for seasonal trip and fall hazards.
  2. Prepare vacancy or severe-weather procedures. Set up freeze, storm, or heat procedures when the property will be unattended.
  3. Restore the property. Restore moved contents, alarm monitoring, equipment settings, filters, covers, and access.
  4. Clean the work areas. Verify no water, batteries, filters, tools, or debris remain.
  5. Complete the Seasonal Maintenance Record. Record every test, filter, battery, humidity observation, leak check, opening condition, equipment concern, and referral.
  6. Update the maintenance calendar. Compare current findings with the prior seasonal cycle and file the completed record in the property binder.

Common Seasonal Inspection

Review every finding using the same checks regardless of which segment of the cycle it came from.

  • Filter check: confirm every serviced filter matches the approved size, type, and airflow direction, with the installation date recorded.
  • Alarm check: confirm every accessible alarm was tested with its built-in control and manufacturer procedure, with results recorded.
  • Wet-area check: confirm every accessible sink, appliance connection, and water-using fixture was inspected and any finding recorded with location and priority.
  • Humidity check: confirm readings were taken at the same representative locations used in the prior cycle.
  • Opening check: confirm every accessible window and exterior door was checked for condensation, frost, drafts, and weatherstripping condition.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Humidity Reading Is Recorded Without Comparison

Probable cause: the reading was taken and filed without checking the prior seasonal cycle's entry for the same location.
Confirmation: retrieve the prior cycle's Seasonal Maintenance Record and compare the same location's reading.
Correction: record both readings side by side and note the direction and magnitude of any change; do not state a mold or air-quality conclusion from either reading alone.
Reinspection: confirm every humidity entry in the current record references the matching prior-cycle location and date.

An Alarm Was Tested Without Required Coordination

Probable cause: an interconnected or monitored alarm was tested before occupants or the monitoring service were notified.
Confirmation: review the notification log against the alarm test record for the affected devices.
Correction: notify the monitoring service, confirm the alarm is restored to monitored status, and retest if required by the service provider.
Reinspection: confirm every interconnected or monitored alarm shows a matching notification entry before it is marked tested.

A Filter Was Installed With the Wrong Airflow Direction

Probable cause: the airflow-direction arrow on the replacement filter was not checked against the equipment's airflow path before installation.
Confirmation: inspect the installed filter's arrow against the known airflow direction at the equipment.
Correction: remove and reinstall the filter in the correct orientation.
Reinspection: confirm the arrow direction on every serviced filter before closing the access cover.

A Wet-Area Finding Was Left Undocumented

Probable cause: a stain, corrosion mark, or drip was noticed but not recorded because the fixture was not actively leaking at the moment of inspection.
Confirmation: review the wet-area checklist against every accessible sink, appliance connection, and utility-room floor for a corresponding entry.
Correction: record the observed condition, its location, and its priority category, and schedule a follow-up observation period.
Reinspection: confirm every wet-area location has either a recorded finding or a recorded "no evidence observed" entry.

Seasonal Equipment Was Returned to Service Without Inspection

Probable cause: a portable heater, humidifier, or dehumidifier was placed back into use from storage without checking its cord, clearance, and prior damage history.
Confirmation: inspect the equipment's cord, housing, clearance, and any owner-reported issues from the prior cycle.
Correction: do not operate equipment with a damaged cord, heat evidence, water exposure, or a known recall; remove it from service and record the referral.
Reinspection: confirm every piece of seasonal equipment returned to service has a recorded inspection entry.

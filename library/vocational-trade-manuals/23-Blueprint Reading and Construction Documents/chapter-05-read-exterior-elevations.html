Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to interpret every exterior elevation in a residential drawing package and convert that information into one coordinated elevation checklist covering vertical controls, roof geometry, openings, materials, grade relationships, penetrations, drainage, and detail references. The finished work product is one checklist section for each exterior elevation in the current approved set, built from the documents received and cross-checked against the site plan, floor plans, sections, structural plans, and finish schedules.

You will confirm each elevation sheet's identity and revision, verify elevation names against actual orientation, trace vertical dimension chains from grade to ridge, identify roof forms and match them to the roof plan and sections, locate and match every opening to its schedule, follow material keynotes to their governing legend or specification, compare grade lines against the site and foundation plans, and record every penetration, drainage feature, and unresolved conflict as it is found.

This chapter builds directly on the navigation package produced in Chapter 1 and the site-control sheet produced in Chapter 2. Exterior elevations cannot be correctly interpreted without knowing which sheets are current and how the building sits on its lot; the elevation checklist produced here is then used to verify heights, roof geometry, and grade relationships in Chapter 6 and later chapters.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-025 — Identify elevation views by orientation, name, and relationship to the site and floor plans.
  • BP-026 — Read exterior heights, including vertical datums, floor elevations, plate heights, and ridge heights.
  • BP-027 — Identify roof forms and match roof slopes and geometry to the roof plan and building sections.
  • BP-028 — Locate siding, trim, and other exterior material callouts and trace them to legends, schedules, and specifications.
  • BP-029 — Interpret grade relationships, including finished grade, existing grade, and foundation exposure, against the site and foundation plans.
  • BP-030 — Create an exterior-elevation checklist covering heights, openings, materials, grade, roof geometry, penetrations, and detail references for every elevation.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 produced a room-by-room floor-plan layout. Exterior elevations are compared directly against that floor plan to confirm that wall lengths and opening locations are coordinated. Chapter 6 uses the elevation checklist produced here to trace building and wall sections from foundation through roof, and Chapter 7 uses the roof geometry identified here to interpret structural roof framing. Chapter 14 and Chapter 15 return to the roof and envelope information gathered here in far greater detail.

The habit built in this chapter — treating an elevation as one coordinated view among several rather than an isolated picture — is used without exception in every remaining chapter that touches the building exterior.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming each elevation sheet's identity, revision, scale, and orientation
  • locating vertical datums and tracing the dimension chain from grade to ridge
  • identifying roof forms and matching them to the roof plan and sections
  • locating and matching every opening to plans and schedules
  • identifying siding, trim, and material callouts and tracing them to legends and specifications
  • comparing grade relationships with the site and foundation plans
  • locating penetrations, drainage features, and accessories
  • producing one checklist section per elevation with complete detail references

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • select, substitute, or resize an exterior material, roof form, or opening not shown or scheduled in the current approved documents
  • derive an exterior height, roof slope, or grade relationship from a reproduced or displayed elevation without verified scale
  • treat a typical elevation detail as governing a condition the documents identify as revised or site-specific
  • resolve a conflict between an elevation and a related plan, section, or schedule by choosing the answer that looks correct

Any elevation condition, material tag, or dimension that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the elevation checklist's unresolved-items column rather than assumed.

Trade Note

An elevation shows a building the way it will appear from one direction. It does not, by itself, prove how that building is framed, how it is finished on the inside, or how it sits relative to the lot. This chapter treats the elevation as one coordinated view that must be checked against the site plan, floor plan, sections, and schedules before any of its information is trusted for field use.

Document-control discipline for this chapter: confirm each elevation sheet's revision before recording any height, material, or geometry from it, and never combine a current elevation with a superseded plan, section, or schedule.

Setup Standard

Do not begin recording heights, materials, or roof geometry (Section F) until every elevation sheet's identity, revision, and orientation have been confirmed against the Chapter 1 navigation package in Steps 1 through 3. An elevation checklist built from an unconfirmed sheet must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Confirm that an elevation labeled "Front" or "North" actually corresponds to the stated orientation on the site plan before using it. Elevation names sometimes follow project convention rather than true compass direction, and using the wrong assumption can misplace an entire coordination effort.

Reference Conventions

Establish these definitions before beginning the elevation checklist, and apply them consistently across every elevation in the package.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every elevation checklist section in this chapter is built the same way regardless of which face of the building it represents: confirm sheet identity, trace the vertical dimension chain, identify roof geometry, match openings, trace materials, compare grade, and record penetrations and drainage. Learning that sequence here, applied consistently across every elevation, means the checklist can be trusted for coordination in every later chapter.

Reading an Elevation Versus Reading a Plan

A floor plan shows horizontal position. An elevation shows vertical position and appearance. Neither view alone confirms a wall length, an opening location, and a height at the same time. When an elevation and a floor plan disagree on an opening's horizontal position, or an elevation and a section disagree on a height, record the conflict as a coordination item rather than assuming either view is correct.

Scale Warning — Verify Reproduction Size Before Scaling a Height or Material Extent

A height, roof pitch triangle, or material band on an elevation is only reliable at the sheet's verified reproduction size. Do not scale a plate height, roof overhang, or grade line from a photocopied, resized, scanned, or arbitrarily zoomed elevation. Check the graphic scale against a known dimension on the same sheet, and only where the project procedure authorizes scaling at all; otherwise rely on the written dimension or elevation value.

Critical Cue — Do Not Assume Grade From the Elevation Alone

An elevation's depicted grade line is a graphic representation, not a surveyed value. Finished-floor elevations, top-of-foundation elevations, and exterior grade relationships shown on the elevation must be checked against the site plan and foundation plan before being treated as reliable. A misread grade relationship can misplace an entire foundation exposure or drainage condition.

Cross-Reference Cue — Match Roof Geometry to the Roof Plan and Sections

A roof form drawn on an elevation must match the same form on the roof plan and be consistent with the ridge, eave, and slope information shown in the building sections. When an elevation appears to show a roof form the roof plan does not confirm, follow the reference back to its source and record the discrepancy rather than resolving it by preference.

RFI Threshold — Inconsistent Materials, Openings, or Roof Geometry

Record any inconsistent opening position, missing height, unmatched material tag, unclear grade relationship, or unresolved roof geometry as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not select the elevation, plan, or section that seems more likely to be correct; document the conflict and obtain clarification through the approved process.

E. Preparation — Confirm Elevation Identity and Coordination Sources

  1. Confirm each elevation sheet, revision, scale, direction, orientation, and relationship to the site and floor plans. Cross-check every elevation sheet number and revision against the Chapter 1 navigation package before recording any content.
  2. Identify front, rear, left, right, courtyard, garage, and partial elevations. List every elevation received and its stated view name in the elevation checklist.
  3. Verify elevation names correspond to actual orientation or project naming. Compare each elevation's stated direction against the north arrow and building orientation on the site plan.

F. Vertical Controls and Heights

  1. Locate vertical datums, finished-floor elevations, top-of-foundation, plate heights, ceiling heights, ridge heights, eave heights, and grade elevations. Record each value found on the elevation in the vertical dimension-chain worksheet.
  2. Distinguish absolute elevations from dimensions relative to a project datum. Label each recorded value as absolute or relative in the worksheet.
  3. Trace the vertical dimension chain from grade or foundation to floors, plates, roofs, and ridges. Confirm each successive value connects to the one before it without a gap.

G. Roof Geometry

  1. Identify roof forms, slopes, ridges, hips, valleys, gables, sheds, overhangs, dormers, and parapets. Record each roof feature shown on the elevation in the checklist's roof-geometry field.
  2. Match roof slopes and forms to the roof plan and building sections. Confirm every roof feature identified on the elevation appears consistently on the roof plan and in the sections.

H. Openings

  1. Locate doors, windows, louvers, vents, garage doors, service doors, and other openings. Record each opening tag and its approximate elevation position in the opening cross-check log.
  2. Match opening tags and sizes to plans and schedules. Confirm each tag appears on the floor plan and in the opening schedule with a consistent size and type.
  3. Check head heights, sill heights, alignments, grouping, and relationships to trim and exterior materials. Record any dimensioned head or sill height in the vertical dimension-chain worksheet.

I. Materials and Finishes

  1. Identify siding, veneer, panel, stucco, masonry, roofing, soffit, fascia, trim, and foundation finish callouts. Record each material tag found on the elevation in the material keynote trace list.
  2. Follow keynotes and material tags to legends, schedules, details, and specifications. Confirm every keynote reaches a governing legend, schedule, or specification section.
  3. Identify material transitions, horizontal bands, water tables, belt courses, control joints, and termination points. Record each transition location and its governing detail reference.

J. Exterior Features and Grade

  1. Locate exterior stairs, landings, decks, porches, railings, guards, handrails, ramps, and retaining walls. Record each feature and its governing detail reference in the checklist.
  2. Identify grade lines, finished grade, existing grade, sloped grade, window wells, areaways, and foundation exposure. Record each grade element depicted on the elevation.
  3. Compare grade relationships with the site and foundation plans. Record the match status for each grade element in the grade-comparison record.

K. Penetrations and Drainage

  1. Locate chimneys, vents, exhausts, meters, lights, receptacles, hose bibbs, downspouts, scuppers, and other penetrations or accessories. Record each item and its approximate elevation location.
  2. Identify roof and wall drainage paths shown or implied by gutters, downspouts, crickets, saddles, and flashing references. Record the drainage path and its governing detail reference.

L. Detail References and Cross-Checks

  1. Locate section and detail callouts for eaves, rakes, openings, decks, porches, material transitions, and foundations. Record each callout and its destination sheet.
  2. Follow representative references and verify they apply to the elevation condition. Confirm at least one traced reference per elevation reaches a destination that matches the condition shown.
  3. Compare wall lengths and opening locations with floor plans. Confirm elevation opening spacing is consistent with the corresponding floor-plan wall length.
  4. Compare heights and roof geometry with sections and structural plans. Confirm plate and ridge heights recorded in Section F match the building sections.
  5. Compare material tags with finish schedules and specifications. Confirm every material recorded in Section I matches its finish schedule or specification entry.
  6. Identify inconsistent opening positions, missing heights, unmatched materials, unclear grade, or unresolved roof geometry. Log each discrepancy found in Steps 20 through 24 on the unresolved-items list.

M. Field Verification and Checklist Assembly

  1. Mark field-verification points for existing grade, existing structures, utility penetrations, and renovation interfaces. Flag each item requiring field confirmation before it is treated as resolved.
  2. Create one checklist section for each elevation with heights, openings, materials, grade, roof, penetrations, drainage, and detail references. Consolidate the findings of Sections F through L into one section per elevation.
  3. Attach photographs or visual extracts only as authorized and label them with source sheet and revision. Confirm any attached image carries its source sheet number and current revision.

N. Checklist Closeout

  1. Prepare RFI-style questions for conflicting elevations, roof forms, material limits, grade relationships, or missing details. Draft one neutral question per unresolved item on the list.
  2. Review the checklist for all elevations, current revisions, complete references, and field-verification notes. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete before closing the chapter.

Common Navigation Inspection

Inspect the finished checklist using the same checks regardless of which elevation is involved.

  • Coverage check: confirm every received elevation, including partial elevations, has its own checklist section.
  • Vertical-chain check: confirm every checklist section traces an unbroken chain from grade to ridge.
  • Coordination check: confirm every opening, roof form, and material tag has been matched to its plan, schedule, section, or specification, or logged as unmatched.
  • Grade check: confirm every elevation's grade depiction has been compared against the site and foundation plans.
  • Reference check: confirm every traced detail reference reached a real destination or was logged as broken.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Vertical Dimension Chain Does Not Close From Grade to Ridge

Probable cause: one elevation value was read from a superseded sheet, or a relative dimension was mistaken for an absolute elevation.
Confirmation: re-trace every value in the chain against its source sheet and confirm each sheet's current revision in the Chapter 1 navigation package.
Correction: rebuild the affected chain link using only current, correctly labeled absolute or relative values.
Reinspection: repeat the vertical-chain check on the corrected worksheet.

An Elevation Roof Form Does Not Match the Roof Plan

Probable cause: the elevation and roof plan are from different revisions, or the elevation shows a conceptual roof form later revised on the roof plan.
Confirmation: compare the revision block of both sheets and confirm both are current.
Correction: if both sheets are current and still disagree, log the discrepancy on the unresolved-items list and draft an RFI-style question rather than choosing one view as correct.
Reinspection: repeat the roof-geometry match check once a response is received and incorporated.

An Opening Shown on the Elevation Cannot Be Found on the Floor Plan

Probable cause: the opening tag was omitted from the floor plan, or the elevation and plan use different tagging conventions.
Confirmation: check the opening schedule for a matching tag and confirm the floor plan's opening symbols against the project legend.
Correction: log the unmatched opening on the unresolved-items list and draft an RFI-style question identifying the exact elevation location and missing plan reference.
Reinspection: repeat the opening cross-check once a response is received.

A Material Keynote Does Not Reach a Legend or Specification Entry

Probable cause: the keynote references a legend not included in the received package, or the material was not carried into the specification section.
Confirmation: check every legend and specification section in the package for the keynote's definition.
Correction: record the undefined keynote on the unresolved-items list; do not assign a material based on similar-looking hatching or general trade familiarity.
Reinspection: repeat the material trace once a definition is received.

An Elevation Grade Line Does Not Match the Site or Foundation Plan

Probable cause: the elevation shows a conceptual or pre-grading condition, or the site plan has been revised after the elevation was last issued.
Confirmation: compare the revision dates of the elevation, site plan, and foundation plan.
Correction: record the conflicting grade relationship on the unresolved-items list and draft an RFI-style question rather than assuming the more recent sheet is automatically correct.
Reinspection: repeat the grade-comparison check once a response is received and incorporated.

Field Terms

Vertical Datum
The project's stated zero-point or reference elevation for measuring heights.
Absolute Elevation
A height stated as an actual project elevation value.
Relative Dimension
A height or distance measured from one building element to another.
Finished Grade
The proposed final ground elevation adjacent to the building after grading.
Existing Grade
The ground elevation present before construction.
Roof Form
The named geometric type of a roof or roof segment, such as gable, hip, or shed.
Material Keynote
A tag on an elevation directing the reader to a governing legend, schedule, or specification.
Plate Height
The elevation at the top of a wall's top plate, used to control door, window, and ceiling relationships.
Eave Height
The elevation at the low edge of a sloped roof where it meets the exterior wall.
Foundation Exposure
The visible height of foundation wall between finished grade and the floor line above.

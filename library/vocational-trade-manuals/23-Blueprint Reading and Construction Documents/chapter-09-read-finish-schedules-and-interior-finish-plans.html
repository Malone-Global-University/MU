Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to receive a residential finish package and convert it into a controlled room-by-room finish matrix before any material is ordered or installed. The finished work product is one room finish matrix covering floor, base, wall, ceiling, trim, casework, countertop, and special-finish information, one marked finish plan showing pattern origins and high-risk coordination zones, and one set of RFI-style questions for every unresolved item.

You will match room names and numbers to the architectural floor plan, match every finish code to the legend or approved selection, coordinate flooring patterns and transitions, identify base, wall, and ceiling finishes by location, compare ceiling finishes with the reflected ceiling plan, identify wet-area waterproofing requirements, and coordinate the finish package with cabinet drawings, opening schedules, and interior elevations.

This chapter depends on the navigation package built in Chapter 1 and the room-by-room floor plan reading completed in Chapter 4. The finish matrix produced here is used directly by the cabinet and millwork interpretation in Chapter 10 and by the trade-scope extraction in Chapter 19.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-049 — Identify finish symbols, codes, abbreviations, and keynote references used across the finish package.
  • BP-050 — Read a room finish schedule and match every entry to the architectural floor plan.
  • BP-051 — Interpret flooring callouts, including material, pattern, transition, and termination information.
  • BP-052 — Identify wall and ceiling finishes by room, elevation, height, and material transition.
  • BP-053 — Locate trim, base, and molding requirements and match them to the correct rooms and transitions.
  • BP-054 — Create a finish-scope matrix coordinating rooms, surfaces, materials, substrates, transitions, and unresolved items.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 4 identified rooms, walls, openings, and fixed equipment from the floor plan. This chapter returns to those same rooms and adds the surface-finish information the floor plan alone does not carry. Chapter 10 depends directly on this finish matrix to coordinate cabinet toe kicks, backsplashes, and countertop-to-wall relationships. Chapter 17 depends on this matrix to compare finish thicknesses against door clearances, fixtures, and floor-elevation changes identified in other disciplines.

The habit of matching every finish code to a legend entry, rather than guessing from familiarity with common products, carries forward into every remaining interior chapter of this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the current room-finish schedule, finish plan, reflected ceiling plan, interior elevations, finish legends, and approved finish selections
  • matching room names and numbers to the architectural floor plan and recording discrepancies
  • matching every finish code to the finish legend, specification, or approved selection
  • identifying flooring material, pattern, layout origin, transitions, and termination conditions
  • identifying base, wall, and ceiling finishes by room, elevation, height, and transition
  • identifying wet-area waterproofing, underlayment, and substrate references
  • producing a finish-scope matrix and a marked finish plan with unresolved items and RFI-style questions

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • select a finish, color, pattern, or product that is not shown on the approved finish schedule, legend, or selection sheet
  • treat tile, grout, sealant, or paint as a complete waterproofing system without confirming the documented substrate and membrane requirements
  • derive finish thicknesses, transition heights, or clearances mathematically without checking door clearances, cabinet drawings, and accessibility requirements
  • resolve a conflicting finish code, undefined limit, or incomplete transition on their own instead of recording it as a clarification item

Any finish code, room, or transition that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the missing-information and RFI-style question list rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A finish schedule and a finish plan are two separate documents that must agree. The schedule states what each room receives; the plan shows where patterns start, where materials change, and where transitions occur. This chapter treats them as two facts that must be checked against each other, not one fact assumed from the other.

Document-control discipline for this chapter: confirm the finish schedule and finish plan are the current approved revision before matching any code, and never carry forward a superseded finish selection into the matrix.

Setup Standard

Do not begin building the room matrix (Step 6) until room names and numbers have been confirmed against the architectural floor plan and every discrepancy has been recorded in Steps 2 and 3. A matrix built on unverified room identity must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Read the finish legend before reading the schedule. A finish code is only as reliable as the legend entry that defines it, and the same code can be reused across projects with a different meaning each time.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every finish package carries floor, base, wall, ceiling, trim, and special-finish information that must be traced back to the same room identity used in the architectural floor plan. Learning to check that identity first, before matching a single finish code, prevents an entire room's finish scope from being built against the wrong space.

Reading a Finish Schedule Versus Reading a Finish Plan

A finish schedule states what each room receives by code. A finish plan shows where each finish begins, ends, and transitions within the room. When the schedule lists a finish for a room but the plan shows no corresponding area, or the plan shows a material boundary the schedule does not address, record the conflict as a missing-information item rather than assuming one document is correct.

Scale Warning — Do Not Scale Pattern Layouts

Flooring pattern origins, borders, and insets are frequently shown at a reduced or diagrammatic scale on the finish plan. Do not scale a layout origin, border width, or inset dimension from the printed sheet unless the reproduction size and graphic scale have been verified under the Chapter 1 navigation procedure and the project procedure authorizes scaling.

Critical Cue — Do Not Assume Tile or Paint Is the Complete Waterproofing System

A wet-area finish call-out is not, by itself, a complete waterproofing specification. Confirm the documented substrate, membrane, backer board, and moisture-testing requirements for every wet-area room before treating the finish as ready for installation. If the documents do not state the waterproofing system, record the omission as a clarification item.

Cross-Reference Cue — Follow Every Finish Code to Its Legend Entry

Every finish code must be traced to a legend entry, specification section, or approved selection before it is entered on the matrix. A code with no matching legend entry is not a minor omission; record it as a missing-information item and do not guess the material from its abbreviation.

RFI Threshold — Conflicting Codes and Incomplete Transitions

Record any conflicting finish code, undefined room limit, incomplete transition detail, or missing wet-area system as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not select a substitute finish or invent a transition detail to fill the gap.

E. Preparation — Confirm Identity and Package Status

  1. Confirm the current room-finish schedule, finish plan, reflected ceiling plan, interior elevations, finish legends, specifications, and approved finish selections. Verify each document's revision against the Chapter 1 document-control register before use.
  2. Identify room numbers and names and compare them with the architectural floor plans. Work down the finish schedule line by line against the Chapter 4 room-by-room layout summary.
  3. Record discrepancies in room naming, numbering, subdivisions, or omitted spaces. Log each discrepancy on the missing-information and RFI-style question list with the exact room identifier involved.
  4. Identify finish codes, material abbreviations, pattern symbols, hatch conventions, and keynote references. List every distinct code found across the schedule and finish plan.
  5. Match every code to the finish legend, specification, or approved selection. Confirm each code resolves to a defined material, product, color, and pattern before proceeding.

F. Room Matrix Construction

  1. Create a room matrix with floor, base, wall, ceiling, trim, casework, countertop, and special-finish columns. Populate one row per room in the document-control log.
  2. Record substrate and preparation requirements where documented. Enter substrate information only where the schedule, plan, or specification states it.
  3. Identify flooring material, orientation, pattern, layout origin, border, inset, transition, movement, and termination information. Record each item from the finish plan for every room with a documented flooring pattern.
  4. Locate floor-finish changes at doors, cased openings, stairs, cabinets, islands, fixtures, and changes in level. Mark each transition location on the marked finish plan.
  5. Identify base type, height, material, finish, corners, returns, and transition conditions. Record base information by room and by wall where it varies within a room.
  6. Identify wall finishes by wall, elevation, height, wainscot, accent area, wet area, backsplash, and material transition. Cross-reference interior elevations for every room with more than one wall finish.
  7. Identify ceiling finishes, heights, soffits, coffers, clouds, bulkheads, access panels, and exposed areas. Record ceiling height and finish by room.
  8. Compare ceiling finishes with reflected ceiling plans, lighting, registers, sprinklers, alarms, and access requirements. Confirm every ceiling feature shown on the reflected ceiling plan is accounted for in the matrix.

G. Finish Material and System Review

  1. Identify paint systems, sheen, color, primer, coating, stain, clear finish, and surface-preparation references. Record each by wall or room where the schedule distinguishes them.
  2. Distinguish field-applied finishes from factory finishes. Note this distinction in the matrix for every applicable surface.
  3. Identify tile, stone, panel, wallcovering, wood, resilient, carpet, concrete, and specialty finish requirements. Record the specific material category for each finish code used.
  4. Locate trim, casing, baseboard, crown, chair rail, panel molding, jamb extensions, sills, aprons, and specialty millwork. Record trim type and profile reference by room.
  5. Identify transition strips, reducers, thresholds, edge trims, movement joints, and sealant joints. Add each to the transition entries recorded in Section F.
  6. Compare finish thicknesses with door clearances, cabinets, stairs, floor elevations, drains, fixtures, and accessibility requirements. Flag any room where a finish thickness has not been confirmed against these conditions.
  7. Identify wet-area waterproofing, underlayment, backer, moisture testing, and substrate references. Record these on the wet-area reference list for every room identified as a wet area.
  8. Do not assume that tile, grout, sealant, or paint is the complete waterproofing system. Confirm the documented membrane or substrate system exists before marking a wet-area room resolved.

H. Coordination and Sequencing Review

  1. Identify finish samples, mockups, attic stock, maintenance products, and owner-approval requirements. Record these requirements against the affected rooms.
  2. Identify sequencing constraints such as substrate acceptance, moisture testing, casework installation, finish protection, and cure. Note any sequencing dependency that affects more than one room or trade.
  3. Compare finish information with cabinet drawings, opening schedules, interior elevations, and specifications. Confirm consistency between the finish matrix and these related documents for every affected room.
  4. Identify missing codes, unmatched finishes, conflicting colors, incomplete transitions, and unclear limits. Log each on the missing-information and RFI-style question list.
  5. Mark rooms requiring field measurements, owner selections, approved samples, or product submittals. Flag these rooms directly on the marked finish plan.

I. Matrix and Plan Closeout

  1. Create a finish-scope matrix listing room, surface, finish code, material, color, pattern, substrate, transition, detail, specification, approval, and unresolved item. Finalize the matrix built in Sections F and G.
  2. Create a marked finish plan showing pattern origins, transitions, and high-risk coordination zones. Compile the markups recorded throughout Sections F through H onto one finish plan.
  3. Prepare RFI-style questions for conflicting codes, undefined limits, thickness conflicts, missing transitions, or incomplete wet-area information. State the document references, observed conflict, field impact, and requested clarification for each item without proposing an unapproved answer.

J. Final Review

  1. Review the matrix for every room, current revisions, approved selections, and field-verification requirements. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete before closing the chapter.

Common Finish-Matrix Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which room or surface is involved.

  • Identity check: confirm every room number and name in the matrix matches the current architectural floor plan.
  • Code check: confirm every finish code entered in the matrix resolves to a legend entry, specification section, or approved selection.
  • Transition check: confirm every floor-finish change at a door, opening, stair, cabinet, fixture, or level change is recorded.
  • Wet-area check: confirm every wet-area room has a recorded waterproofing or substrate reference, or the omission is logged.
  • Coordination check: confirm the matrix has been compared against cabinet drawings, opening schedules, and interior elevations.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Room in the Finish Schedule Cannot Be Located on the Floor Plan

Probable cause: the room was renumbered, subdivided, or renamed on a later architectural revision that was not reflected in the finish schedule.
Confirmation: compare the finish schedule's room list against the current Chapter 4 room-by-room layout summary line by line.
Correction: log the room-identity conflict on the missing-information and RFI-style question list rather than guessing which room the schedule entry describes.
Reinspection: repeat the identity check on the corrected matrix once a response is received.

A Finish Code Does Not Appear in the Legend

Probable cause: the legend was not updated after a specification revision, or the code was introduced by an addendum without an updated legend entry.
Confirmation: check every legend in the package, including any addendum-issued legend supplements, before concluding the code is undefined.
Correction: record the undefined code on the missing-information and RFI-style question list; do not assign a material from general trade familiarity.
Reinspection: repeat the code-resolution check for every matrix row using that code once a definition is received.

A Wet-Area Room Has No Documented Waterproofing System

Probable cause: the finish schedule specifies the surface material but the waterproofing or substrate system was left to a referenced detail or specification section that was not located.
Confirmation: search the specifications, wall sections, and interior details for a waterproofing or substrate reference tied to that room before concluding it is missing.
Correction: if no system is found, record the omission on the missing-information and RFI-style question list and mark the room as unresolved on the wet-area reference list.
Reinspection: repeat the wet-area check once a documented system is received.

A Floor-Finish Transition Has No Recorded Detail

Probable cause: the finish plan shows a material change but does not reference a transition detail, or the referenced detail was not included in the received package.
Confirmation: check the finish plan and the Chapter 1 reference map for a detail callout at the exact transition location.
Correction: log the missing transition detail on the missing-information and RFI-style question list rather than assuming a standard threshold or reducer.
Reinspection: repeat the transition check once the detail is received.

Finish Thickness Conflicts With a Door or Fixture Clearance

Probable cause: the finish schedule and the door or fixture information were developed on different revisions and were not cross-checked.
Confirmation: compare the documented finish thickness against the door undercut, threshold, or fixture clearance shown on the opening schedule or interior elevation.
Correction: record the conflict on the missing-information and RFI-style question list; do not adjust the finish thickness or clearance without an approved response.
Reinspection: repeat the coordination check once a response is incorporated.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a controlled, room-by-room finish matrix ready for use, not a stack of unmatched finish codes.

  • Archive every superseded finish schedule or finish plan separately from the active matrix
  • Store the completed room finish matrix, marked finish plan, and wet-area reference list together as the finish package
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route the missing-information and RFI-style question list through the project's document-control process
  • Retain the finish package for direct reuse in Chapter 10

Field Terms

Room Finish Schedule
The tabular document listing finishes assigned to each room by number and name.
Finish Plan
The drawing showing finish locations, pattern origins, transitions, and material boundaries.
Finish Code
The abbreviation or symbol identifying a specific material, product, or approved selection.
Layout Origin
The point or reference line from which a flooring or wall pattern is set out.
Transition
The documented condition where one finish material meets another.
Wet Area
A room or zone requiring documented waterproofing, moisture protection, or a defined substrate system.
Approved Finish Selection
A color, pattern, or product choice confirmed through the owner or designer approval process.
Wainscot
A wall finish applied to a defined lower height, distinct from the finish above it.

The document-control table listing every room's floor, base, wall, ceiling, trim, casework, countertop, and special-finish information.

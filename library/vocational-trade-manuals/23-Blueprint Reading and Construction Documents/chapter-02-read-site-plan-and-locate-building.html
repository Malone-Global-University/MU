Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to take the navigation package built in Chapter 1 and interpret the site plan for one residential project. The finished work product is one marked site-control sheet that shows the documented building footprint, property lines, setbacks, easements, access, utilities, drainage, elevations, benchmarks, and every location requiring field verification before layout work begins.

You will locate property lines, bearings, and survey control; identify setbacks and buildable area; trace the building footprint back to controlling dimensions; locate easements and utilities; identify drainage and grading information; compare the site plan with the floor plan, foundation plan, and utility plans; and record every unresolved dimension, elevation, or utility conflict as a clarification item rather than assuming an answer.

This chapter depends directly on the navigation package from Chapter 1. You must already know which site, civil, landscape, and utility sheets are current before this chapter's interpretation begins.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-007 — Identify property lines, bearings, distances, monuments, and survey control points from the site plan.
  • BP-008 — Locate required setbacks, buildable area, zoning yards, and lot-coverage information.
  • BP-009 — Read easements, rights-of-way, and no-build areas and record their purpose, width, and holder.
  • BP-010 — Identify existing and proposed utility locations, meters, and service-entry points.
  • BP-011 — Interpret driveway, walk, access, and service-area information and relate it to the building.
  • BP-012 — Mark the building footprint from the site plan with controlling dimensions and produce a building-location summary.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 1 confirmed which sheets are current and where information lives. This chapter uses that navigation package to read the one sheet that controls where the entire building sits on the lot. Chapter 3 uses the building-location summary produced here to confirm that the foundation plan's overall footprint is consistent with the site-plan footprint. Chapter 20 rebuilds this same site-interpretation discipline at full scale as the second step of the capstone.

The habit built here — never deriving a field layout from graphic scale when written dimensions or survey control are missing — is used without exception in every remaining chapter that involves a dimension, elevation, or location.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming the site-plan sheet's identity, revision, and relationship to civil, landscape, utility, and architectural sheets
  • locating property lines, setbacks, easements, and buildable area
  • tracing the building footprint and its controlling dimensions
  • identifying access, drainage, contours, benchmarks, and utility locations
  • comparing the site plan against related architectural and civil drawings
  • marking field-verification needs and producing a building-location summary

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform a boundary or topographic survey, set a benchmark, or establish a legal property corner — those actions require a licensed surveyor
  • derive a field layout from graphic scale when written dimensions or survey control points are missing or incomplete
  • resolve a setback, easement, or utility conflict by choosing a preferred interpretation instead of recording an RFI-style question
  • combine site information from different issues or revisions of the site plan without the project's document-control process authorizing the combination

Any dimension, elevation, or utility location that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the field-verification list and RFI-style question list rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A site plan shows the design team's intended relationship between the building and the lot. It does not replace a licensed survey. Where survey control points are shown, use them as the reference for the building-location summary; where they are missing, record the omission rather than measuring from the printed drawing.

Setup Standard

Do not begin locating property lines (Step 3) until the site-plan sheet's identity, revision, scale, and relationship to related sheets have been confirmed in Step 1 against the Chapter 1 sheet register. A site-control sheet built from an unconfirmed revision must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Distinguish property lines from fences, walls, curbs, and edges of pavement early. Occupancy features frequently do not sit on the actual property line, and treating one as the other is a common source of setback and encroachment errors.

Reference Conventions

Establish these definitions before beginning the site-control sheet, and apply them consistently across every site, civil, landscape, and utility sheet reviewed.

TermDefinition
Property lineThe surveyed legal boundary of the lot, defined by bearings, distances, monuments, and pins, not by any built or planted feature
SetbackThe minimum required distance from a property line, easement, or other zoning reference within which no building or specified structure may be placed
Buildable areaThe portion of the lot remaining after setbacks, easements, and other zoning or lot-coverage restrictions are applied
EasementA recorded right for a specific holder to use a defined portion of the lot for a stated purpose, shown with its own width and location
BenchmarkA fixed point of known elevation used as the reference for all other elevations shown on the site plan
Finished-floor elevationThe documented elevation of the completed floor surface, referenced to the project datum or benchmark
Survey control pointA monument, pin, corner, or other fixed reference used to establish property lines and building location in the field

Common Field Fundamentals

Every site-plan interpretation in this book, from this chapter through the Chapter 20 capstone, opens by confirming the site sheet's revision, then separates surveyed property information from occupancy features, and never accepts a field layout dimension that was scaled instead of read or verified.

Property Lines Versus Occupancy Features

A fence, wall, curb, or edge of pavement is frequently near a property line but is not itself the property line. Treat the surveyed bearings, distances, monuments, and pins as the only reliable source for the property boundary, and record any location where an occupancy feature appears to conflict with the surveyed line as a field-verification item.

Scale Warning — Do Not Derive Layout From Graphic Scale

Do not derive a field layout dimension from the graphic scale when written dimensions or survey control points are missing or incomplete. A site plan reproduced, resized, or displayed at an altered size can misrepresent distances even when the graphic scale bar appears correct. Verify the graphic scale against a known dimension before using it for anything beyond a general visual reference.

Critical Cue — Confirm Setbacks Before Marking the Footprint

Do not mark the building footprint as final until the setbacks, buildable area, and easement locations affecting that footprint have been confirmed. A footprint marked before setbacks are verified can misrepresent a condition that would place the building in violation of a recorded restriction.

Cross-Reference Cue — Compare the Footprint Across Sheets

Compare the building footprint dimensions and orientation shown on the site plan with the floor plan, foundation plan, and grading plan. A footprint that is consistent on the site plan but inconsistent with the floor plan is a coordination problem to record, not resolve by choosing one dimension over the other.

RFI Threshold — Utility and Elevation Conflicts

Record any contradictory setback, unresolved elevation, missing benchmark, or utility conflict as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not select the interpretation that seems most likely to be correct; document the conflict and route it through the RFI-style question process.

E. Preparation — Confirm Site-Plan Identity and Basis

  1. Confirm the site-plan sheet number, title, revision, scale, north arrow, survey basis, and relationship to civil, landscape, utility, and architectural sheets. Check each item against the Chapter 1 sheet register before proceeding.
  2. Identify the project address, parcel information, legal description, lot number, block, subdivision, and jurisdiction information provided. Record these on the Site and Building-Location Identity Record.

F. Property and Zoning Interpretation

  1. Locate property lines, bearings, distances, monuments, pins, corners, and survey control points. Mark each on the site-control sheet using the surveyed information only.
  2. Distinguish property lines from fences, walls, curbs, edges of pavement, and approximate occupancy lines. Label any occupancy feature separately from the surveyed property line.
  3. Identify required setbacks, buildable area, zoning yards, height controls, and lot-coverage information shown. Mark the buildable area boundary on the site-control sheet.

G. Easements, Footprint, and Access

  1. Locate easements, rights-of-way, utility corridors, drainage easements, access easements, and no-build areas. Mark each with a distinct symbol from the markup legend.
  2. Record any easement purpose, width, holder, reference document, and field-verification note. Enter each easement's data directly on the building-location summary.
  3. Identify existing and proposed buildings, additions, garages, accessory structures, decks, porches, steps, and retaining walls. Distinguish existing from proposed using the markup legend.
  4. Trace the proposed building footprint and identify controlling dimensions to property lines, gridlines, baselines, or survey points. Record the controlling dimension chain on the building-location summary.
  5. Distinguish dimensions to foundation, wall face, roof projection, deck edge, or other referenced component. Label each footprint dimension with its exact reference point.
  6. Identify building orientation, entry, garage, driveway, walks, patios, service areas, and construction access. Mark each on the site-control sheet.
  7. Locate street, curb, sidewalk, apron, alley, fire access, emergency access, and delivery constraints. Record any constraint affecting construction access.

H. Elevations, Drainage, and Utilities

  1. Identify existing and proposed contours, spot elevations, benchmarks, finished-floor elevations, top-of-foundation elevations, and grade relationships. Record the benchmark and datum on the building-location summary.
  2. Locate drainage arrows, swales, catch basins, area drains, culverts, retaining systems, and discharge points. Mark each drainage feature on the site-control sheet.
  3. Identify roof-drainage or downspout information that affects the site. Cross-check against the roof plan where available.
  4. Locate visible or documented water, sewer, septic, well, gas, electrical, communications, storm, and irrigation services. Mark each utility line by type.
  5. Distinguish existing, proposed, abandoned, relocated, and approximate utility lines. Label each utility line's status using the markup legend.
  6. Identify utility meters, transformers, poles, pedestals, cleanouts, hydrants, valves, and service-entry points. Mark each service-entry point on the site-control sheet.
  7. Record notes requiring utility locating, potholing, survey, testing, easement approval, or authority coordination. Add each note to the field-verification list.

I. Site Constraints and Coordination

  1. Identify trees, protected vegetation, root zones, wetlands, slopes, flood information, environmental buffers, and other site constraints shown. Mark each constraint affecting the buildable area.
  2. Locate fences, gates, walls, neighboring structures, encroachments, and demolition limits. Mark each on the site-control sheet.
  3. Compare the site plan with the floor plan, foundation plan, exterior elevations, grading plan, and utility plans. Confirm the footprint and orientation are consistent across all compared sheets.
  4. Verify the building footprint dimensions and orientation are consistent across related drawings. Record any inconsistency by exact location and source sheet.
  5. Identify missing dimensions, contradictory setbacks, unresolved elevations, or utility conflicts. Add each to the field-verification list or RFI-style question list as appropriate.

J. Site-Control Sheet Closeout

  1. Do not derive a field layout from graphic scale when written dimensions or survey control are missing. Confirm every dimension used on the building-location summary traces to a written source.
  2. Mark all dimensions and locations requiring surveyor or field verification. Consolidate these into the field-verification list.
  3. Create a site-plan markup legend distinguishing verified document information, field verification, questions, and exclusions. Apply the legend consistently across the site-control sheet.
  4. Produce a building-location summary listing baseline, control points, footprint dimensions, setbacks, easements, access, benchmark, utilities, and drainage. Finalize the summary using the data recorded in Sections F through I.
  5. Prepare RFI-style questions for incomplete, conflicting, or ambiguous site information. Write each question with document references, the observed conflict, and the required-by date.
  6. Review the marked site-control sheet against the current revision and sheet references. Confirm the sheet still matches the Chapter 1 sheet register before closing the chapter.

Common Site-Interpretation Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which zoning district or utility configuration is involved.

  • Property check: confirm every marked property line traces to a surveyed bearing, distance, monument, or pin, not an occupancy feature.
  • Setback check: confirm every setback and easement affecting the buildable area is marked with its documented dimension.
  • Footprint check: confirm the building footprint dimensions and orientation are consistent between the site plan, floor plan, and foundation plan.
  • Utility check: confirm every utility line is labeled existing, proposed, abandoned, relocated, or approximate.
  • Field-verification check: confirm every dimension or location that could not be confirmed from written information is logged, not assumed.

Complete the Site-Control Sheet Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 3.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Marked Building Footprint Does Not Match the Floor Plan

Probable cause: the footprint was traced from the site plan's graphic representation instead of from its written controlling dimensions, or the floor plan and site plan are different revisions.
Confirmation: compare the exact controlling dimensions on the site plan and floor plan and confirm both are the current revision in the Chapter 1 sheet register.
Correction: rebuild the footprint markup from the written dimensions on the current revision of both sheets.
Reinspection: repeat the footprint check across the site plan, floor plan, and foundation plan.

A Property Line Was Marked From a Fence or Wall

Probable cause: the occupancy feature was assumed to sit on the property line without checking the surveyed bearings and distances.
Confirmation: compare the fence or wall location against the surveyed property-line data on the site plan.
Correction: remark the property line using only surveyed bearings, distances, monuments, and pins, and label the occupancy feature separately.
Reinspection: repeat the property check on the corrected site-control sheet.

A Setback Dimension Was Scaled Instead of Read

Probable cause: the reproduction size of the site plan was not verified before a distance was measured with a scale.
Confirmation: check the sheet's actual reproduction size against the title block and verify the graphic scale against a known dimension.
Correction: discard the scaled measurement, rely on the written setback dimension, and log the location as unverified if no written dimension exists.
Reinspection: repeat the setback check on the corrected site-control sheet before permitting further scaling.

An Easement Purpose or Width Cannot Be Confirmed

Probable cause: the easement is shown graphically on the site plan without a supporting note, or the referenced recorded document was not included in the received package.
Confirmation: check the site plan's notes, legend, and any referenced recorded-document callouts for the easement in question.
Correction: record the unresolved easement on the RFI-style question list rather than assuming its purpose or width.
Reinspection: repeat the easement check once the recorded document or clarification is received.

A Utility Line's Status Cannot Be Determined

Probable cause: the utility line lacks a status label, or the site plan and utility plan show conflicting information for the same line.
Confirmation: compare the utility line against the utility plan and any legend defining existing, proposed, abandoned, or relocated status.
Correction: record the conflict or missing status on the field-verification list and request utility locating or authority coordination as applicable.
Reinspection: repeat the utility check once the status is confirmed.

Survey Control Point
A monument, pin, corner, or other fixed reference used to establish property lines and building location in the field.

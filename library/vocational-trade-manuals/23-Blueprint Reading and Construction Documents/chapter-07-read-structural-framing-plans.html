Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to receive a residential structural framing package and convert its members, bearing, connectors, and notes into a framing-scope checklist without redesigning, resizing, or relocating any structural component. The finished work product is one framing checklist organized by layout, material verification, bearing, openings, blocking, connectors, bracing, penetrations, inspection, and concealment, built from the current approved structural documents received for one project.

You will confirm structural sheet identity and design criteria, compare structural grids and dimensions with the architectural set, locate and tag beams, girders, headers, posts, and bearing walls, trace bearing from roof through foundation, identify openings and required framing around them, match connector tags to schedules and details, identify delegated-design items, and compare framing with architectural, plumbing, electrical, and HVAC information without resolving any conflict through an unauthorized field decision.

This chapter depends directly on Chapter 3 (foundation), Chapter 4 (floor plan), Chapter 5 (elevations), and Chapter 6 (sections), and it supplies the load-path and bearing information Chapter 8 needs before opening schedules can be finalized against structural rough framing.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-037 — Identify beams and girders on structural framing plans and match each to its schedule or detail reference.
  • BP-038 — Read joist layouts, including framing direction, spacing, and span limits shown on the documents.
  • BP-039 — Locate headers and trace the framed openings they support back to architectural door, window, and structural information.
  • BP-040 — Interpret roof-framing notes, including ridges, hips, valleys, rafters, trusses, and bearing points.
  • BP-041 — Identify structural connectors, including hold-downs, straps, and anchor bolts, and match each to its schedule or manufacturer reference.
  • BP-042 — Create a framing-scope checklist organized by layout, bearing, openings, connectors, bracing, and inspection points.

Where This Skill Fits

Structural framing plans convert the geometry established in the floor plan, elevations, and sections into an actual load path from roof to footing. Chapter 3 established the foundation this load path bears on. Chapter 6 traced the assembly layers the framing carries. This chapter reads the framing itself: the members, their bearing, their connectors, and the openings cut through them. Chapter 8 depends on this chapter's header and opening information before door and window rough openings can be released, and Chapter 17 depends on this chapter's framing checklist when cross-trade penetrations through joists, beams, and shear walls are compared against plumbing, electrical, and HVAC routes.

The document-control habits from Chapter 1, the site and foundation controls from Chapters 2 and 3, and the assembly-tracing discipline from Chapter 6 all continue in this chapter without exception.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming structural sheet identity, revisions, design criteria, general notes, and engineer information
  • comparing structural grids, dimensions, and levels with the architectural plan set
  • identifying and tagging beams, girders, headers, posts, columns, and bearing or shear walls
  • tracing bearing and load path from roof through floors, walls, beams, posts, foundations, and footings
  • identifying floor and roof framing members, direction, spacing, and documented span limits
  • identifying openings, headers, trimmers, blocking, and required framing around stairs, chimneys, and mechanical penetrations
  • matching connector tags to schedules, details, and manufacturer references
  • identifying delegated-design items and required submittals
  • comparing framing with architectural, plumbing, electrical, and HVAC information and recording conflicts
  • producing a framing-scope checklist with complete document references

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • infer truss-web layout, reactions, member sizes, or connector capacities not shown by the documents
  • resize, relocate, notch, drill, reinforce, or substitute a structural member to resolve a field or coordination conflict
  • convert architectural roof lines into structural framing information without the structural roof-framing plan
  • treat a typical detail as governing when a specific or revised detail applies to the condition being read

Any missing member tag, unmatched schedule, unclear bearing condition, or connector ambiguity found while building the checklist must be recorded as an RFI-style question rather than resolved by the learner.

Trade Note

An architectural roof line shows the finished geometry the roof must achieve. It does not show how that geometry is framed. Only the structural roof-framing plan, truss layout, and connector schedule establish the actual members, bearing points, and uplift connections. Treat these as two separate facts that must be cross-referenced, not one fact assumed from the other.

Setup Standard

Do not begin tagging members (Section F) until structural sheet identity, revisions, and design criteria have been confirmed in Steps 1 through 3. A member register built before design criteria are confirmed must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Trace bearing from the roof down before tagging individual members. Knowing the load path first makes it far easier to recognize which beam, post, or wall a given member tag is actually reporting to.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every framing plan is only useful once it is traced back to a load path that terminates at the foundation. Learning to trace that path before tagging individual members prevents the common error of treating a framing plan as a set of isolated symbols rather than a coordinated structural system.

Reading a Framing Plan Versus Reading an Architectural Roof Line

An architectural elevation or roof plan shows the finished roof geometry the design intends to achieve. A structural roof-framing plan shows the actual members, bearing points, and connections required to build that geometry. When the two appear to disagree on slope, bearing, or geometry, record the conflict as a coordination item rather than assuming either document controls without confirmation.

Critical Cue — Do Not Infer Truss or Beam Design From Architectural Lines

Truss-web layout, reactions, connector capacities, and beam sizes are engineering decisions. Do not infer them from architectural roof lines, ceiling heights, or apparent geometry. Use only the structural framing plan, truss layout, schedules, and connector details, and record any missing information as a clarification item.

Code Alert — Verify Design Criteria Before Relying on a Structural Note

Structural general notes state the design loads, wind, seismic, and code basis the engineer used. Confirm these criteria correspond to the approved project basis and permit documents before relying on any structural note, and do not use a code citation to fill a missing design detail without clarification.

RFI Threshold — Unmatched Tags, Conflicts, and Unclear Bearing

Record any missing member tag, unmatched schedule entry, incomplete detail, or inconsistent grid as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not resize, relocate, notch, drill, reinforce, or substitute a structural member to resolve what you find.

E. Preparation — Confirm Structural Identity and Design Basis

  1. Confirm structural sheet numbers, revisions, design criteria, general notes, symbols, abbreviations, schedules, and engineer information. Enter these directly on the Structural Document Identity Record.
  2. Identify foundation, floor, wall, ceiling, roof, lateral, and detail sheets included in the structural package. List every included sheet in the document-control log before tagging begins.
  3. Compare structural grids, dimensions, levels, and orientation with architectural plans. Confirm the structural set uses the same grid and level references as the Chapter 3 through 6 deliverables; log any mismatch.

F. Member Register Construction

  1. Locate beams, girders, headers, lintels, posts, columns, bearing walls, shear walls, braced walls, collectors, and drag elements. Enter each as a row in the member register with its tag and location.
  2. Match every member tag to the applicable schedule, note, or detail. Record the schedule or detail reference in the member register, or mark the tag unmatched.
  3. Record member material, size, quantity, grade, species, product designation, or delegated-design reference only as documented. Do not enter a value the documents do not state.
  4. Locate floor joists, trusses, I-joists, rafters, roof trusses, purlins, sleepers, and outlookers. Add each framing-member type to the register with its documented framing direction and spacing.
  5. Identify framing direction, spacing, span limits shown, cantilevers, overhangs, and changes in framing orientation. Record these directly against the affected member rows.

G. Load-Path and Bearing Trace

  1. Trace bearing from roof and upper floors down through walls, beams, posts, foundations, and footings. Build the load-path map one bearing line at a time, starting at the roof.
  2. Identify bearing lines, point loads, concentrated reactions, beam pockets, post bases, and required blocking. Record each bearing condition and its supporting member in the load-path map.
  3. Locate roof ridges, hips, valleys, rafters, trusses, girder trusses, bearing points, and uplift connections. Extend the load-path map to include roof-level bearing and uplift.
  4. Do not infer truss-web layout, reactions, or modifications from architectural roof lines. Use only the structural truss layout and schedule for this information; log missing truss information as an RFI item.
  5. Identify wall-framing notes for studs, plates, headers, blocking, sheathing, tall walls, and balloon-framed conditions. Record these notes against the affected wall lines in the load-path map.

H. Openings, Connectors, and Bracing

  1. Locate openings for stairs, chimneys, plumbing, ducts, skylights, attic access, and equipment. Add each opening to the member register with its framing requirements.
  2. Identify headers, trimmers, doubled members, rim members, blocking, bridging, squash blocks, and web stiffeners shown. Record each against its associated opening or bearing condition.
  3. Identify lateral-force-resisting elements, hold-downs, straps, anchor bolts, sheathing nailing, collectors, and boundary conditions shown. Begin the connector register with these entries.
  4. Match connector tags to schedules, details, and manufacturer references. Complete the connector register with schedule and manufacturer references for every tag located.
  5. Record required connector fasteners only from the approved detail or manufacturer information. Do not enter a fastener type, size, or quantity the documents do not state.
  6. Identify field-weld, bolt, screw, nail, adhesive, bearing, notch, drill, and reinforcement restrictions. Record these restrictions directly in the connector register.
  7. Locate temporary and permanent bracing notes, truss-bracing references, and erection requirements. Add a bracing section to the framing-scope checklist with these references.

I. Delegated Design and Cross-Trade Coordination

  1. Identify delegated-design items such as trusses, engineered lumber, stairs, rail systems, or specialty connections. List each delegated-design item and its stated performance criteria in the framing-scope checklist.
  2. Record required shop drawings, submittals, engineering seals, and approvals before fabrication. Add each requirement to the checklist under the applicable delegated-design item.
  3. Compare framing with architectural openings, cabinets, plumbing fixtures, ducts, electrical equipment, and ceiling features. Cross-check the member register against the Chapter 4, 5, and 6 deliverables.
  4. Identify conflicts where routes or openings cross beams, posts, joists, trusses, shear walls, or required blocking. Log each conflict with its exact location and source references.
  5. Identify missing member tags, unmatched schedules, incomplete details, or inconsistent grids. Add each to the RFI-style question list.
  6. Do not resize, relocate, notch, drill, reinforce, or substitute a structural member to resolve a conflict. Route every conflict through the RFI-style question list instead.

J. Checklist Assembly and Closeout

  1. Create a framing checklist organized by layout, material verification, bearing, openings, blocking, connectors, bracing, penetrations, inspection, and concealment. Compile the member register, load-path map, and connector register into this single organized checklist.
  2. Attach sheet, detail, schedule, specification, submittal, and manufacturer references to each checklist item. Confirm no checklist row is missing its source reference.
  3. Prepare RFI-style questions for missing sizes, conflicts, unclear bearing, connector ambiguity, or trade penetrations. Finalize the RFI-style question list using the neutral format from Chapter 17's process.
  4. Review the framing checklist with the architectural and MEP coordination findings before issue. Confirm every cross-trade conflict identified in Section I is reflected in the final checklist before closing the chapter.

Common Structural Inspection

Inspect the finished checklist using the same checks regardless of which member type or bearing condition is involved.

  • Completeness check: confirm every member tag on the structural plans appears in the member register.
  • Matching check: confirm every member and connector tag is matched to a schedule, detail, or manufacturer reference, or logged as unmatched.
  • Continuity check: confirm every bearing point in the load-path map connects to a documented support above and below.
  • Coordination check: confirm structural grids, dimensions, and openings have been compared against the architectural set and every mismatch is logged.
  • No-redesign check: confirm no member has been resized, relocated, notched, drilled, reinforced, or substituted anywhere in the checklist.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Member Tag Does Not Appear In Any Schedule

Probable cause: the schedule was not fully reviewed, the tag belongs to a schedule on a different sheet, or the schedule entry was omitted from the issued set.
Confirmation: check every structural schedule in the package, including sheets not adjacent to the framing plan, before concluding the tag is unmatched.
Correction: log the unmatched tag on the RFI-style question list with its exact sheet and grid location; do not assign a size or material from a similar member elsewhere in the set.
Reinspection: repeat the matching check on the member register once a response is received.

A Load-Path Trace Terminates Without a Documented Support

Probable cause: a bearing point was traced to a wall or member not shown as continuing to the foundation, or a level change was misread.
Confirmation: compare the terminating point against the foundation plan from Chapter 3 and the section information from Chapter 6.
Correction: log the discontinuous load path as a conflict; do not assume an unstated support exists.
Reinspection: repeat the continuity check on the load-path map once a response is received.

A Connector Tag Cannot Be Matched to a Schedule or Manufacturer Reference

Probable cause: the connector schedule was issued separately and not included in the received package, or the tag references a manufacturer catalog not provided.
Confirmation: check the document-control register from Chapter 1 to confirm whether the connector schedule or manufacturer catalog was received.
Correction: log the missing schedule or catalog as a document-request item; do not select a substitute connector based on apparent similarity.
Reinspection: repeat the matching check on the connector register once the missing document is received.

An Architectural Opening Crosses a Beam, Post, or Shear Wall Without a Structural Solution Shown

Probable cause: the architectural and structural sets were developed on different coordination cycles, or a later architectural revision was not reflected in the structural set.
Confirmation: compare the revision dates of the affected architectural and structural sheets against the document-control register.
Correction: log the conflict with its exact location and both source references; do not relocate the opening or modify the structural member to resolve it.
Reinspection: repeat the coordination check once an approved response is incorporated into both sets.

A Roof Geometry Shown on the Elevations Does Not Match the Structural Roof-Framing Plan

Probable cause: the architectural roof line represents design intent that was later revised structurally, or a truss layout was value-engineered after the elevations were issued.
Confirmation: compare the current revision of the elevation sheet against the current revision of the structural roof-framing plan.
Correction: log the geometry conflict; do not average, split the difference, or select the version that appears more buildable.
Reinspection: repeat the coordination check once an approved response reconciles both sheets.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a controlled, coordinated framing-scope checklist ready for use, not a set of isolated member notes.

  • Store the completed member register, load-path map, connector register, and framing-scope checklist together as one framing package
  • Preserve every RFI-style question with its exact sheet, grid, and revision reference
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route the RFI-style question list through the project's document-control process

Field Terms

Bearing
The point or line where a structural member transfers its load to what supports it.
Load Path
The continuous route a load follows from application to foundation and soil.
Member Tag
The label identifying a specific structural member and pointing to its schedule or detail.
Delegated Design
A structural component designed by a separate licensed engineer under stated performance criteria.
Connector
Hardware that transfers load between two structural members.
Shear Wall / Braced Wall
A wall identified as resisting lateral force in addition to gravity load.
Blocking
Short framing members installed for spacing, load transfer, or backing, as documented.
Header
A structural member spanning an opening and carrying the load above it to the members on either side.
Hold-Down
A connector resisting uplift or overturning force at a shear wall or braced wall.
Girder Truss
A truss designed to carry the reaction load of other trusses bearing on it.

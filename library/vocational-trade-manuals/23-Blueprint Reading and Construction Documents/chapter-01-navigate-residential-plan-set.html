Chapter Overview

This chapter qualifies you to receive a residential construction-document package, confirm what it actually contains, and convert it into a controlled navigation tool before any sheet is interpreted for content. The finished work product is one verified sheet register, one revision and clarification summary, one reference map, and one missing-document list, all built from the current approved documents received for one project.

You will confirm project identity and document status, compare the sheet index against the sheets actually received, read title blocks rather than relying on the index alone, group sheets by discipline, read revision blocks and clouds, identify addenda and approved clarifications, locate and verify drawing scales, follow representative references to their destinations, and record every undefined symbol, broken reference, or missing document as it is found.

This is the first chapter in the book. Every later chapter depends on the navigation package produced here: you cannot correctly interpret a foundation plan, a framing plan, or a roof detail if you do not first know which sheet is current, which sheet is superseded, and which references still need to be followed.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • BP-001 — Identify plan sheets by discipline, sheet type, and content category from the received package.
  • BP-002 — Read a title block and extract project identity, sheet identity, scale, and issue information.
  • BP-003 — Use a sheet index to confirm a package is complete and to identify missing, duplicated, or extra sheets.
  • BP-004 — Interpret revision dates, clouds, deltas, and descriptions and distinguish current from superseded information.
  • BP-005 — Locate and verify a drawing scale, including confirming that reproduction size has not invalidated a printed scale.
  • BP-006 — Create a plan-set navigation summary that maps sheet content, references, revisions, and missing information.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter after this one assumes the reader already knows which sheets are current, where a given piece of information lives, and how to follow a reference from one sheet to another. Chapter 2 uses this navigation package to locate the site plan. Chapter 3 uses it to locate the foundation package. Chapter 20 rebuilds this same navigation discipline at full scale, across every discipline in a complete residential set, as the opening step of the capstone.

The document-control habits built here — verifying issue, marking superseded sheets, and never combining information from different revisions — are used without exception in every remaining chapter of this book.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • confirming project identity, document issue, and permit or construction status
  • comparing a sheet index against the sheets actually received
  • reading title blocks, revision blocks, and general notes on every received sheet
  • grouping sheets by discipline and content category
  • identifying addenda, bulletins, and approved clarifications that modify the drawings
  • locating and verifying drawing scales and identifying views that are not to scale
  • following representative references to their destination and back
  • producing a sheet register, revision summary, reference map, and missing-document list

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • interpret the technical content of any plan, detail, or schedule beyond what is needed to classify and index the sheet — that interpretation begins in Chapter 2 and continues through Chapter 19
  • treat a conceptual, preliminary, bid-only, marked-up, or unapproved shop-drawing set as construction authorization
  • scale a dimension from any sheet unless the reproduction size and graphic scale have been verified under Step 19 of this chapter
  • combine information from different issues or revisions of the same sheet without the project's document-control process authorizing the combination

Any sheet, index entry, or reference that cannot be resolved during this chapter must be recorded on the missing-information and document-request list rather than assumed.

Trade Note

A sheet index tells you what the design team intended to issue. It does not tell you what you actually received. This chapter treats those as two separate facts that must be checked against each other, not one fact assumed from the other.

Setup Standard

Do not begin comparing sheets against the index (Step 5) until the project identity, document issue, and permit or construction status have been confirmed in Steps 1 through 3. A sheet register built before document identity is confirmed must be rebuilt.

Field Tip

Read the title block on every sheet even when a sheet index exists. The index can be wrong, outdated, or incomplete; the title block on the actual sheet in hand is the more reliable record of that sheet's own identity.

Reading a Title Block Versus Reading an Index

A sheet index is a planning document produced before every sheet is finalized. A title block is printed on the sheet itself and reflects that sheet's own recorded identity at the time it was issued. When the two disagree on a sheet number, title, or discipline, record the conflict as a missing-information item rather than assuming the index is correct.

Scale Warning — Verify Reproduction Size Before Scaling Anything

A drawing scale printed in a title block is only valid at the sheet's original reproduction size. Photocopying, resizing, scanning, printing at a reduced size, or viewing a PDF at an arbitrary zoom level can all invalidate a printed scale. Check the graphic (bar) scale against a known dimension on the same sheet before treating any measurement as reliable, and only where the project procedure authorizes scaling at all.

Critical Cue — Do Not Field-Use a Superseded Sheet

A superseded sheet left in an active set can be picked up and used by mistake. Mark every superseded sheet clearly, remove it from the active field set, and preserve it only in the archive. If you cannot confirm whether a sheet is current, treat it as unresolved and record it on the missing-information list rather than using it.

Cross-Reference Cue — Follow References Both Directions

When a sheet points to a detail, section, schedule, or keynote on another sheet, follow the reference to its destination and then confirm the destination correctly points back to the source condition. A reference that only works in one direction is a coordination problem to record, not resolve on your own.

RFI Threshold — Undefined Symbols and Broken References

Record any undefined symbol, missing legend entry, unreadable note, incomplete reference, or contradictory abbreviation as a clarification item during this chapter. Do not guess a meaning from general trade familiarity; use only the project's own legend and governing discipline conventions.

E. Preparation — Confirm Identity and Package Status

  1. Obtain the complete drawing package, specifications, addenda, bulletins, and approved clarifications available for the project. Collect everything provided in the transmittal, not only the sheets that appear most relevant.
  2. Record the project name, address, owner, design professionals, document issue, permit or construction status, and date received. Enter these directly on the Project and Document Identity Record.
  3. Confirm whether the package is complete, partial, preliminary, permit, bid, construction, record, or another stated issue. Locate this statement on the cover sheet, transmittal, or specifications; do not infer it from the drawings' apparent completeness.
  4. Locate the cover sheet, sheet index, code summary, project information, abbreviations, legends, and general notes. Flag or tab these sheets for repeated reference during this chapter.

F. Sheet Register Construction

  1. Compare the listed sheets on the index with the sheets actually received. Work down the index line by line and check off each sheet as it is located in the received package.
  2. Record missing, duplicated, unreadable, or extra sheets. Log each discrepancy on the missing-information and document-request list with the exact sheet number involved.
  3. Create a sheet register using sheet number, title, discipline, issue date, revision, scale, and status. Populate one row per received sheet in the document-control log.
  4. Read the title block on every sheet rather than relying solely on the index. Confirm sheet number, title, and discipline directly from each sheet's own title block.
  5. Verify project identity and sheet identity remain consistent across the package. Confirm the project name and address in each title block match the Project and Document Identity Record.
  6. Identify the discipline prefixes and numbering structure used by the project. Record the prefix pattern (for example, A for architectural, S for structural) in the document-control log.
  7. Group civil, landscape, architectural, structural, plumbing, mechanical, electrical, fire-protection, energy, interior, and specialty sheets. Sort the sheet register by discipline group.
  8. Identify cover, plan, elevation, section, detail, schedule, diagram, and specification-reference sheets. Add a content-category column to the sheet register and classify every sheet.

G. Revision and Clarification Review

  1. Read revision blocks, clouds, deltas, dates, and revision descriptions on every revised sheet. Record each finding in the revision and clarification summary.
  2. Compare current revisions with superseded sheets and issued bulletins. Confirm the most recent revision on each sheet matches the most recent bulletin or addendum affecting it.
  3. Mark superseded sheets as inactive and preserve them in the archive. Physically or digitally separate superseded sheets from the active navigation set.
  4. Identify addenda, RFIs, supplemental instructions, sketches, and approved submittals that modify the drawings. Add each to the revision and clarification summary with its affected sheets.
  5. Confirm how the project identifies incorporated and unincorporated changes. Locate the project's stated convention (cloud and delta, sheet reissue, or another method) and record it.

H. Scale Verification

  1. Locate drawing scales and note which views are not to scale. Record the stated scale for each sheet in the sheet register and flag any view labeled "not to scale."
  2. Verify that the reproduction size has not invalidated printed scales. Confirm the sheet's actual reproduction size against the size stated in the title block before relying on any printed scale.
  3. Check graphic scales on representative sheets where scaling may be needed later. Use the engineer's or architect's scale against the printed graphic (bar) scale on at least one sheet per discipline and record the result as verified or unverified.

I. Reference Mapping

  1. Identify key plans, match lines, continuation notes, dependent views, and enlarged plans. Record these relationships in the reference map.
  2. Follow representative section, detail, elevation, schedule, and keynote references to their destinations. Select at least three references per discipline group and trace each to its destination sheet.
  3. Follow the references back to the source location to verify coordination. Confirm the destination correctly identifies the originating sheet and condition.
  4. Identify legends, abbreviations, symbols, line types, hatches, grids, levels, and datum conventions. Record where each governing legend is located for later-chapter use.
  5. Record undefined symbols, conflicting abbreviations, broken references, and illegible notes. Log each on the missing-information and document-request list.
  6. Identify sheets containing dimensions, schedules, specifications, material information, and trade coordination relevant to later chapters. Tag these sheets in the reference map for Chapters 2 through 19.
  7. Create a navigation map showing where site, foundation, plans, elevations, sections, framing, openings, finishes, MEP, roof, and specifications are found. Compile the tagged sheets from the prior step into one organized map.

J. Package Closeout

  1. Create a revision and clarification summary listing all active changes. Finalize the summary built in Section G.
  2. Create a missing-information and document-request list. Consolidate every discrepancy logged throughout this chapter into one list.
  3. Review the final navigation summary for current status, complete references, legibility, and clear separation of active and superseded documents. Confirm every deliverable component in Section B is complete before closing the chapter.

Common Navigation Inspection

Inspect the finished package using the same checks regardless of which discipline or sheet group is involved.

  • Completeness check: confirm every sheet on the index is either present in the register or logged as missing.
  • Identity check: confirm the sheet register's sheet number, title, and discipline match each sheet's own title block.
  • Currency check: confirm superseded sheets are marked inactive and archived, and current revisions are recorded in the revision summary.
  • Scale check: confirm at least one representative sheet per discipline has a verified graphic scale before any scaling is authorized in later chapters.
  • Reference check: confirm every traced reference reached a real destination or was logged as broken.

Complete the Navigation Package Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Sheet Register Does Not Match What Is On Site

Probable cause: the register was built from the sheet index instead of from a direct comparison against the sheets actually received.
Confirmation: physically or digitally recount every received sheet and compare it line by line against the register.
Correction: rebuild the affected register rows directly from the title blocks of the sheets in hand, not from the index.
Reinspection: repeat the completeness check and the identity check on the corrected register.

A Superseded Sheet Remains in the Active Set

Probable cause: the revision block was not checked on every sheet, or a reissued sheet with the same sheet number was not compared against the version already on hand.
Confirmation: compare the revision block date and delta on every sheet sharing a sheet number and identify the most recent one.
Correction: remove the superseded sheet from the active set, mark it inactive, and move it to the archive.
Reinspection: repeat the currency check across the full sheet register.

A Measurement Taken From the Drawing Does Not Match the Written Dimension

Probable cause: the sheet was scaled without verifying reproduction size, or the graphic scale was never checked against a known dimension.
Confirmation: check the sheet's actual reproduction size against the title block and verify the graphic scale with the engineer's or architect's scale.
Correction: discard the scaled measurement, rely on the written dimension, and mark the sheet's scale as unverified until reproduction size is confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the scale check on the corrected sheet before permitting any further scaling.

A Followed Reference Does Not Lead to Real Information

Probable cause: the referenced sheet or detail was not included in the received package, or the callout itself contains an error.
Confirmation: recheck the sheet register for the referenced sheet number and confirm whether it was received.
Correction: log the broken reference on the missing-information and document-request list rather than assuming its content.
Reinspection: repeat the reference check once the missing sheet or corrected callout is received.

A Symbol or Abbreviation Cannot Be Confirmed

Probable cause: the project legend does not define the symbol, or two disciplines use the same abbreviation for different meanings.
Confirmation: check every legend in the package, including discipline-specific legends, before concluding the symbol is undefined.
Correction: record the undefined or conflicting symbol on the missing-information and document-request list; do not assign a meaning from general trade familiarity.
Reinspection: repeat the reference check for any sheet interpreted using that symbol once a definition is received.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a controlled, indexed set ready for use, not a stack of loose sheets.

  • Archive every superseded sheet separately from the active navigation set
  • Store the completed sheet register, revision summary, reference map, and missing-document list together as the navigation package
  • Protect any printed working set from damage and confirm it is legible and current before storage
  • Route the missing-information and document-request list through the project's document-control process
  • Retain the navigation package for direct reuse in Chapter 2

Field Terms

The most recent approved issue of a sheet or specification section.
Superseded Document
A prior issue of a sheet or section that has been replaced and archived.
Sheet Index
The listing of every sheet the design team intends to issue for the package.
Title Block
The bordered information block on each sheet stating project and sheet identity, scale, and issue information.
Revision Block
The table or log on a sheet recording each revision by number or letter, date, and description.
Discipline Prefix
The letter or letter group identifying which discipline produced a sheet.
Reference Callout
A symbol or note directing the reader to related information on another sheet.
Graphic (Bar) Scale
A printed scale bar used to verify a drawing's scale independent of its stated ratio.

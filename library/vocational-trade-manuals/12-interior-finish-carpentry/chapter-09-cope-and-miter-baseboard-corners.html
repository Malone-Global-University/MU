Job Overview

This project qualifies you to fit baseboard into a corner without relying on filler to hide a poor angle. You will complete one coped inside corner and one mitered outside corner using the straight baseboard runs installed in Project 08. The finished work product is a tight coped inside joint and an aligned mitered outside joint that both follow the actual wall angle and floor line at that corner.

You will cut a coped profile on the mating piece at an inside corner, back-bevel and refine that cope until it seats fully against the face of the first piece, measure the actual angle at an outside corner rather than assuming ninety degrees, cut and fit two mitered pieces to that measured angle, and fasten both corners while preserving profile continuity and floor contact.

This project depends directly on the straight baseboard runs and corner preparation completed in Project 08. It in turn prepares the baseboard for the shoe-molding installation in Project 10, which follows the same floor line and corner logic at a smaller scale.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FC-049 — Cut inside-corner copes that expose and follow the baseboard profile.
  • FC-050 — Cut outside miters using the actual measured corner angle rather than an assumed angle.
  • FC-051 — Fit corner joints so the profile, face, and floor contact are continuous across the joint.
  • FC-052 — Adjust cutting and fitting for out-of-square corners identified during layout.
  • FC-053 — Fasten corner pieces without splitting the trim or disturbing the fitted joint.
  • FC-054 — Inspect baseboard transitions for profile continuity, alignment, and floor contact.

Where This Skill Fits

Project 08 installed straight baseboard runs and left the corner ends prepared for this project. This project completes those corners using the coping and mitering methods that recur throughout the book: the same coping logic is used again at crown-molding inside corners in Project 12, and the same measured-angle mitering logic is used again at crown-molding outside corners in Project 12 and at chair-rail corners in Project 13.

The completed corners from this project become the reference line that shoe molding in Project 10 must follow at the same locations.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • inspecting the inside and outside corners prepared in Project 08 for plumb, angle, wall plane, and floor variation
  • cutting and installing the square-ended first piece at an inside corner
  • cutting a coped profile on the mating inside-corner piece, back-beveling it, and test-fitting it against the first piece
  • measuring the actual outside-corner angle and determining the required miter angle for each mating piece
  • cutting and test-fitting a mitered outside corner
  • fastening both completed corners while holding the joint closed and preserving profile continuity, top alignment, and floor contact
  • inspecting both corners and replacing pieces with major gaps or damaged profiles rather than filling them

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • alter the framing, drywall corner angle, or floor condition at the corner; a corner outside the baseboard profile's ability to follow it is documented and referred back to the responsible trade rather than forced into fit
  • fill a major gap caused by an incorrect cope angle, incorrect miter angle, or a missed measurement; the piece is recut or replaced instead
  • glue or mechanically reinforce a miter joint unless the trim schedule or finish specification permits it at that location
  • fasten so close to the corner point that the trim splits; fastener placement follows the schedule in Section F

Any corner that cannot be brought into an acceptable fit using the methods in this project is documented as a deficiency and referred back to Project 08 preparation or to the responsible trade before installation continues.

Trade Note

A coped joint is not a cosmetic alternative to a square-cut inside corner; it is the method that keeps an inside corner tight as seasonal wood movement occurs, because the coped piece's profile face rides against the first piece rather than depending on two mitered faces staying in contact. Do not substitute a mitered inside corner unless the finish specification explicitly permits it for the material in use.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cutting either corner in this project until the corner's inside or outside angle has been measured directly and recorded on the Corner-Angle Worksheet in Section F. A miter cut based on an assumed ninety-degree corner will not close on a wall that is out of square, and forcing the joint closed with fasteners will split the trim or telegraph a gap.

Shop Tip

When coping, cut the reveal cut at a slightly steeper angle than the saw's marked 45-degree stop, then back-bevel the coped edge with the coping saw or file so only the visible profile edge makes contact. This removes wood from behind the profile line rather than at the line itself, which keeps the cope from binding before it reaches full depth.

Common Field Fundamentals

Two different problems are solved in this project, and they use two different methods. An inside corner is coped because a mitered inside joint opens visibly as the wood shrinks; a coped joint stays tight because the profile face of the coped piece rides against the flat face of the square-cut piece rather than depending on two angled faces staying aligned. An outside corner is mitered because there is no profile face to cope against on the outside of a corner; the miter angle is set from the actual measured corner angle, not from an assumed square corner, because very few wall corners are exactly ninety degrees.

Why the Cope Is Cut Freehand After the Reveal Cut

The reveal cut establishes the profile line on the face of the piece, but the coping saw follows that profile line freehand along its full depth rather than continuing the straight miter-saw angle through the piece. Cutting straight through on the miter-saw angle instead of coping the profile will leave a visible triangular gap wherever the profile is not flat.

Measurement Cue — Measure the Corner Before Cutting

Measure the actual inside or outside corner angle with a sliding bevel or angle finder and record it on the Corner-Angle Worksheet before setting the saw. Divide the measured outside-corner angle to determine each piece's individual miter angle rather than defaulting to a 45-degree cut on each side.

Critical Safety Cue — Coping Saw and Filing Hand Position

Secure the workpiece in a vise or with a clamp before coping or filing. Keep the hand holding the workpiece behind the cutting or filing tool's path at all times. Do not hold small corner pieces in an open hand while coping close to the cut line.

Field Safety Cue — Small-Piece Saw Support

Corner test pieces and short cut-offs are small enough to be pulled toward the blade if not supported. Use a stop block or hold-down for any piece shorter than the safe minimum length established at the Project 02 workstation, and never bring fingers inside the marked blade exclusion zone to steady a short piece.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Coping and Profile Tools

Where a rotary profile-grinding tool or specialty coping attachment is used instead of a hand coping saw, follow the tool manufacturer's instructions for blade or bit selection, feed rate, and dust control specific to the baseboard material.

G. Inside-Corner Cope

  1. Inspect the inside corner for plumb, angle, wall plane, and floor variation. Record the measured angle on the Corner-Angle Worksheet.
  2. Establish which piece runs square into the corner. Confirm this against the Project 08 run direction so the coped piece is the one entering from the adjoining wall.
  3. Cut and install the square-ended first piece. Cut to length from the correct reference per Project 03 and fasten it per Section D.
  4. Cut a 45-degree reveal cut on the mating piece to expose the profile. Orient the cut so the exposed profile line is visible on the face of the piece.
  5. Follow the profile with a coping saw, grinder, file, or approved tool. Cut along the exposed profile line at its full depth, working slowly through detailed profile changes.
  6. Back-bevel the cope to allow face contact. Remove material behind the visible profile edge so only that edge will contact the first piece.
  7. Test-fit the coped joint. Hold the coped piece against the installed first piece and check contact along the full profile.
  8. Refine high points without shortening the visible profile. Remove small amounts of material with a file or sanding block at points preventing full seating.
  9. Verify full contact at the face and no interference at the back. Confirm the piece seats flat against the wall with the profile faces in contact.

H. Outside-Corner Miter

  1. Measure the actual outside-corner angle. Use the sliding bevel or angle finder and record the reading on the Corner-Angle Worksheet.
  2. Divide or otherwise determine the required miter angle from the measured corner. Calculate each piece's individual saw angle from the measured total rather than assuming an equal 45-degree split unless the measured angle confirms a square corner.
  3. Cut the first outside-corner piece. Set the saw to the calculated angle and cut from the correct long-point or short-point reference.
  4. Cut the mating piece with the complementary angle. Verify the profile orientation matches before cutting.
  5. Test-fit both pieces together. Hold both pieces in position at the corner and check the joint line for closure.
  6. Adjust for wall angle and floor variation. Make small corrective recuts where the test-fit shows a gap at the top, bottom, or face of the joint.

I. Corner Fastening and Reinforcement

  1. Glue or mechanically reinforce the miter only where specified. Apply glue to the mitered faces only if the trim schedule or finish specification permits it at this location.
  2. Fasten pieces while holding the joint closed. Use a clamp or hand pressure to keep the coped or mitered joint closed while driving each fastener.
  3. Avoid fastening so close to the corner that the trim splits. Follow the pilot-hole and edge-distance limits in Section D.

J. Corner Inspection

  1. Inspect profile continuity, top alignment, floor contact, and joint closure. Check each item against the Acceptance Standards in Section F.
  2. Replace pieces with major gaps or damaged profiles rather than relying on filler. Recut or replace any piece that fails the joint-closure or profile-continuity standard, and reinspect the replacement.

Common Cutting Inspection

Apply the following checks to every coped and mitered corner piece before it is treated as complete.

  • Reveal-cut check: confirm the reveal cut fully exposed the profile line before coping began.
  • Cope-depth check: confirm the coping saw followed the profile line at its full depth with no shortcuts through detailed sections.
  • Back-bevel check: confirm material was removed behind the profile edge and not at the visible edge itself.
  • Miter-angle check: confirm the saw angle used matches the angle recorded on the Corner-Angle Worksheet, not an assumed 45-degree default.
  • Length check: confirm each piece was measured from the correct long-point or short-point reference for its joint type.

Complete the Inspection Checklist section below before fastening either corner permanently.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Coped Joint Shows a Gap Along Part of the Profile

Probable cause: the coping saw cut short of the profile line at one or more points, or the back-bevel was insufficient at that section.
Confirmation: hold the coped piece against the first piece and identify exactly where light or a gap appears.
Correction: file or re-cope the identified section, removing material behind the profile edge rather than shortening the visible profile.
Reinspection: repeat the face-contact test-fit along the full profile.

Coped Piece Will Not Seat Flat Against the Wall

Probable cause: uncleaned material remains at the back of the cope and is holding the piece off the wall.
Confirmation: check for contact between the cope and the wall along the back of the piece.
Correction: back-bevel further at the interfering point until the piece seats flat.
Reinspection: repeat the flat-seating check against the wall.

Mitered Outside Corner Opens at the Top or Bottom

Probable cause: the corner angle used for the saw setting did not match the actual measured angle, or the wall is out of plumb along its height.
Confirmation: remeasure the corner angle at both the top and bottom of the baseboard height and compare to the Corner-Angle Worksheet entry.
Correction: recut one or both pieces to the corrected angle, or apply a small controlled back-bevel to close the specific gap without shortening the visible face.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-closure check at both the top and bottom of the joint.

Fastener Splits the Trim Near the Corner Point

Probable cause: the fastener was placed closer to the corner point than the pilot-hole and edge-distance limits allow, or no pilot hole was drilled where one was required.
Confirmation: inspect the split and compare the fastener location to the Section D schedule.
Correction: remove the fastener, and replace the split piece if the split extends into the visible face; otherwise refasten at the correct location with a pilot hole.
Reinspection: repeat the fastener-condition check on the corrected piece.

Corner Joint Was Closed with Filler Instead of a Correct Cut

Probable cause: a major gap from an incorrect cope or miter angle was concealed with filler rather than corrected by recutting.
Confirmation: inspect the joint under close light for filler depth exceeding what is permitted for minor defects.
Correction: remove the affected piece, recut or replace it using the correct measured angle, and refasten.
Reinspection: repeat the full joint-closure and profile-continuity check on the replacement piece.

Field Terms

Cope
A profile cut on the end of a piece that allows it to fit face-to-face over an adjoining piece at an inside corner.
Reveal Cut
The angled cut made to expose the profile line that a coping saw will follow.
Back-Bevel
Removing material behind the visible profile edge of a cope so only that edge contacts the mating piece.
Long Point / Short Point
The longer and shorter edges of a mitered end, used as measuring references depending on the joint.
Measured Corner Angle
The actual angle of a wall corner as measured with a sliding bevel or angle finder.
Corner-Angle Worksheet
The record of measured inside and outside corner angles used to set saw angles for this project.
Face Contact
Full contact between the visible profile edge of a coped piece and the face of the piece it fits against.
Joint Closure
The condition of a mitered or coped joint being fully closed with no visible gap.
Profile Continuity
An unbroken profile line across a completed joint when viewed at normal room height.
Edge Distance
The minimum distance a fastener must be placed from a corner point or edge to avoid splitting the trim.

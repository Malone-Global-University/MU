Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect a proposed hardscape site and produce a complete, documented readiness plan before any excavation, layout stake, or material order is committed. The finished work product is one written hardscape-readiness plan for one proposed walkway, patio, gravel path, retaining wall, stair, or paver area, covering site conditions, drainage, property limits, utilities, access, materials, preliminary quantities, and an approved, conditional, or rejected readiness decision.

You will gather the governing documents, define the project, verify property and easement limits, request utility locating, inspect the site for buried and surface hazards, record drainage and grade conditions, measure the project area, inspect adjacent building and paving interfaces, evaluate soil and access, identify design-review triggers, select compatible materials, calculate preliminary quantities, and issue a documented decision.

This is the first chapter in the book. Every later chapter depends on the readiness plan produced here: Chapter 2 stages the work area using the access and logistics findings from this chapter, Chapter 3 lays out the project using the boundaries and reference documents confirmed here, and Chapter 20's capstone patio or walkway must trace back to a readiness plan built with this exact procedure.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-001 — Inspect site conditions for hardscape suitability, including obstructions, hazards, and interfaces with existing structures.
  • HC-002 — Identify drainage patterns, including high points, low points, runoff sources, discharge paths, and foundation-water risk.
  • HC-003 — Measure the project area, including length, width, area, perimeter, grade change, and transition elevations.
  • HC-004 — Identify access constraints for delivery, excavation, compaction, cutting, and worker movement.
  • HC-005 — Select hardscape materials compatible with the project type, loading, drainage, and site conditions.
  • HC-006 — Document project readiness, including deficiencies, prerequisites, and a final readiness decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every hardscape installation in this book — a paver walkway, a segmental retaining wall, a set of outdoor steps, a complete patio — depends on an accurate picture of the site before the first stake is driven. A readiness plan built on incomplete drainage information, unverified property limits, or unconfirmed utility locations produces excavation, drainage, and structural problems that cannot be corrected by careful paver work later in the project.

The stop-work discipline in this chapter — halting for disputed boundaries, unidentified utilities, unstable soil, or conditions requiring engineering — is the same discipline carried through every later chapter's excavation, wall, drainage, and capstone work. This chapter is where you learn to recognize those conditions before committing labor and materials to a site that is not ready.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • gathering and reviewing the site plan, property information, grading plan, drainage information, utility records, product requirements, and permit conditions for one proposed hardscape project
  • defining project type, use, loading, dimensions, finish, and surrounding interfaces
  • verifying property lines, setbacks, easements, public right-of-way, and prohibited work zones
  • requesting utility locating and inspecting the site for buried and surface obstructions
  • recording drainage patterns and grade conditions
  • measuring the project area and adjacent interfaces
  • evaluating soil, access, and seasonal exposure
  • identifying conditions that require design review or a qualified professional
  • selecting compatible materials and preparing preliminary quantity and logistics calculations
  • documenting deficiencies, prerequisites, and a final readiness decision

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • excavate, stake, drill, cut, or drive spikes before the required utility-location service has responded and marked the site
  • proceed with layout, excavation, or construction on a site with disputed boundaries, unidentified utilities, unstable slopes, contaminated soil, uncontrolled drainage, or structural conflicts identified in this chapter
  • finalize a retaining-wall, driveway-loading, or structural design that this chapter identifies as requiring an engineer, surveyor, or landscape architect
  • substitute this chapter's preliminary quantity calculation for a final material order; final quantities are confirmed in later chapters against the accepted layout

Any site condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected, or reviewed by the required qualified professional, before Chapter 2 work-area setup begins.

Trade Note

A readiness plan is a decision document, not a formality. Every finding recorded in this chapter — every hazard, every drainage pattern, every access constraint — is a condition the rest of the project must be built around. Treat an approved, conditional, or rejected decision as binding until the identified deficiencies are corrected and the site is reinspected.

Required PPE for this project: closed-toe footwear and high-visibility clothing appropriate to the site; gloves when handling stakes, flags, or probe rods; standard eye protection when probing near hard surfaces.

Setup Standard

Do not probe, stake, or otherwise disturb the ground until the required utility-location service has responded and marked the site. A finding recorded before utilities are marked must be reconfirmed after marking is complete.

Shop Tip

Photograph every hazard, drainage pattern, and interface as you record it, not at the end of the inspection. A finding without a photograph is harder to defend later if the readiness decision is questioned or the site changes before construction begins.

Prerequisites

  • Access to the site plan, property survey, grading plan, drainage information, utility records, and permit conditions for the proposed project, or a documented plan to obtain each before the readiness decision is issued
  • Property owner or authorized-representative permission to inspect the site, probe soil, and place marking flags or stakes
  • A submitted utility-locating request, placed with enough lead time for the required response before ground disturbance
  • A readiness worksheet capable of recording every finding required by this chapter's procedure
  • Identification of the contact for a surveyor, engineer, landscape architect, hardscape contractor, or other qualified professional, in case this chapter's inspection identifies a condition requiring one

Common Field Fundamentals

Every hardscape project in this book, from a straight paver walkway to the Chapter 20 capstone patio, depends on the same readiness sequence: gather governing documents, verify boundaries, locate utilities, inspect for hazards, record drainage, measure the area, evaluate access and soil, identify review triggers, select compatible materials, and document a final decision. Learning that sequence here, before any excavation begins, means every later chapter starts from a site you already understand.

Stop-Work Conditions

This chapter requires the inspector to halt the readiness process, rather than issue an approval, whenever a condition falls outside what a documented plan alone can resolve. These conditions must be referred to the required qualified professional before the readiness decision is finalized.

Critical Safety Cue — Unidentified or Unmarked Utilities

Do not probe, stake, or disturb the ground in any area where a requested utility locate has not yet been completed or where a marking is ambiguous, missing, or contradicted by visible evidence such as a meter, riser, or vent. Contact the utility-locating service again and wait for a confirmed response before continuing in that area.

Code Alert — Disputed Boundaries and Prohibited Zones

Do not finalize a readiness decision for a project that crosses a disputed property line, an unverified easement, or a marked prohibited work zone. Refer the boundary question to a licensed surveyor and record the referral on the readiness worksheet.

Field Safety Cue — Slopes, Ponding, and Unstable Ground

Move carefully on wet, sloped, or previously disturbed ground during inspection. Treat soft spots, visible ponding, and slumped or cracked soil as unstable until confirmed otherwise, and do not stand on or probe an area showing active erosion or slope movement.

Measurement Cue — Record Elevations Relative to a Fixed Reference

Record every high point, low point, and transition elevation relative to one fixed reference point, such as a foundation corner or a permanent survey marker, and note that reference on the worksheet. Elevations recorded without a stated fixed reference cannot be compared reliably in later chapters.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Preliminary Material Selection

When selecting pavers, blocks, base aggregate, bedding, edge restraint, joint material, or drainage components at the preliminary stage, record the manufacturer's stated compatible-use conditions, such as vehicle-load rating or freeze-thaw exposure limits, rather than assuming a product is suitable without checking its stated limits.

E. Preparation — Gather Documents and Define the Project

  1. Obtain the site plan, property information, grading plan, drainage information, utility records, product requirements, and permit conditions. Request from the property owner, prior contractor, municipal records office, or design professional any document not already available, and note on the worksheet any document not yet obtained.
  2. Define project type, use, loading, dimensions, finish, and surrounding interfaces. Complete the Project Definition Record above using the owner's stated intent and the site plan.
  3. Verify property lines, setbacks, easements, public right-of-way, and prohibited work zones. Compare the property survey and site plan against visible property markers and record any discrepancy.
  4. Request required utility locating. Submit the locate request for the full project area with enough lead time for the required response before any ground disturbance.

F. Site Inspection — Hazards, Drainage, and Measurement

  1. Inspect the site for foundations, slabs, walks, driveways, trees, roots, irrigation, drains, wells, tanks, slopes, and buried obstructions. Record each item found within or adjacent to the project area on the site hazard inventory.
  2. Record existing high points, low points, runoff sources, discharge paths, ponding, erosion, and foundation-water risks. Use the line level or laser level and the fixed reference established in the Measurement Cue above.
  3. Measure project length, width, area, perimeter, grade change, and transition elevations. Record every measurement relative to the same fixed reference used for drainage findings.
  4. Inspect doors, gates, steps, siding, vents, weeps, fences, walls, and adjacent paving. Record required clearances and any existing condition that could be affected by the proposed project.
  5. Evaluate visible soil and evidence of previous fill or disturbance. Record color, texture, and any visible signs of prior excavation, fill placement, or grading.
  6. Probe for soft spots and unstable areas. Probe at a regular interval across the project area and record any location where the probe rod penetrates with unusually little resistance.

G. Access, Controls, and Seasonal Evaluation

  1. Identify delivery, pallet, aggregate, excavator, compactor, saw, and worker access. Record route width, overhead clearance, slope, and any obstruction affecting each type of access.
  2. Identify pedestrian, traffic, neighbor, dust, noise, and slurry controls. Record which controls will be required based on the site's location and surrounding use.
  3. Evaluate freeze-thaw exposure, shade, drainage, tree roots, and seasonal ground movement. Record conditions that may affect material selection or long-term performance.

H. Review Triggers and Stop-Work Determination

  1. Identify whether retaining-wall surcharge, step geometry, drainage connection, driveway loading, or work near foundations requires design review. Compare findings against the governing documents and record each trigger as referred, pending, or not applicable.
  2. Stop work for disputed boundaries, unidentified utilities, unstable slopes, contaminated soil, uncontrolled drainage, or structural conflicts. Record the specific condition, the required referral, and the qualified professional contacted.

I. Material Selection and Preliminary Quantities

  1. Select compatible pavers, blocks, base aggregate, bedding, edge restraint, joint material, drainage components, and accessories. Record the manufacturer's stated compatible-use conditions for each selected material against the project definition and site findings.
  2. Prepare preliminary quantity and logistics calculations. Use the recorded area measurements to estimate material quantities and record access or delivery logistics notes from Section G.

J. Deficiency Review and Final Decision

  1. Prepare a deficiency and prerequisite list. List every unresolved condition identified in Sections E through I, with the specific prerequisite required to resolve each one.
  2. Reinspect corrected conditions. Return to the site and reconfirm any condition that was corrected, marked, or resolved after the initial inspection, and record the reinspection date and result.
  3. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected hardscape-readiness decision. State the decision in writing on the worksheet, list any conditions attached to a conditional approval, and list the specific reasons for a rejected decision.

Common Site Inspection

Review every readiness plan using the same checks regardless of project type.

  • Document check: confirm every governing document listed in Section C is obtained or has a documented plan to be obtained before construction begins.
  • Utility check: confirm the locate request was submitted and a confirmed response received before any ground disturbance occurred in the affected area.
  • Boundary check: confirm property lines, setbacks, easements, right-of-way, and prohibited work zones match the property survey and site plan.
  • Drainage and measurement check: confirm every drainage finding and area measurement is recorded relative to a stated fixed reference.
  • Review-trigger check: confirm every condition identified in Section H is referred, pending, or resolved, with no unresolved trigger left off the worksheet.
  • Decision check: confirm the worksheet states one clear readiness decision, signed and dated.

Complete the Hardscape Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Readiness Decision Issued Before Utility Locate Response

Probable cause: the readiness worksheet was completed on a timeline that did not allow the utility-locating service enough lead time to respond before the inspection was closed out.
Confirmation: check the locate ticket date and response date against the worksheet's inspection date.
Correction: withdraw the readiness decision, resubmit or wait for the locate response, and reinspect any area affected by newly marked utilities before reissuing the decision.
Reinspection: repeat the utility check and the boundary check for the full project area.

Drainage Findings Recorded Without a Fixed Reference

Probable cause: high points and low points were estimated by eye or recorded relative to a temporary object instead of a fixed reference point.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for a stated fixed reference; if none is recorded, the elevations cannot be verified or compared in later chapters.
Correction: return to the site, select a permanent fixed reference such as a foundation corner or survey marker, and re-record every drainage finding and area measurement relative to it.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage and measurement check.

A Review Trigger Was Missed

Probable cause: the inspection did not compare site findings against every condition listed in Section H, or a driveway-loading, surcharge, or foundation-proximity condition was not recognized during the site visit.
Confirmation: re-walk the site with the Section H list in hand and compare each condition against the project definition and site findings.
Correction: record the missed trigger, refer it to the required qualified professional, and withhold or revise the readiness decision until the referral is resolved or pending with a stated timeline.
Reinspection: repeat the review-trigger check before reissuing the decision.

Boundary or Easement Conflict Discovered Late

Probable cause: the property survey was not compared against the proposed project footprint before measurements and material selection were completed.
Confirmation: overlay the recorded project footprint on the property survey and check for any encroachment on a property line, setback, or easement.
Correction: stop further readiness work on the affected footprint, refer the boundary question to a licensed surveyor, and revise the project definition once the boundary is resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check and, if the footprint changed, the measurement check.

Soft Spot or Unstable Area Not Investigated

Probable cause: probing was skipped or performed at too wide an interval to detect a localized soft spot.
Confirmation: re-probe the project area at a closer interval, focusing on areas with visible fill, ponding, or vegetation differences.
Correction: record any newly found soft spot on the site hazard inventory and the deficiency list, and add soil correction as a prerequisite before excavation begins in later chapters.
Reinspection: repeat the soil and fill evaluation for the affected area.

Material Selection Made Without Checking Manufacturer Limits

Probable cause: a paver, block, or drainage component was selected based on appearance or availability without checking its stated load rating, freeze-thaw rating, or other compatible-use condition.
Confirmation: review the manufacturer's product literature against the project's loading and seasonal-exposure findings from Section G.
Correction: replace the selected material with one whose manufacturer-stated limits match the project's recorded loading and exposure conditions, and update the preliminary quantity calculation.
Reinspection: repeat the material-selection review before finalizing the readiness decision.

Deficiency List Incomplete at Time of Decision

Probable cause: a finding recorded earlier in the worksheet, such as a site hazard or an access constraint, was not carried forward to the deficiency and prerequisite list.
Confirmation: cross-check every recorded finding in Sections E through I against the deficiency and prerequisite list to confirm each unresolved item appears there.
Correction: add the missing item to the deficiency list with its corrective action, and revise the readiness decision if the missing item would have changed it.
Reinspection: repeat the deficiency review before reissuing the decision.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed readiness worksheet and organized site records, not a verbal impression of the site.

  • Remove or leave in place marking flags and stakes according to the property owner's preference and the requirements of later chapters
  • Store the utility locate ticket, property survey, and any marked-up site plan with the readiness worksheet
  • File site photographs alongside their corresponding worksheet entries
  • Distribute the readiness worksheet to the property owner and any qualified professional identified in Section H
  • Retain the complete readiness plan for reference throughout Chapters 2 through 20

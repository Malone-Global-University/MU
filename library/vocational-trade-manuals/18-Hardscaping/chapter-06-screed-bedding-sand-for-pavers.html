Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to install and screed one controlled bedding-sand section over an aggregate base accepted under Chapter 5. The finished work product is one screeded bedding-sand layer at the approved thickness and slope, free of ridges, hollows, contamination, footprints, or disturbed areas, and covered with pavers before it is exposed to weather, traffic, or further handling.

You will set and support screed rails on the compacted base, place bedding sand ahead of the rails, pull a screed board along the rails to establish a uniform surface, remove the rails and fill the resulting voids, protect the finished surface from disturbance, and inspect the bedding before paver placement begins.

This chapter depends directly on Chapter 5's accepted aggregate base and feeds directly into Chapter 7's paver installation. Bedding sand is a final setting layer for the pavers above it, not a leveling layer for an uneven base below it. A base that has not been accepted under Chapter 5 must not be screeded in this chapter.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-031 — Install screed rails to the required bedding elevation and slope over an accepted base.
  • HC-032 — Place bedding sand ahead of the screed rails without segregating, contaminating, or overloading the base.
  • HC-033 — Screed bedding sand to a uniform surface using a controlled screed-board motion.
  • HC-034 — Maintain uniform bedding thickness across the full screeded area, including rail-void repair.
  • HC-035 — Avoid disturbing the screeded surface through foot traffic, wheeled equipment, or material stockpiling.
  • HC-036 — Inspect the bedding layer for ridges, hollows, contamination, and disturbed areas before paver placement.

Where This Skill Fits

Bedding sand is the layer directly beneath every paver installed in this book, from the straight walkway in Chapter 7 to the capstone patio in Chapter 20. A screeded surface that is uneven, contaminated, or disturbed before pavers are set transfers that defect directly into the finished paver surface, where it cannot be corrected without lifting the pavers and rescreeding.

The discipline of this chapter — never walking, kneeling, wheeling equipment, or stockpiling material on screeded bedding, and covering screeded areas promptly with pavers — is carried forward into every paver-installation chapter that follows. This chapter is where you learn to treat the screeded surface as a finished reference plane, not a work surface.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the approved bedding-material specification, compacted or loose thickness, screed-rail method, base elevation, slope, and work sequence
  • confirming the base is accepted, dry enough, clean, and undisturbed before screeding begins
  • coordinating drains, edges, borders, and fixed elevations before rail placement
  • setting, supporting, and checking screed rails or pipe to the required bedding elevation
  • placing bedding sand ahead of the rails and avoiding segregated, contaminated, frozen, or saturated material
  • screeding a uniform surface with a controlled screed-board motion, filling low areas and removing excess from high areas
  • removing rails, filling rail voids, and striking them flush without overcompacting the bedding
  • protecting the screeded surface from foot traffic, wheeled equipment, and stockpiled material
  • covering screeded areas promptly with pavers
  • inspecting bedding thickness and surface condition, and rescreeding any damaged section completely

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • screed bedding sand over a base that has not been accepted, checked for elevation and slope, and confirmed clean and undisturbed under Chapter 5
  • use bedding sand to correct an uneven, low, high, or improperly sloped aggregate base; a deficient base must be corrected under Chapter 5 before this chapter begins
  • walk, kneel, wheel equipment, or stockpile material on a screeded surface for any reason other than an approved spot-repair method
  • leave a screeded surface exposed and uncovered for longer than the approved sequence permits before paver placement or protective covering

Any base condition that has not been accepted under Chapter 5 must be corrected and reinspected before screeding begins.

Trade Note

A screeded bedding layer is a finished reference surface, not a working platform. Every rake pass, every rail removal, and every void fill in this chapter is judged against one standard: does the finished surface remain uniform, clean, and undisturbed until pavers are set on it. Treat any footprint, wheel track, or stockpile mark as a defect requiring correction, not a cosmetic issue.

Setup Standard

Do not set screed rails on a base that has not been accepted under Chapter 5. Reconfirm base elevation, slope, cleanliness, and dryness immediately before rail placement, even if the base was accepted on an earlier work day.

Shop Tip

Work the screed board toward pavers already installed or toward an exit path that does not cross the screeded surface. Planning the direction of work before the first rail is set avoids the need to walk back across a finished section later.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every bedding-sand installation in this book depends on the same sequence: confirm the base is accepted and ready, set rails to the correct elevation and slope, place and screed sand in a controlled motion, remove rails and fill voids without overcompacting, protect the finished surface from disturbance, and cover it promptly. Learning that sequence here means every paver-installation chapter that follows starts from a bedding layer you can trust.

Stop-Work and Rescreed Conditions

This chapter requires the installer to halt or repeat work whenever the base or the screeded surface does not meet the conditions this chapter depends on.

Critical Safety Cue — Screeding Over a Base Not Accepted Under Chapter 5

Do not set screed rails or place bedding sand over an aggregate base that has not been checked and accepted for elevation, slope, thickness, and stability under Chapter 5. Bedding sand conceals base defects rather than correcting them, and a defect concealed here reappears as settlement or unevenness in the finished paver surface.

Field Safety Cue — Do Not Disturb the Screeded Surface

Do not walk, kneel, wheel a cart, or stockpile material on screeded bedding sand. Plan material staging and work sequence so that every screeded section is approached only from an unscreeded area or from pavers already installed.

Measurement Cue — Set Rail Elevation From the Accepted Base Record

Set screed-rail elevation using the base elevation and slope recorded in the Chapter 5 acceptance record, not by eye against the surrounding grade. Check rail slope and parallel alignment with a line level or laser level before placing any bedding sand.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Bedding Material Moisture and Gradation

Use bedding sand at the gradation and moisture condition specified by the approved bedding-material specification. Do not screed sand that is frozen, saturated, contaminated, or visibly segregated, and do not substitute a different material without confirming compatibility with the specified paver and joint system.

Code Alert — Coordinate Fixed Elevations Before Screeding

Confirm that drains, edge restraints, borders, and other fixed-elevation features are coordinated with the bedding elevation before screeding begins. Screeding over an uncoordinated fixed feature creates a transition defect that cannot be corrected without lifting the affected pavers later.

D. Preparation — Review and Base Confirmation

  1. Review bedding material, compacted or loose thickness, screed-rail method, base elevation, slope, and work sequence. Confirm every value against the approved bedding-material specification and the Chapter 5 acceptance record before staging equipment.
  2. Confirm the base is accepted, dry enough, clean, and undisturbed. Reinspect the base surface immediately before rail placement, even if acceptance occurred on an earlier work day, and record the reconfirmation.
  3. Verify that drains, edges, borders, and fixed elevations are coordinated. Compare the marked locations of these features against the bedding elevation and correct any conflict before proceeding.

E. Screed-Rail Placement

  1. Set screed rails or pipes to the required bedding elevation. Calculate the rail elevation from the accepted base elevation plus the approved bedding thickness, and set each rail to that elevation.
  2. Support rails on the compacted base without creating soft spots. Use stable shim or support material where the base surface requires it, and avoid concentrating rail weight on a single small contact point.
  3. Check rail slope and parallel alignment. Use a line level or laser level to confirm the rail slope matches the accepted base slope and that rails remain parallel across the full screeded width.

F. Placing Bedding Sand

  1. Place bedding sand between and ahead of rails. Distribute sand in a controlled quantity that stays slightly above the final rail elevation, ready for the screed board.
  2. Avoid segregated, contaminated, frozen, or saturated sand. Reject any delivered or stockpiled material that shows visible segregation, contamination, ice, or excess moisture, and do not place it in the screeded area.

G. Screeding the Surface

  1. Pull the screed board along the rails using a controlled motion. Maintain a steady forward and slight side-to-side motion that strikes excess sand without gouging the surface.
  2. Maintain continuous contact with both rails. Keep the screed board seated on both rails throughout the pull to preserve the set elevation and slope.
  3. Fill low areas and rescreed. Add sand to any hollow left after the first pass and repeat the screed-board pull over that section.
  4. Remove excess material from high areas. Clear surplus sand ahead of the screed board rather than allowing it to build into a ridge.

H. Rail Removal and Void Repair

  1. Remove rails carefully. Lift each rail vertically or slide it out along its length without dragging it across the finished screeded surface.
  2. Fill rail voids with matching bedding material. Place the same bedding sand used in the screeded section into the channel left by the removed rail.
  3. Strike rail voids flush without overcompacting the bedding. Level the void fill flush with the surrounding screeded surface using a hand tool, avoiding any tamping that compresses the bedding below the surrounding elevation.

I. Protecting and Covering the Screeded Surface

  1. Do not walk, kneel, wheel, or stockpile material on screeded bedding. Approach every screeded section only from an unscreeded area or from pavers already installed, for the remainder of this chapter and throughout Chapter 7.
  2. Cover screeded areas promptly with pavers. Begin paver placement on each screeded section as soon as practical, and do not leave a screeded surface exposed beyond the approved exposure window without an approved protective covering.

J. Inspection and Correction

  1. Check bedding thickness and surface using approved spot methods. Take spot-check measurements at representative locations across the screeded area and record the results.
  2. Inspect for ridges, hollows, contamination, footprints, and disturbed areas. Visually and physically check the full screeded surface before covering it with pavers.
  3. Rescreed any damaged section completely. Remove and rescreed the full affected section rather than patching a footprint, wheel track, or hollow in place.
  4. Document area and acceptance. Record the final inspection result, spot-check measurements, and acceptance on the bedding work record before beginning Chapter 7.

Common Site Inspection

Review every screeded section using the same checks regardless of area or shape.

  • Base check: confirm the base remained accepted, dry, clean, and undisturbed immediately before screeding.
  • Rail check: confirm rail elevation and slope matched the accepted base record and rails remained parallel.
  • Material check: confirm bedding sand was free of segregation, contamination, freezing, or saturation at placement.
  • Surface check: confirm the screeded surface is free of ridges, hollows, footprints, and disturbed areas.
  • Void-repair check: confirm rail voids are filled flush without overcompaction.
  • Coverage check: confirm every screeded area was covered with pavers or an approved protective covering within the exposure window.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Screeded Surface Shows Ridges or Hollows

Probable cause: the screed board lost contact with one or both rails during the pull, or sand was placed unevenly ahead of the board.
Confirmation: sight along the screeded surface and check with a straightedge at several points across the affected section.
Correction: rescreed the affected section completely, adding or removing sand as needed and maintaining continuous rail contact through the full pull.
Reinspection: repeat the surface check for the rescreeded section before covering it with pavers.

Footprints or Wheel Tracks Found on the Screeded Surface

Probable cause: a worker or wheeled cart crossed the screeded surface instead of approaching from an unscreeded area or installed pavers.
Confirmation: visually inspect the surface for depressions matching foot or wheel patterns.
Correction: rescreed the full affected section rather than smoothing the mark in place, and revise the work sequence to eliminate the crossing.
Reinspection: repeat the surface check and confirm the revised approach route before continuing.

Rail Void Left Low or Overcompacted After Repair

Probable cause: the void fill was tamped rather than struck flush, compressing the bedding below the surrounding elevation.
Confirmation: check the rail-void line with a straightedge against the adjacent screeded surface.
Correction: remove the compacted fill, add fresh matching bedding material, and strike it flush by hand without tamping.
Reinspection: repeat the thickness and surface check along the full rail-void line.

Bedding Thickness Fails a Spot Check

Probable cause: rail elevation was set incorrectly, or the base elevation used to calculate rail elevation was not the accepted Chapter 5 record.
Confirmation: recheck the rail elevation calculation against the Chapter 5 acceptance record and remeasure rail elevation in the field.
Correction: reset rails to the correct elevation and rescreed the affected section.
Reinspection: repeat the thickness spot-check across the corrected section.

Screeded Area Left Uncovered Beyond the Exposure Window

Probable cause: paver placement fell behind the screeding pace, or weather or a work stoppage delayed coverage without an approved protective covering being installed.
Confirmation: compare the screeding completion time against the coverage time recorded on the bedding work record.
Correction: inspect the exposed section for contamination, disturbance, or moisture change; rescreed if any defect is found, or proceed to paver placement if the surface remains undisturbed and within specification.
Reinspection: repeat the full surface and thickness check before paver placement on the affected section.

Bedding Sand Contaminated or Saturated Before Placement

Probable cause: stockpiled sand was left uncovered during rain, or was stored where it could mix with adjacent soil or base material.
Confirmation: visually inspect the stockpile for color change, clumping, or visible soil mixing.
Correction: do not use the affected material; obtain replacement bedding sand meeting the approved specification.
Reinspection: confirm the replacement material's gradation and moisture condition before placement resumes.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a covered or protected screeded surface and a completed bedding work record, not an exposed sand layer left for the next work session.

  • Remove screed rails, support shims, and excess sand from the work area without dragging tools across finished screeded sections
  • Return unused bedding sand to covered, contamination-free storage
  • Store the bedding work record with the Chapter 5 base-acceptance record for reference in Chapter 7
  • Confirm every screeded section is either covered with pavers or protected with an approved covering before leaving the site
  • File photographs of the finished bedding surface alongside the inspection record

Field Terms

Bedding Elevation
The finished top-of-sand elevation before pavers are placed, set below the finished paver elevation by the paver thickness.
Screed Rail
A straight rail or pipe supported on the accepted base and set to the required bedding elevation, used to guide the screed board.
Screed Board
A straight board pulled along screed rails to strike bedding sand to a uniform surface.
Rail Void
The channel left in the bedding surface after a screed rail is removed, requiring fill and strike-off.
Screeded Surface
The finished, struck-off bedding-sand surface between rails, treated as undisturbed until covered by pavers.
Compacted or Loose Thickness
The bedding-sand thickness expressed as either the compacted result or the loose screeded depth, as specified.
Exposure Window
The approved maximum time a screeded surface may remain uncovered before it must be covered with pavers or an approved protective covering.
Segregation
The uneven separation of fine and coarse particles within bedding sand, making the material unsuitable for placement.

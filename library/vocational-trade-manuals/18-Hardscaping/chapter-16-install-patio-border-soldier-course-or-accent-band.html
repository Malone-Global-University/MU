Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to lay out and install one paver border, soldier course, or accent band integrated into an existing, stable paver field. The finished work product is one border or accent band that is dimensionally accurate, visually consistent, structurally locked into the surrounding field, and supported by an edge-restraint system, on the same bedding and base the field pavers already occupy.

You will confirm the field is stable and aligned, mark border reference lines, dry-lay representative units to distribute cuts, remove only the field units required to make room for the border, cut border and adjoining field units, install the border or soldier-course pattern to a controlled joint spacing, integrate the border with the field without creating trapped or unsupported edges, reset edge restraint, compact, fill joints, and inspect the finished border for pattern, color, joint, and edge condition.

This chapter depends on Chapter 7 or Chapter 8's finished paver field, Chapter 9's cutting procedure, and Chapter 10's edge-restraint and joint-sand procedure. Nothing in this chapter authorizes disturbing the accepted bedding layer beyond what removing border-adjacent units requires, and nothing here replaces the base or bedding work performed in Chapters 5 and 6.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-091 — Lay out the border pattern, including inside and outside reference lines, corner treatment, and curve controls.
  • HC-092 — Cut border units to the marked lines using approved silica controls and minimum stable piece size.
  • HC-093 — Install a soldier course or accent band to a controlled, consistent joint spacing.
  • HC-094 — Maintain consistent reveals, alignment, and corner treatment across the completed border.
  • HC-095 — Lock the border into the field using edge restraint, compaction, and joint-sand installation without creating a trapped or unsupported edge.
  • HC-096 — Inspect the finished border for pattern, color distribution, joint consistency, curve smoothness, edge security, and surface plane.

Where This Skill Fits

A border or accent band is a visual and structural transition, not a decorative afterthought. It defines the field's outer boundary, distributes field cuts away from high-visibility edges, and gives the edge-restraint system a clean, uniform surface to anchor against. A border installed without integrating into the field's bedding and joint plan produces a visible seam, a rocking edge course, or a restraint that has nothing solid to bear against.

This chapter builds directly on the field pavers installed in Chapter 7 or Chapter 8. It uses the same cutting procedure from Chapter 9 and the same edge-restraint and polymeric-sand procedure from Chapter 10, applied to the border condition instead of the open field. The discipline of protecting the accepted bedding layer while removing and resetting units carries forward into Chapter 18's repair work.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the approved border profile, unit type, color, pattern, width, corner treatment, curves, joints, and edge restraint
  • confirming the field pavers are stable and aligned before border work begins
  • marking border inside and outside reference lines and dry-laying representative units
  • removing only the field units required for the border while preserving the accepted bedding layer
  • cutting border and adjoining field units using the Chapter 9 procedure
  • installing a soldier course or accent-band pattern with controlled joint spacing, reveals, and corner alignment
  • integrating the border with the field without trapped or unsupported edges
  • installing or resetting edge restraint, compacting, filling joints, and activating polymeric material where used
  • inspecting the finished border for pattern, color, joint, curve, edge, and plane condition

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin border work on a field that has not passed the Chapter 7 or Chapter 8 inspection for line, bond, joints, and slope
  • disturb bedding sand beyond the area required to remove and reset border-adjacent units, or leave disturbed bedding uncovered
  • install polymeric sand on a wet or contaminated joint, or activate it outside the manufacturer's stated weather and watering conditions
  • anchor edge restraint into loose bedding sand instead of the compacted base
  • substitute this chapter's border installation for a redesign of the field pattern established in Chapter 7 or Chapter 8

Any field condition that fails inspection before border work begins must be corrected using Chapter 18's repair procedure before this chapter's work order proceeds.

Trade Note

A border is judged from a standing sightline before it is judged with a tape measure. Distribute cuts symmetrically and check the border's visual rhythm at each corner and curve before committing to compaction and jointing, since compacted and jointed units are far more difficult to reset without disturbing the surrounding field.

Setup Standard

Do not begin border layout on a field that has not passed inspection for line, bond, joints, and slope. A border built against a misaligned or unstable field will inherit and magnify that condition.

Shop Tip

Dry-lay the full border run before cutting a single unit. Small adjustments to the inside reference line at this stage are far easier than recutting units after the border has been fastened and jointed.

Prerequisites

  • A completed and accepted paver field from Chapter 7 or Chapter 8, inspected for line, bond, joints, cut quality, edge support, and slope
  • Access to the approved hardscape and border detail identifying profile, width, pattern, corner treatment, curves, joints, and edge-restraint type

Common Field Fundamentals

A border succeeds or fails on the same fundamentals as the field it surrounds: an undisturbed bedding layer, controlled joint spacing, and an edge restraint anchored into the compacted base rather than into loose sand. The border adds two additional demands: visual symmetry across the full run, and an integration detail that leaves no trapped or unsupported edge where the border meets the field.

Stop-Work Conditions

This chapter requires the installer to halt border work, rather than proceed to cutting or compaction, whenever a condition falls outside what the approved border detail alone can resolve.

Critical Safety Cue — Cutting Near an Unstable Field Edge

Do not cut or fit border units against a field edge that rocks, is unsupported, or has settled. Correct the underlying field condition using Chapter 18's repair procedure before continuing border layout in that area.

Safety Cue — Silica Controls During Border Cutting

Use the wet-cutting, extraction, and PPE controls from Chapter 9 for every border and field-adjoining cut. Do not dry-cut border units, even for a small number of pieces.

Field Safety Cue — Kneeling and Repetitive Work

Use knee protection and rotate tasks during extended border-laying work. Keep hands clear of the compactor's travel path when compacting near the finished border edge.

Measurement Cue — Distribute Cuts Symmetrically

Dry-lay the full border run and mark cut locations before removing any field unit. Adjust the inside reference line, not the cut size, to keep border cuts above the minimum stable piece size on both ends of a run.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Joint Sand and Activation

Confirm the jointing-sand product's stated joint-width range and activation weather conditions before sweeping sand into the border. Do not activate polymeric sand outside the manufacturer's stated watering pattern and quantity.

Code Alert — Accessible Route Surfaces

Where the border falls within a required accessible route, verify that joint width, surface plane, and any change in level at the border-to-field transition meet the adopted accessibility requirement before finalizing the layout.

E. Layout — Border and Pattern Control Lines

  1. Review border profile, unit type, color, pattern, width, corner treatment, curves, joints, and edge restraint. Complete the Border Definition Record above using the approved hardscape and border detail.
  2. Confirm the field pavers are stable and aligned. Review the Chapter 7 or Chapter 8 acceptance record and visually verify line, bond, and slope before marking any border line.
  3. Mark border inside and outside lines. Use a chalk line or string line referenced to the field's established pattern-control lines, not to any wall or fence that may be out of square.
  4. Dry-lay representative units. Place a sample run of border or soldier-course units along the marked lines without adhesive or fasteners.

F. Layout Confirmation and Field Preparation

  1. Adjust the layout to distribute cuts and maintain symmetry. Shift the inside reference line as needed so that cut pieces at both ends of each run meet the minimum stable size stated in the border detail.
  2. Establish straight and curved control lines. Mark tangent points and curve radii according to the approved detail before removing any field units.
  3. Remove field units only where required for the border. Lift only the units needed to make room for the border, keeping removal to the minimum footprint the layout requires.
  4. Preserve the accepted bedding layer. Cover any exposed bedding promptly and avoid walking, kneeling, or stockpiling material on it beyond what removal and resetting require.

G. Cutting and Installation

  1. Cut border and adjoining field units using Chapter 9. Follow the Chapter 9 marking, wet-cutting, and silica-control procedure for every border and field-adjoining cut.
  2. Avoid unstable slivers. Replace any cut piece that falls below the minimum stable size stated in the border detail rather than installing it.
  3. Install soldier-course or accent-band units to controlled joint spacing. Set units on the undisturbed bedding using the joint width specified in the border detail, checked at regular intervals against the control lines.
  4. Align reveals and corners. Maintain the specified reveal between adjoining border units and at every corner, verified against the dry-lay pattern from Section E.

H. Field Integration and Lock-In

  1. Integrate the border with the paver field without creating trapped or unsupported edges. Reset every field unit disturbed during border installation so each unit has full bearing and adjoining support on all sides.
  2. Install or reset edge restraint. Fasten restraint into the compacted base along the full completed border at the spacing specified in Chapter 10, with corner reinforcement where required.
  3. Compact using the approved sequence. Use the plate compactor with the protective mat specified in Chapter 10, following the required pass direction and count.
  4. Fill joints with the specified sand. Sweep joint or polymeric sand into border and adjoining field joints on a clean, dry surface.
  5. Activate polymeric material where used. Follow the manufacturer's exact watering pattern and quantity, preventing runoff, ponding, or residue on finished units.

I. Inspection and Correction

  1. Inspect pattern, color distribution, joint lines, border width, curve smoothness, edge security, and plane. Compare the completed border against the Acceptance Standards table in Section D.
  2. Correct visual drift or unstable units. Reset any unit that rocks, sits high or low, or breaks the border's pattern or color distribution before final documentation.
  3. Document the final border. Record the completed border's measurements, cut count, edge-restraint condition, joint-sand product, and inspection result on the work order.

Common Site Inspection

Review every completed border using the same checks regardless of profile or pattern.

  • Field-condition check: confirm the field passed inspection before border work began, and that no border cut was fitted against an unstable field edge.
  • Symmetry check: confirm cuts are distributed symmetrically at both ends of each run, with no piece below the minimum stable size.
  • Joint check: confirm border and field-adjoining joints match the specified width within the manufacturer's stated tolerance.
  • Integration check: confirm no trapped or unsupported edge exists where the border meets the field.
  • Restraint check: confirm edge restraint is fastened into the compacted base at the specified spacing along the full completed border.
  • Plane and jointing check: confirm the border surface is flush with the field within tolerance, and that joint sand is filled and activated without residue or washout.

Complete the Border Installation Work Order in the Inspection Checklist section below before considering the border finished.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Border Cuts End in Slivers at One or Both Ends of a Run

Probable cause: the border layout was marked directly from the field's existing edge without dry-laying and adjusting the inside reference line for symmetry.
Confirmation: measure the cut pieces at each end of the affected run against the minimum stable size stated in the border detail.
Correction: remove the affected units, shift the inside reference line to redistribute the cut, and reinstall using the Section F layout-adjustment procedure.
Reinspection: repeat the symmetry check for the corrected run.

Border Rocks or Sits Proud of the Field After Compaction

Probable cause: bedding sand was disturbed and not restored to a uniform elevation before the border unit was reset, or a unit was seated on debris left in the joint.
Confirmation: lift the affected unit, inspect the bedding surface for unevenness or debris, and check the unit's underside for damage.
Correction: restore the bedding to the field's established elevation, remove debris, and reset the unit following Section G.
Reinspection: repeat the surface-plane check across the border and adjoining field.

Trapped or Unsupported Edge at the Border-to-Field Transition

Probable cause: a field unit adjoining the border was reset without full bearing against its neighboring units, leaving a gap that allows movement.
Confirmation: inspect the border-to-field transition for gaps, and check for movement by hand pressure on units near the transition.
Correction: remove and reset the affected units with full adjoining support, adjusting joint spacing as needed to close the gap.
Reinspection: repeat the field-integration check along the full transition.

Edge Restraint Pulls Loose Under Light Load

Probable cause: fasteners were driven into bedding sand instead of the compacted base, or fastener spacing exceeded the manufacturer's specification.
Confirmation: expose the base at the affected restraint location and check fastener depth and spacing against the Chapter 10 specification.
Correction: remove and refasten the restraint into the compacted base at the specified spacing, adding corner reinforcement where required.
Reinspection: repeat the restraint check along the full completed border.

Visible Color Break Across the Border Run

Probable cause: border units were installed from a single pallet or lot without blending, producing a visible shade transition.
Confirmation: compare the affected section against the manufacturer's lot documentation and the surrounding units in daylight.
Correction: lift and reset the affected units using blended stock from multiple pallets, following the same blending practice used in the field.
Reinspection: repeat the pattern and color-distribution check for the corrected section.

Polymeric Sand Hazes or Washes Out Along the Border

Probable cause: the border was activated in weather or with a watering pattern outside the manufacturer's stated limits, or sand was not fully removed from unit faces before activation.
Confirmation: inspect for haze, loose sand, or eroded joints, and check the activation date against recorded weather conditions.
Correction: remove haze using the manufacturer's approved method, refill affected joints, and reactivate under conditions meeting the manufacturer's stated limits.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-fill and haze check after the corrected section cures.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a locked-in, jointed border and organized site records, not a set of loose units left for later fastening.

  • Remove all cutting slurry, offcuts, and excess joint sand from the border and adjoining field
  • Store any unused border units, matched by lot, for future repair work
  • Protect the completed border from traffic, rain, and irrigation through the jointing-sand cure period
  • File border photographs alongside the corresponding work-order entries
  • Retain the completed border work order for reference in Chapter 18 repairs and Chapter 19 cleaning and sealing

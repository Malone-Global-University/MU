Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to clean, restore, and where appropriate seal a completed hardscape surface without damaging the paver, block, or stone material, without stripping joint stability, and without discharging wash water, sealer, or cleaner residue into a storm drain, soil, or waterway. The finished work product is one cured hardscape surface — walkway, patio, path, wall cap, or step assembly from a prior chapter — with debris removed, joints inspected and restored, stains addressed with a compatible cleaner, and sealer applied only when the surface, weather, and cure conditions justify it in full.

You will identify the paver, joint, and sealer history of the surface; confirm cure of any polymeric sand, adhesive, or recent repair; correct any active drainage, edge, or settlement problem before cleaning proceeds; select and test the least aggressive compatible cleaning method; contain wash water; remove debris and weeds without destabilizing joints; refill and reactivate joint sand where needed; select and test a compatible sealer; apply sealer within manufacturer coverage and recoat limits; protect the surface through cure; and document the finished condition and maintenance interval.

This is the next-to-last chapter in the book. It depends directly on Chapter 10's polymeric-sand activation, Chapter 14's cap adhesive cure, and Chapter 18's repair work, and it must be completed before the Chapter 20 capstone's own cleaning, joint-sand, and sealer closeout steps.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-109 — Remove debris from a hardscape surface using a method that does not disturb joint sand or damage the paver, block, or stone finish.
  • HC-110 — Clean stains using a test patch and a cleaner compatible with the surface material and any existing sealer.
  • HC-111 — Remove weeds from joints without destabilizing the joint sand or the surrounding units.
  • HC-112 — Refresh joint sand, including refilling, compacting, and activating polymeric sand where specified.
  • HC-113 — Apply sealer where appropriate, selecting a product compatible with the material, porosity, and any previous treatment, and applying it within the manufacturer's coverage and recoat limits.
  • HC-114 — Inspect the finished maintenance condition, including color, sheen, slip resistance, adhesion, joint condition, drainage, and overspray, and document the maintenance interval.

Where This Skill Fits

Every paver, block, and stone surface in this book eventually needs cleaning, joint restoration, and possibly sealing. That work only succeeds when it is treated as a system check, not a cosmetic pass: a surface with an unresolved drainage problem, a failing edge restraint, or an unstable joint will not hold a sealer coat, and cleaning alone will not fix a structural cause of staining or weed growth.

This chapter also carries forward the book's environmental controls. Wash water, cleaner residue, and sealer overspray are subject to the same discharge restrictions as the slurry and compaction controls used throughout excavation and paver installation. The habit of containing runoff before it leaves the work area, established in Chapter 2, is the same habit that governs sealer application here.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying paver, block, stone, joint material, sealer history, efflorescence, damage, and drainage condition of one cured hardscape surface
  • confirming sufficient cure of polymeric sand, adhesives, concrete, mortar, or repairs before cleaning or sealing proceeds
  • correcting active drainage, edge, settlement, and weed-source problems before sealing
  • selecting and testing the least aggressive compatible cleaning method
  • containing wash water and protecting adjacent surfaces during cleaning
  • removing debris and weeds without destabilizing joints
  • treating stains with a tested, compatible cleaner
  • refilling and reactivating joint sand where needed
  • selecting, testing, and applying a compatible sealer within coverage and recoat limits
  • inspecting the finished condition and documenting a maintenance interval

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • seal a surface where polymeric sand, adhesive, or a recent repair has not cured for the manufacturer-stated period
  • apply sealer over an active drainage, edge-restraint, or settlement problem instead of correcting it under Chapter 13, Chapter 17, or Chapter 18
  • discharge wash water, cleaner residue, or sealer overspray to a storm drain, soil, or waterway
  • select a cleaner or sealer without first running a test patch on the actual surface material
  • apply additional sealer coats outside the manufacturer's stated recoat window

Any drainage, edge, or settlement problem discovered during this chapter's inspection must be corrected under the applicable prior chapter before cleaning or sealing proceeds.

Trade Note

Sealer does not correct a structural or drainage problem, and it will not adhere reliably over a surface that has not fully cured. Every finding in this chapter's identification and confirmation steps controls whether sealing may proceed at all. Treat a failed test patch or an uncured joint as a stop condition, not a detail to work around.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cleaning or sealing until wash-water containment is in place and every drainage, edge, or settlement deficiency identified during inspection has been corrected under its controlling chapter. A finding recorded before containment or correction must be reconfirmed once both are complete.

Shop Tip

Run every test patch — cleaner and sealer — in an inconspicuous corner of the actual surface, not on a sample board. Color, sheen, and slip response can differ between a sample and the finished field due to age, prior sealer, and efflorescence history.

Prerequisites

  • A cured hardscape surface completed under a prior chapter, with polymeric sand, adhesive, or repair materials cured for the manufacturer-stated period
  • Access to the product data sheets for the candidate cleaner, joint sand, and sealer, including compatibility, coverage rate, and recoat window
  • Confirmation that no active drainage, edge-restraint, or settlement problem remains uncorrected under Chapter 13, Chapter 17, or Chapter 18
  • Wash-water containment materials staged and ready before cleaning begins
  • A weather forecast confirming temperature, precipitation, and drying conditions fall within the manufacturer's application and cure limits

Common Field Fundamentals

Every maintenance pass in this chapter follows the same sequence regardless of surface type: identify the surface and its history, confirm cure, correct any active structural or drainage cause before cosmetic work begins, clean using the least aggressive compatible method, restore joints, and seal only when the surface and conditions justify it. Skipping the identification or cure-confirmation step is the most common cause of sealer failure, trapped moisture, or repeated staining.

Stop-Work Conditions

This chapter requires the inspector to halt cleaning or sealing, rather than proceed, whenever a condition indicates the surface is not ready or the product is not appropriate. These conditions must be resolved and reconfirmed before the maintenance sequence continues.

Critical Safety Cue — Wash-Water and Sealer Discharge

Do not allow wash water, cleaner residue, or sealer overspray to reach a storm drain, soil, catch basin, or waterway at any point during this chapter. Stop work, contain the discharge, and correct the containment method before cleaning or sealing resumes.

Code Alert — Uncured Joint or Repair Material

Do not seal a surface where polymeric sand, adhesive, or a Chapter 18 repair has not cured for the manufacturer-stated period. Verify the cure record before proceeding, and if no record exists, treat the material as uncured until confirmed.

Field Safety Cue — Wet and Freshly Sealed Surfaces

Treat a wet or freshly sealed surface as a slip hazard. Barricade the area, post signage where the surface is accessible to others, and keep foot and vehicle traffic off the surface through the full manufacturer-stated cure period.

Measurement Cue — Coverage Rate and Recoat Window

Apply sealer at the manufacturer-stated coverage rate for the specific product and surface porosity, and apply any additional coat only within the stated recoat window. A coat applied outside the recoat window, or at a rate outside the stated range, can trap moisture, produce uneven sheen, or fail to bond.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Cleaner and Sealer Compatibility

Select a cleaner and sealer based on the manufacturer's stated compatibility with the paver, block, or stone material, its porosity, its prior sealer history if any, and its intended slip and color performance. Do not select a product based on availability alone, and confirm compatibility with a test patch before full application.

E. Identification and Preparation

  1. Identify paver, block, stone, joint material, sealer, stain, efflorescence, damage, and drainage. Complete the Surface Identification Record above using visual inspection and prior chapter records.
  2. Confirm polymeric sand, adhesives, concrete, mortar, or repairs have cured sufficiently. Compare the installation or repair date against the manufacturer-stated cure period, and treat any material without a confirmed cure date as not yet cured.
  3. Correct active drainage, edge, settlement, and weed-source problems before sealing. Refer any unresolved condition to Chapter 13, Chapter 17, or Chapter 18 and complete the correction before continuing.

F. Cleaning Method and Containment

  1. Select the least aggressive compatible cleaning method. Begin with the mildest method listed on the cleaner product data sheet capable of addressing the identified condition.
  2. Protect buildings, landscaping, drains, metals, vehicles, and adjacent property. Cover or shield surfaces that could be affected by overspray, splash, or runoff before cleaning begins.
  3. Establish wash-water containment. Install the barrier or collection method confirmed under Prerequisites before any water or cleaner contacts the surface.
  4. Remove loose debris with vacuuming, low-dust sweeping, or controlled rinsing. Select the method that does not disturb joint sand or abrade the finish.
  5. Remove weeds manually or using an approved method without destabilizing joints. Extract weeds at the root where practical rather than cutting at the surface, and avoid a method that erodes surrounding joint sand.
  6. Treat stains using a test patch and compatible cleaner. Apply the cleaner to an inconspicuous test patch first, confirm color and surface response, then proceed to the identified stain areas.
  7. Avoid excessive pressure that removes joint sand or damages surfaces. Keep any pressure-washing equipment at the manufacturer-recommended distance and pressure setting for the material.
  8. Rinse and allow the surface to dry. Rinse cleaner residue fully within the contained area and allow the surface to reach the moisture condition required before joint work or sealing.

G. Joint Restoration

  1. Inspect joint depth and stability. Record any joint below the specified fill depth or showing loose, washed, or eroded sand.
  2. Refill joints with approved sand. Use the specified joint-sand product and sweep it into clean, dry joints to the specified depth.
  3. Compact and activate polymeric sand where specified. Compact using the approved protective mat and pass count, then activate exactly according to the joint-sand product data sheet.
  4. Allow joint material to cure. Protect the surface from traffic, rain, and irrigation through the manufacturer-stated joint-sand cure period before sealing.

H. Sealer Selection and Application

  1. Select sealer based on material, porosity, slip, color, moisture, and previous treatments. Compare the candidate sealer's product data sheet against the Surface Identification Record.
  2. Apply a test area. Apply the selected sealer to an inconspicuous test patch and confirm color, sheen, and slip response before full application.
  3. Apply sealer at the specified coverage. Apply using the method and coverage rate stated on the product data sheet, working in the sequence that avoids trapping the applicator on a wet edge.
  4. Avoid puddles, roller lines, overspray, and blocked drainage. Maintain an even film, work sealer out of low points before it sets, and keep overspray off adjacent surfaces and drainage components.
  5. Apply additional coats only within the recoat window. Confirm the elapsed time against the product data sheet before applying any second or subsequent coat.
  6. Protect from rain, irrigation, traffic, and contaminants through cure. Barricade the surface and post signage where accessible to others for the full manufacturer-stated cure period.

I. Final Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect color, sheen, slip, adhesion, haze, joint condition, drainage, and overspray. Compare the finished surface against the Acceptance Standards table and record every finding.
  2. Document cleaning, joint repair, sealer, weather, cure, and maintenance interval. Complete the Hardscape Maintenance Worksheet with every product, method, weather condition, and the recommended maintenance interval, then sign and date the record.

Common Site Inspection

Review every completed maintenance project using the same checks regardless of surface type.

  • Cure check: confirm every polymeric sand, adhesive, or repair material affected by this chapter's work had cured for the manufacturer-stated period before cleaning or sealing began.
  • Deficiency check: confirm no active drainage, edge-restraint, settlement, or weed-source problem remained uncorrected at the time sealing began.
  • Containment check: confirm wash water, cleaner residue, and sealer overspray were contained throughout the sequence.
  • Test-patch check: confirm both the cleaner and the sealer were tested on the actual surface before full application.
  • Joint check: confirm joints are filled to specified depth, compacted, and activated where polymeric sand was used.
  • Sealer check: confirm coverage rate, recoat window, and cure protection were followed and documented.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Sealer Applied Over an Uncured Joint or Repair

Probable cause: the cure record for polymeric sand, adhesive, or a Chapter 18 repair was not checked, or sealing proceeded on an assumed rather than confirmed cure date.
Confirmation: review the maintenance worksheet for a stated cure date and compare it against the manufacturer's stated cure period.
Correction: stop further sealer application, allow the affected material to reach full cure, and reinspect before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the cure-confirmation step and the sealer test-patch step for the affected area.

Wash Water or Overspray Reached a Drain or Adjacent Surface

Probable cause: containment was not installed before cleaning or sealing began, or the containment method did not match the site's slope and drainage layout.
Confirmation: inspect the discharge path and adjacent surfaces for residue, staining, or runoff evidence.
Correction: stop work, clean any affected drain, soil, or adjacent surface according to the product data sheet, and revise the containment method before resuming.
Reinspection: repeat the containment check for the full work area before continuing.

Cleaner or Sealer Applied Without a Test Patch

Probable cause: the full-area application began before a test patch confirmed compatibility, color, or slip response.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for a recorded test-patch location and result; if none is recorded, compatibility has not been verified.
Correction: stop application, run the test patch in an inconspicuous area, and confirm the result before continuing or before selecting an alternate product.
Reinspection: repeat the test-patch check before resuming the affected step.

Joint Sand Stripped During Cleaning

Probable cause: pressure-washing distance or pressure setting exceeded the manufacturer's recommendation for the material, or a wide-angle nozzle was used too close to the joints.
Confirmation: inspect joint depth across the cleaned area and compare against the specified fill depth.
Correction: refill and reactivate joints under Section G before proceeding to sealer application, and adjust the cleaning distance or pressure setting for any remaining area.
Reinspection: repeat the joint-restoration check for the full cleaned area.

Sealer Coat Shows Puddling, Roller Lines, or Uneven Sheen

Probable cause: sealer was applied outside the stated coverage rate, was not worked out of low points before setting, or a second coat was applied outside the recoat window.
Confirmation: inspect the surface in raking light for visible puddled areas, ridges, or sheen variation, and compare application records against the product data sheet.
Correction: follow the manufacturer's stated correction method for the specific defect, which may include light re-application within the recoat window or, for a fully cured film outside that window, mechanical or chemical removal and reapplication under manufacturer guidance.
Reinspection: repeat the final inspection for color, sheen, and adhesion across the corrected area.

Active Drainage or Edge Problem Discovered During Cleaning

Probable cause: a settlement, edge-restraint, or drainage deficiency was not identified, or was identified but not corrected, before cleaning began.
Confirmation: re-walk the surface with the Section E identification steps in hand and compare each finding against the Chapter 13, Chapter 17, and Chapter 18 records.
Correction: stop the maintenance sequence, refer the condition to the controlling chapter for correction, and do not proceed to sealing until the correction is complete and documented.
Reinspection: repeat the deficiency-correction check before resuming Section F.

Maintenance Interval Not Documented

Probable cause: the worksheet was closed out before the sealer or joint-sand product's recommended maintenance interval was recorded.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for a stated maintenance interval; if none is recorded, the next required service date cannot be tracked.
Correction: obtain the recommended interval from the product data sheet and site conditions, and record it on the worksheet before closeout.
Reinspection: repeat the documentation check before final sign-off.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed maintenance worksheet and a fully documented cleaning and sealing record, not a verbal impression of the finished surface.

  • Dispose of contained wash water, cleaner residue, and sealer waste according to the product data sheet and local requirements
  • Clean and store sealer applicators, joint brushes, and containment barriers for reuse
  • Remove barricades and signage only after the manufacturer-stated cure period has elapsed
  • Store the cleaner and sealer product data sheets, test-patch records, and cure records with the maintenance worksheet
  • Retain the complete maintenance record for reference in the Chapter 20 capstone closeout

Field Terms

Test Patch
A small, inconspicuous area of the actual surface used to confirm cleaner or sealer compatibility before full application.
Recoat Window
The manufacturer-stated time range within which an additional sealer coat may be applied over a previous coat.
Coverage Rate
The manufacturer-stated sealer quantity per unit area required for the intended film.
Efflorescence
A white mineral deposit that can form on masonry and concrete as moisture carries soluble salts to the surface.
Wash-Water Containment
The barrier or collection method used to keep wash water and residue from reaching a storm drain, soil, or waterway.
Maintenance Interval
The documented recommended time before the next cleaning, joint-sand refresh, or resealing.
Activation
The specified watering process that bonds polymeric sand within paver joints.
Cure Period
The manufacturer-stated time required for a joint material, adhesive, or sealer to reach its stable, load- or traffic-ready condition.
Haze
A visible surface residue, typically from sealer or cleaner, left on the paver face after application or cleaning.
Least Aggressive Compatible Method
The mildest cleaning method listed on the product data sheet capable of addressing the identified stain or debris condition.

Performance Check

With cleaning, joint restoration, and sealing complete, review the finished maintenance record against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm that every cure period was met before sealing, every drainage or edge deficiency was corrected before sealing, wash water and overspray were fully contained, both cleaner and sealer were tested on the actual surface, joints are filled and stable, sealer coverage and recoat timing match the product data sheet, and the maintenance interval is recorded. If any item is incomplete, correct it and reinspect the affected step before signing off and beginning the Chapter 20 capstone.

Project Closeout

You have completed the nineteenth chapter in the hardscaping sequence: cleaning, sealing, and maintaining hardscape surfaces. This chapter required you to remove debris without disturbing joint sand or the surface finish (HC-109), clean stains with a tested compatible cleaner (HC-110), remove weeds without destabilizing joints (HC-111), refresh joint sand including activation where specified (HC-112), apply sealer where appropriate within manufacturer coverage and recoat limits (HC-113), and inspect and document the finished maintenance condition (HC-114).

You now have a cleaned, joint-restored, and where appropriate sealed hardscape surface with a documented maintenance interval. That record carries directly into the Chapter 20 capstone, which must pass its own layout, excavation, subgrade, base, bedding, paver, drainage, edge, joint, compaction, and final acceptance inspections, including the cleaning, joint-sand, and sealer closeout steps built on this exact procedure.

Retain the completed Hardscape Maintenance Worksheet, cleaner and sealer product data sheets, test-patch records, and cure records. You will reference this exact maintenance procedure for the Chapter 20 capstone closeout.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build a small outdoor step or two-step assembly connecting two finished elevations, using block, stone, or paver components with verified rise, run, support, and drainage. The finished work product is one step or two-step assembly built from the approved step system, with uniform riser height, uniform tread depth, full unit support, controlled tread slope, and a documented final inspection.

You will confirm the top and bottom finished elevations, calculate uniform riser height and tread depth from the approved design, lay out and excavate the step footprint, prepare a compacted base, set riser units to line and level, install adhesive, pins, or connectors as specified, place and compact infill, install tread base and tread units with full bearing, maintain drainage slope on every tread, and inspect the finished assembly for geometry, support, and slip resistance before it is opened to use.

This chapter depends directly on Chapter 1's readiness plan for the top and bottom elevations and the step's relationship to doors, gates, and walkways, on Chapter 4's excavation and compaction procedure for the step base, and on Chapter 9's cutting procedure for any riser, tread, or cap unit that requires trimming. The step assembly built here may connect to the paver walkway or patio built in Chapters 7 and 8, and it is a candidate element for the Chapter 20 capstone project.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-085 — Lay out stair rise and run, including total rise, riser count, tread depth, and landing dimensions.
  • HC-086 — Prepare the step base, including excavation, subgrade compaction, and aggregate base installation.
  • HC-087 — Set riser units to line, level, elevation, and full support, including required adhesive, pins, or connectors.
  • HC-088 — Install the tread surface with full bearing, consistent nosing, and approved drainage slope.
  • HC-089 — Maintain consistent step geometry, including uniform rise, uniform run, and verified transitions.
  • HC-090 — Inspect step safety, including geometry, support, slip resistance, and code triggers for guards or handrails.

Where This Skill Fits

Outdoor steps are one of the few hardscape elements where a small geometry error becomes an immediate trip hazard rather than a cosmetic flaw. A riser that varies by even a small amount from its neighbors, or a tread that slopes the wrong direction, creates a fall risk every time the steps are used. This chapter treats uniform rise and run as a pass/fail condition, not a preference, and requires the learner to verify that geometry before any riser unit is permanently set.

This chapter also introduces the point at which a hardscape project may trigger a code-required guard or handrail. Recognizing that trigger — and referring it rather than improvising a solution — is the same stop-work discipline established in Chapter 1 and carried through the retaining-wall work in Chapters 12 through 14.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing total rise, riser count, tread depth, width, landing, nosing, drainage, slip, handrail, guard, lighting, and permit requirements from the approved step design
  • verifying top and bottom finished elevations and calculating uniform riser height and tread depth
  • locating utilities and verifying clearances to doors, gates, walls, and walkways
  • laying out the step footprint, excavating, and preparing a compacted base
  • setting riser units to line, level, elevation, and full support, with required adhesive, pins, or connectors
  • placing and compacting infill behind risers and installing the tread base and tread units
  • maintaining uniform finished rise and run and approved tread drainage slope
  • barricading the steps during cure or stabilization and inspecting the finished assembly before it is opened to use

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with a step design if the calculated riser height or tread depth cannot meet the approved design's required limits; the design must be corrected before construction continues
  • build a step assembly requiring a guard, handrail, or structural design beyond the approved practice scope without referring the condition to the required qualified professional
  • invent a riser height, tread depth, landing dimension, or drainage slope not supplied by the approved step design or adopted code
  • open a completed step assembly to foot traffic before adhesive cure, connector installation, and the final safety inspection in this chapter are complete

Any step geometry, support, or code condition that fails inspection in this chapter must be corrected and reinspected before the assembly is opened to use.

Trade Note

Every riser in a flight of steps must be within the same small tolerance of every other riser, and every tread must be within the same small tolerance of every other tread. A single riser that is taller or shorter than its neighbors is a documented cause of falls, even when every other part of the project is built well. Verify uniformity before setting any unit permanently.

Setup Standard

Do not proceed with construction if the calculated riser height or tread depth falls outside the approved design's required limits. Correct the design before excavation continues, and do not average an out-of-tolerance riser into the run to make the numbers work.

Shop Tip

Cut and set a story pole marked with every riser elevation before excavation begins. Checking each course against the story pole, rather than re-measuring from the bottom every time, is faster and catches a drifting riser height immediately.

Reference Conventions

Stop-Work Conditions

This chapter requires the builder to halt construction, rather than proceed with an out-of-tolerance or unresolved condition, whenever the following occur.

Critical Safety Cue — Riser Height or Tread Depth Cannot Meet Required Limits

Do not proceed with construction if the calculated riser height or tread depth falls outside the approved design's required limits. Recalculate against the confirmed total rise and approved riser count, and if the geometry still cannot meet the required limits, refer the design to the required qualified professional before continuing.

Code Alert — Handrail or Guard Trigger

Verify whether the total rise, riser count, or adjacent drop-off requires a handrail or guard under the approved design and adopted code. Do not open a step assembly meeting a handrail or guard trigger to use until the required handrail or guard is installed or the condition is otherwise resolved by the required qualified professional.

Field Safety Cue — Barricading During Cure

Barricade the steps and post warning signage for the full duration of adhesive cure or connector stabilization. Do not permit foot traffic on a riser or tread unit before the manufacturer-stated cure time has elapsed.

Measurement Cue — Verify Uniform Rise and Run Before Setting Units Permanently

Dry-fit or story-pole every riser and tread location before applying adhesive or installing pins or connectors. Confirm every riser height and every tread depth is within the approved design's stated tolerance of every other riser or tread before any unit is permanently set.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Adhesive, Pin, and Connector Requirements

Use only the adhesive, pin, or connector system specified for the selected riser and tread units, applied in the specified bead pattern, quantity, or spacing, and cured for the manufacturer-stated time before the steps are opened to use.

E. Preparation — Confirm Elevations and Calculate Geometry

  1. Review total rise, number of risers, tread depth, width, landing, nosing, drainage, slip, handrail, guard, and lighting requirements. Record each value from the approved step design on the Step Geometry Record.
  2. Confirm the approved step system and support detail. Verify the selected riser and tread units and their manufacturer-specified connection method match the approved design.
  3. Verify top and bottom finished elevations. Measure both elevations in the field against the fixed reference established in the Chapter 1 readiness plan and record the confirmed total rise.
  4. Calculate uniform riser height and tread depth from the approved design. Divide the confirmed total rise by the approved number of risers, and record the resulting riser height alongside the approved tread depth.
  5. Do not proceed if geometry cannot meet the required limits. If the calculated riser height or the approved tread depth falls outside the approved design's stated tolerance, stop and refer the design to the required qualified professional before continuing.

F. Layout and Base Preparation

  1. Locate utilities and verify door, gate, wall, and walkway clearances. Confirm the step footprint does not conflict with a marked utility or a required clearance recorded in the Chapter 1 readiness plan.
  2. Lay out the step footprint. Mark the footprint boundaries, riser lines, and landing area using the confirmed geometry and reference lines from Chapter 3 where the steps connect to an existing walkway or patio layout.
  3. Excavate and prepare the approved base. Excavate to the approved depth using the Chapter 4 procedure, removing organics, roots, soft soil, and debris from the step footprint.
  4. Compact subgrade and aggregate in lifts. Compact the subgrade, then place and compact aggregate base material in approved lifts to the specified thickness and elevation for the first riser.

G. Riser and Tread Construction

  1. Set the first riser units to line, level, elevation, and full support. Position the first riser course on the compacted base at the calculated riser-height elevation, checking line and level continuously.
  2. Install specified adhesive, pins, or connectors. Apply the manufacturer-specified connection method for the riser units before infill placement.
  3. Place and compact infill behind the riser. Place the approved infill aggregate behind the riser in controlled lifts and compact each lift without displacing the riser.
  4. Install tread base or units with full support. Set the tread base or tread units on the compacted infill and riser bearing surface, confirming full contact with no unsupported edges.
  5. Maintain approved tread slope for drainage. Check the tread slope against the approved design and adjust bedding or base support until the slope sheds water away from the riser above.
  6. Set the next riser where included. Repeat the riser-setting procedure for each additional riser in the flight, checking each course against the story pole.
  7. Maintain uniform finished rise and run. Compare each completed riser height and tread depth against the Step Geometry Record and correct any unit that falls outside the stated tolerance before continuing.
  8. Install tread caps, pavers, or stone with secure bearing and consistent nosing. Set the finished tread surface using the specified adhesive or bedding method, maintaining consistent nosing at every step.
  9. Preserve movement and drainage details. Maintain any specified joint, movement, or drainage detail between tread units and between the step assembly and adjoining hardscape.

H. Cure, Code Triggers, and Final Inspection

  1. Barricade the steps during adhesive cure or stabilization. Install barricades and warning signage and restrict access until the manufacturer-stated cure time has elapsed.
  2. Inspect geometry, level, slope, nosing, support, slip, edge condition, guard or handrail trigger, and transitions. Complete the Step Safety Inspection Checklist against the Acceptance Standards table.
  3. Correct unsafe conditions before opening to use. Correct any riser, tread, support, drainage, or code-trigger deficiency identified during inspection, and reinspect the corrected condition.
  4. Document final measurements. Record final riser heights, tread depths, slope readings, connection method, cure time, and inspection results on the worksheet.

Common Site Inspection

Review every completed step assembly using the same checks regardless of riser or tread material.

  • Geometry check: confirm every riser height and every tread depth is within the approved design's stated tolerance of every other riser or tread in the flight.
  • Support check: confirm every riser and tread unit is level, fully supported, and free of rocking.
  • Drainage check: confirm every tread sheds water per the approved slope with no ponding against the riser above.
  • Connection check: confirm adhesive, pins, or connectors were installed per manufacturer instructions and cured for the stated time before the steps were opened to use.
  • Code-trigger check: confirm every handrail, guard, or lighting trigger identified in Section H is referred, pending, or resolved.
  • Documentation check: confirm final measurements and inspection results are recorded and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Riser Height Drifts Across the Flight

Probable cause: the confirmed total rise was not divided evenly across the approved riser count, or an intermediate riser was set from the previous riser's top instead of from the story pole.
Confirmation: measure every riser height against the story pole and compare each to the calculated riser height.
Correction: reset the out-of-tolerance riser using the story pole elevation, correcting infill and tread support as needed.
Reinspection: repeat the geometry check for the full flight.

Tread Slopes Toward the Riser Below

Probable cause: the tread base or bedding was not checked for slope before the tread unit was set, or the tread unit shifted during setting.
Confirmation: check tread slope with a level against the approved design's drainage direction.
Correction: lift the tread unit, correct the base or bedding slope, and reset the tread with the required drainage direction confirmed before final seating.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check on the corrected tread and the treads immediately above and below it.

Riser Rocks or Shifts After Infill Placement

Probable cause: infill was compacted in a single thick lift, displacing the riser, or the manufacturer-specified adhesive, pin, or connector was omitted or under-cured before infill was placed.
Confirmation: inspect the riser base for movement and check the connection method against manufacturer instructions.
Correction: remove infill, correct or reinstall the riser connection per manufacturer instructions, and replace infill in controlled lifts with compaction that does not contact the riser directly.
Reinspection: repeat the support check for the corrected riser and any tread it carries.

Tread Unit Lacks Full Bearing

Probable cause: the tread base or riser bearing surface was not brought to a consistent elevation before the tread unit was set, leaving an unsupported edge or corner.
Confirmation: tap the tread surface and listen for a hollow sound, or check for visible gaps at the tread edges and corners.
Correction: remove the tread unit, correct the base or bedding to provide full contact, and reset the tread with the required nosing and slope confirmed before final seating.
Reinspection: repeat the support check on the corrected tread.

Handrail or Guard Trigger Not Evaluated

Probable cause: the total rise, riser count, or adjacent drop-off was not compared against the applicable code trigger before construction began.
Confirmation: review the completed assembly's total rise, riser count, and adjacent conditions against adopted code.
Correction: refer the condition to the required qualified professional and do not open the assembly to use until the trigger is resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the code-trigger check before reissuing the final inspection.

Steps Opened to Use Before Cure Complete

Probable cause: barricades were removed or foot traffic was permitted before the manufacturer-stated adhesive or connector cure time had elapsed.
Confirmation: check the recorded cure start time against the manufacturer's stated cure duration and the date the barricades were removed.
Correction: reinstall barricades, inspect every connection for movement or damage caused by premature use, and correct any disturbed unit before allowing use again.
Reinspection: repeat the connection check and the support check for the full flight.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed step-safety inspection and organized construction records, not a verbal impression of the finished assembly.

  • Remove barricades and warning signage only after cure time has elapsed and the final inspection is signed
  • Clean adhesive residue, dust, and debris from riser faces, tread surfaces, and adjacent hardscape
  • Store the approved step design, manufacturer instructions, and any code-trigger referral with the completed worksheet
  • File site photographs alongside their corresponding worksheet entries
  • Retain the completed Step Safety Inspection Checklist for reference in Chapter 20

Field Terms

Total Rise
The vertical distance from the bottom finished elevation to the top finished elevation.
Riser Height
The vertical face height of one step, calculated by dividing total rise by the approved riser count.
Tread Depth
The horizontal walking surface depth of one step, as stated by the approved design.
Nosing
The projection of the tread edge beyond the riser face below it.
Landing
A level platform at the top, bottom, or intermediate point of the stair.
Uniform Rise and Run
The requirement that every riser height and every tread depth in a flight remain within the approved design's stated tolerance of every other riser or tread.
Handrail/Guard Trigger
A code-defined rise, riser count, or drop-off condition that requires a handrail or guard.
Infill
The approved aggregate placed and compacted behind a riser to support the tread above it.
Story Pole
A marked reference pole used to check each riser elevation during construction.
Cure Time
The manufacturer-stated period an adhesive or connector must stabilize before the connection carries load or foot traffic.

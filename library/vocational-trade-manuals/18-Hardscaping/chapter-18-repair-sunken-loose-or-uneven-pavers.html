Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to diagnose and repair one localized paver failure — a sunken area, a loose or rocking unit, or an uneven section — by correcting the underlying base, bedding, edge, or drainage deficiency that caused it, rather than lifting and resetting pavers over an uncorrected fault. The finished work product is one repaired paver section with the original cause identified and corrected, base and bedding rebuilt to the standards established earlier in this book, pavers reinstalled in the original or approved pattern, and the repair inspected for evenness, slope, rocking, edge support, and drainage.

You will record the defect and its history, mark the full affected area, identify the probable cause, remove and stack the affected pavers, expose and inspect the base, correct the cause, rebuild base and bedding, reinstall the pavers, restore edge restraint and joints, compact the repair, and inspect the finished section against the same standards used for new installation.

This chapter depends on the excavation and compaction procedure from Chapter 4, the aggregate-base procedure from Chapter 5, the bedding-screed procedure from Chapter 6, the paver-cutting controls from Chapter 9, the edge-restraint and polymeric-sand procedure from Chapter 10, and the drainage procedures from Chapters 13 and 17. A repair that skips any of those underlying procedures is a cosmetic patch, not a repair, and is likely to fail again under the same conditions that caused the original defect.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-103 — Remove affected pavers, including joint-sand removal, extraction, and pattern-ordered stacking, without damaging adjacent stable units.
  • HC-104 — Correct base failure, including diagnosis of the probable cause, excavation of failed material, and installation of approved base aggregate in compacted lifts.
  • HC-105 — Rescreed the bedding layer to the same thickness, slope, and undisturbed-surface standard required for new installation.
  • HC-106 — Reinstall pavers in the original or approved pattern, replacing damaged units and maintaining consistent joints.
  • HC-107 — Recompact the repair area using the approved protective mat and pass sequence, and refill and activate joint material.
  • HC-108 — Inspect surface evenness, slope, rocking, pattern match, edge support, and drainage, and document the cause and correction.

Where This Skill Fits

Every paver installation in this book — the Chapter 7 walkway, the Chapter 8 patio field, the Chapter 16 border, and the Chapter 20 capstone — can develop a localized failure after installation from settlement, soft subgrade, washout, root movement, edge failure, a utility trench, or frost action. This chapter is where you learn to trace a visible symptom, such as a sunken paver or a rocking unit, back to its underlying cause in the base, bedding, edge, or drainage system, and correct that cause before any paver is reset.

The stop-work discipline carried from Chapter 1 through this book applies directly here: a repair that proceeds without understanding the cause risks concealing a soil-movement, utility, or drainage problem that will resurface, often in a wider area, after the visible symptom is patched over.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • diagnosing one localized paver defect caused by bedding, base, edge, drainage, or joint deficiency
  • marking the full affected area beyond visibly displaced units
  • removing joint sand, extracting pavers, and stacking them in pattern order for reinstallation
  • exposing and inspecting the base for thickness, contamination, erosion, compaction, moisture, and edge support
  • excavating failed material and correcting the identified drainage or edge-restraint cause
  • installing approved base aggregate in compacted lifts and rescreeding bedding sand
  • reinstalling pavers, replacing damaged units, and restoring edge restraint and joint sand
  • recompacting the repair and inspecting evenness, slope, rocking, pattern, edge support, and drainage

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • lift and reset pavers over a base, bedding, edge, or drainage deficiency without correcting that deficiency first
  • proceed with repair for major soil movement, utility failure, structural settlement, or a widespread drainage problem; these conditions require the stop-work referral described in Section H below
  • substitute a different paver, base, bedding, or joint product than the one identified as compatible with the original installation, unless the original product is unavailable and a documented compatible substitute is selected
  • compact directly against a freshly reinstalled edge restraint before its fasteners have full bearing in compacted base, or activate polymeric sand outside the manufacturer's weather and moisture window

Any condition identified during this chapter's diagnosis that exceeds a localized repair — widespread settlement, an active utility problem, or a drainage system failure affecting more than the marked repair area — must be referred to the required qualified professional before repair work continues.

Trade Note

A paver that has sunk, rocked, or gone uneven is a symptom, not the problem. The problem is almost always underneath: a soft or eroded base, a failed edge restraint, a blocked or reversed drainage path, or joint sand that migrated out through an underlying gap. Treat every repair as a diagnostic procedure first and a reinstallation procedure second.

Required PPE for this project: gloves, closed-toe footwear, and eye protection during extraction and excavation; knee protection during kneeling work; hearing and respiratory protection matching Chapter 9 controls if any cutting or splitting is required for replacement units.

Setup Standard

Do not begin extraction until the full affected area is marked and the probable cause is recorded. Extracting pavers before the area is fully marked risks missing adjoining units that are already failing but not yet visibly displaced.

Shop Tip

Photograph the pattern and number each paver as it is removed, in the order and orientation it occupied. Reinstallation is faster and joint lines stay consistent when pavers return to their original position rather than being reset from a mixed pile.

Prerequisites

  • Completion of Chapter 4 (excavation and subgrade), Chapter 5 (aggregate base), Chapter 6 (bedding screed), Chapter 9 (cutting), and Chapter 10 (edge restraint and polymeric sand), or equivalent demonstrated competency in each
  • Access to the original installation or readiness records for the affected area, including base thickness, bedding thickness, slope, edge-restraint type, and joint material, or a documented plan to determine each by inspection where records are unavailable

Common Field Fundamentals

Every paver repair in this book follows the same sequence: record the defect, mark the full affected area, identify the probable cause, remove pavers in pattern order, expose and inspect the base, correct the cause, rebuild base and bedding, reinstall pavers, restore edge restraint and joints, recompact, and inspect the finished section. Skipping the diagnostic steps to move directly to reinstallation is the most common cause of repeat failure in paver repair work.

Stop-Work Conditions

This chapter requires the repair to halt, rather than proceed to reinstallation, whenever diagnosis reveals a condition beyond a localized base, bedding, edge, or joint deficiency. These conditions must be referred to the required qualified professional before repair work continues.

Critical Safety Cue — Major Soil Movement or Utility Failure

Stop work immediately if excavation exposes a damaged or leaking utility line, active groundwater intrusion, or soil movement extending beyond the marked affected area. Do not backfill or conceal the condition. Contact the utility owner or a qualified professional before continuing.

Code Alert — Structural Settlement Near Foundations or Drainage Structures

Do not treat settlement adjacent to a foundation, retaining wall, drain structure, or utility structure as an ordinary paver repair. Refer the condition to a geotechnical engineer or the design professional of record and record the referral on the repair worksheet.

Field Safety Cue — Open Excavation and Uneven Footing

Barricade or cover the open excavation during the repair whenever the area is accessible to pedestrians, pets, or vehicles. Maintain stable footing around the excavated edge, and do not stand or kneel on unsupported base or bedding.

Measurement Cue — Match the Original Specification

Record the original base thickness, bedding thickness, and slope from the installation or readiness records before excavating, and rebuild the repair to those same values. A repair built to a different thickness or slope than the surrounding field will not achieve the required surface plane match.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Joint Sand and Polymeric Activation

Confirm the joint sand or polymeric sand product used in the repair matches or is documented as compatible with the surrounding field's original product, and activate polymeric sand using the exact watering pattern and quantity specified for that product, following the Chapter 10 procedure.

E. Diagnosis — Record the Defect and Identify the Cause

  1. Record the defect, location, settlement, rocking, heave, water, traffic, edge condition, and history. Complete the Defect Record above and photograph the visible condition from at least two angles.
  2. Mark the full affected area beyond visibly displaced units. Probe and visually inspect adjoining pavers for early rocking or shifting, and extend the marked boundary to include any unit showing early signs of the same defect.
  3. Identify the likely cause as base settlement, soft subgrade, washout, root movement, edge failure, pipe trench, frost, or unresolved. Compare visible evidence, such as nearby erosion, root growth, a known trench location, or edge-restraint displacement, against the recorded history.
  4. Stop work for major soil movement, utility failure, structural settlement, or widespread drainage problems. Apply the Critical Safety Cue and Code Alert above, and record the referral if a stop-work condition is present.

F. Removal — Extract Pavers and Expose the Base

  1. Photograph the pattern and number pavers for reinstallation. Mark each paver's position and orientation on the photograph and, where practical, on the unit itself with a temporary marking crayon.
  2. Remove joint sand from the affected area. Use the joint-sand removal tool and stiff broom, working from the marked boundary inward.
  3. Lift pavers using approved extraction tools. Use the paver puller, flat bars, or suction lifter, keeping extraction tools clear of adjacent stable units.
  4. Stack units in pattern order. Stack on a stable, clean surface away from the excavation, preserving the numbered sequence recorded above.
  5. Remove bedding sand to expose the base. Remove bedding sand across the full marked affected area, not only the area directly under displaced pavers.

G. Base Inspection and Correction

  1. Inspect base thickness, contamination, erosion, compaction, moisture, and edge support. Compare findings against the original specification recorded in Section C, and record the deviation.
  2. Excavate failed material to stable support. Remove soft, contaminated, eroded, or uncompacted base material until stable, supportive material is reached.
  3. Correct drainage or edge-restraint causes. If diagnosis in Section E identified a drainage or edge-restraint cause, correct it now using the Chapter 13 and Chapter 10 procedures before rebuilding the base.

H. Base and Bedding Rebuild

  1. Install approved base aggregate in compacted lifts. Follow the Chapter 5 lift-thickness and compaction procedure, using the material specified in Section B.
  2. Verify elevation and slope. Check the rebuilt base against the original specification and the surrounding accepted base using a straightedge or laser level.
  3. Install and screed new bedding material. Follow the Chapter 6 screed procedure, matching the original bedding thickness and slope.

I. Reinstallation

  1. Reinstall pavers in the original or approved pattern. Return numbered units to their original position and orientation from Section F.
  2. Replace damaged units. Substitute any cracked, chipped, or unstable unit with a matching replacement from Section B.
  3. Check joints and surface plane. Verify joint width matches the surrounding field and the surface plane aligns with adjoining undisturbed pavers within the Section D tolerance.
  4. Reinstall or repair edge restraint. Restore edge restraint per the Chapter 10 procedure wherever it was disturbed or identified as a cause in Section G.

J. Compaction, Joints, and Final Inspection

  1. Compact the repair using the approved mat and passes. Follow the Chapter 10 compaction sequence, using a protective mat over the repaired pavers.
  2. Refill joints and activate polymeric sand where used. Follow the Chapter 10 joint-sand and activation procedure, confirming weather and moisture conditions before activation.
  3. Inspect evenness, slope, rocking, pattern, edge support, and drainage. Check every item in the Section D acceptance standards table.
  4. Recheck after weather or use where specified. Where the original defect involved drainage or traffic loading, schedule and record a follow-up inspection after the next significant rain or period of use.
  5. Document cause and correction. Complete the repair worksheet with the recorded cause, correction performed, materials used, and final inspection result.

Common Site Inspection

Review every completed repair using the same checks regardless of the original cause.

  • Cause check: confirm a probable cause is recorded and, where a stop-work condition applied, confirm the referral was made before reinstallation.
  • Base and bedding check: confirm rebuilt base and bedding match the original specification's thickness, gradation, and slope.
  • Pattern check: confirm reinstalled pavers match the original pattern and joint width, with damaged units replaced.
  • Surface plane check: confirm the repaired section aligns with the surrounding field within tolerance, with no rocking unit under load.
  • Edge and drainage check: confirm edge restraint is fully seated and the repaired section drains toward the original discharge path with no new ponding.
  • Documentation check: confirm the repair worksheet is complete and signed.

Complete the Paver Repair Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before returning the section to service.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Repair Fails Again in the Same Location

Probable cause: pavers were lifted and reset without excavating and correcting the underlying base, bedding, edge, or drainage deficiency.
Confirmation: re-excavate the area and inspect for the same soft spot, contamination, or edge condition recorded in the original diagnosis.
Correction: re-open the repair, excavate to stable support, and complete Sections G and H in full before reinstalling pavers.
Reinspection: repeat the cause check and the base and bedding check.

Repaired Section Sits Higher or Lower Than Surrounding Field

Probable cause: rebuilt base or bedding thickness did not match the original specification, or the screed rails were not referenced to the surrounding accepted elevation.
Confirmation: check rebuilt base and bedding thickness against the Section C original specification, and check surface plane with a straightedge spanning the repair and the adjoining field.
Correction: remove the reinstalled pavers, adjust base or bedding thickness to match the original specification, and rescreed before reinstalling.
Reinspection: repeat the surface plane check.

Units Rock After Reinstallation and Compaction

Probable cause: a unit was reinstalled over an uneven or contaminated bedding surface, or the unit itself is cracked or chipped below the approved minimum condition.
Confirmation: lift the rocking unit, inspect the bedding surface beneath it, and inspect the unit for cracking or edge damage.
Correction: correct the bedding surface or replace the unit, then reinstall and recompact.
Reinspection: repeat the surface plane check for the corrected unit and its immediate neighbors.

Pattern or Joint Lines Do Not Match the Surrounding Field

Probable cause: pavers were not reinstalled in their original numbered position, or replacement units were installed without matching the surrounding bond pattern.
Confirmation: compare the reinstalled pattern against the photograph taken during extraction and against adjoining bond lines.
Correction: lift and reset the misplaced units in their correct position and orientation.
Reinspection: repeat the pattern check.

Water Ponds on or Near the Repaired Section

Probable cause: the rebuilt base or rescreeded bedding did not preserve the original slope, or an edge-restraint or drainage correction from Section G was not completed.
Confirmation: flood-test the repaired section with a controlled water source and observe flow direction and any ponding location.
Correction: correct the slope by rebuilding the affected base and bedding, or complete the outstanding edge or drainage correction from Section G.
Reinspection: repeat the edge and drainage check and the follow-up inspection after the next rain.

Joint Sand Washes Out of the Repaired Section Shortly After Completion

Probable cause: polymeric sand was activated outside the manufacturer's weather or moisture window, or joints were not clean and dry before installation.
Confirmation: review the weather and activation record against the manufacturer's stated conditions.
Correction: remove washed-out sand, allow the joints to dry fully, and reinstall and activate joint sand following the Chapter 10 procedure and manufacturer instructions.
Reinspection: repeat the documentation check and confirm the new activation record meets manufacturer conditions.

Cause Recorded as Unresolved with No Follow-Up Plan

Probable cause: the diagnostic inspection in Section E could not confirm a specific cause, and the repair proceeded without a plan to monitor for recurrence.
Confirmation: review the repair worksheet for a recorded cause; if "unresolved" is recorded, check whether a follow-up inspection date is also recorded.
Correction: schedule and record a specific follow-up inspection date, and refer the section to a qualified professional if the defect recurs.
Reinspection: perform the scheduled follow-up inspection and update the worksheet.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, signed repair worksheet and a section restored to full service, not a visually patched surface over an unresolved cause.

  • Remove excess base aggregate, bedding sand, and joint sand from the surrounding surface and adjacent landscaping
  • Dispose of cracked, chipped, or unstable discarded pavers and any contaminated base or bedding material properly
  • Store any leftover matching pavers, base aggregate, bedding sand, and joint sand for future repairs in the same area
  • File the repair worksheet and photographs with the original installation or readiness records for the affected area
  • Barricade or restrict traffic over the repaired section through any required joint-sand or adhesive cure period

Field Terms

Affected Area
The full area marked for repair, extending beyond visibly displaced pavers to include adjoining units sharing the same underlying cause.
Probable Cause
The base, subgrade, edge, drainage, or joint condition identified as the most likely source of a paver defect.
Pattern Order
The sequence and orientation in which pavers are numbered during extraction so they can be reinstalled in their original position.
Surface Plane Match
The condition where a repaired section's finished surface aligns with the surrounding undisturbed pavers within the original installation tolerance.
Base Failure
Loss of thickness, compaction, or support in the aggregate base layer, typically from erosion, contamination, or inadequate original compaction.
Rescreed
Reinstalling and leveling bedding sand to the original thickness and slope after the base beneath it has been rebuilt.
Edge Failure
Displacement or loss of function in the edge restraint that allows the paver field to shift, spread, or settle at its boundary.
Follow-Up Inspection
A scheduled reinspection of a repaired section after weather or use, used when the original cause could not be fully confirmed.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one complete gravel or crushed-stone path from an approved layout through a finished, drained, edged, and surfaced walking surface. The finished work product is one gravel or crushed-stone path built to the project's approved width, alignment, curves, slope, base, fabric, edging, surface aggregate, and drainage specification.

You will lay out the path and its curves, locate utilities, excavate to the approved depth, remove unsuitable soil, shape and compact the subgrade, install base aggregate where specified, install separation fabric only where the design requires it, install and fasten edging, place and finish surface stone, blend transitions to adjoining surfaces, and inspect the finished path for drainage, edge security, depth, loose-stone migration, trip hazards, and accessibility.

This chapter builds directly on the excavation and compaction skills from Chapters 4 and 5 and applies them to a flexible, unbound wearing surface rather than a paver field. It also introduces edging methods and separation-fabric judgment that carry forward into the retaining-wall and drainage chapters that follow.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • HC-061 — Lay out the path, including width, alignment, curves, and transitions.
  • HC-062 — Excavate the path area to the approved depth, removing roots, organics, soft soil, and debris.
  • HC-063 — Install landscape or separation fabric only where the design requires it, without bridging soft soil.
  • HC-064 — Place base stone in approved compacted lifts to the specified thickness and slope.
  • HC-065 — Install rigid, metal, wood, stone, or other approved edging fastened into stable support.
  • HC-066 — Inspect the finished gravel walkway for drainage, edge security, depth, loose-stone migration, trip hazards, and accessibility.

Where This Skill Fits

A gravel or crushed-stone path uses the same excavation, subgrade, and compaction discipline as the paver walkways and patios built in Chapters 4 through 10, but it finishes as an unbound surface that depends entirely on edge confinement and drainage rather than joint-locking with polymeric sand. Skipping edge restraint, or substituting fabric for base preparation, produces a path that migrates, ruts, and loses depth within one season.

The judgment you build here — when separation fabric helps rather than substitutes for base preparation, and how edging must be fastened into stable support rather than loose surface stone — applies again in Chapter 12's segmental retaining walls and Chapter 17's surface-drainage installations.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the approved path width, alignment, curves, slope, base, fabric, edging, surface aggregate, and drainage specification
  • locating utilities and verifying property boundaries before excavation
  • laying out path edges, curves, entrances, transitions, steps, gates, and drainage crossings
  • excavating to the approved depth and removing roots, organics, soft soil, and debris
  • shaping and compacting the subgrade
  • installing base aggregate in approved lifts where the design specifies a base course
  • installing separation or landscape fabric only where the design requires it
  • installing rigid, metal, wood, stone, or other approved edging fastened into stable support
  • placing and finishing surface gravel or crushed stone with the specified crown or cross slope
  • blending transitions to adjoining surfaces and inspecting the finished path

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • excavate, stake, drill, or drive edging fasteners before the required utility-location service has responded and marked the site
  • use landscape fabric to bridge soft soil or replace subgrade correction and base preparation
  • substitute polymeric sand, mortar, or adhesive for the specified mechanical edge-restraint system
  • direct path drainage toward foundations, entrances, adjacent property, or a discharge point not identified as lawful in the approved plan

Any site condition identified during layout or excavation that falls outside the approved specification must be corrected, or referred to the required qualified professional, before surface stone is placed.

Trade Note

A gravel path is not a lower-effort substitute for a paver walkway. It still requires an excavated, compacted, and drained system below the visible surface. The stone on top only performs as well as the base, fabric decision, and edge confinement built beneath it.

Setup Standard

Do not excavate, stake, drill, or drive edging fasteners until the required utility-location service has responded and marked the site. Confirm property boundaries before excavation limits are marked.

Shop Tip

Dry-lay the edging along a curved section before fastening any of it. Edging that looks acceptable in a straight run can bind, kink, or pull away from the path line once it follows a tight curve, and it is far easier to correct before the first fastener is driven.

Common Field Fundamentals

A gravel path performs as a system: layout controls alignment and width, excavation and subgrade compaction control long-term stability, the base course (where specified) controls load support, separation fabric controls soil intermixing without replacing base preparation, edging controls lateral confinement, and surface stone controls the finished walking plane and drainage. A deficiency in any one of these carries directly into surface rutting, edge migration, or ponding.

Stop-Work Conditions

Halt work on the affected section, rather than continue, whenever a condition falls outside the approved path specification. Refer the condition to the required qualified professional before continuing.

Critical Safety Cue — Unmarked or Ambiguous Utilities

Do not excavate, stake, or drive edging fasteners in any section where the utility locate has not been confirmed, or where a marking is ambiguous, missing, or contradicted by visible evidence such as a meter, riser, or vent. Contact the utility-locating service again and wait for a confirmed response before continuing in that section.

Field Safety Cue — Trenching and Excavation Edges

Keep excavation spoil and stockpiled aggregate back from trench and excavation edges. Do not stand or kneel at an unsupported excavation edge, and do not permit foot traffic across an open excavation without a stable crossing.

Code Alert — Path Drainage Discharge

Verify the lawful discharge point for this path's runoff before establishing crown or cross slope. Do not direct path drainage toward a foundation, entrance, adjacent property, public hazard, or a discharge point not identified as lawful in the approved plan.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Edging Fastener Schedule

Install edging fasteners at the type, length, and spacing specified by the edging manufacturer for the confirmed subgrade or base condition. Do not anchor edging only into loose surface stone, and do not substitute a fastener type or spacing not identified by the manufacturer.

Measurement Cue — Verify Width and Slope at Regular Intervals

Check path width, base or surface elevation, and crown or cross slope at regular intervals along the full path length, not only at the start and end. A path that meets specification at both ends can still drift out of tolerance in the middle section.

E. Layout and Utility Verification

  1. Review path width, alignment, curves, slope, base, fabric, edging, surface aggregate, and drainage. Confirm the approved specification against the Project Definition Record before marking any layout line.
  2. Locate utilities and verify property boundaries. Submit or confirm the utility-locate request and compare property markers against the site plan before any layout stake is set.
  3. Lay out path edges and curves. Set stakes and string, or lay a hose or template for curves, along the centerline and both edges at the approved width.
  4. Mark entrances, transitions, steps, gates, and drainage crossings. Identify and mark every point where the path meets an existing surface, structure, or grade change.

F. Excavation and Subgrade Preparation

  1. Excavate to the approved depth. Excavate the full path width and length to the depth stated in the approved specification.
  2. Remove roots, organics, soft soil, and debris. Clear the excavation of all organic and unsuitable material before proceeding.
  3. Shape and compact the subgrade. Shape the subgrade to the approved slope and compact using the plate compactor or hand tamper appropriate to the soil and area.

G. Base and Fabric Installation

  1. Install base aggregate where specified. Confirm whether the approved design includes a base course before placing any aggregate.
  2. Compact in approved lifts. Place and compact base aggregate in the lift thickness stated in the specification, checking elevation and slope after each lift.
  3. Install separation or landscape fabric only where the design requires it. Do not install fabric where the approved specification does not call for it.
  4. Overlap and secure fabric without creating wrinkles. Follow the manufacturer's stated overlap distance and securing method.
  5. Do not use fabric to bridge soft soil or replace base preparation. If a soft spot appears, correct the subgrade before placing fabric or aggregate over it.

H. Edging Installation

  1. Install rigid, metal, wood, stone, or other approved edging. Position the edging tight to the path line established in Section E.
  2. Fasten edging into stable support. Drive fasteners into the compacted subgrade or base at the manufacturer-specified type and spacing, not into loose surface stone.
  3. Maintain path width and curves. Check width and curve alignment against the layout references at regular intervals as edging is installed.

I. Surface Stone Placement and Finish

  1. Place surface gravel or crushed stone in controlled layers. Distribute surface stone evenly along the confined path section.
  2. Rake to even depth and crown or cross slope as specified. Use the rake and screed board to bring the surface to the specified depth and drainage slope.
  3. Compact or roll the surface where required. Follow the approved specification for surface compaction or rolling.
  4. Blend transitions to adjoining surfaces. Finish each marked transition point so the path surface meets the adjoining surface, structure, or grade change without a trip hazard.

J. Inspection and Correction

  1. Inspect drainage, edge security, depth, loose stone migration, trip hazards, and accessibility. Walk the full path length, checking each item against the Acceptance Standards table.
  2. Correct low spots, unstable edges, and excessive loose material. Address every deficiency found during inspection before closing out the project.
  3. Document materials and final condition. Record base and surface material, fabric use, edging type and fastener schedule, final width and slope measurements, and inspection results.

Common Site Inspection

Review every completed gravel path using the same checks regardless of length, curve count, or edging type.

  • Layout check: confirm path width and curve alignment match the approved specification at every checked interval.
  • Subgrade check: confirm excavation depth, organic removal, and subgrade compaction meet the approved specification.
  • Base and fabric check: confirm base lifts meet the specified thickness where required, and fabric use matches the approved design without bridging soft soil.
  • Edging check: confirm edging is fastened into stable support at the manufacturer-specified spacing and shows no movement under hand-pressure test.
  • Surface check: confirm surface stone depth, crown or cross slope, and finish match the approved specification.
  • Drainage and transition check: confirm runoff reaches the approved lawful discharge point and every transition is free of trip hazards.

Complete the Gravel Path Inspection Checklist below before closing out the project.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Surface Stone Migrating Beyond the Path Edges

Probable cause: edging was fastened only into loose surface stone rather than the compacted subgrade or base, or fastener spacing was wider than the manufacturer specification.
Confirmation: apply hand pressure to the edging at several points along the path and check for movement or lifting.
Correction: remove and reset the affected edging section, fastening into the compacted subgrade or base at the manufacturer-specified spacing.
Reinspection: repeat the edging check along the full corrected section.

Ponding or Ruts Forming on the Path Surface

Probable cause: the subgrade or surface was not shaped to the specified crown or cross slope, or the base was not compacted evenly along the path length.
Confirmation: check surface slope with a line level or laser level at multiple points and compare against the approved specification.
Correction: remove surface stone in the affected area, correct base or subgrade slope, recompact, and reinstall surface stone to the specified depth and slope.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage and measurement check for the corrected section.

Fabric Installed Over an Uncorrected Soft Spot

Probable cause: fabric was placed over a soft or unstable area instead of correcting the subgrade first.
Confirmation: probe the area through the surface stone and fabric for continued softness or pumping.
Correction: remove surface stone, base, and fabric from the affected area, excavate and correct the soft subgrade, then reinstall fabric only if specified, base, and surface stone.
Reinspection: repeat the base and fabric check for the corrected section.

Path Width or Curve Drifts Out of Tolerance Mid-Run

Probable cause: layout references were only checked at the start and end of the path, allowing drift to accumulate in the middle section.
Confirmation: re-measure width and curve offsets at regular intervals against the original layout references.
Correction: reset edging and surface stone in the affected section to match the approved width and curve.
Reinspection: repeat the layout check along the full path length.

Trip Hazard at a Transition Point

Probable cause: the transition to an adjoining surface, step, or gate was not marked during layout, or surface stone depth was not adjusted to blend the elevation change.
Confirmation: measure the elevation difference at the transition point and compare against the acceptable transition standard in the approved specification.
Correction: adjust surface stone depth, edging height, or base elevation at the transition to eliminate the abrupt elevation change.
Reinspection: repeat the transition and trip-hazard check at every marked transition point.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, inspected gravel path and organized project records, not a visually acceptable surface with undocumented base or fabric decisions.

  • Remove excess aggregate, fabric offcuts, and packaging from the work area
  • Return unused edging, fasteners, and fabric to staged storage
  • Sweep or rake loose stone away from adjoining surfaces and drainage paths
  • File the completed Gravel Path Inspection Checklist with the project's readiness and layout records
  • Retain the base, fabric, edging, and surface-stone documentation for reference in later chapters and for future maintenance

Field Terms

Path Centerline
The controlling reference line from which path width is measured equally to each edge.
Base Course
The compacted aggregate layer beneath the surface stone, installed only where the approved design specifies it.
Separation Fabric
Fabric placed at the soil-to-aggregate interface to reduce intermixing; not a substitute for subgrade correction or base thickness.
Edging
The rigid, metal, wood, or stone confinement system fastened into stable support along both sides of the path.
Crown or Cross Slope
The finished surface slope that directs runoff away from the path surface, per the approved plan.
Transition
The point where the path surface, edging, or elevation meets an adjoining surface, structure, step, or gate.
Bridging
The failure condition where fabric is placed over a soft or unstable area instead of correcting the subgrade beneath it.
Discharge Point
The lawful location where path runoff is directed, as identified in the approved drainage plan.

Performance Check

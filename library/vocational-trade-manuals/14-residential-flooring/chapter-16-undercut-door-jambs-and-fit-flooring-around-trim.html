Job Overview

This project qualifies you to undercut a door jamb and casing assembly and fit the finish flooring cleanly through the resulting doorway. The finished work product is one completed doorway cut: a jamb and casing undercut to the exact height of a flooring-and-underlayment gauge, a matching flooring piece cut and fitted beneath the trim, hidden expansion space preserved under the jamb, and the door confirmed operable through its full swing.

You will gauge the cut height directly from a scrap of the actual flooring and underlayment, mark the cut line, check for concealed wiring near the cutting path, undercut the jamb legs and casing with a flat, controlled cut, remove the waste material, measure and cut the flooring piece that passes beneath the trim, slide or set that piece into place without trapping or pinching it, install any required transition, and confirm the door still operates correctly.

This project depends on the substrate and moisture findings from Chapter 1, the expansion requirements of the plank, resilient, or tile system you are installing in Chapters 6 through 13, and it is referenced directly by Chapter 15 transition work and by the Chapter 20 capstone, which requires every doorway in the practice room to be undercut before final flooring pieces are installed through the opening.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-091 — Mark jamb height using a flooring-and-underlayment gauge.
  • FL-092 — Undercut door jambs and casing to the marked height without damaging structural or fire-rated components.
  • FL-093 — Slide or set flooring beneath undercut trim without trapping or pinching it.
  • FL-094 — Cut flooring accurately around casing and jamb profiles.
  • FL-095 — Preserve hidden expansion gaps required by the installed flooring system.
  • FL-096 — Inspect doorway fit, trim support, flooring movement, and door operation.

Where This Skill Fits

Every plank, resilient, and floating-floor chapter in this book — laminate in Chapter 6, luxury vinyl plank in Chapter 7, nail-down hardwood in Chapter 9, and engineered floating hardwood in Chapter 10 — calls this project by reference at the doorway step of its own sequence. Tile work in Chapters 11 through 13 also depends on a correctly undercut jamb wherever a tile field passes beneath trim. Getting the doorway wrong is one of the most common visible defects in a finished floor: an uncut jamb forces an ugly scribed cut around the casing profile, and a jamb cut too high leaves a visible gap once the flooring is in place.

The height-gauge method taught here — using an actual piece of the flooring and underlayment as the cutting reference rather than a tape-measure dimension — is the same method reused at every doorway in the Chapter 20 capstone room, regardless of which flooring system is installed on each side of the opening.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • gauging and marking the jamb cut line directly from the actual flooring and underlayment thickness
  • undercutting wood door-jamb legs and casing using an oscillating multi-tool, jamb saw, or approved hand saw with controlled depth
  • cutting the flooring, resilient, or tile piece that passes beneath the undercut trim
  • sliding or setting that piece beneath the trim while preserving hidden expansion space
  • installing the approved transition where the doorway sequence requires one
  • confirming door operation and inspecting the finished doorway fit

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • cut structural door components, weather seals, fire-rated door or frame components, or thresholds without specific authorization from the approved plan or a qualified professional
  • cut into concealed wiring, plumbing, or other services without first verifying their location and, where required, de-energizing the circuit
  • alter door swing, jamb plumb, casing reveal, or any other trim geometry beyond the undercut height required for the flooring
  • select the flooring system, transition type, or expansion allowance — those decisions are made in Chapters 1, 6 through 14, and 15 and simply carried into this project

Any doorway where the jamb, casing, threshold, or concealed-service condition cannot be verified before cutting must be treated as a stop-work condition until it is resolved.

Trade Note

The height gauge for this cut is never a tape-measure number pulled from memory. It is a physical stack of the actual flooring piece and the actual underlayment, held flat against the jamb, with the saw registered directly on top of that stack. A cut made from a written dimension instead of the physical gauge is the most common cause of an undercut jamb that is too high or too low for the finished floor.

Setup Standard

Do not cut a jamb until the actual flooring thickness and underlayment thickness are known and available as a physical gauge, and until the presence or absence of concealed wiring near the cutting path has been checked. A cut made before both conditions are confirmed must be treated as incomplete and the jamb re-verified before the flooring piece is cut.

Shop Tip

Keep the saw blade registered flat on top of the flooring-and-underlayment gauge stack for the entire cut, not just at the starting point. A blade that lifts even slightly partway through the cut leaves a jamb that is too high at one end, which shows as a visible gap once the flooring is slid into place.

Height Gauging, Concealed-Service Checks, and Cut Verification

Every undercut in this chapter is registered from a physical gauge built from the actual flooring and underlayment used in the room, never from a tape-measure dimension alone. Where the cutting path approaches a wall containing wiring or plumbing, check for concealed services before cutting and de-energize the affected circuit if required. Do not cut structural door components, weather seals, fire-rated components, or thresholds without specific authorization from the approved plan or a qualified professional.

Measurement Cue — Register the Cut Directly on the Gauge Stack

Build the height gauge from one piece of the actual flooring and one piece of the actual underlayment, stacked together and held flat against the jamb. Mark the cut line at the top of the stack and keep the saw registered flat on the stack for the full length of the cut. Do not substitute a written dimension for the physical gauge.

Critical Safety Cue — Concealed Wiring Near the Cutting Path

Before cutting a jamb leg that is close to a wall cavity, check for concealed wiring using a non-contact voltage tester or other approved method. De-energize the affected circuit before cutting if wiring is present or its location cannot be confirmed. Do not proceed with the cut until the concealed-service condition is resolved.

Code Alert — Fire-Rated and Structural Door Assemblies

Do not undercut, trim, or otherwise alter a fire-rated door frame, fire-rated jamb, structural door component, or weather-sealing component without specific authorization from the approved plan or a qualified professional. Altering a fire-rated assembly without authorization can void its rating and create a code violation.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Hidden Expansion Space

Preserve the exact hidden expansion space beneath the undercut trim that the flooring manufacturer's instructions specify for the installed system. Do not reduce or eliminate this space to make the doorway piece fit more tightly, and do not fasten the flooring through the field beneath the trim on a floating floor.

E. Doorway Assessment and Height Gauging

  1. Confirm flooring thickness, underlayment, adhesive, expansion, transition, and door-clearance requirements. Verify these against the flooring system installed or being installed on each side of the doorway before marking any cut line.
  2. Inspect the door frame, casing, threshold, wall, and concealed-service risk. Note any fire-rated component, structural element, weather seal, or wall cavity that may contain wiring or plumbing near the cutting path.
  3. Place a scrap of flooring and underlayment against the jamb as a height gauge. Hold the stack flat against the jamb leg at the location of the cut.
  4. Mark the cut line. Trace the top of the gauge stack directly onto the jamb leg and casing using a pencil or marking knife.

F. Protection and Concealed-Service Verification

  1. Protect the floor and adjacent trim. Cover the surrounding finished flooring and casing before cutting begins.
  2. De-energize nearby circuits if the cutting path approaches concealed wiring. Check with a non-contact voltage tester and de-energize the affected circuit at the panel before proceeding if wiring is present or its location cannot be confirmed.

G. Undercutting the Jamb and Casing

  1. Use an oscillating saw, jamb saw, or approved hand saw with controlled depth. Select the tool suited to the trim material and the doorway's access.
  2. Keep the blade flat to the flooring gauge. Register the blade against the top of the gauge stack for the entire length of the cut.
  3. Cut jamb legs and casing only as far as required. Limit the cut to the marked line and to the components specifically authorized for undercutting.
  4. Do not cut structural door components, weather seals, fire-rated components, or thresholds without authorization. Stop and escalate if the cutting path would reach any of these components.
  5. Remove waste material cleanly. Extract the cut sections without splintering the remaining jamb or casing above the cut line.
  6. Vacuum dust and inspect the cavity. Use the HEPA vacuum and flashlight to confirm the cut is flat, level, and free of debris before fitting flooring.

H. Doorway Flooring Fit and Expansion

  1. Measure the flooring piece passing beneath the trim. Take the dimension directly from the prepared opening, not from the general room layout.
  2. Cut the plank, tile, sheet, or carpet edge for the doorway. Use the cutting tool matching the installed material and preserve pattern orientation.
  3. Maintain hidden expansion space under the jamb. Cut the piece to leave the flooring manufacturer's specified gap beneath the trim once it is in place.
  4. Slide or set the flooring beneath the trim. Fit the piece into the undercut opening without forcing it or damaging the cut edge.
  5. Confirm the floor is not trapped or pinched. Check that the piece can move within its expansion allowance where the flooring system requires free movement, and is not fastened through the field beneath the trim.
  6. Install the approved transition where required. Follow the transition type and clearance specified in the Chapter 15 transition plan for this doorway.

I. Operation Check and Final Inspection

  1. Operate the door. Open, close, and latch the door through its full swing and confirm it does not bind, drag, or rub on the new flooring.
  2. Inspect cut height, trim support, flooring movement, visible gaps, and transition fit. Confirm the undercut is flat and level, the trim above the cut remains fully supported, the flooring can move within its allowance, and no visible gap or misalignment is present at the cut line.
  3. Correct overcuts using approved trim repair or replace damaged casing. Replace any casing section that was cut beyond the marked line or otherwise damaged during the undercut.
  4. Document doorway treatment. Record the gauge components, concealed-service check, cut method, expansion measurement, transition type, and door-operation result on the doorway record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed doorway using the same checks regardless of which flooring system is installed on either side of the opening.

  • Gauge check: confirm the cut was registered from the actual flooring-and-underlayment stack, not a written dimension alone.
  • Concealed-service check: confirm wiring or plumbing near the cutting path was verified and, if present, addressed before cutting.
  • Trim check: confirm the undercut is flat and level and no structural, fire-rated, or weather-sealing component was disturbed.
  • Fit check: confirm the doorway flooring piece passes fully beneath the trim with correct orientation and no visible gap.
  • Expansion check: confirm hidden expansion space beneath the trim meets the flooring manufacturer's specification.
  • Movement check: confirm the flooring is not trapped, pinched, or fastened through the field where free movement is required.
  • Operation check: confirm the door opens, closes, and latches through its full swing without binding.
  • Documentation check: confirm the doorway record is fully completed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Jamb Cut Is Too High, Leaving a Visible Gap Beneath the Trim

Probable cause: the gauge stack was not held flat against the jamb during marking, or the saw blade lifted off the gauge stack partway through the cut.
Confirmation: re-measure the gap beneath the trim at the high point and compare it to the actual flooring-and-underlayment thickness.
Correction: rebuild the height gauge, remark the cut line at the correct height, and recut the affected section of jamb or casing.
Reinspection: confirm the flooring piece passes beneath the trim with no visible gap once the corrected cut is complete.

The Undercut Is Not Flat, Leaving an Uneven Line Along the Cut

Probable cause: the saw blade was not kept registered flat on the gauge stack for the full length of the cut, or the tool was tilted during cutting.
Confirmation: place a straightedge along the cut line and measure any deviation.
Correction: recut the affected section with the blade held flat against the gauge stack for the entire pass.
Reinspection: recheck the cut with a straightedge before fitting the doorway flooring piece.

The Flooring Piece Is Trapped or Pinched Beneath the Trim

Probable cause: the doorway piece was cut without the required expansion allowance, or the piece was forced into an opening narrower than the flooring's expansion clearance.
Confirmation: measure the gap beneath the trim after the piece is in place and compare it to the flooring manufacturer's specified expansion space.
Correction: remove and recut the doorway piece to restore the required expansion allowance.
Reinspection: confirm the piece can move within its allowance once the correction is complete.

The Door Binds or Drags on the New Flooring

Probable cause: the combined flooring and underlayment thickness was greater than accounted for in the original door-clearance check, or the door bottom was not verified against the finished-floor height in Chapter 1.
Confirmation: operate the door through its full swing and identify the exact point of contact with the new flooring.
Correction: escalate to a qualified professional for door-bottom trimming if required, or confirm the flooring thickness against the Chapter 1 finish-height review before altering the door.
Reinspection: re-operate the door through its full swing after any correction.

Casing or Jamb Damage Occurs During the Undercut

Probable cause: the cut extended past the marked line, the tool caught on trim detailing, or excessive force was used while removing waste material.
Confirmation: inspect the jamb and casing above the cut line for splintering, cracking, or an overcut into visible trim.
Correction: replace the damaged casing section with matching stock, or repair minor damage using an approved trim-repair method.
Reinspection: confirm the repaired or replaced trim matches the surrounding profile and finish before proceeding.

Concealed Wiring Is Found During the Cutting-Path Check

Probable cause: wiring was routed through the wall cavity near the jamb closer to the cutting path than expected.
Confirmation: re-check the location with a non-contact voltage tester and, where available, compare against as-built wiring information.
Correction: de-energize the affected circuit at the panel before cutting, or adjust the cutting path to remain clear of the wiring if the flooring plan allows it.
Reinspection: re-verify the circuit is de-energized or the cutting path is clear before resuming the undercut.

Cleanup

This project ends with the doorway cut complete, the flooring fitted and confirmed free to move within its allowance, and the door confirmed operable.

Field Terms

Height Gauge
A physical stack consisting of one scrap piece of the actual flooring material and one scrap piece of the actual underlayment used to register the jamb cut height.
Undercut

Cut Line
The marked horizontal line on the jamb leg or casing indicating the top of the undercut, established from the height gauge.
Hidden Expansion Space
The gap preserved beneath the undercut trim, concealed from view once flooring is installed, required by the flooring manufacturer's expansion specification.
Doorway Piece
The individual flooring, resilient, or tile piece cut and fitted to pass beneath the undercut jamb and casing.
Jamb Saw
A dedicated flooring undercut saw designed to cut a jamb leg at a fixed, controlled depth referenced from the floor or a gauge block.
Fire-Rated Component
A door, frame, or seal component whose fire-resistance rating depends on its unaltered configuration and that must not be cut without specific authorization.
Stop-Work Condition
Any unverified concealed-service location, structural door component, fire-rated component, or weather seal that must halt cutting until corrected, tested, or escalated.

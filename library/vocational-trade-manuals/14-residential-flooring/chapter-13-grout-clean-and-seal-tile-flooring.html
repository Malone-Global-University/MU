Grout, Clean, and Seal Tile Flooring

Grout, clean, cure, and where required seal one completed tile floor while preserving every movement and perimeter joint, producing a finished tile surface with fully packed joints, consistent color, and no haze or pinholes.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to grout, clean, cure, and where required seal one completed tile floor. The finished work product is one grouted, cleaned, cured, and documented tile floor: joints packed completely to the required depth, excess grout removed without disturbing joint fill, initial and final cleaning completed on the manufacturer's schedule, movement and perimeter joints preserved and treated with flexible sealant where specified, penetrating sealer applied only where required and compatible, and a completed grout and sealing record.

You will confirm the tile mortar has cured, inspect the tile field for defects before grouting, remove excess mortar from joints, select and mix the specified grout, apply and pack grout diagonally across the tile, perform sponge cleaning in the correct sequence, shape and clean joints, correct pinholes and low spots while the material still permits it, remove haze after cure, install flexible sealant at movement and perimeter joints, apply a compatible penetrating sealer only when required, and inspect the finished floor for joint fullness, color consistency, haze, cracking, and preserved movement joints.

This project depends on the tile field completed in Chapter 12 and the backer-board substrate completed in Chapter 11. It closes out the tile-flooring sequence and hands the finished floor to Chapter 15 for transition work and to Chapter 19 for the final quality inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-073 — Mix grout using the exact water or additive ratio and required slake and remix procedure.
  • FL-074 — Pack grout joints completely to the required depth without disturbing tile or movement joints.
  • FL-075 — Clean tile surfaces using the correct initial and final sponge-cleaning sequence.
  • FL-076 — Shape grout lines consistently across the tile field.
  • FL-077 — Apply sealer where required, compatible with the tile and grout.
  • FL-078 — Inspect the finished tile floor for joint fullness, color consistency, haze, cracking, pinholes, and preserved movement joints.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 12 set the tile field, joint width, movement joints, and mortar coverage that this project's grout must respect. This project's grout joints, movement-joint sealant, and sealer are the last material placed on the tile field before Chapter 15 transitions, Chapter 17 stair finishes on tiled treads, and the Chapter 19 final inspection. A grout joint that traps a movement joint, a haze left on the tile face, or a sealer applied over an unresolved pinhole becomes a permanent defect once the floor is placed into service.

The cleaning discipline established here — controlled sponge cleaning, frequent rinse-water changes, and a defined cleaning interval — is the same discipline reused whenever a later chapter's finish material must be cleaned without disturbing a curing joint.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • confirming tile mortar cure and inspecting the tile field before grouting
  • removing excess mortar from joints to the required depth without damaging tile edges
  • selecting, mixing, and applying the specified grout
  • packing joints completely while protecting movement and perimeter joints from rigid grout
  • performing initial and final sponge cleaning on the manufacturer's schedule
  • correcting pinholes and low joints while the material still permits correction
  • removing grout haze after cure
  • installing flexible sealant at movement and perimeter joints where specified
  • applying a compatible penetrating sealer only where required
  • inspecting and documenting the finished tile floor

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • set, reset, or realign tile — that work is covered in Chapter 12
  • fill a designated movement or perimeter joint with rigid grout under any circumstance
  • begin grouting before the tile mortar has cured for the period specified by the mortar manufacturer
  • proceed to Chapter 15 transition installation before the grout, sealant, and any required sealer have cured for the specified period

Any tile defect, unresolved movement-joint condition, or mortar-cure deficiency identified before grouting must be corrected under Chapter 12 procedures before this project continues.

Trade Note

Grout is not a structural material and is not a substitute for correct mortar coverage or tile support. Every joint packed in this chapter assumes the tile field passed the Chapter 12 mortar-coverage and lippage inspection. A hollow tile or unsupported edge discovered during this project's pre-grout inspection must be corrected under Chapter 12 before grouting continues.

Setup Standard

Do not begin grouting until the tile mortar has cured for the period specified by the mortar manufacturer, the tile field has passed a pre-grout inspection for loose pieces, lippage, and contamination, and every movement and perimeter joint has been protected from rigid grout. Grouting over an uncured mortar bed, an unaddressed defect, or an unprotected movement joint must be treated as an incomplete setup and corrected before proceeding.

Shop Tip

Mix only the quantity of grout that can be applied and cleaned within its open time. Grout that begins to set in the bucket before it is packed into joints produces inconsistent color, poor joint fill, and a rough surface that will not clean properly during the initial sponge pass.

Grout Selection, Mixing, and Movement-Joint Protection

Use the exact grout type, color, water or additive ratio, slake time, and remix procedure specified for the project. Do not substitute a different grout type or add unapproved water, latex additive, or accelerant. Every movement and perimeter joint identified in the Chapter 12 layout must be protected from rigid grout throughout mixing, application, and cleaning, and treated instead with flexible sealant in a later step of this project.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Mortar Cure Before Grouting

Confirm the tile mortar has cured for the exact period specified by the mortar manufacturer before mixing any grout. Grouting over uncured mortar can trap moisture, disturb tile bond, and produce a joint that cracks or discolors after the floor is placed in service.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Fill Movement Joints With Rigid Grout

Do not allow grout to enter a designated movement or perimeter joint at any point during application or cleaning. A movement joint filled with rigid grout can crack, telegraph through the tile field, or transmit structural movement directly into the tile as the building moves. Remove any grout that enters a movement joint before it sets, and install the specified flexible sealant only after the surrounding grout has cured.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Mixing, Slake, and Cleaning Schedule

Follow the exact water or additive ratio, mixing time, slake time, and remix procedure specified for the grout product. Follow the exact cleaning-stage timing, initial and final cleaning interval, and cure time before haze removal, sealant installation, and sealer application. Do not estimate these timings from experience with a different product.

Safety Cue — Chemical Exposure During Mixing and Cleaning

Wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection when mixing grout, handling sealant, and applying sealer. Provide ventilation appropriate to the specific product being used, particularly when applying a solvent-based penetrating sealer in an enclosed room.

E. Pre-Grout Inspection and Joint Preparation

  1. Confirm tile mortar has cured for the required period. Compare the current date against the mortar cure-start date recorded under Chapter 12 and the exact cure period specified by the mortar manufacturer.
  2. Inspect tile for loose pieces, lippage defects, mortar in joints, and contamination. Record the location of any condition found before proceeding.
  3. Remove excess mortar to the required joint depth without damaging tile edges. Use a joint-raking tool or the method specified for the backer-board and tile system to reach the depth required for full grout fill.
  4. Vacuum joints and tile surfaces. Remove all loose mortar debris and dust from every joint before mixing grout.
  5. Protect movement joints from rigid grout. Mask or otherwise physically protect every designated movement and perimeter joint identified in the Chapter 12 layout before any grout is mixed.

F. Grout Selection and Mixing

  1. Select the specified grout type and color. Confirm the product matches the grouting-area record before opening any packaging.
  2. Mix grout using the exact water or additive ratio. Measure water or additive by volume or weight as specified and mix for the stated time.
  3. Observe slake and remix requirements. Allow the mixed grout to rest for the specified slake time, then remix for the specified duration before application. Do not add additional water during or after the slake period.

G. Grout Application

  1. Apply grout diagonally across tile. Hold the float at the angle specified for the product and work across the joints at a diagonal to the tile edges.
  2. Pack joints completely. Make multiple passes as needed to force grout fully into each joint without leaving voids.
  3. Remove excess grout with the float. Hold the float nearly vertical and draw it diagonally across the tile face to remove excess material without pulling grout from the joints.

H. Initial and Final Cleaning

  1. Allow the grout to firm to the specified cleaning stage. Do not begin sponge cleaning before the grout reaches the firmness stated by the manufacturer.
  2. Perform the initial sponge cleaning with minimal water. Use a damp, well-wrung sponge in light, consistent passes to remove excess grout from the tile face without pulling grout from the joints.
  3. Shape joints consistently. Use the corner trowel, striking tool, or sponge edge to produce a uniform joint profile across the entire field.
  4. Change rinse water frequently. Replace both buckets before the rinse water becomes cloudy enough to redeposit grout residue on the tile face.
  5. Perform final cleaning after the appropriate interval. Wait the exact interval specified by the manufacturer, then perform a final sponge or cloth pass to remove remaining residue.
  6. Avoid washing pigment or binder from the joints. Use light pressure and minimal water during every cleaning pass, particularly on colored or pigmented grout.

I. Correction, Cure, and Haze Removal

  1. Fill pinholes and low joints while the material permits correction. Identify every pinhole or low spot during final cleaning and correct it before the grout sets beyond the manufacturer's correction window.
  2. Allow grout to cure. Protect the floor from traffic, moisture, and disturbance through the exact cure period specified by the manufacturer.
  3. Remove haze using the approved method. Use only the haze-removal product and method compatible with the tile and grout, applied after the grout has cured sufficiently per the manufacturer's instructions.

J. Movement-Joint Sealant and Tile Sealer

  1. Install flexible sealant at movement and perimeter joints where specified. Remove the temporary protection installed in Step 5, prepare the joint per the sealant manufacturer's instructions, and tool the sealant to a consistent profile matching the surrounding grout color.
  2. Apply penetrating sealer only when required and compatible with tile and grout. Confirm compatibility with both the tile and the cured grout before applying, and apply using the applicator, coverage rate, and number of coats specified by the sealer manufacturer.
  3. Remove excess sealer from tile faces. Wipe away any sealer that pools or streaks on the tile surface before it cures, following the manufacturer's working-time window.

K. Protection, Final Inspection, and Documentation

  1. Protect the floor through full cure. Restrict traffic and following-trade work for the exact period specified for the grout, sealant, and sealer.
  2. Inspect joint fullness, color consistency, haze, cracking, pinholes, movement joints, and surface cleanliness. Walk the entire grouting area under normal and raking light and record every deficiency found.
  3. Document grout, batch, water, cleaning, sealant, sealer, and cure. Complete the Grout and Sealing Record with every product, batch number, mixing ratio, cleaning time, sealant and sealer application, and cure date before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed grouting area using the same checks regardless of tile type, room, or grout color.

  • Mortar-cure check: confirm grouting did not begin before the mortar's specified cure period elapsed.
  • Joint-fill check: confirm every joint is packed to full depth with no voids, pinholes, or low spots remaining.
  • Cleaning check: confirm initial and final cleaning were performed on the manufacturer's schedule with no pigment or binder washed from the joints.
  • Movement-joint check: confirm no rigid grout is present in any designated movement or perimeter joint and that flexible sealant is installed where specified.
  • Haze check: confirm no grout haze remains on the tile face after the approved haze-removal method.
  • Sealer check: confirm any applied sealer is compatible, evenly applied, and free of surface residue.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Grout and Sealing Record is fully completed.

Complete the Grout and Sealing Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 14.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Pinholes or Low Spots Appear After Grout Has Set

Probable cause: trapped air during packing, insufficient float pressure, or grout applied past its open time.
Confirmation: inspect the joint surface under raking light and probe suspect areas to confirm depth loss.
Correction: if the correction window specified by the manufacturer has not closed, fill the pinhole or low spot with freshly mixed grout matching the original batch and color. If the window has closed, treat the defect as a Chapter 18 repair.
Reinspection: recheck the corrected joint for depth and color match after cure.

Grout Color Is Inconsistent Across the Field

Probable cause: inconsistent water ratio between mixed batches, uneven cleaning-interval timing, or excessive water used during sponge cleaning.
Confirmation: compare the batch records for water ratio and mixing time against the areas showing color variation.
Correction: identify the affected area and, where the manufacturer's correction window has not closed, reclean or re-grout the section using a freshly mixed, correctly proportioned batch.
Reinspection: recheck color consistency under both normal and raking light after cure.

Haze Remains on the Tile Face After Cleaning

Probable cause: insufficient final cleaning, cleaning performed before the specified interval, or an incompatible haze-removal product.
Confirmation: inspect the tile surface under raking light and confirm the film is grout haze rather than sealer residue.
Correction: apply the haze-removal method and product specified as compatible with the tile and grout, following the manufacturer's dwell time and rinse procedure.
Reinspection: recheck the tile surface under raking light after the haze-removal product has fully rinsed and dried.

Rigid Grout Is Found in a Movement or Perimeter Joint

Probable cause: the joint was not adequately protected before grouting, or grout was packed across the joint during application.
Confirmation: inspect every movement and perimeter joint identified in the Chapter 12 layout for grout intrusion.
Correction: remove the rigid grout from the joint using a method that does not damage adjacent tile edges, then install the specified flexible sealant once the surrounding grout has cured.
Reinspection: recheck the joint to confirm it is free of rigid material and properly sealed with flexible sealant.

Grout Cracks or Crumbles After Cure

Probable cause: incorrect water ratio, insufficient slake or remix time, grouting over uncured mortar, or movement transmitted through a joint that should have received flexible sealant instead of rigid grout.
Confirmation: inspect the cracked or crumbling area, review the batch mixing record, and confirm whether the affected joint was a designated movement joint.
Correction: remove the affected grout to sound material, confirm the underlying cause is corrected, and re-grout using a properly mixed batch, or install flexible sealant if the joint should not have received rigid grout.
Reinspection: recheck the corrected joint after cure for cracking, color match, and correct joint type.

Sealer Leaves Residue or Streaking on the Tile Face

Probable cause: excess sealer not wiped away within the product's working time, or an incompatible sealer applied to the tile finish.
Confirmation: inspect the tile surface under raking light and confirm the sealer product's stated compatibility with the tile finish.
Correction: remove residue using the method specified by the sealer manufacturer, or escalate to the sealer manufacturer if the product is confirmed incompatible with the tile finish.
Reinspection: recheck the tile surface after correction for residue, streaking, and even sheen.

Cleanup

This project ends with the grouting area protected from traffic and following-trade work through the specified cure period.

  • Rinse and store grout floats, sponges, mixing paddles, and buckets before residue cures on the tool
  • Dispose of grout, sealant, and sealer waste according to the product's disposal instructions; do not rinse grout residue into a drain not approved for that purpose
  • Confirm no protective masking remains over cured movement or perimeter joints
  • Store the Grout and Sealing Record with the tile-installation records from Chapter 11 and Chapter 12 for reference by later projects

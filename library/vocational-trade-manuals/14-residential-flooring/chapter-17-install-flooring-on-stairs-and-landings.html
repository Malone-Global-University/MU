Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install an approved finish flooring and nosing system on one controlled stair and landing section. The finished work product is a complete stair and landing finish: riser finish, tread finish, listed stair nosing with full support, and landing flooring, all installed to the exact rise, run, and nosing dimensions recorded from the structural stair.

You will measure every tread, riser, nosing, and landing; confirm the structural stair has already passed inspection; barricade the stair and provide alternate access; dry-fit and cut every finish piece to the actual stair dimensions; install riser finish, tread finish, and nosing in the specified sequence; install landing flooring; and inspect every step for movement, loose edges, proud fasteners, sharp corners, slip hazards, and trip conditions before the stair reopens to normal traffic.

This project depends on an already-accepted structural stair. It does not authorize any change to stair geometry. The Chapter 16 doorway-undercutting skill and the finish-flooring installation skills built in Chapters 6 through 13 are applied here to the added constraints of a walking surface with a fall hazard.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-097 — Inspect stair substrate for structural acceptance, damage, and geometry before finish work begins.
  • FL-098 — Cut stair treads or coverings to the actual measured dimensions of each step.
  • FL-099 — Install stair nosing with full support and the correct projection.
  • FL-100 — Secure flooring on landings while preserving movement and transition requirements.
  • FL-101 — Maintain safe edges, uniform geometry, and slip resistance across every step.
  • FL-102 — Inspect the completed stair finish for movement, trip hazards, and safe reopening.

Where This Skill Fits

Stair finishing sits downstream of every subfloor, flatness, and structural competency built earlier in this book, and it applies the material-specific installation methods from Chapters 6 through 13 to a walking surface where uneven rise, an unsupported nosing, or a proud fastener is a fall hazard rather than a cosmetic defect. The measurement discipline built here — recording rise, run, tread depth, and nosing projection for every step rather than assuming uniformity — is the same discipline reused in Chapter 19's final quality inspection and in Chapter 20's capstone stair or landing interface.

This project also reinforces the stop-work discipline from Chapter 1: a stair that has not already passed a structural acceptance is not a valid stair-finishing project, regardless of how sound the tread material or adhesive system is.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • measuring every tread, riser, nosing, and landing on one controlled, already-accepted stair and landing section
  • barricading the stair and providing alternate access before finish work begins
  • cleaning and repairing only the finish-level stair substrate, not the structural stair itself
  • dry-fitting and cutting tread, riser, plank, tile, carpet, or resilient pieces to the actual stair dimensions
  • installing riser finish, tread finish, and listed stair nosing with full support in the specified sequence
  • installing landing flooring while preserving movement and transition requirements
  • inspecting every step for movement, loose edges, proud fasteners, sharp corners, slip hazards, inconsistent geometry, and trip conditions before the stair reopens

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • alter stair rise, run, tread depth, nosing projection, landing geometry, guard, or handrail conditions — those are structural conditions outside this finish-flooring chapter
  • proceed with any finish work on a stair that is loose, uneven beyond the approved finish correction, damaged, or noncompliant with the approved stair details
  • rely on finish flooring alone to support a projecting nosing
  • reopen the stair to normal traffic before every unsafe condition identified during inspection has been corrected

Any stair found loose, structurally uneven, damaged, or noncompliant during this project's inspection steps must be corrected by a qualified structural professional before finish work continues.

Trade Note

Every dimension used in this chapter is taken from the actual stair, not from a nominal or design dimension. Stairs settle, and hand-framed stairs commonly carry small rise and run variations from step to step. A finish system installed from a single averaged measurement, instead of the measured dimension of each individual tread, riser, and nosing, is the most common cause of an uneven stair finish documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not begin cutting or installing any tread, riser, nosing, or landing piece until the structural stair has been confirmed as already accepted, every tread, riser, nosing, and landing has been individually measured and recorded, and the stair has been barricaded with alternate access established. A stair finish installed from assumed uniform dimensions must be treated as incomplete and corrected before the stair reopens.

Shop Tip

Number and label every tread, riser, and nosing piece to its exact step location as soon as it is cut. Stair steps commonly carry small dimensional differences from one to the next, and a piece cut for one step rarely fits another step cleanly, even on the same stair.

Stair finishing applies the same substrate, layout, cutting, and fastening skills used throughout this book, with one added constraint that overrides every other consideration: the finished stair must remain a safe walking surface with uniform geometry at every step.

Structural Confirmation, Measurement, and Access Control

Every measurement taken in this chapter reflects the actual stair, not a nominal or averaged dimension. Record rise, run, tread depth, nosing projection, and landing dimensions individually for every step before cutting any finish material. Where a stair is found loose, uneven beyond the approved finish correction, damaged, or noncompliant, stop finish work at that step and escalate per the Failure Points section before continuing.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Finish an Unaccepted Stair

Do not begin any stair-finishing step on a stair that has not already passed structural inspection and acceptance. A stair that is loose, uneven beyond the approved finish correction, damaged, or noncompliant with the approved stair details is a fall hazard. Stop work, barricade the stair, and escalate to a qualified structural professional before any finish material is cut or installed.

Field Safety Cue — Barricading and Alternate Access

Barricade the stair and provide a clearly communicated alternate access route before beginning any cutting, adhesive, or fastening step. Do not leave a partially finished stair open to normal traffic between work sessions.

Measurement Cue — Measure Every Step Individually

Measure rise, run, tread depth, and nosing projection at every individual step rather than applying a single averaged dimension to the entire stair. Small step-to-step variations are common in framed stairs, and a finish system cut from one averaged dimension is the most common cause of an uneven finished stair.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Nosing Support and Fastening

Install the listed stair nosing with the full support method specified by the manufacturer for the selected tread material. Do not rely on finish flooring alone to support a projecting nosing. Use the exact adhesive, fastener type, spacing, and cure or set time specified for the tread, riser, nosing, and landing materials selected for this project.

E. Review, Structural Confirmation, and Measurement

  1. Review the approved stair finish, nosing, tread, riser, landing, adhesive, fastener, slip resistance, and geometry details. Confirm the finish schedule matches the stair section defined in the Stair and Landing Identification Record.
  2. Confirm the structural stair has passed inspection. Locate and review the written structural acceptance record before proceeding to Step 3.
  3. Measure every tread, riser, nosing, and landing. Measure each step individually rather than applying an averaged dimension.
  4. Record rise, run, tread depth, nosing projection, width, and transition conditions. Complete this record for every step before cutting any finish material.
  5. Stop work if the stair is loose, uneven beyond the approved finish correction, damaged, or noncompliant. Escalate per the Failure Points section before continuing.

F. Access Control and Substrate Preparation

  1. Barricade the stair and provide alternate access. Confirm the alternate route is communicated to all occupants before work begins.
  2. Remove only finish materials assigned to the project. Do not remove or disturb any structural stair component.
  3. Clean and repair the stair substrate. Vacuum dust and debris, and correct only finish-level substrate conditions assigned to this project.

G. Dry-Fit and Cutting

  1. Dry-fit tread, riser, plank, tile, carpet, or resilient pieces. Label each dry-fit piece to its exact step location.
  2. Cut each piece to the actual stair dimensions. Use the individual measurement recorded for that step, not an averaged dimension.

H. Riser, Tread, and Nosing Installation

  1. Install riser finish in the specified sequence. Confirm each riser piece is seated fully before installing the tread above it.
  2. Install tread finish using the approved adhesive and fasteners. Follow the exact coverage, spacing, and fastening schedule specified for the selected tread material.
  3. Install the listed stair nosing with full support. Confirm the nosing profile matches the approved schedule and achieves the specified projection at every tread edge.
  4. Do not rely on finish flooring alone to support a projecting nosing. Verify the full-support method specified by the manufacturer is in place before proceeding to the next step.
  5. Maintain uniform finished rise and tread depth. Check each completed step against the adjoining steps before installing the next riser.

I. Landing Finish and Fastener Control

  1. Install landing flooring while preserving movement and transition requirements. Follow the material-specific installation method from the applicable earlier chapter for the selected landing finish.
  2. Keep fasteners below walking surfaces and away from concealed services. Confirm fastener depth and location before moving to the next step or landing area.
  3. Clean adhesive or mortar before cure. Remove excess material from tread, riser, nosing, and landing surfaces before it sets.
  4. Protect the stair through cure. Maintain the barricade and alternate access until the specified cure or set time has passed.

J. Final Stair Inspection and Reopening

  1. Inspect every step for movement, loose edges, proud fasteners, sharp corners, slip hazards, inconsistent geometry, and trip conditions. Walk and load each step methodically, including the nosing of every tread.
  2. Correct any unsafe condition before reopening the stair. Do not remove the barricade until every condition identified in this step has been corrected and reinspected.
  3. Document final stair measurements and installation. Record the completed measurements, materials, fastening details, and final inspection results on the stair-finishing record.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed stair and landing section using the same checks regardless of the finish material selected.

  • Structural check: confirm the structural acceptance record was reviewed before any finish work began.
  • Measurement check: confirm rise, run, tread depth, and nosing projection were recorded individually for every step.
  • Access check: confirm the stair was barricaded and alternate access was provided before work began.
  • Nosing check: confirm every nosing piece is installed with full support and correct projection.
  • Geometry check: confirm finished rise and tread depth are uniform across every step.
  • Fastener check: confirm no proud, missing, or improperly seated fastener remains at any location.
  • Safety check: confirm no movement, loose edge, sharp corner, slip hazard, or trip condition remains before the stair reopens.

Complete the stair-finishing record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Project 18.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Step Rocks or Deflects Under Load After Finish Installation

Probable cause: the finish piece was not fully seated in adhesive, a fastener missed its intended structural location, or an underlying structural condition was not resolved before finish work began.
Confirmation: reload the step methodically and inspect fastener seating and adhesive coverage at the affected tread or riser.
Correction: if the cause is finish-level, remove and reinstall the affected piece per the approved adhesive and fastener schedule; if the cause is structural, stop work, barricade the step, and escalate to a qualified structural professional.
Reinspection: reload and recheck the corrected step before continuing to adjoining steps.

Finished Rise or Tread Depth Is Uneven Between Steps

Probable cause: a finish piece was cut from an averaged dimension instead of the individually measured dimension for that step, or a labeled piece was installed at the wrong step location.
Confirmation: re-measure the affected step and compare against the recorded individual measurement and the adjoining steps.
Correction: remove and replace the affected piece with one cut to the correct individual dimension.
Reinspection: re-measure the corrected step and confirm uniformity with adjoining steps before proceeding.

A Nosing Edge Feels Unsupported or Flexes Underfoot

Probable cause: the nosing was installed relying on finish flooring alone for support, or the specified full-support method was not completed before the nosing was fastened.
Confirmation: press firmly along the full length of the nosing and compare its deflection against an adjoining, properly supported nosing.
Correction: remove the nosing, install the full-support method specified by the manufacturer, and reinstall the nosing per the approved fastening schedule.
Reinspection: reload the corrected nosing and confirm it does not deflect before reopening the step.

A Proud Fastener or Sharp Edge Is Found During Final Inspection

Probable cause: a fastener was not fully seated, or a cut edge was not dressed after trimming.
Confirmation: visually and physically inspect the affected tread, riser, nosing, or landing edge.
Correction: reset or replace the proud fastener, or dress the sharp edge per the approved finish schedule.
Reinspection: re-inspect the corrected location before including it in the final safety walk-through.

A Slip Hazard Is Identified on a Finished Tread

Probable cause: the installed tread material does not match the slip-resistance requirement stated in the approved finish schedule, or a finish coating altered surface traction.
Confirmation: compare the installed tread surface against the slip-resistance requirement in the approved finish schedule.
Correction: correct the surface per the approved finish schedule, or escalate to the responsible party for a schedule revision before the stair reopens.
Reinspection: re-verify the corrected surface against the slip-resistance requirement before reopening the stair.

The Stair Was Not Barricaded Before Work Began

Probable cause: access control was skipped or removed before finish work was complete.
Confirmation: check the stair-finishing record for a documented barricade and alternate-access entry.
Correction: stop work, re-barricade the stair, and confirm alternate access before resuming any cutting, adhesive, or fastening step.
Reinspection: confirm access control is documented before continuing the project.

Cleanup

This project ends with the stair and landing section fully finished, inspected, and either reopened to normal traffic or still barricaded pending a correction identified during final inspection.

  • Remove and properly store cutting tools, fastening tools, and adhesive equipment
  • Clean adhesive, mortar, grout, dust, and installation residue from every tread, riser, nosing, and landing surface
  • Leave the barricade in place until every item on the final safety inspection has passed
  • Store the stair-finishing record with the PPE and documentation station for reference by later projects

Field Terms

Rise
The vertical distance from the top of one tread's walking surface to the top of the next tread's walking surface, measured at each individual step.
Run
The horizontal distance from the face of one riser to the face of the next riser, measured at each individual step.
Tread Depth
The horizontal distance from the leading edge of the nosing to the face of the riser above, measured at each individual step.
Nosing Projection
The horizontal distance the tread nosing extends beyond the face of the riser below, measured at each individual step.
Structural Acceptance
The documented condition confirming the framed or fabricated stair has already passed inspection for rise, run, support, and safety before finish work begins.
Full-Support Method
The manufacturer-specified backing, blocking, or fastening method that supports a projecting nosing independent of the finish flooring material.
Landing
A flat platform surface at the top, bottom, or an intermediate point of the stair run.
Trip Condition
Any uneven, loose, sharp, or projecting condition on a finished stair or landing surface capable of catching a foot or causing a fall.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to install a complete stretch-in carpet system in one small room: tack strip around the perimeter, cushion cut and seamed to fit inside the tack strip, carpet seamed and positioned, the carpet power-stretched and hooked onto the tack strip, and the perimeter trimmed and tucked. The finished work product is one completed stretch-in carpet installation with secure tack strip, wrinkle-free cushion, controlled seams, full power-stretch engagement, trimmed and tucked edges, and clean transitions at every doorway and obstacle.

You will lay out and fasten tack strip, install and seam carpet cushion, roll out and seam the carpet, hook and power-stretch the carpet onto the tack strip using the power stretcher in the required sequence, trim and tuck the perimeter, and fit the carpet around vents, posts, and obstacles.

This project depends on the substrate inspection completed in Chapter 1, the moisture and flatness conditions verified there, and the work-area preparation completed in Chapter 2. It follows directly from the transition-planning discipline used throughout Chapters 6 through 13 and feeds directly into Chapter 15 transition and threshold work, Chapter 16 doorway fitting, Chapter 18 carpet-repair procedures, and the Chapter 19 final inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-079 — Install tack strip around the perimeter of the room with the correct gap and fastening method for the substrate.
  • FL-080 — Cut and fit carpet padding (cushion) to the room, seamed and fastened without wrinkles.
  • FL-081 — Seam padding using the approved tape or staple method without overlapping seams.
  • FL-082 — Position carpet in the room with correct pattern, seam orientation, and fitting allowance.
  • FL-083 — Stretch carpet using a power stretcher in the required hook-and-stretch sequence, supplemented only as permitted by a knee kicker.
  • FL-084 — Trim carpet edges and inspect the completed installation for seams, wrinkles, stretch, and edge engagement.

Where This Skill Fits

Stretch-in carpet is a complete system: tack strip, cushion, seams, stretch, and edge securement all depend on one another. A tack strip installed with the wrong gap, a cushion seam that overlaps, or a carpet that is kicked into place instead of power-stretched will produce a floor that wrinkles, ripples, or pulls loose from the tack strip within months, regardless of how the carpet itself looks on installation day.

The substrate-readiness discipline from Chapter 1, the doorway-clearance and transition awareness used in Chapters 15 and 16, and the material-handling habits from Chapter 2 all carry directly into this project. The seaming and stretch procedures taught here are also the foundation for the carpet-patch procedure in Chapter 18 and the carpet-specific checks performed during the Chapter 19 final inspection.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • reviewing carpet type, cushion, tack strip, seam plan, stretch requirement, transitions, stairs, door clearance, and environmental limits
  • laying out and fastening tack strip around the room perimeter with the specified gap from walls and fixed objects
  • installing and seaming carpet cushion perpendicular or as specified relative to carpet seams
  • rolling out, seaming, and positioning carpet with correct pattern and seam orientation
  • power-stretching the carpet onto the tack strip in the required hook-and-stretch sequence
  • trimming and tucking the perimeter, fitting around vents, posts, and obstacles, and installing transitions and edge bars
  • inspecting seams, wrinkles, stretch, edge engagement, door clearance, and pile direction

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • install carpet over a substrate that has not passed the Chapter 1 moisture, flatness, and structural inspection
  • substitute a knee kicker for the required power stretcher except for positioning and limited detail work
  • fasten tack strip, transitions, or edge bars into a floating floor or in a manner that conflicts with an adjoining floor system
  • disturb any suspect legacy flooring or adhesive beneath the existing substrate before it has been screened per Chapter 1
  • cut, notch, or otherwise alter stair rise, run, tread depth, nosing, or landing geometry — that work is limited to Chapter 17

Any substrate, moisture, or structural condition that fails verification in Chapter 1 must be corrected before this project begins.

Trade Note

Every stretch-in carpet installation is confirmed by full power-stretch engagement across the entire field, not by how tight the carpet feels at the point where a knee kicker was last used. Substituting knee-kicker tension for a properly sequenced power stretch is the single most common cause of a rippled or loose carpet documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Shop Tip

Dry-lay the seam location before cutting cushion or carpet, and record the tack-strip gap, cushion type, and seam-tape product on the installation record the moment each is confirmed. A seam cut in the wrong location or a cushion gap that traps the tack strip cannot be corrected without removing and re-fitting material.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every stretch-in carpet installation depends on the same sequence: a secure tack-strip perimeter, a flat and seamed cushion layer, correctly oriented and seamed carpet, full power-stretch engagement, and a trimmed, tucked, secure edge. Skipping or shortcutting any one of these steps produces a floor that will fail within months even if it looks acceptable on installation day.

Tack Strip, Cushion, and Seam Fundamentals

Every tack-strip and cushion installation in this chapter is based on the room dimensions and substrate type confirmed in Chapter 1, fastened using the method compatible with that substrate, and installed at the exact gap specified for the carpet thickness being used. Do not substitute an estimated gap or a fastening method not compatible with the identified substrate.

Measurement Cue — Confirm Tack-Strip Gap Before Fastening

Measure and mark the specified gap between the wall and the inner edge of the tack strip before fastening any section. A gap that is too narrow will not receive the trimmed carpet edge; a gap that is too wide will leave the tack strip exposed after trimming.

Critical Safety Cue — Tack-Strip Pins

Tack-strip pins are sharp and angled to grip carpet backing. Keep pin rows angled toward the wall during installation, keep hands clear of the pin field when positioning or fastening strip, and use knee pads and work gloves whenever kneeling or working near an installed strip.

Field Safety Cue — Power Stretcher Operation

Set up the power stretcher with the tail block braced against a wall protector, never directly against unprotected finished trim or drywall. Keep hands and knees clear of the stretcher head while it is under load, and release tension fully before repositioning the extension poles.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Seam and Cushion Compatibility

Use the exact seam-tape or seam-system product, cushion type, and cushion-to-carpet orientation specified for the selected carpet product. Do not substitute a different seaming method or cushion thickness without confirming compatibility with the carpet manufacturer's installation instructions.

F. Review, Substrate Confirmation, and Layout

  1. Review carpet type, cushion, tack strip, seam plan, stretch requirement, transitions, stairs, door clearance, and environmental limits. Confirm each item against the approved project specification before proceeding.
  2. Confirm the substrate is clean, dry, smooth, and free of projections. Verify this against the Chapter 1 Flooring-Readiness Plan for the room.
  3. Inspect walls, doorways, cabinets, heat sources, floor vents, and transitions. Record the location of every feature that will affect tack-strip layout, cushion cuts, or carpet fitting.

G. Tack-Strip Installation

  1. Lay out tack strips around the room. Mark the perimeter line on the substrate before cutting or fastening any strip.
  2. Maintain the specified gap from walls and fixed objects. Confirm the gap at multiple points along each wall run before fastening.
  3. Cut and fasten tack strips using methods compatible with wood or concrete substrate. Use the fastener and spacing specified in the Fastener and Hardware Schedule for the identified substrate.
  4. Keep strip pins angled toward the wall. Verify pin direction on every section before moving to the next wall run.
  5. Protect hands and finished surfaces from pins. Wear work gloves and keep installed strip covered or flagged until cushion installation begins.

H. Cushion Installation and Seaming

  1. Lay out cushion perpendicular or as specified relative to carpet seams. Confirm the cushion orientation against the approved seam plan before cutting.
  2. Cut cushion to fit inside tack strips. Trim each piece so it does not overlap or cover the tack strip.
  3. Fasten or adhere cushion without wrinkles. Work from the center of each piece outward, smoothing as you fasten.
  4. Seam cushion using the approved tape or staples. Butt adjoining pieces edge to edge before securing the seam.
  5. Do not overlap cushion seams. Trim any overlapping edge flush before proceeding to carpet installation.

I. Carpet Seaming and Positioning

  1. Roll out carpet and allow it to relax as required. Follow the relaxation period specified for the carpet product before cutting or seaming.
  2. Establish pattern and seam orientation. Confirm pile direction and pattern match at every planned seam location before cutting.
  3. Cut carpet with controlled fitting allowance. Leave the specified excess at every wall and seam for trimming after stretching.
  4. Prepare seam edges. Trim factory edges straight using a straightedge and hook-blade knife, and use row-running pliers or shears where required to align pile rows.
  5. Join carpet seams using the approved hot-melt or alternative system. Activate the seam tape with the seaming iron per manufacturer instructions and press the seam closed while the adhesive is workable.
  6. Position the carpet in the room. Center the seamed carpet over the cushion field with the fitting allowance distributed evenly at the walls.

J. Power Stretching and Perimeter Securement

  1. Hook one corner and use a power stretcher in the required sequence. Begin at the starting corner specified in the project plan and stretch toward each adjoining wall in sequence, hooking the carpet onto the tack strip as each section reaches full stretch.
  2. Use a knee kicker only for positioning and limited detail work, not as a substitute for required power stretching. Reserve the knee kicker for corners, closets, and short returns the power stretcher cannot reach directly.
  3. Engage carpet on tack strips. Confirm the backing is fully hooked onto the pins along the entire perimeter before trimming.
  4. Trim perimeter edges. Cut the excess carpet along the wall line using the stair tool and trimming knife, working in short controlled passes.
  5. Tuck carpet securely between tack strip and wall. Use the stair tool to press the trimmed edge fully into the gap along every wall run.
  6. Cut around vents, posts, and obstacles. Fit each obstacle cleanly with relief cuts limited to the immediate area of the obstacle.
  7. Install transitions and edge bars. Install each transition per the Chapter 15 plan without restricting the movement of any adjoining floating floor.

K. Inspection and Documentation

  1. Inspect seams, wrinkles, stretch, edge engagement, door clearance, and pile direction. Walk the full field and check every seam, wall run, and transition against the Acceptance Standards table.
  2. Document cushion, tack strip, seam, stretch, and transitions. Record product information, seam locations, and inspection results on the installation record before closing out the project.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed carpet installation using the same checks regardless of room size or carpet pattern.

  • Tack-strip check: confirm the strip is continuous, securely fastened, and set at the correct gap with no exposed pins after trimming.
  • Cushion check: confirm the field is wrinkle-free with seams butted and not overlapping.
  • Seam check: confirm every carpet seam is flat, secure, and matched in pattern and pile direction.
  • Stretch check: confirm the full field was power-stretched in the required sequence with no wrinkles, ripples, or loose areas.
  • Edge check: confirm every wall run is trimmed cleanly and tucked securely between the tack strip and wall.
  • Obstacle and transition check: confirm every vent, post, doorway, and adjoining floor is fitted and transitioned per the approved plan.
  • Documentation check: confirm the installation record is fully completed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Carpet Ripples or Wrinkles After Installation

Probable cause: the field was kicked into place with a knee kicker instead of power-stretched, or the power stretcher was not used in the full required hook-and-stretch sequence across every wall.
Confirmation: inspect the field under raking light and check whether every wall received a power-stretcher pass in the correct sequence.
Correction: release the carpet from the affected tack-strip sections and re-stretch using the power stretcher in the required sequence.
Reinspection: repeat the stretch check across the full field once re-stretching is complete.

A Seam Is Visible or Peaking

Probable cause: pile direction was not matched across the seam, the seam tape did not bond fully, or the seam was not pressed while the adhesive was still workable.
Confirmation: inspect the seam under raking light from both directions and check for open sections or mismatched pile.
Correction: reheat the seam tape and re-press the seam, or cut out and re-seam the affected section using row-running pliers to align pile rows.
Reinspection: repeat the seam check after the seam has fully cooled and set.

Tack Strip Is Exposed or Carpet Will Not Tuck

Probable cause: the tack-strip gap was installed too wide, too narrow, or inconsistently along the wall run.
Confirmation: measure the gap at multiple points along the affected wall and compare against the specified dimension.
Correction: remove and reposition the affected tack-strip section at the correct gap before re-trimming the carpet edge.
Reinspection: repeat the trim-and-tuck check along the corrected wall run.

Cushion Seam Overlaps or Bunches Under the Carpet

Probable cause: cushion pieces were not trimmed flush before seaming, or excess cushion was left inside the tack-strip perimeter.
Confirmation: lift the affected area of carpet and inspect the cushion seam for overlap or bunching.
Correction: trim the cushion seam flush and re-secure before re-stretching the affected area of carpet.
Reinspection: repeat the cushion check before final stretch and trim of the affected area.

Carpet Pulls Loose from Tack Strip Along One Wall

Probable cause: insufficient stretch tension was applied at that wall, or the tack strip was not securely fastened to the substrate.
Confirmation: inspect the tack-strip fastening at the affected wall and check whether the carpet backing shows full pin engagement.
Correction: re-fasten any loose tack-strip section, then re-stretch the affected area with the power stretcher and re-hook onto the strip.
Reinspection: repeat the edge-engagement check along the corrected wall run.

A Transition Restricts Movement of an Adjoining Floating Floor

Probable cause: the transition or edge bar was fastened through the field of an adjoining floating floor or installed without the required movement space.
Confirmation: inspect the transition fastening against the Chapter 15 transition plan for the adjoining floor system.
Correction: remove and reinstall the transition using the fastening method and clearance specified for the adjoining floating floor.
Reinspection: confirm the adjoining floor can move freely after the transition is corrected.

Field Terms

Tack Strip
A narrow strip of wood or composite material studded with angled pins, fastened around a room perimeter to grip and hold stretched carpet.
Tack-Strip Gap
The specified distance between the inner edge of the tack strip and the wall, sized to receive the tucked carpet edge.
Cushion
The padding layer installed between the substrate and the carpet, cut and seamed to fit inside the tack-strip perimeter.
Power Stretcher
A tool with extension poles and a tail block that applies controlled, even tension to carpet across a room in a defined hook-and-stretch sequence.
Knee Kicker
A hand tool used to position carpet or complete limited detail work, not a substitute for the required power stretch.
Hook-and-Stretch Sequence
The specified order in which carpet is stretched from a starting corner toward each remaining wall and hooked onto the tack strip.
Seam Line
The location where two pieces of cushion or carpet are joined, planned to minimize visibility and avoid high-traffic areas where possible.
Pile Direction
The direction carpet fibers lean, matched across a seam to prevent a visible color or texture break.
Fitting Allowance
The extra carpet width and length left beyond the finished room dimension to permit trimming after stretching.

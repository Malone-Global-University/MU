Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect a room or practice structure and produce a documented flooring-readiness plan before any flooring material is selected or installed. The finished work product is one completed Flooring-Readiness Plan: a substrate identification, a set of recorded moisture-test results, a flatness survey with marked high and low points, a hazard and legacy-material screening, a transition and finish-height review, and a documented approved, conditional, or rejected readiness decision.

You will identify the existing substrate; inspect for moisture sources; measure ambient temperature and relative humidity where required; perform the specified moisture tests; inspect wood and concrete substrates for damage; measure flatness; inspect transitions, finish heights, and hazardous legacy materials; and select a compatible flooring system only after every condition has been verified.

This is the first project in the book. Every later chapter — subfloor repair, flatness correction, underlayment, plank and resilient flooring, tile, carpet, transitions, stairs, repairs, final inspection, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on the substrate, moisture, and flatness findings documented here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FL-001 — Inspect subfloor condition, including substrate type, damage, and support.
  • FL-002 — Identify moisture risks affecting the work area.
  • FL-003 — Check floor flatness using the manufacturer-specified straightedge or measuring method.
  • FL-004 — Identify squeaks and movement in wood subfloors.
  • FL-005 — Select flooring type based on verified substrate, moisture, flatness, and use conditions.
  • FL-006 — Document flooring readiness in a completed Flooring-Readiness Plan.

Where This Skill Fits

Every project in this book — from underlayment and moisture-barrier work in Chapter 5, to plank and resilient flooring in Chapters 6 through 10, to tile in Chapters 11 through 13, to carpet, stairs, repairs, and the Chapter 20 capstone — depends on the substrate condition, moisture readings, and flatness survey recorded here. A missed moisture source, an unverified flatness deviation, or an unscreened legacy material is the most common cause of flooring failure, callback, and rework in residential flooring work.

The documentation habit established in this chapter — recording substrate type, moisture-test method and results, flatness deviations, and hazardous-material screening — is the same discipline reused for the work-area preparation record in Chapter 2 and the final quality inspection in Chapter 19.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • identifying the existing substrate as plywood, oriented-strand board, plank, concrete, underlayment panel, resilient flooring, tile, or another approved surface
  • inspecting the room for roof, plumbing, exterior-wall, slab, crawlspace, and condensation moisture sources
  • performing the moisture tests specified for the substrate and candidate flooring products
  • inspecting wood subfloors for loose panels, squeaks, deflection, delamination, and damage, and concrete for cracks, curling, spalling, and contamination
  • measuring floor flatness and distinguishing flatness from levelness
  • inspecting transitions, finish-height conflicts, and old resilient flooring or other suspect legacy materials
  • selecting an approved flooring type only after confirming moisture, flatness, deflection, use, and manufacturer compatibility
  • issuing a documented approved, conditional, or rejected flooring-readiness decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • disturb, sand, grind, scrape, saw, or otherwise mechanically abrade any suspect legacy flooring, felt, mastic, resilient tile, sheet flooring, or pipe insulation before hazardous-material risk has been cleared
  • repair, patch, level, or replace any subfloor material — that work is covered in Chapters 3 and 4
  • install any underlayment, moisture barrier, or finish flooring — that work is covered in Chapters 5 through 17
  • proceed with material selection or installation planning when a stop-work condition identified in this chapter is present and uncorrected

Any substrate, moisture, flatness, or legacy-material condition that fails verification in this chapter must be corrected, tested by a qualified professional, or escalated before flooring work continues.

Trade Note

Every moisture determination in this chapter is confirmed by the test method specified for the substrate and candidate flooring product — never estimated from appearance, room humidity, or how the floor feels underfoot. Substituting a surface observation for the specified test is the single most common cause of a moisture-related flooring failure documented in this chapter's troubleshooting section.

Setup Standard

Do not select a flooring system or proceed to Chapter 2 work-area preparation until the substrate has been identified, the specified moisture tests have been performed and recorded, flatness has been measured against the manufacturer's tolerance, and any suspect legacy material has been screened. A readiness decision made before every check is complete must be treated as incomplete and not relied upon.

Shop Tip

Record the moisture-meter model, calibration status, and test locations on the Flooring-Readiness Plan the moment each reading is taken. A reading recorded without its location and equipment record cannot be verified later if the floor fails, and re-testing after finish flooring is installed is far more disruptive than testing correctly the first time.

Substrate Identification, Moisture Testing, and Verification

Every moisture determination made in this chapter is based on the test method specified for the identified substrate and the candidate flooring product, performed at representative locations across the inspection area. Do not substitute a single reading, a surface-only observation, or a general room-humidity reading for the specified test. Where the flooring manufacturer requires a specific test method, equipment calibration, number of test locations, or acclimation period before testing, follow that requirement exactly and record it on the plan.

Measurement Cue — Test at Representative Locations, Not a Single Spot

Perform moisture and flatness measurements at multiple representative locations across the inspection area, including areas near exterior walls, plumbing fixtures, and any visible moisture staining. A single favorable reading near the center of a room does not confirm the condition of the entire inspection area.

Critical Safety Cue — Suspect Legacy Flooring Materials

Do not sand, grind, scrape, saw, or otherwise mechanically disturb any existing resilient flooring, sheet flooring, floor tile, felt backing, black mastic, or pipe insulation of unknown age or composition. Treat any such material as a suspect legacy material and stop work until it has been screened by a qualified hazardous-material professional. Disturbing suspect material before it is cleared can release hazardous fibers or dust.

Field Safety Cue — Crawlspace, Attic, and Low-Light Access

Inspect crawlspace, attic, and other low-light or confined access points only with adequate lighting, ventilation, and footing. Do not enter a confined space to trace a moisture source without the controls and supervision required for that access.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Moisture Limits and Test Methods

Use the exact moisture-test method, equipment calibration procedure, number of test locations, and acceptable moisture range specified by the flooring manufacturer for the identified substrate and candidate flooring product. Do not substitute a different test method or assume a moisture limit that is not stated in the manufacturer's instructions or the approved chapter project specification.

E. Documentation Review and Substrate Identification

  1. Obtain the flooring plan, finish schedule, product requirements, substrate information, room use, and transition details. Note any information that identifies the existing substrate or flags a prior moisture or structural issue.
  2. Identify the existing substrate as plywood, oriented-strand board, plank, concrete, underlayment panel, resilient flooring, tile, or another approved surface. State the identified substrate in writing before proceeding to Step 3.

F. Moisture Source and Environmental Inspection

  1. Inspect the room for roof, plumbing, exterior-wall, slab, crawlspace, and condensation moisture sources. Record every source found, even if it does not appear to be actively leaking.
  2. Record ambient temperature and relative humidity where required by the candidate flooring product. Use the thermometer and hygrometer at a representative location away from direct sun or drafts.

G. Moisture Testing

  1. Perform the specified substrate moisture tests at representative locations. Use a pin or pinless meter for wood substrates, and a calcium chloride or in-situ relative-humidity test for concrete where specified by the flooring manufacturer.
  2. Record test method, equipment, calibration status, locations, results, and product limits. Complete this record for every test location before moving to the next inspection step.

H. Structural and Surface Inspection

  1. Inspect wood subfloors for loose panels, squeaks, deflection, delamination, swelling, edge ridges, damaged fasteners, unsupported seams, and rot. Record the location and extent of every condition found.
  2. Inspect concrete for cracks, curling, spalling, laitance, contamination, adhesive residue, curing compounds, moisture, and elevation changes. Record the location and extent of every condition found.

I. Flatness Survey

  1. Check floor flatness using the manufacturer-specified straightedge or measuring method. Place the straightedge across multiple directions of the inspection area.
  2. Mark high spots and low areas. Use feeler gauges or graduated shims to record the measured deviation at each marked point.
  3. Distinguish flatness from levelness. Record flatness deviations against the manufacturer's tolerance independent of whether the floor is pitched or sloped for drainage or design.

J. Transition, Finish-Height, and Hazard Screening

  1. Inspect transitions to adjacent rooms, stairs, cabinets, doorways, exterior doors, and appliances. Measure and record the existing finished-floor height at each transition point.
  2. Identify finished-floor height conflicts. Compare the existing height at each transition against the height that would result from each candidate flooring system.
  3. Inspect for old resilient flooring, black adhesive, felt backing, or other materials that may require hazardous-material testing. Mark the location and extent of any suspect material without disturbing it.
  4. Stop work before disturbing suspect materials. Escalate to a qualified hazardous-material professional per the Failure Points section before any further inspection step disturbs the suspect area.
  5. Check door swing, jamb clearance, toilet or fixture interfaces, baseboard, floor drains, radiant heat, and expansion joints. Record each condition and its location on the plan.

K. Flooring Selection and Readiness Decision

  1. Compare substrate conditions with each candidate flooring system. Check moisture results, flatness deviations, structural condition, and transition findings against the requirements of every flooring type under consideration.
  2. Select the approved flooring type only after confirming moisture, flatness, deflection, use, and manufacturer compatibility. Do not select a flooring type that conflicts with any recorded finding.
  3. Prepare a deficiency list with required structural, moisture, patching, underlayment, transition, and trade corrections. Assign each deficiency to the chapter or qualified trade responsible for correcting it.
  4. Reinspect corrected conditions. Where a deficiency has already been corrected before this project closes out, repeat the relevant test or measurement to confirm the correction.
  5. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected flooring-readiness decision. Record the decision, the reasoning, and any conditions that must be satisfied before Chapter 2 work-area preparation begins.

Common Readiness Inspection

Inspect every completed inspection area using the same checks regardless of which room or flooring type the project involves.

  • Substrate check: confirm the substrate type is correctly identified and recorded.
  • Moisture check: confirm every representative location was tested using the specified method and falls within the manufacturer's acceptable range, or is documented as a stop-work condition.
  • Structural check: confirm every loose panel, squeak, crack, or contaminated area is recorded, with corrections assigned where needed.
  • Flatness check: confirm every marked high spot and low area has a recorded deviation measurement against the manufacturer's tolerance.
  • Hazard check: confirm every suspect legacy material is identified, left undisturbed, and documented with an assigned escalation.
  • Transition check: confirm every doorway, stair, cabinet, and fixed-equipment finish-height conflict is recorded.
  • Documentation check: confirm the Flooring-Readiness Plan is fully completed and signed.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Moisture Reading Exceeds the Manufacturer's Acceptable Range

Probable cause: an active moisture source, insufficient concrete cure time, elevated ambient humidity, or a slab without an effective vapor retarder.
Confirmation: re-test at additional representative locations and re-trace suspected moisture sources identified during the environmental inspection.
Correction: correct the identified moisture source, allow additional cure time, install a compatible moisture-mitigation system per manufacturer instructions, or escalate to a qualified professional before selecting flooring.
Reinspection: repeat the specified moisture test at the same locations after correction.

Flatness Deviation Exceeds the Manufacturer's Tolerance

Probable cause: subfloor deflection, an unrepaired structural condition, an uneven concrete pour, or accumulated surface buildup.
Confirmation: re-measure the deviation with the specified straightedge in multiple directions and confirm the reading is not caused by debris or an unclean surface.
Correction: record the deviation as a deficiency for Chapter 3 structural repair or Chapter 4 flatness correction before flooring selection is finalized.
Reinspection: repeat the flatness survey at the corrected location once the applicable chapter's work is complete.

Suspect Legacy Material Is Found During Inspection

Probable cause: older resilient flooring, felt backing, or mastic of unknown composition remains beneath or adjacent to the inspection area.
Confirmation: visually identify the material's location and extent without disturbing it, and compare against known indicators of legacy flooring materials.
Correction: stop work at the affected area and escalate to a qualified hazardous-material professional for testing before any further inspection or preparation step disturbs the material.
Reinspection: resume inspection only after the qualified professional has cleared or remediated the material.

A Squeak or Loose Panel Is Found During the Structural Inspection

Probable cause: a fastener has backed out, a panel edge is unsupported, or subfloor movement has developed over time.
Confirmation: walk and load the area methodically to confirm the location and extent of the movement.
Correction: record the condition as a deficiency for Chapter 3 subfloor repair before flooring selection is finalized.
Reinspection: repeat the structural inspection at the corrected location once Chapter 3 repair is complete.

A Finish-Height Conflict Is Identified at a Doorway or Transition

Probable cause: the candidate flooring system's thickness, combined with any required underlayment, produces a finished height that conflicts with an adjacent floor, door swing, or fixed equipment.
Confirmation: re-measure the existing height at the transition and calculate the resulting height for each candidate flooring system.
Correction: select a compatible flooring or underlayment thickness, or record the conflict for resolution in Chapter 15 transition planning.
Reinspection: confirm the revised height calculation before finalizing the flooring-readiness decision.

The Inspection Area Was Not Clearly Defined Before Testing Began

Probable cause: the room or rooms included in the inspection were not written down before moisture testing or flatness measurement began.
Confirmation: compare the areas actually tested against the intended scope of the assigned chapter project.
Correction: re-define the inspection area in writing and repeat any step completed without a confirmed area.
Reinspection: repeat the substrate identification, moisture, flatness, and hazard checks for the confirmed area.

Cleanup

This project ends with the inspection area undisturbed except for marked test locations, not altered further until the assigned project begins.

  • Remove and properly store moisture-test equipment and consumables
  • Leave marking tape and chalk marks in place at high spots, low areas, and suspect-material boundaries
  • Confirm any suspect-material area remains undisturbed and flagged
  • Store the Flooring-Readiness Plan with the PPE and documentation station for reference by later projects

Field Terms

Inspection Area
The exact room, rooms, or practice structure floor area the substrate, moisture, flatness, and hazard findings apply to.
Representative Test Location
A location selected to reflect the moisture and flatness condition of the inspection area, including areas near exterior walls, plumbing, and known moisture sources.
Flatness Deviation
The measured gap between the underside of the specified straightedge and the floor surface at any point along its length.
Finished-Floor Height
The elevation of the top surface of the completed flooring system at a given point, used to identify conflicts at doorways, transitions, and fixed equipment.
Suspect Legacy Material
Any existing resilient flooring, felt backing, black mastic, tile, or other material of unknown composition that must be screened before disturbance.
Moisture Vapor Emission Rate
The measured rate at which moisture vapor is emitted from a concrete slab surface, typically measured using a calcium chloride test.
In-Situ Relative Humidity
The measured relative humidity inside a concrete slab, obtained using a probe inserted to a specified depth.
Stop-Work Condition
Any observed moisture reading, structural condition, or suspect material that must halt the project until corrected, tested, or escalated.
Flooring-Readiness Plan
The document recording the substrate type, moisture-test method and results, flatness readings, structural findings, hazard screening, transition review, and final readiness decision.

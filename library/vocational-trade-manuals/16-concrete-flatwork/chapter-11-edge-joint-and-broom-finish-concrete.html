Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to time and complete edging, tooled control joints, surface finishing, and broom texture on one exterior slab that has already been placed, screeded, and bull-floated in Chapter 10. The finished work product is a slab with a cut, radiused perimeter edge; straight, correctly spaced and correctly deep control joints; a finish appropriate to the specified exterior surface; and a uniform broom texture free of tears, ridges, and drag marks.

You will observe bleed water, surface sheen, and firmness rather than a fixed clock time; cut the perimeter free of the forms; run the edger to a consistent radius and depth; mark and tool control joints from the approved joint plan; float or trowel only as the specified finish requires; and pull a consistent broom texture in the approved direction before protecting the finished surface and beginning the record for Chapter 12 curing.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 10's placed, struck-off, bull-floated slab. It does not reopen placement, screeding, or reinforcement decisions. Everything in this chapter is timed by the concrete's actual condition, not by a schedule, and everything finished here is handed off to Chapter 12 for curing and protection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-061 — Edge the slab perimeter to a consistent radius and depth at the proper surface firmness.
  • FW-062 — Tool control joints from the approved joint plan to specified depth, width, and line.
  • FW-063 — Time finishing operations from observed bleed water, sheen, and firmness rather than a fixed schedule.
  • FW-064 — Broom-finish the surface to a uniform, specified texture in the approved direction.
  • FW-065 — Avoid overworking concrete during edging, jointing, floating, or brooming.
  • FW-066 — Inspect the finished texture for edge quality, joint depth, uniformity, and defects.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 10 struck off, consolidated, and bull-floated the slab to the correct plane. This chapter converts that wet plane into the slab's actual working surface: its edges, its joints, and its texture. Every one of those decisions is timed by the concrete itself. Finishing too early traps bleed water beneath a sealed surface and causes scaling or dusting. Finishing too late tears the surface, produces ridges, or forces random cracking before a control joint can be cut.

The joints tooled here are carried forward without modification into Chapter 12 curing, Chapter 17's expanded joint work, and Chapter 18's crack diagnosis. A joint recorded at the wrong depth or spacing here becomes a documented deficiency, not a correction made later by guesswork.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • observing bleed water, sheen, and firmness to determine finishing readiness
  • cutting the slab perimeter free from forms and running the edger to the specified radius and depth
  • marking, transferring, and tooling control joints from the approved joint plan
  • floating or troweling only to the degree the specified exterior finish requires
  • selecting and pulling the specified broom texture in the approved direction
  • inspecting the finished texture, edges, and joints before curing begins

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin final finishing while bleed water remains visible on the surface
  • dust dry cement onto the surface or add water to the surface to make finishing easier
  • invent joint spacing, joint depth, edge radius, or a finish texture that is not supplied by the approved plan or the chapter project specification
  • apply heavy steel-trowel finishes to an exterior walking surface where the specification calls for a broom finish
  • proceed past a slab that is setting unevenly, cracking prematurely, or showing signs the concrete was overwatered or is partially set

Joint spacing, joint depth, edge radius, and finish texture must come from the approved joint plan and finish schedule. This chapter does not invent them.

Trade Note

Every finishing decision in this chapter is governed by the concrete's condition, not by the clock. Two identical slabs placed on the same day can be ready for edging, jointing, or brooming at different times depending on temperature, humidity, wind, and mix. Watch the concrete, not your watch.

Setup Standard

Do not begin edging, jointing, floating, or brooming while bleed water remains visible on the surface. Do not dust dry cement onto the surface or add water to make finishing easier. Working the surface before bleed water has left it traps water beneath the finish and causes scaling, dusting, or delamination that cannot be corrected after curing begins.

Shop Tip

Press a thumb lightly into the surface away from the finished edge. If it leaves a shallow, firm impression without water surfacing around it, the slab is generally ready for edging and jointing. If water rises around the impression, wait.

Common Field Fundamentals

Edging, jointing, and brooming are not a single event but a sequence of operations, each timed by a different concrete condition. Edging and initial floating happen earliest, once bleed water has left the surface but the concrete is still workable enough to close small tool marks. Jointing follows once the surface can hold a straight tooled line without dragging or crumbling. Brooming happens last, at the firmest condition that will still accept a texture without tearing.

Timing Governs Every Step

No single clock time applies to every slab. Temperature, humidity, wind, sun exposure, and the specific concrete mix all change how quickly bleed water leaves the surface and how quickly the slab firms up. Every step in this chapter's sequence is triggered by an observed surface condition, not by an elapsed number of minutes. Recording a step as complete because "enough time had passed," rather than because the surface actually showed the required condition, is the single most common cause of the finishing defects diagnosed later in this chapter.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Finish While Bleed Water Is Present

Do not begin edging, jointing, floating, or brooming while bleed water remains visible on the surface. Do not dust dry cement onto the surface or add water to accelerate or ease finishing. Sealing bleed water beneath a finished surface causes scaling, dusting, and delamination that cannot be corrected after curing begins.

Safety Cue — Repeated Kneeling and Tool Motions

Edging, jointing, and brooming involve repeated kneeling, reaching, and pulling motions over an extended period. Use kneeboards to distribute weight and protect knees and wrists. Rotate tasks among crew members where more than one finisher is available.

Field Safety Cue — Kneeboard Placement

Place kneeboards only on areas already struck to final elevation and firm enough to support them without denting the plane. Do not walk directly on the wet or freshly finished surface outside of kneeboard access.

Code Alert — Slip Resistance on Walking Surfaces

Verify the specified finish texture meets any applicable slip-resistance or accessibility requirement for the slab's use. Do not substitute a smoother float or trowel finish for a specified broom finish on an exterior walking surface.

FW-061 – FW-066

Concrete Finishing Qualification

Edge, Joint, and Broom-Finish an Exterior Slab

Difficulty: Intermediate

Estimated Time: 45–90 minutes, timed to concrete condition

Estimated Cost: Consumable cost only

Finished Work: One edged, jointed, textured, and inspected exterior slab surface

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one exterior slab, already placed and bull-floated per Chapter 10, finished with a cut perimeter edge, tooled control joints from the approved joint plan, the specified exterior texture, and a documented finishing and inspection record.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Observe concrete set, bleed water, surface sheen, firmness, and weather. Confirm the surface condition directly before beginning any finishing step.
  2. Do not begin final finishing while bleed water remains on the surface. Wait and reobserve rather than proceeding on an assumed schedule.
  3. Do not dust dry cement or add water to the surface. Use only the approved concrete already placed.
  4. Cut the slab perimeter free from forms using the approved edging sequence. Separate the slab edge from the form face before it bonds.
  5. Run the edger along forms at the proper surface firmness. Confirm the surface accepts the tool without tearing or closing back up.
  6. Maintain consistent edge radius and depth. Match the radius and depth specified for this slab along its full perimeter.
  7. Mark control-joint locations from the approved plan. Transfer joint spacing directly from the joint plan, not from visual estimation.
  8. Snap or transfer straight joint lines. Use a mason's line, chalk marker, or straightedge to establish each joint line before tooling.
  9. Tool joints to the specified depth and width. Use the groover and straightedge guide to hold the tool on line.
  10. Keep joint lines straight and intersections clean. Correct any wander before the joint firms in place.
  11. Float or trowel only as required for the specified exterior finish. Perform only the finishing operation the finish schedule specifies for this slab.
  12. Avoid excessive steel troweling on broom-finished exterior walking surfaces. Do not apply a dense trowel finish where the specification calls for broom texture.
  13. Allow the surface to reach broom-ready firmness. Confirm by observed firmness, not elapsed time.
  14. Select the specified broom texture. Match bristle type and pass pattern to the finish schedule.
  15. Dampen or clean the broom only as permitted. Follow the finish schedule's instructions on broom preparation.
  16. Pull the broom in the approved direction with consistent pressure. Maintain the same direction and pressure across the entire slab.
  17. Lift cleanly at the end of each pass. Avoid dragging the broom off the edge in a way that tears the texture.
  18. Overlap passes without creating ridges. Maintain a consistent overlap along the full width of the slab.
  19. Detail edges, joints, corners, and penetrations. Confirm texture and edge condition are consistent at every transition and embedded item.
  20. Protect finished areas from footprints, debris, rain, and falling material. Barricade the slab immediately after brooming.
  21. Inspect edge quality, joint depth, broom uniformity, drag marks, tears, ridges, and low spots. Walk the full slab and record findings.
  22. Correct only while the concrete remains workable and without overworking. Do not attempt corrections once the surface has begun to set.
  23. Document finishing times, weather, jointing, and texture. Record every observation and decision on the finishing log for the Chapter 12 curing record.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Finishing log: the completed record of every observation and step in Steps 1–22, including the final inspection findings.
  • Joint record: confirmed joint locations, depth, width, and layout, carried forward into Chapter 12 curing and Chapter 17 joint work.
  • Photographs: finished edges, joints, and texture, referenced in the finishing log.
  • Barricade confirmation: note that the slab has been protected from traffic and debris before leaving the site.

Common Finishing Inspection

Inspect every finished slab using the same checks regardless of finish texture.

  • Timing check: confirm no finishing step began while bleed water remained visible.
  • Edge check: confirm a consistent radius and depth along the full perimeter.
  • Joint check: confirm joint locations, spacing, depth, and width match the approved joint plan.
  • Finish-operation check: confirm only the specified finish operation was performed.
  • Texture check: confirm uniform broom or specified texture, free of ridges, tears, and drag marks.
  • Overworking check: confirm no continued reworking occurred past the point the specified finish required.
  • Protection check: confirm the slab was barricaded immediately after finishing.

Complete the Finishing Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 12.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Surface Scaling or Dusting After Curing

Probable cause: edging, jointing, floating, or brooming began while bleed water was still present, sealing water beneath the finished surface.
Confirmation: review the finishing log for the recorded surface condition at the time each step began, and compare against weather conditions that would have slowed bleed-water evaporation.
Correction: document the defect and its likely cause; this condition is not correctable by further finishing and must be evaluated for repair under Chapter 18.
Reinspection: confirm the finishing log for future slabs requires observed surface condition, not elapsed time, before each step begins.

Ridges or Drag Marks in the Broom Texture

Probable cause: inconsistent broom pressure, uneven overlap between passes, or brooming performed before the surface reached uniform firmness.
Confirmation: inspect the texture under raking light to identify ridge locations and compare against the recorded broom direction and pressure.
Correction: while the concrete remains workable, lightly refloat the affected area and rebroom with consistent pressure and overlap; do not correct once the surface has begun to set.
Reinspection: repeat the texture check across the full slab before curing begins.

Joint Depth Inconsistent Along Its Length

Probable cause: the groover was not guided by a straightedge, or downward pressure varied while tooling the joint.
Confirmation: measure joint depth at multiple points along its length and compare against the approved joint plan.
Correction: while the concrete remains workable, re-tool the joint using a straightedge guide to correct depth; do not attempt correction once the surface has set.
Reinspection: repeat the joint check along the full length of every joint.

Random Cracking Near a Joint Location

Probable cause: the control joint was tooled too late, after the concrete had already begun to crack independently near the planned joint line.
Confirmation: compare the crack location against the approved joint plan and the recorded joint-tooling time.
Correction: document the condition; do not attempt to force a joint over an existing crack. Refer the condition for evaluation under Chapter 17 or Chapter 18 as applicable.
Reinspection: confirm future joint tooling begins as soon as the surface reaches the correct firmness rather than being delayed.

Overworked Surface with Excess Fines

Probable cause: continued floating or troweling beyond what the specified finish required, drawing excess water and fine material to the surface.
Confirmation: inspect the surface for a weak, powdery, or glossy layer inconsistent with the specified texture.
Correction: document the condition on the finishing log; this defect is not correctable by further working and may require evaluation for surface durability under Chapter 18.
Reinspection: confirm future finishing stops once the specified finish operation is complete.

Edge Radius Inconsistent or Crumbling

Probable cause: the edger was run either too early, tearing the still-soft concrete, or too late, crumbling a surface that had already begun to set.
Confirmation: inspect the perimeter for tearing, crumbling, or an inconsistent radius, and compare against the recorded edging time and surface condition.
Correction: while the concrete remains workable, rerun the edger to restore a consistent radius; document any section that cannot be corrected.
Reinspection: repeat the edge check along the full perimeter before curing begins.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a finished, protected, documented slab surface, not a verbal impression of the finish.

  • Clean edgers, groovers, floats, trowels, and brooms in the contained washout area established in Chapter 2
  • Remove joint-layout lines, chalk marks, or mason's lines no longer needed for reference
  • Confirm barricades remain in place to protect the finished surface from foot traffic and debris
  • Return tools to organized, dry storage to prevent residual concrete from hardening on working surfaces
  • File the completed finishing log, joint record, and photographs together for the Chapter 12 curing record

Field Terms

Bleed Water
Water that rises to the surface of freshly placed concrete before it begins to set; finishing must not begin while it remains visible.
Broom-Ready Firmness
The observed surface condition at which concrete will accept a texture without tearing or closing back up.
Approved Joint Plan
The plan or specification stating control-joint locations, spacing, depth, and width for the slab.
Finish Schedule
The document specifying the exterior texture required for the slab.
Overworking
Continued floating, troweling, or reworking past the point required, which draws excess water and fines to the surface.
Edge Radius
The rounded profile cut into the slab's perimeter edge by the edging tool.
Control Joint
A tooled or saw-cut joint intended to control the location of shrinkage cracking.
Kneeboard
A board used to distribute a worker's weight over fresh concrete without denting the finished plane.
Drag Mark
A texture defect caused by uneven broom pressure, lifting, or premature brooming.
Finishing Log
The document recording every observation and decision made during edging, jointing, and brooming.

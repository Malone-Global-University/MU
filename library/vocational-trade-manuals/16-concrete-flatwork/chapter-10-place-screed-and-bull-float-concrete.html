Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to place, consolidate, strike off, and bull-float one small formed slab to the approved plane and elevation. The finished work product is a fully placed slab, struck to form elevation, consolidated at edges and embedded items, filled at low areas, bull-floated, and documented as ready for timed finishing.

You will confirm the pre-pour checklist from Chapters 6, 7, and 8 is complete, place concrete close to its final position without excessive movement, consolidate along forms and embedded items, screed to form height using the approved sawing motion, fill and correct low and high areas, bull-float promptly to embed aggregate and close the surface, and inspect the resulting wet-concrete plane before bleed water and finishing operations begin.

This is the tenth chapter in Book 16. It depends directly on the forms staked and squared in Chapter 6, the slope and elevation set in Chapter 7, the reinforcement placed in Chapter 8, and the mixed or delivered concrete produced in Chapter 9. It hands off a struck, consolidated, bull-floated wet-concrete plane to Chapter 11, which edges, joints, times, and broom-finishes that exact surface. Nothing in this chapter invents a slab thickness, elevation, or slump limit that those earlier chapters and the approved concrete specification do not already supply.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-055 — Place concrete evenly across the formed area without excessive movement or segregation.
  • FW-056 — Consolidate concrete along forms, corners, blockouts, and embedded items.
  • FW-057 — Screed concrete to form height using the approved sawing motion and continuous bearing.
  • FW-058 — Fill low spots and remove excess concrete from high areas before rescreeding.
  • FW-059 — Bull float the surface to embed aggregate and close the plane without overworking it.
  • FW-060 — Inspect the wet-concrete plane for defects before bleed water and finishing stages.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 9 produced concrete within the approved water, consistency, and timing controls; this chapter is where that concrete becomes a slab. Chapter 11 cannot edge, joint, or broom-finish a surface that was never struck to form elevation or properly consolidated at the edges. Chapter 12 cannot begin effective curing on a plane that traps low spots, honeycombing, or unclosed surface voids.

The discipline of timing every operation in this chapter to actual concrete behavior — not a fixed clock time — is the same discipline carried through jointing, troweling, and brooming in Chapter 11 and through the entire capstone sequence in Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • completing the pre-pour checklist for forms, base, reinforcement, joints, access, tools, crew, weather, washout, and curing
  • placing concrete close to its final position across one small formed slab
  • consolidating concrete along forms, corners, blockouts, and embedded items without over-vibrating flatwork
  • screeding to form height, filling low spots, and removing excess concrete from high areas
  • bull-floating the struck surface promptly to embed aggregate and close the plane
  • inspecting the resulting wet-concrete plane for defects before finishing begins

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin placement before the pre-pour checklist from Chapters 6, 7, and 8 is confirmed complete
  • add water to the delivered or mixed concrete beyond the approved mix design and site authorization
  • walk directly on reinforcement, or dump large piles that require excessive movement to spread
  • invent a slab thickness, elevation, slope, or slump limit not supplied by the approved plans, reinforcement schedule, or concrete specification
  • begin edging, jointing, or finishing operations covered in Chapter 11 while bleed water remains on the surface

Any placement condition that reveals unresolved form, base, or reinforcement problems must be corrected before concrete placement continues, not concealed by additional concrete.

Trade Note

Every operation in this chapter — placement, consolidation, screeding, filling, and bull floating — is timed from the actual condition of the concrete in front of you, not from a schedule. Working ahead of what the concrete's set condition allows, or falling behind it, produces the plane defects that Chapter 11 cannot finish around.

Setup Standard

Do not begin placement until the pre-pour checklist for forms, base, reinforcement, joints, access, tools, crew, weather, washout, and curing has been completed and confirmed. Concrete placed against an unverified form, base, or reinforcement condition cannot be corrected once it is struck and floated.

Shop Tip

Slightly overfill ahead of the screed rather than screeding an exactly full pass. A screed that runs short leaves a low area that is far harder to correct cleanly than trimming a small excess from a slightly overfilled pass.

Common Field Fundamentals

Placing, consolidating, screeding, and bull floating are one continuous operation, not four separate tasks performed at leisure. Concrete that sits too long before screeding stiffens unevenly; a struck plane that sits too long before floating cannot be closed without overworking the surface and drawing excess paste upward. Every step in this chapter must keep pace with the concrete actually in front of the crew.

Placement, Consolidation, and Plane as One System

A slab that is placed evenly but never properly consolidated at the edges will show honeycombing once forms are stripped in Chapter 6's stripping sequence. A slab that is well consolidated but poorly screeded will carry a low spot into every later finishing operation. A slab that is struck correctly but bull-floated too late or too aggressively will bring excess water and fines to the surface, weakening the wear surface Chapter 11 finishes. Treat placement, consolidation, screeding, and floating as a single controlled sequence rather than as separate, disconnected tasks.

Critical Safety Cue — Wet Concrete and Struck-By Hazards

Wet concrete and cementitious materials can cause severe skin and eye burns. Wear waterproof gloves, boots, long sleeves, and eye protection throughout placement, consolidation, and floating. Do not stand beneath suspended concrete buckets, pump booms, or chutes, and do not position any crew member where moving concrete, wheelbarrows, or come-alongs could strike them.

Field Safety Cue — Footing and Reinforcement Access

Do not walk directly on reinforcement during placement. Use knee boards or approved access boards for any hand access onto fresh concrete, and keep footing controlled on wet, uneven, or sloped surfaces near forms and edges.

Measurement Cue — One Struck Plane, One Reference

Screed only to the form-top elevation established in Chapter 7. Do not adjust the screed height to compensate for a form that has moved; stop and correct the form condition instead. Check slab thickness where the placement method permits, using the same elevation reference established in Chapter 1 and carried through Chapter 7.

Code Alert — Concealed Reinforcement and Pre-Pour Holds

If any required professional or code inspection of forms, base, or reinforcement has not been completed and documented, do not begin placement. Concrete placed over unverified reinforcement or an unverified base condition conceals conditions that cannot be inspected after the fact.

Manufacturer Cue — Working Time and Consistency

Place, consolidate, screed, and float within the working time and consistency limits established for the batch in Chapter 9. Do not add water to the surface or to the batch during placement to extend working time or ease finishing; any authorized water addition must follow the ready-mix or mix-design documentation and be recorded.

Concrete Placement, Screeding, and Bull Floating Work Order

Difficulty: Intermediate

Estimated Time: 1–3 hours, timed to concrete behavior

Estimated Cost: Included in Chapter 9 concrete cost

Finished Work: One struck, consolidated, bull-floated wet-concrete plane, inspected and documented

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one small formed slab, placed with concrete accepted under Chapter 9, consolidated along forms and embedded items, struck to the Chapter 7 finished elevation, corrected at low and high areas, bull-floated to close the surface, and documented as an accepted wet-concrete plane ready for Chapter 11 finishing.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Complete the pre-pour checklist for forms, base, reinforcement, joints, access, tools, crew, weather, washout, and curing. Confirm every item from Chapters 6 through 9 is verified before concrete arrives at the formed area.
  2. Confirm concrete delivery or batch timing. Verify the accepted batch from Chapter 9 remains within its approved working time.
  3. Wet absorptive base or forms only where the specification permits and without standing water. Do not wet the base or forms if the approved specification does not call for it.
  4. Place concrete close to final position. Direct concrete into the formed area at the location it will remain, minimizing the distance it must be moved.
  5. Do not dump large piles that require excessive movement. Place in controlled quantities matched to the crew's spreading and consolidation pace.
  6. Begin at the planned far edge or controlling location. Follow the placement sequence established for the slab's shape, slope, and access.
  7. Spread concrete using shovels, come-alongs, or rakes designed for concrete. Avoid tools or techniques that segregate aggregate from paste.
  8. Avoid walking directly on reinforcement. Use knee boards or approved access boards for any hand access onto the reinforcement or fresh concrete.
  9. Consolidate concrete along forms, corners, blockouts, and embedded items. Work concrete against every edge and embedded item to eliminate voids.
  10. Do not over-vibrate flatwork. Limit consolidation to the amount needed to close voids without segregating the mix.
  11. Slightly overfill ahead of the screed. Keep a small surplus of concrete moving ahead of the screed board.
  12. Draw the screed across form tops using the approved sawing motion. Maintain a continuous side-to-side motion while advancing forward.
  13. Maintain continuous bearing on forms or screed rails. Keep the screed board in constant contact with the form tops or approved screed rails throughout each pass.
  14. Fill low spots with fresh concrete from the same mix. Add concrete from the same batch to any area that screeds low.
  15. Remove excess concrete from high areas. Take off surplus concrete from any area that screeds high.
  16. Rescreed corrected areas. Repeat the sawing-motion pass over any filled or trimmed section until it matches the form-top elevation.
  17. Check slab thickness where the method permits. Verify thickness against the approved reinforcement and form-height record from Chapters 6 through 8.
  18. Bull-float promptly after screeding to embed aggregate and close the surface. Begin floating as soon as the struck surface allows, without delay.
  19. Keep bull-float passes broad and controlled. Use full-width, even passes rather than short or uneven strokes.
  20. Avoid excessive floating that brings water and paste to the surface. Stop floating once the surface is closed and aggregate is embedded; do not continue working the surface further.
  21. Inspect plane, edges, corners, and depressions and form movement. Walk the full slab and check for low areas, voids, or displaced forms.
  22. Correct wet-plane defects before bleed-water and finishing stages. Address any defect found in Step 21 immediately, while the concrete remains workable.
  23. Record placement start, batch or truck information, weather, and initial surface condition. Complete the placement log before proceeding to Chapter 11.

Common Wet-Concrete Plane Inspection

Inspect every placement using the same checks regardless of slab type.

  • Pre-pour check: confirm every item from the Chapter 6–9 pre-pour checklist was verified before placement began.
  • Placement check: confirm concrete was placed close to final position without large piles or excessive movement.
  • Consolidation check: confirm forms, corners, blockouts, and embedded items show no voids or honeycombing risk at the surface.
  • Plane check: confirm the struck surface matches the Chapter 7 form-top elevation across the full slab.
  • Correction check: confirm every low or high area identified during screeding was filled or trimmed and rescreeded.
  • Float check: confirm the bull-floated surface is closed and aggregate is embedded, without an overworked, paste-heavy surface.
  • Timing check: confirm no bleed water remains on the surface at the point this chapter's inspection is completed.

Complete the Wet-Plane Inspection Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 11.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Honeycombing or Voids at Forms and Corners

Probable cause: concrete was placed and screeded without adequate consolidation along forms, corners, blockouts, or embedded items.
Confirmation: inspect visible edges and corners for surface voids, exposed aggregate pockets, or rough, porous texture immediately after screeding.
Correction: while the concrete remains workable, work concrete against the affected edge or item using approved consolidation methods, then rescreed and re-inspect the area.
Reinspection: repeat the consolidation check across all forms, corners, and embedded items before bull floating.

Low Spot Persists After Screeding

Probable cause: the screed pass was drawn across an underfilled section, or fresh concrete was not added before rescreeding.
Confirmation: sight across the form tops or use a straightedge to compare the suspect area to the surrounding struck plane.
Correction: add fresh concrete from the same batch to the low area and rescreed using the approved sawing motion.
Reinspection: recheck the full plane against the straightedge before bull floating.

Surface Overworked During Bull Floating

Probable cause: bull floating continued past the point the surface was closed, drawing excess water and fine paste to the surface.
Confirmation: inspect the surface for a glossy, watery sheen or a soft, paste-rich layer immediately after floating.
Correction: stop floating immediately; do not attempt to correct an overworked surface with additional floating passes. Note the condition on the placement log for evaluation before Chapter 11 finishing begins.
Reinspection: monitor bleed-water behavior on the affected area before any Chapter 11 finishing operation proceeds.

Form Movement Discovered During Placement

Probable cause: a form was inadequately staked or braced in Chapter 6 and shifted under the pressure of placement or screeding.
Confirmation: compare the current form position and elevation to the Chapter 6 and Chapter 7 layout and elevation records.
Correction: stop placement in the affected area, re-brace or re-stake the form to its verified position, and correct any concrete already placed against the displaced form before continuing.
Reinspection: recheck form line, elevation, and bracing across the full slab before resuming placement.

Segregation From Excessive Movement or Vibration

Probable cause: concrete was dumped in large piles and dragged excessive distances, or consolidation equipment was over-applied to flatwork.
Confirmation: inspect the surface for visible separation of coarse aggregate from paste, or a watery surface layer over a stiffer mass below.
Correction: remove and replace segregated concrete with fresh material from the accepted batch while the area remains accessible; do not attempt to blend segregated material back into the plane.
Reinspection: repeat the placement and consolidation check on the corrected area before rescreeding.

Finishing Begun While Bleed Water Remains

Probable cause: the wet-plane inspection was treated as complete on a fixed schedule rather than on the actual surface condition, and bleed water had not yet risen and disappeared.
Confirmation: inspect the surface directly for standing sheen or water film before any Chapter 11 operation begins.
Correction: hold all further finishing operations until bleed water is no longer present on the surface; do not dust dry cement or add water to accelerate the process.
Reinspection: recheck the surface at intervals until bleed water has fully dissipated before proceeding to Chapter 11.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with an inspected, documented wet-concrete plane, not a visual impression of a finished-looking pour.

  • Clean shovels, rakes, screed boards, and bull floats in the contained washout area established in Chapter 2
  • Do not discharge washout, slurry, or excess concrete to soil, drains, gutters, or waterways
  • Remove excess or spilled concrete from adjacent forms, base, or finished surfaces before it sets
  • Store the placement log and wet-plane inspection worksheet together for reference during Chapter 11
  • Store photographs with clear labels referencing the worksheet entries they support

Field Terms

Form-Top Elevation
The finished elevation established in Chapter 7, against which the screed rides during strike-off.
Wet-Concrete Plane
The struck and bull-floated surface produced by this chapter, inspected before bleed water and finishing operations begin.
Sawing Motion
The side-to-side strike-off technique used to draw the screed across form tops while advancing forward.
Consolidation
Working concrete against forms, corners, blockouts, and embedded items to eliminate voids without over-vibrating.
Working Time
The period, established in Chapter 9, during which the batch remains workable for placement and finishing.
Bleed Water
Surface water that rises from the concrete after consolidation; finishing must not begin while it remains present.
Segregation
Separation of coarse aggregate from paste caused by excessive movement, dragging, or vibration.
Honeycombing
Surface or near-surface voids caused by inadequate consolidation, typically found at forms, corners, or embedded items.
Overworked Surface
A surface floated past the point of closure, drawing excess water and fine paste upward and weakening the wear layer.
Placement Log
The document recording placement start time, batch or truck reference, weather, crew, and initial surface condition.

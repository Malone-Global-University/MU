Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to build one complete residential sidewalk section from an approved path alignment through cured, jointed, finished concrete. The finished work product is a cured sidewalk section with controlled width, longitudinal slope, cross slope, transitions to adjacent surfaces, correctly spaced joints, an approved walking-surface texture, and documented final acceptance.

You will review the approved path alignment, width, slope, and joint plan; confirm property and right-of-way limits; locate utilities; lay out the path and its transitions; excavate and prepare the subgrade; install and compact aggregate base; build and align forms; set slope; install isolation material and reinforcement where specified; place, consolidate, screed, and finish the concrete; edge, joint, and broom the surface; cure and protect the section; strip forms; and inspect the finished work against the approved plan.

This chapter draws directly on Chapters 1 through 12: the site-readiness inspection from Chapter 1, the work-area setup from Chapter 2, the layout method from Chapter 3, the excavation and base procedures from Chapters 4 and 5, the form-building and slope-setting methods from Chapters 6 and 7, the reinforcement procedure from Chapter 8, the mixing procedure from Chapter 9 where bagged concrete is used, and the placement, finishing, and curing procedures from Chapters 10 through 12. Nothing in this chapter invents a width, slope, joint spacing, or curing period that the approved plan and specification do not already supply.

  • FW-080 — Set sidewalk forms to the approved line, width, and elevation.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter is the first in the applied-projects group (Chapters 13–16) to place work directly in a public-facing, pedestrian-load context. Unlike the utility pad in Chapter 13, a sidewalk section may cross the public right-of-way, must meet accessibility requirements for cross slope and surface condition, and must interface cleanly with existing walks, driveway aprons, curbs, and steps that this chapter did not build and may not alter without separate approval.

The joint-timing and joint-spacing discipline built in Chapters 11 and 17 governs this chapter directly: a sidewalk section with joints spaced incorrectly, or cut too early or too late, is far more likely to develop random cracking under repeated pedestrian and occasional vehicle loading than a patio or pad of the same age.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • laying out one residential sidewalk section within an approved alignment, width, and slope plan
  • preparing the subgrade and aggregate base beneath the section
  • building, staking, bracing, and aligning forms to the approved line, width, and elevation
  • setting longitudinal and cross-slope elevations
  • installing isolation material and reinforcement where the specification requires them
  • placing, consolidating, screeding, and bull-floating the concrete
  • edging, jointing, and finishing the walking surface
  • curing, protecting, and inspecting the finished section

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • begin any part of this project before the Chapter 1 site-readiness decision is approved or conditionally approved with all applicable conditions met
  • excavate, drive stakes, saw cut, or drill without a confirmed, valid utility locate
  • build a sidewalk section within the public right-of-way, or one that alters an existing curb, driveway apron, or municipal walk, without the applicable sidewalk, right-of-way, or public-works permit and inspection
  • invent a width, slope, cross slope, joint spacing, joint depth, curing period, or accessibility tolerance not supplied by the approved plan, adopted code, or manufacturer instructions
  • direct sidewalk drainage toward a building, neighboring property, or a location the approved drainage plan does not identify as an acceptable discharge point
  • open the finished section to pedestrian or vehicle traffic before the approved curing period and strength condition are met

Any condition requiring survey, accessibility review, or public-works approval must be documented and referred, not resolved by the learner.

Trade Note

A sidewalk section is judged by people walking on it, often including people using mobility aids, strollers, and bicycles. A slope, joint, or texture defect that would be a minor cosmetic issue on a private patio can be a trip hazard or an accessibility failure on a public walking surface. Hold this chapter's tolerances exactly as specified; do not treat sidewalk work as a smaller version of patio work.

Shop Tip

Walk the full proposed path before excavating and confirm every transition — driveway crossing, step, gate, existing walk — against the approved plan. A sidewalk section that is dimensionally perfect on its own but meets an existing surface at the wrong elevation creates a trip hazard at the joint, not within the new work.

Common Field Fundamentals

A sidewalk section differs from the pad in Chapter 13 and the patio in Chapter 15 in one critical respect: it is a linear path with two slope directions to control at once, longitudinal slope along the direction of travel and cross slope across the width, and it typically has transitions at both ends to surfaces the learner did not build. Every step in this chapter's sequence exists to keep those two slopes and both transitions correct at the same time.

Two Slopes, One Reference

Set longitudinal slope and cross slope from the single elevation reference established in Chapter 1 or Chapter 3. Check both slopes at every form-elevation checkpoint, not only at the start and end of the path. A form line that is correct in longitudinal slope but wrong in cross slope produces a walking surface that sheds water toward one edge in a way the approved plan did not intend.

Measurement Cue — Check Cross Slope at Every Station

Check cross slope at the same stations used to check longitudinal slope, not only at the ends of the path. A form that reads correctly for longitudinal slope at both ends can still twist between them if cross slope is not checked at intermediate points.

Critical Safety Cue — Utility Locate Before Any Ground Disturbance

Confirm the utility locate is valid for the current work date before driving any layout stake, form stake, or excavation tool into the ground. A sidewalk path often crosses a wider area than the original Chapter 1 inspection covered; request an updated locate if the confirmed path extends beyond the originally inspected area.

Field Safety Cue — Pedestrian and Vehicle Traffic Along the Path

A sidewalk work area is frequently adjacent to a driveway, street, or walking route still in active use by residents, neighbors, or the public. Install barricades, cones, and signage before excavation begins, and maintain them through curing.

Code Alert — Accessibility and Right-of-Way Requirements

Cross-slope, width, and surface-condition limits for accessible walking surfaces vary by jurisdiction and by whether the section is within the public right-of-way. Verify the applicable accessibility requirement before setting cross slope, and do not assume a private-property patio tolerance applies to public sidewalk work.

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one residential sidewalk section built between the transitions shown on the approved plan, at the approved width, longitudinal slope, cross slope, thickness, joint spacing, and finish, laid out, excavated, based, formed, reinforced where specified, placed, finished, jointed, cured, and inspected.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review path alignment, width, slope, cross slope, landings, steps, accessibility, drainage, and joint plan. Confirm the approved plan applies to the exact path being built and that no revision is pending.
  2. Confirm property and right-of-way limits. Verify the confirmed boundaries from Chapter 1 remain accurate for the full path length, including any point where the path crosses into the public right-of-way.
  3. Locate utilities. Confirm the utility locate is valid for the current work date and covers the full path length; request an updated locate if the path extends beyond the area previously inspected.
  4. Lay out sidewalk edges and transitions. Use the Chapter 3 layout method to establish both edge lines, squareness at each transition, and offset references beyond the excavation limits.
  5. Verify door, gate, driveway, curb, step, and landscaping interfaces. Record the elevation of each existing interface relative to the single elevation reference before excavating.
  6. Excavate to the approved depth. Use the Chapter 4 excavation procedure, maintaining separation from utilities, foundations, and adjacent paving.
  7. Prepare and compact the subgrade. Use the Chapter 4 subgrade procedure, removing unsuitable material and shaping the required slope before base placement.
  8. Install and compact aggregate base. Use the Chapter 5 procedure, placing and compacting the approved lift thickness along the full path length.
  9. Build, stake, brace, and align sidewalk forms. Use the Chapter 6 procedure, checking line, square at transitions, and width at multiple stations along the path.
  10. Set longitudinal and cross-slope elevations. Use the Chapter 7 procedure, checking both slopes at every station and rechecking after bracing.
  11. Install isolation material at structures and fixed paving. Install full-depth isolation material at every transition to an existing structure or fixed surface identified on the approved plan.
  12. Install reinforcement where specified. Use the Chapter 8 procedure for cutting, placing, supporting, and inspecting reinforcement called for in the approved specification.
  13. Complete the pre-pour inspection. Verify forms, base, isolation material, reinforcement, cover, and joint conflicts before concrete arrives or is mixed.
  14. Place concrete progressively along the path. Place concrete in the sequence and direction established for the full path length, coordinating with delivery or batch timing from Chapter 9 or the ready-mix schedule.
  15. Consolidate edges and around joints. Consolidate concrete along both form lines and at every isolation-joint location before screeding.
  16. Screed to form elevation. Use the Chapter 10 screeding procedure, maintaining continuous bearing on the sidewalk forms along the full path length.
  17. Bull float and time finishing. Use the Chapter 10 bull-floating procedure, then observe bleed water and surface firmness before beginning edging and jointing.
  18. Edge both sides. Use the Chapter 11 edging procedure along both form lines for the full path length.
  19. Install tooled or planned control joints. Install joints at the spacing and depth shown on the approved joint plan, keeping joint lines straight and perpendicular to the path unless the plan specifies otherwise.
  20. Apply the specified broom finish in the approved direction. Use the Chapter 11 brooming procedure, maintaining consistent texture and direction along the full path length.
  21. Cure and barricade the walkway. Begin curing per the Chapter 12 procedure as soon as the finish permits, and install barricades preventing pedestrian and vehicle traffic through the full curing period.
  22. Strip forms when permitted and repair edge voids only with approved methods. Strip forms only after the approved time or strength condition is met, and repair any edge void using the material and method the specification identifies.
  23. Inspect width, slope, cross slope, transitions, joint spacing, texture, trip hazards, and drainage. Compare every measured item against the approved plan and the Acceptance Standards table below.
  24. Document permit and final acceptance where required. Complete the inspection record and file it with the permit and utility-locate records from earlier chapters.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Layout and utility-locate records: confirmed alignment, width, and utility-locate validity for the full path length.
  • Compaction and form-check records: subgrade and base compaction results, and form-elevation checks at every station.
  • Reinforcement photographs: concealed reinforcement and isolation-material placement, where specified.
  • Concrete tickets or batch logs: volume, mix, batch or delivery time, and weather at placement.

  • Final inspection record: completed Acceptance Standards checks and permit sign-off where required, filed with Chapter 1 site-readiness records.

Common Field Fundamentals — Finishing a Public Walking Surface

Inspect every completed section using the same checks regardless of path length.

  • Alignment check: confirm width and alignment against the approved plan at multiple stations, not only at the ends.
  • Slope check: confirm longitudinal slope and cross slope from the single elevation reference at every checked station.
  • Transition check: confirm every interface to an existing surface meets the approved elevation with no lip, gap, or reverse slope.
  • Joint check: confirm joint spacing, depth, and straightness against the approved joint plan.
  • Texture check: confirm broom direction and coverage are consistent along the full path length.
  • Trip-hazard check: confirm no vertical offset or surface defect creates a trip hazard anywhere along the path.
  • Drainage check: confirm surface water follows the approved discharge path with no ponding or building-directed runoff.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Cross Slope Correct at Ends but Twisted Mid-Span

Probable cause: cross slope was checked only at the beginning and end of the path, allowing the form to twist at an unchecked intermediate station.
Confirmation: recheck cross slope at additional stations along the full path length using the single elevation reference.
Correction: loosen and readjust the affected form section to the approved cross slope, then rebrace.
Reinspection: repeat the slope check at all stations before proceeding to concrete placement.

Transition Lip at an Existing Driveway or Step

Probable cause: the existing interface elevation was estimated rather than measured against the single elevation reference before setting form elevation at that end.
Confirmation: measure the actual elevation difference between the new form line and the existing surface at the transition.
Correction: readjust the form elevation at that end to eliminate the lip, gap, or reverse slope, keeping the rest of the path's slope unchanged.
Reinspection: repeat the transition check at both ends of the path.

Joint Spacing Inconsistent With the Approved Plan

Probable cause: joint locations were marked by eye along the path rather than measured and transferred from the approved joint plan.
Confirmation: measure actual joint spacing against the approved plan's spacing and depth requirement.
Correction: where concrete remains workable, correct joint locations using the Chapter 11 tooling procedure; where concrete has set, refer to Chapter 17 for saw-cut correction within the approved time window.
Reinspection: repeat the joint check against the approved plan.

Trip Hazard at a Tooled Joint or Form-Strip Edge

Probable cause: a joint was tooled unevenly, or a form-strip edge void was left unrepaired, creating a vertical offset.
Confirmation: measure the vertical offset at the location using a straightedge across the joint or edge.
Correction: repair the offset using the method and material identified in the approved specification; do not leave an unrepaired offset in a public walking surface.
Reinspection: repeat the trip-hazard check across the full path length.

Ponding at a Low Point Not Identified in the Approved Plan

Probable cause: a local low point developed from an unchecked dip in the subgrade, base, or form elevation that was not caught during the Chapter 7 slope-setting procedure.
Confirmation: observe water behavior during or shortly after rain, or flood-test the section with a controlled water source where permitted by the cure and finish plan.
Correction: document the low point and refer it for repair per Chapter 18 procedures; do not attempt to alter cured concrete slope without an approved repair method.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check after any approved correction.

Section Opened to Traffic Before Curing Complete

Probable cause: barricades were removed, or pedestrian or vehicle traffic was permitted, before the full approved curing period and strength condition were met.
Confirmation: review the curing record against the approved curing duration and any strength-based traffic restriction.
Correction: reinstall barricades immediately and restrict traffic for the remainder of the approved curing period; inspect for surface damage from any premature loading.
Reinspection: repeat the surface inspection once the full curing period is complete.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a cured, inspected, documented sidewalk section, not a section that merely looks finished.

  • Remove and store form lumber, stakes, and bracing only after the approved stripping condition is met
  • Clean tools, screed board, floats, edgers, jointers, and brooms in the contained washout area established in Chapter 2
  • Backfill form-edge voids with approved material once concrete and edge conditions permit
  • Remove barricades only after the full approved curing period and traffic restriction are satisfied
  • File layout, utility-locate, compaction, form-check, reinforcement, concrete, finishing, curing, joint, and inspection records together

Field Terms

Transition

Right-of-Way Limit

Joint Plan

Station
A specific measured point along the path length where slope, width, or elevation is checked.
Discharge Path
The approved route surface water follows away from the sidewalk to an acceptable destination.
Accessibility Limit
The maximum cross slope, running slope, or surface-condition tolerance required for accessible walking surfaces under the applicable adopted code.

Performance Check

With the sidewalk section fully cured, review the completed inspection worksheet against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm width and alignment, longitudinal slope, cross slope, both transitions, joint spacing and depth, surface texture, and drainage all match the approved plan. Confirm no trip hazard exists anywhere along the path or at either transition, and confirm the full approved curing period and traffic restriction were completed before any pedestrian or vehicle traffic was permitted. Refer any unresolved deficiency for correction and reinspection before issuing final acceptance, and document the permit and inspection status before proceeding to Chapter 15.

Project Closeout

You have completed the fourteenth chapter in the concrete flatwork sequence: building a residential sidewalk section. This chapter required you to lay out the path, width, and transitions (FW-079), set sidewalk forms to the approved line and elevation (FW-080), prepare the subgrade and aggregate base beneath the section (FW-081), place concrete progressively along the path (FW-082), finish the walking surface to the specified texture, slope, and joint spacing (FW-083), and inspect the finished sidewalk for slope, joint spacing, trip hazards, and drainage (FW-084).

The layout, slope-setting, reinforcement, placement, finishing, and curing procedures used here carry forward unchanged into the patio project in Chapter 15 and the garage or shed slab practice section in Chapter 16.

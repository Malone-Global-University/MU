Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to implement and document the complete curing and early-age protection plan for one newly placed exterior slab. The finished work product is a continuously cured and protected slab, shielded from rain, wind, rapid evaporation, heat, freezing, and premature traffic through the full approved curing period, with a complete curing log.

You will review the concrete specification and approved curing method, begin curing as soon as the finish permits, select and apply the specified curing method, install rain and weather protection, barricade the slab against traffic, inspect the protection at scheduled intervals, correct any failure in the curing system, and record every weather reading, inspection, and disruption until the approved curing period is complete.

This chapter follows directly from Chapter 11, where edging, jointing, and the broom finish were completed. Nothing in this chapter invents a curing method, duration, or temperature limit that the concrete specification does not already supply. Curing begins the moment the finish permits it — this chapter does not wait for a convenient stopping point.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-067 — Identify curing needs based on the concrete specification, finish condition, and weather.
  • FW-068 — Apply an approved curing method uniformly and without damaging the finished surface.
  • FW-069 — Protect the curing slab from rain without allowing covers to touch and mark the surface prematurely.
  • FW-070 — Protect the curing slab from heat, freezing, and rapid evaporation using the approved plan.
  • FW-071 — Prevent early pedestrian, vehicle, and construction traffic for the full approved no-traffic period.
  • FW-072 — Document the curing method, weather, inspections, disruptions, and curing completion.

Where This Skill Fits

Every chapter before this one — layout, excavation, base, forms, reinforcement, mixing, placement, and finishing — produces a slab that has not yet reached its intended strength or surface durability. Curing is the step that protects that unfinished strength gain from being lost to rapid drying, freezing, heat, or premature loading. A perfectly placed and finished slab that is not cured correctly can still crack, scale, dust, or fail to reach its specified strength.

This chapter's curing record also controls later work. Chapter 17 cannot saw-cut control joints until the approved timing window for this slab is known. Chapter 19 cannot apply sealer until this chapter's curing period, drying condition, and moisture readings confirm the slab is ready. The Chapter 20 capstone repeats this exact curing and protection sequence at full project scale.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reviewing the concrete specification for curing method, duration, and weather limits
  • beginning curing as soon as the surface finish permits
  • applying wet curing, curing compound, plastic sheeting, insulated blankets, or an evaporation reducer as specified
  • installing rain, heat, and cold-weather protection
  • barricading the slab against pedestrian, pet, vehicle, vibration, and construction traffic
  • inspecting curing protection at scheduled intervals and correcting failures
  • documenting weather, curing start, method, inspections, disruptions, and completion

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • invent a curing method, duration, temperature limit, or traffic-restriction period that is not supplied by the concrete specification, ready-mix documentation, manufacturer instructions, or adopted code
  • use unvented combustion heat that carbonates or contaminates the surface
  • allow rain covers, blankets, or sheeting to touch and mark the surface before the finish has set sufficiently
  • open the slab to service, sealing, or saw cutting before the approved strength or time condition is met

Large driveways, garage slabs, structural slabs, municipal sidewalks, and public-right-of-way work may require professional crews, engineered curing plans, testing, and inspection beyond what this chapter covers.

Trade Note

Curing is not an optional add-on performed once the crew has time. It is a required continuation of the concrete's strength-gain process. A slab that is placed and finished correctly but cured poorly can still crack, dust, scale, or fail to reach its specified strength — the record produced in this chapter is what proves the slab actually received the protection its specification requires.

Setup Standard

Curing materials must be staged and on site before concrete placement begins, per Chapter 2 and Chapter 10. Do not begin finishing the slab in Chapter 11 without confirming the curing method, duration, and protection materials required by this chapter are already in hand.

Shop Tip

Begin curing as soon as the finish permits, not after cleanup, tool storage, or truck departure. The window between a finished surface and the point where evaporation begins removing bleed water and surface moisture is shorter than most beginners expect, especially in wind, heat, or low humidity.

Common Field Fundamentals

Concrete gains strength through a continuing chemical reaction between cement and water. That reaction slows or stops if the concrete dries out too quickly, freezes before reaching adequate strength, or overheats. Curing is the deliberate control of moisture and temperature that keeps this reaction going for the full period the concrete specification requires. Protection is the deliberate control of rain, wind, traffic, and vibration that keeps the curing slab from being disturbed before it can support those loads.

Curing, Weather, and Traffic as One System

A curing method selected correctly but applied to a slab left unprotected from rain is not a complete curing plan. A slab protected from rain but left open to foot traffic during the no-traffic period is not a complete curing plan. Every finding recorded in this chapter must be evaluated against how curing method, weather protection, and traffic restriction work together, not as isolated tasks performed independently.

Measurement Cue — Curing Begins at Finish, Not at Convenience

Record the exact time the surface finish was completed and the exact time curing began. The concrete specification's curing period is measured from the point curing actually starts, not from placement, not from truck departure, and not from crew availability.

Critical Safety Cue — No Unvented Combustion Heat

Do not use unvented combustion heaters, salamanders, or open-flame heat sources to protect curing concrete from freezing. Unvented combustion heat can carbonate and weaken the surface and can also create a carbon monoxide hazard in an enclosed area. Use only the cold-weather protection method specified in the approved plan.

Field Safety Cue — Barricades and Site Housekeeping

Curing slabs are often left in active work areas. Secure barricades against wind and accidental displacement, and keep signage visible from every approach the slab could be reached from, including from adjacent yards, driveways, and structures under construction.

Code Alert — Public-Right-of-Way Curing and Traffic Control

Sidewalks, driveway aprons, and other flatwork in the public right-of-way may require specific barricade standards, signage, or inspection before the no-traffic period is considered satisfied. Verify local requirements before removing barricades from work adjacent to a public street or walkway.

Curing and Protection Qualification

Concrete Curing and Early-Age Protection Plan

Difficulty: Beginner to Intermediate

Estimated Time: 30–60 minutes active, plus the full curing period

Estimated Cost: Curing compound, sheeting, blankets, and barricade materials

Finished Work: One continuously cured and protected slab with a complete curing log

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one newly placed exterior slab, cured using the concrete specification's approved method, protected from rain, wind, heat, freezing, and traffic through the full approved curing period, and documented in a completed curing log.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Review concrete specification, curing method, duration, weather, sealer compatibility, and traffic restrictions. Confirm the approved method and period before curing begins.
  2. Begin curing as soon as the surface finish permits. Record the exact finish-completion time and curing-start time.
  3. Select wet curing, curing compound, plastic sheeting, insulated blankets, evaporation reducer, or another approved method. Use only the method identified in the concrete specification.
  4. Verify curing materials are on site before placement. Confirm quantity is sufficient for the full slab area.
  5. For wet curing, maintain continuous moisture without eroding the surface. Use a method that applies water gently and continuously.
  6. For plastic or sheeting, place it without damaging texture. Lay sheeting down evenly without dragging it across the finished surface.
  7. Secure edges against wind. Use weighted edging, boards, or pins placed outside the slab area.
  8. Prevent uneven contact marks where appearance matters. Keep sheeting from bunching or creasing against the surface before the finish has set sufficiently.
  9. For curing compound, use the specified product and application rate. Confirm the manufacturer's coverage rate before spraying.
  10. Apply uniformly with the approved sprayer or roller. Avoid pooling, streaking, or missed areas.
  11. Maintain compatibility with later sealers, coatings, adhesives, or finishes. Confirm the curing compound will not interfere with Chapter 19 sealing.
  12. Install rain protection without allowing covers to touch and mark the surface prematurely. Suspend or support covers clear of the finished surface until it has set sufficiently.
  13. Install hot-weather shading, windbreaks, or moisture retention. Use the specific method identified in the approved plan.
  14. Install cold-weather blankets, enclosures, or heat according to the approved plan. Use only methods and equipment specified for this project.
  15. Do not use unvented combustion heat that carbonates or contaminates the surface. Confirm any heat source is vented and approved for concrete curing.
  16. Barricade the slab against pedestrians, pets, vehicles, vibration, and construction traffic. Install barricades and signage immediately after curing begins.
  17. Mark the no-traffic period. Post the exact start date, time, and end date and time on the barricade signage.
  18. Inspect curing protection at scheduled intervals. Check at the intervals specified in the approved plan or, where none is specified, at least once daily.
  19. Repair displaced, torn, dry, or ineffective curing materials. Correct any disruption immediately upon discovery.
  20. Record weather, curing start, method, inspections, disruptions, and curing completion. Complete the curing log through the full approved period.
  21. Do not open the slab to service before the approved condition. Confirm the curing period and any required strength condition are met before removing barricades.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Curing log: the completed record of every finding in Steps 1–21, including the final curing-completion confirmation.
  • Weather record: temperature, precipitation, and wind conditions logged through the full curing period.
  • Disruption record: every displaced, torn, dry, or ineffective condition identified and its correction, retained for reference through Chapter 17 and Chapter 19.
  • Photographs: curing method in place, barricades and signage, and any corrected disruption, referenced in the log.

Common Curing and Protection Inspection

Inspect every curing slab using the same checks regardless of curing method.

  • Timing check: confirm curing began as soon as the finish permitted, with both times recorded.
  • Method check: confirm the curing method matches the concrete specification exactly.
  • Application check: confirm curing compound coverage rate or sheeting placement meets the approved standard without surface damage.
  • Weather-protection check: confirm rain, heat, and cold-weather protection match the approved plan and do not use prohibited heat sources.
  • Traffic check: confirm barricades and signage were installed immediately and the no-traffic period is posted and enforced.
  • Inspection check: confirm scheduled inspections occurred and every disruption was corrected.
  • Documentation check: confirm the curing log is complete through curing completion.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Curing Started Too Late

Probable cause: curing was delayed until cleanup, tool storage, or truck departure was finished rather than beginning as soon as the surface finish permitted.
Confirmation: compare the recorded finish-completion time to the recorded curing-start time on the curing log.
Correction: begin curing immediately upon discovery and note the delay and its cause on the curing log.
Reinspection: monitor the surface for evidence of premature drying, such as dusting, crazing, or plastic shrinkage cracking, and record any observed condition.

Curing Compound Applied Below Specified Coverage Rate

Probable cause: the sprayer was moved too quickly or the product was diluted or applied inconsistently, resulting in a coverage rate below the manufacturer's specification.
Confirmation: compare product used against area covered to calculate the actual coverage rate achieved.
Correction: apply an additional coat within the product's recoat window to reach the specified total coverage.
Reinspection: recheck coverage uniformity and record the corrected application.

Rain Cover Contacted and Marked the Surface

Probable cause: sheeting or a tarp was placed directly on the surface before the finish had set sufficiently to resist contact marking.
Confirmation: inspect the surface for texture disruption, indentation, or ghosting where the cover contacted the concrete.
Correction: support the cover clear of the surface using stakes, frames, or blocking for the remainder of the rain-protection period; document the affected area for evaluation under Chapter 18 if the marking is significant.
Reinspection: recheck cover clearance at the next scheduled inspection.

Barricades Installed Late or Displaced by Wind

Probable cause: barricades were not installed immediately after curing began, or were not secured against wind and were displaced before the no-traffic period ended.
Confirmation: inspect the barricade position against its original location and check for any footprints, tire marks, or disturbance on the slab.
Correction: reinstall and secure barricades immediately, and document any traffic that reached the slab, including its location and apparent effect on the surface.
Reinspection: increase inspection frequency for the remainder of the no-traffic period.

Unvented Heat Source Used for Cold-Weather Protection

Probable cause: an unvented combustion heater or open-flame source was used to prevent freezing instead of the approved cold-weather method.
Confirmation: inspect the surface for a soft, weak, or dusting condition consistent with carbonation, and review what heat source was actually used.
Correction: remove the unvented heat source immediately, install the approved cold-weather protection method, and document the affected area for evaluation under Chapter 18 if surface damage is confirmed.
Reinspection: recheck surface condition and temperature at the next scheduled inspection.

Slab Opened to Traffic Before the Approved Condition

Probable cause: barricades were removed, or traffic was otherwise permitted, before the curing period or required strength condition specified in the concrete specification was met.
Confirmation: compare the actual elapsed curing time and any required strength confirmation against the approved condition in the concrete specification.
Correction: reinstall barricades immediately, restrict traffic for the remainder of the required period, and document any load or traffic the slab received before the approved condition was reached.
Reinspection: inspect the surface for cracking, indentation, or displacement before permitting traffic again.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed curing log and a slab that has finished its full approved curing period, not a verbal impression that "it's probably cured by now."

  • Remove curing compound application equipment and clean per the manufacturer's instructions
  • Remove and store or dispose of plastic sheeting, blankets, and weighted edging once the curing period is complete
  • Leave barricades and signage in place until the full no-traffic period has elapsed
  • File the completed curing log with the placement and finishing records from Chapter 10 and Chapter 11
  • Store photographs with clear labels referencing the curing log entries they support

Field Terms

Curing Method
The specific approved method — wet curing, curing compound, plastic sheeting, insulated blankets, or evaporation reducer — used for this slab.
Curing Period
The full duration the concrete specification requires the curing method and protection to remain in place.
No-Traffic Period
The duration during which pedestrian, pet, vehicle, vibration, and construction traffic must be excluded from the slab.
Curing Start
The recorded time curing actually began, measured from when the surface finish permitted it.
Coverage Rate
The manufacturer-specified application rate for curing compound, stated as area covered per unit volume.
Disruption
Any displaced, torn, dry, or otherwise ineffective curing protection identified during a scheduled inspection.
Evaporation Reducer
A material applied to reduce surface moisture loss during finishing or early curing without acting as a full curing compound.
Carbonation
Surface weakening caused by unvented combustion byproducts reacting with fresh concrete.
Recoat Window
The manufacturer-specified time period within which an additional coat of curing compound or sealer may be applied.
Curing Log
The document recording every finding from this chapter's curing sequence and the final curing-completion confirmation.

Performance Check

With the curing log fully recorded, review it against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm curing began as soon as the finish permitted, the approved method was used at the correct coverage or placement standard, rain and weather protection did not damage the surface, no unvented combustion heat was used, barricades and signage restricted traffic for the full no-traffic period, every scheduled inspection occurred with disruptions corrected, and the full approved curing period was completed before the slab was opened to any service. If any condition suggests surface damage requiring evaluation, refer it to Chapter 18 rather than concealing it, and record the referral before beginning Chapter 13.

Project Closeout

You have completed the twelfth chapter in the concrete flatwork sequence: curing and protecting fresh concrete. This chapter required you to identify curing needs from the specification and site conditions (FW-067), apply an approved curing method correctly (FW-068), protect the slab from rain without surface damage (FW-069), protect the slab from heat, freezing, and evaporation using only approved methods (FW-070), prevent early traffic for the full no-traffic period (FW-071), and document the entire curing period completely (FW-072).

File your completed curing log with the placement and finishing records from Chapter 10 and Chapter 11. You will reference these exact findings throughout the rest of this book.

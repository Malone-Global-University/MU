Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect an existing site and produce a documented, approved-plan-based readiness and work plan for one proposed exterior slab, pad, walkway, or patio area. The finished work product is a single completed site-readiness and work plan: dimensions, drainage observations, soil evaluation, access notes, utility-locate confirmation, hazard and control plan, and a final approved, conditional, or rejected readiness decision.

You will gather the approved site plan and related documents, define the proposed slab's use and interfaces, verify property and right-of-way limits, request utility locating, inspect existing grade and drainage, measure the project area, evaluate soil, identify access and traffic controls, record weather exposure, and issue a documented readiness decision.

This is the first chapter in Book 16. It establishes the site-and-material system every later chapter builds on: Chapter 2 stages the work area you identify here, Chapter 3 lays out the dimensions you measure here, and Chapter 4 excavates the subgrade you evaluate here. Nothing in this book invents a dimension, depth, or elevation that this chapter's plan does not already supply.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • FW-001 — Inspect existing site conditions against the approved site plan, grading plan, and drainage details.
  • FW-002 — Identify drainage needs, including high points, low points, runoff sources, and discharge paths.
  • FW-003 — Measure the proposed project area, including length, width, area, perimeter, and elevation references.
  • FW-004 — Evaluate visible soil and access conditions for equipment, delivery, and worker movement.
  • FW-005 — Identify flatwork hazards, including utilities, structures, unstable soil, and boundary conflicts.
  • FW-006 — Document project readiness and issue an approved, conditional, or rejected decision.

Where This Skill Fits

Every later chapter in this book depends on the readiness decision produced here. Chapter 2 cannot safely stage a work area, delivery route, or washout station until this chapter has confirmed utility locations, property limits, and hazard controls. Chapter 3 cannot lay out a squared slab until this chapter has confirmed the approved dimensions and elevation references. Chapter 4 cannot excavate a subgrade until this chapter has flagged soft spots, fill, and drainage risks for further evaluation.

The stop-work discipline in this chapter — refusing to proceed past unidentified utilities, unstable soil, disputed boundaries, or unresolved drainage — is the same discipline carried through every remaining chapter in this book, including the Chapter 20 capstone.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • gathering and reviewing the approved site plan, grading plan, utility information, and permit requirements
  • verifying property lines, setbacks, easements, and right-of-way limits
  • requesting and confirming utility locating before any excavation-related activity
  • inspecting existing grade, drainage, and surrounding interfaces
  • measuring the proposed project area and evaluating visible soil conditions
  • identifying access, traffic, and weather-exposure factors
  • producing a documented hazard, deficiency, and readiness plan

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • excavate, drive stakes, saw cut, or drill without a confirmed, valid utility locate
  • invent a slab thickness, excavation depth, gravel-base thickness, compaction target, reinforcement schedule, concrete strength, slump limit, joint spacing, curing period, weather limit, slope, elevation, or code provision that is not supplied by approved plans, adopted code, manufacturer instructions, or the project specification
  • proceed past unidentified utilities, unstable excavation edges, contaminated soil, buried tanks, structural foundations, severe erosion, groundwater, unsuitable fill, or expansive soil
  • issue an approved readiness decision when geotechnical, structural, survey, accessibility, or public-works review is required and has not been obtained

Any condition that requires engineering, survey, or professional review must be documented and referred, not resolved by the learner.

Trade Note

Every dimension, elevation, and slope note recorded in this chapter carries forward into every later chapter without modification. Recording an estimate where a measured or plan-confirmed figure is required, rather than flagging the item as a deficiency, is the single most common source of the layout and drainage errors diagnosed later in this book.

Setup Standard

Do not drive stakes, marking pins, or probe rods into the ground, and do not authorize any excavation-related activity, until utility locating has been requested and confirmed. A readiness decision issued before utility locating is confirmed must be treated as conditional only.

Shop Tip

Inspect drainage during dry conditions and, where practical, again after rain. A site that looks flat and well-drained on a dry day can reveal ponding, erosion, or building-entry risk that only appears once water is actually moving across it.

Drainage, Soil, and Access as One System

A proposed slab's finished performance depends on drainage, soil support, and access working together, not on any one factor alone. A site with excellent soil but no discharge path for runoff, or excellent drainage but no room for a concrete truck to reach the pour area, is not yet a ready site. Every finding recorded in this chapter must be evaluated against how it will affect every later chapter, not treated as an isolated observation.

Measurement Cue — One Elevation Reference for the Whole Project

Select one elevation reference point before recording any elevation note, and use that same reference for every later measurement in this chapter and in Chapter 3 and Chapter 7. Switching reference points partway through inspection is a common source of the drainage and slope errors diagnosed later in this book.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Rely on Surface Markings Alone

Contact the required utility-location service before any marking pin, probe rod, or stake is driven into the ground. Do not rely solely on surface markings when the exact utility depth or path is uncertain. Stop work and refer the project for further evaluation if any utility location remains unidentified.

Field Safety Cue — Uneven Ground and Obstructions

Inspection sites are often uneven, overgrown, or cluttered with existing debris, tools, or landscaping. Watch footing on slopes, near excavation edges, and around exposed roots, irrigation lines, or drainage structures while walking the site.

Code Alert — Boundaries and Public-Works Requirements

Verify property lines, setbacks, easements, and public right-of-way before assuming the proposed project area is fully buildable. Sidewalks, driveways, and aprons that cross into the public right-of-way may require additional permits, inspections, or public-works coordination not required for interior-lot patios or pads.

FW-001 – FW-006

Site Inspection / Planning Qualification

Concrete Flatwork Site-Readiness and Work Plan

Difficulty: Beginner

Estimated Time: 2–4 hours

Estimated Cost: No material cost

Finished Work: One completed site-readiness and work plan, inspected and documented

A. Finished-Project Specification

Scope: one proposed exterior slab, pad, walkway, or patio area, inspected against the approved site plan and documented as a complete site-readiness and work plan including dimensions, drainage, soil, access, utilities, hazards, prerequisites, and a final readiness decision.

B. Required Project Sequence

  1. Obtain the approved site plan, property information, grading plan, utility information, drainage details, flatwork dimensions, and permit requirements. Confirm every document is current and applies to the exact proposed location.
  2. Define the proposed slab use, loading, finish, access, and surrounding interfaces. Record how the finished slab will be used and what it must interface with.
  3. Verify property lines, setbacks, easements, public right-of-way, and prohibited work zones. Compare the approved site plan against visible boundary markers.
  4. Request required utility locating. Contact the required utility-location service before any marking, probing, or excavation-related activity.
  5. Inspect the site for structures, foundations, utilities, trees, roots, irrigation, drains, wells, tanks, and buried obstructions. Walk the full proposed area and its immediate surroundings.
  6. Inspect existing grade and drainage during dry conditions and after rain where practical. Record observed water behavior, not assumed behavior.
  7. Identify high points, low points, runoff sources, discharge paths, ponding, erosion, and building-entry risks. Mark each location on the site plan or a sketch.
  8. Measure the proposed project length, width, area, perimeter, and elevation references. Use the single elevation reference selected for this project.
  9. Identify doors, steps, thresholds, siding, foundations, sidewalks, driveways, and adjacent finished surfaces. Record each interface and its approximate elevation relative to the reference.
  10. Evaluate visible soil as topsoil, fill, clay, sand, gravel, organic material, or unresolved. Record the evaluation as unresolved where visible evidence is insufficient.
  11. Probe for soft spots and evidence of previous disturbance. Use the probe rod at multiple locations across the proposed area.
  12. Identify equipment, concrete-truck, pump, chute, wheelbarrow, material-storage, and worker access. Confirm realistic routes exist for delivery and placement.
  13. Identify traffic, pedestrian, neighbor, noise, dust, and washout controls. Note what controls will be required once work begins.
  14. Record weather exposure, shade, wind, freeze risk, heat, and water availability. Note conditions that will affect curing and finishing in later chapters.
  15. Stop work for unidentified utilities, unstable soil, disputed boundaries, unapproved drainage, contaminated soil, or structural conflicts. Document the specific condition requiring referral.
  16. Determine whether geotechnical, structural, survey, accessibility, or public-works review is required. Record the determination and the basis for it.
  17. Prepare a preliminary quantity and logistics plan without inventing final specifications. Use only figures supplied by approved plans or documented site measurements.
  18. Prepare a hazard and control plan. List every hazard identified and the control planned for it.
  19. Prepare a deficiency and prerequisite list. List every missing document, unresolved condition, and required professional review.
  20. Issue an approved, conditional, or rejected flatwork-readiness decision. Base the decision only on documented findings from this sequence.

D. Documentation and Handoff

  • Site-readiness worksheet: the completed record of every finding in Steps 1–20, including the final readiness decision.
  • Deficiency and prerequisite log: every missing document, unresolved condition, and required professional review, carried forward into later chapters until resolved.
  • Utility-locate record: confirmation of the utility-locate request and result, retained for reference through excavation in Chapter 4.
  • Photographs: existing conditions, drainage patterns, hazards, and boundary markers, referenced in the worksheet.

Common Site-Readiness Inspection

Inspect every proposed project using the same checks regardless of slab type.

  • Document check: confirm every required document is obtained, current, and applies to the exact proposed location.
  • Utility check: confirm the utility locate has been requested and confirmed before any ground disturbance.
  • Boundary check: confirm property lines, setbacks, easements, and right-of-way are verified against the approved site plan.
  • Drainage check: confirm high points, low points, runoff sources, discharge paths, ponding, and erosion have been observed and recorded.
  • Dimensional check: confirm length, width, area, perimeter, and elevation references are recorded from a single stated reference.
  • Soil and access check: confirm soil is recorded honestly, including as unresolved where necessary, and that equipment and delivery access is realistic.
  • Decision check: confirm the readiness decision is based only on documented findings, not on assumption.

Complete the Site-Readiness Worksheet in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 2.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Drainage Direction Assumed Instead of Observed

Probable cause: the site was inspected only during dry conditions, with runoff direction assumed from visual grade rather than confirmed by observation after rain.
Confirmation: revisit the site during or shortly after rain, or review documented drainage evidence such as erosion channels, silt deposits, or staining.
Correction: update the drainage findings on the worksheet with confirmed observations, and note any change in the planned discharge path.
Reinspection: repeat the drainage check before finalizing the readiness decision.

Utility Locate Not Confirmed Before Marking

Probable cause: marking pins, flags, or a probe rod were used before the utility-locate request was confirmed.
Confirmation: review the utility-locate record for a confirmed request and result before the marking or probing occurred.
Correction: stop any further ground disturbance, request the utility locate if it has not been completed, and treat the readiness decision as conditional until confirmed.
Reinspection: repeat the utility check once the locate is confirmed.

Soil Recorded as a Type Without Sufficient Evidence

Probable cause: a soil type was recorded from a quick visual impression rather than from probing and direct inspection at multiple locations.
Confirmation: re-probe the area at additional locations and compare findings to the recorded soil type.
Correction: revise the soil evaluation to "unresolved" where evidence is insufficient, and note whether geotechnical review is required.
Reinspection: repeat the soil evaluation before finalizing the readiness decision.

Elevation Reference Changed Partway Through Inspection

Probable cause: a different reference point was used for later elevation notes than the one selected at the start of inspection.
Confirmation: compare all recorded elevation notes for consistency against the single stated elevation reference.
Correction: re-measure all elevation notes from the original selected reference point.
Reinspection: repeat the dimensional check on the corrected worksheet.

Boundary or Right-of-Way Conflict Overlooked

Probable cause: the proposed project area was compared only to visible fences or landscaping rather than to the approved site plan's property lines, setbacks, and right-of-way limits.
Confirmation: compare the proposed project area directly against the approved site plan's boundary lines.
Correction: flag the conflict as a deficiency, refer it for survey or public-works review as required, and do not issue an approved decision until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat the boundary check once the conflict is resolved or the required review is obtained.

Readiness Decision Issued With Open Deficiencies

Probable cause: an approved decision was issued while unresolved items remained on the deficiency and prerequisite list.
Confirmation: review the deficiency and prerequisite list against the issued decision.
Correction: revise the decision to conditional or rejected until every deficiency is resolved or the required prerequisite is obtained.
Reinspection: repeat the decision check once all items are closed.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, filed worksheet, not a verbal impression of the site.

  • Remove any temporary marking flags placed for photography or measurement that are not needed for utility-locate reference
  • Confirm utility-locate markings intended to remain visible are left undisturbed for Chapter 2 and Chapter 4
  • Return all approved-plan documents to secure, organized storage
  • File the completed site-readiness worksheet, deficiency and prerequisite log, and utility-locate record together
  • Store photographs with clear labels referencing the worksheet entries they support

Performance Check

With the site-readiness worksheet fully recorded, review it against the Acceptance Standards table and confirm every required document was obtained, the utility locate was requested and confirmed, boundaries were verified against the approved site plan, drainage was observed rather than assumed, dimensions and elevations were recorded from a single stated reference, soil was recorded honestly including as unresolved where necessary, and every hazard and deficiency has a documented control or referral.

Job Overview

This project qualifies you to air-seal and insulate a set of accessible rim-joist bays using rigid-foam inserts cut to fit each bay and sealed at their perimeter with a compatible sealant or approved one-component foam. The rim-joist area — the band of framing that sits atop the foundation wall and carries the floor system — is one of the most air-leaky and moisture-sensitive assemblies in a house, and it is also one of the few places where fire protection, termite inspection, and moisture control all intersect in a narrow band of framing only a few inches deep.

You will inspect the rim and sill for water staining, rot, insects, and exterior flashing defects; identify every service penetration, joist hanger, anchor bolt, and combustion-clearance zone in the bay; measure and cut rigid-foam inserts sized to leave a controlled sealant joint at the perimeter; seal that perimeter continuously; add inboard batt insulation only where the approved assembly specifies it; protect exposed foam with the required thermal or ignition barrier; and inspect the finished bays for continuity, fire protection, and preserved access before the work is concealed.

This project depends directly on the moisture-readiness findings from Chapter 1 and the crawlspace or floor-insulation strategy established in Chapter 12. It also feeds directly into Chapter 19, where rim-joist seals, foam protection, termite gaps, and service access are inspected as part of the final thermal-envelope quality inspection.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-073 — Identify rim-joist air leakage, moisture risk, and service penetrations before cutting any insulation.
  • IN-074 — Cut rigid-foam inserts sized to each bay with a controlled perimeter sealant joint.
  • IN-075 — Seal foam edges continuously to the surrounding framing using a compatible sealant or approved foam.
  • IN-076 — Add batt or foam insulation inboard of the rigid-foam insert only where the approved assembly specifies it.
  • IN-077 — Protect moisture-sensitive areas and preserve required termite-inspection gaps and service access.
  • IN-078 — Inspect the finished rim-joist bays for continuity, fire protection, and moisture condition before concealment.

Where This Skill Fits

Rim-joist work sits at the junction of the floor-insulation strategy established in Chapter 12, the service-penetration sealing methods taught in Chapter 4, and the moisture-diagnosis principles taught throughout this book. A rim-joist bay is small, but it typically contains a sill plate, a band of exterior sheathing or masonry, one or more joist hangers, anchor bolts, and frequently a plumbing, electrical, or HVAC penetration — all within a few inches of depth and often within reach of exterior water and soil moisture.

The work performed here is inspected again in Chapter 19 as part of the final thermal-envelope quality inspection, and it is one of the specific systems integrated into the Chapter 20 capstone project.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • inspecting the rim and sill for water staining, rot, insect activity, corrosion, foundation leakage, and exterior flashing defects
  • identifying service penetrations, electrical boxes, joist hangers, anchor bolts, ducts, and combustion-clearance zones in each bay
  • measuring each bay independently and cutting a rigid-foam insert sized for a controlled perimeter sealant joint
  • fitting the foam against the approved surface without trapping active moisture
  • maintaining required termite-inspection gaps where applicable
  • sealing the foam perimeter continuously with compatible sealant or approved one-component foam
  • sealing service penetrations using the Chapter 4 methods
  • installing additional batt insulation inboard of the foam only when the approved assembly requires it
  • protecting exposed foam with the required thermal or ignition barrier
  • preserving access to anchor bolts, valves, electrical boxes, and inspection zones

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • proceed with any bay showing active water entry, structural decay, termite activity requiring inspection, hazardous materials, or damaged wiring
  • place air-permeable batt insulation alone against a leaky rim without the air barrier specified in the approved assembly
  • close a termite-inspection gap required by the local jurisdiction with foam, sealant, or insulation
  • leave exposed foam without its required thermal or ignition barrier in any assembly where one is specified

Any condition meeting a stop-work criterion in Step 3 must halt work in that bay and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before insulation proceeds.

Trade Note

Treat each rim-joist bay as its own small assembly rather than one long continuous run. Bays vary in width, may contain different penetrations, and may show different moisture conditions from one joist space to the next. Measuring, fitting, and inspecting bay by bay catches problems that a single continuous measurement would miss.

Setup Standard

Do not fit or seal rigid foam into any rim-joist bay until active water entry, structural decay, and hazardous-material or wiring concerns identified during inspection have been corrected or referred. Do not proceed in a bay where the moisture meter shows elevated readings until the source is identified and resolved.

Shop Tip

Cut each foam insert slightly smaller than its bay on all four sides rather than attempting a tight friction fit. The resulting gap becomes the controlled sealant joint specified in Step 7, and it is far easier to seal a consistent small gap than to force an oversized panel into an irregular bay.

Common Field Fundamentals

Rim-joist work is inspected, sealed, and insulated bay by bay rather than as one continuous run, because each bay can differ in width, moisture condition, and penetrations. Move systematically along the rim, completing the full sequence — inspect, measure, cut, fit, seal, protect, and inspect again — for each bay before moving to the next.

Foam, Air Barrier, and Batt Insulation Are Separate Functions

The rigid-foam insert provides both the air barrier and the primary thermal resistance in most approved rim-joist assemblies. Inboard batt insulation, where specified, adds thermal resistance but is not itself an air barrier. Do not treat the batt as a substitute for a continuous foam-and-sealant air barrier, and do not place batt alone against an unsealed rim.

Measurement Cue — Size Foam for a Controlled Sealant Joint

Cut each rigid-foam insert smaller than its bay on all sides by the amount specified for the approved sealant or foam product, producing a consistent, sealable gap rather than a tight friction fit. Record the actual bay dimensions and the insert dimensions on the worksheet for each bay.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Active Water, Decay, or Wiring Hazards

Stop work in any bay showing active water entry, structural decay, termite activity requiring inspection, hazardous materials, or damaged wiring. Do not fit, seal, or insulate that bay until the condition has been corrected or referred to the appropriate qualified professional and confirmed dry or repaired.

Code Alert — Termite-Inspection Gaps Vary by Jurisdiction

Confirm the required termite-inspection gap dimension and location with the adopted local code before fitting foam or sealant near the sill or foundation wall. Do not close a required inspection gap with foam, sealant, batt insulation, or any other material.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Ignition-Barrier and Foam Compatibility

Confirm the foam type, thickness, vapor characteristics, and required ignition-barrier system against the manufacturer instructions before installation. Use only the sealant, tape, or barrier material identified as compatible with the specified foam product.

E. Preparation — Review and Inspection

  1. Review the rim-joist assembly, exterior sheathing, foundation, sill, termite requirements, climate strategy, foam product, and fire-protection requirements. Confirm the approved foam type, thickness, ignition-barrier system, and required termite-inspection gap before entering the work area.
  2. Inspect the rim and sill for water staining, rot, insects, corrosion, foundation leakage, and exterior flashing defects. Use the moisture meter at the sill and rim in every bay and record each reading on the worksheet.
  3. Stop work for active water entry, structural decay, termite activity requiring inspection, hazardous materials, or damaged wiring. Record the affected bay as excluded, state the specific reason, and identify the qualified professional required before work can proceed in that bay.
  4. Identify service penetrations, electrical boxes, joist hangers, anchor bolts, ducts, and combustion-clearance zones. Record each item and its location for every bay on the worksheet before cutting any foam.

F. Substrate Preparation and Measurement

  1. Clean loose dust and debris from the rim and surrounding framing. Vacuum each bay before measuring so the foam insert seats against a clean surface.
  2. Measure each bay independently. Record width and height at both the top and bottom of the bay, since rim-joist bays are frequently out of square.
  3. Cut rigid foam slightly smaller than the bay to create a controlled sealant joint. Cut using the manufacturer-approved method for the specified foam product, and verify the resulting gap is consistent on all four sides.
  4. Verify foam type, thickness, vapor characteristics, ignition-barrier requirement, and substrate compatibility. Confirm the cut insert matches the approved product specification before fitting it into the bay.

G. Fitting, Sealing, and Termite-Gap Preservation

  1. Fit the foam against the approved surface without trapping active moisture. Confirm the moisture reading for the bay one final time immediately before fitting the insert.
  2. Maintain required termite-inspection gaps where applicable. Position the foam insert so the required inspection strip remains fully visible and unobstructed.
  3. Seal the foam perimeter continuously with compatible sealant or approved one-component foam. Tool the bead into full contact with both the foam edge and the surrounding framing on all sides.
  4. Seal service penetrations using Chapter 4 methods. Apply the compatible sealant, gasket, or firestop system identified for each penetration type without bonding serviceable joints.

H. Inboard Insulation and Fire Protection

  1. Install additional batt insulation inboard of the foam only when the approved assembly requires it. Fit the batt snugly against the foam without compressing it, and confirm the facing orientation matches the approved vapor-control design.
  2. Do not place air-permeable batt alone against a leaky rim without the specified air barrier. Confirm the foam-and-sealant air barrier is complete and cured before installing any inboard batt.
  3. Protect exposed foam with the required thermal or ignition barrier. Install the barrier material per the manufacturer instructions, covering every exposed foam surface specified by the approved assembly.

I. Access Preservation and Inspection

  1. Preserve access to anchor bolts, valves, electrical boxes, and inspection zones. Confirm each identified item from Step 4 remains visible and reachable after foam and sealant installation.
  2. Inspect edge seals, foam fit, fire protection, moisture condition, and continuity between bays. Check every bay individually rather than sampling a subset.
  3. Correct gaps, loose inserts, incompatible foam, or missed penetrations. Remove and refit any insert that does not meet the Acceptance Standards table before proceeding.
  4. Document product, thickness, bay locations, termite gap, and protection method. Complete the worksheet entry for every bay, including product lot information and photographs of each bay before concealment.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review every completed bay using the same checks regardless of which section of the rim it occupies.

  • Moisture check: confirm every bay was dry at the time of insulation and that no bay was closed over an unresolved water source.
  • Fit check: confirm every foam insert has a consistent, sealable perimeter gap on all four sides.
  • Seal check: confirm every perimeter seal is continuous, fully tooled, and free of gaps, pinholes, or skips.
  • Penetration check: confirm every service penetration is sealed without bonding a serviceable joint.
  • Termite-gap check: confirm the required inspection strip remains fully visible and unobstructed in every applicable bay.
  • Fire-protection check: confirm every exposed foam surface is covered by the required barrier where specified.
  • Access check: confirm anchor bolts, valves, electrical boxes, and inspection zones remain visible and reachable.

Complete the Inspection Checklist in the Rim-Joist Bay Log below before concealing any bay.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Foam Insert Bows or Will Not Seat Flat

Probable cause: the insert was cut oversized for the bay, or the bay itself is out of square and the insert was measured at only one point.
Confirmation: remeasure the bay at both top and bottom, and compare against the cut insert dimensions.
Correction: remove the insert, recut it to the corrected dimensions with a consistent perimeter gap on all sides, and refit.
Reinspection: repeat the fit check and confirm the insert seats flat without bowing before sealing.

Perimeter Sealant Bead Has Gaps or Pinholes

Probable cause: the bead was applied too quickly or was not tooled into full contact with both the foam edge and the framing.
Confirmation: inspect the full perimeter of each insert under direct light for gaps, thin spots, or pinholes.
Correction: clean the area, reapply compatible sealant to the gap, and tool it into full contact with both surfaces.
Reinspection: repeat the seal check after the repaired sealant has cured.

Foam or Sealant Was Installed Over an Unresolved Moisture Source

Probable cause: the moisture reading was taken once at the start of the day and not rechecked immediately before fitting the foam, allowing a slow leak or condensation source to go unnoticed.
Confirmation: remove the foam insert in the affected bay and inspect the rim and sill directly with the moisture meter.
Correction: identify and correct the moisture source before reinstalling any foam or sealant in that bay.
Reinspection: confirm a dry moisture reading immediately before refitting and resealing the bay.

Termite-Inspection Gap Was Partially Covered

Probable cause: the foam insert or sealant bead was positioned without first confirming the exact location and dimension of the required inspection strip.
Confirmation: compare the installed foam position against the jurisdiction's required termite-inspection gap dimension.
Correction: remove and reposition the foam insert to fully expose the required strip, trimming the insert if necessary.
Reinspection: repeat the termite-gap check across every applicable bay, not only the corrected one.

Exposed Foam Was Concealed Without Its Required Barrier

Probable cause: the ignition-barrier or thermal-barrier step was skipped because the foam appeared adequately covered by adjacent framing.
Confirmation: review the approved assembly specification and inspect each bay for uncovered foam surface area.
Correction: install the specified barrier material over every exposed foam surface per the manufacturer instructions before the bay is concealed.
Reinspection: repeat the fire-protection check on every bay before final documentation.

Field Terms

Rim Joist
The band of framing that closes the ends of the floor joists atop the sill plate and foundation wall.
Bay
One individual space between two adjacent floor joists at the rim-joist plane.
Controlled Sealant Joint
The intentional gap between a rigid-foam insert and surrounding framing, sized for a continuous sealant or foam bead.
Termite-Inspection Gap
A required unobstructed strip of foundation or framing that must remain visible for termite inspection.
Ignition Barrier
A required protective material covering exposed foam to delay ignition.
Combustion-Clearance Zone
The required clearance area around a flue, chimney, or heat-producing component that must remain free of foam, sealant, and insulation.
Sill Plate
The framing member bolted to the top of the foundation wall on which the rim joist and floor system bear.
Joist Hanger
A metal connector supporting the end of a floor joist at the rim, which must remain visible and accessible after insulation.

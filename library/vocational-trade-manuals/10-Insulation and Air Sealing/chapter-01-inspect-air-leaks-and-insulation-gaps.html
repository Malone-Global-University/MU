Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect a home's thermal enclosure for air leaks, missing or damaged insulation, moisture risk, ventilation conflicts, and heat-clearance hazards, and to turn those findings into a prioritized, documented work plan. The finished work product is a completed inspection record covering the exterior, interior, attic, wall cavities where accessible, and floor or crawlspace areas, plus a written work plan that sequences the remaining chapters of this book.

You will gather existing records, define safe and excluded inspection boundaries, walk the exterior and interior of the structure, inspect the attic from a safe platform, identify bypasses and thermal weak points, record insulation condition, recognize stop-work hazards, photograph deficiencies, and classify every finding before issuing a proceed, conditional-proceed, or stop-work decision.

This is the first project in the book. It establishes the inspection method, documentation habits, and hazard-recognition skills that every later chapter depends on. Chapter 2 uses this inspection record to set up the work area, and every air-sealing and insulation chapter that follows traces back to a deficiency identified here.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-001 — Identify common air-leak locations throughout a structure.
  • IN-002 — Inspect attic insulation for type, condition, depth, and coverage.
  • IN-003 — Inspect wall and floor cavities through approved, non-destructive access.
  • IN-004 — Identify thermal bypasses that allow air to move around or through insulation.
  • IN-005 — Document deficiencies with location references and photographs.
  • IN-006 — Prepare an insulation work plan that sequences prerequisites, trades, and inspections.

Where This Skill Fits

Every remaining chapter in this book performs work on a specific deficiency category: framing-joint air leaks, service penetrations, attic bypasses, cavity insulation, attic ventilation, and moisture sources. None of that work can be safely sequenced without the inspection and work plan produced here.

The classification system used in this chapter — air leakage, missing insulation, compressed insulation, moisture, ventilation conflict, heat-clearance issue, hazardous material, or professional referral — is the same classification system referenced throughout the book, including the final quality inspection in Chapter 19 and the capstone project in Chapter 20.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • gathering available plans, energy documents, insulation records, repair history, and owner-reported complaints
  • defining safe, accessible inspection boundaries and excluded hazardous areas
  • inspecting exterior conditions that could wet insulation
  • inspecting interior ceilings, walls, floors, and penetrations for signs of air leakage and moisture
  • inspecting accessible attic areas from a safe platform or walkway
  • inspecting accessible wall, floor, crawlspace, and rim-joist cavities through approved non-destructive access
  • identifying thermal bypasses, heat-source clearances, and components requiring special treatment
  • documenting, classifying, and prioritizing every finding into a work plan

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • open finished wall, ceiling, or floor surfaces to inspect concealed cavities beyond approved non-destructive access points
  • disturb, sample, or handle suspected asbestos-containing material, vermiculite, or other suspected hazardous material
  • work around active knob-and-tube wiring, damaged wiring, or energized components that cannot be made safe
  • perform any air-sealing, insulation, or repair work; this chapter is inspection and planning only

Any condition that meets a stop-work criterion in Step 15 must halt the inspection of that area and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before work continues.

Trade Note

Every deficiency you record in this chapter must include a location reference specific enough that a different worker could find and correct it in a later chapter without asking you where it was. A vague note such as "attic leaky" is not an adequate record; "north gable end, above the bathroom exhaust duct penetration" is.

Setup Standard

Do not step off a walk board or joist-supported platform in any attic without first confirming safe footing and adequate lighting. Do not enter a crawlspace, basement, or confined area that has not been checked for standing water, sewage contamination, unsafe wiring, or structural damage.

Shop Tip

Inspect and record the exterior and interior conditions before climbing into the attic. Staining, drafts, or temperature differences you notice from inside the living space often tell you exactly where to look first once you are in the attic or crawlspace.

Prerequisites

  • Written or verbal permission from the owner or occupant to inspect the structure, including attic, crawlspace, and interior spaces
  • Available plans, energy documents, insulation records, repair history, and owner-reported complaints, if any exist
  • Safe, functioning attic access and, where applicable, safe crawlspace or basement access

  • Confirmation that the structure is not under an active hazardous-material abatement order that would restrict entry to any area

Common Field Fundamentals

Every inspection in this project moves from broad to specific: gather records, define boundaries, walk the exterior, walk the interior, then enter the attic and any accessible cavities. This sequence prevents the inspector from missing an obvious complaint-driven clue in the living space before climbing into the attic looking for something unrelated.

Air Leakage, Thermal Bypasses, and Insulation Defects

Air leakage and thermal bypasses are not the same as missing insulation, though they often occur together. Insulation slows heat transfer through a material; the air barrier stops air movement. A wall cavity can be fully packed with insulation and still leak air badly at an unsealed top plate. Record these as separate classifications even when they occur at the same location.

Measurement Cue — Record Approximate Insulation Depth

Where attic insulation is accessible, record approximate depth at several representative locations rather than a single spot check. Note whether depth is consistent or varies across the attic, since variation itself is a deficiency worth recording.

Critical Safety Cue — Stop Work for Hazardous Materials and Unsafe Wiring

Stop the inspection of any area where you suspect asbestos-containing insulation, older vermiculite, loose mineral fibers of unknown origin, lead contamination, widespread mold, sewage contamination, animal waste, fire damage, or structural failure. Stop the inspection around active knob-and-tube wiring, damaged wiring, overheated conductors, open electrical boxes, or unsupported cables. Do not touch, disturb, or attempt to sample any suspected hazardous material. Record the area as excluded and refer it to the appropriate qualified professional.

Field Safety Cue — Attic and Crawlspace Footing

Step only on ceiling joists, a walk board, or another engineered walking surface in an attic; stepping between joists onto insulation-covered drywall can cause a fall through the ceiling. Confirm crawlspace and confined-space footing, headroom, and air quality before fully entering, and keep a second person aware of your location during crawlspace or confined-attic work.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Diagnostic Instrument Limits

If infrared cameras, airflow meters, or smoke pencils are used, operate them only under the conditions specified by the manufacturer and only when the required test conditions, such as a minimum indoor-outdoor temperature difference, are met. Document the test conditions and note that instrument readings are indicative, not a substitute for direct visual inspection.

E. Preparation — Records and Boundaries

  1. Obtain available plans, energy documents, insulation records, repair history, utility observations, and owner complaints. Record the source and date of each document you review, and note if no records exist.
  2. Define the inspection boundaries, accessible assemblies, occupied-space protections, and excluded hazardous areas. Write the boundaries on the inspection worksheet before entering any area, including any zones excluded at the outset because of known hazards or occupant restrictions.
  3. Identify the intended thermal and air-control boundaries for walls, ceilings, floors, attics, crawlspaces, basements, and attached spaces. Use available plans where they exist; where no plans exist, record the boundary as observed from visible insulation and construction type.

F. Exterior and Interior Inspection

  1. Inspect exterior conditions for roof leaks, siding damage, foundation moisture, grading problems, and failed penetrations that could wet insulation. Walk the full perimeter and record every condition that could allow bulk water to reach insulated assemblies.
  2. Inspect interior ceilings, walls, floors, trim, outlets, windows, doors, and access panels for staining, drafts, dust tracking, condensation, or temperature differences. Move room to room and record each observation with its room and location.

G. Attic Inspection

  1. Inspect accessible attic areas from a safe platform or walkway. Confirm walk-board or joist footing and adequate lighting before proceeding past the access opening.
  2. Record insulation type, condition, approximate depth, coverage, disturbance, compression, wind washing, and blocked ventilation. Take representative depth readings at several locations across the attic, not just near the access opening.
  3. Identify attic bypasses at partition tops, plumbing chases, dropped soffits, duct shafts, access hatches, wiring penetrations, and open framing cavities. Record the location of each bypass found.

H. Cavity Inspection

  1. Inspect accessible wall cavities before closure or through approved non-destructive access. Use only access points already open or specifically approved for this inspection; do not cut or remove finished surfaces.
  2. Record missing, compressed, split, wet, unsupported, or poorly fitted cavity insulation. Note the specific cavity location for each finding.
  3. Inspect accessible floor, crawlspace, basement, band-joist, and rim-joist areas. Confirm safe entry conditions before fully entering any confined space.
  4. Identify open joist bays, unsealed penetrations, fallen batts, damp insulation, exposed soil, or missing supports. Record the location of each finding.

I. Bypass, Component, and Hazard Identification

  1. Identify thermal bypasses where air can move around or through insulation. Cross-reference findings from the attic and cavity inspections to confirm each bypass is recorded once with a clear location.
  2. Identify recessed lights, chimneys, flues, electrical equipment, exhaust ducts, and other components requiring clearance or special treatment. Record the component type and its location for use in later chapters.
  3. Stop work for vermiculite, suspected asbestos, knob-and-tube wiring, severe mold, active leakage, animal waste, structural damage, or unsafe access. Record the area as excluded, state the specific reason, and identify the qualified professional required before work can proceed.

J. Diagnostic Instruments, Documentation, and Classification

  1. Use infrared or airflow instruments only under the approved test conditions and document their limitations. Record the instrument used, the test conditions present at the time, and a note that readings are indicative rather than conclusive.
  2. Photograph each significant deficiency with a location reference. Label each photograph with the same location description used on the worksheet.
  3. Classify findings as air leakage, missing insulation, compressed insulation, moisture, ventilation conflict, heat-clearance issue, hazardous material, or professional referral. Assign exactly one primary classification per worksheet entry, noting a secondary classification where a finding genuinely fits more than one category.

K. Work Plan and Decision

  1. Prepare a prioritized work plan identifying prerequisites, materials, access needs, required trades, inspections, and sequence. Order the plan so moisture sources and hazardous-material referrals are addressed before any air-sealing or insulation chapter that depends on them.
  2. Issue a proceed, conditional-proceed, or stop-work decision for Chapter 2. State the decision explicitly on the worksheet, along with any conditions that must be met before Chapter 2 work begins.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed inspection using the same checks regardless of which area produced the finding.

  • Coverage check: confirm every area in the Inspection Area Schedule was inspected or recorded as excluded with a stated reason.
  • Location check: confirm every deficiency has a location reference specific enough for another worker to find it.
  • Classification check: confirm every deficiency has exactly one primary classification.
  • Photograph check: confirm every significant deficiency has a labeled photograph.
  • Hazard check: confirm every stop-work condition was excluded and referred rather than inspected further.
  • Work-plan check: confirm the plan states prerequisites, materials, access needs, required trades, inspections, sequence, and a final decision.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Deficiencies Cannot Be Located by a Different Worker

Probable cause: the location reference was too general, such as "attic" or "bathroom wall," instead of naming the specific framing bay, fixture, or penetration.
Confirmation: attempt to locate each recorded deficiency using only the worksheet description and photograph, without relying on memory.
Correction: revisit the area and rewrite the location reference using a fixed reference point, such as a joist number, wall stud bay, or named fixture.
Reinspection: repeat the location check on the corrected entries.

A Finding Is Missing Its Classification

Probable cause: the finding was photographed or noted quickly during the walk-through and never assigned one of the eight required classifications.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for any entry without a classification column filled in.
Correction: revisit the finding, or the photograph if the condition is well documented, and assign the correct primary classification.
Reinspection: repeat the classification check across the full worksheet.

A Suspected Hazardous Material Was Inspected Too Closely

Probable cause: the stop-work criteria in Step 15 were not reviewed before entering the attic or crawlspace, so the inspector approached or disturbed the material before recognizing it.
Confirmation: ask the inspector to describe exactly what contact, if any, occurred with the suspected material.
Correction: stop all further inspection of that area immediately, record the area as excluded with the specific hazard identified, and refer it to a qualified hazardous-material professional before any further entry.
Reinspection: confirm the excluded-area entry is complete and that no further inspection of that area occurs until the referral is resolved.

The Work Plan Sequences Insulation Before an Unresolved Moisture Source

Probable cause: the work plan was written area by area instead of being reviewed as a whole against the System-Sequence Control principle that moisture sources must be corrected before insulation is installed.
Confirmation: compare every moisture-classified finding against the work-plan sequence and confirm it appears before any insulation step that depends on the same assembly.
Correction: reorder the work plan so every moisture-classified finding is corrected, and drying confirmed, before the dependent insulation step.
Reinspection: repeat the work-plan check on the corrected sequence.

Attic Depth Readings Vary Widely With No Explanation Recorded

Probable cause: depth was recorded at only one location near the access opening, missing settled, disturbed, or wind-washed areas elsewhere in the attic.
Confirmation: revisit the attic and take additional depth readings at a spread of locations, including corners and eave areas.
Correction: record the additional readings and note the specific areas of variation as separate deficiencies where they indicate compression, wind washing, or missing insulation.
Reinspection: repeat the coverage check for the attic area.

Cleanup

This project ends with a complete inspection record and work plan, not a pile of loose notes and photographs.

  • Close and secure attic access, crawlspace access, and any interior access panels opened during the inspection
  • Confirm no tools, ladders, or walk boards were left in the attic or crawlspace
  • Match every photograph to its corresponding worksheet entry and location reference
  • File the completed inspection worksheet and work plan for use in Chapter 2
  • Communicate any stop-work referrals to the owner or occupant before leaving the site

Field Terms

Thermal Boundary
The intended continuous line of insulation separating conditioned space from unconditioned space or the exterior.
Air Barrier
The intended continuous line of air-impermeable material controlling air movement across the thermal boundary.
Thermal Bypass
A path that allows air, and the heat it carries, to move around or through insulation without crossing a continuous air barrier.
Excluded Area
Any space the inspection does not enter because of suspected hazardous material, unsafe access, or occupant restriction.
Location Reference
A written description specific enough that another worker could locate the exact finding without additional guidance.
Deficiency Classification
One of eight required categories: air leakage, missing insulation, compressed insulation, moisture, ventilation conflict, heat-clearance issue, hazardous material, or professional referral.
Wind Washing
Displacement or degradation of insulation performance caused by outside air moving through or across the insulation.
Work Plan
The prioritized document sequencing prerequisites, materials, access, trades, and inspections for the remaining chapters of this book.

Project Closeout

You have completed the first project in the insulation and air-sealing sequence: inspecting a home for air leaks and insulation gaps. This project required you to identify common air-leak locations (IN-001), inspect attic insulation for type, condition, depth, and coverage (IN-002), inspect accessible wall and floor cavities through approved access (IN-003), identify thermal bypasses (IN-004), document deficiencies with location references and photographs (IN-005), and prepare an insulation work plan (IN-006).

You now have a documented, prioritized work plan and a proceed, conditional-proceed, or stop-work decision. That plan carries directly into the next project: setting up a safe, ventilated, hazard-controlled work area for the air-sealing and insulation work the plan identifies.

File your completed inspection worksheet, photographs, and work plan with the project records. You will reference this exact document at every later inspection and closeout stage in this book, including the Chapter 19 final quality inspection.

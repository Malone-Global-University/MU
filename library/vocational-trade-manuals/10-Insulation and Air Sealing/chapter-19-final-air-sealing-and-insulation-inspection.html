Job Overview

This project qualifies you to inspect a complete thermal enclosure after every prior air-sealing and insulation chapter has been performed, and before drywall or any other concealment covers the work. The finished work product is a documented final quality inspection covering every assembly type addressed in this book — framing joints, service penetrations, attic bypasses, ventilation baffles, wall and floor cavities, window and door perimeters, attic insulation, rim joists, rigid foam, vapor control, duct areas, and the attic hatch — together with a deficiency list, correction record, and a formal thermal-envelope readiness decision.

You will assemble every plan, schedule, product record, photograph, and prior inspection report produced by Chapters 1 through 18, confirm that rough plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and fireblocking inspections are complete and that moisture problems have been corrected and dried, and then work systematically through every assembly type in the structure, checking air-sealing continuity, insulation depth and fit, ventilation clearance, vapor-control placement, and preserved service access. You will record measurements at the required sampling locations, perform any approved performance testing, prepare and resolve a deficiency list, and issue an approved, conditional, or rejected readiness status.

This is the release inspection for the entire book. It does not repeat the detailed work of earlier chapters; it verifies that work. The record you produce here is handed off with the structure for the Chapter 20 capstone and ultimately for the drywall trade in Book 11.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • IN-109 — Inspect completed air-sealing work for continuity across framing, penetrations, and bypasses.
  • IN-110 — Inspect insulation depth in attic, floor, and rim-joist assemblies against the approved schedule.
  • IN-111 — Inspect cavity fill in wall assemblies for full-depth contact and absence of compression or voids.
  • IN-112 — Inspect ventilation clearance at baffles, soffits, and heat-producing components.
  • IN-113 — Document deficiencies with location, condition, required correction, responsible party, and reinspection method.
  • IN-114 — Approve thermal-envelope readiness for concealment based on completed correction and reinspection.

Where This Skill Fits

Every deficiency classification and inspection habit established in Chapter 1 is used again here, but at the scale of the entire structure rather than a single finding. This chapter also draws directly on the moisture-diagnosis method from Chapter 18: no assembly may be approved for concealment while an unresolved moisture source remains active behind it.

The readiness decision produced here is the specific gate the System-Sequence Control principle in the book outline describes: Chapter 19 is the release inspection before drywall or other enclosure layers cover the work. The Chapter 20 capstone performs this same inspection as part of its integrated sequence, and the record from this chapter becomes the reference standard for that final review.

Scope and Intended Use

This project covers:

  • assembling plans, schedules, product records, photographs, and prior inspection reports from Chapters 1 through 18
  • confirming that plumbing, electrical, HVAC, fireblocking, and required rough inspections are complete
  • confirming that all bulk-water and moisture problems have been corrected and dried
  • inspecting air-sealing continuity, cavity insulation, attic insulation, floor and rim-joist insulation, window and door perimeters, rigid foam, vapor control, duct-adjacent work, and the attic hatch
  • recording measurements at required sampling locations and performing any approved performance testing
  • preparing a deficiency list, verifying correction, and issuing a formal readiness decision

This project does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform new air-sealing, insulation, or vapor-control installation as part of this chapter; corrections belong to the specific chapter whose method applies to that deficiency
  • conceal any assembly before its deficiencies are corrected and reinspected
  • perform blower-door, infrared, or smoke testing outside an approved test plan
  • approve readiness for an assembly with an unresolved stop-work condition, active moisture source, or unauthorized double vapor barrier

Any condition that meets a stop-work criterion identified in earlier chapters, or that reveals a new hazardous condition, must halt the inspection of that assembly and be referred to the appropriate qualified professional before a readiness decision is issued for it.

Trade Note

This chapter is an audit, not a rebuild. If a deficiency is found, record it, assign it to the chapter and method that already governs that type of work, correct it using that method, and reinspect using this chapter's check. Do not invent a new correction method here.

Why This Chapter Does Not Redesign Prior Work

Every acceptance standard used in this inspection was already established in Chapters 3 through 17. This chapter's job is to verify those standards were met across the whole structure, not to introduce a new or stricter standard. When a deficiency is found, correct it using the method from the governing chapter, then reinspect using that same chapter's check before returning to this chapter's overall sign-off.

Measurement Cue — Sample at the Required Locations, Not Just the Convenient Ones

Record depth or cavity measurements at the required sampling locations across the full structure, including corners, eaves, and areas away from the attic access. A single convenient reading near the access hatch does not represent the whole assembly.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Conceal Work With an Unresolved Moisture Source

Do not approve any assembly for concealment while an active moisture source, unresolved leak, or undried framing or insulation remains behind it. If a Chapter 18 moisture condition has not been fully corrected and confirmed dry, issue a rejected or conditional status for that assembly and refer it back to Chapter 18 procedures.

Field Safety Cue — Attic and Crawlspace Footing During Sampling

Step only on ceiling joists, a walk board, or another engineered walking surface while sampling attic insulation depth. Confirm crawlspace footing, headroom, and air quality before entering to inspect floor or rim-joist insulation.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Performance Testing Limits

Perform blower-door, infrared, smoke, or other performance testing only under the approved test plan and only using the equipment's specified operating conditions. Document any limitation of the test method and treat results as supplementary to, not a replacement for, direct visual inspection.

D. Preparation — Records and Prerequisite Confirmation

  1. Assemble approved plans, insulation schedule, air-barrier details, vapor-control details, product records, photographs, and prior inspection reports. Confirm every Chapter 1–18 record is present before beginning; note any missing document and its source.
  2. Confirm that plumbing, electrical, HVAC, fireblocking, and required rough inspections are complete. Record the inspection dates and approving authority for each.
  3. Verify that all bulk-water and moisture problems have been corrected and dried. Cross-reference every Chapter 18 finding and confirm its correction and drying confirmation are on file.

E. Air-Sealing and Bypass Inspection

  1. Inspect framing-joint air seals for continuity and adhesion. Walk the assigned framing joints and confirm no pinholes, skips, or uncured product remain.
  2. Inspect plumbing, electrical, duct, and mechanical penetration seals. Confirm continuity, correct material selection, and preserved movement allowance at each.
  3. Inspect attic bypass closures, chase blocking, and heat-source clearances. Confirm backing is supported, edges are sealed, and clearance dams remain intact.
  4. Inspect baffles, soffit ventilation, insulation dams, and attic access. Confirm the air channel from soffit intake to attic space remains open and unobstructed.

F. Cavity, Opening, and Attic Insulation Inspection

  1. Inspect fiberglass and mineral-wool cavities for full depth, edge contact, splits around wiring, fit around boxes, and absence of compression. Sample a representative set of cavities across the assigned wall areas.
  2. Inspect window and door perimeter insulation while operating the units. Confirm continuous seal, unblocked drainage, and normal operation through a full open-close-latch cycle.
  3. Inspect loose-fill attic depth, distribution, product quantity, and protected access. Record depth readings at the required sampling grid and compare to the approved target.
  4. Inspect floor and crawlspace insulation for support, moisture protection, and continuity. Confirm supports are intact and no insulation contacts standing moisture or exposed soil improperly.

G. Rim, Rigid-Foam, Vapor, and Mechanical Interface Inspection

  1. Inspect rim-joist seals, foam protection, termite gaps, and service access. Confirm foam edges are sealed and required termite-inspection gaps remain visible.
  2. Inspect rigid-foam panel seams, fasteners, edges, penetrations, and fire protection. Confirm tape adhesion, fastener seating, and any required protective covering are complete.
  3. Inspect vapor-control continuity and verify approved location. Confirm seams are lapped and sealed as specified and that no unapproved double vapor barrier has been created.
  4. Inspect duct-adjacent thermal control and preserved mechanical access. Confirm duct insulation thickness has not been reduced and that filters, dampers, and condensate drains remain reachable.
  5. Inspect the attic hatch for fit, insulation, and perimeter seal. Close the hatch and confirm full-perimeter weatherstripping compression.

H. Access, Clearance, and Testing

  1. Verify that junction boxes, valves, dampers, cleanouts, filters, and equipment remain accessible. Physically confirm reachability rather than relying on prior chapter notes alone.
  2. Verify that insulation does not contact prohibited heat sources and does not block required airflow. Check every recessed light, flue, chimney, and exhaust clearance zone identified in earlier chapters.
  3. Record depth or cavity measurements at the required sampling locations. Log each reading against its location on the inspection worksheet.
  4. Perform blower-door, infrared, smoke, or other performance testing only under the approved test plan. Record test conditions, results, and any documented limitation of the method.

I. Deficiency Resolution and Readiness Decision

  1. Prepare a deficiency list with location, condition, required correction, responsible party, and reinspection method. Assign each deficiency to its governing chapter's correction method.
  2. Correct or assign every deficiency. Confirm each correction is completed by the responsible party using the correct governing method.
  3. Reinspect completed corrections. Repeat the specific check that originally identified each deficiency.
  4. Photograph final concealed conditions. Capture a labeled photograph of every assembly type before it is concealed.
  5. Issue approved, conditional, or rejected thermal-envelope readiness status. State the decision explicitly, along with any conditions that must be met before concealment.
  6. Preserve records for the drywall handoff. File the complete inspection record, deficiency list, corrections, photographs, and test results for Book 11.

Common Inspection Quality Check

Review the completed final inspection using the same checks regardless of which assembly produced the finding.

  • Coverage check: confirm every assembly in the Assembly Inspection Schedule was inspected against its governing chapter's standard.
  • Sampling check: confirm measurements were recorded at the required sampling locations, not a single convenient point.
  • Access check: confirm every junction box, valve, damper, cleanout, filter, and equipment item remains reachable.
  • Clearance check: confirm no insulation contacts a prohibited heat source or blocks required airflow.
  • Deficiency check: confirm every recorded deficiency was corrected using its governing method and reinspected.
  • Decision check: confirm a single approved, conditional, or rejected status is recorded with any conditions stated.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

An Assembly Was Concealed Before Its Deficiency Was Corrected

Probable cause: the deficiency list was prepared but concealment began before every item on it was resolved and reinspected.
Confirmation: compare the concealment schedule against the deficiency list and identify any assembly covered before its correction was reinspected.
Correction: where possible, expose the concealed assembly to complete the correction and reinspection; where not possible, document the condition, notify the responsible parties, and record the assembly as rejected pending exposure and correction.
Reinspection: repeat the deficiency check for the affected assembly once corrected and confirm no further concealment occurs ahead of resolution.

Depth Sampling Was Limited to a Single Location Near the Access Opening

Probable cause: the inspector recorded one convenient reading instead of sampling the required grid across the attic or cavity.
Confirmation: review the worksheet for the number and spread of recorded readings against the required sampling schedule.
Correction: return to the attic or cavity and record additional readings at the missing sampling locations, including corners and eave areas.
Reinspection: repeat the sampling check for that assembly.

A Service Access Point Was Buried by a Later Chapter's Insulation Work

Probable cause: insulation installed under a later chapter did not account for an access point documented earlier in the sequence.
Confirmation: physically attempt to reach the junction box, valve, damper, cleanout, or filter and confirm whether it is obstructed.
Correction: remove the obstructing insulation and reinstall it clear of the access point, using the governing chapter's method.
Reinspection: repeat the access check for that point.

A Vapor-Control Layer Creates an Unintended Double Barrier With Rigid Foam

Probable cause: the vapor retarder and the rigid-foam layer were installed without confirming their combined permeance against the approved assembly design.
Confirmation: review the Chapter 14 and Chapter 15 product records together and identify whether both layers are low-permeance at the same location without an approved hygrothermal design.
Correction: refer the condition to the design professional identified in the approved plan for a corrected assembly detail before concealment.
Reinspection: repeat the vapor-control check once the corrected detail is implemented.

A Moisture Condition From Chapter 18 Was Not Fully Resolved Before This Inspection

Probable cause: the Chapter 18 corrective recommendation was issued but the drying and reinspection condition was not completed before this final inspection began.
Confirmation: cross-reference every Chapter 18 finding against its stated drying and reinspection condition and confirm completion.
Correction: halt approval for the affected assembly, complete the outstanding Chapter 18 correction and drying confirmation, and do not proceed with concealment until resolved.
Reinspection: repeat this chapter's inspection for the affected assembly once the moisture condition is confirmed resolved.

Field Terms

Thermal-Envelope Readiness
A structure-wide determination that air-sealing continuity, insulation quality, moisture readiness, ventilation clearance, and safety have all been verified and are ready for concealment.
Sampling Location
A predetermined point where a measurement is recorded to represent the condition of the surrounding assembly.
Deficiency List
The complete record of every finding requiring correction, with location, condition, correction, responsible party, and reinspection method.
Readiness Status
One of: approved, conditional, or rejected.
Concealment
Any drywall, finish, or enclosure layer applied over inspected assemblies.
Performance Test
Blower-door, infrared, smoke, or other instrumented testing performed only under an approved test plan.
Governing Chapter
The specific earlier chapter whose method and acceptance standard control correction of a given deficiency type.
Double Vapor Barrier
An unapproved condition where two low-permeance layers trap moisture within an assembly without an approved hygrothermal design.

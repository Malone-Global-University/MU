Job Overview

This capstone chapter qualifies you to plan and complete one noncritical mild-steel shop stand or utility frame from a current shop drawing, using every workflow taught in this book: hot-work authorization, material identification, layout, cutting, grinding, joint fit-up, clamping, tack welding, final SMAW or GMAW welding, distortion control, cleaning, dimensional and visual inspection, approved repair, finishing, and documented closeout.

You will select the approved design before procurement, obtain the shop drawing and cut list, prepare the hot-work area and equipment, verify and prepare material, lay out and cut every part, fit and tack the assembly, weld it using the approved sequence, control distortion, inspect and correct the finished welds, finish the assembly, and hand it off as a documented noncritical shop fixture.

This is the final chapter in the book. It draws directly on the work-area setup of Chapter 1, the PPE of Chapter 2, the equipment identification of Chapter 3, the steel preparation of Chapters 4 through 6, the SMAW skills of Chapters 7 through 9, the GMAW and FCAW skills of Chapters 10 through 13, the GTAW skills of Chapters 14 and 15, the fit-up and fabrication practices of Chapters 16 and 17, and the defect and finishing practices of Chapters 18 and 19.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-115 — Integrate layout, cutting, and grinding into one traceable material-preparation sequence for a complete assembly.
  • WF-116 — Fit up, clamp, and tack weld a multi-part mild-steel assembly to verified dimensions.
  • WF-117 — Complete final SMAW or GMAW welding on the assembly using a planned, distortion-controlled sequence.
  • WF-118 — Control and measure distortion, and correct fit-up problems within the approved method.
  • WF-119 — Clean, visually and dimensionally inspect, and correct the completed weldment.
  • WF-120 — Finish the assembly and complete the full welding closeout and documentation record.

Where This Skill Fits

Every earlier chapter in this book produced one piece of this capstone: a safe work area, correct PPE, identified equipment, prepared steel, controlled arcs and beads, fitted and tacked joints, and inspected, finished welds. This chapter does not teach a new arc-starting or bead-running technique. It requires you to sequence and apply the techniques you already hold to one complete, real project.

The finished stand or frame is handed off as a documented noncritical shop fixture and becomes the reference project carried forward into the Metal Fabrication volume.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • selecting one approved noncritical capstone design before material procurement
  • preparing the hot-work area, PPE, and equipment using the readiness practices from Chapters 1 through 3
  • verifying material identity and preparing every part to the approved cut list
  • fitting, clamping, and tack welding the complete assembly to verified dimensions
  • completing final SMAW or GMAW welding using a planned, distortion-controlled sequence
  • cleaning, inspecting, correcting, finishing, and documenting the completed weldment
  • completing the full shutdown, fire-watch, and closeout sequence

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • build a lifting device, jack stand, vehicle-frame component, guardrail, handrail, pressure component, trailer component, overhead support, or any life-safety or structural building component
  • use the finished project to support people, suspend loads, lift equipment, restrain vehicles, contain pressure, or carry structural building loads
  • proof-load the assembly beyond the approved noncritical stability and function check defined in this chapter
  • invent joint dimensions, weld sizes, or acceptance criteria beyond the approved shop drawing, procedure, and inspection criteria

Any weld, dimension, or fixture condition that fails the acceptance standards in this chapter must be corrected before final finish and handoff.

Trade Note

A capstone project is not a longer practice coupon. It is a complete work order: a drawing, a material list, a sequence, an inspection, and a signed record. The most common capstone failure is skipping the planning and traveler steps in Section F and moving straight to cutting — this creates fit-up and dimensional problems that cannot be corrected after welding begins.

Setup Standard

Do not cut material for this capstone until the shop drawing, cut list, material list, weld symbols, tolerances, finish requirement, and use limitation have been reviewed and any missing information has been resolved with the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator. Do not begin final welding until fit-up and tack welds have passed the pre-weld inspection in Section H.

Shop Tip

Build the part traveler before the first cut. Give every part an identifier that survives cutting, grinding, fit-up, and welding, and record every dimension, parameter, and inspection result against that identifier. A capstone without a traveler cannot be traced back to a cause when a dimension or weld fails inspection.

Code Alert — Noncritical Training Boundary

This capstone is a noncritical training weldment. It must not be represented as a certified structural weldment, and it must not be used for lifting, vehicle restraint, pressure containment, structural building loads, or any life-safety purpose. Confirm the selected design and its stated use limitation with the responsible instructor before procurement.

Common Field Fundamentals

Every stage of this capstone follows the same sequence used throughout the book: authorize, identify, prepare, fit, weld, clean, inspect, correct, finish, and close out. The capstone compresses every earlier chapter's individual sequence into one continuous project.

Measurement Cue — One Baseline for the Whole Assembly

Establish a single assembly baseline and origin before laying out the first part. Measure every cut, every fit-up check, and every finished dimension from that same baseline. Do not re-reference from a different edge partway through the build.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Force a Distorted Assembly

Do not force a hot, distorted, tack-welded assembly into alignment by unsafe clamping, hammering, or uncontrolled cooling. Stop, allow controlled cooling, and correct fit-up using the approved method in Section H before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Access for Every Planned Weld

Before tacking, confirm that clamps, fixtures, and stops do not block electrode, gun, or torch access to any joint in the weld map. A clamp that blocks access to a planned weld must be repositioned before tacking, not worked around during final welding.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Parameter Verification on Scrap

Run the parameter-verification bead on scrap matching the assembly's material and thickness before tacking or final welding. Confirm the bead against the approved starting range from the machine and consumable manufacturer before applying the same settings to the assembly.

F. Planning and Authorization

  1. Select the approved capstone design before material procurement. Confirm the design matches the preferred or alternate option described in this chapter.
  2. Confirm the project is noncritical and not intended for lifting, structural support, fall protection, vehicle restraint, pressure, trailers, overhead loads, or public safety. Record this confirmation on the traveler.
  3. Obtain the current shop drawing, revision, material list, cut list, weld symbols, tolerances, finish, intended use, and load limit. Confirm the document set is complete before proceeding.
  4. Review the drawing for missing dimensions, conflicts, inaccessible welds, unstable geometry, and unclear scope. List every open question.
  5. Resolve drawing questions before cutting. Obtain answers from the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator and record the resolution on the traveler.
  6. Select the approved SMAW or GMAW process and procedure. Confirm the process matches the equipment and consumables available at the station.
  7. Identify the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator. Record the name on the traveler.
  8. Prepare the hot-work permit or approved shop authorization. Confirm it covers the location, duration, and process planned for this capstone.
  9. Create the work-area, fire-watch, ventilation, PPE, cylinder, electrical, and emergency plan. Base this plan on the Chapter 1 readiness practice.
  10. Inspect and document the welding area. Repeat the Chapter 1 readiness walk-through for this session.
  11. Inspect and document PPE. Repeat the Chapter 2 PPE inspection.
  12. Inspect and document the welding machine, leads, clamp, holder or gun, gas system, and consumables. Repeat the Chapter 3 equipment-readiness inspection.

G. Material Verification and Part Preparation

  1. Verify material identification, grade or type, thickness, size, and condition. Compare every piece of stock against the approved material list.
  2. Reject unknown, cracked, heavily pitted, contaminated, or prohibited coated material. Do not substitute unidentified stock.
  3. Create a part traveler with identifiers for every component. Assign each identifier before cutting begins.
  4. Lay out parts from a single reference edge. Establish the assembly baseline described in Reference Conventions.
  5. Verify all cut dimensions before cutting. Check every marked line against the cut list.
  6. Set up stock supports, clamps, stops, screens, and spark controls. Confirm the cutting area meets the Chapter 5 setup standard.
  7. Cut parts using approved hand, saw, or abrasive methods. Select the tool approved for each cut per Chapter 5.
  8. Control drop pieces and hot material. Direct cut and drop pieces into the marked hot-metal zone.
  9. Deburr all handling edges. Remove burrs from every cut part before further handling.
  10. Prepare bevels or joint edges only where shown. Do not add a bevel or edge preparation not called out on the drawing.
  11. Remove rust, scale, oil, paint, and contamination from weld and clamp zones. Clean every part to bright metal in the weld and work-clamp areas.
  12. Measure and inspect every finished part. Record dimensions on the traveler against the cut list.
  13. Remake parts outside approved tolerance. Do not carry an out-of-tolerance part into assembly.

H. Fit-Up, Clamping, and Tack Welding

  1. Arrange parts in assembly order. Confirm every part identifier matches the traveler.
  2. Clean the welding table, fixtures, squares, clamps, and stops. Remove residue that could affect fit-up or grounding.
  3. Establish the assembly baseline and origin. Mark it clearly on the table.
  4. Lay out the first frame section. Position parts against the baseline and any stops.
  5. Clamp parts without lifting or twisting them. Confirm parts remain flat against the table.
  6. Check length, width, angle, square, and diagonals. Compare against the drawing tolerance before continuing.
  7. Install spacers, stops, or fixtures needed for repeatable geometry. Confirm these do not shift under clamp pressure.
  8. Confirm access for all required welds. Check every joint on the weld map against the electrode, gun, or torch angle needed.
  9. Attach the work clamp to clean sound metal close to the weld area. Confirm the clamp path avoids bearings, hinges, or unintended components.
  10. Set and record machine polarity, consumable, gas, voltage and wire speed, or amperage as applicable. Record the settings on the traveler.
  11. Run a parameter-verification bead on matching scrap. Use stock of the same material and thickness as the assembly.
  12. Inspect the test bead and obtain approval. Do not proceed to tacking without an accepted test bead.
  13. Place tack welds in the approved balanced sequence. Follow the sequence marked on the traveler.
  14. Inspect each tack for cracking, porosity, fusion, crater, and size. Reject any tack outside the approved condition.
  15. Remove and replace unacceptable tacks. Use the approved repair method before continuing.
  16. Recheck overall dimensions, diagonals, angles, gaps, and flatness. Compare against the drawing tolerance after tacking.
  17. Correct fit-up before final welding. Do not proceed to final welding with an out-of-tolerance tacked assembly.
  18. Mark the final welding sequence on the traveler. Confirm the sequence balances heat input across the assembly.

I. Final Welding and Distortion Control

  1. Begin final welding with short balanced segments where specified. Follow the sequence marked on the traveler.
  2. Maintain approved electrode or gun angle, arc length or stickout, travel speed, and puddle control. Apply the technique standards from Chapters 7 through 13.
  3. Clean slag or contamination between weld segments and passes. Do not weld over uncleaned slag or contamination.
  4. Monitor heat buildup and machine duty cycle. Pause welding if the machine or assembly approaches its limit.
  5. Alternate sides or locations according to the distortion-control plan. Do not weld one side of the assembly to completion before starting the opposite side.
  6. Do not quench the assembly unless the approved procedure permits it. Allow natural cooling between segments.
  7. Pause and remeasure critical dimensions during welding. Check length, width, diagonals, and squareness at the planned checkpoints.
  8. Stop for excessive movement, cracking, unstable fixturing, gas loss, electrical fault, or fire-control failure. Correct the condition before resuming.
  9. Complete all required welds without adding unauthorized weld length or size. Weld only the joints and lengths shown on the weld map.

J. Cooling, Dimensional Check, and Cleaning

  1. Allow controlled cooling in the designated area. Mark the assembly hot until it is verified safe to handle.
  2. Release clamps gradually and observe spring movement. Note any shift as clamps are released.
  3. Measure final length, width, height, diagonals, angles, level, square, and flatness. Record every value on the traveler.
  4. Record distortion and compare it with tolerance. Identify whether any dimension exceeds the drawing tolerance.
  5. Remove slag and spatter. Clean every weld and adjacent surface.
  6. Clean every weld for inspection. Confirm toes, starts, and stops are visible under adequate lighting.

K. Weld Inspection and Repair

  1. Inspect weld location, length, size, profile, toe fusion indicators, undercut, overlap, porosity, crater, starts, stops, arc strikes, and cracks. Use the Chapter 18 defect-identification criteria.
  2. Use approved weld gauges. Measure fillet size, length, and reinforcement against the acceptance standard.
  3. Mark accepted, repair, and review locations on the weld map. Record the status of every weld individually.
  4. Prepare a qualified corrective-action plan for approved repair items. Identify the probable cause before repairing.
  5. Remove defects without reducing required section thickness. Use the approved excavation method.
  6. Reweld and reinspect repaired areas. Confirm the repaired weld meets the original acceptance standard.

L. Finishing and Function Check

  1. Deburr sharp edges and corners. Remove handling hazards from the completed assembly.
  2. Grind only surfaces shown by the drawing or finish specification. Do not grind a weld flush unless the drawing permits it.
  3. Clean the entire assembly for coating. Remove oil, dust, slag, and abrasive residue.
  4. Apply the approved primer, paint, oil, or finish under required ventilation and cure conditions. Follow the manufacturer's cure and ventilation instructions.
  5. Do not coat unaccepted welds. Confirm every weld is marked accepted before coating.
  6. Inspect finish coverage and cure. Confirm the coating is complete and cured before handling.
  7. Perform only the approved noncritical stability and function check. Use the check defined by the drawing or instructor.
  8. Do not proof load beyond the stated test or use limit. Stop the function check at the defined limit.
  9. Attach a permanent or durable project identification and use-limitation label where required. Confirm the label states the noncritical use and load limit.

M. Documentation and Closeout

  1. Complete the material, cut, fit-up, tack, parameter, weld, repair, dimension, finish, and inspection records. Confirm every field on the traveler is filled in.
  2. Compile the drawing, traveler, photographs, machine setup, consumable information, inspection form, and finish data. Assemble the complete capstone record.
  3. Shut down the welding machine. De-energize and secure the station.
  4. Close and secure gas cylinders. Confirm valves are closed and cylinders are upright and secured.
  5. Coil leads and hoses without damage. Store them away from sharp edges and traffic.
  6. Store electrodes, wire, filler, tungsten, tools, and PPE. Return each item to its designated storage location.
  7. Remove scrap, abrasive waste, sharp metal, and combustible residue. Clear the work area completely.
  8. Mark or isolate remaining hot material. Confirm no hot material remains unmarked.
  9. Complete the required fire-watch and post-work area inspection. Confirm the monitoring period from Chapter 1 is observed.
  10. Obtain final evaluator acceptance. Have the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator sign the completed record.
  11. Hand off the capstone as a documented noncritical shop stand or utility frame. Confirm the use-limitation label and record accompany the handoff.
  12. Document the formal transition to the Metal Fabrication volume. Record the capstone as the reference project carried forward.

Common Assembly Inspection

Inspect the capstone using the same checks regardless of the selected design.

  • Material check: confirm every part matches the approved material list and cut list, with no substitutions.
  • Fit-up check: confirm dimensions, diagonals, angles, and gaps meet tolerance before and after tacking.
  • Weld check: confirm every planned weld is present, correctly sized, and free of rejectable defects.
  • Distortion check: confirm final dimensions, square, and flatness meet the drawing tolerance.
  • Finish check: confirm cleaning, approved grinding, and coating occur only on accepted welds and surfaces.
  • Documentation check: confirm the traveler, weld map, and inspection record are complete and signed.

Complete the Capstone Weld Inspection and Closeout Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before handoff.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Overall Dimensions Drift Out of Tolerance During Tacking

Probable cause: tacks were placed in an unbalanced order, or clamps were released before dimensions were rechecked.
Confirmation: measure length, width, and diagonals against the drawing and compare with the pre-tack fit-up record.
Correction: loosen the affected clamp, realign the assembly against the baseline, and re-tack using the approved balanced sequence.
Reinspection: repeat the fit-up dimensional check before continuing to final welding.

The Assembly Bows or Twists During Final Welding

Probable cause: welding proceeded on one side to completion before the opposite side was welded, concentrating heat and shrinkage on one face.
Confirmation: compare the weld sequence actually used against the distortion-control plan on the traveler.
Correction: pause welding, allow controlled cooling, and resume using the approved alternating sequence for the remaining joints.
Reinspection: remeasure length, diagonals, squareness, and flatness before continuing.

A Planned Weld Is Inaccessible Once the Assembly Is Clamped

Probable cause: clamp or fixture position was set for dimensional stability without checking electrode, gun, or torch access to every joint on the weld map.
Confirmation: compare the weld map against the current clamp and fixture layout.
Correction: reposition the clamp or fixture to restore access without releasing fit-up outside tolerance.
Reinspection: repeat the access check for every remaining planned weld before welding continues.

A Finished Weld Fails Visual Inspection After Cleaning

Probable cause: the weld was welded with incorrect parameters, technique, or contaminated fit-up, or slag was not fully removed before inspection.
Confirmation: compare the defect against the Chapter 18 criteria and the parameters recorded on the traveler.
Correction: prepare a corrective-action plan, remove the defect without reducing required section thickness, and reweld using corrected parameters or technique.
Reinspection: reinspect the repaired weld against the original acceptance standard.

The Function Check Reveals Instability Within the Stated Load Limit

Probable cause: a base or foot member is not flat, a weld is undersized, or distortion has shifted the assembly's bearing points.
Confirmation: recheck flatness, level, and weld size at the affected joint against the acceptance standards.
Correction: correct the affected joint or bearing surface using the approved repair method; do not proceed to finish coating until the correction is inspected.
Reinspection: repeat the noncritical stability and function check after correction.

The Documentation Record Is Incomplete at Handoff

Probable cause: parameters, tack inspections, or dimensional checks were performed but not recorded on the traveler as the build progressed.
Confirmation: compare the traveler against the required fields in Section M.
Correction: reconstruct missing entries from the weld map, photographs, and machine settings while they remain traceable, and note any entry that cannot be reconstructed.
Reinspection: confirm the completed record is reviewed and signed by the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator before handoff.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a finished, documented weldment, not an open work area. Closeout here protects both the finished project and every future session in this booth.

  • Shut down and de-energize the welding machine
  • Close and secure gas cylinders in the upright, secured position
  • Coil leads and hoses without kinks or damage
  • Store electrodes, wire, filler, tungsten, tools, and PPE in their designated locations
  • Remove scrap, abrasive waste, sharp metal, and combustible residue from the work area
  • Mark or isolate any remaining hot material and complete the required fire-watch period
  • Store the signed capstone record with the drawing, traveler, and photographs

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to take a set of cut, deburred, and cleaned mild-steel parts and turn them into one dimensionally verified, squared, clamped, and tacked assembly that is ready for final welding. You will lay out the parts from a single reference edge, fixture and square them against stops and clamps, verify overall dimensions and diagonals, place a balanced sequence of sound tack welds, inspect and correct every tack, and recheck the full assembly before releasing it for final welding.

This is a fit-up and tacking chapter. It does not include final welding. The assembly you produce here is the exact assembly that Chapter 17 welds, cleans, and inspects, and the same fit-up-and-tack discipline is the one you repeat, in compressed form, on the Chapter 20 capstone.

This chapter uses cutting, deburring, and cleaning skills from Chapters 4 through 6, and machine setup and parameter recording skills from Chapters 7 through 15. It does not introduce a new welding process; it introduces the fit-up-and-tack discipline that every later fabrication chapter depends on.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-091 — Lay out steel parts from an approved shop drawing and a single reference edge.
  • WF-092 — Square an assembly using overall dimensions and diagonal measurements.
  • WF-093 — Clamp components without lifting them from the layout table or blocking weld access.
  • WF-094 — Tack weld corners and joints in a balanced sequence that controls shrinkage.
  • WF-095 — Control distortion through tack placement, release sequence, and approved correction methods.
  • WF-096 — Inspect fit-up, tacks, and dimensions before releasing the assembly for final welding.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 4 through 6 produced clean, accurately cut, deburred steel parts. Chapters 7 through 15 taught you to set up and run SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, and GTAW beads with recorded parameters. This chapter is where those parts and that machine setup come together for the first time as an assembly rather than a single coupon.

The dimensionally verified, tacked assembly you release at the end of this chapter is the exact assembly that Chapter 17 welds into a finished noncritical utility bracket or shop fixture. The same lay-out, square, clamp, tack, inspect, and release sequence scales directly into the Chapter 20 capstone shop stand or utility frame.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • verifying every part against the approved cut list before assembly begins
  • laying out parts from a single reference edge and assembly origin
  • squaring the assembly using overall dimensions and matched diagonals
  • clamping components without lifting them from the table or blocking weld access
  • placing a balanced sequence of sound tack welds that controls shrinkage and distortion
  • inspecting fit-up, tacks, and dimensions, and correcting failures before releasing the assembly

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • perform final welding on the assembly; final welding is Chapter 17's scope and does not begin until this chapter's pre-weld fit-up acceptance is signed
  • force a distorted tack-welded assembly into alignment by unsafe clamping, hammering, or cooling
  • substitute an unidentified part, a different joint configuration, or a different tack pattern for the one shown on the approved shop drawing
  • build a structural, lifting, pressure, vehicle, or life-safety assembly; this chapter's project remains a noncritical training or shop-fixture assembly

Any part, dimension, diagonal, tack, or clamp condition that fails the pre-weld inspection in this chapter must be corrected before the assembly is released for final welding.

Trade Note

Fit-up is not "close enough because it will get welded anyway." A joint that is out of square, out of gap tolerance, or held together with oversized or poorly fused tacks stays out of tolerance after final welding, and it distorts further as heat is added. Every minute spent checking diagonals before tacking is cheaper than every minute spent trying to correct a welded assembly afterward.

Setup Standard

Do not begin final welding on any joint in this assembly until the pre-weld fit-up inspection in this chapter is complete and the assembly is signed off. An assembly that is tacked but not yet inspected and released is not ready for Chapter 17.

Shop Tip

Measure both diagonals before you place a single tack, and measure them again after every tack sequence. A square assembly has equal diagonals within the drawing tolerance. Unequal diagonals are the earliest and clearest sign that a part has shifted, and they are far easier to correct before tacking than after.

Squaring by Diagonal Measurement

A rectangular assembly is square when its two diagonals, measured corner to corner, are equal within the drawing tolerance. Overall length and width alone do not confirm squareness; a parallelogram can have correct length and width measurements while its corners are out of square. Measure both diagonals before tacking, after the first tack sequence, and again after every subsequent tack sequence.

Measurement Cue — Diagonals Before and After Every Tack Sequence

Measure both diagonals before placing the first tack, immediately after the first balanced tack sequence, and after every subsequent tack sequence. Do not proceed to the next tack sequence, and do not release the assembly for final welding, until both diagonals are equal within the drawing tolerance.

Critical Safety Cue — Do Not Force a Distorted Assembly

Do not force a hot, tack-welded, or distorted assembly into alignment by unsafe clamping, hammering, or cooling. Uncontrolled cooling can create steam, cracking, or altered material properties. If a tacked assembly has moved out of tolerance, remove and replace the offending tack using the approved repair method rather than forcing the parts into position around it.

Field Safety Cue — Clamp Placement and Weld Access

Position clamps so they hold the joint firmly without lifting parts off the layout table and without blocking the electrode, gun, torch, or inspection access required for tacking. Confirm each clamp path does not create an unintended current path through bearings, hinges, or other components not part of the assembly.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Tack Parameters Match the Recorded Setup

Use the same machine, consumable, polarity, and starting parameter range recorded for this material and thickness in Chapters 7 through 13. Do not select a new parameter range for tacking without recording it and verifying it on matching scrap first.

E. Preparation — Drawing Review and Part Verification

  1. Review the approved shop drawing, dimensions, tolerances, material, joint type, process, tack plan, and final weld sequence. Confirm the drawing revision matches the parts on hand.
  2. Verify every part against the cut list. Check each part's identifier, quantity, material, and size against the approved cut list before continuing.
  3. Inspect part length, angle, squareness, bevel, burrs, cleanliness, and identification. Confirm each part meets the acceptance standard set in Chapters 4 through 6.
  4. Reject or correct parts outside tolerance before assembly. Return an out-of-tolerance part to the cutting or grinding process rather than fitting around the error.
  5. Clean the table, fixtures, squares, clamps, magnets, stops, and contact surfaces. Remove oil, scale, and debris that could affect layout accuracy or clamp contact.

F. Layout, Squaring, and Clamping

  1. Establish a primary reference edge and assembly origin. Mark the origin on the table and confirm it will remain fixed for the full fit-up sequence.
  2. Lay out the first two parts against stops or layout lines. Position each part from the reference edge, not from the previously placed part.
  3. Verify orientation, inside and outside faces, and joint location. Confirm each part is installed in the orientation shown on the drawing.
  4. Set the specified gap or contact condition. Use a gauge, spacer, or shim to hold the approved gap rather than estimating it by eye.
  5. Clamp the first joint without lifting parts from the table. Confirm the clamp holds full contact with the table or fixture surface.
  6. Add remaining parts in the specified order. Follow the drawing's assembly sequence rather than an order of convenience.
  7. Use a square to check corners. Check every corner the drawing identifies as a right angle or specified angle.
  8. Measure overall length and width. Record both measurements against the drawing tolerance.
  9. Measure both diagonals on rectangular assemblies. Record both diagonal measurements before any tack is placed.
  10. Adjust the assembly until dimensions and diagonals meet tolerance. Reposition parts, not clamps alone, to correct an out-of-tolerance reading.
  11. Verify flatness against the table. Use a straightedge across the assembly to confirm it lies flat within the drawing tolerance.
  12. Add temporary strongbacks, spacers, or stops only as approved. Do not introduce a fixturing method the drawing does not call for.
  13. Confirm clamps will not block torch, gun, electrode, or inspection access. Reposition any clamp that would require the tack to be placed blind or at an unsafe angle.

G. Tack-Weld Planning and Placement

  1. Plan tack locations to balance shrinkage. Identify a sequence that alternates sides and corners rather than tacking around the assembly in one direction.
  2. Attach the work clamp to clean sound metal on the assembly or fixture as approved. Confirm the clamp location matches the drawing or fixture instructions.
  3. Set and record the approved welding parameters. Use the machine, consumable, polarity, and setting recorded in the applicable process chapter for this material and thickness.
  4. Place the first tack at the specified location. Confirm the tack falls within the approved location and length range before proceeding.
  5. Allow brief cooling as required. Do not proceed to the next tack while the previous tack area remains too hot to inspect safely.
  6. Place opposing or balanced tacks in the planned order. Follow the recorded sequence rather than tacking the nearest untacked joint.
  7. Keep tack size and length within the approved range. Confirm each tack matches the size specified for this material thickness and joint.
  8. Do not place unnecessary oversized tacks. An oversized tack increases shrinkage and is harder to incorporate cleanly into the final weld.

H. Tack Inspection, Dimensional Recheck, and Release

  1. Inspect each tack for crack, porosity, poor fusion, crater, and contamination. Clean the tack before inspecting it if slag or spatter obscures the surface.
  2. Remove defective tacks using the approved method. Do not weld over a cracked, porous, or poorly fused tack.
  3. Recheck dimensions, diagonals, squareness, gap, and flatness after each tack sequence. Compare every reading against the drawing tolerance before continuing.
  4. Correct movement before adding more weld metal. Reposition and reclamp the affected part rather than tacking around an out-of-tolerance condition.
  5. Do not force a severely distorted tack-welded assembly into position. Remove and replace the tacks causing the distortion instead.
  6. Add required intermediate tacks. Complete the planned tack sequence once dimensions and diagonals are confirmed in tolerance.
  7. Clean tack welds if they will be incorporated into final welds. Remove slag and spatter from tacks that Chapter 17 will weld over.
  8. Blend tack ends where the procedure requires it. Taper the tack ends only when the approved procedure calls for it.
  9. Mark the planned final welding sequence on the drawing or traveler. Record the order Chapter 17 will follow to balance heat input.
  10. Release selected clamps gradually and observe movement. Watch for spring-back or shift as each clamp is released.
  11. Reclamp only through the approved correction method. Do not reclamp by forcing a part that has already moved out of tolerance.
  12. Complete the pre-weld fit-up inspection. Verify every item in Section D against the physical assembly.
  13. Record part dimensions, gaps, diagonals, tacks, distortion, corrections, and accepted fit-up. Complete the fit-up and tack record established at the start of this chapter.
  14. Do not begin final welding until the assembly is signed off. Hold the assembly for Chapter 17 only after the responsible person signs the pre-weld fit-up acceptance record.

Common Fit-Up Inspection

Inspect every assembly using the same checks regardless of joint type or part count.

  • Part check: confirm every part matches the approved cut list in identifier, material, size, and condition.
  • Reference check: confirm a single reference edge and assembly origin were used for every measurement.
  • Dimensional check: confirm overall length, width, and diagonals meet the drawing tolerance.
  • Flatness check: confirm the assembly lies flat against the table within tolerance.
  • Clamping check: confirm clamps hold full contact without lifting parts or blocking weld access.
  • Tack check: confirm every tack is sound, correctly sized, and placed in the planned sequence.
  • Release check: confirm the pre-weld fit-up inspection record is complete and signed before final welding begins.

Complete the Pre-Weld Fit-Up Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 17.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Diagonals Are Unequal After the First Tack Sequence

Probable cause: one part shifted during clamping or the first tack pulled the joint out of position before opposing tacks were placed.
Confirmation: measure both diagonals again and compare each corner against the square used during layout.
Correction: remove the tack causing the shift, reposition the part from the reference edge, reclamp, and recheck before retacking.
Reinspection: repeat the diagonal and squareness check before placing the next tack.

A Tack Weld Shows Cracking or Poor Fusion

Probable cause: insufficient heat input, contamination at the joint, or an oversized tack that cooled too quickly.
Confirmation: clean the tack and inspect it under adequate lighting; compare its appearance with the accepted tack samples from earlier chapters.
Correction: remove the defective tack using the approved method and replace it using the recorded parameters.
Reinspection: inspect the replacement tack and recheck dimensions and diagonals.

The Assembly Rocks or Will Not Lie Flat on the Table

Probable cause: one or more parts were bowed or twisted before assembly, or a clamp lifted a part slightly off the table during tacking.
Confirmation: check each part individually against the table with a straightedge before checking the full assembly.
Correction: remove the tacks holding the affected part, correct the part using an approved cold-straightening method or replace it, and reclamp flat against the table.
Reinspection: repeat the flatness check for the full assembly.

A Clamp Blocks Access for the Next Tack

Probable cause: clamps were positioned for convenience during initial layout without checking access for every planned tack location.
Confirmation: compare the planned tack sequence against the current clamp positions before energizing the machine.
Correction: reposition or add a clamp so the joint remains held while the electrode, gun, or torch can reach the planned tack location safely.
Reinspection: repeat the clamping check before resuming tacking.

Overall Length and Width Are In Tolerance but the Assembly Is Visibly Out of Square

Probable cause: only the overall length and width were checked, and the diagonals were not measured, allowing a parallelogram shape to pass an incomplete check.
Confirmation: measure both diagonals and compare them against each other and the drawing tolerance.
Correction: loosen the affected clamps, reposition the assembly from the reference edge until both diagonals match, and reclamp.
Reinspection: repeat the full dimensional and diagonal check before tacking continues.

The Assembly Is Tacked but the Fit-Up Record Is Not Signed

Probable cause: tacking was completed, but the responsible person was not asked to review the dimensions, diagonals, and tack inspection before the learner moved toward final welding.
Confirmation: check the pre-weld fit-up inspection record for a completed signature and date.
Correction: locate the responsible person, review the completed record with them, and obtain the signature before Chapter 17 begins.
Reinspection: confirm the signed record is stored with the traveler before final welding is authorized.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a released, tacked assembly, not a finished weldment. Housekeeping here protects the fit-up record and the assembly for Chapter 17.

  • Mark the assembly hot until it has cooled enough for safe handling and storage
  • Store the tacked assembly flat and supported so it cannot distort before final welding
  • Return unused strongbacks, spacers, and fixtures to their storage location
  • Clean the layout table, squares, and clamps for the next fit-up
  • Store the signed pre-weld fit-up inspection record with the traveler and shop drawing where it can be referenced in Chapter 17

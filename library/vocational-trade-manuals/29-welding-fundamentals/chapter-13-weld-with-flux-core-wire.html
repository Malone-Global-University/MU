Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to configure an approved flux-cored arc welding (FCAW) setup — either self-shielded or gas-shielded, as specified by the approved wire and procedure — and to produce a documented series of flat-position practice beads on identified mild-steel coupons with correct wire installation, polarity, parameters, slag removal, and inspected bead quality.

You will identify the wire type required by the approved procedure, install and feed the correct flux-cored electrode, verify polarity, connect and leak-check shielding gas where the wire requires it, set voltage and wire-feed speed from the approved chart, run a short test bead on scrap, then produce and inspect a series of flat-position stringer beads while managing the heavier slag coating characteristic of flux-cored welding.

This chapter builds directly on the wire-feed setup skills from Chapter 10 and the bead-running skills from Chapter 11, applied to a process that shares equipment with GMAW but is never treated as an interchangeable technique.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-073 — Set up FCAW equipment, including wire installation, feed system, and gas connection where required.
  • WF-074 — Select flux-core wire by classification, diameter, and polarity requirement matched to the approved procedure.
  • WF-075 — Adjust machine voltage and wire-feed speed settings within the approved starting range.
  • WF-076 — Run flat-position flux-core beads with controlled travel, angle, and contact-tip-to-work distance.
  • WF-077 — Remove slag completely from flux-core welds using approved chipping and brushing methods.
  • WF-078 — Inspect flux-core bead consistency, including width, tie-in, porosity, and slag-inclusion indicators.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 13 depends on the welding-area authorization from Chapter 1, the PPE selection from Chapter 2, the equipment-identification and readiness record from Chapter 3, and the prepared coupons from Chapters 4 through 6. It applies the wire-feeder setup pattern taught in Chapter 10 and the bead-control technique taught in Chapter 11 to flux-cored wire, which carries different polarity, slag, and fume characteristics than solid GMAW wire.

The parameter-verification, slag-management, and defect-analysis habits built in this chapter carry forward into Chapter 16 through Chapter 20, where any process — SMAW, GMAW, or FCAW — may be selected for fit-up, fabrication, and the capstone project.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • identifying whether the approved procedure calls for self-shielded or gas-shielded flux-cored wire
  • installing the approved flux-cored electrode with matched drive rolls, polarity, and (where required) shielding gas
  • setting voltage and wire-feed speed from the approved starting chart and verifying with a test bead
  • running flat-position stringer beads with controlled contact-tip-to-work distance, travel, and angle
  • removing slag completely and inspecting bead consistency, including porosity and slag-inclusion indicators

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • select machine parameters, wire classification, diameter, or gas mixture beyond what the approved wire manufacturer, welding procedure specification, or instructor supplies
  • weld out-of-position, on coated, painted, galvanized, or unidentified metal, or on any joint configuration not covered by this chapter's flat-position bead exercise
  • treat FCAW technique as interchangeable with SMAW, GMAW, or GTAW technique from earlier or later chapters
  • proceed without ventilation appropriate to the specific wire, since flux-cored processes can generate significant fume volume

Any test bead, parameter setting, or bead that fails the inspection criteria in this chapter must be corrected before proceeding to production beads or advancing to Chapter 14.

Trade Note

Flux-cored wire deposits a heavier, more brittle slag layer than solid GMAW wire. Complete slag removal between beads, and between any weld passes required by a later chapter, is not optional housekeeping — undetected slag trapped beneath a subsequent pass is a rejectable defect that visual inspection alone may not catch after coating.

Setup Standard

Do not strike an arc until the welding-area readiness record from Chapter 1 is current for this session, PPE from Chapter 2 is staged and inspected, the machine and gun from Chapter 3 have passed equipment inspection, and this chapter's wire, polarity, and gas (where required) have been verified against the approved procedure.

Shop Tip

Run the required test bead on matching scrap before touching the practice coupon. A voltage or wire-speed setting that looks correct on the chart can still produce excessive spatter, poor tie-in, or unstable arc on the actual machine and wire in front of you — the test bead is what confirms the setting, not the chart alone.

Common Field Fundamentals

Flux-cored welding shares its power source and wire-feed hardware with GMAW, but the process is not interchangeable with it. The flux core changes polarity requirements, fume volume, slag behavior, and — for self-shielded wire — eliminates the external gas system entirely.

Self-Shielded Versus Gas-Shielded Wire

Confirm which type of flux-cored wire the approved procedure specifies before beginning any setup step. Self-shielded wire requires no external gas system; gas-shielded wire requires a leak-checked gas system in addition to the flux core. Installing the wrong nozzle configuration, drive rolls, or polarity for the selected wire produces an unstable arc and unacceptable bead quality regardless of how carefully the voltage and wire-feed speed are set.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Wire and Polarity Match

Verify polarity against the wire manufacturer's information before energizing the station. Flux-cored wire polarity is not always the same as solid GMAW wire polarity, and using the wrong polarity for the selected flux-cored wire produces poor arc characteristics and unacceptable weld quality even when every other setting matches the chart.

Critical Safety Cue — Fume Volume and Ventilation

Flux-cored welding, particularly self-shielded wire, can generate significantly more visible fume than solid-wire GMAW. Confirm local exhaust ventilation is positioned to capture fumes at the source before striking any test or practice arc. Do not rely on general shop ventilation alone for extended flux-core welding sessions. Stop and reassess ventilation if fume is not being effectively captured.

Safety Cue — Slag Chipping

Wear safety glasses with side protection and a face shield while chipping slag. Direct the chipping motion away from your body and other workers. Allow the bead to cool sufficiently before chipping unless the approved procedure specifically permits hot chipping.

Measurement Cue — Contact-Tip-to-Work Distance

Confirm and maintain the contact-tip-to-work distance specified by the approved procedure for the wire in use. Flux-cored wire commonly runs a longer stickout than solid GMAW wire; do not default to the GMAW distance from Chapter 11 without confirming the value for this wire.

E. Preparation — Wire and Procedure Review

  1. Identify whether the approved process uses self-shielded or gas-shielded flux-cored wire. Confirm the determination against the welding procedure specification or instructor direction before continuing.
  2. Review wire classification, diameter, polarity, gas where applicable, contact tip, drive roll, material, and parameter range. Record each value on the FCAW Setup Record.
  3. Confirm ventilation and respiratory requirements for the selected wire and material. Reassess local exhaust positioning for the specific fume volume expected from this wire.
  4. Complete hot-work, PPE, fire, and area controls. Confirm the Chapter 1 readiness record remains current for this session.

F. Feeder and Wire Installation

  1. Turn off and isolate the machine before feeder setup. Confirm the machine is de-energized before opening the wire compartment.
  2. Inspect spool, wire, drive rolls, guides, liner, gun, contact tip, and nozzle. Confirm each component matches the selected wire type and diameter.
  3. Install the approved wire in the correct feed direction. Verify the spool label against the recorded classification and diameter before installing.
  4. Use drive rolls appropriate to the wire without crushing or deforming it. Confirm the groove profile matches the wire type.
  5. Set spool brake and drive tension to approved starting conditions. Record the starting tension setting.
  6. Verify polarity from the wire manufacturer information. Confirm output-terminal connections match the required polarity before energizing.

G. Shielding Gas Setup (Gas-Shielded Wire) or Configuration (Self-Shielded Wire)

  1. For gas-shielded FCAW, secure and connect the approved gas system. Confirm the cylinder is upright, secured, and labeled for the correct gas.
  2. Check gas connections for leakage. Use the approved leak-check method at every fitting.
  3. Set gas flow only to the approved starting range. Record the flow rate on the FCAW Setup Record.
  4. For self-shielded FCAW, remove or configure gas components as directed by the machine and wire instructions. Confirm the nozzle configuration matches the self-shielded setup.

H. Parameter Setting and Test Bead

  1. Set voltage and wire-feed speed from the approved chart. Record both settings on the FCAW Setup Record.
  2. Trim wire to the specified extension. Confirm the trimmed length matches the procedure before striking an arc.
  3. Attach the work clamp to clean metal. Confirm clamp contact is sound and close to the test location.
  4. Prepare and mark the flat coupon. Mark bead lines and endpoints per the procedure.
  5. Perform a wire-feed and gas check. Confirm smooth feed and, for gas-shielded wire, correct flow before striking an arc.
  6. Run a short test bead on matching scrap. Complete the test bead under instructor authorization before proceeding.
  7. Inspect arc stability, slag, spatter, bead shape, and shielding. Compare the test bead against the acceptance standards in Section D.
  8. Adjust only within the approved parameter range. Record any adjustment and the reason for it.

I. Running Flat-Position Practice Beads

  1. Position the coupon flat and assume a stable stance. Confirm clear travel access along the full marked bead line.
  2. Use the approved drag or travel technique for the wire. Confirm the technique matches the wire manufacturer's guidance rather than defaulting to GMAW technique.
  3. Maintain contact-tip-to-work distance. Hold the distance recorded in Section H throughout the bead.
  4. Maintain work and travel angles. Confirm both angles against the approved procedure.
  5. Travel steadily while observing the puddle and slag. Watch the slag trailing behind the puddle as an indicator of stable travel speed.
  6. Terminate the bead using approved crater control. Avoid an abrupt arc break that leaves an uncontrolled crater.

J. Slag Removal and Inspection

  1. Allow the bead to cool before slag removal. Confirm the bead has cooled sufficiently for safe handling unless the procedure permits hot chipping.
  2. Wear eye and face protection while chipping slag. Confirm safety glasses and face shield are in place before chipping begins.
  3. Remove slag completely and brush the bead. Inspect the full bead length for any remaining slag before brushing is considered complete.
  4. Inspect bead width, reinforcement, tie-in, undercut, overlap, porosity, slag inclusion indicators, worm tracks, spatter, and crater. Compare findings against the acceptance standards in Section D.
  5. Compare multiple beads for consistency. Identify any bead-to-bead variation and its likely cause.
  6. Identify whether defects relate to voltage, wire speed, stickout, travel, angle, contamination, gas, or wire condition. Record the probable cause for any rejected bead.
  7. Make approved corrections one variable at a time. Do not change more than one parameter or technique element between beads.
  8. Record wire, polarity, gas, settings, technique, defects, and accepted result. Complete the parameter log for every bead produced.

K. Shutdown

  1. Close gas where used, shut down equipment, store wire, and complete fire watch. Confirm the cylinder valve is closed, the machine is de-energized, and the required post-work monitoring period is observed before leaving the area.

Common Area Inspection

Inspect the FCAW station and finished beads using the same checks regardless of whether the wire is self-shielded or gas-shielded.

  • Wire and polarity check: confirm classification, diameter, and polarity match the approved procedure before any arc is struck.
  • Gas check (if applicable): confirm leak-checked connections and flow within the approved starting range.
  • Test-bead check: confirm a stable, approved test bead precedes every production bead.
  • Technique check: confirm contact-tip-to-work distance, angle, and travel speed match the approved procedure throughout each bead.
  • Slag-removal check: confirm complete slag removal and full brushing before final inspection.
  • Bead-quality check: confirm width, tie-in, porosity, and slag-inclusion indicators meet the acceptance standards.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Arc Is Unstable Even Though Voltage and Wire Speed Match the Chart

Probable cause: polarity does not match the requirement for the selected flux-cored wire, or the wire type installed does not match the approved procedure.
Confirmation: compare the machine's output-terminal connections and the installed wire spool label against the wire manufacturer's polarity requirement.
Correction: de-energize the machine, correct the polarity connection or install the correct wire, and repeat the test-bead check.
Reinspection: re-run the wire-and-polarity check and produce a new test bead before continuing.

Slag Remains Trapped Along the Bead Edges After Brushing

Probable cause: travel speed was too fast for the puddle to release slag cleanly, or the work or travel angle deviated from the approved procedure.
Confirmation: inspect the bead edges under adequate lighting and compare travel speed and angle notes from the parameter log against the approved procedure.
Correction: chip and rebrush the affected area completely, then correct travel speed or angle on the next bead.
Reinspection: repeat the slag-removal check on the corrected bead.

Worm Tracks Appear on the Bead Surface

Probable cause: subsurface porosity, often from contaminated wire, incorrect gas flow (gas-shielded wire), wind disturbance, or a damaged liner.
Confirmation: inspect the wire spool for contamination, verify gas flow if applicable, and check for drafts at the welding station.
Correction: replace contaminated wire, correct gas flow, or shield the work area from wind before rewelding.
Reinspection: produce a new test bead and repeat the porosity and worm-track check.

Excessive Spatter Accumulates Around the Bead

Probable cause: voltage or wire-feed speed is outside the approved range for the wire diameter, or contact-tip-to-work distance is inconsistent.
Confirmation: compare recorded voltage and wire-feed speed against the approved chart and review contact-tip-to-work distance notes.
Correction: adjust one parameter at a time within the approved range and confirm improvement on a new test bead.
Reinspection: repeat the test-bead check before resuming production beads.

Visible Fume Is Not Being Captured at the Work Location

Probable cause: local exhaust was positioned for a lower-fume process and was not repositioned for the selected flux-cored wire.
Confirmation: observe fume movement relative to the exhaust intake during a short test bead.
Correction: reposition local exhaust or add respiratory protection per the ventilation assessment before continuing.
Reinspection: repeat the ventilation check before resuming production beads.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, inspected set of practice beads, not a finished assembly. Housekeeping here protects the parameter log and the FCAW station for the next session.

  • Close the shielding-gas cylinder valve where used and confirm the cylinder remains upright and secured
  • De-energize and isolate the welding machine
  • Store the flux-cored wire spool in a dry, identified location
  • Remove slag debris and abrasive residue from the work surface
  • Mark and separate any remaining hot coupons in the designated cooling zone
  • Store the completed parameter log with your other project records
  • Complete the required fire-watch and post-work area inspection before leaving

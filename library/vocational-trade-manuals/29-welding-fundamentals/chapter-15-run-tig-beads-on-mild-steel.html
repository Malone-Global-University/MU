Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to produce controlled GTAW practice beads on flat mild-steel coupons using the station you assembled and inspected in Chapter 14. You will run an autogenous bead — a bead made without filler metal — before progressing to filler-added beads, coordinating torch angle, arc length, filler timing, and hand movement while managing heat input as the coupon warms.

You will verify the station's gas, polarity, tungsten condition, and filler readiness, mark bead lines and endpoints on prepared coupons, establish and maintain a stable puddle, add filler at a consistent rhythm without contaminating the tungsten, terminate each bead with controlled crater fill and adequate post-flow, and inspect the finished beads for width, consistency, oxidation, undercut, crater condition, and contamination before recording results.

This chapter builds directly on Chapter 14's station setup and depends on the material preparation completed in Chapters 4 through 6. It prepares you for Chapter 16, where the fit-up and tacking skills used across all book processes are applied to a small steel assembly.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-085 — Control torch angle throughout arc initiation, puddle formation, and travel.
  • WF-086 — Establish a stable GTAW weld puddle on flat mild steel.
  • WF-087 — Add filler rod to the puddle using approved hand position and timing.
  • WF-088 — Coordinate torch movement, filler addition, and current control.
  • WF-089 — Control heat input across a sequence of practice beads.
  • WF-090 — Inspect TIG bead appearance for profile, oxidation, and defects.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 14 assembled and leak-checked the GTAW station without striking a sustained arc. This chapter is where that station is put to work: every setup item verified in Chapter 14 — tungsten preparation, gas flow, polarity, filler cleanliness, and work-clamp connection — is confirmed again here before beads begin.

The puddle-control, filler-coordination, and heat-management skills demonstrated here are the GTAW-specific counterpart to the SMAW bead skills built in Chapter 8 and the GMAW bead skills built in Chapter 11. Chapter 16 does not require GTAW specifically, but the fit-up and tack-welding judgment used there depends on the arc-control habits built across all three processes.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • verifying GTAW setup readiness before striking a sustained arc
  • producing autogenous beads without filler metal on flat mild-steel coupons
  • producing filler-added beads with coordinated torch, filler, and current control
  • managing tungsten contamination, gas coverage, and heat buildup across a bead sequence
  • visually inspecting finished beads for profile, oxidation, undercut, porosity, and crater condition

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • weld a joint, fillet, or assembly; joint welding with GTAW is outside this chapter's practice-bead scope
  • weld stainless steel, aluminum, or any material other than the identified mild-steel coupons prepared for this chapter
  • use any tungsten type, filler classification, gas, polarity, or amperage range other than the one specified by the approved GTAW procedure verified in Chapter 14
  • continue welding with a contaminated, cracked, or damaged tungsten; regrind or replace it before the next bead

Any bead produced with an unstable puddle, contaminated tungsten, inadequate gas coverage, or unmanaged heat buildup must be identified, analyzed, and repeated rather than accepted.

Trade Note

GTAW rewards a still, supported hand more than any other process taught in this book. Forearm support, a stable seated or standing position, and unhurried torch movement matter more here than in SMAW or GMAW, where a moving puddle and slag cover more technique inconsistency. Establish body position before striking the first arc, not after the first bead goes poorly.

Setup Standard

Do not strike a sustained arc until the Chapter 14 GTAW setup checklist has been reverified for this session — gas flow, polarity, tungsten condition, filler cleanliness, and work-clamp connection — and the coupon and filler rod are prepared to clean bright metal.

Shop Tip

Run the autogenous bead exercise completely, including inspection, before adding filler rod to any bead. A learner who struggles to hold a stable arc length and travel speed without filler will struggle more, not less, once a second hand is added to the task.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before welding begins, and do not change them without re-verifying the affected setup item.

TermDefinition
Autogenous beadA GTAW bead produced by melting the base metal alone, without filler rod added to the puddle
Work angleThe torch angle measured across the joint or bead line, perpendicular to the direction of travel
Travel angleThe torch angle measured along the direction of travel, tilted from vertical in the direction the arc is heading or trailing
PuddleThe localized pool of molten base metal, and filler where added, maintained ahead of and beneath the arc
Post-flowThe period of continued shielding-gas coverage after the arc is extinguished, protecting the cooling weld and tungsten
Heat-affected zoneThe area of base metal adjacent to the bead whose properties are altered by welding heat without being melted

Common Field Fundamentals

Every GTAW bead in this chapter follows the same sequence: verify setup, establish position, strike and stabilize the puddle, travel with or without filler, terminate with crater control and post-flow, cool, inspect, and record. Learning that sequence on autogenous beads first makes the filler-added beads that follow easier to control.

Coupon zoneClean bright-metal flat coupon secured on the welding table

Puddle Control and Filler Coordination

A stable GTAW puddle is established before travel begins and maintained through consistent arc length and current, not through torch speed. Filler rod is added to the leading edge of the puddle in a consistent rhythm, then withdrawn slightly without leaving the shielding-gas envelope, so it is never removed from gas protection between additions.

Measurement Cue — Arc Length and Tungsten Extension

Maintain the short, consistent arc length specified by the approved procedure for the entire bead. Confirm tungsten extension from the cup matches the setting established in Chapter 14 before striking the arc, and do not change extension mid-bead to compensate for a wandering arc length.

Critical Safety Cue — Tungsten Contamination

Stop welding immediately if the tungsten contacts the filler rod, the puddle, or the coupon. A contaminated tungsten produces an unstable arc and unreliable shielding. Regrind or replace the tungsten using the approved dedicated grinding equipment from Chapter 14 before continuing.

Field Safety Cue — Post-Flow and Hot Tungsten

Maintain post-flow coverage over the weld and tungsten after the arc is extinguished for the duration specified by the approved procedure. Do not move the torch away from the cooling puddle before post-flow ends, and do not touch the cup or tungsten until it has had time to cool.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Amperage and Slope Controls

Set current type, start method, amperage limit, slope, and any pulse control only to the values specified by the approved GTAW procedure and machine manual referenced in Chapter 14. Do not adjust these controls mid-session without instructor direction and a recorded reason.

E. Preparation — Review and Readiness Verification

  1. Review the approved coupon layout, tungsten, filler, gas, polarity, amperage limit, and technique. Confirm these match the Chapter 14 setup record before proceeding.
  2. Verify work-area, PPE, ventilation, cylinder, machine, torch, tungsten, and filler readiness. Reinspect each item rather than assuming it remains unchanged since Chapter 14.
  3. Prepare the coupon and filler rod to clean bright metal. Remove any oxidation that has formed since Chapters 4 through 6.

F. Layout and Station Verification

  1. Mark bead lines and endpoints. Use soapstone or marker referenced to the coupon edge established in Chapter 4.
  2. Secure the coupon flat and attach the work clamp. Confirm the clamp contacts clean sound metal close to the practice area.
  3. Verify gas flow, pre-flow, post-flow, and torch cooling. Confirm each function operates as set in Chapter 14 before striking an arc.

G. Body Position and Torch Control

  1. Assume a stable seated or standing position with forearm support where appropriate. Confirm the position allows uninterrupted travel along the full marked bead line.
  2. Arrange torch lead, pedal cable, and gas hose to prevent drag. Confirm no cable pulls the torch off the intended path during travel.
  3. Hold the torch at the approved work and travel angle. Confirm the angle matches the approved procedure before striking the arc.
  4. Position the tungsten close to the start without touching the work. Confirm tungsten extension before initiating the arc.

H. Autogenous Bead — Arc Initiation and Travel

  1. Lower the helmet and initiate the arc using the approved start method. Confirm the arc establishes cleanly before proceeding.
  2. Increase current gradually under controlled input where applicable. Do not jump directly to full welding current if the machine and procedure provide a ramp-up control.
  3. Establish a small stable puddle. Hold the torch in place until the puddle forms before beginning travel.
  4. Maintain a short consistent arc length. Do not allow arc length to drift as travel begins.
  5. Travel along the marked line without filler for the first exercise. Keep travel speed steady rather than accelerating or slowing to correct puddle size.
  6. Control puddle size through current and travel. Adjust only current or travel speed, not both simultaneously, to correct a puddle that is too large or too small.
  7. Terminate current gradually using the approved method. Use the machine's slope-out or foot-pedal control rather than an abrupt current cutoff.
  8. Maintain shielding gas over the cooling puddle during post-flow. Hold the torch in position for the full post-flow duration.
  9. Do not move the torch away prematurely. Confirm post-flow has ended before repositioning for the next bead.
  10. Inspect the tungsten for contamination. Check the tip for discoloration, balling, or pickup before the next arc strike.
  11. Regrind contaminated tungsten before continuing. Use the dedicated grinding equipment and procedure from Chapter 14.
  12. Inspect the autogenous bead for width, consistency, oxidation, undercut, crater, and heat-affected zone. Record findings before proceeding to filler practice.

I. Filler-Added Bead — Coordination, Heat Control, and Inspection

  1. For filler practice, hold the filler rod in the approved hand position. Confirm the rod angle and grip allow controlled, repeated additions.
  2. Keep the filler end within the shielding envelope without touching the tungsten. Maintain a consistent distance between the filler tip and the arc.
  3. Establish the puddle. Confirm puddle formation before the first filler addition.
  4. Add filler to the leading edge using a consistent rhythm. Dip the filler at the front of the puddle rather than into the arc itself.
  5. Withdraw the filler slightly without removing it from gas protection. Keep the rod tip inside the shielding-gas envelope between additions.
  6. Coordinate torch movement, filler addition, and current control. Advance the torch only as fast as filler additions and puddle control allow.
  7. Maintain bead width and puddle size. Adjust filler frequency and travel speed together to hold a consistent bead profile.
  8. Control heat buildup as the coupon warms. Watch for an enlarging puddle or increasing travel difficulty as later beads are run on the same coupon.
  9. Use approved pauses, sequence, or coupon rotation rather than excessive speed changes. Allow the coupon to cool between beads instead of speeding up travel to compensate for heat buildup.
  10. Fill the final crater. Add filler through the termination sequence rather than stopping filler addition abruptly.
  11. Maintain post-flow until the tungsten and weld are protected. Hold torch position for the full post-flow duration before moving away.
  12. Allow the coupon to cool in the designated location. Mark the coupon hot and place it in the hot-metal cooling zone.
  13. Clean the bead only with approved uncontaminated tools. Use the dedicated brush identified for mild steel.
  14. Inspect bead appearance, width, reinforcement, ripple spacing, toe fusion, undercut, porosity, oxidation, crater, and contamination. Compare each bead against the Acceptance Standards table.
  15. Compare heat input and color among beads. Identify which beads show excessive discoloration consistent with overheating.
  16. Identify defects caused by arc length, angle, filler placement, gas coverage, travel, current, or tungsten contamination. Record the probable cause for each defect found.
  17. Repeat with one controlled correction at a time. Change only one variable before running the next comparison bead.
  18. Record settings, technique, filler, gas, tungsten condition, defects, corrections, and accepted result. Complete the bead practice log for the full session.
  19. Close gas, isolate the machine, protect the torch, and complete fire watch. Shut down the station according to the Chapter 1 fire-watch requirement before leaving the area.

Common Bead Inspection

Inspect every bead using the same checks regardless of whether it was produced with or without filler.

  • Profile check: confirm width and reinforcement remain consistent along the bead length.
  • Oxidation check: confirm surface coloration is consistent with adequate shielding-gas coverage for the approved procedure.
  • Toe check: confirm no undercut exceeds the approved gauge criteria.
  • Porosity check: confirm no visible surface porosity is present.
  • Crater check: confirm the crater is filled with no crater crack.
  • Tungsten check: confirm the tungsten remains free of contamination, splits, or excessive erosion after each bead.

Complete the Bead Practice Inspection Log in the Inspection Checklist section below before proceeding to Chapter 16.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

The Puddle Never Stabilizes Before Travel Begins

Probable cause: travel started before a puddle had actually formed, or arc length drifted during the initial hold.
Confirmation: repeat the arc initiation with the torch held stationary and observe puddle formation before moving.
Correction: hold the torch stationary longer at the start point and maintain a shorter, more consistent arc length.
Reinspection: repeat the autogenous bead exercise and confirm puddle stability before proceeding to filler practice.

Filler Rod Contacts the Tungsten

Probable cause: the filler rod was advanced too far into the arc rather than dipped at the leading edge of the puddle.
Confirmation: inspect the tungsten tip for discoloration, pickup, or a split immediately after the incident.
Correction: stop welding, regrind or replace the tungsten using the approved dedicated equipment, and adjust filler-rod approach angle and distance before restarting.
Reinspection: confirm tungsten condition before striking the next arc and inspect the resulting bead for arc instability.

Bead Surface Shows Heavy Discoloration or Sooting

Probable cause: inadequate shielding-gas coverage from low flow, wind exposure, torch angle exposing the puddle, or contaminated gas supply.
Confirmation: verify gas flow rate against the approved setting and check for drafts, torch angle, and cup condition.
Correction: correct flow rate, block wind exposure, or adjust torch angle and cup size as approved, then run a comparison bead.
Reinspection: compare bead coloration against an accepted reference bead.

Later Beads on the Same Coupon Show Excessive Puddle Size and Distortion

Probable cause: heat input accumulated across the bead sequence without adequate cooling between beads.
Confirmation: compare bead color and coupon temperature across the sequence and note the point where puddle control became difficult.
Correction: allow additional cooling time between beads, use coupon rotation, or reduce current within the approved range.
Reinspection: repeat the affected bead after the coupon has cooled and confirm puddle size returns to the expected range.

Undercut Appears Along One or Both Toes

Probable cause: travel speed too fast for the current, arc length too long, or torch angle directing heat away from filler addition.
Confirmation: measure undercut with the approved gauge and compare travel speed, arc length, and angle notes for the affected bead.
Correction: reduce travel speed, shorten arc length, or correct torch angle, changing only one variable before the next bead.
Reinspection: measure undercut on the corrected bead against the approved gauge criteria.

Crater Cracks at the End of the Bead

Probable cause: current was cut abruptly instead of sloped out, or filler was withdrawn before the crater was filled.
Confirmation: inspect the crater under adequate lighting and compare the termination method used against the approved procedure.
Correction: use the slope-out or foot-pedal control to reduce current gradually while continuing filler addition through the crater.
Reinspection: inspect the corrected bead's crater for completeness and absence of cracking.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented set of practice beads, not a finished assembly. Housekeeping here protects the station and consumables for the fit-up work in Chapter 16.

  • Close the shielding-gas cylinder valve and relieve line pressure where required
  • Isolate the welding machine and coil torch, pedal, and work leads without sharp bends
  • Protect the torch, cup, and tungsten from impact during storage
  • Store remaining filler rod in its clean labeled holder
  • Mark or isolate any coupon that remains hot
  • Complete the fire-watch and post-work area inspection required by Chapter 1
  • Store the completed bead practice log with your other project records

Field Terms

Autogenous Bead
A GTAW bead produced by melting the base metal alone, without added filler rod.
Work Angle
The torch angle measured across the bead line, perpendicular to the direction of travel.
Travel Angle
The torch angle measured along the direction of travel.
Puddle
The localized pool of molten metal maintained ahead of and beneath the arc.
Post-Flow
Continued shielding-gas coverage after the arc is extinguished, protecting the cooling weld and tungsten.
Heat-Affected Zone
The area of base metal adjacent to the bead whose properties are altered by welding heat without being melted.
Crater
The depression left at the termination point of a weld bead.
Tungsten Contamination
Pickup of filler metal, base metal, or other foreign material on the tungsten tip, producing an unstable arc.

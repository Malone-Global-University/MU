Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to bring one completed noncritical welded project — such as an SMAW or GMAW coupon assembly, bracket, or shop fixture produced in earlier chapters — through its final cleaning, dimensional check, visual weld inspection, approved repair, surface preparation, and finish so that it can be documented, signed off, and handed off.

You will remove slag and spatter, decide which welds remain as-welded and which are blended or ground flush according to the drawing, inspect every required weld against the acceptance criteria used throughout this book, check overall dimensions and distortion against tolerance, repair only approved defects, prepare the surface, apply a finish only when the project specification includes one, and complete a signed weld inspection record.

This chapter assumes the welding itself is already complete. It uses the same welding area, PPE, and hot-work controls established in Chapters 1 through 3 whenever grinding, repair welding, or coating work is performed, and it draws on the defect-identification and corrective-action skills built in Chapter 18.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-109 — Remove slag and spatter from a completed welded project without damaging the base metal or required weld reinforcement.
  • WF-110 — Grind or blend weld surfaces only where the drawing or finish specification allows it, without reducing required weld size or base-metal thickness.
  • WF-111 — Inspect weld surfaces against the visual-acceptance criteria used throughout this book.
  • WF-112 — Check overall dimensions, distortion, and squareness against the drawing tolerance.
  • WF-113 — Prepare cleaned, inspected metal for a finish coating without concealing an uninspected weld.
  • WF-114 — Complete a documented weld inspection record and obtain evaluator signoff.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 7 through 17 produced SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, and GTAW welds and small fabricated assemblies. Chapter 18 taught you to identify and analyze weld defects. This chapter is where those two threads meet on a real finished project: you clean and inspect the actual welds you made, apply the same defect terminology and corrective-action pattern from Chapter 18 to any condition you find, and produce the documented, signed record that a finished project needs before it is used, displayed, or handed off.

The cleaning, inspection, repair, and documentation sequence in this chapter is also the sequence used at the end of the Chapter 20 capstone. Completing this chapter carefully prepares you to close out the capstone project without inventing a finishing or inspection step on the fly.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • removing slag, spatter, and contamination from a completed welded project
  • deciding which welds remain as-welded and which are blended or ground flush according to the drawing or finish specification
  • visually inspecting every required weld for presence, location, length, continuity, profile, tie-in, undercut, overlap, porosity, cracks, crater, arc strikes, spatter, and finish damage
  • measuring overall dimensions, diagonals, angles, hole centers, level, square, and flatness against drawing tolerance
  • repairing only approved defects with a documented method and reinspecting the repair
  • preparing the surface and applying a finish only when the chapter project includes one
  • completing a documented weld inspection record and obtaining evaluator signoff

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • grind away a crack, remove weld reinforcement, or reduce base-metal thickness to hide a defect rather than repairing it
  • invent a repair procedure for a structural, code-controlled, or life-safety weld; this book's projects remain noncritical training pieces
  • coat, paint, or otherwise conceal any weld that has not passed the visual inspection in this chapter
  • proof-load, pressure-test, or certify the finished project beyond the noncritical stability or function check the chapter project specifies

Any weld, dimension, or finish condition that fails the inspection in this chapter must be corrected, reinspected, and recorded before the evaluator signs the weld inspection record.

Trade Note

A weld is not finished because it has cooled. It is finished only after slag and spatter are removed, the weld has been measured and visually inspected against the acceptance criteria, any approved repair has been completed and reinspected, and the result has been recorded. Coating or handing off a project before that sequence is complete conceals defects rather than correcting them.

Setup Standard

Do not apply primer, paint, oil, or any other finish to a weld or the surrounding base metal until that weld has passed the visual inspection in Section H of the work order and has been marked accepted on the weld map. A weld marked repair or review must be corrected and reinspected before it is coated.

Shop Tip

Inspect every weld before deciding which surfaces to grind. Grinding first and inspecting afterward risks removing the exact evidence — undercut depth, porosity distribution, crater shape — that the inspection is supposed to record.

Slag, Spatter, and the Line Between Cleaning and Damage

Slag and spatter removal must expose the true weld surface without gouging the base metal or removing required weld reinforcement. A chipping hammer, wire brush, and controlled scraper or pliers are matched to this task; an aggressive grinder pass is not a substitute for proper slag removal and risks concealing the very surface the inspection depends on.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm the Project Is Cool Before Handling

Treat any area of the project that has not been specifically verified as cool as hot. Mark any area that remains hot and allow it to cool in the designated hot-metal location before chipping, brushing, grinding, or handling with bare hands.

Safety Cue — Eye and Face Protection During Slag and Spatter Removal

Wear safety glasses with side protection and a face shield while chipping slag or removing spatter with a scraper, chisel, or pliers. Direct chips and fragments away from other workers.

Code Alert — Do Not Remove Required Weld Reinforcement

Do not remove weld reinforcement unless the drawing specifically permits it. Removing reinforcement without authorization can reduce the weld below its required size even when the surface appears smooth and acceptable.

Measurement Cue — Grind Only Approved Surfaces, and Only So Far

Identify which welds the drawing or finish requirement designates as as-welded and which are designated for blending or flush grinding before starting any grinder. When blending or flush-grinding is approved, stop grinding immediately upon reaching the required weld size or base-metal thickness, and stop entirely for any crack, deep porosity, or lack of remaining weld exposed during grinding.

Field Safety Cue — Grinder Setup for Finishing Work

Inspect the grinder guard, wheel or flap disc, speed rating, and spark path before use, and secure the project before grinding. Position the spark stream away from other workers, cylinders, and combustible material, consistent with the grinding controls established in Chapter 6.

E. Preparation — Review and Safe Handling

  1. Review the drawing, weld symbols, dimensions, tolerances, finish requirement, use limitation, and inspection criteria. Confirm the project in hand matches the drawing identifier and revision.
  2. Confirm the project has cooled to a safe handling temperature. Check every welded area, not only the last weld completed.
  3. Mark any areas that remain hot. Isolate them from handling until they reach a safe temperature.

F. Slag, Spatter, and Approved Grinding

  1. Remove loose slag using approved eye and face protection. Use a chipping hammer or approved tool for SMAW and FCAW welds.
  2. Wire-brush welds with a tool compatible with the base metal. Use a dedicated bare-steel brush; do not cross-contaminate with a stainless-only brush.
  3. Remove spatter using a scraper, chisel, pliers, or grinder without gouging the base metal. Select the tool by spatter size and location, not by convenience.
  4. Do not remove weld reinforcement unless the drawing permits it. Confirm reinforcement is intact after spatter removal.
  5. Identify welds designated to remain as-welded. Mark these on the weld map before any grinder is used.
  6. Identify welds designated for blending or flush grinding. Mark these separately on the weld map.
  7. Inspect the grinder, guard, wheel, speed rating, and spark path. Confirm the wheel or flap disc is rated for the tool and task.
  8. Secure the project before grinding. Clamp or fixture the assembly to prevent movement during grinding.
  9. Blend only the approved areas. Do not extend grinding to as-welded surfaces.
  10. Maintain required weld size and base-metal thickness. Check size frequently during grinding rather than only at the end.
  11. Stop grinding for cracks, deep porosity, lack of remaining weld, or excessive heat. Treat any of these as a stop-work condition until evaluated.

G. Visual Weld Inspection

  1. Clean all weld toes and starts and stops for visual inspection. Confirm no residual slag or spatter obscures the toe line.
  2. Provide adequate lighting and access. Reposition the project or add lighting rather than inspecting a shadowed weld.
  3. Inspect every required weld for presence, location, length, continuity, profile, tie-in, undercut, overlap, porosity, cracks, crater, arc strikes, spatter, and finish damage. Work systematically through the weld map rather than inspecting welds at random.
  4. Use approved gauges to measure fillet size, length, gap, reinforcement, or undercut as applicable. Record each measurement against its acceptance value.
  5. Mark each weld as accepted, repair, or review. Use the same three-way classification for every weld on the map.
  6. Do not coat welds that remain uninspected. Complete inspection of the entire weld map before moving to surface preparation.

H. Dimensional Check and Distortion Review

  1. Measure overall length, width, height, diagonals, angles, hole centers, level, square, and flatness as applicable. Use the same reference points established when the project was fabricated.
  2. Record dimensional deviation. Compare each measurement against the drawing tolerance individually rather than judging the assembly by eye.
  3. Determine whether distortion is within the drawing tolerance. Mark the assembly for repair or review if any dimension falls outside tolerance.

I. Repair of Approved Defects

  1. Prepare a repair plan for approved defects. Identify the defect type, location, likely cause, and defined removal method before touching the weld.
  2. Remove defects using the approved method. Use grinding or gouging methods appropriate to the defect and consistent with the original process.
  3. Reclean the repair area. Return the area to bright base metal before rewelding.
  4. Reweld with recorded parameters. Use the same electrode or wire classification, polarity, and parameter range recorded on the project's original record unless the evaluator specifies a corrected setting.
  5. Reinspect repaired welds. Apply the identical visual and dimensional criteria used in Sections G and H.

J. Finishing Preparation and Handling Safety

  1. Remove sharp edges, burrs, and handling hazards. Deburr all edges the assembly presents to bare-hand handling.
  2. Clean oil, dust, slag, abrasive residue, and fingerprints from the project. Use an approved cleaner appropriate to the specified finish.
  3. Prepare the surface by approved abrasion, blasting, chemical cleaning, or conversion process. Match the method to the finish system specified for this project.
  4. Do not trap rust, soot, slag, moisture, or solvent under coating. Confirm the surface is fully dry and clean immediately before coating.
  5. Protect machined surfaces, threads, grounding points, labels, and uncoated areas. Mask or cover areas the finish specification excludes.

K. Finish Application (When Specified)

  1. Apply primer, paint, oil, or other finish only when the chapter project includes it. Confirm the finish requirement against the original chapter specification before opening any coating material.
  2. Observe coating ventilation, ignition, temperature, humidity, film, and cure requirements. Follow the coating manufacturer's instructions exactly.
  3. Inspect finish coverage without allowing coating to conceal cracks or unaccepted defects. Confirm every coated weld was marked accepted before this step.

L. Documentation and Handoff

  1. Attach the project label or identification. Confirm the label matches the project identifier on the weld inspection record.
  2. Complete the weld inspection record with drawing revision, material, process, consumable, parameters, weld map, dimensions, defects, repairs, finish, and result. Fill in every field; leave none blank or marked "N/A" without a stated reason.
  3. Obtain evaluator signoff. Do not release the project as complete without the evaluator's signature and date.
  4. Store or hand off the project within its documented use limitation. Confirm the receiving party is informed of the stated noncritical use and load limit.
  5. Clean the shop, isolate hot metal, shut down equipment, and complete fire watch. Close out the work area per the Chapter 1 standard if any hot work occurred during repair.

Common Area Inspection

Inspect the finished project using the same checks regardless of which chapter originally produced it.

  • Cleaning check: confirm slag and spatter are fully removed without gouging or reinforcement loss.
  • Grinding check: confirm only approved surfaces were blended or flush-ground, with required size and thickness maintained.
  • Weld check: confirm every required weld is marked accepted, repair, or review on the weld map, with no weld left unclassified.
  • Dimensional check: confirm overall dimensions, diagonals, angles, and flatness are recorded and compared against tolerance.
  • Repair check: confirm every repaired weld was reinspected against the original acceptance criteria.
  • Finish check: confirm no uninspected weld was coated, and that any applied finish followed the manufacturer's cure and ventilation requirements.
  • Documentation check: confirm the weld inspection record is complete and signed before handoff.

Complete the Weld Inspection Record in the Inspection Checklist section below before handing off the project.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Weld Surface Looks Rough After Grinding but Was Not Supposed to Be Ground

Probable cause: the weld map was not consulted before grinding, and an as-welded surface was blended by mistake.
Confirmation: compare the ground surface against the drawing's as-welded/blended designation for that location.
Correction: stop grinding immediately; if weld size or base-metal thickness has already been reduced below the required value, treat the location as a repair item and follow Section I.
Reinspection: reinspect the affected weld for size and profile before continuing to any other weld.

Undercut Is Found During Inspection After the Weld Was Already Ground

Probable cause: grinding was performed before the visual inspection was completed, removing the surface evidence needed to measure the undercut accurately.
Confirmation: measure the remaining undercut with the approved gauge and compare it against the acceptance limit.
Correction: if within limits, mark accepted; if outside limits, follow the approved repair method in Section I.
Reinspection: reinspect after any repair, and in future work, complete Section G before any grinding beyond slag and spatter removal.

The Assembly Is Out of Square or Diagonal Tolerance After Welding

Probable cause: heat input during final welding was not balanced, or the assembly was not restrained according to the original distortion-control plan.
Confirmation: remeasure both diagonals and compare each against the drawing tolerance.
Correction: follow the evaluator's approved correction method; do not force the assembly into tolerance by unsafe clamping, hammering, or cooling.
Reinspection: repeat the full dimensional check after correction.

A Crack Is Found During Inspection

Probable cause: rapid cooling, excessive restraint, or a fusion problem during the original weld.
Confirmation: mark the location for qualified evaluation; do not attempt to size or characterize the crack by grinding into it.
Correction: follow the evaluator's approved defect-removal and repair method only after qualified review confirms the extent of the crack.
Reinspection: reinspect the repaired area using the original acceptance criteria before marking it accepted.

A Finish Was Applied Before a Weld Was Fully Inspected

Probable cause: surface preparation and coating began before every weld on the map was classified accepted, repair, or review.
Confirmation: check the weld map against the coated area to identify which welds were coated without a final classification.
Correction: remove the finish from the affected area using an approved method, complete inspection of the exposed weld, repair if required, and reapply the finish only after acceptance.
Reinspection: confirm the weld map is fully classified before any further coating proceeds.

The Weld Inspection Record Is Complete but Not Signed

Probable cause: the inspection and documentation steps were completed, but the evaluator was not asked to review and sign the record before the project was set aside for handoff.
Confirmation: check the weld inspection record for a completed evaluator signature and date.
Correction: locate the evaluator, review the completed record with them, and obtain the signature before releasing the project.
Reinspection: confirm the signed record is stored with the project or its documentation file.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented, finished project, not a bare weld coupon. Housekeeping here protects both the project and the inspection record.

  • Remove slag, spatter, and abrasive debris from the work surface and floor
  • Inspect the area for hidden sparks or smoldering material if any grinding or repair welding occurred
  • Store cleaning solvents, abrasive materials, and finish products according to their labels
  • Return the grinder, gauges, and hand tools used for finishing to their staged locations
  • Store the signed weld inspection record with the project or its documentation file

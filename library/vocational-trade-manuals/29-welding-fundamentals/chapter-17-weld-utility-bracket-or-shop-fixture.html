Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to fabricate one complete, noncritical mild-steel utility bracket or shop fixture from a simple shop drawing. You will read the drawing, create a cut list, cut and prepare the parts, fit and tack the assembly, weld it using an approved SMAW or GMAW procedure, inspect the finished welds and dimensions, correct any approved defects, and prepare the fixture for its finish.

This is the first chapter in the book in which you carry a project completely from a drawing to a finished, inspected, documented, noncritical shop fixture using a single continuous process. Earlier chapters practiced individual skills — cutting, grinding, SMAW beads, GMAW beads, fit-up, and tacking — in isolation. This chapter integrates those skills into one small fabrication job.

The bracket or fixture you build here must remain within its stated noncritical, nonstructural use limitation. It is not a lifting device, a vehicle component, a pressure component, or a life-safety item. Chapter 20 repeats this same authorize-protect-identify-prepare-fit-weld-clean-inspect-correct-finish-close workflow at a larger scale for the book's capstone shop stand or utility frame.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-097 — Read a simple shop drawing and extract the material, dimensions, tolerances, joint symbols, weld length, finish, and use limitation.
  • WF-098 — Cut steel pieces to the cut list using measured layout, supported stock, and an approved cutting tool.
  • WF-099 — Prepare joints by deburring, cleaning, and removing coating, rust, oil, and scale from weld and clamp zones.
  • WF-100 — Tack weld the assembly in a balanced sequence and verify dimensions before final welding.
  • WF-101 — Complete final SMAW or GMAW welds using approved parameters, travel, and heat-input control.
  • WF-102 — Inspect bracket alignment, weld quality, and overall strength within the project's stated use limitation.

Where This Skill Fits

This chapter draws directly on Chapter 1's work-area setup, Chapter 2's PPE, Chapter 3's equipment identification, Chapters 4 through 6's steel preparation, Chapters 7 through 9's SMAW skills, Chapters 10 through 13's GMAW and FCAW skills, and Chapter 16's fit-up, clamping, and tack-welding project. This chapter, in turn, is the direct rehearsal for the Chapter 20 capstone. The drawing-to-cut-list-to-fit-up-to-weld-to-inspection-to-finish sequence you practice here at bracket scale is the same sequence, expanded, that produces the book's final shop stand or utility frame.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • reading a simple shop drawing and building a complete cut list from it
  • cutting, deburring, and cleaning every part to the drawing's dimensions and weld-zone requirements
  • fitting, clamping, squaring, and tacking the assembly, and verifying dimensions before final welding
  • completing final SMAW or GMAW welds using an approved procedure with controlled heat input and distortion
  • inspecting weld size, profile, and fusion indicators, measuring final dimensions, and completing an approved repair only where needed
  • preparing the finished fixture for its stated coating or bare-metal protection and recording its use limitation

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • redesign the approved shop drawing, invent a missing dimension, or select an unspecified fastener, joint, or bevel
  • build a bracket or fixture intended for lifting, vehicle, pressure, fall-protection, structural-building-load, or public-safety use
  • proof-load the finished fixture beyond the approved noncritical function check described in this chapter
  • coat or conceal any weld that has not passed the visual and dimensional inspection in this chapter

Any part, tack, weld, or dimension that fails the inspection standards in this chapter must be corrected before the fixture is labeled and handed off.

Trade Note

A shop bracket is not "done" when the last weld is struck. It is done only after slag and spatter are removed, every weld is visually inspected, every critical dimension is measured against the drawing, and the fixture is labeled with its use limitation. Skipping the inspection sequence at the end of this chapter, rather than any single welding mistake, is the most common reason a training bracket is later mistaken for a certified structural part.

Reading the Shop Drawing Before Any Cutting

Every dimension, angle, joint symbol, weld length, and finish requirement used in this chapter comes from the approved shop drawing. Do not estimate a missing dimension, invent a bevel angle, or select an unspecified fastener. If the drawing is missing information needed to complete a part or joint, stop and obtain the corrected or completed drawing from the responsible instructor before cutting.

Measurement Cue — Build the Cut List Before Cutting Stock

Extract every part identifier, material, length, angle, and quantity from the shop drawing into a written cut list before making the first cut. Verify each cut list entry against the drawing a second time. Do not cut a part from memory or from a partial reading of the drawing.

Critical Safety Cue — Confirm Nonstructural Use Before Fabrication

Confirm the shop drawing states a nonstructural use and a defined load limit before cutting any stock. Do not fabricate a bracket or fixture intended for lifting, suspending loads, restraining a vehicle, containing pressure, carrying structural building loads, or serving as a certified safety device as a project in this chapter.

Field Safety Cue — Fit-Up Access for Torch, Gun, or Electrode

Confirm every clamp, stop, or fixture used during fit-up leaves clear access for the electrode, gun, or torch and for chipping and inspection tools at every joint. Reposition a clamp that blocks weld access rather than working around it.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Consumable and Parameter Match

Confirm the electrode classification and diameter, or the wire classification, diameter, and shielding gas, match both the approved shop drawing's process and the manufacturer's starting parameter range for the material thickness. Do not substitute a different consumable classification without an approved procedure change.

E. Drawing Review and Cut List

  1. Review the shop drawing, title block, revision, material, dimensions, tolerances, joint symbols, weld length, finish, and use limitation. Confirm the drawing is the current approved revision before proceeding.
  2. Confirm the project is nonstructural and not intended for lifting, vehicle, pressure, fall protection, or life-safety use. Stop and contact the responsible instructor if the drawing does not state a clear use limitation.
  3. Create a cut list with part identifiers, material, size, length, angle, and quantity. Write every entry from the drawing onto the part traveler.
  4. Verify stock identity, thickness, straightness, and condition. Confirm the available stock matches the cut list's material specification before cutting.

F. Cutting and Joint Preparation

  1. Mark parts from a common reference. Use a single reference edge for every layout mark on a given piece of stock.
  2. Cut each part using the approved tool and stock support. Apply the cutting-tool inspection, mounting, and body-position practices from Chapter 5.
  3. Deburr and inspect each cut. Confirm length, angle, and squareness against the cut list before moving to the next part.
  4. Prepare weld edges and remove coating, rust, oil, and scale from weld zones. Extend cleaning beyond the expected weld and work-clamp area.
  5. Mark each part identifier. Use a traceable marking method that will survive fit-up and welding.

G. Fit-Up, Squaring, and Tacking

  1. Lay out the assembly on a clean stable table. Establish the assembly baseline before positioning parts.
  2. Set stops, blocks, squares, and clamps. Confirm every stop and clamp matches the drawing's fit-up requirement.
  3. Verify orientation and final accessible faces. Confirm the assembly matches the drawing's stated orientation before clamping fully.
  4. Measure overall dimensions before tacking. Record length, width, and height against the drawing tolerance.
  5. Check angles, parallel lines, centers, and diagonals as applicable. Adjust the assembly until every check is within tolerance.
  6. Attach the work clamp close to the weld area. Confirm the clamp is on clean sound metal.
  7. Select the approved SMAW or GMAW procedure. Confirm the process matches the drawing's specification.
  8. Record machine, consumable, polarity, gas, and starting parameters. Enter each value on the part traveler before striking an arc.
  9. Place tack welds in the planned balanced sequence. Follow the sequence that distributes shrinkage evenly across the assembly.
  10. Inspect tack quality. Reject any tack with cracking, porosity, or poor fusion before continuing.
  11. Recheck all critical dimensions after tacking. Compare the tacked assembly against the drawing tolerance a second time.
  12. Correct minor movement before final welding. Use the approved correction method rather than forcing the assembly.

H. Final Welding

  1. Plan final weld order to balance heat input. Identify which joints weld first, second, and last before striking the first final arc.
  2. Weld short opposing or distributed segments where the plan requires it. Follow the planned sequence rather than completing one full joint before moving to the next.
  3. Maintain approved travel, angle, arc or stickout, and puddle control. Apply the SMAW or GMAW technique standards from Chapters 7 through 12.
  4. Clean slag or contamination between passes. Remove slag completely before adding any additional pass.
  5. Monitor assembly temperature and distortion. Pause the welding sequence if distortion approaches the drawing's tolerance limit.
  6. Do not quench unless the project procedure permits it. Allow the assembly to cool in still air unless the drawing states otherwise.
  7. Complete all required welds without adding uncalled-for weld metal. Stop each weld at the length and size specified by the drawing.

I. Cooling, Cleaning, Inspection, and Correction

  1. Mark the assembly hot and allow controlled cooling. Use the hot-metal cooling location established in Chapter 1.
  2. Remove slag and spatter. Clean every weld toe and adjacent surface before inspection.
  3. Inspect weld size, length, profile, fusion indicators, undercut, overlap, porosity, cracks, crater, starts, stops, and arc strikes. Use the visual-inspection standard from Chapter 18.
  4. Measure final length, width, height, angle, square, hole location, and flatness as applicable. Compare every measurement against the drawing tolerance.
  5. Compare final dimensions with drawing tolerances. Record any deviation on the part traveler.
  6. Identify distortion and whether it affects intended noncritical function. Confirm any distortion remains within the drawing's stated tolerance.
  7. Repair only approved weld defects with a documented method. Define the defect-removal and rewelding method before beginning any repair.
  8. Reinspect repaired areas. Apply the same visual and dimensional criteria used on the original weld.

J. Finish Preparation and Documentation

  1. Grind only surfaces identified by the drawing or finish requirement. Do not blend or flush-grind a weld the drawing requires to remain as-welded.
  2. Deburr handling edges. Remove sharp corners and burrs from every edge a user will handle.
  3. Clean the fixture for coating or bare-metal protection. Remove oil, dust, slag, and abrasive residue from the full surface.
  4. Perform a controlled noncritical function check within the stated load limit. Apply only the check described in the approved drawing.
  5. Do not proof load the fixture beyond the approved test. Stop the function check at the drawing's stated limit.
  6. Record material, cut list, fit-up, tacks, parameters, weld inspection, dimensions, repair, function check, and use limitation. Complete every field on the part traveler.
  7. Label the project as a training or noncritical shop fixture where required. Attach the label before handoff or storage.

Common Fabrication Inspection

Inspect the bracket or fixture using the same checks regardless of whether it was welded with SMAW or GMAW.

  • Cut-list check: confirm every part matches its recorded length, angle, and quantity within tolerance.
  • Weld-zone check: confirm every weld zone was cleaned to bright metal before fit-up.
  • Fit-up check: confirm overall dimensions, angles, and diagonals were within tolerance before tacking and again after tacking.
  • Tack check: confirm every tack was inspected and any defective tack was replaced before final welding.
  • Final weld check: confirm every required weld was completed to the specified size and length with acceptable profile and fusion indicators.
  • Dimensional check: confirm final measurements are within the drawing's tolerance and any distortion does not affect the fixture's intended noncritical function.
  • Finish and labeling check: confirm coating, if applied, was applied only after weld acceptance and that the use-limitation label is attached.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Cut Part Is the Wrong Length After Cutting

Probable cause: the cut list entry was transcribed incorrectly from the drawing, or the layout mark was measured from the wrong reference edge.
Confirmation: compare the cut list entry against the drawing a second time and check which reference edge was used for the layout mark.
Correction: correct the cut list entry if it was transcribed wrong, and remake the part to the correct length using the specified reference edge.
Reinspection: measure the remade part against the corrected cut list entry before returning it to the assembly sequence.

The Assembly Is Out of Square After Clamping but Before Tacking

Probable cause: a stop or clamp was set without checking the diagonal measurements, or a part was cut slightly out of tolerance.
Confirmation: measure both diagonals and compare them against the drawing's squareness tolerance; recheck each part's length and angle.
Correction: adjust clamps and stops until the diagonals match within tolerance, or replace an out-of-tolerance part.
Reinspection: repeat the fit-up dimensional check, including diagonals, before placing any tack weld.

A Tack Weld Cracks or Shows Poor Fusion

Probable cause: the weld zone was not cleaned to bright metal, the tack was undersized for the joint, or the parameters were outside the approved range.
Confirmation: inspect the tack for cleanliness at the joint, compare its size against the approved range, and check the recorded parameters against the procedure.
Correction: remove the defective tack using the approved method, reclean the joint, and replace the tack using verified parameters.
Reinspection: inspect the replaced tack for cracking, porosity, and fusion before continuing the tack sequence.

The Assembly Distorts Beyond Tolerance During Final Welding

Probable cause: the final weld order did not balance heat input across the assembly, or a joint was welded in one continuous pass instead of the planned segments.
Confirmation: measure the current distortion against the drawing tolerance and compare the actual weld order used to the planned sequence in Section H.
Correction: pause welding, allow controlled cooling, and correct alignment using the approved method before completing the remaining welds in the planned balanced order.
Reinspection: remeasure critical dimensions after correction and before completing the remaining welds.

A Finished Weld Shows Undercut, Porosity, or a Poor Profile

Probable cause: travel speed, arc length, angle, or parameters drifted from the approved range during final welding, or the weld zone was contaminated before welding.
Confirmation: compare the defect location and appearance against the reference criteria in Chapter 18 and check the recorded parameters and cleaning steps for that joint.
Correction: remove the defect using the approved method, reclean the joint, and reweld using corrected technique or parameters.
Reinspection: reinspect the repaired weld using the same visual and dimensional criteria as the original.

The Fixture Is Requested for a Use Beyond Its Stated Limitation

Probable cause: the finished fixture's noncritical appearance is mistaken for structural or load-bearing capability.
Confirmation: compare the requested use against the use-limitation label and the original shop drawing.
Correction: do not use or authorize the fixture for any purpose beyond its documented use limitation; obtain a new design, procedure, and qualified review for a different use.
Reinspection: not applicable; the fixture's use limitation does not change without a new design and inspection process.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed, inspected, and labeled fixture, not merely a finished weld. Housekeeping here protects the documentation record and the shop area for the chapters that follow.

  • Remove offcuts, abrasive waste, slag, and spatter from the work area
  • Confirm all consumables used are recorded and remaining stock is stored correctly
  • Shut down and de-energize the welding station and coil leads and hoses
  • Close and secure any gas cylinders used during welding
  • Store the completed part traveler with the shop drawing and inspection record
  • Complete the required fire-watch period before leaving the area

Skill Check

  1. Why must the complete cut list be built and verified against the drawing before the first part is cut?
  2. Why must every weld zone be cleaned to bright metal before fit-up rather than after tacking?
  3. Why are diagonals measured both before and after tacking, rather than only once?
  4. Why must final weld order be planned to balance heat input before the first final arc is struck?
  5. What must the learner do if distortion approaches the drawing's tolerance limit during final welding?
  6. Why must slag and contamination be removed between passes rather than only at the end of welding?
  7. Why must a coating never be applied to a weld that has not yet been inspected?
  8. What is the difference between the approved noncritical function check and a proof load, and why does this chapter prohibit the second one?
  9. Why must the finished fixture carry a use-limitation label rather than relying on the builder's memory of its intended use?
  10. What must the learner do if the shop drawing is missing a dimension or joint detail needed to complete a part?

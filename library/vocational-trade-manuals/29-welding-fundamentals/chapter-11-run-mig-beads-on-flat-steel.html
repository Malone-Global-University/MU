Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to produce a series of flat-position GMAW stringer beads on identified mild-steel coupons using the station you configured in Chapter 10. You will verify hot-work authorization, ventilation, PPE, cylinder security, and machine setup for the specific session; prepare coupons to clean bright metal; set and record approved starting voltage and wire-feed speed; run beads with a controlled gun angle, contact-tip-to-work distance, and travel speed; and inspect, measure, and document the finished beads against an approved acceptance sample.

This chapter does not weld a joint. It teaches the bead control that Chapter 12 depends on when you fit and weld GMAW lap, T-, and butt joints on the same coupons.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-061 — Position flat coupons for a GMAW stringer-bead practice sequence.
  • WF-062 — Set GMAW machine parameters from an approved starting range and record them.
  • WF-063 — Establish and maintain stable puddle control through voltage, wire speed, and travel.
  • WF-064 — Maintain the approved gun angle and contact-tip-to-work distance throughout a bead.
  • WF-065 — Maintain a consistent travel speed and bead spacing across a coupon.
  • WF-066 — Inspect and measure finished MIG bead quality against acceptance criteria.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapter 10 configured the GMAW station — wire, drive rolls, polarity, shielding gas, and starting parameters — and ended with a short authorized test weld. This chapter is where that station is used to build a real, welded skill: a repeatable stringer bead with controlled puddle, angle, distance, and travel. Chapter 12 uses this exact bead control to weld lap, T-, and square-butt joints. Chapter 13 changes wire type to flux-cored but reuses the same puddle-control and travel-speed vocabulary taught here.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • verifying hot-work, ventilation, PPE, cylinder, machine, gas, wire, and polarity conditions before welding
  • preparing flat mild-steel coupons and marking parallel bead lines
  • setting and recording approved starting voltage and wire-feed speed
  • running flat-position stringer beads with controlled gun angle, contact-tip-to-work distance, and travel speed
  • inspecting, measuring, and documenting bead quality and identifying probable causes of defects

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • weld a lap, T-, or butt joint; joint welding is taught in Chapter 12
  • select or install wire, drive rolls, or shielding gas; station configuration is completed in Chapter 10
  • invent voltage, wire-feed speed, contact-tip-to-work distance, or gun-angle values not supplied by the approved GMAW procedure, machine manufacturer, or consumable manufacturer
  • weld coated, unidentified, or contaminated material; only clean identified mild-steel coupons are used

Any bead, parameter, or shutdown condition that fails the inspection standards in this chapter must be corrected before the coupon set is accepted.

Trade Note

A stringer bead is not judged by whether an arc was maintained. It is judged by whether the resulting bead is straight, consistently wide, properly wetted at the toes, and free of porosity, undercut, and overlap for its full length. Consistency across a full coupon — not one good inch — is the pass condition for this chapter.

Setup Standard

Do not trigger the gun until hot-work authorization, ventilation, PPE, cylinder security, machine setup, gas flow, wire, and polarity have all been verified for this session. A bead started before verification is complete must stop and the coupon must be scrapped or re-marked.

Shop Tip

Watch the leading edge of the puddle, not the arc itself. The leading edge tells you whether the puddle is keeping pace with your travel speed — a puddle that outruns the arc is moving too fast, and a puddle that piles up behind the arc is moving too slow.

Reference Conventions

Establish these references before welding begins, and do not change them mid-coupon without re-marking and re-verifying.

TermDefinition

Common Field Fundamentals

Every GMAW stringer bead in this chapter follows the same sequence: verify the station, prepare the coupon, set and record parameters, establish the puddle, maintain angle and distance, travel at a steady speed, terminate with crater control, cool, clean, and inspect. That sequence, repeated with discipline, is what separates a controlled bead from a lucky one.

Puddle Control and Travel Speed

The puddle is the primary indicator of whether the current parameters and travel speed are correct for the coupon thickness. A stable, oval puddle with visible wetting at both edges indicates correct heat input and speed. A puddle that grows uncontrollably indicates travel that is too slow or heat that is too high; a narrow, ropy bead with poor tie-in indicates travel that is too fast or heat that is too low.

Measurement Cue — Verify Contact-Tip-to-Work Distance Before Each Bead

Confirm the contact-tip-to-work distance matches the approved procedure before starting each bead, and re-check it if the gun angle or stance changes mid-coupon. Do not estimate stickout by feel alone; measure or visually verify it against the approved reference before triggering the arc.

Critical Safety Cue — Gas Coverage and Arc Exposure

Do not weld with the shielding-gas nozzle clogged, damaged, or positioned so wind or drafts disturb gas coverage. Do not remove the helmet or safety glasses to inspect the arc directly. Lower the helmet fully before triggering the gun and keep it in position until the arc is broken and the puddle is no longer glowing.

Field Safety Cue — Gun Cable and Body Position

Support the gun cable so it does not pull the gun off the marked line or drag across hot metal. Maintain a stable stance that allows uninterrupted travel along the full length of the marked bead without repositioning your feet mid-bead.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Voltage and Wire-Feed Speed Chart

Set starting voltage and wire-feed speed only from the approved chart or procedure for the wire diameter, shielding gas, and base-metal thickness in use. Do not adjust beyond the approved starting range without instructor authorization and documentation of the change.

E. Preparation — Verification and Coupon Setup

  1. Review the approved GMAW practice procedure and coupon layout. Confirm base-metal thickness, wire, gas, and starting parameter range match the station configured in Chapter 10.
  2. Verify hot-work controls, ventilation, PPE, cylinder security, machine setup, gas, wire, and polarity. Confirm the Chapter 1 readiness record remains in effect and full GMAW PPE from Chapter 2 is worn.
  3. Prepare the coupon to clean bright metal. Remove rust, scale, oil, and contamination from the full coupon surface, not only the bead path.
  4. Mark parallel bead lines and start and stop points. Space bead lines to avoid overlap unless the exercise specifies otherwise.
  5. Secure the coupon flat and attach the work clamp nearby. Confirm the clamp contacts clean sound metal close to the practice area.

F. Parameter Setting and Recording

  1. Verify gas flow immediately before welding. Confirm flow matches the approved starting range recorded in Chapter 10.
  2. Set voltage and wire-feed speed to the approved starting values. Use the approved chart for the wire diameter, gas, and coupon thickness.
  3. Record contact-tip-to-work distance, gun angle, and travel direction from the procedure. Enter these values on the Session Parameter Record before welding begins.
  4. Trim wire to the approved extension. Cut the wire end cleanly immediately before starting each bead.

G. Bead Execution

  1. Assume a balanced stance with the gun cable supported. Confirm the stance allows uninterrupted travel along the full marked line.
  2. Position the gun at the start point. Align the contact tip over the marked start line at the approved contact-tip-to-work distance.
  3. Lower the helmet and trigger the arc. Confirm the helmet has darkened correctly before the arc is struck.
  4. Establish a stable puddle before beginning travel. Hold position briefly at the start point until the puddle forms.
  5. Maintain the approved work angle. Check gun position against the reference angle throughout the bead.
  6. Maintain the approved push or drag travel angle for the exercise. Do not switch travel angle mid-bead.
  7. Maintain consistent contact-tip-to-work distance. Adjust stance rather than allowing stickout to drift as you travel.
  8. Travel along the marked line at steady speed. Match travel speed to puddle behavior rather than a fixed count.
  9. Observe the leading edge and sides of the puddle. Use puddle shape, not arc brightness, to judge travel speed.
  10. Keep shielding-gas nozzle clear of excessive spatter. Stop and clean the nozzle if spatter buildup begins to disturb gas coverage.
  11. Release the trigger at the endpoint using the approved crater-control method. Fill the crater before breaking the arc.
  12. Keep the gun near the end briefly if required for post-flow coverage. Do not withdraw the gun until post-flow gas has protected the cooling puddle.
  13. Mark the coupon hot and allow safe cooling. Place the coupon in the designated cooling zone.

H. Between-Bead Inspection and Repeat Beads

  1. Inspect the nozzle, tip, and wire extension before the next bead. Correct spatter buildup, tip wear, or incorrect extension before continuing.
  2. Remove spatter only after cooling and with appropriate eye and face protection. Do not handle a hot coupon or bead with bare hands.
  3. Inspect bead straightness, width, reinforcement, wetting, toe transition, start, stop, porosity, undercut, overlap, and spatter. Compare the completed bead against the Acceptance Standards table.
  4. Measure bead width and length. Record measurements using the bead/fillet gauge and steel rule.
  5. Identify whether the arc appeared too cold, too hot, unstable, erratic, or inadequately shielded. Note the observation on the parameter log.
  6. Check for wire-feed problems, gas disturbance, dirty metal, or excessive stickout. Investigate any bead that does not match the acceptance sample.
  7. Change only one parameter or technique variable at a time. Record the specific change before running the next bead.
  8. Run additional parallel beads. Continue until the minimum bead count specified in Section B is complete.
  9. Keep bead spacing consistent and avoid welding over previous beads unless specified. Verify spacing against the marked lines before each new bead.
  10. Monitor machine duty cycle. Allow cooling time between beads as required by the machine manufacturer.
  11. Inspect the coupon for distortion and arc strikes. Check flatness against the welding table and note any accidental arc strikes outside the bead path.
  12. Compare beads produced with approved small parameter adjustments. Identify which settings produced the most consistent bead.
  13. Select the best parameter set under instructor review. Confirm the selected values with the responsible evaluator.

I. Documentation and Shutdown

  1. Record final voltage, wire speed, gas flow, angle, travel, defects, and corrections. Complete the Session Parameter Record for the accepted bead set.
  2. Obtain qualified visual inspection. Have the instructor or qualified evaluator review the completed coupon against the acceptance sample.
  3. Close the cylinder, relieve the system as required, shut down the machine, and complete fire watch. Follow the Chapter 1 shutdown and fire-watch requirements before leaving the area.

Common Bead Inspection

Inspect every bead using the same checks regardless of which coupon or session produced it.

  • Straightness check: confirm the bead follows the marked line without excessive wander.
  • Profile check: confirm consistent width, reinforcement, and toe wetting along the full bead.
  • Start/stop check: confirm controlled starts and filled craters at every termination.
  • Surface-defect check: confirm no visible porosity, undercut, overlap, or excessive spatter.
  • Distortion check: confirm the coupon remains within the flatness tolerance for the exercise.
  • Documentation check: confirm parameters, technique observations, and inspection results are recorded for each accepted bead set.

Complete the MIG Bead Inspection Checklist in the Inspection Checklist section below before beginning Chapter 12.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

Bead Wanders Off the Marked Line

Probable cause: unstable stance, unsupported gun cable, or inconsistent work angle causing the gun to drift during travel.
Confirmation: review stance and cable support during the next bead and compare gun angle against the reference at multiple points along the line.
Correction: reposition to a stable stance, support the cable so it does not pull the gun, and re-mark the coupon if the current bead is unacceptable.
Reinspection: repeat the straightness check on the next bead.

Bead Shows Undercut Along One or Both Toes

Probable cause: travel speed too fast for the current heat input, or gun angle directing excessive arc force at one toe.
Confirmation: compare travel speed and gun angle used on the defective bead against the approved procedure and the beads that passed inspection.
Correction: reduce travel speed, correct gun angle, or adjust voltage and wire-feed speed within the approved range, changing one variable at a time.
Reinspection: repeat the undercut check on the next bead produced with the corrected variable.

Bead Shows Surface Porosity

Probable cause: disturbed or inadequate shielding-gas coverage from wind, a clogged nozzle, incorrect flow, or contaminated base metal.
Confirmation: inspect the nozzle for spatter buildup, verify gas flow against the approved range, and check the coupon surface for missed contamination.
Correction: clean or replace the nozzle, correct gas flow, add wind protection, or reclean the coupon before rewelding.
Reinspection: repeat the porosity check on the next bead after the correction.

Puddle Grows Uncontrollably or Burns Through

Probable cause: excessive heat input from voltage or wire-feed speed set too high for the coupon thickness, or travel speed too slow.
Confirmation: compare current voltage and wire-feed speed against the approved starting range for the coupon thickness in use.
Correction: reduce voltage or wire-feed speed within the approved range, or increase travel speed, and record the corrected values.
Reinspection: run a short test bead on matching scrap before continuing on the coupon.

Inconsistent Bead Width Across the Coupon

Probable cause: drifting contact-tip-to-work distance or inconsistent travel speed during the bead.
Confirmation: review stickout and travel-speed notes recorded during welding and compare bead width measurements at multiple points along the same bead.
Correction: re-establish and hold the approved contact-tip-to-work distance and maintain a steadier travel speed on the next bead.
Reinspection: measure width at multiple points on the corrected bead.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a documented set of practice beads, not a welded joint. Housekeeping here protects the station and coupons for Chapter 12.

  • Close the shielding-gas cylinder and relieve system pressure as required
  • Shut down and de-energize the welding machine
  • Coil the gun cable and work lead without sharp bends
  • Store accepted coupons labeled and separated from rejected coupons
  • Clean spatter, wire clippings, and abrasive debris from the work surface
  • Complete the required fire-watch period before leaving the area

Field Terms

Stringer Bead
A weld bead run in a straight line without side-to-side weaving unless the exercise specifies otherwise.
Contact-Tip-to-Work Distance
The distance from the gun's contact tip to the base-metal surface, held constant through the bead.
Work Angle
The gun angle measured across the joint or plate surface.
Travel Angle
The gun angle measured along the direction of travel, either push or drag.
Wetting
The degree to which the puddle blends smoothly into the base metal at the bead toes.
Crater-Control Method
The approved technique for filling the bead termination to avoid a depression or crack-prone crater.
Post-Flow
Shielding gas that continues to flow briefly after the trigger is released to protect the cooling puddle.

Job Overview

This chapter qualifies you to inspect a set of sample welds and identify the visible defect conditions present using correct terminology, connect each defect to its probable machine, parameter, technique, or fit-up cause, and decide whether a sample should be accepted, monitored, repaired, sectioned for destructive testing, or remade. You will then prepare and carry out a controlled correction plan under instructor authorization and reinspect the repaired area against the original criteria.

You will work from cooled, cleaned, and identified sample welds produced earlier in this book — SMAW stringer beads and fillets from Chapters 7 through 9, GMAW and FCAW beads and joints from Chapters 10 through 13, and GTAW beads from Chapters 14 and 15 — along with any additional instructor-supplied reference samples. No new arc is struck to produce the samples themselves; the arc time in this chapter is limited to the approved repair segment described in the work order.

This is the defect-recognition chapter of the book. Every earlier welding chapter asked you to inspect your own work at the point of production. This chapter slows that same inspection down, gives it complete vocabulary and measurement tools, and requires you to reason from symptom to cause before touching a grinder or striking a repair arc. That same recognition-before-correction discipline carries directly into Chapter 19's full cleaning, inspection, and finishing sequence and into the Chapter 20 capstone.

Skills You Will Demonstrate

  • WF-103 — Identify porosity in a sample weld by location, size, distribution, and recurrence.
  • WF-104 — Identify undercut along weld toes and measure it with the approved gauge and criteria.
  • WF-105 — Identify lack-of-fusion indicators from toe shape, bead placement, and cold profile while acknowledging the limits of visual inspection.
  • WF-106 — Identify excessive spatter and arc strikes and distinguish them from acceptable surface conditions.
  • WF-107 — Identify poor bead profile, including excessive convexity, concavity, inconsistent width, excessive reinforcement, and poor contour.
  • WF-108 — Prepare corrective-action notes that connect each identified defect to a probable cause and a defined, instructor-approved correction method.

Where This Skill Fits

Chapters 7 through 15 taught you to inspect your own beads and joints immediately after welding, using the terminology and criteria introduced in those chapters one process at a time. This chapter collects that terminology into one complete defect-recognition and corrective-action skill and applies it across SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, and GTAW samples side by side.

Chapter 19 assumes you can already name a defect correctly, measure it where a gauge applies, and reason from symptom to probable cause before it walks you through the full clean-inspect-correct-finish sequence for one completed project. Chapter 20's capstone repeats this same defect-analysis step at full scale, on a multi-joint assembly, under the same probable-cause reasoning you practice here.

Scope and Intended Use

This chapter covers:

  • visually inspecting cooled, cleaned sample welds under adequate lighting and, where approved, magnification
  • identifying porosity, undercut, overlap or cold lap, poor bead contour, incomplete fill, crater depression, abrupt starts and stops, excessive spatter, arc strikes, surface cracks, visible slag inclusions, and fusion-related surface indicators
  • measuring undercut, fillet size, and bead dimensions with the approved gauges
  • reasoning from each identified defect to one or more probable machine, parameter, technique, fit-up, cleaning, gas, consumable, or material causes
  • deciding whether a sample is accepted, monitored, repaired, sectioned, destructively tested, or remade
  • carrying out one instructor-authorized practice-defect removal and repair weld under the corrected setup, then reinspecting the repair
  • recording a defect log and corrective-action notes for every sample examined

This chapter does not authorize the learner to:

  • grind over, hide, or conceal a surface crack on any sample; a crack found on any sample is rejectable and requires qualified evaluation, not learner-directed grinding or repair
  • invent a repair procedure for a structural, code-governed, or production weld; this chapter's repair authorization applies only to the noncritical practice coupons and samples described in this book
  • perform destructive testing without the guards, tools, and instructor control identified in the chapter that produced the sample
  • assign a single cause to a defect without considering the other probable-cause categories listed in this chapter

Any sample containing a crack must be set aside and referred to the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator rather than repaired by the learner.

Trade Note

A defect log that lists only the name of a defect is incomplete. The value of this chapter is in the reasoning that connects a visible symptom to its probable cause and then to a specific, controlled correction. A learner who can name "porosity" but cannot connect it to a probable gas, cleaning, or technique cause has not yet demonstrated WF-108.

Setup Standard

Do not begin defect analysis on a sample that has not cooled to a safe handling temperature, has not been cleaned of loose slag and spatter, or is not clearly identified by process, material, and origin. Do not authorize a repair weld on any sample until the corrective-action notes for that sample are complete and the responsible instructor has reviewed the plan.

Shop Tip

Work through the samples in the same order they were produced — SMAW, then GMAW, then FCAW, then GTAW — and keep the accepted reference sample for each process next to the sample under inspection. Comparing against a known-good example at the same time you compare against the defect criteria table catches profile problems that criteria alone can miss.

Prerequisites

  • Completion of Chapters 7 through 15, or an equivalent instructor-supplied set of cooled, cleaned, identified sample welds representing SMAW, GMAW, FCAW, and GTAW
  • Every sample used in this chapter has cooled to a safe handling temperature and is free of loose slag, scale, and contamination that would obscure a defect

Common Field Fundamentals

Every defect examined in this chapter is evaluated using the same four-part reasoning chain: identify the defect by name and location, measure it where a gauge applies, propose one or more probable causes drawn from the categories below, and decide a disposition. Skipping directly from "I see a defect" to "grind and reweld" without the middle two steps is the single most common error this chapter is designed to correct.

Machine-relatedPolarity, output stability, duty-cycle limits, worn contact tip, feeder problems
Parameter-relatedAmperage, voltage, wire speed, travel speed set outside the approved range
Technique-relatedArc length, work angle, travel angle, filler timing, hand movement
Fit-up / cleaning / gas / consumable / materialJoint gap, contamination, shielding-gas coverage, wrong or damaged consumable, unidentified base metal

Visual Inspection Conditions

Visual inspection must occur under adequate lighting, with the sample cleaned of loose slag, scale, and spatter that would obscure the weld toe, face, and adjacent base metal. Use magnification for suspected fine porosity, hairline surface cracks, or arc strikes that are not clearly visible at normal viewing distance. Use a mirror to inspect the reverse side of butt-joint samples where the chapter that produced the sample identified an approved penetration indicator.

Critical Safety Cue — Cracks Are Not a Learner Repair

A surface crack found on any sample is rejectable and requires qualified evaluation. Do not grind over a crack to hide it, and do not attempt a learner-directed repair on a cracked sample. Set the sample aside, record it on the defect log, and refer it to the responsible instructor or qualified evaluator.

Measurement Cue — Use the Gauge, Not the Eye, for Undercut and Fillet Size

Undercut depth and fillet leg size must be measured with the approved gauge and criteria, not estimated visually. Record the measured value on the defect log even when the defect is also visually obvious. A visual impression of "looks a little undercut" is not a disposition basis by itself.

Field Safety Cue — Handling Sharp Arc-Strike and Spatter Edges

Arc-strike craters and spatter beads can leave sharp, brittle edges on sample welds. Wear gloves when handling samples during close inspection, and deburr any handling hazard identified during the inspection before passing the sample to another learner.

Manufacturer Instruction Cue — Gauge Use

Use the fillet-weld gauge and undercut gauge according to the gauge manufacturer's instructions for placement, reading, and zero-point reference. A gauge misapplied to the wrong reference surface produces a measurement that is not comparable to the acceptance criteria.

E. Preparation — Review Criteria and Confirm Sample Safety

  1. Review the approved visual-inspection criteria and defect terminology. Confirm the Reference Conventions table and the criteria from the chapter that produced each sample before beginning analysis.
  2. Confirm the samples are cool, cleaned, identified, and safe to handle. Do not analyze a sample that has not cooled to a safe handling temperature or is not clearly labeled by process and origin.
  3. Record process, material, joint, position, consumable, and settings when known. Complete the Sample and Analysis Record panel for each sample before inspecting it.

F. Visual Inspection Under Controlled Conditions

  1. Inspect samples under adequate lighting. Reposition the sample or the light source until the full bead length and both toes are clearly visible.
  2. Use magnification, mirror, gauges, and straightedge where approved. Apply magnification to suspected fine porosity or hairline cracks and a mirror to the reverse side of butt samples with an approved penetration indicator.
  3. Examine starts, stops, craters, toes, face, edges, and adjacent base metal. Inspect the full length of each bead or joint, not only its midpoint.

G. Identify Porosity, Undercut, and Overlap

  1. Identify surface porosity by location, size, distribution, and recurrence. Note whether porosity clusters at starts, stops, or along the full bead length.
  2. Distinguish visible pores from slag pits, spatter scars, or cleaning marks. Use magnification to confirm a true pore versus a surface mark before recording it.
  3. Identify undercut along weld toes. Sight along the toe line under raking light to locate any groove cut into the base metal.
  4. Measure undercut only with the approved gauge and criteria. Record the measured depth and length of each undercut location.
  5. Identify overlap or cold lap at the toe. Look for weld metal that has flowed onto the base metal surface without fusing to it.

H. Identify Profile, Fill, and Termination Defects

  1. Identify excessive convexity, concavity, inconsistent width, excessive reinforcement, and poor contour. Compare the bead face against a straightedge and against the accepted reference sample.
  2. Identify incomplete fill, crater depression, and abrupt starts or stops. Note whether the crater was filled using the approved method taught in the chapter that produced the sample.
  3. Identify excessive spatter and arc strikes. Distinguish scattered acceptable spatter from heavy spatter that indicates an unstable arc, and locate any arc-strike crater outside the intended weld path.

I. Identify Cracks, Slag Inclusions, and Fusion Indicators

  1. Identify surface cracks and stop evaluation for qualified review. Set the sample aside immediately upon finding a crack and record it on the defect log.
  2. Do not grind over a crack to hide it. Leave the crack visible for the responsible instructor's review.
  3. Identify visible slag inclusions or trapped slag indications. Look for surface pitting or worm-track indications consistent with trapped slag, particularly on FCAW samples.
  4. Identify fusion concerns from toe shape, bead placement, cold profile, or destructive-test evidence while acknowledging visual limitations. Record fusion concerns as an indication requiring further evaluation rather than a confirmed internal condition.
  5. Identify burn-through, excessive penetration indication, distortion, and melted edges where applicable. Note these conditions on butt-joint and thin-material samples in particular.

J. Compare, Document, and Reason to Probable Cause

  1. Compare each defect with accepted reference samples. Place the sample under inspection beside its matching accepted reference sample for the same process.
  2. Record location and photograph each defect. Mark the location on the sample and capture a photograph referenced by the defect log entry.
  3. Identify possible machine-related causes. Consider polarity, output stability, worn contact tip, feeder condition, and duty-cycle limits.
  4. Identify possible parameter-related causes. Consider amperage, voltage, wire speed, and travel speed relative to the approved starting range recorded for the sample.
  5. Identify possible technique-related causes. Consider arc length, work angle, travel angle, filler timing, and hand movement.
  6. Identify possible fit-up, cleaning, gas, consumable, or material causes. Consider joint gap, surface contamination, shielding-gas coverage, wrong or damaged consumable, and unidentified base metal.
  7. Do not assign a single cause without considering alternatives. Record every category considered on the defect log, even where only one is judged most likely.

K. Disposition and Authorized Repair

  1. Select whether the sample should be accepted, monitored, repaired, sectioned, destructively tested, or remade. Base the decision on the defect type, size, location, and recurrence recorded on the defect log.
  2. Determine whether sufficient base material remains for repair. Check remaining thickness at any location where defect removal would remove base metal.
  3. Define the approved defect-removal method. State the specific method — grinding, gouging, or mechanical removal — approved for the defect and sample.
  4. Define re-preparation, cleaning, fit-up, preheat, parameter, or technique changes where supplied by the qualified procedure. Record only changes supported by the approved procedure or instructor direction; do not invent a value.
  5. Do not invent a repair procedure for structural or code welds. Confirm the sample is a noncritical practice coupon before proceeding with any learner-directed repair.
  6. Remove a practice defect only under instructor control. Obtain authorization before grinding, gouging, or otherwise removing any defect.
  7. Inspect the excavated area before rewelding. Confirm the defect has been fully removed and the surface is clean before continuing.
  8. Reweld using the approved corrected setup. Apply the machine, parameter, and technique corrections identified in the corrective-action notes.
  9. Allow cooling and clean the repair. Follow the same slag- and spatter-removal steps used in the original process chapter.
  10. Reinspect the repaired area using the original criteria. Apply the same acceptance standards used for the sample's process and joint type.
  11. Compare repaired and original conditions. Record whether the repair eliminated the original defect without introducing a new one.

L. Documentation and Log Completion

  1. Prepare corrective-action notes for porosity, undercut, fusion concerns, spatter, and poor profile. Complete one entry per defect type identified across the sample set.
  2. Record whether the correction addressed the likely cause. State the evidence supporting that conclusion, such as the reinspection result.
  3. Update the learner defect log and recurring-error plan. Note any defect type that recurred across more than one sample and the plan to address it in future chapters.

Common Area Inspection

Apply the same four checks to every sample regardless of process.

  • Terminology check: confirm every recorded condition uses the terms defined in Reference Conventions.
  • Measurement check: confirm undercut and fillet size are recorded from a gauge reading, not a visual estimate.
  • Cause check: confirm each defect entry considers machine, parameter, technique, and fit-up/cleaning/gas/consumable/material causes.
  • Crack check: confirm any crack is set aside and referred to the responsible instructor rather than repaired by the learner.
  • Disposition check: confirm every sample has one recorded disposition supported by the evidence gathered.
  • Repair check: confirm any repair weld was authorized before the arc was struck and reinspected afterward against the original criteria.

Failure Points and Troubleshooting

A Defect Is Named but No Probable Cause Is Recorded

Probable cause: the analysis stopped at identification and skipped the machine, parameter, technique, and fit-up/cleaning/gas/consumable/material review required by Section J.
Confirmation: review the defect log entry and confirm whether each of the four cause categories was actually considered and recorded, not only the category judged most likely.
Correction: return to the sample and the settings recorded in the Sample and Analysis Record, and work through each cause category before finalizing the entry.
Reinspection: confirm the completed entry lists at least one consideration from each relevant cause category.

Undercut Is Recorded from a Visual Impression Instead of a Gauge Reading

Probable cause: the toe condition was judged "undercut" by eye without applying the approved undercut gauge.
Confirmation: re-examine the toe with the gauge positioned according to the manufacturer's instructions.
Correction: record the measured depth and length; if the gauge shows no measurable undercut, correct the log entry to reflect the actual reading.
Reinspection: confirm every undercut entry in the log carries a numeric gauge reading.

A Suspected Crack Is Ground Before Instructor Review

Probable cause: the learner treated the suspected crack as an ordinary defect and proceeded directly to grinding without setting it aside.
Confirmation: compare the remaining sample and any photograph taken before grinding against the original defect description.
Correction: stop further work on the sample, notify the responsible instructor of what occurred, and follow their direction for the sample's disposition.
Reinspection: confirm the defect log records the incident and the instructor's determination.

A Repair Weld Is Struck Before the Corrective-Action Notes Are Reviewed

Probable cause: the disposition of "repair" was selected without completing the corrective-action notes or obtaining instructor authorization first.
Confirmation: check whether Section K's defect-removal method and corrected setup were documented and reviewed before the arc was struck.
Correction: stop, complete the corrective-action notes, and obtain instructor review before any further repair welding on that sample.
Reinspection: confirm the authorization step is recorded on the defect log before the repair result is accepted.

The Repaired Area Still Shows the Original Defect

Probable cause: the defect was not fully removed before rewelding, or the corrected setup did not address the actual probable cause identified in Section J.
Confirmation: compare the reinspection result against the original defect photograph and the corrective-action notes.
Correction: reexamine the probable-cause reasoning, identify what was missed, and repeat the defect-removal and repair sequence under a revised corrected setup.
Reinspection: repeat the full acceptance-standard check for the sample's process and joint type.

The Same Defect Type Recurs Across Multiple Samples Without Being Flagged

Probable cause: each sample was logged individually without comparing entries across the full sample set.
Confirmation: review the completed defect log for repeated defect types across different samples and processes.
Correction: add the recurring-error entry to the learner defect log and identify the shared probable cause, such as a consistently worn contact tip or a consistently short arc length.
Reinspection: confirm the recurring-error plan is recorded before closing out the chapter.

Cleanup

This chapter ends with a completed defect log and, where applicable, one reinspected repair — not a finished fabricated project. Housekeeping here protects the sample set and documentation used by later chapters.

  • Return all gauges, magnifiers, mirrors, and measuring tools to their staged location
  • Label and store analyzed samples separately from unanalyzed samples
  • Confirm any sample referred to the responsible instructor for crack evaluation is clearly marked and set aside
  • Store completed defect logs and photographs with other project records
  • Sweep or vacuum grinding or chipping debris from any authorized repair location and confirm the fire-watch period for that repair is complete

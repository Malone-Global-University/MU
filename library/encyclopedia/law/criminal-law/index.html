Criminal Law Overview

Criminal law studies the rules that define prohibited conduct, establish criminal responsibility, regulate prosecution, protect accused persons, and determine punishment after conviction. It includes both substantive criminal law, which explains what counts as a crime and what mental state or conduct must be proven, and criminal procedure, which governs investigation, arrest, search, interrogation, charging, trial, and appeal. This discipline is central to legal systems because criminal law represents one of the strongest uses of public power: the ability of the state to accuse, prosecute, punish, and restrict liberty. By studying criminal law, learners gain a framework for understanding offenses, defenses, constitutional protections, sentencing systems, prosecutorial authority, defense strategy, and the balance between public safety and individual rights.

Categories

  • Criminal Defense

    Study the legal strategies, constitutional protections, factual investigations, defenses, plea negotiations, trial advocacy, and procedural tools used to represent accused persons.

  • Criminal Procedure

    Examine the rules governing investigation, arrest, search and seizure, interrogation, charging, bail, plea bargaining, trial rights, appeals, and constitutional limits on criminal prosecution.

  • Sentencing

    Explore how courts determine punishment after conviction, including sentencing guidelines, aggravating and mitigating factors, imprisonment, probation, fines, restitution, rehabilitation, and proportionality.

  • Substantive Criminal Law

    Learn how crimes are legally defined through prohibited acts, required mental states, causation, harm, defenses, justification, excuse, attempt, conspiracy, and liability principles.

  • White-Collar Crime

    Study financially motivated and organizational offenses, including fraud, bribery, insider trading, embezzlement, money laundering, regulatory crimes, corporate misconduct, and professional liability.

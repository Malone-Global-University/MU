Administrative Law Overview

Administrative law studies the legal framework that governs public agencies and the exercise of delegated government authority. It explains how agencies are created, how they issue rules, how they decide individual cases, how they enforce regulatory requirements, and how courts or higher officials review agency action. This discipline is central to modern law because many public decisions are not made directly by legislatures or courts, but by administrative bodies responsible for areas such as labor, finance, health, environment, transportation, education, immigration, utilities, and public safety. By studying administrative law, learners gain a clearer understanding of how regulatory power operates in practice, how procedural safeguards protect affected parties, and how legal systems balance expertise, accountability, discretion, and oversight.

Categories

  • Administrative Review

    Study how agency decisions are reviewed by courts, oversight bodies, or higher administrative authorities, including standards of review, appeals, remedies, and limits on agency discretion.

  • Adjudication

    Examine how agencies resolve individual disputes, conduct hearings, evaluate evidence, issue orders, and apply legal rules to specific parties or regulated activities.

  • Agency Structure

    Explore how administrative agencies are created, organized, staffed, supervised, delegated authority, and positioned within larger systems of executive, legislative, and regulatory power.

  • Regulatory Enforcement

    Learn how agencies monitor compliance, investigate violations, impose penalties, negotiate settlements, issue orders, and use enforcement tools to carry out statutory mandates.

  • Rulemaking

    Study how agencies create regulations through notice, comment, public participation, technical analysis, statutory interpretation, policy judgment, and formal or informal rulemaking procedures.

Environmental Physics

Environmental physics applies physical principles to Earth's atmosphere, oceans, climate, energy flows, radiation, and environmental systems.

Overview

Environmental physics uses physics to study the natural environment and human interactions with it. It examines energy transfer, radiation, heat, fluids, waves, atmospheric motion, ocean circulation, climate processes, pollution movement, and measurement systems.

Energy Balance

Environmental systems depend on energy entering, leaving, and being stored. Earth's climate is strongly shaped by solar radiation, reflection, absorption, and infrared emission.

Radiation

Radiation transfer determines how sunlight warms Earth and how infrared energy leaves the surface and atmosphere.

Atmospheric Physics

Atmospheric physics studies air pressure, temperature, humidity, wind, clouds, storms, and large-scale circulation.

Ocean Physics

Ocean physics includes waves, currents, density differences, heat storage, mixing, and interactions with the atmosphere.

Climate Physics

Climate physics studies long-term patterns of energy flow, feedbacks, greenhouse gases, ice, oceans, clouds, and circulation.

Pollution Transport

Fluid dynamics and atmospheric motion help explain how pollutants move through air and water.

Environmental Measurement

Sensors, satellites, radar, lidar, buoys, weather stations, and models help measure environmental conditions.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is treating environmental physics as only weather. It includes radiation, energy systems, fluids, waves, materials, and measurement.

Why This Matters in Physics

Environmental physics connects physical science to climate, energy, water, air, ecosystems, hazards, infrastructure, and policy decisions.

Specific Heat

Specific heat describes how much energy is required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of a substance by one degree.

Overview

Specific heat is a material property that describes resistance to temperature change. Substances with high specific heat require more energy to change temperature than substances with low specific heat. This concept explains why water warms and cools slowly compared with many other materials.

Material Property

Specific heat depends on the substance. Equal masses of different materials can require different amounts of energy to produce the same temperature change.

Heat and Temperature Change

When heat is added to a substance without a phase change, its temperature change depends on the mass, the material's specific heat, and the energy transferred.

Water's High Specific Heat

Water has a high specific heat compared with many common substances. This helps stabilize climate, moderate coastal temperatures, and regulate living organisms.

Thermal Inertia

Materials with high specific heat have greater thermal inertia, meaning they change temperature more slowly for a given energy transfer.

Connection to Calorimetry

Calorimetry often uses specific heat to calculate heat transfer from measured temperature changes.

Units

Specific heat is commonly measured in joules per kilogram per degree Celsius or joules per kilogram per kelvin.

Applications

Specific heat matters in cooking, climate science, engineering, electronics cooling, materials selection, oceanography, and biology.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is assuming all substances warm at the same rate when given the same energy. Different specific heats produce different temperature changes.

Why This Matters in Physics

Specific heat links energy transfer to temperature change and makes thermal calculations practical for real materials.

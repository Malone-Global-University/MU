Laws of Thermodynamics

The laws of thermodynamics describe energy conservation, heat flow, entropy, temperature, and the limits of engines and physical processes.

Overview

The laws of thermodynamics are broad principles governing heat, work, energy, temperature, entropy, and equilibrium. They apply to engines, refrigerators, living systems, stars, materials, chemical reactions, climate, and many technological systems.

Zeroth Law

The zeroth law states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This supports the concept of temperature measurement.

First Law

The first law is a statement of energy conservation. The internal energy of a system changes when heat is transferred or work is done.

Second Law

The second law describes the direction of natural processes. Heat flows spontaneously from hotter objects to colder objects, and entropy tends to increase in isolated systems.

Third Law

The third law concerns behavior as temperature approaches absolute zero. It places limits on reaching absolute zero and relates to entropy at very low temperatures.

Heat Engines

Heat engines convert some thermal energy into work, but the second law limits their efficiency. No real heat engine can convert all input heat into useful work.

Refrigerators and Heat Pumps

Refrigerators and heat pumps move thermal energy from cooler regions to warmer regions by using external work.

Entropy

Entropy is a thermodynamic quantity associated with energy dispersal and the number of microscopic arrangements compatible with a macroscopic state.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is treating the second law as saying energy is destroyed. Energy is conserved, but useful energy becomes more dispersed and less available for work.

Why This Matters in Physics

The laws of thermodynamics connect energy, heat, work, engines, climate, chemistry, biology, astronomy, and the arrow of time.

Angular Momentum

Angular momentum measures rotational motion and is conserved when no external net torque acts on a system.

Overview

Angular momentum is a measure of rotational motion. It is the rotational counterpart of linear momentum. An object can have angular momentum because it spins about its own axis, moves around a point, or participates in a rotating system.

Angular momentum is important because it is conserved when no external net torque acts on a system. This conservation principle explains many behaviors in skating, astronomy, machinery, gyroscopes, and rotating systems.

Angular Momentum and Rotational Motion

For a rotating object, angular momentum depends on rotational inertia and angular velocity. Rotational inertia describes how mass is distributed relative to the axis. Angular velocity describes how quickly the object rotates.

An object with greater rotational inertia or greater angular velocity has greater angular momentum, assuming the same axis of rotation.

Dependence on Axis

Angular momentum is defined relative to an axis or point. Changing the reference axis can change the angular momentum description.

This is why rotational problems must specify the axis of rotation or the point about which angular momentum is being considered.

Relationship to Torque

Net torque changes angular momentum. This is the rotational parallel to the way net force changes linear momentum.

If the external net torque on a system is zero, angular momentum remains constant. If external net torque acts, angular momentum changes in the direction and amount associated with that torque over time.

Conservation of Angular Momentum

Conservation of angular momentum means the total angular momentum of a system remains constant when no external net torque acts. Internal changes can redistribute angular momentum, but the total remains the same.

A classic example is a spinning skater pulling in their arms. Pulling the arms inward reduces rotational inertia, so angular velocity increases to keep angular momentum conserved.

Angular Momentum in Astronomy

Angular momentum is central in astronomy. Planets, moons, stars, galaxies, and accretion disks involve rotating or orbiting matter. When large clouds of gas contract under gravity, conservation of angular momentum can increase their rotation rate.

This principle helps explain why many astronomical systems form disks and why spinning objects can rotate faster as their mass distribution moves inward.

Gyroscopes and Stability

Gyroscopes use angular momentum to resist changes in orientation. A rapidly spinning object can maintain its axis direction unless acted on by an external torque.

This behavior is useful in navigation, stabilization systems, bicycles, spacecraft, and many mechanical devices.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is assuming angular momentum only applies to objects spinning in place. Objects moving around a point can also have angular momentum relative to that point.

Another mistake is applying conservation of angular momentum when external torque is not zero. Conservation requires the external net torque to be absent or negligible for the system being analyzed.

Connection to Linear Momentum

Angular momentum is related to linear momentum but includes rotational geometry. A moving object has angular momentum about a point if its motion has a turning effect relative to that point.

This connection makes angular momentum useful in both particle motion and extended-body rotation.

Why This Matters in Physics

Angular momentum is one of the major conservation ideas in mechanics. It gives a powerful way to analyze systems where rotation matters.

It connects torque, rotation, astronomy, machinery, sports, and stability into one broad principle.

Efficiency

Efficiency measures how much input energy or work becomes useful output rather than being dissipated into less useful forms.

Overview

Efficiency measures how effectively a system converts input energy, work, or power into useful output. Real systems always transfer some energy into less useful forms such as heat, sound, vibration, friction, or deformation.

Input and Output

Efficiency compares useful output with total input. The input may be energy, work, or power, and the useful output is the portion that accomplishes the intended task.

Energy Losses

Energy is not destroyed when efficiency is less than 100 percent. It is transferred or transformed into forms that are not useful for the intended task.

Mechanical Efficiency

Mechanical efficiency describes how well a machine converts input work into useful output work. Friction and deformation commonly reduce mechanical efficiency.

Power Efficiency

Efficiency can also be expressed using power by comparing useful output power with input power.

Efficiency and Conservation of Energy

Efficiency does not violate conservation of energy. A low-efficiency system still conserves total energy when all forms are counted.

Real-World Tradeoffs

High efficiency may require better materials, higher cost, more maintenance, tighter tolerances, or more complex design.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is saying lost energy disappears. Another is assuming the most powerful machine is automatically the most efficient.

Applications

Efficiency is used in machines, engines, power plants, transportation, electrical systems, buildings, tools, appliances, and environmental analysis.

Why This Matters in Physics

Efficiency connects conservation of energy to practical system design and explains why real machines differ from ideal machines.

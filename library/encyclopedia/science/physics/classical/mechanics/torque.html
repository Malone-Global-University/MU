Torque

Torque measures the rotational effect of a force, depending on the force size, direction, and distance from the axis of rotation.

Overview

Torque is the rotational effect of a force. A force can cause an object to rotate if it is applied in a way that produces turning about an axis or pivot. Torque depends not only on how large the force is, but also on where and in what direction the force is applied.

Torque explains why a door is easier to open by pushing near the handle than near the hinge, why longer wrenches make bolts easier to turn, and why balance depends on both weight and distance from a pivot.

Rotational Effect of Force

A force does not automatically produce rotation. If the force acts through the axis of rotation, it may produce no torque about that axis. If it acts at a distance from the axis and has a perpendicular component, it can produce rotation.

The rotational effect is strongest when the force is applied perpendicular to the lever arm. The rotational effect is weaker when the force is applied at a smaller angle.

Lever Arm

The lever arm, or moment arm, is the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line of action of the force. A longer lever arm gives the force more rotational effect.

This is why tools such as wrenches, crowbars, and levers are useful. They increase the distance from the pivot or turning point, allowing the same force to produce more torque.

Direction of Torque

Torque has direction. In many introductory two-dimensional problems, torque is classified as clockwise or counterclockwise. One direction is usually chosen as positive and the other as negative.

Consistent sign convention is essential. If counterclockwise torque is positive, clockwise torque should be treated as negative throughout the problem.

Net Torque

The net torque is the sum of all torques about a chosen axis. If the net torque is zero, the object has no angular acceleration about that axis. It may be rotationally at rest or rotating with constant angular velocity.

If the net torque is not zero, the rotational motion changes. This is the rotational parallel to how net force changes linear motion.

Torque and Equilibrium

Static equilibrium requires both zero net force and zero net torque. An object can have balanced forces but still rotate if the torques are not balanced.

For example, a seesaw can have equal total downward weight on both sides but still rotate if the weights are placed at different distances from the pivot.

Applications

Torque appears in doors, engines, bicycle pedals, screws, bolts, gears, cranes, seesaws, bridges, joints, and human movement. It is central to mechanics wherever rotation or balance matters.

In engineering, torque analysis helps determine whether structures will tip, rotate, fail, or remain stable under load.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is considering only the force and ignoring the distance from the axis. Another is measuring the wrong distance. The lever arm must be perpendicular to the line of action of the force.

Students also sometimes forget that the axis of rotation must be specified. Torque is always calculated about a chosen point or axis.

Connection to Rotational Motion

Torque is to rotational motion what force is to linear motion. Net force causes linear acceleration, while net torque causes angular acceleration.

This connection leads into rotational dynamics, angular momentum, rotational energy, and equilibrium analysis.

Why This Matters in Physics

Torque introduces the mechanics of turning, balance, and rotational change. It expands physics beyond objects that only slide or move in straight lines.

Understanding torque is necessary for rotational motion, equilibrium, machines, structural stability, and many real-world mechanical systems.

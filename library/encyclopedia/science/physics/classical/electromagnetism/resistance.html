Resistance

Resistance is opposition to electric current, affecting how much current flows for a given voltage.

Overview

Resistance describes how strongly a material or circuit element opposes electric current. Higher resistance reduces current for the same voltage, while lower resistance allows more current. Resistance is central to circuits, heating, electronics, power transmission, and electrical safety.

Opposition to Current

Resistance limits the rate of charge flow. It converts some electrical energy into thermal energy as charges interact with atoms and structure inside the material.

Ohms

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm. A resistance of one ohm allows one ampere of current when one volt is applied, under ideal Ohm's law conditions.

Material Effects

Resistance depends on material. Metals generally have low resistance, while insulators have very high resistance. Semiconductors can be controlled to behave between these extremes.

Length and Area

A longer wire has greater resistance, while a thicker wire has lower resistance, assuming the same material and temperature.

Temperature Effects

Resistance often changes with temperature. In many metals, resistance increases as temperature rises.

Resistors

A resistor is a circuit component designed to provide a specific resistance. Resistors control current, divide voltage, protect components, and shape circuit behavior.

Energy Dissipation

Resistance often transfers electrical energy into thermal energy. This effect is useful in heaters but can be wasteful or damaging in power lines and electronics.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is thinking resistance destroys current. Resistance limits current and transfers electrical energy into other forms, but charge is conserved.

Why This Matters in Physics

Resistance prepares students for Ohm's law, circuits, power, electronics, safety, heating, and material behavior.

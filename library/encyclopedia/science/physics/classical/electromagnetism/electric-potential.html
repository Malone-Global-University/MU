Electric Potential

Electric potential describes electric potential energy per unit charge at a location in an electric field.

Overview

Electric potential is a way to describe how much electric potential energy a charge would have at a location per unit charge. It is closely related to voltage. Electric potential helps simplify electrical problems by focusing on energy per charge rather than force on each individual charge.

Energy per Charge

Electric potential is electric potential energy divided by charge. It describes the energy condition of a location in an electric field.

Potential Difference

A potential difference exists when two points have different electric potentials. Charges can gain or lose energy when moving between those points.

Connection to Voltage

Voltage is electric potential difference. Batteries, outlets, and power supplies create potential differences that can drive current through circuits.

Reference Point

Electric potential is measured relative to a chosen reference point. The zero point can be selected for convenience, while potential differences are usually what matter physically.

Fields and Potential

Electric fields point in the direction that positive charge would be pushed. Electric potential changes across space according to the field arrangement.

Positive and Negative Charges

Positive and negative charges respond differently to electric potential. A positive charge tends to move toward lower potential energy, while a negative charge responds in the opposite charge-dependent way.

Equipotential Surfaces

Equipotential surfaces are regions where electric potential is the same. Moving charge along an equipotential surface requires no change in electric potential energy.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is confusing electric potential with electric potential energy. Potential is energy per charge; potential energy depends on the actual charge present.

Why This Matters in Physics

Electric potential prepares students for voltage, circuits, capacitance, batteries, electrical energy, and field-based reasoning.

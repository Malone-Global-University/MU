Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum is the full range of electromagnetic radiation, from radio waves to gamma rays.

Overview

The electromagnetic spectrum includes all types of electromagnetic radiation, arranged by wavelength, frequency, and energy. Visible light is only a small part of this spectrum. Other regions include radio waves, microwaves, infrared, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

Electromagnetic Radiation

Electromagnetic radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that can travel through vacuum. It transfers energy and can interact with matter in different ways depending on frequency and wavelength.

Radio Waves

Radio waves have long wavelengths and low frequencies compared with visible light. They are used in broadcasting, communication, radar, navigation, and astronomy.

Microwaves

Microwaves are used in cooking, radar, satellite communication, wireless networks, and scientific instruments. They have shorter wavelengths than many radio waves.

Infrared

Infrared radiation is associated with thermal emission from many objects. It is used in remote controls, thermal imaging, heating, astronomy, and sensing.

Visible Light

Visible light is the small portion of the spectrum detectable by human eyes. Different visible wavelengths are perceived as different colors.

Ultraviolet

Ultraviolet radiation has higher frequency than visible light and can cause chemical changes, fluorescence, and biological effects such as sunburn.

X-Rays and Gamma Rays

X-rays and gamma rays have very high frequencies and energies. They are used in medical imaging, materials analysis, astronomy, and nuclear science, but can also pose radiation hazards.

Frequency, Wavelength, and Energy

Across the electromagnetic spectrum, higher frequency corresponds to shorter wavelength and greater photon energy. Lower frequency corresponds to longer wavelength and lower photon energy.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is thinking visible light is separate from electromagnetic radiation. Visible light is one region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Why This Matters in Physics

The electromagnetic spectrum connects optics, radiation, communication, astronomy, medicine, imaging, energy transfer, climate, and modern technology.

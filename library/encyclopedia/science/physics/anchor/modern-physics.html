Modern Physics

Overview

Modern physics is the branch of physics that developed when classical physics could no longer explain certain observations. It includes relativity, quantum theory, atomic physics, nuclear physics, particle physics, and the study of matter and energy at extreme speeds, small scales, or high energies.

Modern physics does not erase classical physics. Instead, it explains where classical models work, where they fail, and what deeper models are needed. Newtonian mechanics remains useful for ordinary objects, but relativity becomes necessary near the speed of light, and quantum theory becomes necessary at atomic and subatomic scales.

Limits of Classical Physics

Classical physics struggled to explain blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, atomic spectra, the stability of atoms, and the behavior of light and matter at small scales. It also assumed time and space were absolute in ways that failed when objects approached the speed of light.

These failures were productive. They forced physicists to replace familiar assumptions with models based on quantized energy, wave-particle behavior, probability, spacetime, and the relationship between mass and energy.

Relativity

Relativity examines motion, space, time, gravity, and energy under conditions where classical assumptions are insufficient. Special relativity deals with objects moving at constant velocity, especially near the speed of light. It introduces time dilation, length contraction, and mass-energy equivalence. General relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

Relativity matters for high-speed particles, astrophysics, cosmology, gravitational waves, black holes, and precision technologies such as GPS.

Quantum Theory

Quantum theory describes matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales. It introduces quantized energy levels, wave functions, probability, uncertainty, photons, matter waves, and the idea that measurement affects what can be known about a system.

Quantum theory explains atomic spectra, chemical bonding, semiconductors, lasers, nuclear behavior, and many technologies that define the modern world. It also challenges everyday intuition because small-scale systems do not behave like tiny versions of ordinary objects.

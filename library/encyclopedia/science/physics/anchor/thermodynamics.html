Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics studies heat, temperature, energy transfer, work, engines, entropy, phase changes, and the large-scale behavior of matter.

Overview

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that studies heat, temperature, work, energy transfer, and the behavior of matter when many particles act together. Instead of tracking every atom or molecule one at a time, thermodynamics describes large-scale properties such as pressure, volume, temperature, internal energy, and entropy.

This branch explains engines, refrigerators, weather systems, body temperature, cooking, insulation, combustion, phase changes, power plants, climate processes, and the limits of energy conversion. It is one of the most practical branches of physics because every real machine and every living system must obey energy and heat-transfer constraints.

Core Quantities

Temperature measures the thermal condition of a system and relates to the average kinetic energy of particles in ordinary matter. Heat is energy transferred because of a temperature difference. Work is energy transferred by a force acting through a distance or by pressure changing volume. Internal energy is the microscopic energy stored in the particles of a system.

Pressure, volume, and temperature are especially important for gases and fluids. A gas can expand, compress, warm, cool, do work, or receive work. Thermodynamics organizes these changes without requiring direct knowledge of every molecular collision.

Laws of Thermodynamics

The laws of thermodynamics establish the rules that govern energy and heat. The zeroth law supports the idea of thermal equilibrium and temperature measurement. The first law is conservation of energy applied to heat and work. The second law describes the direction of natural processes and introduces entropy. The third law concerns the behavior of systems as temperature approaches absolute zero.

These laws are not merely formulas. They are constraints on what can happen. They explain why heat naturally flows from hot to cold, why no engine can convert all input heat into useful work, and why real processes produce waste heat and irreversibility.

Applications

Thermodynamics is used in mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, meteorology, materials science, biology, environmental science, and energy systems. Engines convert heat into work. Refrigerators and heat pumps move thermal energy against its natural direction by using work. Buildings use insulation and ventilation to manage heat transfer. Climate systems involve radiation, convection, evaporation, condensation, and energy balance.

Thermodynamics also connects physics to everyday experience. Boiling water, melting ice, sweating, cooking food, inflating tires, cooling electronics, and operating engines all involve thermodynamic ideas.

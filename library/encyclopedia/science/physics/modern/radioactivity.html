Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous decay of unstable nuclei, releasing particles or electromagnetic radiation.

Overview

Radioactivity occurs when unstable atomic nuclei transform into more stable configurations by emitting radiation. Common types include alpha decay, beta decay, and gamma emission. Radioactivity is natural, measurable, and important in medicine, energy, geology, astronomy, and environmental monitoring.

Unstable Nuclei

A nucleus is unstable when its arrangement of protons and neutrons has excess energy or an unfavorable balance. Decay moves the nucleus toward a more stable state.

Alpha Decay

Alpha decay emits a helium nucleus consisting of two protons and two neutrons. It changes the atomic number and mass number of the parent nucleus.

Beta Decay

Beta decay involves transformation between neutrons and protons, emitting beta particles and associated neutrinos or antineutrinos.

Gamma Emission

Gamma emission releases high-energy electromagnetic radiation from an excited nucleus without changing proton or neutron number.

Half-Life

Half-life is the time required for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is a statistical property of a radioactive isotope.

Radiation Safety

Radiation effects depend on type, energy, exposure time, shielding, distance, and biological context. Safety practices reduce unnecessary exposure.

Applications

Radioactivity is used in medical imaging, cancer therapy, smoke detectors, radiometric dating, sterilization, tracing, energy, and scientific research.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is thinking all radiation is equally dangerous. Different radiation types have different penetration, ionization, and biological effects.

Why This Matters in Physics

Radioactivity connects nuclear structure to real processes in medicine, energy, geology, environmental science, and astrophysics.

Quantum Theory

Quantum theory describes physical behavior at atomic and subatomic scales, where energy, matter, and measurement follow rules unlike everyday classical mechanics.

Overview

Quantum theory is the framework used to describe atoms, electrons, photons, nuclei, and microscopic systems. It introduced quantized energy, wave-particle duality, probability, uncertainty, superposition, and measurement effects.

Quantization

Quantization means certain physical quantities occur in discrete allowed amounts rather than any continuous value. Atomic energy levels are a central example.

Wave-Particle Duality

Quantum objects can show both wave-like and particle-like behavior depending on the experiment.

Probability

Quantum theory often predicts probabilities rather than definite classical paths. A wavefunction contains information about possible measurement outcomes.

Superposition

Superposition means a quantum system can be described as a combination of possible states before measurement.

Uncertainty

The uncertainty principle places limits on how precisely certain pairs of quantities, such as position and momentum, can be known at the same time.

Measurement

Measurement plays a special role because observing a system can affect the state being measured.

Atomic Structure

Quantum theory explains orbitals, spectra, chemical bonding, electron behavior, and the stability of matter.

Technology

Quantum theory supports lasers, semiconductors, transistors, LEDs, solar cells, MRI, atomic clocks, and quantum information science.

Common Mistakes

A common mistake is treating quantum uncertainty as ordinary measurement error rather than a fundamental feature of quantum systems.

Why This Matters in Physics

Quantum theory is essential for modern physics, chemistry, electronics, materials, light, atoms, nuclei, and emerging technologies.

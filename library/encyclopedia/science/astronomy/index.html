Astronomy Overview

Astronomy studies the objects, systems, motions, origins, and large-scale structure of the universe. This discipline examines planets, moons, stars, galaxies, nebulae, black holes, cosmic radiation, and the physical laws that shape celestial behavior. It includes celestial mechanics, cosmology, observational astronomy, planetary science, stellar astronomy, and telescopes and instrumentation, each of which provides a different way to understand the universe through measurement, theory, and observation. By studying astronomy, learners gain a framework for understanding how gravity organizes space, how stars and planetary systems form, how scientists observe distant objects, and how evidence from light, motion, and matter reveals the history and structure of the cosmos.

Categories

  • Celestial Mechanics

    Study the motion of planets, moons, satellites, comets, and other celestial bodies through gravity, orbits, perturbations, trajectories, and mathematical models of motion.

  • Cosmology

    Explore the origin, expansion, structure, and fate of the universe, including the Big Bang, cosmic background radiation, dark matter, dark energy, and large-scale cosmic evolution.

  • Observational Astronomy

    Learn how astronomers gather and interpret data from light, radiation, images, spectra, surveys, and time-based observations to study distant celestial objects and events.

  • Planetary Science

    Examine planets, moons, asteroids, comets, rings, atmospheres, surfaces, interiors, formation processes, habitability, and comparative features of planetary systems.

  • Stellar Astronomy

    Study stars, including their formation, structure, classification, energy production, life cycles, spectra, clusters, supernovae, neutron stars, and stellar remnants.

  • Telescopes and Instrumentation

    Explore the tools used to observe the universe, including optical telescopes, radio telescopes, space observatories, detectors, spectrographs, imaging systems, and measurement technologies.

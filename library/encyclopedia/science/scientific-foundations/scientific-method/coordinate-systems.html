Coordinate Systems

Coordinate systems provide reference frames for describing position, direction, motion, fields, and spatial relationships in physics.

Overview

A coordinate system is a structured way to describe location and direction. Physics uses coordinate systems to turn spatial relationships into measurable and calculable quantities.

Without a coordinate system, statements about position and motion can remain vague. With a coordinate system, a location can be described using numbers, axes, signs, and units.

Reference Frames

A reference frame is the perspective or system from which motion and position are described. The same object can have different measured motion depending on the chosen reference frame.

For example, a person sitting on a moving train may be at rest relative to the train but moving relative to the ground. Physics requires the reference frame to be clear.

Cartesian Coordinates

Cartesian coordinates use perpendicular axes, commonly labeled x, y, and sometimes z. They are widely used because they work well for straight-line motion, rectangular geometry, and component-based vector analysis.

In one dimension, position may be described along a single number line. In two dimensions, position is described with ordered pairs. In three dimensions, position is described with ordered triples.

Sign Conventions

A sign convention defines which directions count as positive and negative. For example, right may be positive and left negative, or upward may be positive and downward negative.

The choice of sign convention is flexible, but it must be used consistently. Many physics mistakes come from changing sign conventions in the middle of a problem.

Polar and Other Coordinate Systems

Some problems are easier in coordinate systems other than Cartesian coordinates. Polar coordinates use distance from an origin and an angle from a reference direction. They are useful for rotation, circular motion, and radial patterns.

The best coordinate system depends on the structure of the problem. A good choice can simplify the mathematics and make the physical pattern easier to see.

Connection to Vectors

Coordinate systems are essential for vector work. They allow vectors to be described in terms of components along axes. A force, velocity, or displacement can then be handled with clear numerical direction.

Once axes are chosen, vector addition, subtraction, and resolution become more systematic.

Rakim

MC and lyricist widely regarded as one of the most influential figures in hip-hop history

Biography

Rakim, born William Michael Griffin Jr. in Long Island, New York, is widely considered one of the greatest MCs in hip-hop history. He rose to prominence in the mid-1980s as part of the duo Eric B. & Rakim, where his lyrical approach fundamentally reshaped rap music. His style introduced a more fluid, introspective, and technically complex approach to MCing.

Before his breakthrough, Rakim developed a unique writing style influenced by jazz phrasing and rhythmic experimentation. Unlike earlier MCs who relied heavily on simpler rhyme patterns, he introduced internal rhymes, multi-syllabic structures, and a calm, controlled delivery that emphasized precision over volume.

His debut collaboration with Eric B., Paid in Full (1987), is regarded as a turning point in hip-hop history. Songs such as “Eric B. Is President” and “I Ain’t No Joke” demonstrated a new level of lyrical sophistication and helped redefine what MCing could be. The album’s influence extended across every major evolution of hip-hop that followed.

Rakim continued his artistic development on albums such as Follow the Leader, Let the Rhythm Hit ’Em, and Don’t Sweat the Technique. Each project refined his signature approach, combining philosophical themes, street narratives, and intricate rhythmic structures. His work consistently elevated lyrical standards within the genre.

Legacy

Rakim is widely credited with transforming hip-hop lyricism into a more advanced and literary art form. His influence can be heard in nearly every generation of MCs who followed, particularly those focused on technical skill and complex rhyme construction.

He is often described as a foundational architect of modern rap flow, shaping the way lyrics interact with rhythm and production. His contributions remain central to discussions of excellence in hip-hop performance and writing.

Cultural Impact

Beyond his recordings, Rakim’s influence extends into education, analysis, and artistic study of hip-hop as a literary form. His approach helped elevate rap into a respected lyrical discipline and continues to influence artists, producers, and scholars. He remains one of the most studied and respected figures in the history of the genre.

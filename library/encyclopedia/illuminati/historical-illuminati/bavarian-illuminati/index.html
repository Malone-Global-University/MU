Evidence Note

This article concerns the documented Bavarian Illuminati as a historical organization. Later claims about a continuing Illuminati, global control, celebrity symbolism, or hidden world government belong to modern conspiracy traditions and should be evaluated separately from the eighteenth-century historical record.

Overview

The Bavarian Illuminati was a short-lived secret society founded in Bavaria in 1776 during the European Enlightenment. Unlike the modern myths that imagine the Illuminati as an ancient or all-powerful organization, the historical order was a specific eighteenth-century group with a known founder, a documented membership network, and a limited period of activity. Its members were interested in education, moral reform, rational inquiry, and the reshaping of society according to Enlightenment ideals. The group operated secretly because Bavaria was a conservative Catholic state where open criticism of church authority, absolutist politics, and traditional social hierarchy could attract serious punishment.

The order began under the leadership of Adam Weishaupt, a professor of canon law at the University of Ingolstadt. Weishaupt wanted to create an organization that could cultivate independent thinking, oppose superstition, and train members for positions of influence in public life. The Illuminati borrowed some methods from older fraternal and initiatory traditions, including ranks, symbolic names, selective recruitment, and internal instruction. These structures helped the group protect itself, but they also later made it easier for opponents to portray the order as dangerous, deceptive, and politically subversive.

Historically, the Bavarian Illuminati is important because it sits at the intersection of Enlightenment reform, secret-society culture, religious authority, and state power. Its rise shows how intellectual movements could spread through private networks when public institutions were tightly controlled. Its suppression shows how governments and church authorities responded when they believed private associations threatened political stability or religious order. Although the original organization was banned in the 1780s and did not survive as a functioning institution, its name became detached from its historical setting and entered a much larger world of rumor, fear, conspiracy literature, and popular mythology.

For encyclopedia study, the Bavarian Illuminati should be approached in two separate ways. First, it should be studied as a real historical organization shaped by the politics and intellectual culture of late eighteenth-century Bavaria. Second, it should be studied as a symbol that later writers used to explain revolution, social change, elite influence, and hidden power. Keeping those two layers separate is essential. The historical Illuminati belongs to the world of Enlightenment Europe, while the modern “Illuminati” often belongs to the world of conspiracy theory, political imagination, and symbolic interpretation.

What Was the Historical Illuminati?

The historical Illuminati was the Bavarian Illuminati, an eighteenth-century secret society founded on May 1, 1776, in Ingolstadt, Bavaria. It was not an ancient order, a supernatural organization, or a hidden world government. It was a real but relatively small association that emerged within the intellectual world of the European Enlightenment. Its members believed that human society could be improved through reason, education, moral discipline, and criticism of inherited authority. In its original setting, the word “Illuminati” referred to people who saw themselves as enlightened, instructed, or intellectually awakened, not to the vast modern mythology that later grew around the name.

The order was founded by Adam Weishaupt, a professor at the University of Ingolstadt who was dissatisfied with the religious and political restrictions of his society. Bavaria at the time was strongly Catholic and socially conservative, and many institutions of education and government were still influenced by church authority, aristocratic privilege, and traditional hierarchy. Weishaupt wanted to build a disciplined network of educated men who could promote rational thought and reform from within the existing social order. The group’s secrecy was partly practical: open opposition to established religious and political powers could be dangerous, so private organization offered protection.

The Illuminati used a structured system of recruitment, ranks, instruction, and internal supervision. Members often used symbolic names, and the order presented itself as a path of intellectual and moral development. Its structure resembled other fraternal societies of the period, especially Freemasonry, but its goals were more directly tied to Enlightenment reform. Members were encouraged to study, refine their character, question superstition, and prepare themselves to influence society. The organization sought capable recruits from educated circles, including students, professors, civil servants, writers, and members of existing fraternal lodges.

At its height, the Bavarian Illuminati spread beyond its original circle and gained members in several German-speaking regions. It attracted both supporters and enemies because it operated privately while discussing ideas that could be seen as politically and religiously disruptive. Critics accused the order of undermining monarchy, church authority, and public order. Bavarian authorities eventually banned the group in the 1780s, and its papers, internal correspondence, and organizational plans became evidence used against it. These records helped prove that the order existed, but they also fueled exaggerated claims about its power and intentions.

The historical Illuminati ended as an organized movement within a relatively short period, but its reputation outlived the organization itself. After the French Revolution, conservative writers linked the Illuminati to revolutionary upheaval and used it as an explanation for dramatic political change. Over time, the name “Illuminati” became less a reference to the actual Bavarian society and more a flexible symbol for hidden influence, elite control, and secret coordination. Understanding the historical Illuminati therefore requires separating the documented eighteenth-century order from the later legends that transformed its name into one of the most recognizable symbols in conspiracy culture.

Enlightenment Bavaria and the Intellectual Climate

The Bavarian Illuminati emerged during the European Enlightenment, a period when educated writers, philosophers, scientists, and reformers questioned inherited authority and argued that human society could be improved through reason. Enlightenment thinkers emphasized education, natural law, critical inquiry, religious toleration, administrative reform, and the spread of useful knowledge. These ideas did not affect every region of Europe in the same way. In some places, rulers encouraged reform while still preserving monarchy and social hierarchy. In others, Enlightenment thought circulated through universities, salons, reading societies, Masonic lodges, and private correspondence rather than through open political institutions.

Bavaria presented a difficult environment for reform-minded intellectuals. It was a Catholic territory with strong ties between religion, education, censorship, and state authority. Public criticism of church power, aristocratic privilege, or absolutist government could be viewed as dangerous or disloyal. Even after the suppression of the Jesuit order in the 1770s, Catholic intellectual life remained deeply shaped by older institutions and habits of authority. Reformers who wanted broader education, freer inquiry, and more rational administration often had to operate carefully inside a society that did not fully welcome public challenges to established power.

This tension helps explain why private associations became important in the intellectual life of the period. Secret and semi-secret organizations offered educated men a place to discuss ideas, exchange books, form alliances, and imagine reform outside the official channels of church and state. Freemasonry, reading circles, scholarly societies, and political clubs all helped create networks of communication that crossed local boundaries. These groups were not all revolutionary, and many were loyal to existing governments, but they created spaces where members could practice forms of equality, debate, and self-improvement that were less available in ordinary public life.

The Illuminati grew out of this atmosphere of restricted reform. Its founder and early members believed that ignorance, superstition, and unexamined obedience prevented society from becoming more rational and humane. Their secrecy was not simply theatrical; it reflected the limits of open speech in their world. At the same time, secrecy created its own risks. A private organization that recruited members, used internal ranks, and discussed moral and political reform could easily be interpreted as a threat by rulers who feared disorder, religious dissent, or revolutionary ideas. The same conditions that made secrecy attractive also made it suspicious.

Enlightenment Bavaria was therefore a setting of intellectual ambition and political constraint. Reformers wanted education, reason, and moral progress, but they lived under institutions that guarded tradition and social order. The Bavarian Illuminati was one response to that contradiction. It attempted to create a hidden school of reform-minded individuals who could influence society gradually from within. Whether viewed as idealistic, manipulative, naïve, or subversive, the order cannot be understood apart from the broader intellectual climate that produced it: a world where reason was rising in prestige, but authority still controlled many of the places where reason could be spoken aloud.

Adam Weishaupt and the Founding of the Order

Adam Weishaupt was the central figure behind the creation of the Bavarian Illuminati. Born in 1748, he became a professor of canon law at the University of Ingolstadt, an institution shaped by Catholic scholarship, clerical influence, and the intellectual tensions of the Enlightenment. Weishaupt was educated within the structures of the society he later criticized, which gave him both insider knowledge and a strong sense of frustration. He believed that education should free the mind from superstition, blind obedience, and inherited prejudice, but he lived in a world where religious and political institutions still placed limits on public discussion.

Weishaupt founded the order on May 1, 1776, in Ingolstadt. The organization was first called the Order of the Perfectibilists, a name that reflected the belief that human beings could be morally and intellectually improved through disciplined instruction. The name later became associated with illumination, enlightenment, and the cultivation of reason. From the beginning, the order was designed as more than a casual discussion group. Weishaupt imagined it as a structured school of character formation, intellectual development, and social reform. Members would be selected carefully, trained gradually, and guided toward a broader vision of human improvement.

The founding of the order reflected Weishaupt’s dissatisfaction with the options available to reform-minded thinkers in Bavaria. Universities were not fully free spaces for intellectual experimentation, and public politics offered little room for open opposition. Existing fraternal societies, especially Masonic lodges, provided models of secrecy, ritual, and selective membership, but Weishaupt wanted an organization more directly focused on rational education and reformist influence. His solution was to create a private network that could shape individuals from within and then allow those individuals to influence institutions through their ordinary social and professional roles.

Weishaupt’s plan relied heavily on discipline and supervision. He believed that reform required more than abstract ideas; it required the steady formation of people who could think clearly, control their passions, resist corruption, and act strategically. The order therefore used ranks, internal reports, assigned readings, moral instruction, and symbolic identities. These methods were meant to create loyalty and intellectual progress, but they also made the organization vulnerable to criticism. To supporters, the system looked like a serious method for educating reformers. To enemies, it looked like manipulation, secrecy, and hidden political ambition.

The early Illuminati began with a small circle of students and associates, but Weishaupt hoped it would grow into a wider network of educated men committed to Enlightenment principles. Its founding was both idealistic and strategic. It was idealistic because it imagined society becoming more rational, moral, and free through education. It was strategic because it accepted that direct public confrontation with authority might fail or provoke repression. The order’s origins therefore reveal the central tension that followed it throughout its existence: it claimed to serve enlightenment and human improvement, but it pursued those goals through secrecy, hierarchy, and concealed organization.

Goals, Beliefs, and Reformist Aims

The goals of the Bavarian Illuminati were shaped by Enlightenment confidence in reason, education, and moral improvement. The order believed that human beings could become wiser and more virtuous if they were freed from ignorance, fear, superstition, and unthinking obedience. Its members did not simply want private self-improvement; they imagined a broader reform of society through the gradual training of capable individuals. In this sense, the Illuminati was both educational and political. It sought to form people who could think independently, act ethically, and eventually influence public life from positions of learning, administration, and leadership.

One of the order’s central beliefs was that inherited authority should be examined rather than accepted automatically. This did not always mean immediate revolution or open rebellion. Instead, the order emphasized intellectual discipline, critical judgment, and the slow replacement of irrational customs with more rational forms of social life. Members were encouraged to question systems that preserved privilege, censorship, religious intolerance, and arbitrary power. The Illuminati’s reformist vision was rooted in the idea that society could be improved when educated people learned to judge institutions by reason, justice, and human welfare rather than by tradition alone.

The order also placed strong emphasis on moral formation. Weishaupt and his associates believed that social reform required disciplined individuals, not merely clever thinkers. Members were expected to examine their own habits, control selfish impulses, cultivate honesty, and become useful to others. This moral emphasis gave the organization a self-consciously educational character. The Illuminati wanted to create people who could serve as examples of enlightened conduct. Its ideal member was not only informed, but also steady, discreet, purposeful, and committed to the improvement of human society.

At the same time, the Illuminati’s methods created a serious tension between its ideals and its practice. The order claimed to oppose manipulation, ignorance, and unjust domination, yet it used secrecy, hierarchy, selective disclosure, and internal supervision to guide its members. Leaders often revealed information gradually, and lower-ranking members did not always know the full structure or intentions of the organization. Supporters might describe this as careful education in a hostile environment. Critics could describe it as contradiction: a movement devoted to enlightenment using methods that depended on concealment and control.

The reformist aims of the Bavarian Illuminati should therefore be understood as ambitious but limited by the conditions of its time. It wanted to advance reason, weaken superstition, improve education, reduce unjust authority, and prepare enlightened individuals to influence society. It did not possess the global reach imagined in later conspiracy theories, but it did represent a real attempt to organize reform through private networks. Its beliefs reflected the hope that society could be changed by changing the minds, morals, and loyalties of those who would one day shape its institutions.

Organization, Recruitment, and Internal Structure

The Bavarian Illuminati was organized as a selective and hierarchical society rather than an open public association. Its structure reflected Adam Weishaupt’s belief that reform required discipline, secrecy, and gradual instruction. Members were not meant to receive the full plan of the order all at once. Instead, they moved through levels of membership, learned ideas in stages, and were expected to prove their character before receiving deeper responsibilities. This layered design helped leaders control information, protect the group from outside opposition, and shape members according to the order’s educational and reformist goals.

Recruitment was cautious and targeted. The order sought men who were educated, ambitious, intelligent, and socially positioned to influence institutions over time. Students, professors, civil servants, writers, professionals, and members of existing fraternal networks were especially attractive recruits. The Illuminati did not simply look for people who already agreed with every part of its program. It looked for individuals who could be formed, guided, and connected to a larger reform project. A recruit’s personality, social contacts, beliefs, and usefulness to the organization all mattered.

Because the order operated in a politically and religiously sensitive environment, secrecy was central to its internal life. Members often used symbolic names, and communication could be controlled through trusted intermediaries. The use of aliases helped create a sense of identity inside the group while also protecting members from exposure. The order’s private language, records, and ranks gave it the atmosphere of an initiatory school. Members were made to feel that they were entering a disciplined community of enlightenment, not merely joining a debating club or social society.

The internal structure also depended on observation and reporting. Members could be asked to reflect on their own conduct, describe other potential recruits, and provide information useful to the order’s leaders. This practice fit Weishaupt’s educational theory: he believed people had to be studied before they could be properly guided. However, it also contributed to later suspicion. What the order saw as moral supervision and careful instruction could appear to outsiders as surveillance, manipulation, and secret political management. This tension became one of the main reasons the Illuminati was feared by its opponents.

The Illuminati’s hierarchy gave the order coherence, but it also created internal problems. A system based on secrecy and selective disclosure can produce mistrust even among members. Lower-ranking members might not know the full intentions of higher-ranking leaders, while leaders had to worry about loyalty, discipline, and exposure. As the group expanded, it became harder to control information and maintain unity. The same structure that helped the Illuminati grow also made it vulnerable when authorities began investigating secret societies in Bavaria.

Overall, the order’s organization and recruitment strategy reveal its basic character. It was not a mass movement seeking public support, nor was it the all-powerful institution imagined by later mythology. It was a private network that tried to educate and coordinate carefully selected members within elite and educated circles. Its internal structure combined Enlightenment ideals with the tools of secrecy, hierarchy, and fraternal discipline. That combination made it effective enough to attract attention, but controversial enough to provoke fear and eventual suppression.

Relationship with Freemasonry

The Bavarian Illuminati developed in a world where Freemasonry was already an important part of educated social life. Masonic lodges offered private spaces where men could gather across lines of rank, profession, and local identity. They used ritual, symbolism, degrees, moral instruction, and selective membership to create a sense of disciplined fraternity. For reform-minded thinkers, lodges could provide access to educated networks beyond the ordinary control of church, university, or state institutions. This made Freemasonry attractive to people interested in Enlightenment ideas, even though Masonic lodges varied widely in belief, purpose, and political attitude.

Adam Weishaupt initially viewed Freemasonry as both a model and an opportunity. The Illuminati borrowed certain organizational features from fraternal culture, including initiation, secrecy, ranks, symbolic names, and the idea of moral advancement through stages. However, Weishaupt did not simply want to create another Masonic lodge. He wanted a more directed organization with a clearer reformist program. Where many Masonic settings emphasized fellowship, symbolism, charitable conduct, and personal moral improvement, the Illuminati aimed to shape members into agents of broader intellectual and social change.

As the Illuminati expanded, Freemasonry became a major recruitment channel. Existing lodges gathered exactly the kinds of people the order wanted: educated, socially connected men accustomed to private association and symbolic discipline. Illuminati organizers could enter Masonic circles, identify promising individuals, and draw them into the order’s more specialized structure. This overlap did not mean that all Freemasons were Illuminati, or that Freemasonry as a whole was controlled by the Illuminati. It means that the Illuminati used parts of the Masonic world as a network through which it could grow.

The relationship between the two movements was therefore complex. The Illuminati depended on Masonic culture for methods, contacts, and legitimacy, but it also tried to work within and beyond Masonic institutions for its own purposes. Some members belonged to both groups, while others would have seen them as distinct organizations with different aims. Freemasonry was broader, older, and more varied. The Illuminati was narrower, younger, and more ideologically focused. Confusing the two can lead to serious historical misunderstanding, especially because later conspiracy writers often blended them together into a single imagined system of hidden control.

This overlap also increased suspicion. Authorities and critics who already distrusted secret societies could point to the connection between Illuminati members and Masonic lodges as evidence of a wider hidden network. The use of degrees, oaths, private meetings, and symbolic language made both groups easy targets for accusation. When the Bavarian government moved against secret societies, the Illuminati’s activity inside Masonic circles helped create the impression that private associations were politically dangerous. In this way, Freemasonry helped the Illuminati expand, but the connection also made the order more visible and more vulnerable.

The historical relationship should be understood without exaggeration. Freemasonry was not identical to the Bavarian Illuminati, and the Illuminati was not proof that all Masonic activity was political conspiracy. Their connection was real, but specific. The Illuminati drew from Masonic forms, recruited through Masonic networks, and competed for influence within some fraternal environments. That relationship became one of the reasons the order mattered historically, and one of the reasons its reputation grew far beyond its actual size after its suppression.

Suppression and Decline

The decline of the Bavarian Illuminati was closely tied to the political and religious anxieties of late eighteenth-century Bavaria. The order had grown through private recruitment, internal ranks, symbolic identities, and connections with Masonic circles, all of which made it suspicious to authorities who feared secret organization outside the control of church and state. Even if the Illuminati saw itself as a reformist and educational movement, its opponents viewed it as a hidden association capable of undermining religious loyalty, political obedience, and public order. In a society where censorship and official supervision were still powerful, secrecy itself could be treated as evidence of danger.

Bavarian authorities began moving against secret societies in the 1780s. Official pressure increased as reports about the Illuminati circulated and as critics accused the order of hostility toward religion, monarchy, and traditional authority. Government edicts restricted or banned unauthorized secret associations, placing the Illuminati in a position where it could no longer operate safely. Members faced investigation, exposure, loss of position, and public suspicion. The order’s private records and correspondence became especially damaging once they entered official hands, because they revealed internal methods that opponents could interpret as proof of manipulation and subversion.

The suppression weakened the Illuminati from both outside and inside. From the outside, the state made continued organization dangerous. From the inside, secrecy, rivalry, and disagreement had already created strain. A hierarchical group that depends on trust can become unstable when members fear betrayal, exposure, or punishment. As pressure increased, the order lost the protection that secrecy had once provided. Networks that had helped it expand now became routes through which information could spread to enemies and investigators.

Adam Weishaupt himself was forced from his position and eventually left Bavaria. His personal downfall symbolized the wider collapse of the order’s original project. Without secure leadership, protected meeting spaces, and confidence among members, the Illuminati could not maintain itself as an organized institution. By the late 1780s, the Bavarian Illuminati had effectively ceased to function as the structured society Weishaupt had founded. Its historical life was brief, lasting only about a decade from its founding in 1776 to its suppression and disintegration in the 1780s.

The decline of the order did not end the influence of its name. In fact, suppression helped create the later mythology. Once the Illuminati was exposed, banned, and discussed in hostile writings, it became easier for later authors to imagine it as more powerful than it had been. Documents that showed a real secret society were used to support claims about vast hidden conspiracies. The disappearance of the actual organization left behind a name, a set of fears, and a symbolic role that could be reused in political arguments for generations.

The suppression and decline of the Bavarian Illuminati therefore mark the dividing line between history and legend. Historically, the order was a reformist secret society that attracted enough attention to alarm Bavarian authorities and provoke state action. Mythologically, its destruction helped transform it into a symbol of hidden power that seemed to survive precisely because it could no longer be easily observed. The real order declined under pressure from government repression, internal weakness, and public scandal, but its imagined afterlife became far larger than the organization itself had ever been.

Historical Sources and Evidence

The historical Bavarian Illuminati is not known only through rumor or legend. It is documented through surviving writings, government records, internal papers, correspondence, hostile publications, and later historical scholarship. These sources show that the order was a real organization founded in eighteenth-century Bavaria, with identifiable leaders, members, goals, methods, and conflicts. At the same time, the evidence must be handled carefully because many sources about the Illuminati were produced during moments of fear, investigation, political controversy, or ideological opposition.

Some of the most important evidence comes from the order’s own records and writings. Internal documents reveal how the organization described its aims, structured its membership, communicated with members, and imagined its educational mission. These materials are valuable because they bring historians closer to how the Illuminati understood itself. They also show the order’s use of ranks, symbolic names, recruitment practices, moral instruction, and secrecy. However, internal documents do not automatically provide a complete or neutral picture. Like many organizational records, they reflect ambition, strategy, self-presentation, and sometimes disagreement among members.

Government records are another major source of evidence. Bavarian authorities investigated and suppressed the order, and official actions against secret societies created a paper trail. Edicts, confiscated documents, reports, and legal or administrative materials help explain why the state considered the Illuminati dangerous. These records are useful because they show the conflict between private reformist networks and public authority. They also reveal how officials interpreted secrecy as a political problem. Still, government sources must be read with caution because they were often shaped by the assumptions and fears of authorities who wanted to justify repression.

Hostile writings and anti-Illuminati publications also shaped the historical record. Critics of the order accused it of attacking religion, monarchy, family life, and social stability. After the French Revolution, some writers treated the Illuminati as a hidden cause of revolutionary upheaval, giving the order a role far larger than the evidence can support. These works are important because they explain how the Illuminati’s reputation grew, but they are not reliable when read as straightforward descriptions of what the organization actually controlled. They often reveal more about the fears of the period than about the real reach of the Bavarian Illuminati.

Modern historians compare these different kinds of evidence against one another. They separate internal records from state accusations, distinguish documented membership from speculative claims, and place the order within the wider world of Enlightenment reform and fraternal societies. This method helps prevent two opposite errors. One error is to deny the historical order altogether, as if the Illuminati were purely fictional. The other is to accept later conspiracy claims as if they were proven history. The evidence supports the existence of a real, secretive, reform-minded organization, but it does not support the idea of an all-powerful hidden government directing world events.

For encyclopedia purposes, historical sources and evidence should be used to keep the subject grounded. The Bavarian Illuminati can be studied through documents, dates, people, institutional conflicts, and political context. Its mythology can also be studied, but as a later development in the history of ideas, fear, and conspiracy literature. The strongest approach is to ask what each source can actually prove, who produced it, why it was written, and how later readers used it. That distinction allows the Bavarian Illuminati to be treated seriously as history without repeating the exaggerations that made the name famous.

Legacy and Modern Mythology

The legacy of the Bavarian Illuminati is much larger than the historical organization itself. As a functioning order, it existed for only a short period in the late eighteenth century and was limited mainly to educated circles in parts of German-speaking Europe. Its actual influence was tied to Enlightenment reform, secret-society culture, and conflict with Bavarian authorities. Its later reputation, however, expanded far beyond that setting. After the order was suppressed, the name “Illuminati” became available as a symbol for hidden power, secret planning, and unseen coordination behind public events.

This transformation was strengthened by the political shock of the French Revolution. Many conservative writers in Europe struggled to explain how monarchy, church privilege, and traditional social order could be challenged so dramatically. Some argued that revolution was not only the result of economic pressure, political conflict, social inequality, or Enlightenment ideas, but also the work of secret societies. In this atmosphere, the Illuminati became a convenient explanation for social upheaval. The real Bavarian order had already been weakened or destroyed, but its secrecy made it useful to writers who wanted to imagine a hidden cause behind visible change.

Over time, the Illuminati shifted from a specific historical organization into a flexible myth. In conspiracy literature, the name could be attached to many different fears: revolution, banking power, elite control, media influence, religious decline, global government, or cultural manipulation. The details changed from one writer to another, but the basic pattern remained the same. Public events were interpreted as surface effects of a hidden group acting behind the scenes. The historical Illuminati became less important than the idea of the Illuminati as a permanent invisible authority.

Modern popular culture has carried this mythology even further. The Illuminati appears in novels, films, music commentary, internet memes, political claims, and symbolic speculation. Images such as pyramids, eyes, hand signs, and coded numbers are often treated as supposed evidence of hidden affiliation, even when those symbols have broader artistic, religious, commercial, or historical meanings. In this modern setting, “Illuminati” often functions less as a reference to Adam Weishaupt’s organization and more as a general label for suspicion toward elites, institutions, celebrity culture, and centralized power.

The persistence of the mythology shows how powerful hidden-cause explanations can be. When social events feel confusing, threatening, or too large to understand, conspiracy narratives can offer a simple structure: someone planned it, someone controls it, and someone benefits from it. This does not mean that powerful institutions never coordinate, conceal information, or act in self-interest. It means that claims about secret control require evidence, proportion, and historical discipline. Without those standards, the real history of the Bavarian Illuminati disappears beneath speculation.

The most useful way to understand the Illuminati’s legacy is to separate historical influence from symbolic afterlife. The historical order belonged to Enlightenment Bavaria and can be studied through documents, people, dates, and institutions. The modern mythology belongs to the history of political fear, popular imagination, distrust of authority, and conspiracy culture. Both are worth studying, but they are not the same subject. The Bavarian Illuminati was a real organization; the modern “Illuminati” is a much larger cultural idea built from fragments of history, exaggeration, symbolism, and suspicion.

Political Economy Overview

Political economy studies how political institutions, public authority, markets, firms, social groups, and economic policies shape one another. This discipline examines how governments regulate markets, how economic interests influence political decisions, how public budgets allocate resources, how trade rules affect national development, and how institutions shape incentives, growth, inequality, and stability. It includes state-market relations, development policy, trade policy, fiscal governance, and institutional economics, each of which connects economic behavior to political power and public decision-making. By studying political economy, learners gain a framework for understanding how laws, institutions, markets, budgets, industries, and social priorities interact within domestic and global systems.

Categories

  • Development Policy

    Study policies used to promote economic growth, poverty reduction, infrastructure, education, industrialization, institutional capacity, public welfare, and long-term national development.

  • Fiscal Governance

    Examine how governments raise revenue, spend public funds, manage budgets, issue debt, design taxes, allocate resources, and maintain fiscal responsibility.

  • Institutional Economics

    Explore how laws, norms, property rights, organizations, enforcement systems, political rules, and institutional arrangements shape economic behavior and market outcomes.

  • State-Market Relations

    Learn how governments and markets interact through regulation, privatization, public ownership, subsidies, competition policy, welfare systems, industrial policy, and economic coordination.

  • Trade Policy

    Study how governments manage international trade through tariffs, quotas, agreements, sanctions, export controls, trade negotiations, supply chains, and economic diplomacy.

Geometry Overview

Geometry studies the properties, measurements, positions, and relationships of figures in space. It includes Euclidean geometry, coordinate geometry, solid geometry, transformations, and geometric proof, each of which develops a different way to reason about shape, size, location, symmetry, and spatial structure. This discipline is foundational because geometric reasoning appears in architecture, engineering, design, art, physics, mapping, computer graphics, robotics, and everyday measurement. By studying geometry, learners gain tools for understanding how figures are defined, how spatial relationships are proven, how algebra can describe geometric objects, how transformations change or preserve shapes, and how three-dimensional forms can be measured and analyzed.

Categories

  • Coordinate Geometry

    Study how algebra and geometry connect through coordinate planes, graphs, distance, midpoint, slope, equations of lines, circles, curves, and spatial relationships.

  • Euclidean Geometry

    Examine classical geometry involving points, lines, angles, triangles, polygons, circles, congruence, similarity, parallel lines, and logical reasoning from axioms and theorems.

  • Geometric Proof

    Learn how geometric conclusions are justified through definitions, postulates, theorems, diagrams, logical steps, congruence arguments, similarity arguments, and deductive reasoning.

  • Solid Geometry

    Explore three-dimensional figures, including prisms, cylinders, pyramids, cones, spheres, volume, surface area, cross-sections, spatial relationships, and measurement in space.

  • Transformations

    Study how figures move or change through translations, rotations, reflections, dilations, symmetry, congruence, similarity, and coordinate-based transformations.

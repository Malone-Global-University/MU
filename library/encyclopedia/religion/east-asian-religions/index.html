East Asian Religions Overview

East Asian religions studies the religious, philosophical, ritual, and cultural traditions that developed across China, Japan, Korea, and surrounding regions. This discipline includes Confucianism, Taoism, Shinto, and folk religions, each of which shaped ideas about family, ancestors, moral cultivation, cosmic order, nature, governance, ritual life, and everyday spiritual practice. East Asian religious traditions often interact with one another rather than existing as completely separate systems, with communities drawing from multiple traditions for ethics, ceremony, cosmology, healing, ancestor veneration, and social identity. By studying East Asian religions, learners gain a framework for understanding how religion can operate through philosophy, ritual, household practice, state tradition, local custom, and reverence for harmony between human life, society, and the natural world.

Categories

  • Confucianism

    Study Confucian teachings on virtue, family, ritual propriety, education, social responsibility, governance, moral cultivation, hierarchy, filial piety, and ethical relationships.

  • Folk Religions

    Explore local religious practices involving ancestors, spirits, household rituals, festivals, divination, healing, temples, protective deities, regional customs, and everyday spiritual life.

  • Shinto

    Examine Japanese religious traditions centered on kami, shrines, purification, seasonal festivals, ritual practice, sacred places, imperial associations, and reverence for nature.

  • Taoism

    Learn about Taoist philosophy and religion, including the Tao, natural harmony, non-forcing action, cosmology, ritual practice, immortality traditions, meditation, and sacred texts.

Religious Foundations Overview

Religious foundations introduces the major concepts, questions, methods, and interpretive frameworks used to study religion as a field of knowledge. This discipline examines how religious traditions understand ultimate reality, revelation, belief, sacred texts, divine authority, ritual meaning, moral obligation, spiritual experience, and the relationship between faith and reason. It includes philosophy of religion, theology, comparative religion, religious epistemology, and sacred text studies, each of which provides a different way to analyze religious ideas and practices. By studying religious foundations, learners gain a framework for understanding religion not only as belief or worship, but as a complex human and intellectual system involving doctrine, interpretation, tradition, experience, community, and claims about truth.

Categories

  • Comparative Religion

    Study similarities and differences among religious traditions, including beliefs, rituals, sacred texts, ethical systems, institutions, symbols, practices, and historical development.

  • Philosophy of Religion

    Explore philosophical questions about God, ultimate reality, faith, reason, evil, miracles, religious experience, divine attributes, immortality, and arguments for or against religious belief.

  • Religious Epistemology

    Examine how religious knowledge and belief are justified, including revelation, faith, testimony, experience, reason, tradition, authority, doubt, certainty, and forms of spiritual understanding.

  • Sacred Text Studies

    Learn how sacred writings are interpreted, transmitted, translated, canonized, studied, and applied within religious communities, scholarly traditions, and historical contexts.

  • Theology

    Study systematic reflection on divine reality, doctrine, revelation, creation, salvation, worship, human nature, religious authority, and the meaning of faith within religious traditions.

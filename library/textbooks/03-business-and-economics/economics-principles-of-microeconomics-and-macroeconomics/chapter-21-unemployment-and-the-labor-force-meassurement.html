Chapter 21 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every month, economists and policymakers around the world watch a single number closely: the unemployment rate. When that number rises, it signals that fewer people are finding work, that households are under strain, and that the economy may be weakening. When it falls, it suggests that labor markets are tightening, that firms are hiring, and that income is flowing to more workers and families.

But what does "unemployed" actually mean? Is a person unemployed if they stopped looking for work last week? What about someone who works ten hours a week but wants forty? What about a software engineer who lost a job in Chicago but cannot afford to move to a city where her skills are in demand?

These questions matter enormously — not just for economists, but for anyone trying to understand what is actually happening to working people in an economy. The unemployment rate is one of the most important and most carefully watched statistics in macroeconomics. It is also one of the most frequently misunderstood.

Opening Question

This chapter teaches you how economists define and measure unemployment, what the major types of unemployment are, what the labor force participation rate measures, and what the official statistics leave out. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to read unemployment data critically, classify types of unemployment accurately, and understand why even a good labor market measure is still an incomplete picture of human economic experience.

Section 21.1

Why Labor Market Measurement Matters

The labor market is where workers offer their time, skills, and effort and where firms and organizations hire that labor to produce goods and services. For most households, wages and salaries from employment are the primary source of income. Employment provides not only income but also structure, purpose, social connection, and a sense of contribution for many people.

When the labor market functions well — when people who want jobs can find them at wages that reflect their skills and productivity — households are more stable, firms can produce more, and economic growth tends to be stronger. When the labor market struggles, households face serious hardship, and the economy often weakens.

This is why economists, governments, central banks, and policymakers pay close attention to labor market data. Employment and unemployment statistics are used to monitor the health of the economy, identify recessions and recoveries, guide monetary and fiscal policy decisions, evaluate education and training programs, and understand the distributional effects of economic change on different groups.

Labor market data are among the most politically sensitive economic statistics. Rising unemployment often signals distress for voters, workers, and communities. Falling unemployment is often cited as evidence of economic success. Understanding how these statistics are constructed — and what they include and exclude — is essential for interpreting them accurately.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics

In the United States, labor market data are produced primarily by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), an agency of the federal government. The most important regular release is the Employment Situation Summary, published on the first Friday of each month, containing the national unemployment rate, job gains and losses, wage data, and detailed breakdowns by age, race, education, and industry. The International Labour Organization (ILO) helps establish common definitions so that unemployment statistics can be compared across countries.

Section 21.2

Employed, Unemployed, and Not in the Labor Force

To measure unemployment, economists must first define who is counted and who is not. The civilian noninstitutional population is the starting point — all people aged 16 and older who are not in the military and not in an institution such as a prison, hospital, or nursing facility.

Within this population, every person falls into one of three categories.

CategoryDefinitionExample
EmployedDid any paid work during the reference week — even one hour; includes self-employed, unpaid family workers (15+ hrs/week), and those temporarily absentA part-time cashier who worked 6 hours last week
UnemployedDid no paid work, was available to work, and actively looked for work in the past four weeksA laid-off accountant who applied to five firms this month
Not in the labor forceNeither employed nor unemployed by the definitions above; not currently searching for workA full-time student, a retiree, a discouraged former job seeker
Boundary Cases Matter

The requirement that someone must have actively searched for work is critical. A person who would like a job but has not taken steps to find one — sending applications, contacting employers, using job boards — does not meet the official definition of unemployed. A person who worked two hours last week but wanted full-time work is still classified as employed. A person who stopped applying three weeks ago out of frustration is not unemployed — he is not in the labor force. These rules reflect practical decisions about how to define a measurable, consistent labor market status, but they create gaps between the official rate and the fuller picture, explored fully in Section 21.10.

Section 21.3

The Labor Force

The labor force consists of all people who are either employed or unemployed. It does not include people who are not in the labor force.

Formula

Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed

CategoryNumber of People
Employed155,000,000
Unemployed7,000,000
Not in the labor force98,000,000
Total civilian noninstitutional population260,000,000

In this example, the labor force is 155,000,000 + 7,000,000 = 162,000,000.

The size of the labor force changes over time for many reasons: population growth, demographic shifts (such as the aging of a large generation), changes in social norms around work and family, educational enrollment, and wages and economic opportunity. During a recession, some discouraged workers exit the labor force because they stop believing jobs are available. During a strong expansion, previously inactive workers may reenter. These flows affect the unemployment rate in ways that can be counterintuitive, as explored in Section 21.5.

Section 21.4

Labor Force Participation Rate

The labor force participation rate (LFPR) measures the fraction of the civilian noninstitutional population that is in the labor force — either working or actively looking for work.

Formula

LFPR = (Labor Force / Civilian Noninstitutional Population) × 100

Using the numbers from Section 21.3: LFPR = (162,000,000 / 260,000,000) × 100 = 62.3%. This means 62.3 percent of working-age adults are either employed or actively looking for work; the remaining 37.7 percent are not in the labor force.

Example 21.4.1
Why participation rates change the meaning of an unemployment rate
  • SituationCountry A and Country B both report an unemployment rate of 5 percent.
  • Country ALabor force participation rate of 70 percent — most working-age adults are engaged with the labor market in some way.
  • Country BLabor force participation rate of only 55 percent — a much larger share of the population is not participating at all, perhaps due to discouragement, disability, caregiving responsibilities, or early retirement.
  • ConclusionA 5 percent unemployment rate in Country B may represent more widespread labor market weakness than the identical rate in Country A.
Key takeaway: The unemployment rate must always be read alongside the participation rate.
Long-Run Trends

In the United States, women's labor force participation rose dramatically during the second half of the twentieth century. Overall participation peaked in the late 1990s and has generally declined since, driven by an aging population and, after 2000, by the exit of working-age men from the labor force. Young adults (16–24) have lower participation in recent decades partly because more are enrolled in college. Participation also varies significantly by education, race, gender, and region.

Section 21.5

Unemployment Rate

The unemployment rate is the percentage of the labor force that is unemployed.

Formula

Unemployment Rate = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100

Using our continuing example: Unemployment Rate = (7,000,000 / 162,000,000) × 100 = 4.3%.

Example 21.5.1
Calculating LFPR and the unemployment rate together
  • SituationTotal working-age civilian population: 500,000. Employed: 310,000. Actively looking for work: 20,000. Not working and not looking: 170,000.
  • Step 1Labor Force = 310,000 + 20,000 = 330,000
  • Step 2LFPR = (330,000 / 500,000) × 100 = 66.0%
  • Step 3Unemployment Rate = (20,000 / 330,000) × 100 = 6.1%
Key takeaway: Always compute the labor force first; both the LFPR and the unemployment rate depend on it.
Four Things to Remember

The unemployment rate measures a rate, not a total number. The denominator is the labor force, not the total population — so if discouraged workers exit the labor force, the rate can fall even if fewer people are actually employed. Historical context matters: U.S. unemployment has ranged from under 4 percent in strong labor markets to over 14 percent during the 2020 recession. And unemployment affects people unequally — youth, workers without a high school diploma, and certain regions consistently face higher rates than national averages suggest.

Section 21.6

Frictional Unemployment

Even in a healthy, well-functioning economy, some unemployment always exists. Frictional unemployment is the short-term unemployment that occurs because workers are in the process of moving between jobs. It arises because matching workers to jobs takes time — workers must search, apply, interview, and evaluate offers, while employers post positions, review candidates, and make hiring decisions.

ExampleWhy It Is Frictional
Recent college graduate searching for her first jobEntering the labor force for the first time; matching takes time
Developer who voluntarily left to find a better opportunityJob-to-job search; not caused by a downturn or skill mismatch
Worker who relocated to a new cityGeographic re-entry into a new local labor market
Frictional Unemployment Is Not All Bad

Some frictional unemployment is a sign of a healthy, dynamic economy: workers can leave jobs that are a poor fit and search for better ones. An economy with zero frictional unemployment would mean workers never moved between jobs — an economically damaging rigidity. Job search platforms have reduced matching time in many sectors, but matching still takes time, especially for specialized roles. Frictional unemployment is generally short — weeks to a few months — and workers in this category are often not in deep economic distress.

Section 21.7

Structural Unemployment

Structural unemployment occurs when workers' skills, locations, or characteristics do not match the jobs that are available. Unlike frictional unemployment, which is about the time it takes to find a match, structural unemployment reflects a deeper mismatch between the labor force and the structure of labor demand.

SourceDescriptionExample
Technological changeTechnology replaces a category of jobsData-entry clerks replaced by software
Industry declineAn industry shrinks or relocates entirelyCoal miners in a region where mines have closed
Geographic mismatchJobs available in one region, workers concentrated elsewhereA homeowner unable to relocate to where jobs exist
Skill mismatchEmployers require qualifications unemployed workers lackGrowing tech sector requiring coding credentials
Why Structural Unemployment Persists

Structural unemployment tends to last much longer than frictional unemployment. Retraining takes time and money, relocation is difficult and costly, and age discrimination can make it harder for older displaced workers to find new employment. Because it is more persistent and harder to resolve through simple job searching, structural unemployment often requires policy responses: job retraining programs, education support, relocation assistance, and investment in declining regions or industries. This process is part of what economists call creative destruction — old industries decline while new ones rise, with real costs for real workers and communities even when long-run effects on productivity may be positive.

Section 21.8

Cyclical Unemployment

Cyclical unemployment is the unemployment that results from downturns in the business cycle. When the economy contracts — when aggregate demand falls and firms cut production — businesses lay off workers and reduce hiring. This increase in unemployment is directly tied to the overall state of the economy, not to individual skill mismatches or job-switching. It rises during recessions, falls during expansions, and affects workers across many industries and skill levels, often causing sudden and painful income loss.

Because cyclical unemployment is caused by macroeconomic downturns, it is the primary target of stabilization policy — both monetary and fiscal. When unemployment rises due to a recession, central banks may lower interest rates to encourage borrowing and spending, while governments may increase spending or cut taxes to stimulate demand. These policies aim to restore aggregate demand, which in turn restores employment. Monetary policy is studied fully in Chapter 26, fiscal policy in Chapter 27.

Section 21.9

Seasonal Unemployment

Seasonal unemployment occurs when the demand for certain types of labor fluctuates predictably with the seasons of the year. Some industries hire large numbers of workers at certain times of year and far fewer at others — agricultural workers during planting and harvest, ski resort employees in winter, summer camp counselors, holiday retail staff, and construction workers in cold-weather regions.

Seasonal unemployment is highly predictable. Workers and employers generally know in advance that employment will be lower during the off-season, and many seasonal workers structure their finances accordingly.

Why Data Are Seasonally Adjusted

Because seasonal unemployment recurs in consistent patterns, economists and government agencies typically report seasonally adjusted unemployment data, which removes the effects of predictable seasonal swings so the underlying trend is easier to see. A January rise in retail unemployment does not necessarily mean the economy is weakening — it may just be the predictable post-holiday pattern. When you hear monthly unemployment reports in the news, they typically refer to the seasonally adjusted rate.

Section 21.10

Underemployment, Discouraged Workers, and Measurement Limits

The official unemployment rate is an important and carefully constructed measure, but it does not capture all forms of labor market difficulty. Two broad categories fall outside the standard measure: underemployment and discouraged workers.

Underemployment refers to situations in which workers are employed but not in ways that fully use their time, skills, or both. Some workers are employed part-time not because they prefer fewer hours, but because they cannot find full-time work — these workers are counted as employed even though they would prefer more work. Others hold jobs that require significantly less skill, education, or experience than they possess — a form of underemployment that is harder to measure but economically significant.

Discouraged workers want to work and are available to work, but have stopped actively looking because they believe no jobs are available for them. Because they are not actively searching, they do not meet the BLS definition of unemployed — they are classified as not in the labor force, so the official unemployment rate does not count them. During deep recessions, the number of discouraged workers can rise significantly, causing the official rate to understate true labor market weakness.

The BLS also tracks marginally attached workers — people not in the labor force who want a job and have looked for work in the past year but not in the past four weeks. Discouraged workers are a subset of marginally attached workers.

MeasureDescription
U-1Persons unemployed 15 weeks or longer
U-2Job losers and people who completed temporary jobs
U-3Official unemployment rate (unemployed and actively seeking work)
U-4U-3 plus discouraged workers
U-5U-4 plus all marginally attached workers
U-6U-5 plus part-time workers for economic reasons
U-3 vs. U-6

The U-6 measure is sometimes called the "broadest" measure of labor market slack, consistently running several percentage points above U-3. During the Great Recession, U-3 peaked at about 10 percent while U-6 peaked at about 17 percent — a much larger share of the working-age population was experiencing some form of labor market difficulty than the headline number suggested.

Example 21.10.1
Why a falling unemployment rate can mask a weaker labor market
  • SituationA region's unemployment rate falls from 8.0% to 6.5% over six months, which a local news report calls "great news."
  • Closer lookDuring the same period, 5,000 long-term unemployed workers gave up searching and exited the labor force. They are now classified as not in the labor force, not unemployed.
  • Effect on the mathThe labor force shrank, so even with no new jobs created, the unemployment rate's denominator dropped — pushing the reported rate down.
  • ConclusionThe fall in the U-3 rate alone does not prove the labor market improved. A careful analyst would also check the labor force participation rate and broader measures like U-6.
Key takeaway: A single statistic should never be read in isolation.
What Even U-6 Does Not Measure

Even the broadest BLS measures leave some things out: the quality and stability of employment, the precariousness of informal or gig work, geographic and demographic variation masked by national averages, and well-being beyond employment — wages, working conditions, job satisfaction, and economic security. Responsible use of labor market statistics requires understanding both what they measure and what they leave out.

Key Terms Review

Bureau of Labor Statistics
The U.S. federal agency responsible for collecting and publishing labor market data, including the monthly unemployment rate.
civilian noninstitutional population
All people aged 16 and older who are not in the military and not in an institution; the base population for labor force statistics.
employed
A person who performed any paid work during the reference week, including part-time and temporary work.
unemployed
A person who did no paid work, was available for work, and actively searched for work in the past four weeks.
not in the labor force
A classification for people who are neither employed nor unemployed, including retirees, students, caregivers, and discouraged workers.
labor force
The sum of all employed and unemployed persons; the base from which the unemployment rate is calculated.
labor force participation rate
The percentage of the civilian noninstitutional population that is in the labor force.
unemployment rate
The percentage of the labor force that is unemployed.
frictional unemployment
Short-term unemployment from the normal process of job searching and matching; present even in healthy economies.
structural unemployment
Unemployment from a mismatch between worker skills, locations, or characteristics and available jobs; tends to be longer-lasting.
cyclical unemployment
Unemployment caused by downturns in the business cycle; rises in recessions and falls in expansions.
seasonal unemployment
Predictable, recurring unemployment from seasonal changes in labor demand, such as agriculture or tourism.
underemployment
A condition where workers are employed but not to the full extent of their desired hours or skills.
discouraged workers
People who want and are available to work but have stopped searching because they believe no jobs are available; classified as not in the labor force.
marginally attached workers
People not in the labor force who want a job and looked for work in the past year, but not the past four weeks; discouraged workers are a subset.
U-6 measure
The broadest BLS unemployment measure, including the officially unemployed, discouraged and marginally attached workers, and part-time workers for economic reasons.

Real-World Applications

  1. Look up the most recent U-3 and U-6 unemployment rates published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. How large is the gap between the two measures? What might explain the size of that gap at this point in the economic cycle?

Error Analysis

Each statement below contains an economic reasoning error. Identify the error and explain the correct understanding.

  1. "The unemployment rate fell from 7 percent to 6 percent this month. This proves that 1 percent more of the population found jobs."
  2. "If someone is working only 10 hours a week but wants 40, they should be counted as unemployed."
  3. "Frictional unemployment is always bad. The goal of labor policy should be to eliminate it entirely."
  4. "If the labor force participation rate falls, that means more people are working."
  5. "Cyclical unemployment can be solved by retraining workers with new skills."

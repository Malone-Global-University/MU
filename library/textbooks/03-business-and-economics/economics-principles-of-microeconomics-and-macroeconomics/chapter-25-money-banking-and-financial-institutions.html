Chapter 25 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every day, billions of economic transactions take place around the world. A student buys lunch. A factory orders raw materials. A government pays its employees. A family takes out a loan to purchase a home. A business invests in new equipment. Each of these transactions requires something that nearly everyone uses but few people stop to think carefully about: money.

Money is so woven into daily life that it can seem like a natural feature of the world, as obvious as gravity. But money is not a natural resource that exists in the ground or grows on trees. Money is a social institution — a set of agreements and practices that societies have developed, over thousands of years, to make exchange faster, more reliable, and more productive.

This chapter introduces money and the financial system. It asks: What is money, and why does it matter? What do banks actually do? How do financial institutions connect savers with borrowers? What makes a financial system work well, and what makes it fragile?

Opening Question

By the end of this chapter, you will understand that money is not simply wealth — it is a tool that makes the creation of wealth possible. You will understand how banks intermediate between people who have funds and people who need them. You will understand credit, risk, and interest. And you will be prepared to study how central banks and monetary policy shape the macroeconomy — which is the subject of Chapter 26.

Section 25.1

What Money Does

When most people hear the word money, they think of coins and paper bills. But to an economist, money is defined not by its physical form but by what it does — by the economic functions it performs.

To understand what money does, imagine an economy without it. In a pure barter economy, people trade goods and services directly for other goods and services. Barter can work, but it runs into a serious problem: the double coincidence of wants. For a barter trade to happen, each person must want exactly what the other person is offering, at the same moment. As an economy grows more complex, barter becomes increasingly impractical.

Money solves the double coincidence of wants. A seller accepts money for goods, then uses that money to buy whatever they need from someone else entirely. Money breaks the chain of direct exchange into flexible, indirect exchange.

Liquidity

Liquidity is how quickly and easily an asset can be converted into cash or used to make a purchase without significant loss of value. Money is the most liquid of all assets — other assets such as real estate, stocks, and gold may store value well but cannot be spent instantly.

Section 25.2

Medium of Exchange, Unit of Account, and Store of Value

For something to function effectively as a medium of exchange, it should be widely accepted, portable, divisible, durable, recognizable, and difficult to counterfeit. Throughout history, many different things have served this role — shells, beads, salt, livestock, metal coins, paper notes, and now digital entries in computer records. What matters is not the material but whether people trust and accept it.

For money to work well as a unit of account, the value of the currency unit must be reasonably stable over time. Severe inflation destroys this function: in economies with hyperinflation, people sometimes abandon the currency for a more stable foreign currency or for barter.

For money to preserve value as a store of value, it must maintain purchasing power over time. Inflation — even moderate, steady inflation — gradually erodes this function, which is why people often hold other assets such as savings accounts, bonds, or real estate rather than keeping all wealth in cash.

Tradeoffs Among the Functions

Ideal money needs to be liquid (a good medium of exchange), stable in value (a good unit of account), and durable (a good store of value). Real monetary systems balance these demands with varying success — and severe inflation can cause money to fail at several functions simultaneously.

Common Misconception

Students sometimes assume that any rise in a single price is "inflation." Inflation specifically refers to a sustained rise in the overall price level, not just one good becoming more expensive. This distinction connects directly to Chapter 22.

Section 25.3

Commodity Money and Fiat Money

Commodity money is money whose value comes from the material it is made of — gold, silver, or copper coins are classic examples. These forms of money had intrinsic value: even if a society stopped using gold coins as currency, the gold would still be worth something. Commodity money has advantages, since its supply cannot be inflated arbitrarily, but it is heavy, inconvenient, and its supply is tied to how much of the commodity is mined.

An important historical intermediate form is representative money: paper certificates redeemable for a fixed amount of a commodity held in reserve, as under a gold standard.

Fiat money has value because the government declares it legal tender and because people trust and accept it — not because it is made of anything intrinsically valuable. Modern currencies, including the U.S. dollar, the euro, and the yen, are all fiat money. The strength of fiat money is its flexibility; its weakness is that it requires institutional trust.

Section 25.4

Money Supply at an Introductory Level

Economists and policymakers need to measure how much money is circulating in an economy. The most obvious form of money is currency — paper bills and metal coins held by the public — but currency is only part of the money supply. Checking accounts (demand deposits) function like money because balances can be used immediately to pay for goods and services.

M1 is the narrowest measure, including currency, demand deposits, and other highly liquid deposits. M2 is a broader measure, including everything in M1 plus savings account balances, small time deposits, and retail money market mutual fund shares.

Note for Students

You do not need to memorize the precise technical definitions of M1 and M2 for this course. The key concept is that economists measure money along a spectrum from most liquid (currency) to somewhat less liquid (savings accounts), because liquidity determines how quickly money can function in transactions.

Section 25.5

Banks and Deposits

A bank is a financial institution that accepts deposits from savers and uses those funds to make loans to borrowers. Banks sit in the middle of the financial system, connecting savers — people with funds they are not currently using — and borrowers, who need funds for spending or investment.

When you deposit money into a checking or savings account, you are lending money to the bank. Your deposit appears on the bank's balance sheet as a liability — an obligation the bank owes to you, since you can demand it back. What the bank owns — its assets — are the loans it has made, the securities it holds, and the reserves it keeps.

Type of DepositLiquidityTypical Interest
Demand deposits (checking)Withdrawable at any timeNone or very low
Savings accountsWithdrawable, sometimes with restrictionsModerate, variable
Time deposits (CDs)Locked for a set periodHigher, fixed
Common Misconception

Students sometimes assume a deposit is the bank's asset, since the bank is "holding" the money. In fact, a deposit is the bank's liability: the bank owes that money back to the depositor on demand.

Section 25.6

Loans and Interest

A loan is an agreement in which a lender provides funds to a borrower, who agrees to repay the principal plus interest — a fee charged for the use of the funds, typically expressed as a percentage of the principal per year.

Banks charge interest for several reasons: time preference, inflation risk, default risk, and opportunity cost. Banks earn profit in part from the interest rate spread — the difference between the rate charged to borrowers and the rate paid to depositors.

Example 25.6.1
Calculating simple interest
  • SituationYou borrow $10,000 from a bank at an annual interest rate of 5%.
  • InterestInterest = $10,000 × 0.05 = $500
  • Total owedTotal owed = $10,000 + $500 = $10,500
Key takeaway: Interest is the price of using someone else's money for a period of time.
Collateral and Creditworthiness

Banks assess creditworthiness — a borrower's ability and willingness to repay — using income, credit history, collateral, and debt-to-income ratio. Collateral is an asset the lender can seize if the borrower defaults, such as a house securing a mortgage.

Section 25.7

Reserves

Reserves are the portion of deposits banks keep on hand rather than lending out, either as vault cash or as deposits at the central bank. Modern banks practice fractional reserve banking: they keep only a fraction of deposits in reserve and lend out the rest.

This process, called the money multiplier, allows the banking system as a whole to create more total deposits than the original deposit alone, because the same funds cycle through multiple rounds of lending and re-depositing.

Round 1: Deposit $1,000 → Reserve $100 (10%) → Loan $900 Round 2: Deposit $900 → Reserve $90 (10%) → Loan $810 Round 3: Deposit $810 → Reserve $81 (10%) → Loan $729 ... Total new deposits supported = Initial deposit × (1 ÷ reserve ratio) = $1,000 × (1 ÷ 0.10) = $10,000
Bank Runs

Fractional reserve banking works because, on an ordinary day, only a small fraction of depositors withdraw funds simultaneously. But if depositors lose confidence and all try to withdraw at once — a bank run — even a healthy bank can be overwhelmed, because the funds have already been lent out.

Section 25.8

Financial Intermediation

Financial intermediation is the process by which financial institutions channel funds from savers to borrowers. Without intermediaries, savers and borrowers would need to find each other directly and negotiate terms on their own — impractical for most individuals and small businesses.

Financial intermediaries make borrowing and lending efficient by pooling funds, reducing information costs, managing the maturity mismatch between short-term deposits and long-term loans, and diversifying risk across many borrowers.

Real-World Connection

Research has found that countries with deeper, more efficient financial systems — more banks, better credit markets, stronger property rights — tend to grow faster and have higher living standards, a theme developed further in Chapter 28.

Section 25.9

Credit and Risk

Credit is the ability to borrow — to receive goods, services, or money now in exchange for a promise to pay later. Credit allows economic activity to happen sooner than it otherwise could, which is one reason well-functioning credit markets are associated with economic growth.

Financial risk refers to the uncertainty surrounding future returns. Important types include default risk, interest rate risk, liquidity risk, and systemic risk — the risk that problems in one institution spread throughout the financial system.

A fundamental principle of finance is that higher risk is associated with higher expected return: riskier borrowers pay higher interest rates to compensate lenders for the added uncertainty.

Type of RiskDescription
Default riskThe risk a borrower fails to repay a loan
Interest rate riskThe risk that rate changes affect the value of assets and liabilities
Liquidity riskThe risk of being unable to convert assets to cash quickly enough
Systemic riskThe risk that one institution's failure spreads through the system

Section 25.10

Financial Institutions in the Economy

Commercial banks are the most familiar financial institutions, accepting deposits and making loans to households and businesses. Credit unions are member-owned, nonprofit cooperatives offering similar services. Investment banks help corporations and governments raise capital and advise on mergers, rather than taking retail deposits. Mutual funds pool investor money into diversified portfolios, while insurance companies and pension funds are major long-term institutional investors.

The shadow banking system includes intermediaries that perform bank-like functions without traditional bank charters or regulation, which proved a source of significant risk during the 2007–2008 global financial crisis.

Deposit insurance — such as FDIC coverage in the United States — guarantees deposits up to a set limit, preventing bank runs by removing depositors' incentive to withdraw at the first sign of trouble. However, deposit insurance also creates moral hazard, reducing depositors' incentive to monitor whether a bank is taking excessive risks.

Key Concept

Deposit insurance protects individual depositors and prevents bank runs, but creates moral hazard by reducing depositors' incentive to monitor bank behavior. This is why regulation accompanies insurance.

Example Review 1
Reserves and the money multiplier
  • SituationA bank receives a new deposit of $5,000. The required reserve ratio is 20%.
  • Required reserves$5,000 × 0.20 = $1,000
  • Available to lend$5,000 − $1,000 = $4,000
  • Multiplier1 ÷ 0.20 = 5
  • Max new deposits$5,000 × 5 = $25,000
Key takeaway: A lower reserve ratio produces a larger multiplier and greater potential expansion of deposits.

Simple interest on a loan

Real-World Applications

  1. Research a currency that collapsed rapidly (such as Zimbabwe in the 2000s, the Weimar mark in 1923, or the Venezuelan bolívar in the 2010s). What happened to each of money's three functions? What did people use instead? What does this teach about what gives money value?
  2. Look up a local bank or credit union. What is its savings interest rate? Its mortgage rate? Its approximate spread? Does it advertise deposit insurance?
  3. Research credit scores. What factors affect them? How does a lower score affect interest rates paid? What economic concept explains this?
  4. Research microfinance institutions that make very small loans in lower-income countries. How does microfinance serve financial intermediation? What risks might these institutions face that traditional banks in wealthy countries face less acutely?

Mixed Review

Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Most of the markets students encounter in daily life do not look like perfect competition, where dozens of identical sellers compete purely on price. Nor do they look like monopoly, where one firm rules alone. Instead, students find something in between: coffee shops that each claim a distinct atmosphere, smartphone makers that compete fiercely but watch each other's every move, airlines that match fares almost instantly, and restaurant chains that advertise constantly to tell customers why their burger is different.

This chapter studies the two market structures that fill the large middle ground between perfect competition and monopoly: monopolistic competition and oligopoly. Both are common. Both combine elements of competition and market power. But they work quite differently, and understanding each requires different tools.

In monopolistic competition, many firms sell differentiated products. Each firm has a little market power because its product is somewhat unique, but entry is easy and competition keeps profits modest in the long run. In oligopoly, a small number of large firms dominate the market. Each firm must think carefully about what rivals will do, making strategy, interdependence, and the temptation to collude central concerns.

Chapter Goal

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to identify each market structure, explain how firms behave within them, analyze real-world examples, and evaluate what these structures mean for consumers, firms, and public policy.

Section 14.1

Markets Between Competition and Monopoly

Chapters 12 and 13 introduced two extreme models. Perfect competition imagines many sellers offering identical products with free entry and exit, so no single seller has any pricing power. Monopoly imagines one seller protected by high barriers, with full pricing power and no close competitors.

Reality rarely matches either extreme perfectly. Most real markets fall somewhere between these poles. Firms have some power to set prices above cost, but they are not the only sellers. They face competitors, but those competitors sell products that are not quite identical to their own. They can earn profits, but rivals can enter and erode those profits over time.

Economists classify markets using four main criteria:

  1. 1Number of sellers — Are there many, a few, or just one?
  2. 2Product differentiation — Are the products identical or different?
  3. 3Barriers to entry — Is it easy or hard for new firms to enter?
  4. 4Pricing behavior — Do firms take the market price as given, or do they set their own price?

Using these criteria, economists identify two major intermediate market structures. Monopolistic competition has many sellers, differentiated products, low barriers to entry, and some pricing power. Each firm acts somewhat like a monopolist over its own brand but competes with many similar rivals. Oligopoly has few sellers, products that may be identical or differentiated, significant barriers to entry, and strong strategic interdependence. Each firm must think about how rivals will respond to its decisions.

Both monopolistic competition and oligopoly are widespread. Restaurants, hair salons, clothing retailers, and coffee shops are often monopolistically competitive. Airlines, automobile manufacturers, internet service providers, and major technology platforms are often oligopolistic. Understanding where a market falls on this spectrum helps predict firm behavior, prices, output, profit, advertising, innovation, and policy challenges.

Section 14.2

Product Differentiation

The key feature that distinguishes monopolistic competition from perfect competition is product differentiation: the practice of making a product distinct enough that consumers perceive it as meaningfully different from rival products.

In perfect competition, products are identical. A bushel of hard red winter wheat from one farm is indistinguishable from a bushel grown by a neighboring farm. No buyer will pay more for one than the other. The seller has no pricing power. In monopolistic competition, firms work hard to make their products different. A restaurant is not just selling food; it is selling a particular cuisine, atmosphere, location, speed of service, and reputation. Two Italian restaurants in the same city compete with each other, but neither is a perfect substitute for the other in the eyes of every customer.

Product differentiation can take several forms.

FormDescriptionExample
Physical differentiationActual differences in ingredients, materials, design, durability, flavor, or featuresOne laptop has a longer battery life than a rival
Location differentiationOtherwise identical products that vary in convenience by locationA gas station near a highway exit charges a small premium
Quality differentiationOne version of a product is genuinely higher qualityA handmade leather bag versus a mass-produced canvas bag
Perceived differentiationConsumers believe products differ even when physical differences are smallStrong brand loyalty between cola brands despite blind-taste similarity
Service differentiationThe buying, using, or support experience differs even for similar productsOne electronics store offers better customer service and return policy

Product differentiation gives firms in monopolistic competition a downward-sloping demand curve for their own product. Because a firm's product is somewhat unique, it can raise its price without immediately losing all customers — some customers will stay because they prefer this particular product. But the demand curve is still fairly elastic, because many substitutes exist nearby.

This is different from perfect competition, where a firm faces a horizontal demand curve (any price increase sends all customers to identical rivals), and different from monopoly, where the firm faces the full market demand curve (no close substitutes exist at all).

Section 14.3

Branding and Advertising

Branding is the process of creating a recognizable identity for a product or firm. A brand is a name, symbol, design, or combination of these that identifies a seller's product and distinguishes it from rivals. Brands can create loyalty, trust, and willingness to pay a premium.

Advertising is any paid communication designed to inform potential buyers about a product, create demand, and shape perception. Branding and advertising play a much larger role in monopolistic competition and oligopoly than in perfect competition, because in perfect competition products are identical and advertising one firm's wheat over another's would accomplish nothing.

Why Firms Advertise

To inform. Advertising can tell consumers that a product exists, what it costs, where to find it, and what it does — especially valuable for new products or new market entrants.

To persuade. Advertising can create preferences, build associations, and change how consumers perceive a product even when the product itself has not changed.

To signal quality. Spending heavily on advertising can signal that a firm is confident enough in its product to invest in its reputation. A firm with a poor product would lose that bet, which is why heavy advertising can, under some conditions, serve as a credible signal of quality.

Costs and Benefits of Advertising

Advertising informs consumers about choices, facilitates entry by new firms, and can increase competition if it makes consumers aware of alternatives. But advertising is also expensive, and those costs are passed along to consumers. If advertising only creates perceived differentiation where no real difference exists, it may reduce consumer welfare and raise barriers to entry for firms that cannot match established advertising budgets. Whether any particular campaign creates or destroys value is an empirical question that cannot be answered in general.

Branding in Oligopoly

In oligopoly, an advertising campaign by one firm may directly pull customers from rivals, who respond with their own campaigns. The result can be advertising "arms races" in which all firms spend more without any firm gaining lasting advantage — and consumers may not benefit, even though firms might collectively prefer to spend less.

Section 14.4

Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition is a market structure with many sellers, differentiated products, low barriers to entry and exit, and some degree of pricing power for each firm.

Characteristics

FeatureDescription
Many sellersNo single firm dominates; firms attend to general market conditions, not specific rivals
Product differentiationEach firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve because its product is perceived as distinct
Low barriers to entryNew firms can enter relatively easily, especially in the long run
Some pricing powerLimited by the availability of close substitutes

Examples of monopolistically competitive markets include restaurants, coffee shops, hair salons, clothing boutiques, bookstores, nail salons, tutoring services, accounting firms, web designers, food trucks, and many retail services.

Short-Run Behavior

In the short run, a firm in monopolistic competition behaves somewhat like a monopolist. It faces a downward-sloping demand curve for its own product, meaning it can charge a higher price only if it accepts lower quantity, and vice versa. The firm seeks to produce the quantity at which marginal revenue equals marginal cost. Because demand slopes downward, marginal revenue lies below the demand curve, just as in monopoly. The firm then sets its price above marginal cost, which can produce economic profit if demand is strong relative to costs.

Worked Example 14.1
Short-Run Profit in Monopolistic Competition
  • SituationA coffee shop in a busy neighborhood faces: price per cup $5.00; average total cost per cup $3.50; quantity sold per day 200 cups.
  • FormulaProfit = (Price − Average Total Cost) × Quantity
  • CalculationProfit = ($5.00 − $3.50) × 200 = $1.50 × 200
  • ResultProfit = $300 per day
Key takeaway: In the short run, this coffee shop earns positive economic profit.

Long-Run Adjustment: Entry and Zero Profit

Here is where monopolistic competition differs sharply from monopoly. In monopoly, barriers to entry prevent rivals from entering, so monopoly profit persists. In monopolistic competition, barriers are low. If existing firms are earning economic profit, new firms enter. A new coffee shop opens nearby. Another follows. Each new entrant takes some customers from existing firms. The demand curve facing each individual firm shifts left and may become more elastic as customers gain more close substitutes.

This process continues until economic profit is driven to zero. In the long run, firms in monopolistic competition earn only normal profit — enough to keep the firm in the market, but no more.

This long-run equilibrium has an important consequence: the firm operates with excess capacity. In perfect competition, long-run equilibrium occurs where price equals the minimum of average total cost — the most efficient scale of production. In monopolistic competition, the firm produces less than this efficient quantity. Resources are not used as efficiently as in perfect competition.

Whether this inefficiency is worth the product variety it creates is a genuine question. Consumers in monopolistic competition get a wide variety of differentiated products serving different tastes. Some economists argue that this variety is itself valuable, and that the mild inefficiency is a reasonable price for choice.

Non-Price Competition

Because firms in monopolistic competition cannot dramatically undercut rivals on price without triggering responses that erode the advantage, they often compete more intensively on non-price dimensions: quality, service, location, hours, atmosphere, product variety, and convenience. A local coffee shop may not match a chain's price exactly, but it can compete by offering a quieter space, a signature roast, faster service, or loyalty rewards.

Section 14.5

Oligopoly

Oligopoly is a market structure in which a small number of large firms dominate the market. The firms may sell identical products (as in some commodity markets) or differentiated products (as in automobiles or smartphones). The defining feature of oligopoly is not the specific number of firms but the strategic interdependence among them.

Characteristics

FeatureDescription
Few dominant sellersPerhaps two, three, five, or a handful more — each large enough to affect rivals
Significant barriers to entryHigh setup costs, economies of scale, legal protections, network effects, brand loyalty, or control of key resources
Identical or differentiated productsSteel and chemicals are nearly identical; automobiles and smartphones are highly differentiated
Strategic interdependenceEach firm anticipates rivals' reactions before making decisions

Examples of oligopolistic industries include commercial aviation, automobile manufacturing, major internet search engines and social platforms, wireless telecommunications, beer brewing, breakfast cereals, credit card networks, and major streaming services.

Why Oligopolies Form

Economies of scale mean that average cost falls as output rises over a wide range; only large firms can produce at low enough cost to compete profitably. High fixed costs in industries like aerospace, semiconductor manufacturing, or pharmaceutical research require enormous upfront investment that only large firms can spread across many units. Network effects occur when a product becomes more valuable as more people use it, so once a platform reaches critical mass, new entrants face a nearly impossible challenge. Brand loyalty and switching costs can lock consumers into relationships with established firms. Control of essential resources or distribution channels can prevent entry in some industries.

Section 14.6

Interdependence Among Firms

The most important consequence of oligopoly is strategic interdependence: each firm must consider how rivals will respond to its decisions before deciding what to do. In perfect competition or monopolistic competition, no single firm is large enough to affect its rivals meaningfully. In oligopoly, this is not true. If an airline cuts fares on a major route, rival airlines notice immediately. If a smartphone maker introduces a new feature, rivals begin developing responses. If a wireless carrier launches a promotional plan, competitors react within days.

Price changes carry risk. If a firm raises its price, rivals may not follow, leaving the price-raising firm with fewer customers. If a firm cuts its price, rivals may match the cut, eliminating the advantage and pushing everyone's profits lower. This asymmetry can make prices in oligopoly surprisingly stable, even when costs change.

Capacity decisions are strategic. Building a new factory signals commitment to high output. If a firm credibly commits to producing a large quantity, rivals may retreat. But if all firms expand simultaneously, prices fall and all suffer.

Product and innovation choices are interdependent. The decision to develop a new product depends partly on what rivals are developing. If one firm introduces a major improvement, rivals must respond or lose market share.

The Kinked Demand Curve (Conceptual)

One informal way economists explain price stability in oligopoly is the idea of a kinked demand curve. If a firm raises its price above the going market price, rivals will not follow (they gain customers by staying put), so the firm loses sales sharply. But if a firm cuts its price, rivals will match the cut to avoid losing customers, so the price-cutting firm gains few new customers.

This asymmetry — rivals ignore price increases but match price cuts — means the demand curve facing an oligopolist has a "kink" at the current price. Above the current price, demand is elastic. Below the current price, demand is inelastic. As a result, firms have little incentive to change prices in either direction, which helps explain observed price stickiness in many oligopolistic markets.

Note for Students

The kinked demand curve is a useful intuition, but economists debate how well it explains actual pricing behavior. It is best understood as a conceptual tool rather than a complete theory.

Section 14.7

Game Theory at an Introductory Level

Game theory is the study of strategic decision-making — situations where the outcome of a decision depends not only on what you do but on what others do in response. In economics, game theory is particularly useful for analyzing oligopoly.

The Prisoner's Dilemma

The most famous game in introductory economics is the prisoner's dilemma, which illustrates how individually rational choices can produce collectively poor outcomes. The classic version involves two suspects questioned separately. Each can confess (defect) or stay silent (cooperate).

Suspect B: Stay SilentSuspect B: Confess
Suspect A: Stay SilentA gets 1 year, B gets 1 yearA gets 10 years, B goes free
Suspect A: ConfessA goes free, B gets 10 yearsA gets 5 years, B gets 5 years

Each suspect reasons: if B stays silent, I do better by confessing (go free vs. 1 year). If B confesses, I do better by confessing (5 years vs. 10 years). So I should confess regardless of what B does. Both suspects reach this conclusion. Both confess. Both serve 5 years — a worse outcome for both than if they had both stayed silent (1 year each). Individual rationality produced collective irrationality.

The Oligopoly Version: Price or Production

The prisoner's dilemma has a direct application to oligopoly. Replace "confess" with "compete aggressively" (cut prices or expand output) and "stay silent" with "cooperate" (hold prices high or restrict output). Consider two airlines, Alpha and Beta, serving the same route.

Beta: High PriceBeta: Low Price
Alpha: High PriceAlpha $400K, Beta $400KAlpha $100K, Beta $550K
Alpha: Low PriceAlpha $550K, Beta $100KAlpha $250K, Beta $250K

If both airlines set high prices, total industry profit is $800K, split evenly. But each airline has an incentive to defect: if Beta holds a high price, Alpha can earn $550K by cutting prices instead of $400K. Alpha cuts. Beta, facing the same incentives, also cuts. Both end up earning $250K — far less than the $400K each could have earned by cooperating. Cooperation produces the best joint outcome, but individual incentives push both firms toward competition, producing a worse outcome for both.

Dominant Strategies and Nash Equilibrium

A dominant strategy is a choice that produces the best outcome for a player regardless of what the other player does. In the examples above, confessing (or cutting prices) is a dominant strategy for both players. When both players have dominant strategies and those strategies together produce an equilibrium, that equilibrium is called a Nash equilibrium — named for mathematician John Nash. In a Nash equilibrium, no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy alone.

In the airline example, both setting low prices is a Nash equilibrium: Alpha cannot improve by switching to high prices while Beta is low, and Beta cannot improve by switching to high prices while Alpha is low.

Repeated Games and Cooperation

The prisoner's dilemma assumes the game is played once. Real oligopolies play repeatedly — firms interact with the same rivals year after year. In a repeated game, firms can adopt strategies that reward cooperation and punish defection. The classic example is tit-for-tat: start by cooperating, then in each subsequent round, do whatever your rival did last round.

Tit-for-tat and similar strategies can sustain cooperation in repeated games, because defection today triggers punishment tomorrow. The long-run cost of a price war can exceed the short-run gain from undercutting, making cooperation rational. This is why oligopolistic markets sometimes maintain high prices without explicit agreements.

However, this form of tacit cooperation breaks down when firms expect the relationship to end soon, when market conditions change dramatically, when a new entrant disrupts established patterns, or when one firm faces a desperate short-run need for revenue.

Section 14.8

Collusion and Cartels

When the repeated game logic of cooperation takes an explicit form, it becomes collusion: a formal or informal agreement among firms to limit competition, typically by fixing prices, dividing markets, or restricting output. A cartel is a group of producers who formally coordinate their behavior to act as a collective monopolist.

The most famous international cartel is OPEC (the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries), which coordinates the oil production levels of member nations. When OPEC successfully restricts output, oil prices rise and member nations earn higher revenues. When member discipline breaks down and nations exceed their quotas, prices fall.

Why Cartels Are Unstable

Cartels face a fundamental problem: each member has an incentive to cheat. If all cartel members are holding prices high and restricting output, any individual member can earn more by secretly producing slightly more than its quota and selling at just below the cartel price. This defection is profitable for the cheater — but if all members defect, the cartel collapses and prices return to competitive levels.

Cartel stability requires monitoring (can cheating be detected?), enforcement (can cheaters be punished?), and limited temptation (is the gain from cheating smaller than the benefit of continued cooperation?). These conditions are difficult to sustain in practice. Many cartels collapse: members defect, disagreements arise over quotas, new producers outside the cartel expand output, or demand conditions shift.

Collusion and the Law

In most market economies, collusion is illegal. In the United States, the Sherman Antitrust Act prohibits agreements among firms to fix prices, divide markets, or restrict competition. Similar laws exist in the European Union, Canada, and many other countries. Firms found guilty of price-fixing face large fines, civil damages, and in some jurisdictions criminal prosecution of executives. The prohibition reflects the economic logic that cartels harm consumers and create deadweight loss — output that consumers would have valued more than it cost to produce goes unproduced.

Section 14.9

Price Leadership and Nonprice Competition

Not all coordination in oligopoly requires illegal collusion. Two legal phenomena help explain how oligopolistic markets sometimes maintain orderly behavior without explicit agreement.

Price Leadership

Price leadership occurs when one firm in an industry — typically the dominant or largest firm — sets a price that rivals then follow. No explicit agreement is necessary. Rivals simply observe that the leader has changed its price and adjust their own prices accordingly. When a major airline announces a fare increase, watch for whether rivals match within 24 to 48 hours. If they do, the fare increase holds. If rivals do not match, the first airline often reverses the increase.

Price leadership is not illegal, but antitrust regulators watch for patterns that suggest it has crossed into explicit coordination. The line between following market conditions and illegal signaling can be difficult to draw in practice.

Nonprice Competition

Because price competition in oligopoly can trigger ruinous price wars, firms often prefer nonprice competition: competing on dimensions other than price.

FormDescription
Product quality and featuresSafety, fuel efficiency, camera quality, processing speed, battery life
Advertising and brandingBuilding brand loyalty so strong that consumers keep buying even at a higher price
Customer service and experienceService quality, loyalty programs, customer experience
Innovation and new productsDelivering valued innovation before rivals can

Nonprice competition is rational from the firm's perspective: it builds loyalty and differentiation that reduces price sensitivity, without triggering the immediate retaliation that price cuts provoke. But it can also be costly. Advertising budgets, research investment, and service quality all cost money, and consumers ultimately bear those costs through higher prices.

Section 14.10

Antitrust and Market Structure Policy

When markets are monopolistically competitive, government intervention is rarely considered necessary. Entry is easy, profits are competed away in the long run, and consumers enjoy variety. The main concern — excess capacity and mildly higher prices than perfect competition — is generally accepted as the cost of differentiation.

Oligopoly raises more significant policy concerns. The combination of market power, barriers to entry, and potential for coordination or collusion can lead to prices well above competitive levels, reduced output, diminished innovation, and harm to consumers.

Antitrust Policy

Antitrust policy refers to laws and enforcement actions designed to prevent monopoly, punish anticompetitive behavior, and preserve competitive market structures. The primary antitrust laws in the United States include:

LawWhat It Does
Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)Prohibits contracts, combinations, and conspiracies in restraint of trade (Section 1) and monopolization (Section 2)
Clayton Antitrust Act (1914)Addresses specific practices such as price discrimination, exclusive dealing, and mergers that substantially lessen competition
Federal Trade Commission Act (1914)Created the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to enforce antitrust laws and prevent unfair methods of competition

Antitrust enforcement involves several types of action. Price-fixing prosecution targets firms caught agreeing to set prices, leading to large fines and potential criminal prosecution. Merger review occurs when large firms propose to merge; agencies such as the FTC and Department of Justice assess whether the merger would substantially reduce competition, and may block it or require divestitures. Abuse of dominant position can occur even without collusion, when a firm with monopoly or near-monopoly power uses that power to crush competitors through predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, or tying arrangements.

Market Concentration Measures

Market share is the percentage of total market sales controlled by each firm. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) is calculated by summing the squares of each firm's market share percentages. A market with one firm (share = 100%) has an HHI of 10,000. A market with ten equal firms (each 10%) has an HHI of 1,000. The U.S. Department of Justice considers markets with HHI below 1,500 unconcentrated, between 1,500 and 2,500 moderately concentrated, and above 2,500 highly concentrated. Mergers that significantly increase the HHI in an already concentrated market receive close scrutiny.

Worked Example 14.2
Calculating Market Concentration
  • SituationA regional broadband market has four providers: A (45%), B (30%), C (15%), D (10%).
  • FormulaHHI = sum of (market share)² for every firm
  • CalculationHHI = 45² + 30² + 15² + 10² = 2,025 + 900 + 225 + 100
  • ResultHHI = 3,250
Key takeaway: This market is highly concentrated by Department of Justice guidelines. A merger between Provider A and Provider B would raise the HHI dramatically and would likely receive close regulatory scrutiny.

Debates in Antitrust Policy

Antitrust policy involves genuine economic debates. Some economists argue that large firms are often large because they are efficient, and that breaking them up may reduce efficiency without benefiting consumers. Others argue that concentrated markets produce lasting harm to consumers and innovation, and that regulators have been too permissive — particularly in technology markets where network effects create very durable dominance.

A key tension in antitrust analysis is between static efficiency (are prices low and output high today?) and dynamic efficiency (is the market producing innovation and improvement over time?). A large firm might maintain high prices today while funding research that produces major innovations tomorrow. Or it might suppress innovation to protect its existing position. Determining which is happening requires careful investigation.

Modern antitrust debates also grapple with digital platforms that may offer services at zero monetary price to consumers while competing intensely for advertising revenue and data. Traditional antitrust tools that focus on price effects may not fully capture the competitive dynamics of these markets.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 14.1Markets fall along a spectrum defined by number of sellers, product differentiation, barriers to entry, and pricing behavior. Monopolistic competition and oligopoly are the two intermediate structures between perfect competition and monopoly.
  • Sec 14.2Product differentiation — physical, location, quality, perceived, or service — gives firms in monopolistic competition a downward-sloping demand curve and limited pricing power.
  • Sec 14.3Firms advertise to inform, persuade, or signal quality. Advertising can create or destroy consumer value depending on the case, and plays a larger strategic role in oligopoly.
  • Sec 14.4In monopolistic competition, firms can earn economic profit in the short run, but low barriers to entry drive profit to zero in the long run, leaving firms with excess capacity.
  • Sec 14.5Oligopoly features few dominant firms, high barriers to entry, and strategic interdependence. Economies of scale, fixed costs, network effects, and brand loyalty all encourage oligopoly to form.
  • Sec 14.6Strategic interdependence means each firm's decisions depend on anticipated rival responses. The kinked demand curve offers an intuitive explanation for price stability in oligopoly.
  • Sec 14.7Game theory, especially the prisoner's dilemma, explains why individually rational firm decisions can produce worse joint outcomes. Repeated games allow strategies like tit-for-tat to sustain cooperation.
  • Sec 14.8Collusion and cartels attempt explicit coordination to restrict output and raise prices, but they are inherently unstable due to the incentive to cheat, and illegal in most jurisdictions.
  • Sec 14.9Price leadership and nonprice competition allow firms to avoid destructive price wars without engaging in illegal collusion.
  • Sec 14.10Antitrust policy — built on the Sherman Act, Clayton Act, and FTC Act — uses tools like the HHI to evaluate market concentration and weighs static against dynamic efficiency in deciding how aggressively to intervene.

Key Terms

monopolistic competition
A market structure with many sellers, differentiated products, low barriers to entry, and some pricing power for each firm.
oligopoly
A market structure dominated by a small number of large firms, with significant barriers to entry and strategic interdependence among firms.
product differentiation
The practice of making a product distinct from rivals through physical characteristics, quality, location, service, branding, or perceived differences.
branding
Creating a recognizable identity for a product or firm that distinguishes it from competitors.
advertising
Paid communication designed to inform, persuade, or signal quality to potential buyers.
excess capacity
In monopolistic competition, the condition in which firms produce less than the output that minimizes average total cost in long-run equilibrium.
nonprice competition
Competing on dimensions other than price, such as quality, advertising, service, features, or innovation.
strategic interdependence
The condition in oligopoly where each firm's best decision depends on what rival firms do.
game theory
The study of strategic decision-making in situations where outcomes depend on the choices of multiple parties.
prisoner's dilemma
A game in which individually rational choices lead to an outcome worse for all players than an alternative they could have reached through cooperation.
dominant strategy
A choice that produces the best outcome for a player regardless of what other players choose.
Nash equilibrium
An outcome in which no player can improve their result by changing their own strategy, given the strategies of other players.
collusion
A formal or informal agreement among firms to limit competition, typically by fixing prices, dividing markets, or restricting output.
cartel
A group of producers who formally coordinate their behavior to act collectively as a monopolist.
price leadership
A pattern in which one dominant firm sets a price that rivals then match, without formal agreement.
antitrust policy
Laws and enforcement actions designed to prevent monopoly, punish anticompetitive behavior, and preserve competitive market structures.
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)
A measure of market concentration calculated by summing the squares of each firm's market share percentages. Higher values indicate greater concentration.

Game Theory Scenario Practice

For each scenario: identify the strategic situation, determine whether a dominant strategy exists, predict the likely outcome, and explain what would need to change to produce a cooperative outcome.

Scenario 1
Advertising Arms Race

Two competing fast food chains, Burger One and Burger Two, each must decide whether to run a major advertising campaign this quarter. If both advertise, each spends $10 million on advertising and earns $30 million in profit (net of advertising costs). If neither advertises, each earns $35 million. If only one advertises, the advertiser earns $45 million and the non-advertiser earns $15 million.

  1. Build the payoff table for this game.
  2. Does either firm have a dominant strategy?
  3. What is the Nash equilibrium?
  4. Is the Nash equilibrium the best joint outcome? Explain.
  5. What would you need to know to predict whether tacit cooperation is possible?
Scenario 2
Production Quota

Two oil producers, NordPetro and SunOil, each decide independently how much oil to produce. If both restrict output to 1 million barrels per day, the oil price is high and each earns $200 million per year. If both produce 2 million barrels per day, the price falls and each earns $120 million. If one restricts and one produces at full capacity, the restricting firm earns $80 million and the full-capacity firm earns $250 million.

  1. Build the payoff table.
  2. Identify any dominant strategies.
  3. What is the Nash equilibrium?
  4. Explain why cartels are unstable using this example.
  5. If these firms expected to interact for 20 years, how might that change the analysis?

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "Oligopolies always collude."

Not all oligopolists collude, and explicit collusion is illegal in most countries. Many oligopolistic markets are intensely competitive. Airlines engage in fierce price competition on certain routes. Smartphone makers compete intensely on features and quality. Game theory shows that competitive outcomes are entirely possible even when cooperation would make all firms better off.

Misconception 2 — "Advertising always wastes resources."

Advertising can be purely persuasive and socially wasteful, but it can also provide valuable information, signal quality, support the entry of new products, and fund media content consumers value. Whether any particular campaign is welfare-enhancing or wasteful is an empirical question.

Misconception 3 — "Breaking up big companies always helps consumers."

Large firms are sometimes large because they are efficient — they have achieved economies of scale that allow them to produce at lower cost and offer lower prices. Antitrust analysis must weigh the benefits of competition against potential efficiency losses. The goal is competitive markets, not small firms for their own sake.

Misconception 4 — "Game theory predicts that firms will always compete and never cooperate."

In a one-shot prisoner's dilemma, non-cooperation is the Nash equilibrium. But real firms interact repeatedly. In repeated games, cooperation can be sustained through strategies that reward cooperation and punish defection. Game theory predicts an outcome that depends on the specific structure of the game, the time horizon, and what strategies are available.

Misconception 5 — "Monopolistic competition is just another name for monopoly."

In monopoly, one firm faces the full market demand with no close substitutes and high barriers to entry. In monopolistic competition, many firms compete, products are closely substitutable, and entry is easy. Long-run profit is zero in monopolistic competition but can persist in monopoly. The "monopolistic" in monopolistic competition refers only to each firm's modest pricing power over its differentiated product — not to market dominance.

Error Analysis

Each statement below contains an economic error. Identify the error, explain why it is wrong, and correct it.

  1. "In the long run, firms in monopolistic competition earn positive economic profit because they have pricing power over their differentiated products."
  2. "If two firms in an oligopoly have a dominant strategy to compete aggressively, the best outcome for consumers is that they cooperate instead, so regulators should encourage them to do so."
  3. "A cartel can be stabilized if all members agree to produce at the profit-maximizing level, because everyone benefits from high prices."
  4. "Game theory only applies to oligopoly because perfect competitors and monopolists do not need to think strategically."
  5. "Antitrust law is designed to ensure that no company becomes too large, regardless of how it achieved that size or how it treats consumers."

Real-World Applications

  1. Telecommunications. In most countries, three to five major wireless carriers serve the market. Research the current state of your national wireless market and answer:
    1. How many major carriers operate? What are their approximate market shares?
    2. What is the approximate HHI?
    3. Is this market best classified as oligopoly, monopolistic competition, or something else? Justify your answer.
    4. How do carriers in this market compete? Do they primarily compete on price, or on nonprice dimensions?
  2. Fast Food. Fast food restaurants in most cities serve many customers and compete with dozens of nearby options.
    1. Is fast food best classified as monopolistic competition or oligopoly at the local level? At the national brand level? Can both classifications apply?
    2. Choose two competing fast food brands and analyze how they differentiate their products.
    3. Estimate how easily a new fast food restaurant could enter your local market. What barriers exist, if any?
  3. Pharmaceutical Industry. The market for brand-name prescription drugs has features of both monopoly and oligopoly.
    1. What barriers to entry protect brand-name drug manufacturers?
    2. When a patent expires and generic competitors enter, what typically happens to price?
    3. Why might a pharmaceutical company advertise directly to consumers (patients) rather than only to physicians?
  4. Technology Platforms. Major internet search, social networking, and e-commerce platforms exhibit strong network effects.
    1. Explain how network effects can create barriers to entry.
    2. If a dominant platform offers services at zero monetary price to users, how should antitrust regulators think about consumer harm?
    3. Is it possible for a market to have one dominant firm and still be competitive in meaningful ways? Explain.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 15: Labor Markets, Wages, and Income

Chapter 14 completed the study of market structures by examining the large middle ground between perfect competition and monopoly. You now understand how firms behave when they compete with many differentiated rivals, and how firms behave when they compete with just a few powerful strategic opponents. Chapter 15 turns from product markets to factor markets — the markets where inputs are bought and sold. The most important factor market is the labor market, where workers sell their time and skills and firms purchase labor to produce goods and services. Labor markets determine wages, employment, and ultimately the incomes that households use to buy all the goods and services studied in Part II and Part III. Chapter 15 will introduce the demand for labor, the supply of labor, wage determination, human capital, education and training, compensating differentials, unions, minimum wages, and the sources of income differences across workers and households.

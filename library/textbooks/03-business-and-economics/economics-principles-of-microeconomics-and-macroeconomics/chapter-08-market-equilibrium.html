Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every day, millions of separate decisions happen without any central authority directing them. Wheat farmers in Kansas decide how much to plant. Bakeries in Boston decide how much flour to buy. Neither group talks to the other, yet bread appears on shelves at prices that most buyers and sellers find workable. How does this coordination happen?

The answer lies in market equilibrium — the point at which the decisions of buyers and sellers match up. When a market reaches equilibrium, the quantity that buyers want to purchase at a given price is exactly equal to the quantity that sellers want to supply at that same price. The result is a market that clears: no unsatisfied sellers stacking up unsold goods, no frustrated buyers unable to find what they want.

Chapter 6 taught you how demand works. Chapter 7 taught you how supply works. This chapter brings them together. You will learn how markets find equilibrium, what happens when they are knocked out of it, how prices carry information, and what occurs when governments set prices above or below market levels.

A market graph is not a picture to memorize. It is a reasoning tool — a way of organizing what you know about buyers and sellers to predict outcomes. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at any demand-and-supply diagram and use it to think.

Opening Question

Think of a time you could not find something you wanted to buy — concert tickets, a popular toy, an apartment in a tight rental market. Now think of a time a store seemed to have far more of something than anyone wanted, and it eventually went on clearance. Both situations are examples of markets out of equilibrium. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain exactly why each happened and what forces pushed the market back toward balance.

Section 8.1

Bringing Demand and Supply Together

In Chapter 6, you studied demand schedules and demand curves. A demand curve shows the quantity of a good that buyers are willing and able to purchase at each possible price, holding everything else constant. The law of demand tells us that as price rises, quantity demanded falls, so demand curves slope downward.

In Chapter 7, you studied supply schedules and supply curves. A supply curve shows the quantity of a good that sellers are willing and able to offer at each possible price, holding everything else constant. The law of supply tells us that as price rises, quantity supplied rises, so supply curves slope upward.

When we draw both curves on the same graph, we can analyze how buyers and sellers interact. The horizontal axis measures quantity — both the quantity buyers demand and the quantity sellers supply, measured in the same units. The vertical axis measures price. Both buyers and sellers respond to the same price signals in the same market.

Graph 8.1 — Demand and Supply Together
The demand curve D slopes downward; the supply curve S slopes upward. The two curves cross at a single point — explored in Section 8.2.

Notice that below the crossing point, the demand curve is above the supply curve. This means buyers would pay a higher price than sellers would need to accept — so there is potential for trade. Above the crossing point, the supply curve is above the demand curve, meaning sellers would need a higher price than buyers are willing to pay. At the crossing point itself, the two curves meet: buyers and sellers agree.

Section 8.2

Equilibrium Price and Quantity

The point where the demand curve and the supply curve intersect is called the market equilibrium. At this point, the price that buyers are willing to pay exactly matches the price that sellers are willing to accept, and the quantity that buyers want to purchase exactly matches the quantity that sellers want to sell.

Equilibrium price — the price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. This is sometimes called the market-clearing price because at this price, every buyer who wants to buy finds a seller, and every seller who wants to sell finds a buyer.

Equilibrium quantity — the quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.

Graph 8.2 — Market Equilibrium
D and S intersect at E. Dashed lines mark equilibrium price P* and equilibrium quantity Q*.

The asterisk (*) notation is a common economics convention meaning "equilibrium value." Whenever you see P* or Q* in this textbook, they refer to the equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity in that market.

Section 8.3

Shortages

What happens when the price in a market is below the equilibrium price? At a price below equilibrium, the quantity demanded is greater than the quantity supplied. Buyers want to purchase more than sellers are willing to offer. This condition is called a shortage, or sometimes an excess demand.

Using Table 8.1: at a price of $1.00 per pound, buyers want 1,000 pounds of apples per week but sellers are only willing to supply 600 pounds. The shortage is 1,000 − 600 = 400 pounds per week.

A shortage creates competition among buyers. When there is not enough of a good to go around at the existing price, buyers compete with one another to get what is available. Some buyers are willing to pay more rather than go without. This competition puts upward pressure on price.

Graph 8.3 — Shortage
At price P₁ below P*, quantity demanded (Q_D) exceeds quantity supplied (Q_S). The gap is the shortage, and price faces upward pressure.

How a Shortage Resolves

As buyers compete and bid prices up, two things happen simultaneously: rising prices reduce quantity demanded (movement up the demand curve) and increase quantity supplied (movement up the supply curve). The shortage shrinks. This process continues until the price rises to P*, at which point quantity demanded equals quantity supplied and the shortage disappears.

Shortages in Real Life

Concert tickets priced below what the market would bear often sell out instantly and create secondary markets. Gasoline shortages occur during supply disruptions when prices are slow to adjust. Housing shortages can develop in cities where building supply does not keep pace with demand.

Section 8.4

Surpluses

What happens when the price in a market is above the equilibrium price? At a price above equilibrium, the quantity supplied is greater than the quantity demanded. Sellers are offering more than buyers are willing to purchase. This condition is called a surplus, or sometimes an excess supply.

Using Table 8.1 again: at a price of $2.00 per pound, sellers are willing to supply 1,000 pounds of apples per week, but buyers only want to purchase 600 pounds. The surplus is 1,000 − 600 = 400 pounds per week.

A surplus creates competition among sellers. When there is more of a good available than buyers want at the existing price, sellers compete with one another to make sales. Some sellers are willing to accept lower prices rather than see their goods go unsold. This competition puts downward pressure on price.

Graph 8.4 — Surplus
At price P₂ above P*, quantity supplied (Q_S) exceeds quantity demanded (Q_D). The gap is the surplus, and price faces downward pressure.

How a Surplus Resolves

As sellers compete and reduce prices, falling prices increase quantity demanded (movement down the demand curve) and reduce quantity supplied (movement down the supply curve). The surplus shrinks until price falls to P*, at which point quantity demanded equals quantity supplied and the surplus disappears.

Surpluses in Real Life

Agricultural surpluses develop when governments guarantee farmers prices above equilibrium. Unsold merchandise at the end of a retail season ends up on clearance. A glut of new homes in a slow real estate market creates downward pressure on prices.

Section 8.5

How Markets Adjust

The analysis of shortages and surpluses reveals a pattern: markets have a built-in tendency to move toward equilibrium. When price is below equilibrium, competition among buyers pushes price up. When price is above equilibrium, competition among sellers pushes price down. In both cases, the market moves toward P*, where the shortage or surplus disappears.

This self-correcting tendency is one of the most important insights in economics. Markets do not need a central authority to set prices or allocate goods. The behavior of buyers and sellers — each responding to the prices they face — pushes the market toward equilibrium on its own.

If Price Is...Step-by-Step Adjustment
Below equilibriumShortage exists → buyers compete, offering higher prices → higher prices attract more sellers and discourage some buyers → quantity supplied rises, quantity demanded falls → shortage shrinks → process continues until price reaches P*
Above equilibriumSurplus exists → sellers compete, lowering prices → lower prices attract more buyers and discourage some sellers → quantity demanded rises, quantity supplied falls → surplus shrinks → process continues until price falls to P*
How Fast Do Markets Adjust?

The speed of adjustment varies by market. Stock markets and foreign currency markets adjust in seconds. Labor markets and housing markets adjust more slowly because of search costs, contracts, regulations, and the time it takes to build new supply or find new employment. The principle is the same in all cases; the time required differs.

This adjustment process represents a form of decentralized coordination. Millions of buyers and sellers, none of whom knows anything about the overall market, each respond to the price they observe. Their individual responses — buy more if price falls, sell more if price rises — collectively push the market toward a state where plans are mutually consistent, without central direction.

Section 8.6

Price Signals

Why do markets adjust toward equilibrium? The answer lies in what prices communicate. In a market economy, prices do not merely describe transactions — they carry information and create incentives.

Prices as information: A rising price signals that a good is becoming scarcer relative to demand. It tells buyers: "This resource is more valuable right now — consider substitutes." It tells sellers: "There is money to be made supplying more of this — expand production." A falling price signals relative abundance.

Prices as incentives: Prices do not just carry information — they create incentives to act on it. A farmer who sees corn prices rising has a direct financial incentive to plant more corn next season. A driver who sees gasoline prices rising has a direct financial incentive to drive less or buy a more fuel-efficient vehicle.

The Price System as Coordination

Because prices simultaneously signal scarcity and create incentives to respond, they coordinate the decisions of people who never communicate directly. The buyer of bread in Boston and the wheat farmer in Kansas do not know each other. But the chain of prices — from wheat to flour to bread — coordinates their decisions so that production and consumption align. Economist Friedrich Hayek called this the marvel of the price system.

When Prices Cannot Signal

Price signals work best when prices are free to move in response to supply and demand conditions. When prices are prevented from adjusting — by law, regulation, or market power — the information they carry becomes distorted. We return to this issue in Section 8.10 and in Chapter 16.

Section 8.7

Demand Shifts and Equilibrium Changes

Markets reach equilibrium, but equilibrium is not permanent. When the conditions underlying demand or supply change, the equilibrium itself changes. This section examines what happens when demand shifts. Section 8.8 covers supply shifts.

Recall from Chapter 6 that demand can shift for many reasons: changes in income, changes in the prices of related goods, changes in tastes, changes in expectations, or changes in the number of buyers. A shift in demand means the entire demand curve moves — consumers are willing to buy more or less at every price.

Graph 8.5 — Increase in Demand
D shifts right from D₁ to D₂. The new equilibrium E₂ has a higher price (P₂*) and higher quantity (Q₂*) than the original E₁.

Suppose demand for apples increases — perhaps a health study reports that apples significantly reduce health risks, and many more people begin buying apples regularly. At the original price P₁*, the rightward shift of demand creates a shortage: buyers now want more than sellers are supplying. This shortage puts upward pressure on price. Price rises, increasing quantity supplied and reducing quantity demanded, until a new equilibrium is established at E₂. The result of an increase in demand: both equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity are higher.

Now suppose demand for apples decreases — perhaps a popular new diet discourages fruit consumption. The demand curve shifts left. At the original price, the leftward shift creates a surplus, which puts downward pressure on price until a new, lower equilibrium is reached. The result of a decrease in demand: both equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity are lower.

Change in DemandEffect on Equilibrium PriceEffect on Equilibrium Quantity
Increase (shift right)RisesRises
Decrease (shift left)FallsFalls

Notice that when demand shifts, price and quantity move in the same direction. This is a reliable pattern you can use to predict market outcomes.

Section 8.8

Supply Shifts and Equilibrium Changes

Supply can also shift. Recall from Chapter 7 that supply shifts when input costs change, when technology changes, when taxes or subsidies change, when the number of sellers changes, or when seller expectations change. A supply shift means sellers offer more or less at every price.

Graph 8.7 — Increase in Supply
S shifts right from S₁ to S₂. The new equilibrium E₂ has a lower price (P₂*) and higher quantity (Q₂*) than the original E₁.

Suppose supply of apples increases — perhaps a technological improvement in farming significantly reduces production costs, and farmers expand orchards. At the original price, the rightward shift creates a surplus, putting downward pressure on price. Price falls until a new equilibrium is established: equilibrium price falls and equilibrium quantity rises.

Now suppose supply decreases — perhaps a late frost destroys much of the apple crop. At the original price, the leftward shift creates a shortage, putting upward pressure on price. Price rises until a new equilibrium is established: equilibrium price rises and equilibrium quantity falls.

Change in SupplyEffect on Equilibrium PriceEffect on Equilibrium Quantity
Increase (shift right)FallsRises
Decrease (shift left)RisesFalls

Notice that when supply shifts, price and quantity move in opposite directions — the reverse of the demand pattern. This difference is useful for identifying what caused a price change in real markets.

  3. Several new firms enter a competitive industry. Predict the effect on P* and Q*.
  4. A hurricane destroys a major production facility. Predict the effect on P* and Q*.
Section 8.9

Simultaneous Shifts

Real markets rarely change just one thing at a time. Both demand and supply can shift simultaneously in response to different events. When this happens, the analysis becomes more complex, and in some cases the direction of one of the equilibrium outcomes becomes indeterminate — it depends on the relative size of the shifts.

Table 8.2 — Summary of Simultaneous Shift Effects
Demand ChangeSupply ChangeEffect on PriceEffect on Quantity
IncreaseIncreaseAmbiguousRises
DecreaseDecreaseAmbiguousFalls
IncreaseDecreaseRisesAmbiguous
DecreaseIncreaseFallsAmbiguous

Reading the table: When both curves shift in the same direction, the quantity outcome is clear but the price outcome is ambiguous. When the curves shift in opposite directions, the price outcome is clear but the quantity outcome is ambiguous.

Worked Example
Drought and Rising Income in the Apple Market
  • SituationA drought reduces the apple harvest at the same time that a rise in household income increases consumer demand for apples.
  • ShiftsSupply decreases (drought shifts supply left). Demand increases (higher income shifts demand right).
  • Apply Table 8.2This is the "Increase / Decrease" row: price definitely rises, but the quantity outcome is ambiguous.
  • InterpretationIf the demand increase is larger than the supply decrease, quantity rises. If the supply decrease is larger, quantity falls. Without data on relative sizes, we cannot determine quantity — but we can confidently predict higher prices.
Key takeaway: Ambiguity reflects reality, not a failure of analysis — qualitative reasoning can identify what is certain and what requires more data.

Section 8.10

Price Ceilings and Price Floors at an Introductory Level

So far, we have assumed prices are free to adjust until markets reach equilibrium. But governments sometimes intervene to set prices above or below market equilibrium. These policies have predictable economic consequences that follow directly from the analysis of surpluses and shortages.

Price Ceilings

A price ceiling is a maximum legal price that can be charged for a good or service. The ceiling is set below the equilibrium price with the intent of making the good more affordable.

Graph 8.10 — Price Ceiling
The price ceiling P_c sits below P*. At P_c, Q_D exceeds Q_S, creating a persistent shortage that the law prevents from being eliminated through price increases.

At the ceiling price, buyers want to purchase more than sellers are willing to offer — the same condition analyzed in Section 8.3. But the normal market mechanism for eliminating the shortage (price rising to P*) is blocked by law. The shortage persists.

ConsequenceDescription
Queues and waiting linesBuyers who arrive early get the good; others do not. Time becomes the effective price.
Reduced qualitySellers have less incentive to maintain quality because buyers must take what is available.
Black marketsSome buyers and sellers make illegal transactions above the ceiling price.
Non-price discriminationSellers allocate goods based on personal connections, favoritism, or political influence.
Binding vs. Non-Binding

A price ceiling set above the equilibrium price is non-binding — it has no effect, because the market equilibrium price is already below the ceiling. A ceiling only creates a shortage when it is set below the equilibrium price.

Price Floors

A price floor is a minimum legal price for a good or service. The floor is set above the equilibrium price with the intent of guaranteeing sellers a higher price.

Graph 8.11 — Price Floor
The price floor P_f sits above P*. At P_f, Q_S exceeds Q_D, creating a persistent surplus that the law prevents from being eliminated through price decreases.
ConsequenceDescription
Unsold goodsThe surplus must go somewhere — storage, waste, or government purchase.
Reduced transactionsFewer units are bought and sold than at equilibrium.
Efficiency costSome mutually beneficial transactions that would occur at the equilibrium price do not occur at the floor price.
PolicySet Relative to EquilibriumEffect
Price ceilingBelow equilibriumCreates a shortage
Price floorAbove equilibriumCreates a surplus
Price ceilingAbove equilibriumNon-binding — no effect
Price floorBelow equilibriumNon-binding — no effect
A Word on Policy Evaluation

This section has analyzed the economic consequences of price controls. It has not argued that price controls are always wrong or always right. People argue that the benefits of a price control — protecting renters, supporting farm income, ensuring affordability during emergencies — may justify the economic costs. Evaluating those tradeoffs is a policy question involving both positive analysis (what happens) and normative judgment (what should we do). Economics provides tools to analyze consequences; it does not automatically determine which consequences matter most or how to weigh them.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1The combined demand-and-supply graph places D (downward-sloping) and S (upward-sloping) on the same axes, letting us compare what buyers want and what sellers offer at every price.
  • Sec 8.2Market equilibrium is the point where D and S intersect. P* is the equilibrium price; Q* is the equilibrium quantity. Equilibrium can be read from a graph or located in a demand-and-supply table.
  • Sec 8.3A shortage (excess demand) occurs when price is below equilibrium. Competition among buyers pushes price up until the shortage disappears.
  • Sec 8.4A surplus (excess supply) occurs when price is above equilibrium. Competition among sellers pushes price down until the surplus disappears.
  • Sec 8.5Markets are self-correcting. Disequilibrium creates pressure — upward from shortages, downward from surpluses — that moves price toward P* without central direction.
  • Sec 8.6Prices act as signals (carrying information about scarcity) and as incentives (motivating buyers and sellers to respond), coordinating decentralized decisions across an entire economy.
  • Sec 8.7Demand shifts move price and quantity in the same direction: an increase in demand raises both P* and Q*; a decrease lowers both.
  • Sec 8.8Supply shifts move price and quantity in opposite directions: an increase in supply lowers P* but raises Q*; a decrease raises P* but lowers Q*.
  • Sec 8.9When demand and supply shift simultaneously, one outcome (price or quantity) is determinate and the other is ambiguous, depending on whether the shifts move in the same or opposite directions.
  • Sec 8.10Price ceilings (below equilibrium) create shortages; price floors (above equilibrium) create surpluses. Controls set on the "wrong side" of equilibrium are non-binding. Evaluating these policies requires both positive analysis and normative judgment.

Key Terms Review

market equilibrium
The condition in which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied; the market clears.
equilibrium price (P*)
The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied; also called the market-clearing price.
equilibrium quantity (Q*)
The quantity bought and sold when the market is in equilibrium.
shortage (excess demand)
A condition in which quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied at the current price; occurs below equilibrium.
surplus (excess supply)
A condition in which quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded at the current price; occurs above equilibrium.
market adjustment
The process by which price moves toward equilibrium in response to shortages or surpluses.
price signal
The information about scarcity and value carried by a price, which creates incentives for buyers and sellers to change behavior.
disequilibrium
Any condition in which price is not at the equilibrium level, creating either a shortage or a surplus.
price ceiling
A maximum legal price set below the equilibrium price; creates a shortage.
price floor
A minimum legal price set above the equilibrium price; creates a surplus.
non-binding price control
A ceiling set above equilibrium or a floor set below equilibrium; has no economic effect.
simultaneous shift
A change in both demand and supply at the same time, requiring analysis of both shifts to determine the new equilibrium.

Error Analysis

Each statement contains an economic error. Identify the error and explain the correct analysis.

  1. "There was a surplus of unsold cars on the lot, so the car dealer raised prices to make sure each car sold would generate enough revenue."
  2. "The government set a price ceiling above the equilibrium price. This created a shortage of the good."
  3. "When both demand and supply increase at the same time, equilibrium price must rise because more people want the good."
  4. "After the supply of wheat decreased, the equilibrium quantity of wheat also decreased, so equilibrium price must have decreased too."
  5. "A shortage can persist indefinitely in a free market with no government interference, because sellers have no reason to change their prices."

Looking Ahead
Chapter 9: Elasticity

Chapter 8 showed you how equilibrium price and quantity are determined and how they change when supply or demand shifts. But knowing that price changes is only the beginning of the story. How much does quantity demanded change when price rises? How much does quantity supplied change when price falls? Do buyers respond a lot or a little to price increases? The answers to these questions matter enormously for businesses setting prices, governments evaluating taxes, and policymakers designing programs. Chapter 9 introduces elasticity — a precise measure of how responsive quantity is to changes in price or income. You will learn how to calculate price elasticity of demand, interpret elastic and inelastic demand, connect elasticity to total revenue, and understand what determines how responsive buyers and sellers are. Elasticity will become one of the most useful tools in your economics toolkit.

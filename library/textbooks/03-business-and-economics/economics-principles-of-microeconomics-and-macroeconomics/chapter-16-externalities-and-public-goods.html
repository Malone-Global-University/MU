Chapter 16 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Markets are powerful tools for organizing economic activity. When buyers and sellers come together voluntarily, they usually generate gains from trade, allocate resources toward their highest-valued uses, and coordinate decisions without any central authority directing them. Much of microeconomics is devoted to understanding how markets do this and how well they do it.

But markets are not infallible. There are conditions under which the price system fails to produce the outcome that would be best for society as a whole. Economists call these situations market failures — cases where the uncoordinated decisions of buyers and sellers lead to outcomes that are inefficient, meaning total well-being is lower than it could be.

This chapter introduces two of the most important sources of market failure: externalities and public goods. An externality exists when a transaction between a buyer and a seller affects a third party who is not part of that transaction. A public good is a good with characteristics that make it difficult for markets to supply efficiently on their own.

Understanding externalities and public goods is essential for anyone who wants to reason carefully about environmental policy, public health, infrastructure, research, and education. These concepts do not automatically tell us what policy to adopt — but they do sharpen the questions we ask and the tradeoffs we face.

Opening Question

Think of a time your choices affected someone who had no say in the matter — a neighbor's music, a factory's smoke, a free vaccination that protected people you never met. Were those effects priced into anyone's decision? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to explain why markets sometimes ignore these effects entirely, and what tools societies use to bring them back into view.

Section 16.1

What Market Failure Means

In earlier chapters, you learned that competitive markets with well-functioning prices tend to produce efficient outcomes. Efficiency here means that the total gains from trade — the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus — are as large as possible. No resources are wasted; no mutually beneficial transaction is left undone.

For markets to achieve this result, several conditions generally need to hold: buyers and sellers must have reasonably good information; property rights must be clearly defined and enforced; the costs and benefits of transactions must fall entirely on the buyers and sellers involved; and competition must prevent any single buyer or seller from dominating the market. When these conditions hold, Adam Smith's metaphor of the "invisible hand" applies — individual self-interest, guided by prices, tends to produce socially beneficial results.

Market failure occurs when one or more of these conditions breaks down. The result is that markets may produce too much of some goods, too little of others, or may fail to produce certain goods at all.

Common Misconception

Market failure does not mean that markets produce bad outcomes in every case, that government intervention will necessarily improve things, that the solution to every market failure is a new law or regulation, or that prices are wrong or profits are harmful. Market failure is a technical concept: in certain identifiable situations, uncoordinated market activity fails to maximize total economic well-being, leaving room for potential improvement. Whether and how to respond is a separate question involving tradeoffs, institutional capacity, costs, and values.

Section 16.2

External Costs and External Benefits

An externality is a cost or benefit that falls on someone who is not a buyer or seller in a given market transaction. The word "external" signals that the effect falls outside the market transaction itself — it lands on a third party.

Consider a factory that produces steel and sells it to buyers. The factory and its customers both benefit from the transaction. But suppose the factory also emits pollution that drifts into a nearby river, harming the fish, fouling the water, and reducing quality of life for people who live downstream. Those downstream residents are not part of the steel market transaction — they did not agree to accept the pollution. Yet they bear a real cost. That pollution is a negative externality.

Now consider a homeowner who plants a beautiful garden that blooms every spring. Neighbors and passersby enjoy the flowers at no cost to themselves. The gardener did not plan to benefit her neighbors, but they receive a benefit they did not pay for. This is a positive externality.

Why Externalities Break Efficiency

Markets are efficient when the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive accurately reflect the full social cost and full social benefit of a good. With a negative externality, the seller does not pay the full cost of production — some cost is shifted onto third parties — so the seller produces more than is socially optimal. With a positive externality, the buyer captures only part of the benefit, so the market produces less than is socially optimal.

Graphing Intuition

In a market with a negative externality, the social marginal cost curve lies above the private supply curve, and the market produces more than the socially optimal quantity. In a market with a positive externality, the social marginal benefit curve lies above the private demand curve, and the market produces less than the socially optimal quantity. Section 16.3

Negative Externalities

A negative externality exists when a transaction imposes an external cost on a third party who did not agree to bear that cost. The most familiar examples involve pollution, but negative externalities arise in many contexts: air and water pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, noise, secondhand smoke, distracted driving, inadequate workplace safety, overfishing, and traffic congestion are all common examples.

In each case, the person or firm generating the externality does not bear the full cost of their action. They face their private cost — the cost they must actually pay — while the social cost is higher, because it includes the private cost plus the external cost imposed on others. When private cost is lower than social cost, the producer has an incentive to produce more than is socially efficient. This is not about firms being uncaring; it is about incentives. When someone can shift costs onto others without compensating them, they will tend to produce more of the activity that generates those costs.

CategoryExamples
EnvironmentalAir pollution from manufacturing; agricultural runoff; greenhouse gas emissions; noise pollution near airports or construction
Health and safetySecondhand smoke; distracted or speeding drivers; inadequate workplace safety investment
OtherExcessive fishing that depletes shared stocks; a neighbor's loud late-night music; traffic congestion on shared roads
Markets Overproduce the Externality-Generating Good

Relative to what would be socially optimal: more pollution is emitted, more of the polluting good is produced, the price of the good is lower than it should be (because external cost is excluded from price), and third parties bear costs not reflected in market prices. The gap between the market quantity and the socially optimal quantity represents a loss to society.

Section 16.4

Positive Externalities

A positive externality exists when a transaction confers a benefit on a third party who does not pay for it. Education is one of the most discussed examples: an educated person benefits directly through higher earnings, but society also benefits through greater productivity, civic engagement, innovation, and lower crime — spillovers that accrue to people who did not pay for that person's education.

Vaccination generates a positive externality through herd immunity: a vaccinated person reduces the probability of transmitting disease to others, including people who cannot be vaccinated for medical reasons. Basic research and innovation often generate large positive externalities, because other firms and researchers can build on new knowledge in ways the original innovator never captures financially. Infrastructure and neighborhood improvements — a new park, a renovated home — raise surrounding property values and benefit neighbors who paid nothing toward the improvement.

Why Markets Underproduce Goods with Positive Externalities

Buyers only consider their own private benefit when deciding how much to buy. Because the good's price reflects only private benefit, it understates the good's true value to society — which includes the external benefit third parties receive. Producers respond to the (too-low) price signal by producing less than the socially optimal amount.

Section 16.5

Social Cost and Social Benefit

To reason carefully about externalities, economists use two key concepts. Social cost is the full cost of producing a good, including both private costs paid by the producer and any external costs imposed on third parties:

Core Formulas

Social Cost = Private Cost + External Cost

Social Benefit = Private Benefit + External Benefit

In a market without externalities, private cost equals social cost and private benefit equals social benefit, so prices reflect full social values and market equilibrium is efficient. With a negative externality, social cost exceeds private cost and the market overproduces. With a positive externality, social benefit exceeds private benefit and the market underproduces.

The socially optimal quantity is the output level where social marginal cost equals social marginal benefit. At this point, the full cost to society of one more unit exactly equals the full benefit to society of consuming it. Measuring external costs and benefits precisely is genuinely difficult — economists estimate these values using health data, property values, productivity studies, and more — but real uncertainty remains, and policy responses must grapple with that honestly.

When a market produces more or less than the socially optimal quantity, the result is deadweight loss — a loss of total economic well-being that no one receives. It is not transferred from one group to another; it simply disappears. With a negative externality, deadweight loss comes from producing units whose social cost exceeds their social benefit. With a positive externality, it comes from not producing units whose social benefit would have exceeded their social cost.

Section 16.6

Public Goods

Externalities arise when market transactions impose costs or confer benefits on people outside those transactions. Public goods present a different kind of market failure — one rooted not in external effects but in the fundamental characteristics of certain goods.

A public good is a good that is simultaneously nonrival and nonexcludable. A good is nonrival if one person's use of it does not reduce the amount available to others — national defense protects every citizen simultaneously. A good is nonexcludable if it is impossible or prohibitively costly to prevent someone from benefiting, whether or not they paid — once a country is defended, there is no practical way to exclude any resident from that protection.

Compare these to a hamburger (rival — eating it leaves none for anyone else) or a private movie theater (excludable — non-payers can be turned away at the door). Nonrivalry means the marginal cost of serving one more person is zero. Nonexcludability is the source of the free-rider problem, discussed in Section 16.7.

ExcludableNonexcludable
RivalPrivate good — food, clothing, cars, haircutsCommon resource — ocean fish, shared groundwater, an unpriced congested road

Example 16.6.1

  • BreadRival — eating it consumes it. Excludable — the baker can refuse to sell without payment. Private good.
  • Weather radioNonrival — one listener does not block another. Nonexcludable — anyone with a radio can receive it. Public good.

Section 16.7

Free Riders

The free-rider problem arises when people can benefit from a good without paying for it. Because nonexcludable goods cannot be withheld from non-payers, individuals have an incentive to let others pay while enjoying the benefits themselves. Consider a neighborhood trying to fund a private security patrol: every household benefits if it exists, but every household also knows the patrol will protect their street whether or not they personally contribute. If everyone reasons this way, the patrol may never be funded — even though everyone would benefit from it. This is a coordination failure, not a failure of caring.

Markets are organized around the ability to charge prices, but prices require excludability. Because public goods are nonexcludable, private sellers cannot effectively charge for them, so private firms have little incentive to produce them in the first place. This is why public goods are typically provided by governments through taxation — which avoids the free-rider problem by making contributions compulsory — or by nonprofits relying on donations.

Voluntary provision does happen: people donate to charities, support public radio, and volunteer. But voluntary provision typically falls short of the socially optimal level, because however generous one person is, their neighbors can still enjoy the result while contributing nothing.

A Careful Distinction

The free-rider problem explains why markets underprovide public goods — it does not automatically prove that government will provide the optimal quantity. Government provision still requires raising revenue, estimating what citizens value, and delivering the good efficiently. Whether government intervention improves outcomes is a separate empirical and institutional question.

Section 16.8

Common Resources

Common resources are goods that are rival but nonexcludable: fish in open-access oceans, shared groundwater, open rangeland, congested untolled roads, and shared hunting grounds are all examples. Unlike public goods, one person's use does reduce availability to others; unlike private goods, non-payers cannot easily be excluded.

Economist Garrett Hardin popularized the phrase "tragedy of the commons" to describe the overuse of common resources. If a pasture is open to every herder in a village, each herder gains personally from adding another animal, but if every herder does the same, the pasture becomes overgrazed and eventually collapses — harming everyone. The cost of overgrazing is spread across all herders, but the benefit of adding one more animal accrues entirely to the individual who adds it. This asymmetry drives overuse, and it is a story about incentive structures, not selfishness or short-sightedness.

Section 16.9

Policy Tools for Externalities and Public Goods

Market failures from externalities and public goods motivate a range of policy tools, though they do not automatically justify any particular response. The main categories are corrective taxes and subsidies, regulations and standards, tradable permits, government provision, and property rights clarification.

A Pigouvian tax — named after economist Arthur Pigou — is a tax equal to the external cost of a negative externality. It raises private cost toward social cost, giving producers an incentive to reduce output or find cheaper ways to reduce the externality, while also raising revenue. A subsidy does the reverse for positive externalities, lowering the effective price or cost to encourage more of a beneficial activity, as with vaccination subsidies or research funding.

Regulations and standards — emission limits, safety codes, speed limits — directly mandate behavior, but a uniform standard may be more or less efficient for different firms and provides less ongoing incentive to improve once a firm meets the standard. A tradable permit system (cap-and-trade) sets a total cap on an activity and lets firms buy and sell permits, so firms that can reduce the activity cheaply do so and sell their unused permits to firms for which reduction is costly — combining environmental certainty with cost-minimizing flexibility.

For public goods, government provision funded by taxation is the most common response, though it raises its own questions about how much to provide, how to fund it, and how efficiently agencies will deliver it.

Economist Ronald Coase observed that if property rights are well-defined and transactions are costless, private parties can sometimes negotiate efficient solutions to externality problems without government intervention — the Coase Theorem. This insight is most useful as a diagnostic tool, pointing to the absence of clear property rights as a root cause of many externality problems, though it applies most cleanly when transaction costs are low and the number of parties is small.

Worked Example 16.9.1
Calculating a corrective tax
  • SituationA coal plant's private marginal cost of producing one megawatt-hour (MWh) is $60. Air pollution from that MWh imposes external health and environmental costs of $20.
  • Social costSocial MC = Private MC + External MC = $60 + $20 = $80 per MWh
  • DiagnosisAt a price of $60, the market prices electricity below its true social cost, so it produces more electricity than is socially optimal.
  • Tax neededPigouvian tax = $20 per MWh
Key takeaway: A correctly sized Pigouvian tax raises effective cost to $80, moving the market toward the socially optimal quantity — assuming the $20 estimate is accurate.

Section 16.10

Evaluating Market Failure Claims Carefully

Identifying a market failure is the beginning of policy analysis, not the end. Several questions must be asked before concluding that intervention is worthwhile: Is the market failure significant, or is it small enough that intervention is not worth the cost? Can the externality actually be measured with reasonable confidence? What are the administrative, compliance, and political costs of the proposed intervention? And — just as markets can fail — governments can fail too, lacking information, facing political pressures, or implementing policy inefficiently. The relevant comparison is between an imperfect market outcome and an imperfect policy outcome, not between an imperfect market and an idealized government.

This chapter has focused mainly on positive questions — what markets do and why they sometimes produce inefficient outcomes — which is largely uncontroversial among economists who share the underlying framework. But policy responses involve normative questions that go beyond positive economics: how much weight should future generations get in evaluating environmental externalities? How should efficiency be balanced against equity when a corrective tax raises prices for low-income households? These questions do not have purely economic answers. Reasonable people can examine the same evidence and reach different conclusions based on different values.

Three Habits for Reasoning Well

Distinguish positive facts from normative judgments. Look for unintended consequences. Maintain epistemic humility about uncertain measurements and predictions. These habits apply to externalities and public goods — and to nearly every policy topic in this textbook.

Chapter 16 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 16.1Market failure occurs when uncoordinated market activity fails to maximize total social well-being. The three main sources are externalities, public goods, and information failures. Market failure is a diagnosis, not an automatic case for government action.
  • Sec 16.2An externality is a cost or benefit falling on a third party outside a market transaction. Markets are efficient only when prices reflect full social cost and benefit; externalities break that condition.
  • Sec 16.3Negative externalities (such as pollution) make private cost lower than social cost, causing markets to overproduce the externality-generating good.
  • Sec 16.4Positive externalities (such as education and vaccination) make private benefit lower than social benefit, causing markets to underproduce the good.
  • Sec 16.5Social Cost = Private Cost + External Cost; Social Benefit = Private Benefit + External Benefit. The socially optimal quantity occurs where social marginal cost equals social marginal benefit; gaps from this point create deadweight loss.
  • Sec 16.6Public goods are nonrival and nonexcludable. Goods can be classified into private goods, public goods, club goods, and common resources based on rivalry and excludability.
  • Sec 16.7The free-rider problem — the ability to benefit from a nonexcludable good without paying — causes private markets to underprovide public goods. Government provision funded by compulsory taxation is the most common solution, though it is not automatically optimal.
  • Sec 16.8Common resources are rival but nonexcludable, creating the tragedy of the commons: individually rational use leads to overuse and possible depletion. Regulation, privatization, community management, and tradable permits are possible responses.
  • Sec 16.9Policy tools include Pigouvian taxes and subsidies, regulation and standards, tradable permit systems, government provision, and property rights clarification under the Coase Theorem. Each has distinct advantages and limitations.
  • Sec 16.10Identifying a market failure does not automatically justify a specific policy. Significance, measurability, intervention costs, and the possibility of government failure must all be weighed, and normative value judgments are inseparable from real policy choices.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 16.A
Identifying an externality from a scenario
  • ScenarioA chemical plant produces fertilizer and releases nitrogen compounds into a nearby river. Downstream, a shellfish farm's oysters die from nitrogen-induced algal blooms.
  • PartiesThe plant and its fertilizer customers are the market transaction. The shellfish farm is a third party that did not agree to accept pollution.
  • DiagnosisThis is a negative externality. The plant's private cost excludes the damage to the shellfish farm, so social cost exceeds private cost.
  • ConsequenceThe fertilizer market overproduces relative to the socially optimal quantity, and price is lower than it would be if social costs were included.
Possible responses: a tax on nitrogen emissions equal to the external cost, emission regulation, a discharge permit system, or negotiation if transaction costs are low.

Error Analysis

  2. "A negative externality means the firm is being unethical and breaking the law."
  3. "If the government imposes a Pigouvian tax on pollution, the firm will have no reason to reduce pollution at all — it will just pay the tax."
  4. "Public goods should never be provided privately, because private firms will always refuse to produce anything they can't charge for."
  5. "The tragedy of the commons means that any resource shared by more than one person will inevitably be destroyed."
  6. "Because education has positive externalities, the government should subsidize it infinitely."
  7. "A corrective tax on gasoline is just a money grab by the government — it doesn't actually fix the pollution problem."
  8. "The Coase Theorem proves that government environmental regulation is never necessary, because private parties can always negotiate efficient outcomes."

For each statement, identify the error and explain the correct economic reasoning.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 17: Information Problems and Behavioral Economics

Chapter 16 showed that externalities and public goods can cause markets to overproduce some goods and underproduce others — and that when third-party effects are large and nonexcludable, private provision may fail entirely. Chapter 17 introduces a second major category of market failure: information problems. Markets work best when buyers and sellers have good information about what they are exchanging, but in many real markets one side knows significantly more than the other. This asymmetry can produce adverse selection, moral hazard, and market unraveling. Chapter 17 will also introduce behavioral economics, examining what happens when real people deviate from the perfectly rational decision-making standard models assume — rounding out the picture of when markets fall short and what institutions and policies respond to that shortfall.

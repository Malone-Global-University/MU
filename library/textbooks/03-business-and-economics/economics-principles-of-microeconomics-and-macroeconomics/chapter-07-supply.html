Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every market has two sides. Chapter 6 studied the buyers — how the quantity they demand responds to price and how their behavior can be summarized in a demand curve. Chapter 7 turns to the other side: the sellers. Supply describes how producers respond to price. When the price of a good rises, producers generally become more willing and more able to offer larger quantities for sale.

When costs rise, or when conditions change, producers adjust their behavior — sometimes dramatically. Understanding supply helps explain why prices change, why some goods become scarce, why firms enter and exit industries, and why government policies that affect producers ripple through markets in predictable ways.

This chapter builds the concept of supply carefully, beginning with what supply means and what the law of supply says, then examining supply schedules and supply curves, the difference between movement along a curve and a shift of the curve, and the major factors that shift supply. By the end of this chapter, you will be ready to combine supply with demand in Chapter 8 and analyze how markets reach equilibrium.

Opening Question

Think about a business you know — a farm, a bakery, a factory, a freelancer. What would make that producer willing to sell more at a given price? What would make it willing to sell less? By the end of this chapter, you will have a precise vocabulary and a graphical model for answering exactly that question.

Section 7.1

What Supply Means

In everyday language, "supply" sometimes means the total amount of something that exists in the world — as in, "the global supply of fresh water." Economists use the word differently.

In economics, supply refers to the relationship between the price of a good or service and the quantity that producers are willing and able to offer for sale during a particular time period, holding all other factors constant.

Three elements of this definition matter: willing — a producer must actually want to sell at a given price, since the price must be high enough to make selling worthwhile given the producer's costs and alternatives; able — a producer must have the capacity to produce and sell, meaning the inputs, technology, and time required; and at a given price — supply is not a single number, but a relationship between price and quantity, where different prices lead to different quantities supplied.

Supply describes producer behavior in the same way that demand describes consumer behavior. Just as demand focuses on the buyer's side, supply focuses on the seller's side. Both together will explain how markets work.

Key Term — Supply

The relationship between the price of a good or service and the quantity that producers are willing and able to offer for sale during a given time period, all else equal.

Key Term — Quantity Supplied

The specific quantity that producers are willing and able to sell at a particular price.

Supply vs. Quantity Supplied

It is important not to confuse supply with quantity supplied. Supply refers to the entire relationship — the whole schedule or curve. Quantity supplied refers to a specific amount at a specific price. This distinction becomes critical when analyzing what shifts supply versus what moves us along a supply curve.

Section 7.2

The Law of Supply

The central proposition about producer behavior is the law of supply: all else equal, when the price of a good or service rises, the quantity supplied increases; when the price falls, the quantity supplied decreases. Price and quantity supplied move in the same direction — the opposite of demand, where price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions.

Why does the law of supply hold? Higher prices make production more profitable. If a wheat farmer can sell wheat for $8 per bushel instead of $5, growing wheat becomes more financially rewarding, giving the farmer an incentive to plant more acres and produce a larger crop. Higher prices attract new producers who previously found the market unprofitable. Higher prices justify accepting higher costs, since producing more often means using less ideal inputs or less efficient equipment, and producers will supply these costlier units only if the price covers those costs. Lower prices make production less profitable, leading producers to reduce quantity supplied or exit the market.

The law of supply is not an absolute physical law — it is a generalization about producer incentives that holds reliably across a wide range of goods and markets, assuming other relevant factors stay constant.

Reasoning Check
Why does a higher price increase quantity supplied?
  • IncentiveA higher price means more revenue per unit sold, which increases the reward for producing.
  • EntryProducers who were previously unprofitable at the old price may now find it worthwhile to enter.
  • Cost coverageHigher-cost units that were not worth producing before become worth producing once price rises enough to cover them.
Key takeaway: The law of supply reflects producer incentives, not a mechanical rule.

The upward slope of the supply curve reflects the law of supply: as price rises (moving up the vertical axis), quantity supplied increases (moving right along the horizontal axis). The supply curve is a simplified picture of producer behavior; in reality, supply relationships can be curved or kinked, but the essential feature — upward slope — captures the fundamental economic behavior we need to analyze.

Individual supply curves show the supply behavior of a single producer. Market supply curves show the supply behavior of all producers combined. Market supply curves are also upward sloping, but they are often flatter (more elastic) than individual curves, because as price rises, both existing producers supply more and new producers enter the market.

Section 7.5

Individual Supply and Market Supply

Individual supply describes a single firm's willingness and ability to sell at various prices. A single wheat farmer, a single software company, or a single bakery each has its own supply curve. Market supply describes the total supply from all firms in a market, constructed by adding, at each price, the quantities that all individual producers are willing to supply.

This horizontal summation is straightforward in a table: at each price, add up all producers' quantities. On a graph, market supply is drawn to the right of any individual curve because it represents a larger total quantity.

When we analyze markets — how prices are determined, how they respond to shocks, how policies affect producers — we almost always work with market supply. Individual supply curves matter when we study firm behavior in Part III. But for understanding market outcomes, the market supply curve is the relevant tool.

If new firms enter the market, market supply increases. If firms exit, market supply decreases. This is one of the important shifters of supply examined in Section 7.7.

Section 7.6

Movement Along a Supply Curve

A movement along a supply curve occurs when a change in price causes a change in the quantity supplied, with all other factors held constant. This movement is also called a change in quantity supplied. When the price of a good rises, we move up and to the right along the existing supply curve — quantity supplied increases. When price falls, we move down and to the left — quantity supplied decreases.

A change in quantity supplied is a movement along the supply curve caused by a price change. The curve itself stays in place. Compare this with a change in supply, which is a shift of the entire curve — discussed in the next section.

Section 7.7

Shifts in Supply

A shift in supply occurs when something other than price changes, causing producers to supply a different quantity at every price. When supply shifts, the entire supply curve moves — either to the right (supply increases) or to the left (supply decreases). A rightward (outward) shift means producers supply more at every price than before. A leftward (inward) shift means producers supply less at every price than before.

Key Term — Change in Supply

A shift of the entire supply curve caused by a change in a factor other than price.

Key Term — Increase in Supply / Decrease in Supply

An increase in supply is a rightward shift (producers supply more at every price). A decrease in supply is a leftward shift (producers supply less at every price).

Section 7.8

Input Costs and Technology

The two most fundamental determinants of supply are the costs of inputs and the state of technology available to producers.

Input costs. To produce any good or service, producers must purchase or hire inputs — also called factors of production — including labor, raw materials, energy, equipment, and land. When input costs fall, supply increases: lower costs mean higher profit margins, incentivizing more production at every price. When input costs rise, supply decreases: higher costs squeeze profit margins and make some production unprofitable.

ExampleEffect on Supply
Rising oil prices raise costs for transportation, plastics, and manufactured goodsSupply decreases
A drought raises the price of corn (a livestock feed input)Supply of beef decreases
A fall in computer chip prices reduces costs for electronics manufacturersSupply of laptops and smartphones increases
Higher minimum wages increase labor costs for restaurantsSupply of restaurant services decreases

Technology refers to the knowledge and methods used to turn inputs into outputs. A technological improvement means producers can get more output from the same quantity of inputs, or produce the same output at lower cost. When technology improves, supply increases — at every price, producers can now offer more output, shifting the supply curve right. Technological change rarely makes supply decrease; an older method of production does not typically displace a newer, more efficient one unless there are regulatory barriers or other constraints.

Examples of Technology Increasing Supply

The internal combustion engine dramatically increased the supply of transportation. Hydraulic fracturing technology increased the supply of oil and natural gas. Advances in semiconductor manufacturing have continually increased the supply of computing power while reducing cost per unit. Better seed varieties and precision agriculture have increased the supply of food crops over decades.

Key Term — Inputs (Factors of Production)

The labor, raw materials, capital equipment, and other resources used to produce a good or service.

Key Term — Technology

The methods and knowledge used to produce output from inputs; improvements in technology reduce the cost of production and increase supply.

Section 7.9

Taxes, Subsidies, Expectations, and Number of Sellers

Taxes. A tax on production is an increase in costs for producers. When a government taxes a good — for example, an excise tax on each unit sold — the effective cost of supplying that good rises, shifting the supply curve to the left: producers supply less at every price. Examples include a gasoline tax, a corporate income tax on profits, or a carbon tax on emissions.

Subsidies. A subsidy is a payment by the government to producers, effectively reducing their cost of production. Subsidies shift the supply curve to the right — at every price, producers are willing and able to supply more, because their effective costs have been reduced. Examples include agricultural subsidies for corn, wheat, and cotton, and subsidies to electric vehicle manufacturers.

Expectations. Producers are forward-looking and make decisions today based partly on what they expect future prices and costs to be. If producers expect the price of their good to rise in the future, they may withhold current supply, reducing current supply. If they expect the price to fall, they may rush to sell more now, increasing current supply. If they expect input costs to rise, they may stockpile production now, temporarily increasing current supply. Expectations matter most in industries where output can be stored, such as oil, grain, and metals.

Number of sellers. Market supply is the sum of all individual supplies. When more firms enter a market, market supply increases — the supply curve shifts right. When firms exit, market supply decreases — the supply curve shifts left. In competitive markets, high prices attract new entrants, eventually pushing supply up and prices back down — a dynamic central to Chapter 12.

Natural conditions. For some industries, weather, natural disasters, disease, and environmental conditions are important supply shifters, particularly in agriculture, fishing, mining, and energy. A drought reduces the supply of crops; a hurricane disrupts oil production; a particularly good growing season increases supply.

Key Term — Excise Tax

A per-unit tax levied on the production or sale of a good, which raises the cost of production and shifts supply left.

Key Term — Subsidy

A government payment to producers that reduces their cost of production and shifts supply right.

Section 7.10

Supply in Real Markets

The supply model introduced in this chapter is a simplification — a tool for reasoning — not a complete description of every real-world industry. But it captures the most important features of producer behavior in a wide range of markets.

Agricultural markets. Farmers decide how much to plant based on expected prices, input costs, and expectations about weather. A drought shifts supply left and raises food prices. A bumper crop shifts supply right and can actually harm farmers by driving prices so low that total revenue falls — a concept related to elasticity studied in Chapter 9. Government agricultural policies, including price supports, subsidies, and crop insurance, shift supply curves in predictable ways.

Energy markets. Oil, natural gas, coal, and electricity markets are intensely supply-driven. The discovery of new reserves, improvements in extraction technology, and geopolitical disruptions all shift supply. The shale revolution dramatically increased the supply of oil and natural gas in North America through hydraulic fracturing, lowering prices. Environmental regulations can shift supply leftward by raising extraction or use costs.

Technology and manufacturing. In electronics, computing, and manufacturing, supply has been shifting rightward for decades due to technological improvements, reducing prices and making products more widely available.

Labor markets. Labor supply, introduced fully in Chapter 15, behaves similarly to the supply of goods — higher wages generally attract more workers — though with important differences at very high wage levels.

Services. Service industries such as healthcare, education, financial services, and hospitality also have supply curves, though supply is harder to expand quickly when workers must be trained or licensed. A shortage of nurses, teachers, or software engineers reflects a supply constraint that wages alone cannot quickly resolve.

Common Misconceptions About Supply

"Supply" is not the total stock of something that exists. Supply is a relationship between price and quantity offered for sale, not a fixed stock. A decrease in supply does not mean less of something exists in the world — it means producers offer less at every price, reflecting changed costs, taxes, or technology. Rising prices do not always indicate decreasing supply; they can also result from increased demand, which requires analyzing both sides of the market (Chapter 8). Supply and production are not the same thing; production is the physical act of creating output, while supply relates price to the quantity producers are willing to sell.

Error Analysis

Each statement below contains an economic error. Identify the error and explain the correct reasoning.

  1. "The supply of electric vehicles increased last year because Tesla raised its prices."
  2. "When a drought hits wheat farmers, the quantity of wheat supplied decreases because consumers buy less wheat."
  3. "A government subsidy given to electric car buyers will increase the supply of electric cars."
  4. "If input costs rise, the supply curve shifts to the right because producers try to sell more to cover their costs."

Real-World Applications

  1. Energy Markets. In the early 2010s, hydraulic fracturing technology allowed producers to extract oil and natural gas from rock formations that were previously inaccessible. Using supply analysis, explain what happened to the supply of oil and natural gas in North America. What was the likely effect on prices?
  2. Labor as an Input. A shortage of truck drivers raises wages in the trucking industry. Use supply analysis to explain how this affects the supply of goods transported by truck, such as retail products and grocery items.
  3. Coffee and Climate. Coffee is grown in narrow equatorial regions where temperature and rainfall must fall within specific ranges. Scientists project that climate change will reduce the area suitable for coffee cultivation over the next 30 years. Using supply analysis, explain what this suggests about the future supply and price of coffee, all else equal.
  4. Housing Supply. Many cities in the United States have strict zoning laws that limit new residential construction. Using supply analysis, explain how zoning restrictions affect the supply of housing and the likely consequence for housing prices.

Chapter 29 — Front Matter

Chapter Introduction

Every morning, millions of people interact with the global economy before they even leave their homes. The coffee in a morning cup may have been grown in Ethiopia, roasted in Germany, and sold by a company headquartered in the United States. The smartphone on the nightstand may contain chips designed in California, manufactured in Taiwan, assembled in China, and shipped through ports in South Korea. The shirt hanging in the closet may have been sewn from cotton grown in India, spun into fabric in Bangladesh, and distributed through a logistics network spanning four continents.

This is not an accident. It is the product of comparative advantage, voluntary exchange, specialization, and the institutions—trade agreements, exchange rate systems, ports, shipping networks, financial markets—that make international commerce possible.

Chapter 29 brings together the microeconomic and macroeconomic reasoning developed throughout this textbook and applies it to the global economy. Students will learn why countries trade, how prices adjust across borders, what exchange rates are and why they matter, and how trade policy shapes winners and losers. They will also learn that international economics is not simply economics applied to larger maps. Trade across national borders involves currency conversion, political decisions, distributional consequences, and adjustment costs that purely domestic markets do not.

Opening Question

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain why countries export and import, calculate comparative advantage in a trade context, analyze the effects of tariffs and quotas, read exchange rate tables, reason through appreciation and depreciation, understand what global supply chains are and how they work, and evaluate trade policy debates using economic reasoning rather than slogans.

Section 29.1

Why Countries Trade

Why does Japan import wheat from the United States? Why does the United States import electronics from South Korea? Why does Germany export automobiles to countries that manufacture their own cars?

The answer comes from a principle introduced in Chapter 5: comparative advantage. Countries—like individuals and firms—can benefit from specialization and exchange even when one country is more productive than another at producing everything. What matters is not absolute productivity but relative opportunity cost.

Recall from Chapter 5 that when two parties have different opportunity costs, they can both gain by specializing in the good where their opportunity cost is lower and trading for the good where their opportunity cost is higher. This same logic applies to nations. If France can produce wine at a lower opportunity cost than Germany, and Germany can produce precision machinery at a lower opportunity cost than France, then both countries gain by specializing and trading. Both countries consume outside their own production possibilities.

Why Opportunity Costs DifferDescriptionExample
Factor endowmentsRelative abundance of land, labor, capital, and natural resourcesBrazil's land suited to coffee and soybeans; Saudi Arabia's petroleum reserves
Technology and human capitalAccumulated knowledge, technical skill, and production methodsEducated workforces with lower opportunity costs in knowledge-intensive industries
Economies of scaleCosts fall as output rises when production has large fixed costsLarge-scale aircraft, semiconductor, and pharmaceutical industries
Historical and institutional factorsPast investment, industry clusters, and supply chain networksEstablished manufacturing hubs that persist beyond current cost advantages
Trade Expands Consumption Possibilities

Trade expands what a country can consume beyond what it can produce. A country can specialize in goods where it has comparative advantage, export those goods, and use the export revenue to import goods it does not produce efficiently. This is why even large, diversified economies—the United States, Germany, Japan, China—engage heavily in trade. Trade is not a sign of economic weakness; it is a mechanism for increasing total production and consumption.

Section 29.2

Imports and Exports

Exports are goods and services produced domestically and sold to buyers in other countries. Imports are goods and services produced in other countries and purchased by domestic buyers. Both goods and services—transportation, finance, tourism, education, consulting, software—can be traded internationally, and services trade is growing rapidly relative to goods trade in many developed economies.

Total trade is often measured as exports plus imports, expressed in dollar value or as a percentage of GDP. Countries with high trade-to-GDP ratios—Singapore, Belgium, the Netherlands, South Korea—are sometimes called open economies. Countries with lower ratios are said to be more closed. The United States has a relatively moderate trade-to-GDP ratio because of its large domestic market; many smaller European countries have higher ratios because of regional economic integration.

The Trade Balance

Trade Balance = Exports − Imports

When exports exceed imports, a country runs a trade surplus. When imports exceed exports, a country runs a trade deficit. Trade deficits are not automatically bad, and trade surpluses are not automatically good—the meaning depends on economic context.

Bilateral vs. Multilateral Trade

A country may run a surplus with some trading partners and a deficit with others. The bilateral trade balance measures the balance between two specific countries. The multilateral trade balance measures the total across all partners. A bilateral trade deficit with a specific country is not always economically meaningful—a household does not expect to run a balanced "trade account" with every store it shops at. What matters economically is the overall pattern.

Section 29.3

Comparative Advantage in International Trade

Comparative advantage—introduced in Chapter 5—is the foundation of trade theory. It explains why mutually beneficial trade is possible even when one country is more productive in absolute terms. A country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce that good at a lower opportunity cost than its trading partner.

Wheat (bushels per worker)Cloth (yards per worker)
Northland105
Southland64
Example 29.3.1
Calculating comparative advantage between Northland and Southland
  • Step 1Opportunity cost of wheat in Northland: 5/10 = ½ yard of cloth per bushel. In Southland: 4/6 = ⅔ yard of cloth per bushel.
  • Step 2Opportunity cost of cloth in Northland: 10/5 = 2 bushels of wheat per yard. In Southland: 6/4 = 1.5 bushels of wheat per yard.
  • Step 3Northland's opportunity cost of wheat (½) is lower than Southland's (⅔) → Northland has comparative advantage in wheat.
  • Step 4Southland's opportunity cost of cloth (1.5) is lower than Northland's (2) → Southland has comparative advantage in cloth.
  • ConclusionNorthland should specialize in and export wheat; Southland should specialize in and export cloth—even though Northland is absolutely more productive in both goods (10 > 6 wheat, 5 > 4 cloth).
Key takeaway: Absolute advantage in both goods does not eliminate the gains from trade. Comparative advantage does.
Terms of Trade

The terms of trade are the actual prices at which countries exchange goods. For trade to benefit both parties, the terms of trade must fall between the two countries' domestic opportunity costs—in this example, between ½ and ⅔ yard of cloth per bushel of wheat.

Limits of the Simple Model

Real trade involves many countries and millions of goods. Comparative advantage shifts over time as technology, skills, and factor endowments change. Trade has distributional effects—some groups gain, others face adjustment costs. Strategic considerations (defense, supply chain security, food security) sometimes lead governments to support industries regardless of comparative advantage. These complications do not invalidate the framework; they remind students that economic models are tools for reasoning, not perfect descriptions of reality.

Section 29.4

Trade Balances

A country's trade balance reflects the difference between what it sells abroad and what it buys from abroad. But the trade balance is not just an accounting entry—it is connected to the broader macroeconomy through saving, investment, and international capital flows.

The Saving-Investment Connection

Trade Balance ≈ National Saving − Domestic Investment

When a country saves more than it invests domestically, the surplus saving flows abroad and the country runs a trade surplus. When a country invests more than it saves domestically, it attracts foreign capital and tends to run a trade deficit. A country cannot eliminate a trade deficit simply by imposing tariffs on one country if the underlying saving-investment imbalance remains.

Resist Simple Narratives

"Trade deficits mean we are losing." Not necessarily—deficits can reflect high consumer demand and attractive investment opportunities. "Trade surpluses always mean strength." Not necessarily—a surplus may reflect weak domestic consumption. "Every country should have balanced trade." Not generally true—capital flows naturally create imbalances. What matters is whether imbalances are sustainable and what they reflect about underlying economic conditions.

Practice 29.4Trade Balance Reasoning

  2. Why might a tariff on one trading partner fail to reduce a country's overall trade deficit?
  3. Give one reason a trade deficit could coincide with a strong economy, and one reason a trade surplus could coincide with a weak economy.

Section 29.5

Tariffs and Quotas

Even when trade is mutually beneficial in aggregate, governments frequently intervene in trade. The two most common tools of trade policy are tariffs and quotas. A tariff is a tax on imported goods, collected by the government of the importing country when goods cross the border.

Effects of a Tariff

Suppose the United States imposes a tariff on imported steel. The tariff raises the price of imported steel in the U.S. market: domestic steel producers benefit from higher prices and sell more; domestic steel consumers lose, paying higher prices; the government collects tariff revenue equal to the tariff rate multiplied by the quantity imported; and a deadweight loss arises because resources are used to produce steel domestically that could have been imported more cheaply.

Who Really Pays the Tariff?

Tariff incidence matters: in most cases, domestic consumers bear a substantial share of the cost through higher prices, even though importers formally pay the tariff at the border. In some cases, foreign exporters absorb part of the cost by lowering prices to maintain market share.

Quotas and Other Trade Barriers

A quota is a legal limit on the quantity of a good that can be imported. Restricting imports raises the domestic price above the world price, similar to a tariff. Domestic producers benefit; domestic consumers pay higher prices. Unlike a tariff, a quota does not generate government revenue (unless import licenses are auctioned)—the value that would have been tariff revenue instead flows to whoever holds the right to import, often foreign exporters or domestic license holders. Quotas can be harder to administer and do not automatically let more goods in if demand rises.

Other trade barriers include subsidies to domestic producers, regulatory barriers (sanitary standards, labeling requirements), local content requirements, and antidumping duties.

Example 29.5.1
Calculating tariff effects on steel
  • SetupWorld price of steel = $400/ton. Domestic supply = 1,000 tons; domestic demand = 4,000 tons at $400. A $100 tariff raises price to $500; domestic supply rises to 1,500 tons; domestic demand falls to 3,200 tons.
  • Imports before4,000 − 1,000 = 3,000 tons
  • Imports after3,200 − 1,500 = 1,700 tons
  • Tariff revenue1,700 tons × $100 = $170,000
  • DistributionProducers gain (500 more tons sold at a $100 higher price); consumers lose (paying $100 more on 3,200 tons); deadweight loss arises from the inefficient 500-ton domestic increase and the 800 tons of lost consumption.
Key takeaway: A tariff redistributes income from consumers to producers and government, while shrinking total economic surplus.

Section 29.6

Trade Agreements

Trade agreements are formal arrangements between countries that set rules for trade—typically reducing or eliminating tariffs, establishing dispute-resolution procedures, and setting common standards for intellectual property, investment, labor, and environmental practices.

TypeDescriptionExample
BilateralAgreement between two countriesA U.S.–South Korea free trade agreement
RegionalAgreement among multiple countries in a geographic regionUSMCA; the European Union; ASEAN
MultilateralAgreement operating through international institutionsThe World Trade Organization (WTO), with 164 member countries
Free trade areaEliminates tariffs between members; each sets its own external tariffsMany regional free trade agreements
Customs unionEliminates internal tariffs and sets a common external tariffThe European Union
Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion

Trade agreements can both create trade (replacing costly domestic production with cheaper imports from partner countries) and divert trade (shifting imports from a low-cost non-member country to a higher-cost member country because tariffs make non-member goods more expensive). Trade creation improves efficiency; trade diversion can reduce it.

Section 29.7

Exchange Rates

When a U.S. company wants to import goods from Japan, it must pay in Japanese yen. The exchange rate is the price at which one currency trades for another. Exchange rates can be expressed as direct quotation (price of one unit of foreign currency in domestic currency, e.g., $1.10 per euro) or indirect quotation (price of one unit of domestic currency in foreign currency, e.g., €0.91 per dollar).

Currency PairExchange Rate

USD / GBP ($ per £)1.25
USD / JPY ($ per ¥)0.0067

Cross-Rates

To find euros per pound from the table above: 1.25 / 1.10 ≈ 1.14 euros per pound.

Nominal vs. Real Exchange Rates

Real Exchange Rate = Nominal Exchange Rate × (Domestic Price Level / Foreign Price Level)

The real exchange rate matters for trade competitiveness. If a country's prices rise faster than its trading partners', its real exchange rate appreciates even if the nominal rate is unchanged—making exports more expensive and imports cheaper.

Exchange rates in most major economies are determined by supply and demand in foreign exchange markets. Demand for a currency comes from foreigners who want to buy that country's goods, services, or assets. Supply comes from domestic residents who want to buy foreign goods, services, or assets. Relative interest rates, relative inflation, income levels, expectations, and central bank intervention all shift these curves.

Section 29.8

Appreciation and Depreciation

When a currency's value rises relative to other currencies, it has appreciated. When it falls, it has depreciated. If the dollar moves from $1.00 per euro to $1.20 per euro, the euro has appreciated (it costs more dollars); equivalently, the dollar has depreciated.

Memory Aid

Appreciation = a currency buys more of another currency. Depreciation = a currency buys less.

ChangeEffect on ExportsEffect on ImportsEffect on Trade Balance
Currency appreciatesMore expensive for foreign buyers → exports fallCheaper for domestic buyers → imports riseMoves toward deficit (or surplus shrinks)
Currency depreciatesCheaper for foreign buyers → exports riseMore expensive for domestic buyers → imports fallMoves toward surplus (or deficit shrinks)
Example 29.8.1
Exchange rate conversion: a German camera
  • SetupA German camera costs €800. Exchange rate is 1.15 USD per euro.
  • Dollar price$800 × 1.15 = $920
  • After appreciationIf the dollar appreciates to 1.05 USD per euro: $800 × 1.05 = $840
  • InterpretationDollar appreciation makes the camera cheaper in dollar terms, which would tend to increase U.S. imports of German goods.
Key takeaway: Currency appreciation works like a price decrease for foreign goods bought with the appreciating currency.

Section 29.9

Global Supply Chains

A supply chain is the sequence of steps involved in producing and delivering a product from raw materials to final consumer. In modern international trade, supply chains are often global—spread across many countries, each contributing a different stage of production. A modern aircraft may contain fuselage sections from Japan, engines from France and the UK, and panels from Italy.

DriverWhy It Mattered
Lower trade barriersReduced tariffs made it viable to move components across borders
Transportation technologyContainerized shipping cut the cost of moving goods across oceans
Information and communication technologyDigital coordination across continents in real time
Factor cost differencesLabor-intensive stages shifted to lower-wage countries; knowledge-intensive stages stayed in high-wage countries
Trade Statistics Can Mislead

If a U.S. smartphone contains components from South Korea, Japan, Germany, and China, assembled in China and exported to the United States, the full value of the phone may be counted as a Chinese export—even though much of the value was added elsewhere. Value-added trade measures try to capture how much value was actually produced in each country, and often show countries are more interdependent than bilateral balances suggest.

Resilience vs. Efficiency

The COVID-19 pandemic revealed supply chain vulnerabilities: when production concentrates in a few countries and a crisis disrupts those countries, supply chains can fail. Many governments have reconsidered the tradeoffs between efficiency and resilience as a result.

Section 29.10

Globalization, Adjustment Costs, and Policy Tradeoffs

Globalization refers broadly to the increasing integration of economies, cultures, and societies across national borders—through trade, foreign direct investment, global supply chains, capital flows, migration, and the spread of technology and information. The pace of economic globalization accelerated sharply after World War II and especially after the 1980s.

Economic research provides substantial evidence that trade raises average living standards over time. South Korea moved from one of the world's poorest countries in the 1950s to a high-income economy, driven substantially by export-led growth. Economies that isolated themselves from trade have remained much poorer than their geographic neighbors. Lower prices for manufactured goods have benefited consumers globally, with particular gains for lower-income households.

Gains Are Real but Unequally Distributed

Trade tends to benefit factors of production used intensively in export industries and tends to hurt factors that compete with imports. In high-income countries, workers with high skills and education tend to gain as knowledge-intensive industries expand, while workers in import-competing manufacturing face wage pressure, layoffs, and plant closures—often concentrated in specific communities.

Adjustment costs are real: unemployment during transition, geographic immobility, and skills mismatch. Recent research—particularly on rapid import competition from China in the 2000s—has shown these costs can be substantial and persistent for affected workers and communities.

TradeoffDescription
Efficiency vs. distributionFree trade maximizes total output, but gains and losses are distributed unequally
Short run vs. long runProtection may save jobs short-term but encourages inefficiency; liberalization may cost jobs short-term but expands long-run possibilities
Domestic vs. global welfareTariffs may benefit domestic producers at the expense of domestic consumers and foreign exporters; governments answer to domestic voters
Security vs. efficiencyEfficient global production can create supply chain vulnerability; domestic production of critical goods may be worth an efficiency cost
Policy Responses to Adjustment Costs

Economists broadly favor allowing trade to proceed while compensating those who bear adjustment costs: trade adjustment assistance, education and retraining investment, place-based policies for affected communities, and progressive redistribution. Even well-designed assistance programs have had mixed records—retraining often does not fully replace lost wages, and workers displaced in their 40s and 50s face particularly difficult transitions. These realities argue for taking adjustment costs seriously, not for abandoning trade.

Chapter 29 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 29.1Countries trade based on comparative advantage—relative, not absolute, opportunity cost. Differences in factor endowments, technology, economies of scale, and history explain why opportunity costs differ across nations, and trade lets countries consume beyond their own production possibilities.
  • Sec 29.2Exports are domestic output sold abroad; imports are foreign output purchased domestically. The trade balance is exports minus imports. Surpluses and deficits are not automatically good or bad, and bilateral imbalances matter less than the overall multilateral pattern.
  • Sec 29.3A country has comparative advantage in a good if its opportunity cost of producing that good is lower than a trading partner's. Terms of trade must lie between the two countries' opportunity costs for trade to benefit both. The simple model has real-world limits.
  • Sec 29.4The trade balance is linked to national saving minus domestic investment. Simple narratives—deficits as losses, surpluses as strength, balance as the goal—oversimplify a relationship rooted in macroeconomic fundamentals.
  • Sec 29.5Tariffs tax imports, raising domestic prices, benefiting domestic producers, generating government revenue, and creating deadweight loss. Quotas restrict import quantities, raising prices without generating revenue. Protectionist arguments have situational merit but generally carry real efficiency costs.
  • Sec 29.6Bilateral, regional, and multilateral trade agreements reduce barriers and set rules. Free trade areas and customs unions differ in their treatment of external tariffs. Agreements can create trade (efficiency gain) or divert trade (efficiency loss).
  • Sec 29.7The exchange rate is the price of one currency in another. It can be quoted directly or indirectly, and the real exchange rate adjusts the nominal rate for price-level differences. Exchange rates are set by supply and demand for currencies in foreign exchange markets.
  • Sec 29.8Appreciation makes a country's exports more expensive and its imports cheaper; depreciation does the opposite. Exchange rate regimes range from fully floating to fixed pegs to managed floats, each with tradeoffs between stability and monetary independence.
  • Sec 29.9Global supply chains spread production stages across many countries, driven by lower trade barriers, shipping technology, communication technology, and factor cost differences. They boost efficiency but can distort trade statistics and create vulnerability to disruption.
  • Sec 29.10Globalization has raised average living standards but distributed gains and losses unevenly, concentrating adjustment costs on specific workers and communities. Sound policy lets trade proceed while addressing distributional consequences through assistance, retraining, and redistribution.

terms of trade
The actual price ratio at which countries exchange goods in international markets.
trade adjustment assistance
Government programs providing income support, job training, and other assistance to workers displaced by import competition.
trade agreement
A formal arrangement between countries establishing rules, tariff levels, and procedures for international trade.
trade balance
Exports minus imports; positive when exports exceed imports (surplus), negative when imports exceed exports (deficit).
trade creation
The efficiency gain when a trade agreement leads countries to replace costly domestic production with cheaper imports from partner countries.
trade diversion
The efficiency loss when a trade agreement shifts imports from low-cost non-member countries to higher-cost member countries.
value-added trade
Trade measured by the value contributed by each country in a global supply chain, rather than the full value of the final good.
World Trade Organization (WTO)
The international institution that establishes and enforces rules governing trade among its member countries.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 29.1
Calculating Comparative Advantage — Canada and Mexico
  • SetupCanada: 20 lumber or 5 automobiles per worker per day. Mexico: 8 lumber or 4 automobiles per worker per day.
  • Step 1Canada's opportunity cost of lumber = 5/20 = ¼ automobile
  • Step 2Mexico's opportunity cost of lumber = 4/8 = ½ automobile
  • Step 3Canada's lower cost (¼ < ½) → Canada has comparative advantage in lumber.
  • Step 4Canada's opportunity cost of autos = 20/5 = 4 lumber; Mexico's = 8/4 = 2 lumber
  • Step 5Mexico's lower cost (2 < 4) → Mexico has comparative advantage in automobiles.
Conclusion: Canada exports lumber; Mexico exports automobiles. Mutually beneficial terms of trade lie between ¼ and ½ automobile per unit of lumber.
Worked Example 29.2
Exchange Rate Conversions — A German Camera
  • SetupExchange rate 1.15 USD per euro; camera costs €800.
  • At 1.15$800 × 1.15 = $920
  • At 1.05$800 × 1.05 = $840
Conclusion: Dollar appreciation makes the camera cheaper in dollar terms, tending to increase U.S. imports from Germany.
Worked Example 29.3
Tariff Effects on the Steel Market
  • SetupWorld price $400/ton; domestic supply 1,000, demand 4,000 at $400. After a $100 tariff (price $500), supply = 1,500, demand = 3,200.
  • Imports before4,000 − 1,000 = 3,000 tons
  • Imports after3,200 − 1,500 = 1,700 tons
  • Tariff revenue1,700 × $100 = $170,000
Conclusion: Producers gain, consumers lose, and deadweight loss arises from inefficient added domestic production plus lost consumption.

Section B: Comparative Advantage Problems

  1. Two countries—Arcadia and Borealia—produce fish and timber. Per labor-hour, Arcadia produces 12 fish or 3 timber; Borealia produces 8 fish or 4 timber.
    1. What is the opportunity cost of one fish in each country?
    2. What is the opportunity cost of one unit of timber in each country?
    3. Which country has comparative advantage in fish? In timber?
    4. Which good should Arcadia export? What terms of trade would benefit both countries?
  2. A small country produces 100 clothing or 50 electronics per worker per year; its partner produces 60 clothing or 30 electronics.
    1. Calculate opportunity costs for each good in each country.
    2. Which country has comparative advantage in each good?
    3. Explain why comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, determines the direction of trade.
  3. Country M's opportunity cost of wheat is 2 units of cloth; Country N's is 3 units of cloth.
    1. Which country has comparative advantage in wheat?
    2. Which has comparative advantage in cloth?
    3. Identify a terms-of-trade ratio allowing mutually beneficial exchange.

Section C: Exchange Rate Problems

  1. The exchange rate is 110 yen per dollar. A U.S. video game costs $60.
    1. How many yen does the game cost a Japanese buyer?
    2. If the dollar appreciates to 130 yen per dollar, how many yen does the game cost?
    3. Has the game become more or less expensive for Japanese buyers? What is likely to happen to U.S. exports?
  2. The exchange rate is 1.20 USD per euro. A French handbag costs €400.
    1. What is the dollar price for a U.S. buyer?
    2. The dollar depreciates so 1 euro costs $1.40. What is the new dollar price?
    3. How does dollar depreciation affect U.S. consumers buying European goods, and U.S. exporters selling to Europe?
  3. The exchange rate between the Canadian dollar and U.S. dollar is 1.30 C$ per U.S. dollar.
    1. How much does a $500 U.S. computer cost in Canadian dollars?
    2. How much does a C$780 Canadian ski jacket cost in U.S. dollars?
    3. If Canada experiences higher inflation than the U.S. and the nominal rate stays fixed, what happens to the real exchange rate and Canada's trade competitiveness?

Section D: Tariff and Quota Analysis

  1. Imported furniture: world price $200/unit; domestic supply at $200 = 300 units, demand = 800 units. After a $50 tariff (price $250): supply = 400, demand = 650.
    1. How many units were imported before and after the tariff?
    2. How much tariff revenue does the government collect?
    3. Who gains from the tariff? Who loses?
    4. Explain why there is a deadweight loss.
  2. Compare a tariff and a quota that both reduce textile imports from 500,000 to 300,000 units per year, producing the same price increase.
    1. Which policy generates government revenue? Which does not?
    2. Who captures the value of the import restriction under each policy?
    3. If import demand grows, which policy automatically allows more imports? Explain.
    4. Which policy do economists tend to prefer as a trade restriction, and why?
  3. A government proposes a steel tariff on national security grounds.
    1. Explain the national security argument in economic terms.
    2. Identify two groups that benefit and two that lose.
    3. What is the efficiency cost of a tariff versus a domestic subsidy to achieve the same security goal?
    4. Does the national security argument justify the tariff? What additional information would help evaluate this claim?

Section E: Global Trade and Globalization

  1. Define a global supply chain. Give an original example of a product involving production stages in at least four countries, and explain each country's contribution.
  2. Why might bilateral trade statistics overstate or distort the trade balance between two countries linked through global supply chains?
  3. A country joins a regional free trade agreement. Trade with member Country A expands because A's goods are cheaper than domestic production. Trade with non-member Country B (previously cheaper) falls because the tariff makes A's goods appear cheaper.
    1. Identify the trade creation and trade diversion.
    2. Which effect improves global efficiency? Which may reduce it?
    3. What additional information would help evaluate whether joining improves overall welfare?
  4. A manufacturing worker earning $55,000 loses her job when her employer moves production overseas. After 18 months, she finds a job paying $40,000.
    1. What is the adjustment cost in this scenario?
    2. Is this consistent with the claim that trade increases average national income? Explain.
    3. What policies might reduce adjustment costs for workers like her?
    4. Should adjustment costs change your view of whether free trade is good for the country overall? Explain using the positive/normative distinction.

Economic Reasoning Questions

  1. A politician argues: "We have a $500 billion trade deficit with Country X. We should impose tariffs on Country X until they buy as much from us as we buy from them." Evaluate using economic reasoning: What determines the trade balance? Can tariffs on one country eliminate the overall deficit? Who bears the cost?
  2. A country with a floating exchange rate and independent monetary policy considers joining a currency union, giving up independent monetary policy. Identify at least three tradeoffs that should guide this decision.
  3. Economist A says free trade always increases total national income and is therefore right policy. Economist B says free trade raises average income but creates winners and losers and requires careful attention to distribution. Is Economist A making a positive claim, a normative claim, or both? Is Economist B's critique a reason to oppose trade or to accompany it with other policies?
  4. A country has comparative advantage in a commodity subject to volatile world prices and dwindling natural supply. Should it specialize and trade, or diversify even at higher-than-world-market cost? Use opportunity cost, risk, short-run vs. long-run tradeoffs, and adjustment costs to structure your answer.

Error Analysis

Identify the error in each statement and explain the correct economic reasoning.

  1. "Our country has a trade deficit with Country Z, so we are losing in our trade relationship with them. Our money is flowing out and never coming back."
  2. "A tariff is free money for the government. It makes foreign companies pay for the privilege of selling in our market."
  3. "Country A has absolute advantage in producing both wheat and cloth. Therefore, there is no basis for trade between Country A and Country B."
  4. "When the dollar depreciates, it is bad for the United States because everything becomes more expensive."
  5. "Free trade agreements just eliminate trade barriers. They cannot have any negative effects on any group within a country."
  6. "Trade deficits must be eliminated because they represent money leaving the country that is never returned."

Looking Ahead
Chapter 30: Economics, Society, and Public Decision-Making

Chapter 30, the capstone chapter of this textbook, brings together the reasoning tools developed across all seven parts—opportunity cost, incentives, marginal thinking, market analysis, market failure, macroeconomic measurement, policy evaluation, and international economics—and applies them to broad questions of public decision-making. It revisits the distinction between positive and normative economics, examines how economic reasoning applies to environmental decisions, education, healthcare, technology, inequality, and global cooperation, and asks how students should use economic knowledge responsibly as citizens, workers, and decision-makers. The key lesson of Chapter 29 to carry forward: trade involves real tradeoffs. Markets for goods and currencies coordinate enormous amounts of international economic activity, but coordination does not mean that outcomes are equally good for everyone. Understanding who gains, who loses, and why is an essential part of economic literacy—and of good citizenship.

Chapter 1 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

“Economics is the study of how society manages its scarce resources.” — N. Gregory Mankiw

Every day, without realizing it, you are an economist. When you decide whether to sleep an extra hour or study for a test, you are weighing costs and benefits. When a city council decides whether to build a new park or repair old roads, it is choosing how to use limited money. When a company decides whether to hire more workers or invest in new machines, it is responding to incentives and expectations. When a government debates whether to raise taxes or cut spending, it is navigating tradeoffs between competing goals.

Economics is the disciplined study of all of these decisions. It is not just the study of money, businesses, or stock markets. It is the study of how people, firms, governments, and societies make choices when resources are limited. This chapter opens the door to economic thinking — introducing the scope of economics, the tools economists use, the distinctions that matter, and the habits of mind that will serve you throughout this course and far beyond it.

Opening Question

Think about a decision you made today that involved a tradeoff — how you spent an hour, a dollar, or your attention. What did you choose, and what did that choice cost you in terms of the next best alternative? By the end of this chapter, you will have precise vocabulary — positive versus normative claims, models and assumptions, microeconomics versus macroeconomics — for analyzing choices like this one across personal life, business, and public policy.

Section 1.1

What Economics Studies

At the heart of economics is a single, unavoidable fact: resources are limited, but human wants are not. There is only so much time in a day, only so much land, water, and labor — yet people want food, shelter, education, healthcare, entertainment, and much more. Because resources cannot satisfy every desire, choices must be made.

Definition

Economics is the social science that studies how individuals, households, firms, governments, and societies make choices under conditions of scarcity.

Social science. Economics studies human behavior and social systems, much like sociology, political science, and psychology. Unlike the natural sciences, which study the physical world, economics focuses on decisions and their consequences.

Individuals, households, firms, governments, and societies. Economics studies choices made at every level — a teenager choosing between two jobs, a household deciding how to spend its income, a firm deciding what to produce, a city government deciding how to spend tax revenue, and an entire society deciding how to organize its economy.

Choices under scarcity. Because we cannot have everything, we must choose — and every choice involves giving something up, even when the cost is invisible.

What Economics Covers

Many students expect economics to be mainly about stock markets, banking, taxes, or business strategy. These topics are part of economics, but the field covers far more — how prices are set in markets for food, housing, and labor; why some countries are rich and others poor; what causes unemployment and inflation; how firms grow; how trade affects workers and consumers; and what policies might reduce poverty or address climate change.

This is an enormous range of topics. What holds them together is not a single subject matter, but a way of thinking.

Economics as a Way of Thinking

Economists ask the same handful of questions about almost any decision: What are the costs and benefits? What is being given up? What incentives does this create? What happens at the margin — a little more or a little less? Who gains and who loses? What does the evidence show? Learning to ask these questions clearly is the central skill this course develops.

Section 1.2

Scarcity and Choice

Scarcity means that resources are insufficient to satisfy all human wants. Scarcity is not the same as poverty — a wealthy country and a wealthy individual face scarcity too, simply at a higher level of resources. Even a billionaire has only twenty-four hours in a day; even a government with a large budget cannot fund every program citizens might want.

Scarce ResourceWhy It Is LimitedExample
TimeEvery person has exactly 24 hours a day; time used one way cannot be used anotherHours spent working cannot also be spent sleeping or studying
Natural resourcesLand, water, minerals, and forests exist in finite quantitiesLand used for farming cannot simultaneously be used for housing
Capital goodsFactories, machines, and infrastructure are produced by human effort and limited in numberBuilding one type of infrastructure means not building another
Labor and skillWorkers have limited time, energy, and capabilityA surgeon performing one operation cannot perform another at the same time
MoneyHousehold incomes, government revenues, and firm budgets are all finiteA household cannot spend more than its income and savings allow

Because of scarcity, every choice involves a tradeoff — choosing one thing means giving up something else. Economists sometimes describe human wants as "unlimited," but this just means people always have additional things they would prefer to have if they could; there is no natural point at which a person or society wants nothing more. This does not make people greedy or irrational — it means that in a world of scarcity, choices always have costs.

Example 1.2.1
Scarcity forces a tradeoff in a city budget
  • SituationA city has a fixed transportation budget. It could repair existing roads, build a new subway line, expand bus service, or add bicycle paths.
  • The constraintThe budget is limited, so the city cannot fund every option at the level residents might want.
  • The choiceChoosing one option means the budget is no longer available for the others — the city must decide, and economics provides tools for thinking about that decision clearly.
Key takeaway: Scarcity does not just describe individuals — it shapes the choices of firms, governments, and entire societies.
Section 1.3

Microeconomics and Macroeconomics

To organize the study of such a large field, economists divide it into two broad branches. Microeconomics studies individual units of economic decision-making — the choices of consumers and households, the production and pricing decisions of firms, and the behavior of specific markets. The prefix micro- comes from the Greek word for "small."

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole — total output, employment, the overall price level, growth, government spending and taxation, the money supply, and international trade. The prefix macro- comes from the Greek word for "large."

BranchFocusExample Questions
MicroeconomicsIndividual consumers, firms, and specific marketsHow does a rise in gasoline prices affect how much gasoline people buy? Why do pharmaceutical firms charge different prices in different countries?
MacroeconomicsThe economy as a whole — output, employment, prices, growthWhat causes inflation to accelerate? Why do some countries grow much faster than others over long periods?
Trees and the Forest

Microeconomics studies individual trees; macroeconomics studies the forest. Macroeconomic outcomes — like total employment — result from millions of individual microeconomic decisions, while macroeconomic conditions, like a recession, shape the environment in which individuals and firms decide. This textbook covers both: Parts I through IV focus on microeconomics, and Parts V through VII focus on macroeconomics and international economics.

Section 1.4

Households, Firms, Governments, and Societies

Economics studies four main types of decision-makers, each facing its own constraints and incentives.

Decision-MakerRoleExample Decisions
HouseholdsThe basic units of consumption — a person, family, or group sharing income and expensesHow much to spend versus save; how many hours of labor to supply; how to invest savings
FirmsOrganizations that produce goods and services, from a one-person bakery to a global corporationWhat to produce; how many workers to hire; what prices to charge; how much to invest
GovernmentsInstitutions with authority to make and enforce rules, collect taxes, and provide public servicesWhat taxes to collect; what services to provide; how to regulate markets and redistribute income
SocietiesThe broadest unit — households, firms, and governments plus the institutions, norms, and legal systems that shape how they interactHow production and distribution are organized; who decides; what rules govern economic behavior
A Key Insight

Government policies, like market decisions, have consequences — intended and unintended, short-run and long-run, efficient and inefficient. Evaluating these consequences requires the same careful reasoning economists apply to any decision, whether made by a household, a firm, or a nation.

Section 1.5

Goods, Services, Resources, and Factors of Production

Goods are tangible products — physical objects that can be touched, stored, and transported, such as food, clothing, and smartphones. Services are intangible activities performed for others, such as a haircut, a medical examination, or banking. Services cannot be stored; they are produced and consumed simultaneously. Most modern economies produce a mix of both, with services making up an increasingly large share of output in high-income countries.

To produce goods and services, producers need resources — inputs economists group into four factors of production.

FactorDescriptionExample
LandAll natural resources provided by nature rather than produced by human effortPhysical land, water, minerals, timber, oil, solar and wind energy
LaborThe time and effort of human workers — physical and mentalAssembling a product, programming software, teaching a class
CapitalProduced means of production — goods used to make other goodsMachinery, tools, factories, computers, infrastructure
EntrepreneurshipThe ability to organize the other factors, start businesses, take risks, and innovateCombining land, labor, and capital in a new way to launch a venture
Why the Distinction Matters

The prices of these factors — wages for labor, rent for land, interest for capital — determine the incomes of households. A worker's wage depends on the demand for labor in their field; a landowner's rent depends on the demand for land in their location. Understanding income distribution starts with understanding how markets for factors of production work.

Section 1.6

Positive Economics and Normative Economics

One of the most important distinctions in economics — and one students frequently blur — is the difference between positive economics, which deals with facts and testable cause-and-effect claims, and normative economics, which deals with values and judgments about what should be done.

TypeWhat It ClaimsExample
PositiveA claim about what is or will happen — testable against evidence"Raising the minimum wage increases labor costs for employers."
NormativeA judgment about what is good, fair, or desirable — not settled by data alone"The government should raise the minimum wage."
Why This Distinction Matters

Consider the minimum wage debate. The positive question — what happens to employment, wages, and prices when the minimum wage rises — can be studied with evidence and models. The normative question — should we raise it — depends on how we weigh the interests of workers who gain higher wages against those who might lose jobs, and on values about fairness and the role of government. Evidence informs but does not settle normative questions. A careful thinker always asks: is this claim positive, or is it normative?

Section 1.7

Models, Assumptions, and Simplification

The real economy involves billions of people making decisions every day across millions of markets. No one can analyze all of this complexity directly. To make progress, economists build models — simplified representations of reality that highlight the most important features of a situation while setting aside less important details. A model is a tool for thinking, the way a map leaves out most physical detail but still helps you navigate.

Every model rests on assumptions — simplifications that make the model tractable and let economists isolate the relationship they want to study. The goal is not to find assumptions that are perfectly realistic, but assumptions that are useful: ones that produce a model whose predictions match real-world patterns closely enough to be informative.

Ceteris Paribus

Ceteris paribus is a Latin phrase meaning "all other things being equal" or "all other things held constant." When economists say a rise in gasoline prices will reduce the quantity of gasoline demanded, they mean this holds true assuming incomes, the prices of other goods, and consumer preferences stay unchanged.

In the real world, many things change at once. Analyzing one relationship at a time, holding other factors constant, is how economists build a clear understanding of complex interactions.

Evaluating a Model

A model should be judged not by whether its assumptions are perfectly realistic, but by the accuracy of its predictions, its internal logical consistency, and its usefulness — whether it illuminates something important that would otherwise be obscure. No single model captures everything; a good economist uses several, understands their assumptions, and knows their limits.

Section 1.8

Economics in Daily Life

Economics is not an abstract discipline disconnected from ordinary experience. It shows up in decisions made every day by individuals, families, businesses, and governments.

Decision TypeWhat's InvolvedExample
PersonalWeighing opportunity costs, tradeoffs, and incentives in everyday choicesChoosing a college major by weighing expected earnings against personal interest and cost
BusinessComparing additional cost against additional revenue before actingDeciding whether to raise prices, expand production, or hire more workers
PolicyWeighing who gains and who loses, and what is given up, when government actsRaising interest rates to slow inflation; restricting imports from a trading partner
A Note on Complexity

Economics provides tools for clearer thinking, but not simple, automatic answers to complex questions. Real-world decisions involve uncertainty, incomplete information, and conflicting values. Good economic reasoning helps navigate that complexity — separating facts from values, identifying opportunity costs, tracing incentive effects, and evaluating evidence — without eliminating it.

Section 1.9

How to Study Economics Successfully

Economics has its own methods, vocabulary, and modes of reasoning. Students who do well develop specific study habits.

Study Strategy
Habits of a Strong Economics Student
  • Read graphs actively — check both axes, the meaning of the slope, and whether a change is a movement along a curve or a shift of the curve.
  • Master key terms precisely — "demand" and "cost" mean something more specific in economics than in everyday speech.
  • Reason through examples — apply each concept to a decision from your own life, a news story, or a policy debate.
  • Distinguish the concept from the formula — understand what a calculation means before practicing the arithmetic.
  • Ask "what would happen if?" — trace the consequences of a price change, a tax, or a new technology using the models you learn.
  • Engage with evidence — practice asking what a table or graph shows, what it does not show, and whether the pattern could be explained differently.
  • Connect micro and macro — look for how individual decisions shape aggregate outcomes, and how aggregate conditions shape individual choices.
Chapter 1 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 1.1Economics is the social science that studies how individuals, households, firms, governments, and societies make choices when resources are limited. It covers scarcity, choice, incentives, tradeoffs, markets, prices, production, income, employment, inflation, money, growth, institutions, and policy.
  • Sec 1.2Scarcity is the fundamental economic condition: resources are insufficient to satisfy all human wants. Because scarcity exists, every choice involves a tradeoff — choosing one thing means giving something else up.
  • Sec 1.3Microeconomics studies individual decision-makers — consumers, firms, and markets. Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole — total output, employment, inflation, growth, and policy. Both branches are part of economics, and both are covered in this course.
  • Sec 1.4The main economic decision-makers are households (which consume goods and services and supply labor), firms (which produce goods and services), governments (which collect taxes, provide public goods, regulate markets, and redistribute income), and societies (which develop the institutions, norms, and legal systems that shape economic behavior).
  • Sec 1.5Production requires resources — land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship. The prices of these factors determine the incomes of households.
  • Sec 1.6Positive economics makes factual, testable claims about what is or will be. Normative economics makes value-based claims about what should be. Clear economic reasoning requires keeping these two types of claims distinct.
  • Sec 1.7Economists use models — simplified representations of reality — to analyze complex relationships. Models rest on assumptions, including the ceteris paribus assumption that other factors are held constant. Models should be judged by whether they produce useful, accurate insights, not by whether their assumptions are perfectly realistic.
  • Sec 1.8Economic reasoning applies to personal decisions, business decisions, and public policy. Learning to think like an economist — asking about opportunity costs, incentives, tradeoffs, and evidence — is useful far beyond any particular course.
  • Sec 1.9Students who read graphs actively, master technical vocabulary precisely, reason through examples, distinguish concepts from formulas, and engage with evidence develop a durable command of economic reasoning that transfers far beyond this course.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "Economics is just about money."

Correction: Money is one resource economists study, but economics is really about choices, scarcity, and tradeoffs in the broadest sense. Economists study time, labor, land, attention, healthcare, education, environmental quality, and much more. The economic way of thinking applies anywhere there is scarcity and choice — which is nearly everywhere.

Misconception 2 — "Scarcity only affects poor people or poor countries."

Correction: Scarcity is universal. Every person, firm, and government faces constraints, regardless of wealth. A wealthy country still must choose how to allocate its budget. A wealthy individual still has only 24 hours in a day. Scarcity does not mean poverty; it means that resources are finite relative to desires.

Misconception 3 — "If economists disagree, economics must not be a real science."

Correction: Economists do disagree — especially about normative questions and about empirical questions where the evidence is genuinely complex. But much of economics involves well-established positive findings that are widely agreed upon. Disagreement does not make economics unscientific; all scientific fields have areas of active debate. What matters is whether claims are tested against evidence and revised when evidence points against them.

Misconception 4 — "Economic models are useless because they don't reflect reality perfectly."

Correction: No model perfectly reflects reality — that is what makes it a model rather than reality itself. The question is whether a model is useful: does it generate accurate predictions in the situations we care about? The supply-and-demand model does not capture every feature of real markets, but it has proven enormously useful for understanding price determination across a huge range of goods and services.

Misconception 5 — "Positive economics is neutral and objective; normative economics is biased."

Correction: Positive economics aims to be objective, but doing it well still requires judgment about which questions to study and which evidence to emphasize. Normative economics is not automatically biased; it is simply explicit about values. Being clear about the values underlying a policy recommendation is more honest than pretending that all economic recommendations are purely technical.

Review Questions

Comprehension

  1. Define economics in your own words. What is the relationship between economics and scarcity?
  2. What is the difference between microeconomics and macroeconomics? Give two examples of questions each branch addresses.
  3. Name and briefly describe each of the four factors of production. Why does it matter which factors a firm uses?
  4. What is the difference between a positive economic statement and a normative economic statement? Why does this distinction matter?
  5. What is an economic model? What role do assumptions play in models? What is the ceteris paribus assumption?

Application

  1. A local government must choose between spending its available budget on (a) building a new elementary school, (b) repairing roads, or (c) expanding the public library. Explain the role of scarcity and tradeoffs in this decision.
  2. Classify each of the following as microeconomics or macroeconomics, and explain: (a) the effect of a new competitor on the prices charged by an airline; (b) the national unemployment rate in the aftermath of a recession; (c) a supermarket's decision to offer self-checkout to reduce labor costs; (d) the relationship between government borrowing and interest rates.
  3. Your friend argues: "We should cut corporate tax rates because lower taxes on businesses will cause them to invest more, hire more workers, and grow the economy." Separate the positive claims from the normative claims in this argument.
  4. A textbook model of the labor market assumes that wages adjust freely to bring supply and demand into balance. In reality, wages are often sticky — they do not adjust immediately. Does this mean the model is useless? Explain.
  5. Give one example from your own life of an economic decision that involved opportunity cost. What did you choose, and what did you give up?

Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Markets are powerful tools for coordinating economic decisions. When they work well, markets direct resources toward productive uses, set prices that reflect costs and benefits, and create incentives for innovation and efficiency. But markets do not always produce outcomes that societies consider fair, sustainable, or complete. Some goods and services are underprovided by markets. Some costs are shifted onto people who did not agree to bear them. Some outcomes leave large numbers of people without adequate income or access to basic necessities.

Governments respond to these situations through taxes, subsidies, regulation, public spending, and redistribution. These interventions shape the economic environment in which households, firms, and markets operate. They also create their own costs, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences.

This chapter introduces the economic role of government in a market economy. It explains why governments intervene, how they intervene, and what tradeoffs arise when they do. It covers taxes and tax incidence, subsidies, regulation, public spending, redistribution, and policy evaluation. The goal is not to tell students which policies are right, but to give them tools for thinking clearly about what government policies do, who is affected, and at what cost.

Opening Question

Think about a tax you have paid — sales tax on a purchase, or a tax withheld from a paycheck you have seen. Who actually decided the rate? Who do you think really bears the cost of that tax — is it always the party who legally pays it? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to answer that question precisely, for any tax, subsidy, or regulation you encounter.

Section 18.1

Why Governments Intervene in Economies

Markets rest on voluntary exchange. When a buyer and seller agree to a transaction at a mutually acceptable price, both typically expect to be better off. This is the core logic behind market efficiency: voluntary trades generate gains, and competition pushes prices toward levels that coordinate supply and demand effectively.

But markets operate within a broader social and institutional context. They require property rights, contract enforcement, and rule of law to function. They can fail to account for effects on people outside transactions. They can produce distributions of income and wealth that societies consider unacceptable. And they sometimes underproduce goods whose benefits spread widely beyond the buyer.

Economists identify several broad categories of reasons governments intervene in economies.

RationaleDescriptionExample
Correcting market failuresAligning private incentives with social costs and benefits when markets are inefficientTaxing pollution; subsidizing vaccination
Establishing and enforcing rulesDefining property rights, enforcing contracts, preventing fraudCourts, contract law, fraud statutes
Providing public goodsFunding goods whose benefits cannot be restricted to paying customersNational defense, basic research
Redistributing income and wealthAltering market-driven distributions through taxes, transfers, and spendingProgressive income tax; food assistance
Stabilizing the economyModerating recessions, inflation, and financial instabilityFiscal and monetary policy (Chapters 26–27)
Regulating industries and marketsConstraining outcomes such as unsafe products or monopoly pricingSafety standards, antitrust enforcement
Intervention Is Not Free

Government intervention involves real costs and tradeoffs. Administrative costs consume resources. Taxes create distortions. Regulations create compliance burdens. Well-intended policies can produce unintended consequences. Evaluating government intervention requires comparing benefits against costs — not assuming intervention is always better than markets, or always worse.

Section 18.2

Taxes

A tax is a compulsory payment collected by a government from households, firms, or other entities. Taxes are the primary source of revenue for governments, and they fund public spending, transfer programs, and debt service.

Taxes come in many forms. Understanding the basic types helps students analyze who pays taxes, how much they pay, and how taxes affect behavior.

18.2.1 Types of Taxes

Tax TypeWhat It TaxesNotes
Income taxEarnings from work, investments, businessPersonal and corporate; typically progressive
Payroll taxWages and salariesOften split between employee and employer; funds social insurance
Sales taxPurchases of goods and servicesProportional in rate, often regressive in effect
Excise taxSpecific goods (tobacco, alcohol, gasoline)Raises revenue and discourages negative-externality goods
Property taxValue of real estate or other assetsPrimary revenue source for many local governments
Estate/inheritance taxWealth transferred at deathIntended to limit intergenerational wealth concentration
TariffImported goodsCovered fully in Chapter 29

18.2.2 Tax Rates: Marginal and Average

The average tax rate is total tax paid divided by total income. The marginal tax rate is the rate applied to the last dollar of income earned. In a progressive system, additional income can push a taxpayer into a higher bracket, so the marginal rate may exceed the average rate.

Formulas

Average tax rate = Total tax paid ÷ Total income

Marginal tax rate = rate applied to the next (last) dollar earned

Example 18.2.1
Calculating average and marginal tax rates
  • Brackets$0–$20,000 at 10%; $20,001–$50,000 at 20%; above $50,000 at 30%.
  • HouseholdEarns $60,000.
  • Calculate10% × $20,000 = $2,000; 20% × $30,000 = $6,000; 30% × $10,000 = $3,000 Total tax = $11,000
  • Average$11,000 ÷ $60,000 ≈ 18.3%
  • Marginal30% — the rate on the last dollar earned, applying only to income above $50,000.
Key takeaway: The marginal rate applies only to income in the top bracket, not to the entire income.

18.2.3 Progressive, Proportional, and Regressive Taxes

A progressive tax takes a larger percentage of income from higher earners; the average rate rises with income. A proportional tax (flat tax) takes the same percentage at all income levels. A regressive tax takes a smaller percentage from higher earners; the average rate falls as income rises. Sales and many excise taxes are regressive in distributional effect even when their stated rate is uniform, because lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on taxable purchases.

Section 18.3

Tax Incidence

Taxes may be legally imposed on one party but economically borne by another. Tax incidence refers to who actually bears the economic burden of a tax — who bears the real cost in terms of reduced purchasing power or real income.

The economic incidence of a tax differs from its legal or statutory incidence. A tax levied on sellers can be passed on to buyers through higher prices. A tax levied on employers can be passed on to workers through lower wages. Who actually bears the burden depends on the relative elasticities of supply and demand.

18.3.1 Tax Incidence on Buyers and Sellers

Consider a tax of $T per unit imposed on sellers in a competitive market. The supply curve shifts upward by the amount of the tax. After the tax: the price paid by buyers rises above the original equilibrium price; the price received by sellers (after paying the tax) falls below the original equilibrium price; and the quantity traded falls. The gap between what buyers pay and what sellers receive equals the tax per unit.

Key Insight

The burden is shared between buyers and sellers. Neither fully escapes the tax if the other side has any price responsiveness.

18.3.2 Elasticity and Tax Incidence

ConditionWho Bears More of the BurdenExample
Demand inelastic, supply elasticBuyersCigarette and gasoline taxes
Demand elastic, supply inelasticSellersA taxed brand with many close substitutes

General principle: the side of the market with lower price responsiveness (more inelastic) bears more of the tax burden.

18.3.3 Tax Incidence in Labor Markets

The same logic applies to labor markets. If a payroll tax is imposed on employers and labor supply is relatively inelastic, workers bear most of the burden through lower wages, even though the tax is legally imposed on employers. The statutory incidence (who sends the payment) differs from the economic incidence (who bears the real cost). Research in labor economics consistently finds that workers bear a substantial share of payroll taxes nominally imposed on employers.

Common Misconception

"If a tax is collected from sellers, sellers pay it." Statutory incidence tells you who writes the check. Economic incidence tells you whose real income actually falls — and these are often different parties.

Example 18.3.1
Identifying incidence from price changes
  • Before taxPrice $10, quantity 500 units.
  • Tax$2 per unit on sellers. Buyer price rises to $11.50; seller net price falls to $9.50.
  • SplitBuyers: $1.50 of $2.00 = 75%  ·  Sellers: $0.50 of $2.00 = 25%
  • InterpretationBuyers bear the larger share, implying demand is less elastic than supply.
Key takeaway: Comparing the size of the buyer-side and seller-side price changes reveals relative elasticities.
Practice 18.3Tax Incidence

  2. A payroll tax is legally imposed on employers, yet wages in the affected industry fall by nearly the full amount of the tax. Explain this outcome using the concept of economic incidence.
  3. Explain, with an example, why a tax intended to fall on high-income households might shift part of its burden onto lower-income households.
Section 18.4

Subsidies

A subsidy is a payment from the government to a producer, consumer, or other entity, typically intended to encourage a particular activity or reduce the cost of a particular good or service. Subsidies are the mirror image of taxes: where a tax raises costs and discourages activity, a subsidy lowers costs and encourages activity.

18.4.1 How Subsidies Work

A per-unit subsidy to producers shifts the supply curve downward, reducing the price buyers pay and increasing quantity traded. The price sellers receive rises by less than the full subsidy. The benefit is shared between buyers and sellers in proportion to their relative elasticities — when demand is relatively inelastic, buyers capture more of the benefit; when supply is relatively inelastic, sellers capture more.

18.4.2 Rationale for Subsidies

RationaleExample
Correcting positive externalitiesEducation, vaccination, clean energy research
Reducing costs for low-income groupsFood, housing, healthcare, childcare subsidies
Supporting strategically important industriesNational security or technology sectors (contested)
Stabilizing prices or incomes in a sectorAgricultural subsidies

18.4.3 Costs and Tradeoffs of Subsidies

Subsidies must be funded through taxes, borrowing, or reduced spending elsewhere. They can lead to overproduction or overconsumption relative to what is socially efficient, and they can be captured by well-organized industries rather than flowing to intended beneficiaries.

Evaluating a Subsidy

Compare the benefits of the subsidized activity — corrected externalities, improved access, or other goals — against the full costs, including fiscal cost, distortions, and administrative overhead.

Section 18.5

Regulation

Regulation refers to rules and requirements that governments impose on the behavior of firms, industries, or individuals. Regulations are distinct from taxes and subsidies in that they directly constrain or require behavior rather than changing financial incentives. Regulation is pervasive in modern economies, covering product safety, environmental emissions, financial practices, workplace conditions, food and drug standards, land use, telecommunications, energy, transportation, and more.

18.5.1 Types of Regulation

TypeHow It WorksExample
Command-and-controlSpecific requirements firms must meet regardless of their own cost of complianceFixed emission limits, safety standards
Performance-basedOutcome targets with flexibility in methodFuel-economy targets met however firms choose
Market-basedPrice signals used to achieve goals while allowing flexible responseCarbon taxes, tradable emissions permits
Information disclosureRequires accurate information rather than restricting behavior directlyNutrition labels, financial disclosures
AntitrustPreserves competition by blocking monopolization or collusionMerger review (see Chapters 13–14)

18.5.2 Benefits of Regulation

Regulation can correct market failures arising from externalities, asymmetric information, or market power — limiting pollution, correcting information asymmetries about product or workplace risk, and reducing systemic financial risk. Effective regulation can bring private behavior closer to socially efficient levels and protect health, safety, and rights in ways markets do not automatically deliver.

18.5.3 Costs and Tradeoffs of Regulation

Regulation imposes compliance costs that can fall disproportionately on smaller firms. It can reduce flexibility and innovation, especially when rules specify particular technologies or processes. Poorly designed regulation can create unintended consequences — for example, shifting production to countries with weaker standards, or pushing consumers toward less safe alternatives.

Regulatory Capture

Regulatory capture occurs when regulated industries gain disproportionate influence over the agencies that regulate them, potentially producing regulations that serve industry interests more than public interests.

Section 18.6

Public Spending

Public spending refers to expenditures by governments on goods, services, and transfer payments, funded primarily through taxes and borrowing. Public spending serves several broad functions: providing public goods, funding government services and infrastructure, providing transfers to households, and paying for the machinery of government itself.

18.6.1 Categories of Public Spending

CategoryDescriptionCounted in GDP?
Government purchasesSpending on defense, education, infrastructure, courts, research, administrationYes
Transfer paymentsPayments to households not in exchange for current goods or services (retirement, disability, unemployment insurance, food and housing assistance)No
Interest on public debtRequired payments to service past borrowingNo
Why the Distinction Matters

Government purchases directly use resources — employing workers and buying inputs — so they are counted in GDP (Chapter 20). Transfer payments redistribute income but do not themselves represent the purchase of goods or services, so they are excluded from GDP even though they are a large share of many government budgets.

18.6.2 Public Goods and Government Provision

Public goods — nonexcludable and nonrival — tend to be underprovided by private markets because firms cannot capture sufficient revenue from benefits available to all. National defense, basic research, and disease surveillance typically require government provision or subsidy. Governments also provide some excludable, rival goods (such as public university education or toll roads) for reasons tied to equity or positive externalities.

18.6.3 Public Investment

Spending on infrastructure, education, research, and institutions can raise long-run productive capacity — reducing transaction costs, generating knowledge, and building human capital. These investments can support growth, but the scale and composition of public investment involve tradeoffs with current consumption, taxation, and debt.

Practice 18.6Public Spending

  2. Explain why transfer payments are excluded from GDP even though they represent large amounts of government spending.
  3. Give one example of public investment not mentioned in the text, and explain how it might raise long-run productivity.

Section 18.7

Redistribution and Transfer Programs

Market economies generate distributions of income and wealth that reflect productivity, education, capital ownership, family background, geography, and luck. Redistribution refers to policies that alter the distribution of income or wealth from what markets would produce, occurring through progressive taxes, transfer payments, targeted subsidies, and public provision of services.

18.7.1 Why Societies Redistribute

RationaleCore Idea
Poverty and minimum standardsEnsuring access to necessities regardless of labor market outcomes
Insurance and risk-sharingSocial insurance against unemployment, disability, illness, recession
Equity and fairnessAddressing inequalities tied to circumstances outside individual control
Efficiency argumentsA dollar may carry more marginal value to a low-income household than a high-income one

18.7.2 Types of Transfer Programs

TypeDescriptionTradeoff
UniversalBenefits regardless of incomeNo stigma, full coverage; costly
Means-testedBenefits only below an income/wealth thresholdTargets need; can create poverty traps, has eligibility costs
CategoricalTargets specific groups (children, elderly, disabled)Simplifies targeting within a category
In-kindSpecific goods/services (food, housing, healthcare)Ensures intended use; may not match preferences
CashMoney to use as recipient choosesRespects preferences; may be spent differently than intended

18.7.3 Transfer Programs and Labor Supply

When benefits phase out as income rises, recipients face high effective marginal tax rates on earned income — for each additional dollar earned, they lose some amount in benefits. This can reduce the financial return to working. Balancing adequate support against preserving strong work incentives is a central design challenge in transfer program policy.

The Poverty Trap

A poverty trap arises when high benefit-reduction rates create such high effective marginal tax rates that working more provides little or no net financial gain — sometimes discouraging work even though the recipient would prefer to earn more.

Section 18.8

Efficiency and Equity

Many policy debates involve a fundamental tension between efficiency — producing the maximum possible output from available resources, such that no reallocation could make someone better off without making someone else worse off — and equity, fairness in the distribution of economic outcomes. Efficiency concerns the size of the economic pie; equity concerns who gets what share.

18.8.1 The Efficiency-Equity Tradeoff

Policies that redistribute income can create efficiency costs. Taxes used to fund transfers may reduce incentives to work, save, or invest. Transfer programs with high effective marginal tax rates may reduce work effort. The size of this efficiency cost depends on the specific policy design and on how responsive behavior is to the changed incentives — in some cases modest, in others substantial.

18.8.2 Multiple Dimensions of Fairness

ConceptDefinition
Horizontal equityPeople in similar economic circumstances should be treated similarly
Vertical equityPeople with greater ability to pay should bear greater burdens
Equality of opportunitySimilar chances to develop skills and succeed, regardless of background
Equality of outcomesFocus on actual distributions of income and wealth themselves

18.8.3 Separating Positive and Normative Questions

Estimating the size of efficiency costs and measuring distributional effects is positive economics. Deciding how much efficiency to trade for how much equity is a normative judgment about values. Economics can clarify what is given up and gained, but it cannot resolve the ultimate choice on its own.

Practice 18.8Efficiency and Equity
  1. Explain, in your own words, the difference between horizontal and vertical equity. Give one example of each.
  2. A tax system imposes different total burdens on two households with identical incomes due to different deduction rules. Which dimension of equity does this violate?
  3. Identify which of the following is a positive claim and which is normative: (a) "Raising the top tax rate by 5 percentage points would reduce after-tax income for the top 1% by an estimated $40 billion." (b) "The government should raise the top tax rate because it is fairer."
Section 18.9

Administrative Costs and Unintended Consequences

Government intervention does not come free. Beyond the direct fiscal costs of programs, intervention involves administrative costs and often produces unintended consequences that must be taken into account when evaluating policy.

18.9.1 Administrative Costs

Collecting taxes requires enforcement and compliance systems. Means-tested transfer programs require eligibility determination, verification, and ongoing monitoring. Regulatory agencies require staff to write rules, inspect compliance, and adjudicate disputes. These administrative costs consume real resources. For recipients, administrative burdens — completing applications, gathering documentation, attending interviews — can deter eligible households from receiving benefits they are entitled to.

18.9.2 Behavioral Responses

Government policies change incentives, and people respond. A tax on a specific good may reduce its consumption (often the intent) but may also shift consumption to untaxed substitutes or create incentives for illegal markets. Minimum wage laws intended to raise worker incomes may reduce employment if firms hire fewer workers or substitute capital for labor. These responses mean intervention must be designed carefully, not that intervention is wrong.

18.9.3 Unintended Consequences

Unintended consequences typically arise from the logic of incentives and the interconnectedness of markets, not from random chance.

PolicyIntended EffectCommon Unintended Consequence
Rent controlKeep housing affordableReduced rental housing supply over time
Agricultural price supportsStabilize farm incomeBenefits flow disproportionately to large farms; overproduction; higher consumer food prices
Import-protecting regulationProtect domestic firmsHigher consumer prices; reduced competitive pressure for efficiency

Section 18.10

Evaluating Public Policy

Evaluating public policy requires careful reasoning that goes beyond supporting or opposing government intervention in general. Good policy analysis asks specific questions about what a specific policy does, for whom, at what cost, and compared to what alternatives.

18.10.1 A Framework for Policy Evaluation

Eight Steps to Evaluate a Policy
Apply this framework to any tax, subsidy, regulation, or spending program
  1. Identify the problem the policy is intended to address — market failure, equity concern, or stabilization goal.
  2. Explain the economic mechanism — what incentives does it change, and what market forces does it engage?
  3. Identify who bears costs and who receives benefits across income classes, age groups, industries, regions, or generations.
  4. Estimate the magnitude of effects — small benefits at large cost differ from large benefits at small cost.
  5. Separate positive and normative claims — keep factual claims distinct from value judgments.
  6. Identify tradeoffs — efficiency vs. equity, short-run vs. long-run, benefits for some vs. costs for others.
  7. Compare alternatives — not just to doing nothing, but to other policies with similar goals.
  8. Account for uncertainty — acknowledge that behavioral and market responses are difficult to predict precisely.

18.10.2 The Role of Evidence

Economic research on minimum wage laws, tax changes, welfare programs, and environmental regulations should inform policy evaluation. But evidence has limits: results from one time and place may not transfer to another, studies may conflict, and effects may change as behavior adjusts. Good policy reasoning takes evidence seriously without treating any single study as definitive.

18.10.3 Values and Policy Choices

Even with complete information about policy effects, people with different values will often reach different conclusions. This is not a failure of economic analysis — it reflects that policy choices ultimately involve value judgments about what societies want to achieve and what tradeoffs are acceptable. Economics can make tradeoffs visible; it cannot replace the judgment itself.

Practice 18.10Policy Evaluation Framework
  1. Apply the eight-step framework to a proposed increase in the minimum wage. Write one or two sentences addressing each step.
  2. Explain why "the evidence says X" does not automatically settle a policy debate, even when the evidence is solid.
  3. Give an example of two people who agree on all the factual effects of a policy but still disagree about whether it should be adopted. What explains their disagreement?

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1Governments intervene to correct market failures, establish rules, provide public goods, redistribute income, stabilize the economy, and regulate industries. Each rationale addresses a way markets may be incomplete, inefficient, or inconsistent with collective goals.
  • Sec 18.2Taxes are the main revenue source for governments. Major types include income, payroll, sales, excise, and property taxes. Marginal and average tax rates differ, and tax structures can be progressive, proportional, or regressive.
  • Sec 18.3Tax incidence — the real economic burden of a tax — falls more heavily on the side of the market that is less price-responsive, regardless of who is legally required to pay. Statutory and economic incidence often differ.
  • Sec 18.4Subsidies lower costs to encourage activities with positive externalities or equity goals. Like taxes, their benefits are shared between buyers and sellers based on relative elasticities, and they carry fiscal and distortionary costs.
  • Sec 18.5Regulation sets rules for behavior through command-and-control, performance-based, market-based, and disclosure approaches. Regulation can correct market failures but imposes compliance costs and risks regulatory capture.
  • Sec 18.6Public spending includes government purchases (counted in GDP), transfer payments (not counted in GDP), and interest on public debt. Public goods and public investment justify much government spending beyond direct service delivery.
  • Sec 18.7Redistribution occurs through progressive taxes, transfers, subsidies, and public services, motivated by poverty reduction, insurance, equity, and efficiency rationales. Transfer program design affects recipients' work incentives.
  • Sec 18.8The efficiency-equity tradeoff is central to redistribution debates. Multiple dimensions of fairness — horizontal equity, vertical equity, equality of opportunity, equality of outcomes — can point in different directions.
  • Sec 18.9Administrative costs and behavioral responses are real costs of intervention. Unintended consequences arise predictably from incentive logic and market interconnections, not from random chance.
  • Sec 18.10A systematic eight-step framework — identifying the problem, mechanism, distribution of costs and benefits, magnitude, positive/normative separation, tradeoffs, alternatives, and uncertainty — supports rigorous policy evaluation.

Fairness in the distribution of economic outcomes; a normative concept that depends on values.
horizontal equity
The principle that people in similar economic circumstances should be treated similarly.
vertical equity
The principle that people with greater ability to pay should bear greater tax burdens.
efficiency-equity tradeoff
The tension between maximizing total economic output and distributing it fairly.
administrative costs
The real resource costs of running government programs, including compliance, enforcement, and reporting.
unintended consequences
Effects of a policy beyond those intended by policymakers, arising from incentive responses and market interconnections.
poverty trap
A situation in which high benefit-reduction rates create high effective marginal tax rates that reduce the financial return to working.
regulatory capture
The process by which regulated industries gain disproportionate influence over the agencies that regulate them.

Graph Review

Tax Incidence Graph
A Tax of $T per Unit Imposed on Sellers
  • ShiftSupply curve shifts upward by $T.
  • Buyer priceRises above the original equilibrium price.
  • Seller priceFalls below the original equilibrium price (price received after paying the tax).
  • QuantityFalls from Q₁ to Q₂.
  • RevenueTax revenue = T × Q₂, shown as the rectangle between buyer and seller prices over quantity Q₂.
Reading tip: the height of the tax rectangle splits into a buyer-borne portion (upper) and a seller-borne portion (lower); the split reflects relative elasticities.
Subsidy Graph
A Subsidy per Unit Given to Sellers
  • ShiftSupply curve shifts downward by the subsidy amount.
  • Buyer priceFalls below the original equilibrium price.
  • Seller priceRises above the original equilibrium price (including the subsidy).
  • QuantityRises above the original equilibrium quantity.
  • ExpenditureTotal subsidy cost = subsidy per unit × new quantity.
Reading tip: the benefit splits between buyers and sellers, again in proportion to relative elasticities.

Data Review

Reading tax incidence data: look for changes in prices paid by buyers and prices received by sellers before and after a tax. If buyer prices rise by most of the tax, incidence falls mainly on buyers; if seller net prices fall by most of the tax, incidence falls mainly on sellers. Payroll tax research often measures whether net wages fall when employer-side taxes rise.

Reading tax rate data: identify whether average tax rates rise, fall, or stay stable as income rises across a data table. Rising rates indicate progressivity; stable rates indicate proportionality; falling rates indicate regressivity. Remember that combined incidence across income, payroll, sales, and excise taxes may differ from the incidence of any single tax.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 1
Average and Marginal Tax Rates
  • Brackets$0–$30,000 at 12%; $30,001–$80,000 at 22%; above $80,000 at 35%. Household earns $100,000.
  • Calculate0.12 × $30,000 = $3,600  ·  0.22 × $50,000 = $11,000  ·  0.35 × $20,000 = $7,000 Total tax = $21,600
  • Average rate$21,600 ÷ $100,000 = 21.6%
  • Marginal rate35% — applies only to income above $80,000.
Key takeaway: average and marginal rates can differ substantially in a progressive system.
Worked Example 2
Identifying Incidence from Price Data
  • Before taxPrice $10, quantity 500 units. After a $2 tax: buyer price $11.50; seller net price $9.50.
  • Compute splitBuyers: $1.50 ÷ $2.00 = 75%  ·  Sellers: $0.50 ÷ $2.00 = 25%
  • ConclusionBuyers bear the larger share, implying demand is less elastic than supply.
Key takeaway: the relative size of buyer-side and seller-side price changes reveals relative elasticities.

Practice Questions

Questions 1–5: Vocabulary in Context

  1. A $1.50-per-gallon tax is imposed on gasoline retailers. After the tax, gasoline prices at the pump rise by $1.20. Which party bears more of the economic burden of this tax — buyers or sellers? What does this imply about the relative elasticities of supply and demand for gasoline?
  2. A state government provides low-income families with grocery vouchers that can only be spent on food. Is this an example of an in-kind transfer or a cash transfer? What is one advantage and one disadvantage of this design choice?
  3. Consider a flat tax of 20% applied to all income. A household earning $40,000 and a household earning $200,000 both pay 20%. What are the dollar amounts each pays? Is this tax progressive, proportional, or regressive?
  4. A government regulation requires all new cars sold in a country to meet a specific fuel efficiency standard. What type of regulation is this? What are two likely costs and one likely benefit of this approach?
  5. A payroll tax is legally imposed on employers at a rate of 6% of wages. Research finds that wages in affected industries fell by approximately 5% after the tax was introduced. What does this finding imply about the economic incidence of the tax?

Questions 6–10: Graph and Table Reasoning

  1. Draw a supply and demand graph showing a market before and after a per-unit tax of $3 imposed on sellers. Label the original equilibrium price and quantity, the new price paid by buyers, the new price received by sellers, and the tax revenue rectangle. Assume buyers bear $2 of the $3 tax.
  2. A government imposes a $10-per-unit tax on sellers of a good with perfectly elastic supply. Who bears the entire economic burden of this tax? Draw a graph to illustrate.
  3. The following table shows income and tax paid for four households. Calculate the average tax rate for each. Determine whether the overall tax structure is progressive, proportional, or regressive.
    HouseholdAnnual IncomeTotal Tax Paid
    A$25,000$2,500
    B$50,000$5,500

  4. A subsidy of $4 per unit is given to producers of a good. Before the subsidy, the market price is $20. After the subsidy, buyers pay $17 and sellers receive $21. How much of the subsidy benefit goes to buyers? How much goes to sellers? What do the relative shares tell you about elasticity in this market?
  5. In a market with perfectly inelastic demand, explain in words and with a graph what happens to the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive when a per-unit tax is imposed on sellers. Who bears the full burden? Why?

Economic Reasoning Questions

  1. A city imposes a new parking tax of $5 per day on all downtown parking garages. The tax is collected from garage operators. After the tax, parking rates rise from $15 to $18 per day.
    1. How much of the $5 tax do buyers bear? How much do sellers bear?
    2. Does it matter that the tax is legally imposed on garage operators rather than on drivers? Explain using the concept of tax incidence.
    3. If the city raised the parking tax to $12 per day and daily parking rates rose to only $18 (the same as before), what would this imply about price responsiveness at higher price levels?
  2. A government introduces a means-tested housing assistance program. Benefits phase out at a rate of 50 cents for every dollar of earned income above a threshold.
    1. What effective marginal tax rate does this create for recipients earning above the threshold?
    2. How might this affect recipients' decisions about whether to work more hours or take a higher-paying job?
    3. Identify the tradeoff policymakers face in setting the benefit phase-out rate. What happens if the phase-out is made very slow? Very fast?
  3. A government is debating whether to address air pollution through a command-and-control regulation requiring all firms in an industry to cut emissions by 40%, or through a cap-and-trade permit system that limits total industry emissions but lets firms trade permits.
    1. What is one advantage of the permit system over the uniform standard?
    2. What is one concern about the permit system that the standard avoids?
    3. Both approaches impose costs on firms. What type of cost is not captured by looking only at government budget expenditures?

  3. "If a government wants to reduce smoking, it should tax cigarette manufacturers, not buyers. That way, consumers won't have to pay more."
  4. "Subsidizing renewable energy helps everyone equally because the price of renewable electricity falls for all consumers."
  5. "Because the government can always print money to cover program costs, there is no true tradeoff between government spending and efficiency."

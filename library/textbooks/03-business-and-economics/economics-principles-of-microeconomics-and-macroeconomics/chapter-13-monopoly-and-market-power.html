Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In the previous chapter, you studied perfect competition — a market in which many sellers offer identical products, prices are set by the market, and firms earn only normal profit in the long run. That model is a useful benchmark, but many real markets look nothing like it. Some markets have one dominant seller. Some have a seller that controls a critical resource, holds a government-granted patent, or provides a service where having two competing systems would waste enormous resources. These markets have monopoly power, and they behave very differently from competitive markets.

This chapter introduces monopoly as a market structure, explains how a monopolist sets price and output, and examines what this means for consumers and society. It also introduces price discrimination, regulation, and antitrust policy. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to analyze a monopoly using graphs and economic reasoning, explain why monopoly can create efficiency problems, and evaluate policy responses carefully.

Opening Question

Think of a product or service in your life where you feel you have little or no choice of seller — a utility, an internet provider, a single hospital in your area. Why is there only one seller? What would happen to the price and quality of that product if a serious competitor entered the market tomorrow? By the end of this chapter, you will have precise economic tools for answering these questions.

What Monopoly Is and Why Market Power Persists
Defining Monopoly · Market Power · Barriers to Entry · Natural Monopoly
Section 13.1

What Monopoly Means

The word monopoly comes from Greek: mono means one, and polein means to sell. A monopoly is a market structure in which a single seller supplies a product or service for which there are no close substitutes and into which entry by other sellers is blocked or extremely difficult.

Three features define monopoly:

  1. One seller. A single firm supplies the entire market. That firm is the industry.
  2. No close substitutes. Buyers who want this product cannot easily switch to something similar. If substitutes existed, the single seller would lose customers to rivals the moment it raised its price.
  3. Barriers to entry. Something prevents other firms from entering the market and competing away the monopolist's position. Without barriers, a profitable monopoly would attract competitors and eventually begin to look more like a competitive market.

These three features together give the monopolist a kind of power that competitive firms never have: the power to influence price.

The Monopolist as a Price Setter

Recall that in a perfectly competitive market, a single firm cannot affect the market price. It is a price taker. It sells as much as it wants at the going market price, but if it tries to charge even a penny more, buyers simply purchase from one of the many identical competitors.

A monopolist faces a very different situation. Because it is the only seller, its output is the market supply. If the monopolist produces more, the market price falls. If it produces less, the market price rises. The monopolist therefore chooses not just how much to produce but, indirectly, what price to charge. It is a price setter (sometimes called a price maker).

This does not mean a monopolist can charge any price it pleases. Buyers still have demand curves. If the price is too high, buyers simply go without the product, buy far less of it, or seek distant substitutes. The monopolist faces the downward-sloping market demand curve directly. Its choice of output determines the price that the market will bear.

A Note on Language

Economists use the word monopoly precisely. It refers to a single seller that controls a market with no close substitutes and faces barriers to entry. In everyday conversation, people sometimes call any dominant company a monopoly. That usage is looser. Throughout this chapter, monopoly refers to its strict economic meaning.

Section 13.2

Market Power

Market power is the ability of a seller to set a price above the competitive level without losing all its customers. Perfect competitors have zero market power. A pure monopolist has the maximum market power a single seller can possess, though even monopolists are constrained by demand.

Market power is not unique to monopoly. It exists on a spectrum. A local gas station that is the only station for many miles has some market power. A pharmaceutical company holding a patent on a widely prescribed medication has substantial market power. A national utility providing electricity to a city with no competing grid has very high market power. Market power matters because it allows sellers to earn higher profits than a competitive market would produce — and those higher profits come, at least in part, from buyers who pay more or go without.

Market Power and the Demand Curve

The key consequence of market power is the shape of the demand curve the firm faces.

  • A competitive firm faces a horizontal demand curve at the market price. It can sell any quantity at that price and nothing above it.
  • A monopolist faces the downward-sloping market demand curve. To sell more units, it must lower the price — not just for the additional unit, but for all units, because it sells at a single price to all buyers (unless it price discriminates, discussed in Section 13.9).

This difference has important consequences for how a monopolist thinks about revenue and output decisions.

Why Market Power Persists

In competitive markets, above-normal profits attract entrants, competition grows, prices fall, and profits return to normal. Market power persists when something blocks this process. Sections 13.3 through 13.5 examine those blocking forces — barriers to entry.

Section 13.3

Barriers to Entry

Barriers to entry are factors that prevent or significantly discourage new firms from entering a market to compete with an established seller. Without barriers, even a temporary monopoly would erode as competitors flooded in. Barriers explain why some monopolies last. Economists identify several categories of barriers.

Barrier TypeDescriptionExample
Legal barriersGovernments grant exclusive rights — patents, copyrights, franchises, licenses — that legally bar competitorsA 20-year patent on a new medication
Resource controlA firm owns or controls an essential input that cannot be easily replicated or obtained elsewhereSole ownership of a rare mineral deposit
Network effectsA product becomes more valuable as more people use it, discouraging switching to new entrantsA dominant messaging or social platform
Natural monopolyOne firm can serve the entire market at lower average cost than two or more firms couldAn electricity transmission grid
Economies of scaleAverage costs fall as output expands, giving large established firms a cost advantage over small entrantsA firm whose massive scale lets it underprice new entrants

Legal barriers. Governments sometimes grant exclusive rights to a single seller. The clearest examples are patents, which give an inventor the sole right to produce an invention for a specified period; copyrights, which apply the same idea to creative works; and government franchises and licenses, which authorize a single firm to provide a service such as a regional cable provider or public utility.

Control of an essential resource. If a single firm owns or controls a resource that is essential to production and that cannot be easily replicated or obtained elsewhere, it can exclude competitors. This type of barrier has become less common as global trade has increased the number of potential resource suppliers, but it remains real in some markets and regions.

Network effects. Network effects occur when a product becomes more valuable as more people use it. If nearly everyone uses a particular platform, a new entrant faces a massive disadvantage: its product is less valuable because fewer people use it. Network effects can create powerful barriers that no law enforces but that are economically formidable.

Natural monopoly. Perhaps the most economically important source of barriers, examined fully in Section 13.5.

Economies of scale. When a firm's average costs fall continuously as it expands output, a large established firm can produce at far lower cost than a smaller entrant. This is not quite the same as natural monopoly, though related to it — it describes a cost advantage that discourages entry even if a second firm could technically survive.

Section 13.4

Patents, Control of Resources, and Legal Barriers

This section examines legal and resource-based barriers more closely, because they appear frequently in policy discussions and in students' daily economic lives.

Patents in Detail

The economics of patents involves a deliberate tradeoff. Without patent protection, inventors may be unwilling to invest in costly research and development. Once an invention is created and disclosed, competitors can copy it without bearing the original development costs. This free-rider problem would reduce incentives to innovate. Patents solve this by temporarily granting monopoly power as a reward for innovation.

The cost of this solution is the price and output distortion that monopoly creates (discussed in Section 13.7). Society accepts a period of monopoly pricing in exchange for faster innovation. When a pharmaceutical company develops a new drug and receives a patent, it can charge high prices during the patent period, often far exceeding manufacturing cost. After the patent expires, generic competitors enter, prices fall sharply, and consumers gain access at much lower cost. The patent period is society's payment to the innovator.

Policy Debate

A longer patent period provides stronger incentives to innovate but extends the period of monopoly harm. A shorter period limits monopoly harm but may reduce investment in research. There is no purely economic answer to the "correct" patent length — it is a tradeoff that depends on values as well as evidence.

Resource Control

Control of an essential input — a mineral, a location, a water source — can block entry as effectively as a legal privilege. The economic principle is simple: if you cannot obtain the necessary input, you cannot produce the product. Resource monopolies have been more common historically than today, partly because international trade and substitution have increased access to alternatives. But in localized markets — a town served by a single water utility, a region with a single viable port — resource or location control can still generate significant market power.

Exclusive Government Franchises

Regulated utilities — electricity transmission, natural gas distribution, water and sewerage systems — are often granted exclusive operating rights within a territory. The government authorizes a single provider and then regulates its prices and practices. This is quite different from an unregulated monopoly: the firm has legal protection from competition but operates under regulatory oversight. Whether regulation succeeds in protecting consumers is examined in Section 13.10.

Section 13.5

Natural Monopoly

A natural monopoly exists when a single firm can supply the entire market demand at lower average cost than two or more competing firms could. This happens when the fixed costs of production are extremely high and the marginal cost of serving one more customer is very low.

The Logic of Natural Monopoly

Consider a water distribution system. Building the pipes, pumping stations, filtration plants, and infrastructure requires enormous fixed investment. Once built, the cost of serving one more household — running a little more water through the existing pipes — is small. Average cost falls as output expands because the large fixed cost is spread across more and more customers.

Now imagine two competing water companies both building separate pipe networks to the same neighborhood. The total fixed cost doubles, but neither firm can achieve the low average cost that one firm serving all customers would achieve. Having two firms may produce more competition but at higher total cost to society. In industries with this cost structure, the most cost-efficient outcome is a single producer serving the entire market — which is why we speak of a natural monopoly, arising naturally from the economics of production rather than from law or resource control.

Common Examples of Natural MonopolyWhy
Electricity transmission gridsHigh fixed infrastructure cost; very low marginal cost per added customer
Natural gas pipelinesSame cost structure; duplicating pipelines is wasteful
Water and sewerage systemsPipe networks are extremely costly to duplicate
Commuter rail in dense urban corridorsFixed track and station costs spread across riders
Local cable or broadband infrastructure (in some contexts)High fixed cable-laying cost, low marginal delivery cost

The long-run average cost curve (LRAC) slopes downward throughout the relevant range of demand. A regulated price set equal to average cost lets the firm cover its costs; a price equal to marginal cost (MC) would require a subsidy.
The Policy Problem

Natural monopoly creates a difficult policy challenge. If left unregulated, the single firm will exercise market power — setting prices above competitive levels and restricting output. But breaking the monopoly up into multiple competitors would raise costs and reduce efficiency. This tension motivates the regulatory frameworks discussed in Section 13.10.

Section 13.6

Price Setting and Output Decisions

How does a monopolist decide how much to produce and what price to charge? The answer uses the same logic as any firm — produce up to the point where additional revenue equals additional cost — but the monopolist's revenue calculations differ from those of a competitive firm.

Revenue for a Monopolist

Recall that a competitive firm's price is fixed by the market. Its marginal revenue — the additional revenue from selling one more unit — equals the market price.

A monopolist faces a downward-sloping demand curve. To sell more units, it must lower the price on all units sold (assuming a single price for all buyers). This means that marginal revenue is less than the price for every unit beyond the first. When the monopolist lowers its price to sell one more unit, it gains revenue from that new unit but loses revenue because all previous units are now sold at a lower price. Marginal revenue captures the net effect. It is always below the price.

Reading the Table

Notice that marginal revenue falls faster than price and becomes negative before price does. A monopolist will never voluntarily produce in the range where marginal revenue is negative — additional output would reduce total revenue.

The Monopolist's Output Decision

The monopolist maximizes profit by producing the quantity at which marginal revenue equals marginal cost (MR = MC). This is the same profit-maximizing condition used in competitive markets. The difference is that for a monopolist, MR lies below price.

The profit-maximizing sequence:

  1. Find the quantity where MR = MC. Call this Q*.
  2. Find the price that corresponds to Q* on the demand curve. Call this P*.
  3. The monopolist produces Q* and charges P*.

Because P* lies on the demand curve above the MR = MC intersection, the monopolist's price exceeds its marginal cost. In a competitive market, price equals marginal cost. The gap between price and marginal cost in monopoly is one measure of the distortion monopoly introduces.

Graph 13.2 — Monopoly Profit Maximization
The profit-maximizing output Q* occurs where MR = MC. The monopoly price P* is read from the demand curve directly above Q*. Because MR lies below demand, P* exceeds marginal cost.

The monopolist's profit per unit is the difference between price and average total cost at Q*. If the price exceeds average total cost, the monopolist earns positive (above-normal) economic profit. Unlike competitive firms, the monopolist can sustain this profit as long as barriers to entry remain effective. No competing firm enters to drive the price down.

Section 13.7

Monopoly and Consumer Welfare

Monopoly affects consumers in measurable ways. The key comparison is between the monopoly outcome and what a competitive market would have produced.

Comparing Monopoly and Competition

Under competition, price equals marginal cost and output is at its efficient level. Consumers pay the lowest price consistent with covering production costs. Under monopoly, output is restricted below the efficient level and price is raised above marginal cost. This produces two consequences:

  1. Consumers pay more. Those who buy the product pay a higher price than they would in a competitive market. They are worse off — they receive less value relative to what they pay.
  2. Some consumers are priced out. Some buyers who value the product above its marginal cost but below the monopoly price do not buy it. They and the monopolist both lose potential gains from trade that a competitive market would have realized.

Consumer Surplus and Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between what buyers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. In a competitive market, consumer surplus is high because prices are low and many buyers are served. In a monopoly market, consumer surplus falls because the price is higher and fewer units are sold.

Producer surplus is the difference between what sellers receive and the minimum they would have accepted. The monopolist captures higher producer surplus (profit) than a competitive firm would earn. But this gain does not fully offset the loss to consumers — some potential surplus simply disappears. That disappearance is the deadweight loss, discussed next.

Section 13.8

Deadweight Loss at an Introductory Level

Deadweight loss is the loss of total economic surplus — the combined well-being of buyers and sellers — that occurs when a market does not produce at the efficient quantity. In a monopoly, deadweight loss arises because the monopolist restricts output below the competitive level.

Where Deadweight Loss Comes From

At the competitive output level, every unit for which a buyer's willingness to pay exceeds the marginal cost of production is produced and exchanged. All the potential gains from trade are realized.

The monopolist produces less than this efficient quantity. For each unit between the monopoly output (Q*) and the competitive output (Qc), there is a buyer willing to pay more than it costs to produce that unit — but the exchange does not happen because the monopolist has cut output to raise its price. The gains from those missed transactions are lost to both buyer and seller. They go to no one. This is the deadweight loss.

Graph 13.3 — Deadweight Loss Under Monopoly
The deadweight loss triangle lies between the monopoly output (Q*) and the competitive output (Qc), bounded above by the demand curve and below by marginal cost. It represents value that mutually beneficial trades would have created but that disappears under monopoly output restriction.
Efficiency, Not a Moral Judgment

Deadweight loss represents a real cost to society — value that could have been created but was not. It is one of the primary economic justifications for antitrust policy and regulation of monopolies. But deadweight loss is a measure of efficiency loss, not a moral judgment. Whether a particular monopoly is socially acceptable depends on additional considerations — including whether the monopoly arose from innovation that benefits society in the long run, as patent-based monopolies are designed to do.

Section 13.9

Price Discrimination at an Introductory Level

So far we have assumed the monopolist charges the same price to all buyers. In practice, some monopolists (and firms with substantial market power) charge different prices to different buyers for the same product. This practice is called price discrimination.

What Price Discrimination Requires

Three conditions are needed for price discrimination to work:

  1. Market power. A competitive firm cannot price discriminate — if it raised the price for any buyer, that buyer would simply purchase from a competitor.
  2. Ability to identify willingness to pay. The seller must be able to identify — or approximate — which buyers are willing to pay more and which require a lower price to make a purchase.
  3. Prevention of resale. If buyers who purchase at low prices can resell to buyers who would have paid high prices, the seller cannot maintain price discrimination. Preventing arbitrage is essential.
Examples
Price discrimination in real markets
  • AirlinesBusiness travelers who book last-minute typically pay more than leisure travelers who book weeks in advance. The airline identifies business travelers as having higher willingness to pay.
  • DrugsThe same medication may sell at very different prices in different countries. Pharmaceutical companies segment national markets and use legal restrictions to prevent resale across borders.
  • DiscountsMovie theaters, museums, and transit systems charge lower prices to students and seniors, who are typically more price-sensitive.
  • CouponsOffering coupons allows firms to charge lower prices to price-sensitive buyers who bother to collect and use them, while charging higher prices to others.

Effects of Price Discrimination

Price discrimination can seem unfair — why should two people pay different prices for the same product? But its economic effects are nuanced.

For the seller: Price discrimination increases revenue and profit by capturing value that a single-price policy would leave on the table.

For consumers: Some consumers benefit from price discrimination — those who receive the lower price and who would not have purchased at the single monopoly price are now served. Others — those charged higher prices — are worse off than they would be in a competitive market.

For efficiency: In the extreme case of perfect price discrimination (charging each buyer exactly their willingness to pay), the monopolist sells to every buyer whose willingness to pay exceeds marginal cost. Output reaches the competitive level, and deadweight loss is eliminated. But all consumer surplus is captured by the seller. In practice, perfect price discrimination is impossible because sellers cannot observe each buyer's exact willingness to pay.

Section 13.10

Regulation and Antitrust Policy

Society has developed two broad approaches to dealing with monopoly: regulation and antitrust policy. They operate differently and apply to different situations.

Regulation

Regulation involves government rules that govern how a monopolist behaves — typically its prices, output, or quality. Regulation is most common for natural monopolies, where breaking up the monopoly would sacrifice economies of scale and raise costs for everyone.

Price regulation is the most common form. Rather than letting the natural monopolist set profit-maximizing prices, regulators set a maximum price. The central policy challenge is choosing the right regulated price.

ApproachHow It WorksTradeoff
Marginal cost pricingSets price equal to marginal cost, achieving economic efficiencyIn a natural monopoly, marginal cost lies below average cost, so the firm operates at a loss without a subsidy
Average cost pricing (rate-of-return regulation)Sets price equal to average total cost, letting the firm earn a normal return with no economic profitAvoids the subsidy problem but leaves price above marginal cost and creates weak incentives to control costs
Regulation Is Imperfect

Regulators may lack information about true costs. Firms may behave strategically to influence regulatory outcomes. The regulated firm may lobby to protect its position. Despite these challenges, regulated natural monopolies are common in electricity, water, sewerage, and telecommunications.

Antitrust Policy

Antitrust policy (called competition policy in many countries) refers to laws and enforcement actions designed to promote competition and prevent monopoly power from being created, maintained, or abused in markets where competition is possible. Unlike regulation, antitrust policy does not govern how a monopolist behaves. Instead, it tries to prevent monopoly from forming or to restore competition where it has been suppressed.

Antitrust policy addresses several types of behavior:

  • Mergers and acquisitions: Authorities review significant mergers to determine whether they would harm competition, and may block mergers, require divestitures, or impose conditions.
  • Predatory pricing: A dominant firm may temporarily set prices below cost to drive competitors out, then raise prices once rivals are eliminated.
  • Exclusive dealing and tying: A dominant firm may require buyers to purchase only from it, or bundle products in ways that foreclose competition in secondary markets.
  • Cartel behavior and collusion: When firms in an oligopoly agree to fix prices or divide markets, they act like a collective monopoly — illegal in most countries (discussed further in Chapter 14).

The Limits and Tradeoffs of Policy

Antitrust and regulatory policy face genuine tradeoffs. Aggressive antitrust enforcement may limit economies of scale that benefit consumers. Breaking up a firm that has grown large partly because of efficiency may raise costs. Distinguishing competition that harms rivals because it is predatory from competition that harms rivals because it is vigorous and efficient is genuinely difficult.

Regulation may suppress prices but also suppress investment — a utility that cannot earn a return on new infrastructure may underinvest in maintenance and expansion. Heavy regulation can also become a tool for established firms to protect themselves from new entrants by shaping regulatory rules in their favor.

Economic analysis does not automatically produce one right answer to every monopoly policy question. It helps identify the tradeoffs, the relevant mechanisms, and the likely consequences — which is exactly what good policy reasoning requires.

Key Terms Review

monopoly

price setter (price maker)
A firm that can influence the market price by choosing its output level, because it faces a downward-sloping demand curve.
price taker
A firm that accepts the market price as given and cannot influence it through its own output decisions.
market power
The ability of a seller to set a price above the competitive level without losing all customers.
barrier to entry
A factor that prevents or significantly discourages new firms from entering a market to compete with an established seller.
patent
A government-granted legal right giving an inventor the exclusive right to produce an invention for a specified period.
network effects
The phenomenon by which a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it.
natural monopoly
A market in which a single firm can serve all customers at lower average cost than two or more competing firms, due to very high fixed costs and low marginal costs.
marginal revenue
The additional revenue a firm earns from selling one more unit of output.
deadweight loss
The loss of total economic surplus — gains to buyers and sellers combined — that results when output is below the efficient level, as in monopoly.
price discrimination
The practice of charging different prices to different buyers for the same product or service.
regulation
Government rules governing the behavior of firms, particularly natural monopolies, including rules on prices, output, and quality.
antitrust policy
Laws and enforcement actions designed to promote competition and prevent monopoly power from being created, maintained, or abused.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 13.1
Finding the Profit-Maximizing Output
  • ProblemA monopolist faces the demand schedule in the Data Review table. Its marginal cost is constant at $4 per unit. Find the profit-maximizing quantity and price.
  • Step 1Find the quantity where MR = MC. MR = $4 at Q = 4 (and MR = $2 at Q = 5). Since MR = MC exactly at Q = 4, set Q* = 4.
  • Step 2Find the price on the demand curve at Q* = 4. From the table, when Q = 4, P = $7.
  • ResultQ* = 4 units    P* = $7
  • InterpretationThe competitive outcome (P = MC = $4) would occur at Q = 7. The monopolist produces less and charges more. The deadweight loss is the value of units 5, 6, and 7 — transactions buyers value above $4 but that do not occur at the $7 monopoly price.
Key takeaway: Apply MR = MC to find Q*, then read the price off demand — never off the MR curve.
Worked Example 13.2
Identifying Barriers to Entry
  • ProblemClassify each as a legal, resource, or cost-structure (natural monopoly) barrier: (a) a city grants one company the right to operate the municipal water system; (b) a biotech firm holds a 20-year patent on a cancer drug; (c) one company owns all economically viable deposits of a rare mineral; (d) a telecom firm has laid fiber to every home in a rural county and can serve more users at near-zero cost.
  • Answer (a)Legal barrier — government-granted exclusive franchise.
  • Answer (b)Legal barrier — patent protection.
  • Answer (c)Resource barrier — control of an essential input.
  • Answer (d)Cost-structure barrier (natural monopoly) — high fixed infrastructure cost with very low marginal cost per additional user.
Key takeaway: The same monopoly outcome can arise from very different underlying causes — always classify the source of the barrier before recommending a policy response.

The Last Hospital

Looking Ahead
Chapter 14: Monopolistic Competition and Oligopoly

Chapter 13 examined one extreme of market structure — a single seller with complete market control. Chapter 14 moves toward the middle. Monopolistic competition and oligopoly are the market structures that most industries actually inhabit: multiple sellers, some product differentiation, limited market power, and strategic interaction. You will learn how firms use branding and advertising, how oligopolists must consider rivals' reactions when making decisions, and why game theory — even at an introductory level — can illuminate choices from pricing to advertising to international trade negotiations.

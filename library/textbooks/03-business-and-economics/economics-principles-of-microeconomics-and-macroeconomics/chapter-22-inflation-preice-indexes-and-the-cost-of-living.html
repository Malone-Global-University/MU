Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In the summer of 1923, a loaf of bread in Germany cost more than 200 billion marks. Workers were paid twice a day because prices rose so fast that wages lost their value by lunchtime. People used wheelbarrows to carry enough currency to buy groceries. Savings accumulated over a lifetime were wiped out within weeks.

That episode — one of the most extreme inflations in modern history — illustrates why economists, governments, households, and businesses pay such close attention to changes in the general price level. Inflation does not just mean that prices go up. It means that the purchasing power of money goes down. It reshapes who benefits and who loses. It disrupts planning, savings, contracts, and trust.

Most economies do not experience hyperinflation. But even moderate inflation matters. A worker who receives a 3 percent pay raise in a year when prices rise 5 percent has actually taken a pay cut in real terms. A family living on fixed retirement income loses purchasing power every year that prices rise faster than their income adjusts. A firm that signed a long-term supply contract at fixed prices may find its costs have risen far beyond what the contract assumed.

Understanding inflation — what it is, how it is measured, what it costs, and how it differs from a single price rising — is essential economic literacy. This chapter builds that understanding step by step.

Opening Question

If your favorite snack doubled in price next month, would that mean the economy has inflation? What would you need to know about every other price in the economy to answer that question with confidence? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to calculate an inflation rate, convert a nominal wage into a real wage, and explain why a 5 percent raise can sometimes be a pay cut.

What Inflation Is and How It Is Measured

Section 22.1

What Inflation Means

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services across an economy over time.

Several words in that definition matter. Sustained means inflation is not a single price spike. If gasoline prices surge for one month and then fall back, that is a price fluctuation, not inflation. General price level means across many goods and services — not just one item. If avocado prices double because of a drought, but the increase is offset by falling prices elsewhere, the general price level may have barely moved. Over time means inflation is measured as a rate of change — typically compared to the same period a year earlier.

When economists say the inflation rate is 4 percent, they mean the general price level is about 4 percent higher than it was twelve months earlier.

What Inflation Is Not

Inflation is not the same as a high price level — a country can have high prices today but low inflation if those prices are now stable. Inflation is not the same as one price rising — that is a relative price change. Inflation is not the same as prices being unaffordable — a good can be expensive without any inflation occurring. And inflation is not always bad: economists generally consider low, stable inflation of about 1 to 3 percent annually to be normal and manageable.

Section 22.2

Price Levels and Individual Prices

Every day, prices change in particular markets. The price of wheat rises after a drought; the price of televisions falls as technology improves. These are movements in relative prices — the prices of particular goods compared to other goods. Relative price changes signal where resources are scarce or abundant and guide consumers and producers, but they are not the same thing as inflation.

The general price level is a weighted average summarizing what is happening to prices across the whole economy. Inflation occurs when that average rises.

A Useful Mental Model: Weighted Average

Imagine households buy only food, housing, and clothing. Food prices rise 10%, housing rises 5%, clothing falls 3%. If households spend 50% on housing, 30% on food, and 20% on clothing:

(0.50 × 5%) + (0.30 × 10%) + (0.20 × −3%) = 2.5% + 3.0% − 0.6% = 4.9%

The general price level rose about 4.9 percent, even though clothing prices fell. Items with larger budget shares receive larger weights in the overall average.

Practice 22.2Weighted Price Changes
  1. A household spends 40% on housing, 35% on food, and 25% on transportation. Housing rises 4%, food rises 6%, transportation falls 2%. Calculate the weighted price change.
  2. Explain why a sharp rise in one good's price might not raise the general price level much.
Section 22.3

Price Indexes

A price index is a measure of the general price level relative to a base period. It has two key features: a base period, typically assigned an index value of 100, and a set of goods and services whose prices are tracked over time.

If a price index equals 100 in the base period and rises to 112 three years later, prices have risen 12 percent above the base period level. If the same index falls from 112 to 108, that is deflation — the price level is now 8 percent above the original base, not 12 percent.

The Most Widely Known Index

Several types of price indexes exist in economics. The most widely known for measuring consumer prices is the Consumer Price Index (CPI), covered in Section 22.5.

Practice 22.3Reading Index Values
  1. An index equals 100 in the base year and 118 five years later. How much have prices risen?
  2. An index falls from 130 to 124. Is this inflation or deflation? Express the change relative to the base year.
Section 22.4

Market Baskets

The practical foundation of a price index is the market basket — a defined collection of goods and services meant to represent typical purchases. To construct a price index, economists survey households to learn what they buy, define a fixed basket of items and quantities, track the prices of those items, calculate the basket's cost each period, and divide by the base period cost (multiplied by 100) to express it as an index.

The fixed basket approach keeps measurement consistent over time, but it can fail to capture how consumers substitute toward cheaper alternatives when prices rise — a limitation explored further in Section 22.10.

A Limitation Worth Noting Early

If coffee becomes expensive, many households switch to tea — but a fixed basket keeps measuring the original coffee quantities. This can cause the index to overstate how much rising coffee prices actually raise the cost of living, since the index does not reflect substitution.

Section 22.5

Consumer Price Index at an Introductory Level

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is the most widely used measure of inflation for households. It tracks how much a fixed market basket of consumer goods and services costs over time. In the United States, the CPI is produced by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), which collects price data from thousands of stores, rental units, hospitals, and service providers each month.

CPI Formula

CPI = (Cost of basket in current period ÷ Cost of basket in base period) × 100

Example 22.5.1
Calculating the CPI
  • SituationA market basket in the base year costs $800. In a later year, the same basket costs $880.
  • CalculationCPI = (880 ÷ 800) × 100 = 110
  • MeaningAn index value of 110 means the price level is 10 percent higher than in the base year.
Key takeaway: An index above 100 always means prices are higher than in the base year — by exactly that percentage above 100.

Section 22.6

Calculating Inflation Rates

The inflation rate measures how much the price level changed from one period to the next, expressed as a percentage. It is a standard percentage-change formula applied to index numbers.

Inflation Rate Formula

Inflation Rate = [(CPI now − CPI before) ÷ CPI before] × 100

Example 22.6.1
From Year 2 to Year 3, and Year 4 to Year 5
  • Year 2–3(106.0 − 103.0) ÷ 103.0 × 100 ≈ 2.9%
  • Year 4–5(114.0 − 110.0) ÷ 110.0 × 100 ≈ 3.6%
  • InsightBoth periods rose 4.0 index points, but the inflation rate was higher in Year 4–5 because the starting base was larger. Equal point gains do not mean equal percentage gains.
Key takeaway: Always divide by the prior period's index value, not by 100, when calculating an inflation rate between two non-base years.
Don't Confuse the Rate with the Level

A CPI of 200 with 1% inflation means prices are double the base year but rising slowly. A CPI of 110 with 8% inflation means prices are only 10% above base but rising quickly. Policy concern usually centers on the inflation rate, not the absolute price level.

Section 22.7

Purchasing Power

Purchasing power refers to the quantity of goods and services that a unit of money can buy. When prices rise, the purchasing power of money falls. Purchasing power and the price level move in opposite directions: when the price level rises 10 percent, the purchasing power of a dollar falls by approximately 9 percent.

Example 22.7.1
Purchasing power after a price rise
  • SituationA basket costs $500 in the base year and $550 a year later — a 10% price rise.
  • Effect on savingsA household holding $500 can now afford only about 91% of the same basket.
  • Effect on needed incomeTo maintain the same standard of living, the household needs about 10% more money than before.
Key takeaway: A given price-level increase reduces purchasing power by a slightly smaller percentage than the price increase itself.

The Rule of 70

Dividing 70 by the annual rate approximates the number of years needed for a quantity to double — or, applied to the rate of decline, for purchasing power to halve. Even 3 percent annual inflation halves purchasing power in roughly 23 years.

Section 22.8

Nominal and Real Wages

Nominal wages are wages measured in current dollars. Real wages adjust nominal wages for inflation to reflect actual purchasing power. A worker who received $15 per hour last year and $16 this year saw a nominal increase of about 6.7 percent — but if inflation was 4 percent, the real increase was only about 2.7 percent. If inflation had been 8 percent, that same nominal raise would represent a real wage decrease.

Nominal-to-Real Conversion Formula

Real Value = (Nominal Value ÷ Price Index) × 100

Example 22.8.1
Converting a nominal wage to a real wage
  • SituationA worker earns a nominal wage of $20 per hour in a year when the CPI is 125.
  • CalculationReal Wage = (20 ÷ 125) × 100 = $16 (base-year dollars)
  • Meaning$20 today buys only as much as $16 would have bought in the base year.
Key takeaway: This conversion applies to any nominal amount — income, prices, GDP, rents, interest payments, and savings.

A Nominal Illusion

In the table above, the nominal wage rose 25 percent from Year 1 to Year 5 — but the CPI also rose 25 percent, so the real wage in Year 5 equals the real wage in Year 1. The worker earned more dollars but could buy exactly the same amount of goods and services.

Cost of Living Adjustments (COLAs)

Many wages, benefits, and contracts include automatic increases tied to a price index — often the CPI — to preserve purchasing power. Social Security benefits include an annual COLA. But COLAs can also embed inflation into cost structures: if wages rise automatically with prices, businesses may raise prices further, triggering further wage increases — a self-reinforcing pattern called a wage-price spiral.

Section 22.9

Deflation and Disinflation

Deflation occurs when the general price level falls — negative inflation. Although falling prices might sound appealing, sustained deflation carries serious risks: consumers may delay purchases waiting for lower prices (reducing demand and employment), the real burden of fixed-dollar debts rises, and firms with fixed wage or debt obligations see profits squeezed as revenues fall. Japan's prolonged near-zero inflation and periodic deflation beginning in the 1990s — the "Lost Decade" — illustrates why central banks treat deflation as a serious threat.

Disinflation is a slowing of the inflation rate — prices still rise, just more slowly. If inflation falls from 8 percent to 4 percent, that is disinflation, not deflation. Disinflation is often a desired policy outcome when inflation has been too high.

Section 22.10

Costs and Measurement Limits of Inflation

Inflation imposes real costs. Erosion of purchasing power harms anyone holding money or receiving fixed payments. Redistribution between debtors and creditors occurs because borrowers repay fixed-rate loans with dollars that are worth less when inflation rises unexpectedly — benefiting debtors and harming creditors. Menu costs are the administrative costs of updating prices; shoe leather costs are the time and effort spent managing cash to avoid its erosion. Inflation can also distort price signals, increase planning uncertainty, and harm international competitiveness if a country's inflation outpaces its trading partners'.

GroupEffect of Unexpected Inflation
Fixed nominal income recipients (no COLA)Real income declines
Savers in low-interest cash accountsLose purchasing power if interest is below inflation
Creditors / bondholders at fixed ratesRepaid in dollars worth less in real terms
Borrowers at fixed ratesOften benefit as real debt value falls
Workers with strong bargaining powerMay keep pace with or outrun inflation
Owners of real assets (housing, land)Often preserve or increase real wealth
Limitations of the CPI

Substitution bias causes a fixed basket to overstate inflation when consumers switch to cheaper alternatives. The new goods problem means new products may not enter the basket immediately. Quality changes are difficult to separate from pure price changes. The CPI is also a national average, so it may not reflect geographic variation or the spending patterns of households whose budgets differ from the typical urban household represented in the basket.

Still a Useful Tool

None of these limitations make the CPI useless. It remains a carefully constructed, consistent tool for comparing price levels over time. Alternative indexes — such as the Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) index used by the Federal Reserve, or the Producer Price Index (PPI) — provide additional perspectives.

Chapter 22 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 22.1Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level. It is not the same as a high price level, a single price rising, or unaffordability. Low, stable inflation is normal in a healthy economy.
  • Sec 22.2Relative price changes affect individual goods; the general price level is a weighted average across the whole economy. Inflation refers to that average rising.
  • Sec 22.3A price index measures the price level relative to a base period, typically set at 100. Index values above or below 100 show the percentage change in prices since the base period.
  • Sec 22.4A market basket is a fixed collection of goods and services used to track prices consistently over time. Fixed baskets enable consistent comparison but can miss consumer substitution.
  • Sec 22.5The CPI divides the current cost of the basket by its base-period cost, multiplied by 100. It is the most widely used measure of consumer-level inflation in the United States.
  • Sec 22.6The inflation rate is the percentage change in a price index from one period to the next. Equal point changes in the index can represent different inflation rates depending on the starting value.
  • Sec 22.7Purchasing power falls as the price level rises. Even low inflation compounds significantly over time, as shown by the Rule of 70.
  • Sec 22.8Nominal values are measured in current dollars; real values adjust for inflation. COLAs preserve purchasing power but can contribute to a self-reinforcing wage-price spiral.
  • Sec 22.9Deflation is a falling price level and carries serious economic risks. Disinflation is a slowing inflation rate, often a desired policy goal, and is distinct from deflation.
  • Sec 22.10Inflation imposes costs including purchasing power erosion, debtor-creditor redistribution, menu costs, and shoe leather costs. The CPI is useful but limited by substitution bias, the new goods problem, quality change issues, and geographic variation.

Key Terms Review

inflation
A sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services across an economy over time.
general price level
An average of prices across many goods and services in an economy, weighted by their importance in typical household spending.
relative price
The price of one good or service compared to other goods and services.
price index
A measure of the general price level relative to a base period, calculated from the prices of a defined market basket.
base period
The reference time period assigned an index value of 100, against which later price levels are compared.
market basket
A defined collection of goods and services, in fixed quantities, used to track price changes over time.
Consumer Price Index (CPI)
A price index measuring how much a fixed market basket of consumer goods and services costs over time.
inflation rate
The percentage change in a price index from one period to the next.
purchasing power
The quantity of goods and services that a unit of money can buy; falls when the price level rises.
nominal wage
A wage measured in current dollars, without adjustment for inflation.
real wage
A wage adjusted for inflation to reflect actual purchasing power, expressed in base-period dollars.
cost of living adjustment (COLA)
An automatic increase in a payment tied to a price index, designed to preserve purchasing power.
deflation
A sustained decrease in the general price level; negative inflation.
disinflation
A slowing of the rate of inflation; prices still rise, but more slowly than before.
hyperinflation
Extremely rapid inflation in which prices rise dramatically over a short period.
menu costs
The costs businesses incur when updating prices during periods of inflation.
shoe leather costs
The time and effort people spend managing cash holdings to avoid the erosion of purchasing power during inflation.
substitution bias
The tendency of a fixed-basket price index to overstate inflation because it does not account for consumers switching to cheaper substitutes.

Case Study

Case Study 22.1
A Household Tracking Its Own Cost of Living
  • BackgroundThe Alvarez family spends 55% of its budget on housing, 25% on food, and 20% on transportation. Over one year, housing costs rise 7%, food prices rise 4%, and transportation costs fall 1%.
  • Weighted change(0.55 × 7%) + (0.25 × 4%) + (0.20 × −1%) = 3.85% + 1.0% − 0.2% = 4.65%
  • Comparison to national CPIIf the national CPI reports 3.0% inflation that year, the Alvarez family is experiencing a higher personal inflation rate than the national average — largely because housing, their largest expense, rose faster than the overall basket.
  • ImplicationNational inflation statistics are averages. Individual households can experience meaningfully different inflation depending on their specific spending patterns.

Chapter 28 — Front Matter

Chapter Introduction

In 1900, the average American lived to about age 47. Most households had no electricity, no indoor plumbing, and no telephone. A typical factory worker earned wages that would equal roughly $15,000 per year in today's purchasing power. Infant mortality was devastating. Infectious diseases killed millions. Travel between cities took days.

Today, life expectancy in the United States exceeds 76 years. Most households have access to medical care that would have seemed miraculous a century ago. Workers across dozens of industries earn wages that allow not just survival but comfort, education, and recreation. Billions of people around the world, while still facing enormous hardship, are better fed, longer-lived, and better educated than their great-grandparents were.

What produced this transformation? The answer is economic growth — specifically, sustained growth in productivity that compounded over decades and generations until it reshaped what human life is like.

This chapter studies economic growth: what it is, how economists measure it, what causes it, and why it matters so profoundly. Economic growth is not guaranteed. Some countries have grown rapidly for sustained periods and lifted billions out of poverty. Others have stagnated for generations. Understanding why requires looking carefully at productivity, capital, technology, institutions, and the choices societies make.

We will also reckon honestly with what growth does not capture: environmental costs, inequality in who benefits, distributional tradeoffs, and the quality of life dimensions that GDP cannot measure. Economic growth is powerful and important, but it is not the whole story.

Why This Chapter Matters

By the end of this chapter, you should understand what drives long-run economic growth and why small differences in growth rates produce enormous differences in living standards over time.

Section 28.1

Why Economic Growth Matters

Economic growth refers to a sustained increase in the real output of an economy over time. Economists most commonly measure it as the percentage increase in real GDP from one year to the next, or as the increase in real GDP per capita — real GDP divided by population.

The distinction between total real GDP growth and real GDP per capita growth matters enormously. A country whose population grows 3 percent per year while its real GDP also grows 3 percent per year has not increased the average standard of living at all — it has simply produced more total output to support more people. What raises living standards is growth in output per person.

Why Growth Rates Are Not Just Numbers

Growth rates look like small numbers, but they are not. Because of compounding — the process by which growth builds on itself year after year — even small differences in growth rates produce dramatically different outcomes over time.

A useful rule of thumb is the Rule of 70: divide 70 by the annual growth rate to estimate how many years it takes for a quantity to double.

Annual Growth RateYears to Double
1%70 years
2%35 years
3%23 years
4%17.5 years
7%10 years

A country growing at 1 percent per year doubles its income per person in 70 years. A country growing at 3 percent per year doubles its income per person in roughly 23 years. Over a century, those two countries arrive at radically different places. The first country's income per person grows by a factor of about 2.7. The second country's income per person grows by a factor of about 19.

These are not trivial differences. They are the difference between persistent poverty and broad prosperity. They explain why some nations that were poor in 1950 are now wealthy, while others have remained trapped in low-income status.

Growth and Human Welfare

The connection between economic growth and human welfare is real and substantial, though not automatic. As economies grow, typical patterns include:

Typical Patterns Associated with Growth

Rising incomes that allow more households to afford food, housing, healthcare, and education.

Greater government revenue to fund public services, infrastructure, and social insurance.

Technological improvements in medicine, agriculture, transportation, and communication.

Declining rates of extreme poverty, rising life expectancy, declining child mortality, and expanding access to education.

A Note of Caution

None of this means that growth automatically distributes its gains fairly, that growth has no environmental costs, or that GDP per capita is a complete measure of human well-being. We will return to these complications throughout the chapter. But the basic connection — that sustained economic growth over long periods is one of the most powerful forces for reducing material deprivation in human history — is difficult to dispute.

Section 28.2

Real GDP per Capita and Living Standards

When economists study economic growth and living standards, they use real GDP per capita rather than nominal GDP. Nominal GDP measures total output in current prices and rises when prices rise, even if no additional goods or services are actually produced. Real GDP adjusts for inflation by expressing output in the prices of a base year, allowing meaningful comparisons across time. Real GDP per capita divides real GDP by population, reflecting the average output available per person — the relevant measure for comparing living standards.

The Long-Run Picture

Long-run data on real GDP per capita reveal the enormous impact of sustained growth. The United States in the early twenty-first century has a real GDP per capita roughly eight times higher than it had in 1900. This kind of transformation does not happen through lucky events or temporary booms. It happens through compounding growth in productivity sustained over many decades.

Comparing Countries

Real GDP per capita comparisons across countries are more complicated than comparisons within a single country over time. Economists use purchasing power parity (PPP) adjustments to account for the fact that the same dollar buys different quantities of goods in different countries. Even with these adjustments, enormous differences in real GDP per capita across countries reflect genuine differences in average material living standards. In the early twenty-first century, the wealthiest countries have real GDP per capita (PPP-adjusted) that is 50 to 100 times higher than the poorest countries.

What Real GDP per Capita Does Not Capture

Real GDP per capita is incomplete. It does not capture distribution (a high average can mask widespread poverty), unpaid work (household production, childcare, volunteer work), leisure, environmental quality, security and political freedom, or health and longevity directly. Economists supplement it with measures such as the United Nations Human Development Index, which combines income, education, and life expectancy.

Section 28.3

Productivity

The fundamental source of long-run economic growth is productivity — specifically, the ability of workers and firms to produce more output per unit of input. Labor productivity is the most commonly discussed measure: it equals real output divided by the number of workers, or real output divided by total hours worked.

Labor Productivity Formula

Labor Productivity = Real Output ÷ Hours Worked

When workers produce more output per hour, the economy generates more goods and services with the same amount of labor. This is the engine of rising living standards.

Total factor productivity (TFP) — sometimes called multifactor productivity — measures how efficiently all inputs (labor, physical capital, and others) are used together. When TFP rises, the economy is getting more output from the same combination of inputs. TFP growth is often associated with technological progress, better management, improved institutions, and knowledge gains.

Why Productivity Is Central

If workers produce more per hour worked, employers can pay higher wages without raising prices to cover their costs. Higher wages allow workers to purchase more goods and services. Over time, rising productivity is the primary mechanism through which an economy raises real wages and living standards.

An economy cannot permanently raise living standards by simply printing more money, borrowing more, or distributing existing output differently. Redistribution can change who gets what in the short run, but it cannot create more goods and services to distribute. Only higher productivity — producing more from a given set of resources — allows total real income to rise.

Worked Example 28.2
Calculating labor productivity growth
  • ProblemIn Year 1, an economy produces $800 billion of real output with 160 million hours of labor. In Year 2, it produces $840 billion of real output with 160 million hours of labor. What is the labor productivity growth rate?
  • Year 1$800 billion ÷ 160 million hours = $5,000 per hour
  • Year 2$840 billion ÷ 160 million hours = $5,250 per hour
  • Growth($5,250 − $5,000) ÷ $5,000 = 5.0% growth
Key takeaway: The economy became 5% more productive — producing 5% more output per hour of labor — without any increase in hours worked.
Output Growth vs. Productivity Growth

If instead employment had also grown — say, to 105 million workers while output rose to $540 billion from $500 billion — labor productivity would have grown only from $5,000 to about $5,143 per worker, roughly 2.9%, rather than the 8% it would show if employment stayed constant. Output growth driven mainly by adding workers does not raise average living standards the way productivity-driven growth does.

Section 28.4

Physical Capital

One of the most straightforward ways to raise worker productivity is to give workers more physical capital to work with — tools, machines, vehicles, computers, buildings, and infrastructure. Physical capital refers to manufactured goods used to produce other goods and services.

Capital deepening occurs when the amount of physical capital per worker increases. When each worker has access to more machines and equipment, each worker can typically produce more output per hour. A carpenter without power tools produces far less lumber per day than a carpenter with a circular saw, a drill press, and other powered equipment — the difference lies in capital, not skill or effort.

Figure 28.2
The Production Function and Capital Deepening
A curve showing output per worker on the vertical axis and capital per worker on the horizontal axis. The curve rises as capital per worker increases but with decreasing slope — illustrating diminishing returns to capital. Points along the curve show that early increases in capital per worker produce large gains in output per worker; later increases produce smaller gains.
What to read in this graph: The flattening slope illustrates diminishing returns. Technological progress in this framework shifts the entire curve upward, allowing more output per worker from any given amount of capital.

Diminishing Returns to Capital

Capital deepening raises productivity, but with diminishing returns: each additional unit of capital added to a fixed amount of labor tends to raise productivity by smaller and smaller amounts. A factory with 10 workers and 1 machine gains dramatically from a second machine, less from a third, and very little from a twentieth — the workers cannot efficiently operate that many machines.

This insight — that capital deepening alone cannot sustain indefinite growth — points toward technology and knowledge as the ultimate drivers of long-run growth.

Investment and Capital Accumulation

Physical capital accumulates through investment — spending on new productive capacity. For capital to accumulate, investment must exceed depreciation — the wearing out and obsolescence of existing capital. Net investment — gross investment minus depreciation — must be positive for the capital stock to grow.

Worked Example 28.3
Net investment and capital stock
  • ProblemAn economy has a capital stock of $10 trillion. It invests $1.5 trillion in new capital. Depreciation is 8% of the capital stock per year. Is the capital stock growing or shrinking? By how much?
  • Depreciation8% × $10 trillion = $0.8 trillion
  • Net investment$1.5 trillion − $0.8 trillion = $0.7 trillion
  • ConclusionNew capital stock = $10 trillion + $0.7 trillion = $10.7 trillion
Key takeaway: The capital stock is growing by $0.7 trillion (7% of the original stock). If investment fell to $0.8 trillion, net investment would be zero; below that, the capital stock would shrink.

Section 28.5

Human Capital

Human capital refers to the knowledge, skills, health, and experience that workers accumulate — the productive capacity embedded in people themselves. Like physical capital, human capital raises worker productivity. A surgeon with medical training produces vastly more value per hour than an untrained person attempting the same procedures.

Human capital accumulation occurs through formal education, vocational training, on-the-job learning, and health.

Returns to Education

Economists measure the private return to education by comparing the lifetime earnings of workers with different levels of education. On average, across many countries and time periods, each additional year of schooling is associated with an 8–12 percent increase in earnings. This suggests that education raises productivity enough that employers are willing to pay substantially more for educated workers.

The social returns to education may be even higher than the private returns, since better-educated workers innovate, train colleagues, and participate more productively in civic and economic life — spillovers that benefit others. This is one reason why governments typically subsidize education.

The Human Capital–Growth Connection

Cross-country data consistently show that countries with higher levels of educational attainment tend to grow faster and achieve higher incomes per capita. Some of the most dramatic economic growth stories of the twentieth century — South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore — involved simultaneous and substantial investments in education alongside open trade, investment in physical capital, and stable institutions.

Health as Human Capital

Sick workers are less productive. Children with poor nutrition and health learn less effectively. Regions with high burdens of disease suffer productivity losses that perpetuate poverty. The dramatic reductions in child mortality and improvements in adult health that accompanied economic development in the twentieth century were not only humanitarian achievements; they were also investments in human capital that supported further economic growth.

Section 28.6

Technology and Innovation

Capital accumulation — both physical and human — is subject to diminishing returns. Yet economies have managed to sustain growth for very long periods, which implies that something beyond capital accumulation must be driving productivity forward. That something is technology — the knowledge and methods used to combine inputs and produce outputs.

Technological progress means producing more output from the same inputs, or the same output from fewer inputs, because of improvements in knowledge and methods. Crucially, technology does not face the same diminishing returns that capital does. A new idea can be applied across the entire economy without being depleted — an economic property called nonrivalry: one person using an idea does not prevent another person from using it simultaneously.

Figure 28.3
Technology and the Production Function
Two versions of the production function curve from Figure 28.2 — one at a lower level and one shifted upward, representing technological progress. A given quantity of capital per worker now produces more output per worker after the technological advance.
What to read in this graph: Technological progress is represented as an upward shift of the production function — this is how technology can sustain growth even as capital deepening faces diminishing returns.

Innovation and Research

New technology is produced through deliberate effort: research and development (R&D), experimentation, and the creative work of scientists, engineers, inventors, and entrepreneurs. Governments fund basic research — the exploration of fundamental science without immediate commercial application — because private markets tend to underinvest in it. Since the knowledge produced by basic research quickly becomes publicly available, private firms cannot capture all the benefits, so they have insufficient incentive to fund research at the socially optimal level.

A Positive Externality

The social return to research and development typically exceeds the private return. Firms and researchers capture some of the gain from their discoveries, but much of the benefit spills over to other firms, workers, and consumers — a standard justification for public funding of universities and basic science.

Diffusion and Adoption

An innovation creates value only when it is adopted and used. The diffusion of new technology — its spread from early adopters to widespread use — is itself an important economic process, and can be slowed by high upfront costs, missing complementary skills, regulatory barriers, or network effects. Countries that rapidly adopt and adapt frontier technologies can grow quickly even without being the original source of innovation.

Section 28.7

Institutions and Incentives

All of the inputs to growth — physical capital, human capital, and technology — depend on incentives. Workers invest in skills when they expect their skills to be rewarded. Firms invest in capital when they expect returns on investment. These incentives are shaped by institutions — the formal rules, laws, and informal norms that govern economic activity.

InstitutionWhy It Matters for Growth
Secure property rightsIndividuals and firms can own assets and expect that ownership to be protected from arbitrary seizure
Rule of lawContracts are enforced, laws apply equally, and the legal system is reliable
Political stabilityBusiness and investment decisions require predictability; chaos and instability destroy investment incentives
Low corruptionWhen officials demand bribes or redirect public resources, investment and productive activity are discouraged
Open and competitive marketsEntry and exit are possible, monopoly power is limited, and competition rewards efficiency

Institutions and the Cross-Country Evidence

The importance of institutions is dramatically illustrated by natural experiments in economic history. North Korea and South Korea were one country until 1945, sharing geography, language, culture, and historical heritage. After partition, North Korea developed a command economy with restricted property rights and authoritarian control, while South Korea developed market institutions, secure property rights, and integration with the global economy.

By the early twenty-first century, South Korea's real GDP per capita was roughly 20 to 25 times higher than North Korea's. Similar natural experiments — comparing East and West Germany, or regions within countries that experienced different colonial institutions — consistently point to institutions as a fundamental determinant of long-run growth.

The Institutional Catch

Identifying good institutions is easier than creating them. Institutions are embedded in history, culture, political power, and social trust. They cannot simply be transplanted from successful economies to struggling ones — improving institutions requires political will, sustained effort, and often generational change. It is genuine work, not a policy checklist.

Section 28.8

Savings, Investment, and Infrastructure

Physical capital accumulation requires investment, and investment requires financing. In a closed economy, the pool of funds available for investment is limited by national savings — the income that households, firms, and governments set aside rather than consuming. Higher national savings rates, sustained over time, support higher investment rates and larger capital stocks per worker, which support higher productivity and growth.

Countries with high savings and investment rates — including the fast-growing East Asian economies in the late twentieth century — consistently built capital stocks rapidly and achieved high growth rates. Open economies can supplement domestic savings with foreign investment, which makes the savings constraint less binding but introduces other considerations, since foreign investment must be repaid and capital flows can be volatile.

Public Investment and Infrastructure

Infrastructure — the shared physical systems that support economic activity — raises private sector productivity by reducing transportation and communication costs, enabling market integration, and allowing specialization. A farm with no road access cannot sell its products easily; a factory with unreliable electricity cannot maintain production. When well-targeted, infrastructure investment has historically shown high social returns.

Time Preferences and the Growth Tradeoff

A fundamental tradeoff in economic growth is between present consumption and future output. Saving more and investing more means consuming less today in order to have more productive capacity tomorrow. Societies with lower time preferences — greater willingness to defer current consumption for future gain — tend to invest more and grow faster. This tradeoff is real, and neither choice is obviously wrong; it is a value judgment embedded in economic policy.

Section 28.9

Entrepreneurship and Creative Destruction

Physical capital, human capital, and technology all require agents to combine them productively. Entrepreneurs — individuals who organize production, take risks, and introduce new products, processes, and business models — are the human actors who drive much of the economic dynamism behind growth. Entrepreneurship flourishes where institutions support it: where property rights protect the returns from innovation, where entry into markets is open, and where failure is not catastrophic and permanently stigmatized.

Creative Destruction

One of the most important and uncomfortable ideas in growth economics is creative destruction — the process described by economist Joseph Schumpeter in which new innovations, firms, and methods of production displace old ones. When steam engines displaced horses, when automobiles displaced carriage-making, when digital photography displaced film, the economy gained new productive capacity — but also destroyed the value of old capital and eliminated jobs tied to the old technology.

Creative destruction means that economic growth is not smooth and painless. It produces genuine winners and losers. The benefits of new technology flow broadly to consumers and to those in expanding industries, but often not to those most disrupted by the change.

Managing the Tension

The benefits of growth are diffuse and long-run; the costs of disruption are concentrated and immediate. Managing these tensions — providing adjustment support for displaced workers, facilitating retraining, and maintaining social insurance — is one of the legitimate challenges that growth policy must address. Creative destruction does not mean disruption is always good, nor that displaced workers should simply be told growth will eventually help them.

Section 28.10

Growth, Environment, and Quality of Life

Economic growth raises real output and income, but it also places pressure on natural systems. More production means more energy use, more extraction of natural resources, more emissions, and more waste. At the same time, several forces push in the other direction: technology and efficiency gains, structural change toward services, regulatory response as incomes rise, and deliberate investment in green technology.

The Environmental Kuznets Curve — A Contested Pattern

The hypothesis that environmental degradation first rises and then falls as per capita income grows is sometimes called the environmental Kuznets curve, though it is contested and does not apply to all pollutants equally — particularly greenhouse gases, whose effects accumulate globally over centuries.

Beyond GDP: Quality of Life

Growth in real GDP per capita is associated with, but not identical to, improvements in human well-being. Life expectancy and GDP per capita are strongly correlated, especially at low income levels, though gains slow at higher income levels. Self-reported happiness rises with income but also shows diminishing gains. Societies face real tradeoffs between income and leisure, and GDP per capita averages can mask how unevenly the gains of growth are distributed.

Sustainable Development

Sustainable development — meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs — integrates economic growth with environmental and distributional concerns. Economists use concepts like genuine savings — national savings adjusted for resource depletion and environmental degradation — to capture whether an economy's total capital stock, including natural capital, is actually growing or shrinking.

The hypothesis that environmental degradation first rises and then falls as per capita income grows; contested and variable across pollutants.
sustainable development
Economic development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
genuine savings
National savings adjusted for resource depletion and environmental degradation; a measure of whether total capital stock is growing or shrinking.

A curve showing output per worker rising with capital per worker but at a decreasing rate, illustrating diminishing returns to capital.
What to read in this graph: Early increases in capital per worker produce large gains in output per worker; later increases produce smaller gains.

Technology and the Production Function
Two production function curves — one before and one after technological progress — showing the curve shifted upward.
What to read in this graph: Technological progress shifts the entire curve upward, so a given amount of capital per worker yields more output per worker than before.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 28.1
Applying the Rule of 70
  • ProblemCountry Alpha grows at 3.5% per year. Country Beta grows at 1.75% per year. Both have the same current income per capita. How long does each take to double? How many times will Alpha have doubled while Beta doubles once?
  • Alpha70 ÷ 3.5 = 20 years to double
  • Beta70 ÷ 1.75 = 40 years to double
  • ComparisonIn 40 years, Alpha doubles twice (4× starting level) while Beta doubles once (2× starting level). Alpha ends up with twice the income per capita of Beta after 40 years.
Key takeaway: Doubling time scales directly with the growth rate, and repeated doublings compound quickly.

  1. Country X has a real GDP per capita of $8,000 and grows at 3.5% per year. Country Y has a real GDP per capita of $32,000 and grows at 1% per year.

    (a) How long will it take each country to double its income per capita?
    (b) What will be Country X's income per capita after 20 years?

Section D — Scenario Analysis

  1. Two neighboring countries separated by a river were once at similar income levels. Country North developed secure property rights, independent courts, and open markets. Country South developed a system in which officials could seize businesses at will and contracts were rarely enforced. Fifty years later, Country North has income per capita four times higher than Country South. Use economic reasoning to explain why this divergence occurred.
  2. A new machine allows factory workers to produce three times as many units per hour. The machine is expensive. Factories that adopt it earn higher profits. Factories that do not face competition from those that do.

    (a) What happens to workers in factories that adopt the machine?
    (b) What happens to workers in competing industries that still make the old products?
    (c) What happens to consumers?
    (d) Is this an example of creative destruction? Explain.
  3. Country Verdana has a high savings rate, strong educational institutions, and stable government. Country Novara has a low savings rate, underfunded schools, and political instability. Both have similar natural resources and populations. Predict which country will grow faster and explain the mechanisms.
  4. A government is deciding whether to fund basic scientific research at universities. A politician argues: "Private companies should fund research. If the research is valuable, they'll pay for it." An economist responds that private markets will underinvest in basic research. Who is right? Explain using the concept of positive externalities.

Economic Reasoning Questions

  1. Marginal thinking and growth: A country is deciding how to allocate an additional $100 billion in government spending between (a) road and infrastructure investment and (b) current government consumption. From a long-run growth perspective, what factors should determine which allocation produces more future output? What information would you want to know?
  2. Tradeoffs between present and future: Explain in economic terms why raising the savings rate increases future living standards but requires a sacrifice in the present. Who might prefer a low savings rate, and who might prefer a high savings rate? Is there a "correct" savings rate?
  3. Distributional effects of growth: Real GDP per capita grows by 3% per year, but almost all of the gain goes to the top 20% of earners, while median household income is flat. Has the country grown? Has living standards for the typical household improved? How should economists think about this situation?
  4. Institutions and incentives: Explain why a system in which government officials can seize a successful business without compensation reduces the incentive to invest and innovate, even for firms that have not been seized.
  5. Short-run vs. long-run effects of creative destruction: A new technology eliminates 500,000 jobs in a particular industry. An economist says: "In the long run, the new technology creates more jobs and raises living standards." A displaced worker responds: "I can't wait 20 years. I have a family to support now." How should economic policy address this tension? What tools might help?

Policy Scenario Questions

  1. R&D tax credits: A government introduces tax credits for private firms that invest in research and development.

    (a) What market failure is this policy trying to correct?
    (b) What is the mechanism by which this policy could raise long-run growth?
    (c) What are the potential costs and risks?
    (d) Separate the positive claim (what will happen) from the normative claim (whether the government should do this).
  2. Immigration and human capital: A country debates whether to allow high-skilled immigration.

    (a) How could allowing high-skilled immigrants affect labor productivity and technological progress?
    (b) Are there potential costs or distributional effects?
    (c) What evidence would an economist want to examine before making a recommendation?
  3. Environmental regulation and growth: A new environmental regulation will reduce carbon emissions but also raise production costs for manufacturers. Critics say it will reduce economic growth. Supporters say the damage from climate change will reduce future growth more than the regulation will.

    (a) How should an economist think about the tradeoff between current costs and future environmental benefits?
    (b) What does sustainable development thinking add to this analysis?
    (c) What are the distributional implications of the regulation and of climate change itself?
  4. Education investment in a low-income country: An international development organization is advising a low-income country on how to accelerate growth. It proposes large investments in primary and secondary education.

    (a) Through what mechanisms would education investment raise future growth?
    (b) What tradeoffs does this involve in the short run?
    (c) Are there complementary investments (infrastructure, health) that would increase the effectiveness of education investment?

Identify the error in each statement and explain the correct reasoning.

  2. "Since a country grows at 3% this year and 3% next year, the total growth over two years is 6%."

Real-World Applications

  1. The East Asian miracle: South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Hong Kong all grew extremely rapidly in the second half of the twentieth century, transforming from low-income to high-income economies in two to three generations. Research suggests that their growth involved high savings rates, heavy investment in education, rapid adoption of foreign technology, export-oriented trade policies, and relatively strong institutional quality.

    (a) Connect each of these factors to the growth framework in this chapter.
    (b) What does the East Asian experience suggest about whether poor countries can grow rapidly?
  2. Automation and labor productivity: New automation technologies in manufacturing and logistics significantly raise output per worker but reduce employment in those sectors.

    (a) Is this an example of productivity growth? Explain.
    (b) What happens to total output? What happens to the workers displaced?
    (c) Is this creative destruction? What historical examples does it resemble?
  3. Infrastructure and growth in rural areas: A rural region with poor roads has low agricultural productivity because farmers cannot easily transport crops to markets, cannot receive inputs efficiently, and face high prices for goods from cities.

    (a) How does infrastructure investment fit into the growth framework?
    (b) What is the mechanism by which road construction could raise labor productivity for farmers?
    (c) What other investments might complement infrastructure to maximize growth in this region?
  4. Genuine savings and resource-rich countries: Some countries have high current GDP growth because they are extracting and selling large quantities of oil, minerals, or timber. But their natural capital is being depleted. Using the concept of genuine savings, evaluate whether this growth is truly sustainable.

Preview of Chapter 29: International Trade, Exchange Rates, and Globalization

Chapter 28 focused on the sources of long-run growth within an economy. Chapter 29 extends the analysis to international economics. Countries do not grow in isolation — they trade goods and services, exchange currencies, participate in global supply chains, and are affected by each other's economic decisions. Chapter 29 will introduce imports and exports, comparative advantage in international trade, tariffs and quotas, exchange rates, appreciation and depreciation, globalization, and the policy debates around trade.

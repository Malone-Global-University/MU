Chapter 9 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Markets communicate through prices. When the price of gasoline rises, some drivers cut back on long trips. When the price of a concert ticket doubles, some fans decide to stay home. When the price of insulin rises, most diabetic patients continue buying it because they have no choice.

The question economists ask is not simply whether buyers and sellers respond to price changes — they almost always do. The question is how much they respond. A small price increase might cause a huge drop in sales for one product and barely any drop for another. A tax on cigarettes might raise enormous government revenue or almost none, depending on how responsive smokers are to higher prices.

Elasticity is the economic concept that measures this responsiveness. It turns a qualitative question — "Do buyers respond to price?" — into a quantitative one: "By how much, exactly?" Businesses use it to set prices and predict revenue. Governments use it to estimate the effects of taxes and regulations. Farmers, pharmaceutical companies, retailers, labor unions, and tax authorities all need to understand elasticity to make good decisions.

Opening Question

Think of one product you would keep buying even if its price doubled, and one product you would stop buying after even a small price increase. What makes those two products different? By the end of this chapter, you will have precise language and a formula for explaining exactly why.

Section 9.1

What Elasticity Measures

Suppose a local coffee shop raises the price of a medium coffee from $3.00 to $3.30 — a 10 percent increase. After the price change, the shop sells 8 percent fewer coffees per day. Was that response large or small? It depends on what you are comparing. Most regular coffee drinkers accepted the higher price without changing their habits.

Now suppose the same shop raises the price of bottled water from $1.00 to $1.50 — a 50 percent increase. After the price change, the shop sells 70 percent fewer bottles of water, because customers simply walk to the water fountain down the hall or buy water at the grocery store next door. The response to the water price increase was much larger, relative to the price change, than the response to the coffee price increase. Water customers are more price-responsive than coffee customers — at least in this setting.

Elasticity gives economists a way to measure and compare these responses precisely.

The General Formula

Elasticity = (Percentage change in the outcome variable) ÷ (Percentage change in the cause variable)

This ratio answers the question: for every 1 percent change in the cause, how many percent does the outcome change? For price elasticity of demand, the cause is price and the outcome is quantity demanded. For price elasticity of supply, the cause is price and the outcome is quantity supplied.

Why Percentages, Not Units

Expressing elasticity as a ratio of percentage changes makes it unit-free. Comparing "a change of 10 gallons" to "a change of 10 car stereos" is meaningless, because the goods are measured in different units and cost different amounts. Comparing "a 10 percent change in milk sales" to "a 10 percent change in car stereo sales" is perfectly sensible. Elasticity works across any product, market, currency, or time period because it always speaks in percentages.

Elasticity connects price changes to real-world outcomes. A firm raising its price needs to know whether it will earn more or less revenue afterward. A government imposing a tax needs to know whether buyers will absorb it or cut back sharply. A farmer facing falling crop prices needs to know whether producing more or less will improve income. In each case, the answer depends on elasticity.

Section 9.2

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in its price. It answers: when price changes by 1 percent, by what percentage does quantity demanded change?

Formula

E_d = (Percentage change in quantity demanded) ÷ (Percentage change in price)

Percentage change for any variable: (New value − Old value) ÷ Old value × 100

Example 9.2.1
Calculating price elasticity of demand for concert tickets
  • SituationThe price of a concert ticket rises from $50 to $60, and quantity demanded falls from 1,000 tickets to 800 tickets.
  • % Price($60 − $50) ÷ $50 × 100 = 20%
  • % Quantity(800 − 1,000) ÷ 1,000 × 100 = −20%
  • E_dE_d = −20% ÷ 20% = −1.0
Key takeaway: Price elasticity of demand for concert tickets at this point is 1.0 in absolute value — unit elastic.
Sign Convention

Price elasticity of demand is almost always negative, because demand curves slope downward. Economists usually report the absolute value to make comparisons easier. When this chapter says "elasticity equals 2.0," it means the absolute value is 2.0.

A Subtle Problem: Direction Matters

The basic percentage-change formula gives a different elasticity depending on whether you go from $50 to $60 or from $60 to $50, because the denominator changes. The midpoint method solves this by using the average of the two values as the base:

% Change in quantity = (Q₂ − Q₁) ÷ [(Q₁ + Q₂) ÷ 2] × 100

% Change in price = (P₂ − P₁) ÷ [(P₁ + P₂) ÷ 2] × 100

For introductory purposes, using the basic formula with a stated direction is acceptable. The midpoint method is presented here so you recognize it in more advanced settings.

Section 9.3

Elastic, Inelastic, and Unit Elastic Demand

Not all demand responds to price in the same way. Economists classify demand into categories based on the elasticity value.

Elastic demand (E_d > 1): Quantity demanded changes by a larger percentage than price. Buyers are highly responsive — they have good substitutes, are not urgent about the purchase, or the item is not a large necessity. Examples: luxury clothing, vacation travel, restaurant meals, brand-name electronics.

Inelastic demand (E_d < 1): Quantity demanded changes by a smaller percentage than price. Buyers are not very responsive — they may have few substitutes, need the good urgently, or the purchase may be a small share of their budget. Examples: insulin, gasoline in the short run, cigarettes for addicted smokers, electricity, basic food staples.

Unit elastic demand (E_d = 1): Quantity demanded changes by exactly the same percentage as price. No real market is exactly unit elastic at all prices, but the concept is a useful dividing line between elastic and inelastic demand.

The Extreme Cases

Perfectly inelastic demand (E_d = 0): Quantity demanded does not change at all when price changes — a vertical demand curve. Rare, but life-saving medications with no alternatives come close.

Perfectly elastic demand (E_d = infinity): Buyers will buy any quantity at one price but none at a higher price — a horizontal demand curve. Individual wheat farmers face nearly perfectly elastic demand; they can sell any amount at the market price, but lose all buyers if they charge even a penny more.

Elasticity Value (absolute)LabelMeaning
0Perfectly inelasticQuantity does not respond at all
Between 0 and 1InelasticQuantity responds less than price
Exactly 1Unit elasticQuantity responds equally to price
Greater than 1ElasticQuantity responds more than price
InfinityPerfectly elasticBuyers buy all at one price, none above

Section 9.4

Determinants of Demand Elasticity

Why is demand elastic for some goods and inelastic for others? Several factors determine how responsive buyers are to price changes.

DeterminantMore Elastic When…More Inelastic When…
SubstitutesClose substitutes are readily availableFew or no substitutes exist
Necessity vs. luxuryThe good is a luxuryThe good is a necessity
Share of budgetThe good takes a large share of spendingThe good is a tiny share of spending
Time horizonBuyers have had a long time to adjustBuyers must respond immediately, in the short run
Market definitionThe market is narrowly defined (one brand)The market is broadly defined (the whole category)
Substitutes Are the Most Important Determinant

The demand for Coca-Cola is more elastic than the demand for cola drinks generally, which is more elastic than the demand for all beverages together. At the broadest category, fewer substitutes exist, so demand is less elastic. This is why a specific brand almost always has higher elasticity than its broader product category.

Example 9.4.1
Time horizon and gasoline demand
  • Short runWhen gasoline prices rise sharply, drivers cannot immediately sell their cars and buy fuel-efficient models. They continue driving and paying — demand is inelastic.
  • Long runOver years, drivers replace old vehicles with efficient ones, move closer to work, or switch to public transit. The long-run response is much larger — demand is more elastic.
Key takeaway: Demand tends to be more elastic in the long run than in the short run, because buyers have more time to adjust.

Section 9.5

Elasticity and Total Revenue

One of the most important applications of price elasticity of demand is understanding how a price change affects a seller's total revenue.

Total Revenue

Total Revenue = Price × Quantity Sold

When a seller raises price, two forces work in opposite directions: the price effect (each unit sold brings in more revenue) and the quantity effect (fewer units are sold). Which effect wins depends on elasticity.

Demand TypePrice Increase → Total RevenuePrice Decrease → Total Revenue
Elastic (E_d > 1)FallsRises
Unit elastic (E_d = 1)UnchangedUnchanged
Inelastic (E_d < 1)RisesFalls
The Pricing Rule

A firm wanting to increase revenue should raise price if demand is inelastic, and lower price if demand is elastic. Raising price when demand is elastic is a common and costly mistake — it reduces both customers and revenue.

Example 9.5.1
A bakery raises its price: two possible outcomes
  • Baseline200 loaves per week at $3.00 per loaf. Total revenue = $3.00 × 200 = $600 per week. Price rises to $3.60 — a 20% increase.
  • Scenario AQuantity falls to 120 loaves (a 40% drop). E_d = 40% ÷ 20% = 2.0 (elastic). New revenue = $3.60 × 120 = $432 — revenue fell
  • Scenario BQuantity falls to 190 loaves (a 5% drop). E_d = 5% ÷ 20% = 0.25 (inelastic). New revenue = $3.60 × 190 = $684 — revenue rose
Key takeaway: Identical price increases produce opposite revenue outcomes depending entirely on elasticity.
Practice 9.5Revenue and Elasticity
  1. A streaming service raises its subscription price by 5 percent. Subscribers are highly loyal and rarely cancel. What likely happens to total revenue? Explain.
  2. A coffee shop lowers the price of its specialty drinks by 10 percent. Many competing coffee shops are nearby. What likely happens to total revenue? Explain.
  3. A government study estimates that demand for a certain medication has price elasticity of 0.15. The manufacturer raises the price by 30 percent. What happens to total revenue? What does this mean for patients who need the drug?
  4. Wheat farmers produce a record harvest. The price of wheat falls 20 percent. Demand for wheat is highly inelastic (E_d ≈ 0.3). What happens to total farm revenue from wheat? Is this a surprise?

Section 9.6

Price Elasticity of Supply

Price elasticity of supply measures how responsive the quantity supplied of a good is to a change in its price. Because supply curves slope upward — when price rises, quantity supplied rises — both percentage changes move in the same direction, so price elasticity of supply is positive.

Formula

E_s = (Percentage change in quantity supplied) ÷ (Percentage change in price)

CategoryMeaning
Elastic supply (E_s > 1)Quantity supplied changes by more than price; sellers expand production easily
Inelastic supply (E_s < 1)Quantity supplied changes by less than price; sellers cannot easily expand
Unit elastic supply (E_s = 1)Quantity supplied changes by the same percentage as price
Perfectly inelastic supply (E_s = 0)Quantity supplied does not change at all; vertical supply curve
Perfectly elastic supply (E_s = infinity)Sellers supply any quantity at one price, none below it; horizontal supply curve
Example 9.6.1
Calculating elasticity of supply for residential painting
  • SituationThe price of residential painting rises from $2,000 to $2,500 per job — a 25% increase. The number of painters offering services rises from 80 to 96 — a 20% increase.
  • E_sE_s = 20% ÷ 25% = 0.8
  • InterpretationSupply is inelastic. Painters cannot expand their numbers quickly, even at higher prices — it takes time to find workers, train them, and acquire equipment.
Key takeaway: A value below 1 means sellers respond less than proportionally to the price change.

Section 9.7

Determinants of Supply Elasticity

Why is supply elastic for some goods and inelastic for others? Several factors determine how responsive sellers are.

DeterminantMore Elastic When…More Inelastic When…
Time horizonSellers have had a long time to expand capacitySellers must respond immediately, in the short run
Availability of inputsInputs are readily available and quick to obtainInputs are scarce, specialized, or slow to produce
Flexibility of productionResources can shift easily between usesProduction is highly specialized
Inventory and storageGoods can be stored and released as neededGoods are perishable and cannot be stored
Entry of new firmsNew firms can enter easilyEntry requires large capital, rare resources, or approvals
Time Is the Most Important Determinant

In the short run, sellers are constrained by fixed resources — factory size, equipment, land, and specialized workers. In the long run, they can build new factories, hire and train workers, acquire more land, and adopt new technology. As a result, supply is almost always more elastic in the long run than in the short run. When oil prices rise sharply, oil companies cannot immediately open new wells; over years, they invest in new exploration, drilling, and refining capacity.

Software Versus Wine

A software company can add digital copies of its product almost instantly at low cost — highly elastic supply. A vineyard producing premium wine cannot quickly expand because premium grapes take years to grow — very inelastic supply. The same logic of input availability explains both cases.

Section 9.8

Income Elasticity at an Introductory Level

Not all demand responses are about price. Income also matters. When household incomes rise, spending patterns change. Income elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in consumer income.

Formula

Income Elasticity = (Percentage change in quantity demanded) ÷ (Percentage change in income)

Income ElasticityType of GoodExample
NegativeInferior goodInstant noodles, low-quality generic products
Between 0 and 1Normal good (necessity)Food staples
Greater than 1Normal good (luxury)Foreign travel, yachts, private schools

When income rises 10 percent and quantity demanded of restaurant meals rises 15 percent, income elasticity is 1.5 — restaurant meals are a luxury. As incomes rise, consumers can afford better alternatives and switch away from inferior goods, so demand for those goods falls even as overall spending power increases.

  2. A country's incomes rise by 20 percent, and sales of low-quality generic canned food fall by 10 percent. Calculate income elasticity for this canned food and classify the good.
  3. Give one example of a good you believe is inferior in your own household's spending and explain your reasoning.
Section 9.9

Cross-Price Elasticity at an Introductory Level

Demand for one good can respond to price changes in other goods — particularly substitutes and complements. Cross-price elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded of one good is to a change in the price of a different good.

Formula

Cross-Price Elasticity = (% Change in quantity demanded of Good A) ÷ (% Change in price of Good B)

Cross-Price ElasticityRelationshipExample
PositiveSubstitutesButter and margarine
Near zeroUnrelated goodsShoes and motor oil
NegativeComplementsSmartphones and smartphone cases
Example 9.9.1
Substitutes versus complements
  • SubstitutesIf the price of butter rises 20% and quantity demanded of margarine rises 10%, cross-price elasticity is +0.5. Butter and margarine are substitutes.
  • ComplementsIf the price of smartphones rises 15% and quantity demanded of smartphone cases falls 12%, cross-price elasticity is −0.8. Smartphones and cases are complements.
Key takeaway: The sign tells you the relationship; the size tells you how strong that relationship is.

Section 9.10

Elasticity in Business and Public Policy

Elasticity is not just theoretical — it has direct, practical applications in pricing decisions, tax policy, agriculture, labor markets, and drug policy.

Business Pricing

Firms want to know their demand elasticity before changing prices. A firm with inelastic demand — perhaps because it has a unique product or strong brand loyalty — can raise prices and increase revenue. Airline pricing illustrates this: business travelers with inelastic demand pay high prices for flexible tickets, while leisure travelers with elastic demand receive deeply discounted fares with restrictions. This is price discrimination based on different elasticities. Brand-name medications face similarly inelastic demand from patients without alternatives, while generic drugs face much more elastic demand because many manufacturers offer the same compound.

Tax Policy and Who Pays

When a government taxes a product, who actually bears the cost depends on the relative elasticities of supply and demand. If demand is inelastic and supply is elastic, buyers bear most of the burden, since they cannot easily reduce purchases. If demand is elastic and supply is inelastic, sellers bear more of the burden, because buyers will sharply cut back if prices rise.

Cigarette Taxes

Cigarette taxes are partly justified because demand for cigarettes is relatively inelastic, especially for addicted adult smokers — taxes raise significant revenue because purchases do not fall much. Elasticity is higher among young people who have not yet become addicted, which is one argument for why high taxes may reduce smoking initiation even if they do little to change established smokers' behavior.

Agricultural Prices and the Farm Problem

Agricultural products tend to have inelastic demand — food is a necessity, and people do not eat vastly more simply because prices fall. When farmers have an exceptional harvest, supply rises and prices fall. Because demand is inelastic, the percentage price drop exceeds the percentage quantity increase, so total revenue to farmers can fall even though production rose. This is the farm problem, and it helps explain why agricultural price supports and crop insurance exist in many countries.

Drug Policy and Minimum Wage

If demand for an illegal drug is inelastic, policies that reduce supply and raise street prices may not substantially reduce use, but may instead increase dealer income and user-driven crime. Policies targeting demand — treatment, prevention, education — may be more effective when demand is inelastic. This is a positive observation about likely consequences, not a complete policy recommendation.

Debates about minimum wage often turn on the elasticity of labor demand. If labor demand is inelastic, a minimum wage increase raises wages with few job losses; if labor demand is elastic, the same increase may cause significantly more job losses. The empirical evidence on this elasticity remains an active area of research, a topic Chapter 15 returns to.

Chapter 9 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 9.1Elasticity measures responsiveness — how much one variable changes relative to a change in another — expressed as a ratio of percentage changes, which makes it unit-free and comparable across markets.
  • Sec 9.2Price elasticity of demand equals percentage change in quantity demanded divided by percentage change in price. It is reported as an absolute value, and the midpoint method removes the direction problem in the basic formula.
  • Sec 9.3Demand is elastic when E_d > 1, inelastic when E_d < 1, and unit elastic when E_d = 1. Perfectly inelastic and perfectly elastic demand represent the theoretical extremes of zero and infinite responsiveness.
  • Sec 9.4Demand elasticity depends on the availability of substitutes (the most important factor), whether the good is a necessity or luxury, its share of the buyer's budget, the time horizon for adjustment, and how broadly the market is defined.
  • Sec 9.5When demand is elastic, a price increase reduces total revenue. When demand is inelastic, a price increase raises total revenue. When demand is unit elastic, total revenue is unchanged.
  • Sec 9.6Price elasticity of supply equals percentage change in quantity supplied divided by percentage change in price, and is always positive because supply curves slope upward.
  • Sec 9.7Supply elasticity depends mainly on the time horizon, plus availability of inputs, flexibility of production, ability to hold inventory, and ease of entry for new firms. Supply is generally more elastic in the long run.
  • Sec 9.8Income elasticity of demand classifies goods as normal (positive elasticity, further split into necessities and luxuries) or inferior (negative elasticity).
  • Sec 9.9Cross-price elasticity of demand classifies the relationship between two goods: positive values indicate substitutes, negative values indicate complements, and values near zero indicate unrelated goods.
  • Sec 9.10Elasticity guides business pricing and price discrimination, determines who bears the burden of a tax, explains the farm problem, informs drug policy debates, and shapes the economic analysis of minimum wage laws.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — Steepness equals inelasticity

Steeper demand curves do indicate more inelastic demand, but the visual slope depends on the units used on the axes. Elasticity is calculated from percentage changes, not from slope directly. Two curves with the same slope can have different elasticities at different points.

Misconception 2 — Needs mean perfectly inelastic demand

Many goods people "need" still have real elasticity. Someone needs transportation but can substitute biking for driving. Someone needs protein but can substitute beans for beef. True perfect inelasticity is extremely rare.

Misconception 3 — Raising prices always hurts sellers

Whether a price increase helps or hurts total revenue depends entirely on elasticity. When demand is inelastic, raising prices increases revenue, and many successful businesses deliberately create inelastic demand through branding and quality.

Misconception 4 — Elasticity is fixed for a product

Elasticity changes over time, across locations, at different income levels, and at different points on the demand curve. The elasticity of gasoline demand differs between the short run and the long run, and even differs at high versus low prices.

Misconception 5 — Cross-price elasticity only applies to obvious pairs

Any two goods can have cross-price elasticity, not just obvious competitors or complements. If streaming movie prices rise enough, some consumers may substitute reading or outdoor activities instead.

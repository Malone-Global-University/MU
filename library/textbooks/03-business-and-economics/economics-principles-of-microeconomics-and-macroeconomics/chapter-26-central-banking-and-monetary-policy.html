Chapter 26 — Front Matter

Chapter Introduction

Every modern economy depends on money. But money by itself is not enough. Someone must manage the supply of money, watch over the banking system, and respond when credit freezes, inflation surges, or recession threatens. That someone is the central bank.

Central banking sits at the intersection of economics and institutions. A central bank is not a regular bank. It does not take deposits from households or make car loans. Its job is to maintain the conditions that allow the whole financial system to function — stable prices, a reliable banking network, and interest rates that support growth without stoking inflation.

This chapter explains what central banks do, how they use their tools, and what tradeoffs they face. Students will see how interest rates connect to borrowing, spending, and investment across the whole economy. They will learn how monetary policy can push against a recession or cool down an overheating economy — and why neither task is simple.

Why This Chapter Matters

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain the goals and tools of central banking, reason through the effects of expansionary and contractionary monetary policy, and evaluate the tradeoffs that monetary policymakers face between inflation, employment, and economic stability.

Section 26.1

What Central Banks Do

A central bank is a public institution that manages a country's money supply, oversees its banking system, and uses monetary policy to pursue economic goals. Almost every country in the world has one. The United States has the Federal Reserve, often called "the Fed." Other well-known central banks include the European Central Bank (ECB), the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan, and the People's Bank of China.

Central banks are unusual institutions. They are neither ordinary government agencies nor private profit-seeking firms. In most countries, the central bank operates with significant independence from political leadership — meaning that elected officials cannot simply order the central bank to print money or hold interest rates at a politically convenient level. This independence exists because monetary policy works best when it is guided by economic conditions rather than short-term political pressures.

Core FunctionDescription
Managing the money supplyAdjusting the amount of money circulating in the economy to help keep growth and inflation on a stable path
Setting short-term interest ratesInfluencing the rates banks charge each other overnight, which ripple outward into mortgages, business loans, and credit cards
Supervising and regulating banksEnsuring commercial banks remain solvent, follow sound lending practices, and hold adequate reserves
Acting as lender of last resortLending to banks facing a sudden cash shortage to prevent panics from spreading
Issuing currencyServing as the sole authorized issuer of a country's paper bills and coins
Conducting monetary policyUsing its tools to influence inflation, employment, and economic growth
The Federal Reserve: A Closer Look

The Federal Reserve was created by the U.S. Congress in 1913, after a series of banking panics demonstrated that the country needed a lender of last resort. The Fed is organized as a system with 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks spread across the country, overseen by a Board of Governors in Washington, D.C. The most important policymaking body is the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC), which meets roughly eight times per year to decide on the federal funds rate — the target interest rate for overnight loans between banks.

Key Term

A central bank is a public institution that manages the money supply, oversees the banking system, acts as lender of last resort, and conducts monetary policy to pursue national economic goals.

Section 26.2

The Banking System and Reserves

A commercial bank takes in deposits from households and businesses and makes loans. It earns profit by charging borrowers a higher interest rate than it pays to depositors. Banks are essential intermediaries: they channel savings from people who have extra money toward people and businesses who need it.

Every bank holds reserves — funds it keeps on hand rather than lending out. Reserves exist as vault cash (physical currency in the bank's vault) and as reserve deposits at the central bank (funds commercial banks hold on account at the Federal Reserve, which are immediately accessible).

Historically, central banks set a required reserve ratio — a minimum percentage of deposits banks must keep as reserves rather than lend out. Reserves held beyond the required minimum are called excess reserves. Banks may hold excess reserves for caution, or because the central bank pays interest on them, making it attractive to park money at the Fed rather than lend it at a low rate.

Note for Students

In 2020, the Federal Reserve reduced required reserve ratios to zero for U.S. banks, partly because other tools for controlling the money supply had become more important. However, the concept of reserve ratios remains foundational for understanding how central banks influence money creation, and most economics instruction continues to use it as a teaching framework.

How Banks Create Money

One of the most important and often misunderstood features of banking is that banks create money through lending. When a bank takes in $1,000 of deposits and lends out $900, the borrower deposits that $900 somewhere else, and a second bank can then lend out $810 of it, and so on. This process is called the money multiplier.

Formula

Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ Reserve Ratio

With a reserve ratio of 10 percent (0.10): Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ 0.10 = 10. This means $1 of new reserves can theoretically support up to $10 of total deposits in the banking system. In practice, the actual multiplier is lower because households hold some cash outside of banks and banks may hold excess reserves rather than lending out every available dollar.

Worked Example 26.1
Calculating the money multiplier
  • SituationA bank receives a new deposit of $500. The required reserve ratio is 20 percent.
  • Required reserves$500 × 0.20 = $100
  • Maximum loans$500 − $100 = $400
  • Multiplier1 ÷ 0.20 = 5
  • Max new money$500 × 5 = $2,500
Key takeaway: $2,500 is the theoretical upper limit if every loan is re-deposited and re-lent at every stage.
Key Term

Reserves are funds that commercial banks hold as vault cash or deposits at the central bank rather than lending out. Excess reserves are reserves held beyond the required minimum.

Section 26.3

Interest Rates and Borrowing

Interest rates are the price of borrowing money. Just as the price of wheat influences how much wheat buyers demand and sellers supply, the interest rate influences how much borrowing and lending take place across the economy.

The federal funds rate is the interest rate at which commercial banks lend their reserve deposits to one another overnight. The Fed targets a specific range for this rate — and its decisions ripple outward throughout the financial system, because almost every other interest rate in the economy is connected to it.

When the Federal Funds Rate RisesWhen the Federal Funds Rate Falls
Banks pay more to borrow reserves overnightBanks pay less to borrow reserves overnight
Banks charge higher rates on all types of loansBanks charge lower rates on all types of loans
Mortgage, car loan, business loan, and credit card rates riseMortgage, car loan, business loan, and credit card rates fall
Borrowing becomes more expensive; spending tends to slowBorrowing becomes cheaper; spending tends to increase

Firms make investment decisions — building factories, buying equipment, expanding operations — based partly on the cost of borrowing. When interest rates are low, an investment is more likely to clear the profitability threshold. Households respond similarly: lower mortgage and car loan rates make big-ticket purchases more affordable, while higher rates can cause those purchases to be deferred.

Nominal Versus Real Interest Rates

The nominal interest rate is the stated rate on a loan. The real interest rate is the nominal rate minus the inflation rate. A loan at 5 percent interest when inflation is 3 percent has a real interest rate of only 2 percent, because the purchasing power of the dollars repaid is being eroded by inflation. Central banks pay close attention to real interest rates, because it is the real rate — not the nominal rate — that determines the true cost of borrowing.

Formula

Real Interest Rate ≈ Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

Key Term

The federal funds rate is the short-term interest rate at which commercial banks lend reserve deposits to one another overnight, and it is the primary target of Federal Reserve monetary policy.

Section 26.4

Monetary Policy Goals

Central banks pursue their monetary policy within a framework of goals. The goals are not chosen arbitrarily — they reflect judgments about what makes an economy healthy and stable over time.

Price stability means keeping the inflation rate low and predictable. High inflation erodes purchasing power, distorts business planning, and distributes income arbitrarily — hurting savers and those on fixed incomes while benefiting debtors. Deflation can be equally destructive: when prices are expected to fall, households delay purchases, firms cut back, and unemployment rises. Most modern central banks target an inflation rate of around 2 percent per year, leaving a buffer against deflation.

Many central banks — including the Federal Reserve — are also charged with supporting maximum employment, meaning the highest level of employment consistent with price stability. Full employment does not mean zero unemployment; it means an unemployment rate consistent with normal job turnover, sometimes called the natural rate of unemployment.

The Dual Mandate

The Federal Reserve operates under a dual mandate: pursue both price stability and maximum employment. This creates a fundamental policy tension. Lower interest rates stimulate employment but can also raise inflation. Higher rates reduce inflation but can increase unemployment. Other central banks, like the European Central Bank, have a primary mandate focused more narrowly on price stability.

Financial Stability

Beyond its formal mandates, the central bank also cares about financial stability — preventing bank failures, credit freezes, and financial panics that can devastate the real economy. The 2008 financial crisis was a stark reminder that financial instability can cause economic damage far beyond what normal recessions produce.

Section 26.5

Open Market Operations at an Introductory Level

Open market operations are the central bank's primary day-to-day tool for implementing monetary policy. The name refers to buying and selling securities — primarily U.S. government bonds — in the open financial market.

When the Fed wants to increase the money supply and push interest rates down, it buys government bonds. The sellers receive payment, which shows up as new reserve deposits at the Fed. Banks now have more reserves, can make more loans, and the federal funds rate tends to fall. When the Fed wants to decrease the money supply, it sells government bonds, withdrawing reserves and pushing the federal funds rate up.

Fed ActionEffect on ReservesEffect on Money SupplyEffect on Interest Rates
Buys bonds (securities)Reserves increaseMoney supply expandsInterest rates fall
Sells bonds (securities)Reserves decreaseMoney supply contractsInterest rates rise
Why Government Bonds?

Government bonds are ideal for open market operations because they are safe, liquid, and traded in enormous volume every day. The Fed can buy or sell billions of dollars worth of bonds without disrupting normal market functioning.

Quantitative Easing

After the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed expanded its open market operations in an unconventional way: instead of only buying short-term government securities, it purchased long-term government bonds and mortgage-backed securities in massive quantities. This strategy — called quantitative easing (QE) — was designed to push down long-term interest rates when short-term rates were already near zero. It was used again during the COVID-19 economic shock in 2020.

Key Term

Open market operations are the central bank's purchases and sales of government securities in financial markets, used to expand or contract bank reserves and influence the money supply and interest rates.

Section 26.6

Reserve Requirements at an Introductory Level

Reserve requirements are a second tool of monetary policy, though in practice they are used far less frequently than open market operations. By raising the reserve requirement, the central bank forces banks to hold more money as reserves and lend out less — contracting the money supply. By lowering the reserve requirement, the central bank frees banks to lend more — expanding the money supply.

Reserve RequirementDepositsRequired ReservesMaximum Loans
10%$1,000,000$100,000$900,000
20%$1,000,000$200,000$800,000
5%$1,000,000$50,000$950,000
Limits of Reserve Requirements as a Policy Tool

Central banks rarely adjust reserve requirements as a regular monetary policy instrument because large changes create uncertainty for banks and disrupt lending relationships, banks often hold significant excess reserves anyway, and open market operations achieve similar results with more flexibility and precision. In the United States, required reserve ratios were cut to zero in 2020, and the Fed now relies primarily on its interest rate target and interest paid on reserve balances.

Key Term

A reserve requirement is the minimum percentage of deposits that banks must hold as reserves rather than lend out, set by the central bank as a tool for influencing the money supply.

Section 26.7

Discount Lending at an Introductory Level

The central bank's third traditional monetary policy tool is its role as lender of last resort — the ability to make emergency loans to commercial banks through the discount window. The discount rate is the interest rate the central bank charges when it lends directly to commercial banks. It is typically set above the federal funds rate, making Fed borrowing a backup option that banks turn to only when necessary.

If the central bank raises the discount rate, banks become more cautious about extending loans, reducing the money supply. If it lowers the discount rate, banks can lend more confidently, expanding the money supply.

Lender of Last Resort and Bank Runs

If depositors fear their bank may fail, they may rush to withdraw money — a bank run — which can push a sound bank into crisis. Knowing that the central bank stands ready to lend gives banks a safety net and gives depositors confidence. During the 2008 financial crisis and again during the COVID-19 shock in 2020, the Federal Reserve dramatically expanded its lending facilities beyond the traditional discount window to stabilize financial markets.

Key Term

The discount rate is the interest rate the central bank charges commercial banks for direct loans through the discount window, serving as a tool for monetary policy and a backstop for financial stability.

Section 26.8

Expansionary Monetary Policy

Expansionary monetary policy is the set of central bank actions taken to increase the money supply, lower interest rates, and stimulate economic activity. It is typically used when the economy is in recession or when unemployment is high and growth is sluggish.

The Transmission Chain

Fed buys securities → Bank reserves increase → Federal funds rate falls → All borrowing rates fall → Household borrowing rises → Business investment rises → Aggregate demand increases → Real GDP rises → Unemployment falls

Expansionary Policy and Inflation Risk

Expansionary policy carries a risk: if the economy is pushed beyond its productive capacity, inflation rises. Too much money chasing too few goods pushes prices up. Central banks must calibrate expansion carefully to avoid trading a recession problem for an inflation problem.

During the Great Recession (2007–2009), the Fed cut the federal funds rate from over 5 percent to nearly zero and kept it there for years. During the COVID-19 recession in 2020, the Fed again cut rates to near zero within weeks, combined with large-scale quantitative easing.

The Zero Lower Bound

When interest rates reach zero — called the zero lower bound — the traditional tool of cutting rates runs out. The Fed cannot easily push nominal rates below zero. This forces policymakers to rely on unconventional tools like quantitative easing and forward guidance.

Worked Example 26.2
Tracing the effects of an interest rate cut
  • Step 1Fed buys government securities → bank reserves rise
  • Step 2Federal funds rate falls from 4% to 2%
  • Step 3Mortgage rates fall (e.g., from 7% to 5%)
  • Step 4Business loan rates fall (e.g., from 6% to 4%)
  • Step 5More households qualify for mortgages; home buying increases
  • Step 6More firms borrow to invest in equipment and expand
  • Step 7Aggregate demand rises
  • Step 8Real GDP rises; unemployment falls
  • Step 9If expansion continues, inflation may start to rise
Key takeaway: An interest rate cut sets off a long chain of effects that take time to fully play out.
Key Term

Expansionary monetary policy refers to central bank actions that increase the money supply and reduce interest rates in order to stimulate borrowing, spending, investment, and economic growth.

Section 26.9

Contractionary Monetary Policy

Contractionary monetary policy is the set of central bank actions taken to reduce the money supply, raise interest rates, and slow economic activity. It is typically used when inflation is rising too fast or when the economy is overheating.

The Transmission Chain

Fed sells securities → Bank reserves decrease → Federal funds rate rises → All borrowing rates rise → Household borrowing falls → Business investment falls → Aggregate demand decreases → Inflation pressure eases → GDP growth slows

Contractionary Policy and Unemployment Risk

Just as expansionary policy risks inflation, contractionary policy risks unemployment. Slowing the economy reduces inflationary pressure but also slows hiring and can trigger layoffs. The central bank must judge how much restraint is needed to tame inflation without driving the economy into recession.

The most dramatic example of contractionary monetary policy in U.S. history occurred in the early 1980s. Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker raised interest rates dramatically — the federal funds rate peaked above 19 percent in 1981 — to break the high inflation of the 1970s. Inflation fell sharply but unemployment surged to nearly 11 percent, triggering a severe recession. Many economists credit this episode with establishing the Fed's credibility as an inflation-fighter.

In 2022–2023, the Federal Reserve raised rates aggressively again — the fastest series of rate hikes since the Volcker era — to bring down inflation that had surged to 40-year highs following the COVID-19 pandemic and related supply disruptions.

The Sacrifice Ratio

Economists use a concept called the sacrifice ratio to measure the cost of reducing inflation: how many percentage points of output (or unemployment) must be "sacrificed" to achieve a 1 percentage point reduction in inflation. A higher sacrifice ratio means the cost of fighting inflation is greater.

Key Term

Contractionary monetary policy refers to central bank actions that reduce the money supply and raise interest rates in order to slow borrowing, reduce spending, ease inflationary pressure, and cool an overheating economy.

Section 26.10

Inflation, Employment, and Policy Tradeoffs

Monetary policy is not a precise science. Policymakers operate with incomplete information, uncertain models, and significant time lags. Understanding the tradeoffs they face is essential for evaluating monetary policy decisions intelligently.

A widely observed but contested empirical relationship in economics is the Phillips curve, which suggests that in the short run, lower unemployment tends to be associated with higher inflation, and higher unemployment tends to be associated with lower inflation. The Phillips curve is useful but imperfect. In the 1970s, the U.S. experienced stagflation — high inflation and high unemployment simultaneously — which seemed to contradict a simple Phillips curve. Economists came to understand that the relationship depends heavily on inflation expectations.

Economic ConditionUnemploymentInflation
Overheating economyLowRising
RecessionHighFalling
Stagflation (unusual)HighHigh
Ideal stable growthModerateLow and stable

Lags in Monetary Policy

Monetary policy operates with significant time lags: a recognition lag (time to collect and analyze data), a decision lag (time for the central bank to meet and decide), an implementation lag (time for rate changes to filter through the financial system), and an effect lag (time for households and businesses to respond). Economists estimate that monetary policy changes may take 12 to 18 months or more to have their full effect on inflation and output.

The Taylor Rule

Target rate = Inflation rate + 2% + 0.5 × (Inflation gap) + 0.5 × (Output gap)

The inflation gap is the difference between actual inflation and the target rate (usually 2%). The output gap is the difference between actual GDP and potential GDP, expressed as a percentage. If inflation is above target and the economy is above potential output, the Taylor rule suggests raising interest rates. It is a guideline, not a rigid formula.

Central Bank Independence and Credibility

Central bank independence matters because monetary policy credibility affects inflation expectations. If households and firms believe the central bank will maintain price stability, they set wages and prices accordingly — making the job of controlling inflation easier. A government facing an election might prefer lower interest rates even if inflation is rising; an independent central bank can resist that pressure.

Limits of Monetary Policy

Monetary policy cannot directly fix structural problems like skills mismatches or infrastructure deficits. It cannot prevent recessions caused by supply shocks without accepting more inflation or more unemployment, since the two goals pull in opposite directions when a supply shock hits. It is less effective near the zero lower bound, operates with long lags, and cannot substitute for sound fiscal policy or strong economic institutions.

Key Term

The Phillips curve is an observed short-run relationship in which lower unemployment tends to be associated with higher inflation, and higher unemployment tends to be associated with lower inflation, though this relationship shifts depending on inflation expectations and other factors.

Real-World Applications

  1. During the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, the Federal Reserve cut the federal funds rate to near zero within weeks of the pandemic's onset and began purchasing massive quantities of Treasury securities and mortgage-backed securities. Why did the Fed respond so quickly and aggressively? What was it trying to prevent?
  2. In 2022, U.S. inflation surged to 9% — the highest rate since the early 1980s. The Fed responded by raising interest rates from near zero to over 5% in roughly 18 months. What risks did the Fed face in raising rates so quickly? What was it trying to achieve, and what were the tradeoffs?
  3. Many countries, including the United States and members of the European Union, maintain central banks with significant independence from elected governments. Some critics argue that this is undemocratic — monetary policy decisions affect millions of people but are made by unelected officials. How do economists defend central bank independence?   4. Japan maintained near-zero interest rates for decades to fight deflation and economic stagnation, with limited success. What does Japan's experience suggest about the limits of monetary policy? What other factors may contribute to long-run economic growth beyond central bank policy?
  5. A community bank in a small town holds $5 million in deposits. The reserve requirement is 8%. What is the maximum additional amount of money the bank could lend? Looking Ahead
Chapter 27: Fiscal Policy, Deficits, and Public Debt

Chapter 27 introduces fiscal policy — the government's use of spending and taxation to influence the economy. While monetary policy works through interest rates and the money supply, fiscal policy works through government budgets. Students will learn about automatic stabilizers, discretionary fiscal policy, budget deficits, public debt, and the long-run tradeoffs between short-run stimulus and fiscal sustainability. Together, fiscal policy and monetary policy form the two main instruments of macroeconomic stabilization — and understanding both is essential for analyzing economic events and policy debates.

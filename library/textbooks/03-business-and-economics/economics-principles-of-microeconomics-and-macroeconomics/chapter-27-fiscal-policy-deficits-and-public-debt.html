Chapter 27 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every year, governments make two fundamental decisions: how much money to collect, and how much money to spend. These decisions affect nearly everyone — workers, students, businesses, retirees, and future generations. When a government collects more in taxes than it spends, it runs a surplus. When it spends more than it collects, it runs a deficit. Over years of running deficits, governments accumulate public debt.

But fiscal policy is about more than bookkeeping. It is one of the most powerful tools governments have to stabilize a struggling economy, cool down an overheated one, and — over the long run — shape the path of growth, investment, and public services.

This chapter introduces fiscal policy: what it is, how it works, why governments use it, what it can and cannot do, and what tradeoffs it creates. You will learn about government budgets, taxes, transfers, automatic stabilizers, discretionary choices, deficits, surpluses, public debt, and the long-run challenges of fiscal sustainability.

Opening Question

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain how fiscal policy affects aggregate demand, distinguish between expansionary and contractionary policy, understand why deficits and debt are related but different, and reason carefully about policy tradeoffs rather than simply taking political sides.

Section 27.1

What Fiscal Policy Is

Fiscal policy refers to the decisions a government makes about its spending and taxation in order to influence economic conditions. The word fiscal comes from the Latin fiscus, meaning a basket or treasury — the public purse.

Fiscal policy is one of two major tools governments use to manage the macroeconomy. The other is monetary policy, which you studied in Chapter 26. While monetary policy is carried out by a central bank adjusting interest rates and the money supply, fiscal policy is carried out by the elected branches of government — legislatures and executives — who approve budgets, set tax rates, and authorize spending programs.

The central idea behind fiscal policy is straightforward: government spending and taxation affect the total level of spending in the economy, which in turn affects output, employment, and the price level. When the economy is in recession and unemployment is high, a government may increase spending or cut taxes to stimulate activity. When the economy is overheating and inflation is rising, a government may reduce spending or raise taxes to cool things down.

Fiscal Policy and Aggregate Demand

You learned in Chapter 23 that aggregate demand (AD) is the total demand for goods and services in the economy at different price levels. It has four components:

AD = C + I + G + NX

where C is consumption, I is investment, G is government purchases, and NX is net exports.

Government purchases (G) are a direct component of aggregate demand. When the government builds a road, hires a teacher, or purchases military equipment, it adds directly to GDP and shifts the AD curve to the right. When the government cuts spending, AD shifts left.

Taxation affects aggregate demand indirectly. When taxes fall, households have more after-tax income available for consumption, so C rises and AD shifts right. When taxes rise, households have less to spend, so C falls and AD shifts left. Similarly, business taxes affect investment spending.

Transfer payments — such as unemployment insurance, Social Security, or welfare benefits — are not counted directly in G because they do not purchase goods or services. But they do affect consumption by putting money in the hands of households, which then spend much of it.

Two Dimensions of Fiscal Policy

Economists distinguish between two broad dimensions of fiscal policy:

Automatic fiscal policy refers to changes in government spending and tax revenues that occur automatically as economic conditions change, without any new legislative decisions. These are built into the system.

Discretionary fiscal policy refers to deliberate legislative decisions to change tax rates, spending programs, or transfer payments in order to influence economic conditions. This requires action by the government.

Both types matter, and both are studied in this chapter.

Section 27.2

Government Budgets

To understand fiscal policy, you first need to understand how government budgets work.

A government budget is a plan showing expected revenues and expected expenditures over a period — usually one year. In most countries, the national government produces an annual budget that must be approved by the legislature.

Revenue: Where Money Comes From

Revenue SourceDescription
Individual income taxesTaxes on wages, salaries, investment income, and other earnings. The largest revenue source in most wealthy countries.
Payroll taxesTaxes on wages shared between employers and employees, often dedicated to retirement pensions and unemployment insurance.
Corporate income taxesTaxes on business profits.
Consumption taxesTaxes on spending, including sales taxes, value-added taxes (VAT), and excise taxes on gasoline, alcohol, and tobacco.
Property taxesLevied on real estate and other assets, often collected by state, provincial, or local governments.
Other revenuesFees, fines, tariffs, royalties, and other sources.

Expenditure: Where Money Goes

Expenditure CategoryDescription
Government purchases (G)Direct purchases — defense, infrastructure, education facilities, public health, research, and government salaries. Counted directly in GDP.
Transfer paymentsPayments to individuals — Social Security, unemployment insurance, disability payments, health coverage, and income support. Not counted in G.
Interest payments on debtInterest owed to creditors when governments borrow. Can become a substantial budget item in highly indebted countries.
Grants to lower levels of governmentIn federal systems, transfers from the national government to states, provinces, or municipalities.

The Budget Balance

The budget balance is the difference between revenues and expenditures:

Budget balance = Revenues − Expenditures

If revenues exceed expenditures, the government runs a budget surplus. It is collecting more than it spends. If expenditures exceed revenues, the government runs a budget deficit. It is spending more than it collects. If revenues exactly equal expenditures, the government has a balanced budget.

Key Distinction

A deficit is a flow — it measures the gap between revenues and spending over one period (usually one year). Public debt is a stock — it accumulates as deficits pile up over many years. The debt does not fall just because the deficit shrinks; the debt falls only if the government runs surpluses large enough to pay it down.

Section 27.3

Taxes, Spending, and Transfers

To reason clearly about fiscal policy, it helps to understand how taxes and spending each affect the economy — and why they do not affect it in exactly the same way.

How Taxes Affect the Economy

Taxes reduce disposable income for households and after-tax profits for businesses. This reduces consumption and investment spending, lowering aggregate demand.

However, not every dollar of a tax cut (or tax hike) translates into the same dollar change in spending. Households do not spend every extra dollar of after-tax income — they save some of it. The share of an extra dollar of income that a household spends is called the marginal propensity to consume (MPC). If the MPC is 0.8, for example, households spend 80 cents of every additional dollar and save 20 cents.

Because some of a tax cut leaks into savings, a dollar of tax reduction typically produces less than a dollar of additional spending initially. Over time, that initial spending creates income for others, who spend a share of it, and so on — producing a multiplier effect. But the tax multiplier is smaller than the spending multiplier, because the first round of a spending increase goes entirely into purchases, while the first round of a tax cut is partly saved.

How Government Purchases Affect the Economy

When the government directly purchases a good or service — a bridge, a soldier's salary, a research grant — that spending becomes someone's income. Those recipients spend a share of their new income, which becomes income for others, who spend a share, and so on.

This chain reaction is called the fiscal multiplier: a change in government purchases has a larger effect on total output than the initial change itself. If the MPC is 0.8, for example, every $1 of new government purchases eventually leads to roughly $5 of total economic activity as the initial dollar circulates through the economy (though real-world multipliers are smaller due to taxes, imports, and other leakages).

How Transfer Payments Affect the Economy

Transfer payments occupy a middle position. The government does not directly purchase output, but it puts money into households' hands. Recipients typically have high marginal propensities to consume — particularly lower-income households who receive most transfers. So transfers can be quite effective at stimulating consumption, even if they work slightly differently than direct purchases.

Summary: Three Fiscal Levers

Fiscal LeverDirectionMechanism
Government spending ↑ExpansionaryDirect increase in G
Government spending ↓ContractionaryDirect decrease in G
Tax cutExpansionaryHigher disposable income → more C
Tax increaseContractionaryLower disposable income → less C
Transfer increaseExpansionaryMore household income → more C
Transfer cutContractionaryLess household income → less C
Why the Multiplier Differs

A government purchase puts the full dollar into the spending stream immediately. A tax cut or transfer first passes through a household decision about how much to spend versus save. This is why, dollar for dollar, government purchases tend to have a larger initial multiplier than tax cuts of the same size — though both can ripple through the economy over time.

Section 27.4

Automatic Stabilizers

One of the most important features of modern fiscal systems is that they contain automatic stabilizers: features of the budget that automatically increase the deficit during recessions and reduce it during expansions — without any new decisions by lawmakers.

How Automatic Stabilizers Work

Consider what happens during a recession:

As employment falls, workers' incomes fall. Because income tax revenues depend on income, tax revenues fall automatically — even with no change in tax rates. Government collects less simply because there is less income to tax.

As unemployment rises, more workers become eligible for unemployment insurance, so government spending on transfers rises automatically.

As incomes fall, more households become eligible for means-tested programs like food assistance, and spending on these programs rises.

The result: during a recession, the government automatically spends more and collects less, even with no new policy decisions. This injects money into the economy at precisely the moment when private spending has contracted — helping to cushion the downturn.

The reverse happens during expansions: employment and incomes rise, so tax revenues rise automatically; fewer workers need unemployment insurance, so transfer payments fall; fewer households need means-tested benefits, so those expenditures shrink. This automatically withdraws money from the economy when growth is strong, moderating the expansion and reducing inflationary pressure.

Why Automatic Stabilizers Matter

AdvantageExplanation
SpeedDiscretionary fiscal policy can take months or years to legislate and implement. Automatic stabilizers act immediately, without any deliberation.
TimingDiscretionary policy risks being too early or too late relative to the business cycle. Automatic stabilizers respond in real time to actual economic conditions.
Political neutralityAutomatic stabilizers operate by formula, reducing the risk that politics delays or distorts the fiscal response.
Limitation

Automatic stabilizers soften recessions, but they do not eliminate them. In severe downturns — like the recessions of 2008–2009 or 2020 — they may be insufficient, and governments often supplement them with discretionary action.

Common Misconception

When economists say the deficit rose during a recession, it does not always mean lawmakers chose to spend more. Much of the rise in deficits during downturns is automatic — the result of built-in stabilizers responding to falling incomes and rising unemployment.

Section 27.5

Discretionary Fiscal Policy

While automatic stabilizers respond to economic conditions without new decisions, discretionary fiscal policy involves deliberate legislative choices: passing a new spending bill, cutting tax rates, creating a new program, or canceling an existing one.

Discretionary policy tends to be debated, designed, and implemented over months or years. It involves real political tradeoffs among competing priorities.

When Is Discretionary Policy Used?

Governments typically consider discretionary fiscal action when a recession is severe and automatic stabilizers are insufficient; when monetary policy is constrained — for example, when interest rates are already near zero and the central bank cannot cut them further; when specific infrastructure, education, or technology investments are judged to have high long-run returns; or when equity or distributional goals call for targeted changes to taxes or transfers.

The Challenge of Lags

LagDescription
Recognition lagEconomists need time to gather data and recognize that the economy has entered a recession. Economic data is often revised, and early signals can be misleading.
Decision lag (legislative lag)Even after recognizing the problem, Congress or Parliament must debate, draft, and pass legislation — taking months in normal times, longer with political disagreement.
Implementation lagOnce legislation passes, new programs take time to implement: projects must be designed and contracted; checks processed; workers hired.
Effect lagEven after money reaches households and firms, it takes additional time for spending to ripple through the economy via the multiplier.
A Serious Timing Problem

By the time a discretionary fiscal stimulus fully arrives in the economy, the recession may already be ending — meaning the stimulus arrives just as the economy is recovering, potentially amplifying an already-strengthening expansion and contributing to inflation. This timing problem is one of the most serious challenges in using discretionary fiscal policy as a countercyclical tool.

Section 27.6

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Expansionary fiscal policy refers to government actions that increase aggregate demand — either by increasing spending, cutting taxes, or increasing transfers. The goal is to raise output and employment when the economy is operating below its potential.

When Is Expansionary Policy Appropriate?

The AD-AS framework from Chapter 23 provides the analytical foundation. When the economy faces a recessionary gap — output below the full-employment level, unemployment above its natural rate — expansionary fiscal policy can shift the AD curve to the right, closing the gap.

Figure 27.1: Expansionary fiscal policy shifts AD from AD₁ to AD₂, raising output from Y₀ toward the full-employment level Y*.

Forms of Expansionary Policy

Spending increases — building infrastructure, hiring teachers, funding research, increasing defense spending, or expanding transfer programs. These increase G directly or put money into households' hands.

Tax cuts — reducing income tax rates increases households' disposable income, stimulating consumption. Cutting business taxes may stimulate investment.

Transfer increases — expanding unemployment benefits, food assistance, or income support puts money in the hands of households with high marginal propensities to consume.

The Multiplier in Expansionary Policy

Because of the multiplier effect, a well-timed fiscal expansion can have a larger impact on output than the size of the initial spending or tax change. The precise size of the multiplier is debated among economists and depends on the state of the economy (multipliers tend to be larger when there is substantial slack and smaller near full employment), whether monetary policy is accommodating, the degree of openness to trade, and whether the expansion is deficit-financed.

Example 27.6.1
Fiscal stimulus during a recession
  • SituationThe economy is in recession, with real GDP $400 billion below its potential. The government passes a $100 billion infrastructure spending package.
  • Round 1Construction firms receive $100 billion in contracts and pay workers and suppliers.
  • Round 2Recipients spend a share of their new income (MPC = 0.75), generating $75 billion in additional spending.
  • Round 3That spending becomes income for others, who spend 75% of it: $56 billion. And so on.
  • Total effectWith a multiplier of approximately 2.5, the total effect would be $250 billion in additional output — potentially closing much of the $400 billion gap (a stylized estimate that ignores many real-world complications).
Key takeaway: A fiscal expansion's total effect on output is typically larger than the initial dollar amount spent, because of the multiplier.

Section 27.7

Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Contractionary fiscal policy refers to government actions that reduce aggregate demand — by decreasing spending, raising taxes, or reducing transfers. The goal is to reduce inflationary pressure when the economy is running above its sustainable capacity.

When Is Contractionary Policy Appropriate?

When the economy is in an inflationary gap — output above the long-run sustainable level, with rising price pressures — contractionary fiscal policy can shift AD to the left, reducing inflationary pressure without relying solely on monetary tightening.

In practice, contractionary discretionary fiscal policy is used much less frequently than expansionary policy. Cutting spending or raising taxes is politically costly. Governments face pressure from many constituencies to maintain or expand programs. As a result, contractionary fiscal policy often falls primarily to central banks through monetary tightening rather than to legislatures through spending cuts or tax increases.

Automatic stabilizers, however, do provide some automatic contraction during expansions — as tax revenues rise and transfer payments fall without any new decisions.

Fiscal Consolidation

A related concept is fiscal consolidation — deliberate steps to reduce a structural budget deficit, typically through some combination of spending restraint and revenue increases. This is not primarily aimed at cooling an overheating economy but rather at improving long-run fiscal sustainability.

Fiscal consolidation involves difficult tradeoffs. If done during a recession or weak recovery, it can deepen the downturn — the contractionary effect of deficit reduction arrives at the wrong moment. If done during a strong expansion, it is more likely to succeed without significant output costs. The design matters: consolidation concentrated on spending cuts affects the economy differently than consolidation concentrated on tax increases.

Section 27.8

Deficits and Surpluses

When a government runs a deficit, it must finance the gap between spending and revenue. When it runs a surplus, it can pay down debt or accumulate savings. Understanding the relationship between deficits, surpluses, and fiscal decisions is essential for reading policy debates clearly.

Structural vs. Cyclical Deficits

Not all deficits are alike. Economists distinguish between a cyclical deficit — the portion of the deficit that arises because the economy is below full employment, which would shrink or disappear if the economy recovered — and a structural deficit — the portion that would remain even if the economy were at full employment, reflecting a fundamental imbalance between spending and revenues built into the budget.

This distinction matters enormously for policy. A government running a large cyclical deficit during a recession may not face a long-run fiscal problem — the deficit will partly heal as the economy recovers. But a government running a large structural deficit is on an unsustainable path even in good times.

The Cyclically Adjusted Balance

To see through the noise of the business cycle, economists use the cyclically adjusted budget balance — an estimate of what the deficit or surplus would be if the economy were at full employment. This provides a cleaner picture of the underlying fiscal stance.

MeasureWhat It Shows
Actual deficitRaw gap between revenue and spending in a given year
Cyclical deficitPortion due to economic slack (automatic stabilizers)
Structural deficitUnderlying gap that would persist at full employment
Cyclically adjusted balanceEstimate of balance at full employment

Can Deficits Ever Be Good?

This is one of the most debated questions in macroeconomics. The answer is nuanced.

Deficits Can Be Appropriate When

The economy is in a deep recession and private demand has collapsed; the government is investing in high-return capital — infrastructure, education, research — that raises future productive capacity, especially if returns exceed the interest cost of debt; or borrowing costs are very low and inflation is subdued.

Deficits Are Problematic When

They are structural and growing, indicating an unsustainable fiscal path; they crowd out private investment by driving up interest rates; they finance current consumption rather than productive investment; or they accumulate to levels where debt service consumes a large share of the budget.

Section 27.9

Public Debt

Public debt (also called government debt or national debt) is the total stock of outstanding debt owed by a government. It accumulates as deficits pile up over time: each year the government runs a deficit, it borrows to cover the gap, and that borrowing adds to the total debt.

Debt = Previous debt + Current deficit

Or equivalently: debt falls when the government runs surpluses large enough to pay down the outstanding balance.

Measuring Debt

Raw debt levels can be misleading on their own. A $1 trillion debt means something very different for a small country than for a large one. Economists typically express debt as a percentage of GDP — the debt-to-GDP ratio — which puts the debt in context relative to the economy's capacity to generate income and tax revenue.

Debt-to-GDP ratio = (Total public debt ÷ GDP) × 100

A rising debt-to-GDP ratio over time suggests the debt is growing faster than the economy — potentially unsustainable. A stable or falling ratio, even with continued deficits, suggests the economy is growing fast enough to manage the debt.

Who Holds Government Debt?

Government debt is held by domestic holders (banks, pension funds, insurance companies, individual investors), foreign holders (foreign governments, central banks, and investors), and in some circumstances by the central bank itself as part of its monetary operations.

Common Misconception

A common misconception is that all public debt is owed to foreign countries. In most major economies, a significant share of government debt is held domestically. This matters for policy: debt owed to domestic residents involves redistributive effects (taxpayers pay interest to domestic bondholders) rather than a net transfer of resources abroad.

Debt Sustainability

Debt sustainability refers to whether a government's debt path can continue without requiring drastic future policy changes.

The primary balance — the budget balance before interest payments — is a key measure. If the primary balance is in surplus, the government is actively paying down the debt, assuming interest payments are not too large. If the primary balance is in deficit, the debt may be growing even if the overall deficit looks small.

For debt to be sustainable, roughly speaking, the real interest rate on debt must not persistently exceed the real growth rate of the economy. If r > g (the interest rate exceeds the growth rate), a government must run primary surpluses to stabilize the debt ratio — otherwise debt grows faster than GDP indefinitely. If r < g, the economy grows faster than interest costs, and a government can sustain modest primary deficits without an ever-rising debt ratio.

Section 27.10

Fiscal Policy Tradeoffs and Long-Run Sustainability

Fiscal policy involves real tradeoffs. There is rarely a free lunch. Understanding those tradeoffs clearly is part of becoming an economically literate citizen.

Tradeoff 1: Stabilization vs. Sustainability

Short-run fiscal stimulus can prevent real economic damage — job losses, business failures, skills erosion, and long-term scarring effects on workers. But sustained deficits add to public debt, which must eventually be financed by higher taxes or lower spending. There is no single right answer to how much fiscal stimulus is appropriate; it depends on the severity of the recession, the interest rate environment, the starting level of debt, the quality of the spending, and the expected recovery path.

Tradeoff 2: Spending Today vs. Taxes Tomorrow

Deficit spending today essentially means shifting the tax burden into the future. Future taxpayers must service or repay the debt through higher taxes, reduced public services, or both. This raises questions of intergenerational equity: is it fair for the current generation to incur debt that future generations must service? The answer depends partly on what the debt finances — long-lived public investments benefit future generations, while debt-financed current consumption does not.

Tradeoff 3: Crowding Out

When the government borrows, it competes with private borrowers for available funds in financial markets. This can drive up interest rates, making it more expensive for businesses to borrow for investment and for households to borrow for homes, education, or durable goods. The resulting reduction in private investment is called crowding out.

The magnitude of crowding out depends on how much slack exists in financial markets, whether monetary policy accommodates the fiscal expansion, and whether the economy is open to international capital flows. In severe recessions — especially when interest rates are near zero — crowding out is much weaker. At full employment, crowding out is larger and limits the effectiveness of fiscal stimulus.

Tradeoff 4: Efficiency vs. Equity

Tax and spending choices involve efficiency and equity tradeoffs that cannot be resolved by economic analysis alone — they involve value judgments. Progressive income taxes reduce inequality but may affect incentives to work, save, and invest. Regressive consumption taxes are sometimes argued to be more efficient but raise equity concerns. Transfer programs reduce poverty but may affect work incentives at the margin. Economists can analyze the magnitudes and directions of effects, but the weights placed on efficiency versus equity reflect social values, not only economic efficiency calculations.

Tradeoff 5: Aggregate Demand vs. Supply-Side Effects

Most fiscal analysis focuses on demand-side effects: how fiscal policy shifts aggregate demand. But fiscal policy also affects the supply side of the economy. Tax policy affects incentives to work, invest, start businesses, and take risks. Spending on education, infrastructure, and research raises productivity and shifts aggregate supply to the right over time. The full evaluation of a fiscal policy requires considering both its demand-side effects (short run) and its supply-side effects (long run).

  • Sec 27.1Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Government purchases enter aggregate demand directly; taxes and transfers affect it indirectly through household and business spending. Fiscal policy can be automatic or discretionary.
  • Sec 27.2A government budget shows expected revenues (mainly taxes) and expenditures (purchases, transfers, interest, and grants). The budget balance — revenues minus expenditures — determines whether the government runs a surplus, deficit, or balanced budget.
  • Sec 27.3Taxes, government purchases, and transfers each affect aggregate demand differently. The marginal propensity to consume determines how much of an income change becomes spending, and the multiplier effect amplifies the total impact on GDP.
  • Sec 27.4Automatic stabilizers — falling tax revenue and rising transfer spending during recessions, and the reverse during expansions — cushion the business cycle quickly, predictably, and without new legislation.
  • Sec 27.5Discretionary fiscal policy requires deliberate legislative action and is slowed by recognition, decision, implementation, and effect lags, which can cause stimulus to arrive at the wrong point in the business cycle.
  • Sec 27.6Expansionary fiscal policy — higher spending, tax cuts, or increased transfers — shifts AD rightward and is appropriate during a recessionary gap. The multiplier effect can magnify its impact on output.
  • Sec 27.7Contractionary fiscal policy shifts AD leftward and is appropriate during an inflationary gap, but is politically difficult to enact. Fiscal consolidation targets structural deficits and works best during strong expansions.
  • Sec 27.8Deficits can be cyclical (tied to economic slack) or structural (persisting at full employment). The cyclically adjusted balance isolates the underlying fiscal stance. Deficits can be appropriate or problematic depending on what they finance and the broader economic context.
  • Sec 27.9Public debt is the accumulated stock of past deficits, typically measured as a share of GDP. Debt sustainability depends on the relationship between interest rates, growth rates, and the primary balance — not on the debt level alone.
  • Sec 27.10Fiscal policy involves tradeoffs between stabilization and sustainability, present spending and future taxes, crowding out of private investment, efficiency and equity, and demand-side versus supply-side effects. None of these tradeoffs has one automatic correct answer.

Key Terms Review

fiscal policy
Government decisions about spending and taxation used to influence macroeconomic conditions.
government budget
A plan showing expected revenues and expenditures for a given period, usually one year.
budget surplus
The condition in which government revenues exceed expenditures.
budget deficit
The condition in which government expenditures exceed revenues.
balanced budget
The condition in which revenues and expenditures are equal.
transfer payments
Government payments to individuals (Social Security, unemployment insurance, food assistance) not in exchange for goods or services.
automatic stabilizers
Features of the budget that automatically increase deficits during recessions and reduce them during expansions, without new legislative action.
discretionary fiscal policy
Deliberate legislative decisions to change taxes, spending, or transfers to influence economic conditions.
expansionary fiscal policy
Fiscal actions that increase aggregate demand — higher spending, tax cuts, or increased transfers.
contractionary fiscal policy
Fiscal actions that reduce aggregate demand — lower spending, tax increases, or reduced transfers.
recognition lag
The time it takes to identify that an economic problem has developed.
decision lag
The time required to debate and pass new fiscal legislation; also called the legislative lag.
implementation lag
The time between passing legislation and actually delivering spending or tax changes to the economy.
multiplier effect
The process by which an initial change in spending leads to a larger total change in GDP as income circulates through the economy.
marginal propensity to consume
The fraction of an additional dollar of income that a household spends rather than saves.
cyclical deficit
The portion of a budget deficit caused by the economy operating below full employment.
structural deficit
The portion of a budget deficit that would persist even if the economy were at full employment.
cyclically adjusted balance
An estimate of what the budget balance would be if the economy were at full employment.
public debt
The total stock of outstanding debt owed by a government; the accumulated sum of past deficits minus past surpluses.
debt-to-GDP ratio
Public debt expressed as a percentage of GDP; used to compare debt levels across countries and over time.
primary balance
The budget balance before interest payments on outstanding debt.
crowding out
The reduction in private investment that can occur when government borrowing drives up interest rates.

Graph Review

Figure 27.1
Expansionary Fiscal Policy and the AD-AS Model

An expansionary fiscal policy — such as an increase in government spending or a tax cut — shifts the aggregate demand curve to the right (from AD₁ to AD₂). Starting from a recessionary gap (actual output Y₀ below potential Y*), the rightward shift of AD raises real output toward Y* and reduces the unemployment gap. In the short run, the price level also rises somewhat.

Reading the graph: the shift in AD moves the equilibrium along the SRAS curve, raising both output and the price level. If the economy were already at Y*, the same shift would produce more inflation and less real output gain — illustrating why timing matters.
Figure 27.2
Automatic Stabilizers and the Business Cycle

This conceptual diagram shows the budget balance over a business cycle. During recessions, tax revenues fall and transfer payments rise automatically, producing larger deficits. During expansions, tax revenues rise and transfers fall, reducing the deficit (or producing surpluses). The automatic stabilizers narrow the swings in output compared to what would occur without them.

Budget balance = Revenues − Expenditures = $1,300 − $1,650 = −$350 billion. The government is running a deficit of $350 billion this year, which will be added to the outstanding public debt.
Worked Example 2
Distinguishing cyclical from structural deficits

Suppose economists estimate that at full employment, income tax revenues would be $200 billion higher than their current depressed level, and transfer payments would be $80 billion lower. The current actual deficit is $350 billion (from Worked Example 1).

Estimated cyclical deficit = $200B + $80B = $280 billion. Estimated structural deficit = $350B − $280B = $70 billion. Interpretation: most of this deficit is cyclical and would shrink as the economy recovers, but a $70 billion structural deficit reflects underlying spending exceeding revenues even at full employment.
Worked Example 3
Simple multiplier reasoning

The government increases infrastructure spending by $50 billion. The MPC is estimated at 0.8.

Simple multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ 0.2 = 5. Estimated total increase in GDP = $50 billion × 5 = $250 billion. Caution: real-world multipliers are typically smaller, ranging from about 0.5 to 2.0, because of taxes, imports, and other leakages.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 28: Economic Growth, Productivity, and Living Standards

Chapter 27 has examined how governments use fiscal policy to stabilize the economy in the short run and why long-run fiscal sustainability matters. But there is a deeper question beneath all of this: what determines how fast an economy grows over the long run? Chapter 28 examines economic growth, productivity, and living standards. You will study why some economies have grown rich over generations while others have not, what drives productivity improvements, how physical capital, human capital, technology, and institutions shape long-run output, and why even small differences in growth rates compound dramatically over decades. Long-run growth is the most powerful force in economics for improving human living standards. Understanding it — and the policies that support or impede it — is essential for evaluating both fiscal choices and broader economic policy.

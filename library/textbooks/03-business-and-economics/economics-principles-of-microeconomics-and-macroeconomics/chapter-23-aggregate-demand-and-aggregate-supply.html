Chapter 23 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

In the earlier parts of this textbook, you studied individual markets — the market for apples, the market for labor in a city, the market for apartments in a town. Supply and demand worked at the level of specific goods and specific prices. Macroeconomics asks a different question. Instead of asking why the price of apples rose this year, macroeconomics asks: why did prices across the entire economy rise? Instead of asking why employment fell at one firm, macroeconomics asks: why did employment fall for millions of workers at once?

To answer these bigger questions, economists use a different set of tools. The most important of these tools is the aggregate demand and aggregate supply model — often written as the AD-AS model. It is the macroeconomic equivalent of the supply and demand graph you learned in Part II. Instead of showing one price and one quantity, it shows the overall price level of the economy and the economy's total output.

This chapter introduces the AD-AS model carefully and step by step. You will learn what aggregate demand is, what determines it, what aggregate supply is in both the short run and the long run, how the economy reaches equilibrium, and how shocks — sudden changes — push the economy away from equilibrium and create recessions, inflation, or both.

Opening Question

Why do recessions happen? Why does inflation sometimes rise sharply? What can governments and central banks do when the economy runs into serious trouble, and what tradeoffs are involved in those decisions? By the end of this chapter, you will have a structured model for thinking through each of these questions.

Section 23.1

Why Economists Use Aggregate Models

When you studied supply and demand in Chapter 6 through Chapter 8, you focused on one market at a time. The market for coffee. The market for construction workers. The market for used cars. Each market has its own supply curve, its own demand curve, and its own equilibrium price and quantity.

This approach works well when you want to understand why the price of gasoline rises after a hurricane. But it runs into a problem when you want to understand the whole economy at once. The whole economy includes millions of different markets and trillions of dollars of output each year. You cannot draw a separate supply and demand curve for every market and add them all up.

Economists solve this problem by aggregating — combining everything together into a single, simplified picture. Instead of tracking the price of each individual good, macroeconomists track the overall price level — an average of prices across the economy. Instead of tracking the output of each firm, they track real GDP — total output adjusted for inflation.

What the AD-AS Model Shows

The aggregate demand and aggregate supply model places the overall price level on the vertical axis and real GDP on the horizontal axis. It shows how much total output buyers in the whole economy want and producers in the whole economy are willing to supply at different price levels — the macroeconomic counterpart of the single-market supply and demand graph.

One of the most important ideas in the model is that the economy behaves differently in the short run than in the long run. In the short run, many prices and wages are slow to adjust — contracts lock in wages, menu prices are not changed every day. This stickiness means the economy can produce more or less than its normal capacity for a period of time. In the long run, prices and wages adjust fully, and the economy tends to return to its full-capacity level of output.

Section 23.2

Aggregate Demand

Aggregate demand (AD) is the total quantity of goods and services that buyers throughout the entire economy want to purchase at each possible overall price level, holding everything else constant. Aggregate demand includes purchases by households, businesses, governments, and foreign buyers — it is the sum of all spending in the economy.

The AD curve slopes downward: when the price level is lower, the quantity of real GDP demanded is higher, and vice versa. There are three specific reasons for this downward slope.

EffectMechanismResult When Price Level Falls
Wealth effectA lower price level raises the real purchasing power of money holdings and savingsHouseholds feel wealthier and spend more
Interest rate effectA lower price level reduces the money needed for transactions, pushing interest rates downBorrowing is cheaper; investment and credit purchases rise
Net exports effectA lower domestic price level makes domestic goods cheaper relative to foreign goodsExports rise, imports fall, net exports increase
Figure 23.1 — Aggregate Demand Curve

The AD curve slopes downward. At a higher price level (P₁), the quantity of real GDP demanded is lower (Y₁). At a lower price level (P₂), the quantity of real GDP demanded is higher (Y₂). The three reasons for this relationship are the wealth effect, the interest rate effect, and the net exports effect.

Common Misconception

These three effects are related to, but distinct from, the reasons an individual demand curve slopes downward in a single market, which rest on substitution effects and diminishing marginal utility. Do not explain the AD curve's slope using single-market reasoning.

Section 23.3

Components of Aggregate Demand

Aggregate demand is built from the same components you studied in the GDP expenditure approach in Chapter 20: Real GDP = C + I + G + NX, where C is consumption, I is investment, G is government purchases, and NX is net exports.

It is important to distinguish a movement along the AD curve (caused by a change in the price level) from a shift of the AD curve (caused by something other than the price level that changes total spending at every price level).

FactorChangeEffect on AD
Consumer confidence risesMore spending at every price levelAD shifts right
Consumer confidence fallsLess spending at every price levelAD shifts left
Business investment risesMore spending at every price levelAD shifts right
Government purchases increaseMore spending at every price levelAD shifts right
Taxes decreaseMore disposable income, more spendingAD shifts right
Interest rates fallMore borrowing and spendingAD shifts right
Interest rates riseLess borrowing and spendingAD shifts left
Foreign incomes riseMore exportsAD shifts right
Domestic currency depreciatesExports become cheaper for foreignersAD shifts right
Expectations Can Be Self-Fulfilling

If households and businesses expect a recession, they may cut spending preemptively, shifting AD left. Their collective action can help cause the very recession they feared — a reminder that expectations are not just a forecast of the economy but an active force shaping it.

Section 23.4

Short-Run Aggregate Supply

Aggregate supply (AS) is the total quantity of goods and services that producers throughout the economy are willing and able to supply at each possible overall price level. There are two versions: the short-run aggregate supply (SRAS) curve and the long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve, and they behave very differently.

The SRAS curve slopes upward: a higher price level is associated with more real output supplied. The key reason is sticky wages and prices. When the price level rises but wages haven't yet adjusted, a firm's revenue rises while its labor costs stay flat, improving profit margins and encouraging more production. When the price level falls but wages stay relatively high, profit margins shrink and firms cut back.

FactorChangeEffect on SRAS
Wages riseHigher production costsSRAS shifts left
Wages fallLower production costsSRAS shifts right
Oil or energy prices riseHigher production costsSRAS shifts left
Technology improvesHigher productivity, lower costsSRAS shifts right
Imported input prices riseHigher production costsSRAS shifts left
Business taxes riseHigher costsSRAS shifts left
Business subsidies increaseLower costsSRAS shifts right
Common Misconception

A higher price level causes producers to move along the SRAS curve, not to shift it. The SRAS curve shifts only when input prices, technology, or policy change production costs at every price level.

Section 23.5

Long-Run Aggregate Supply at an Introductory Level

Wage and price stickiness is temporary. Over a longer period, wages and other input prices adjust to reflect the actual state of the economy. When all prices have fully adjusted, the economy reaches its potential output — also called full-employment output or natural output.

The long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve is a vertical line at potential output. This means that in the long run, the quantity of real GDP supplied does not depend on the price level — the economy produces at its potential regardless of whether prices are high or low, because wages have fully adjusted.

Short Run vs. Long Run

Short run: producers supply more at a higher price level, less at a lower price level — because wages have not fully adjusted.

Long run: producers supply the same amount (potential output) regardless of the price level — because wages have fully adjusted.

The position of the LRAS curve is determined by the economy's real productive capacity. It shifts right when productive capacity grows — through growth in the labor force, accumulation of physical capital, human capital investment, technological progress, or improvements in institutions. These are the same factors behind long-run economic growth, studied in full in Chapter 28.

Figure 23.2 — Long-Run Aggregate Supply Curve

The LRAS curve is a vertical line at potential output (Y*). The price level can be high or low, but the economy's potential output does not change. The LRAS shifts right when productive capacity grows — through more labor, more capital, better technology, or better institutions.

Section 23.6

Macroeconomic Equilibrium

Macroeconomic equilibrium in the short run occurs where the AD curve and the SRAS curve intersect. At this point, the price level and real GDP are at the levels where total quantity demanded equals total quantity supplied in the short run.

Equilibrium does not necessarily mean the economy is at full employment. The short-run equilibrium may be below potential output (a recession), at potential output (a healthy economy), or above potential output (an overheating economy, which cannot persist for long).

CasePosition Relative to LRASDescription
At potential outputAD and SRAS intersect exactly at LRASFull employment; stable prices; "long-run equilibrium"
Below potential outputIntersection to the left of LRASRecessionary gap; unemployment above normal
Above potential outputIntersection to the right of LRASInflationary gap; unemployment below normal; rising wage and price pressure
Figure 23.3 — Macroeconomic Equilibrium

All three curves — AD, SRAS, and LRAS — are shown together. Point E₁ shows equilibrium at potential output (long-run equilibrium). Point E₂ shows equilibrium below potential output (recessionary gap). Point E₃ shows equilibrium above potential output (inflationary gap).

Section 23.7

Demand Shocks

A demand shock is a sudden, unexpected shift of the AD curve. Demand shocks can be negative (AD shifts left) or positive (AD shifts right).

A negative demand shock — caused by events such as a financial crisis, a collapse in confidence, sharp interest rate increases, or a major trading partner's recession — shifts AD left. The new equilibrium shows a lower price level and lower real GDP: a recession.

A positive demand shock — caused by events such as a confidence surge, an investment boom, increased government spending, or falling interest rates — shifts AD right. The new equilibrium shows a higher price level and higher real GDP. If the new equilibrium exceeds potential output, an inflationary gap opens.

Figure 23.4 — Negative Demand Shock

The AD curve shifts left from AD₁ to AD₂. The new equilibrium (E₂) shows lower real GDP (Y₂) and a lower price level (P₂) compared to the original equilibrium (E₁). The gap between Y₂ and potential output Y* is a recessionary gap.

Worked Example 23.1
Diagnosing a Demand Shock
  • ScenarioA large stock market crash destroys household wealth. Consumer confidence falls sharply, and households cut spending considerably.
  • Identify shockThis is a negative demand shock — households are spending less because of a wealth and confidence effect.
  • ShiftThe AD curve shifts left.
  • New equilibriumThe new intersection of AD (shifted left) and SRAS (unchanged) shows a lower price level and lower real GDP.
  • GapReal GDP falls below potential output: a recessionary gap opens.
  • Self-correctionOver time, high unemployment puts downward pressure on wages. As wages fall, SRAS shifts right, and the economy gradually returns to Y* at a lower price level.
Key takeaway: A negative demand shock lowers both output and the price level, distinguishing it from a supply shock.

Section 23.8

Supply Shocks

A supply shock is a sudden, unexpected shift of the SRAS curve. Unlike demand shocks, supply shocks move the price level and real GDP in opposite directions, creating distinctive policy dilemmas.

A negative supply shock — caused by a sharp rise in oil prices, a drought, a pandemic, a natural disaster, or a sudden wage spike — shifts SRAS left. The new equilibrium shows a higher price level and lower real GDP: a combination called stagflation.

A positive supply shock — caused by falling oil prices, a productivity breakthrough, workforce growth, or a bountiful harvest — shifts SRAS right. The new equilibrium shows a lower price level and higher real GDP: more growth with less inflation.

Figure 23.5 — Negative Supply Shock

The SRAS curve shifts left from SRAS₁ to SRAS₂. The new equilibrium (E₂) shows lower real GDP (Y₂) — a recessionary gap — and a higher price level (P₂). This combination of lower output and higher prices is called stagflation.

Worked Example 23.2
Diagnosing a Supply Shock
  • ScenarioThe price of oil triples in one year due to a major global oil market disruption. Oil is a key input across manufacturing, transportation, and agriculture.
  • Identify shockThis is a negative supply shock — production costs rise across the economy due to higher energy prices.
  • ShiftThe SRAS curve shifts left.
  • New equilibriumThe new intersection of AD (unchanged) and SRAS (shifted left) shows a higher price level and lower real GDP.
  • OutcomeReal GDP falls below potential output (a recessionary gap), while the price level rises — stagflation.
  • DilemmaStimulating AD to fight the recession would push prices higher; contracting AD to fight inflation would worsen the recession. Neither option is clearly better.
Key takeaway: Negative supply shocks create the genuine policy dilemma of stagflation.
Type of ShockPrice LevelReal GDP
Negative demand shockFalls (or rises more slowly)Falls
Positive demand shockRisesRises
Negative supply shockRisesFalls (stagflation)
Positive supply shockFallsRises
A Diagnostic Shortcut

If output and prices move in the same direction, suspect a demand shock. If output and prices move in opposite directions, suspect a supply shock. The 1970s oil shocks and the late-1990s productivity boom are the textbook examples of each pattern.

Section 23.9

Recessionary and Inflationary Gaps

A recessionary gap exists when short-run equilibrium real GDP is below potential output — the AD and SRAS curves intersect to the left of LRAS. Unemployment is above normal, and resources are underutilized. Over time, high unemployment creates downward pressure on wages, shifting SRAS right until the economy returns to potential output at a lower price level.

An inflationary gap exists when short-run equilibrium real GDP is above potential output — the intersection occurs to the right of LRAS. Unemployment is below normal and wages are under upward pressure. As tight labor markets push wages up, SRAS shifts left until the economy returns to potential output, but at a higher price level.

Figure 23.6 — Recessionary Gap and Inflationary Gap

Left panel: Recessionary gap. AD and SRAS intersect at E₁, to the left of LRAS. Actual output Y₁ is below potential output Y*. Unemployment is above normal. Right panel: Inflationary gap. AD and SRAS intersect at E₂, to the right of LRAS. Actual output Y₂ is above potential output Y*. Unemployment is below normal and wages are rising.

Common Misconception

A recessionary gap does not mean the economy will never recover. The economy has a self-correction mechanism: wages eventually fall, SRAS shifts right, and output returns to potential. The real debate is how long this takes and whether policy should accelerate it.

Section 23.10

Adjustment and Policy Questions

The economy's self-correction mechanism is real, but economists disagree about how fast it works and how much suffering occurs in the meantime. This is the central question of stabilization policy: can fiscal policy (Chapter 27) and monetary policy (Chapter 26) accelerate adjustment by shifting aggregate demand?

For a recessionary gap: expansionary fiscal or monetary policy shifts AD right, closing the gap faster. Risks include overshooting into an inflationary gap, timing problems, and debt accumulation.

For an inflationary gap: contractionary fiscal or monetary policy shifts AD left, closing the gap and preventing accelerating inflation. The cost is reduced growth and higher unemployment in the short run.

For a negative supply shock: there is no clean policy solution. Fighting the recession with expansionary policy worsens inflation; fighting inflation with contractionary policy worsens the recession; waiting for self-correction can take years. This is the central policy dilemma of stagflation.

The Fundamental Macroeconomic Tradeoff

Policy choices generally trade off between stabilizing inflation and stabilizing output and employment — rarely achieving both perfectly at the same time.

What the Model Can and Cannot Tell You

The AD-AS model helps you identify the type of shock, predict the direction of changes in prices and output, and understand why policy involves tradeoffs. It cannot tell you the exact size of changes or precisely how long adjustment will take — humility about these limits is part of using the model well.

Key Terms Review

aggregate demand (AD)
The total quantity of real goods and services demanded across the entire economy at each possible overall price level.
aggregate supply (AS)
The total quantity of real goods and services producers in the entire economy are willing and able to supply at each price level.
short-run aggregate supply (SRAS)
The aggregate supply relationship when input prices, especially wages, are sticky and have not fully adjusted.
long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)
The economy's potential output, shown as a vertical line, reflecting that long-run real GDP is determined by productive capacity, not the price level.
potential output
The level of real GDP the economy can produce when resources are used at normal, sustainable rates; also called full-employment output.
macroeconomic equilibrium
The point where aggregate demand and short-run aggregate supply intersect, determining the price level and real GDP in the short run.
recessionary gap
A situation where short-run equilibrium real GDP is below potential output; unemployment is above its normal level.
inflationary gap
A situation where short-run equilibrium real GDP is above potential output; unemployment is below its normal level.
demand shock
A sudden, unexpected shift of the aggregate demand curve caused by changes in confidence, investment, government spending, taxes, or interest rates.
supply shock
A sudden, unexpected shift of the short-run aggregate supply curve caused by changes in input prices, technology, or production capacity.
stagflation
The simultaneous occurrence of stagnating or declining output and rising inflation, typically caused by a severe negative supply shock.
wealth effect
The mechanism by which a lower price level increases real purchasing power, encouraging spending and contributing to AD's downward slope.
interest rate effect
The mechanism by which a lower price level tends to lower interest rates, encouraging borrowing and spending.
net exports effect
The mechanism by which a lower domestic price level makes domestic goods relatively cheaper, raising exports and lowering imports.
sticky wages
The tendency for wages to adjust slowly, a key reason SRAS slopes upward and self-correction can be slow.
self-correction mechanism
The process by which an economy eventually returns to potential output through wage and price adjustment, without policy intervention.

Graph Review

The standard AD-AS diagram has the overall price level (P) on the vertical axis and real GDP (Y) on the horizontal axis. The AD curve slopes downward; the SRAS curve slopes upward; the LRAS line is vertical at potential output (Y*).

  • To find short-run equilibrium: locate the intersection of AD and SRAS.
  • To determine a gap: compare equilibrium Y to Y*. Below Y* is a recessionary gap; above Y* is an inflationary gap.
  • To show a demand shock: shift the AD curve right (positive) or left (negative).
  • To show a supply shock: shift the SRAS curve right (positive) or left (negative).

Case Study

Case Study 23.1
The 1970s Oil Shocks and Stagflation
  • BackgroundOil prices quadrupled in 1973–1974 following an OPEC embargo, then rose sharply again in 1979–1980.
  • MechanismHigher energy costs raised production costs across manufacturing, transportation, and heating, shifting SRAS sharply to the left.
  • OutcomeThe United States and many oil-importing countries experienced high unemployment and high inflation simultaneously — stagflation — which contradicted earlier economic thinking that assumed these problems could not occur together.
  • Policy bindStimulating demand to fight unemployment risked worsening inflation; tightening policy to fight inflation risked worsening unemployment.
Discussion: How does this case illustrate why negative supply shocks are harder to manage than demand shocks?

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1

"Higher prices cause aggregate supply to shift." Correction: a higher price level causes a movement along the SRAS curve, not a shift. SRAS shifts only when input prices, technology, or policy change production costs at every price level.

Misconception 2

"The AD-AS model is the same as regular supply and demand." Correction: the "price" in AD-AS is the overall price level across the entire economy, and the reasons for the curves' slopes and shifts are distinct from single-market reasoning.

Misconception 3

"A recessionary gap means the economy will never recover." Correction: the economy has a self-correction mechanism. The real debate is how long recovery takes and whether policy should accelerate it.

Misconception 4

"Stagflation cannot happen because inflation and recession are opposites." Correction: negative supply shocks produce both higher prices and lower output simultaneously — a well-documented and explainable phenomenon.

Misconception 5

"Policy can always close gaps quickly and precisely." Correction: policy faces information delays, implementation delays, and impact delays, which can make timing very difficult in practice.

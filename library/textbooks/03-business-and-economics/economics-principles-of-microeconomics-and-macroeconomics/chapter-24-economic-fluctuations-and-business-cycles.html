Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Economies do not grow in a straight line. Real GDP rises over long periods, but along the way it rises fast, slows down, contracts, and recovers. Workers find jobs during some years and lose them during others. Firms expand in good times and lay off workers or close in bad times. Families feel the difference between a year when wages are rising and jobs are easy to find and a year when unemployment is high and hours are being cut.

These recurring patterns of expansion and contraction are called business cycles. They are one of the most important features of real-world macroeconomics, and understanding them is essential for interpreting economic news, evaluating policy, and making decisions as a worker, voter, or business owner.

This chapter explains what business cycles are, how economists measure and describe them, what causes them, and how governments and central banks attempt to respond to them. It connects the measurement tools from Chapters 20, 21, and 22 and the aggregate demand and aggregate supply framework from Chapter 23 to the lived reality of economic fluctuations.

Opening Question

Think about a time you heard adults talk about "the economy" being good or bad. What were they describing — rising prices, job losses, a booming stock market, a slowdown at a parent's workplace? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to describe exactly which phase of the business cycle they were experiencing, what likely caused it, and what tools policymakers have to respond.

Section 24.1

What Business Cycles Are

A business cycle is a recurring but irregular pattern of expansion and contraction in overall economic activity. The word "cycle" suggests something that repeats, and it does — but not with the regularity of the seasons or the ticking of a clock. Business cycles vary in length, depth, and cause. Some expansions last two years. Others last more than a decade. Some recessions are mild. Others are severe, with unemployment rising sharply and output falling for many consecutive quarters.

Economists measure business cycles primarily through real GDP — total output adjusted for inflation. When real GDP is rising consistently, the economy is in an expansion. When real GDP falls for a significant period, the economy is in a recession. These phases, their turning points, and the indicators that track them make up the study of business cycle analysis.

Why Business Cycles Matter

Business cycles affect nearly every dimension of economic life. Employment, income, poverty rates, firm profits, government budgets, and consumer confidence all rise and fall with the cycle. Policymakers try to smooth these fluctuations using monetary policy and fiscal policy. Understanding the cycle helps citizens evaluate those policies honestly.

Key Point

Business cycles are fluctuations around a long-run trend. The long-run trend in real GDP is generally upward — economies grow over decades. Business cycles are the short-run and medium-run movements up and down around that trend.

Section 24.2

Expansions

An expansion is a period of rising real GDP, falling unemployment, rising employment, growing business profits, and increasing consumer spending. Expansions are sometimes called booms, though economists prefer the neutral term "expansion" to describe the normal upward phase of the cycle.

Expansions do not last forever. As an economy approaches full capacity — when most workers are employed and most capital is in use — upward pressure on wages and prices can build. At some point, the expansion slows, peaks, and either moves into a mild slowdown or a contraction.

FeatureWhat Happens During an Expansion
Real GDPRises quarter after quarter
UnemploymentFalls as more workers find jobs
Business investmentIncreases as firms gain confidence about future demand
Consumer confidenceRises, and households spend more
Tax revenuesRise as incomes grow
Government deficitsOften shrink automatically as unemployment insurance spending falls and revenues rise
Longest Expansions on Record (United States)

The expansion from June 2009 to February 2020 was the longest on record in American history at the time, lasting approximately 128 months. The expansion of the 1990s lasted from March 1991 to March 2001, about 10 years.

Section 24.3

Peaks

A peak is the high point of a business cycle — the moment when real GDP is at its highest level before the economy begins to contract. A peak is only identified in hindsight because it is impossible to know at the time whether the economy is at a turning point or merely experiencing a temporary slowdown.

At the peak, real GDP is at its highest level in the current cycle, unemployment is typically near its lowest level, business activity and investment are near their highest, and inflation may be rising because the economy is running at or near full capacity. After the peak, the economy transitions into contraction — gradually or suddenly, depending on what caused the slowdown.

Note
The National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER)

In the United States, the NBER Business Cycle Dating Committee is the official body that identifies the dates of peaks and troughs in the business cycle. They use multiple measures — including real GDP, real income, employment, and industrial production — to date turning points, sometimes with a delay of many months after the event.

Section 24.4

Recessions

A recession is a period of declining real GDP. A common informal definition used in journalism is two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth. The NBER's official definition is more nuanced: a significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy and lasts more than a few months, reflected in real GDP, real income, employment, industrial production, and consumer spending.

Recessions cause significant harm. Unemployment causes lost income, stress, health problems, and long-term damage to workers' earnings potential. Businesses that fail during recessions may not reopen. Skills erode when workers are unemployed for long periods. Young workers who enter the labor market during recessions often face lasting wage disadvantages even after recovery.

FeatureWhat Happens During a Recession
Real GDPFalls
Unemployment rateRises as firms lay off workers or stop hiring
Business investmentFalls as firms become uncertain about future demand
Consumer spendingDeclines as income falls and confidence weakens
Tax revenuesFall automatically as incomes and profits decline
Government deficitsTend to increase automatically as transfer payments rise and revenues fall
Severity Varies

Not all recessions are equally severe. A mild recession might see unemployment rise by 1 or 2 percentage points and real GDP fall by 1 percent. A severe recession — sometimes called a depression if it is deep and prolonged enough — can see unemployment rise by 10 or more percentage points and real GDP fall significantly. The Great Depression of the 1930s remains the most severe economic contraction in American history.

Section 24.5

Troughs and Recoveries

A trough is the low point of a business cycle — the moment when real GDP is at its lowest before the economy begins to recover. Like the peak, the trough is identified in hindsight. After the trough, the economy enters a recovery — a period in which real GDP begins to rise again from its recessionary low point.

Recoveries vary in strength. A strong recovery features rapid GDP growth, fast job creation, and quickly declining unemployment. A weak recovery or jobless recovery features positive GDP growth but slow job creation, leaving unemployment elevated for years after the trough.

Okun's Law (Introductory Version)

There is a general relationship between changes in real GDP and changes in the unemployment rate. When real GDP grows faster than its long-run trend, unemployment tends to fall. When real GDP grows more slowly or falls, unemployment tends to rise. This relationship is sometimes called Okun's Law after economist Arthur Okun. It is an empirical pattern, not an exact formula, and the relationship varies across countries and time periods.

Recovery and Recession Are Asymmetric in Time

Recoveries typically take longer than the recessions that precede them. A severe recession that causes unemployment to rise sharply may require many years of expansion before unemployment returns to its pre-recession level.

Section 24.6

Economic Indicators

Economists, investors, policymakers, and businesses use economic indicators to track where the economy is in the business cycle and to anticipate future changes. Economic indicators are statistics that measure economic activity and provide information about economic conditions. There are three categories of economic indicators.

Leading Indicators

Leading indicators change before the economy as a whole changes. They tend to rise before an expansion and fall before a recession. Because they move ahead of the cycle, they are used to forecast future economic conditions.

CategoryExamples
Leading indicatorsNew orders for manufactured goods, building permits, stock market prices, consumer confidence surveys, average weekly manufacturing hours, initial unemployment insurance claims
Coincident indicatorsReal GDP, total nonfarm employment, personal income, industrial production
Lagging indicatorsUnemployment rate, average duration of unemployment, outstanding loans, interest rates on business loans
Important Caution

Leading indicators are imperfect. They generate false signals. Stock prices, for example, are a leading indicator but have predicted many recessions that did not occur. The famous observation attributed to economist Paul Samuelson is that the stock market has "predicted nine of the last five recessions." No single leading indicator is reliably accurate alone.

Why Lagging Indicators Still Matter

Even though they lag the cycle, lagging indicators confirm turning points and can signal whether a recovery is durable or fragile. Rising unemployment long after a trough can signal a weak recovery.

Because no single indicator is reliable alone, economists often combine multiple leading indicators into a composite index such as the Conference Board's Leading Economic Index (LEI) in the United States. Composite indexes smooth out the noise of individual indicators and provide a more stable signal.

Section 24.7

Demand Shocks and Supply Shocks

Business cycles can be triggered by a variety of disturbances. Economists classify many of these as shocks — sudden, unexpected changes that push the economy away from its previous equilibrium. Two major categories of shocks drive business cycle fluctuations: demand shocks and supply shocks.

Demand Shocks

A demand shock is an unexpected change in aggregate demand — the total spending on goods and services in the economy. Demand shocks shift the aggregate demand curve, changing output and the price level in the short run.

TypeExamplesEffect on Output and Prices
Negative demand shockCollapse in consumer confidence, decline in business investment, credit tightening, decline in government spending or untimely tax increase, fall in exportsOutput falls below potential; recessionary gap; unemployment rises
Positive demand shockSurge in government spending, large tax cut, surge in business optimism, housing boomOutput rises above potential; inflationary pressure builds

Supply Shocks

A supply shock is an unexpected change in production costs or productive capacity — factors that shift the aggregate supply curve rather than the aggregate demand curve.

TypeExamplesEffect on Output and Prices
Negative supply shockSharp oil price increase, drought or natural disaster, global supply chain disruptionOutput falls AND price level rises simultaneously — stagflation
Positive supply shockSharp decline in oil prices, major technological improvement, large increase in labor supplyOutput rises and price level falls
Historical Note
Stagflation in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries

The oil price shocks of 1973–1974 and 1979–1980 were the most prominent negative supply shocks of the twentieth century, triggering stagflation in many economies. The COVID-19 pandemic triggered severe supply disruptions beginning in 2020, contributing to inflationary pressures in subsequent years.

Why the Shock Type Matters for Policy

Demand shocks and supply shocks create different policy challenges. A negative demand shock calls for policies that stimulate spending. A negative supply shock — stagflation — is harder to address because the usual policy tools involve a tradeoff: stimulating demand can reduce unemployment but worsen inflation; reducing inflation through contractionary policy worsens unemployment further. Policymakers must navigate carefully when supply shocks drive the cycle.

Section 24.8

Financial Shocks

One important category of shock deserves separate discussion because of its power to trigger deep recessions: financial shocks — sudden disruptions to the financial system that impair its ability to channel savings into investment and credit.

In normal times, the financial system — banks, credit markets, bond markets, stock markets — connects savers with borrowers. Firms borrow to invest. Households borrow to buy homes and consumer durables. Governments borrow to finance spending gaps. This credit flow supports economic activity. When the financial system is disrupted, the effects on the real economy can be severe.

MechanismWhat Happens
Credit tightensBanks become unwilling to lend because they fear borrowers cannot repay. Firms and households cannot get credit. Spending falls.
Asset prices fall sharplyWhen stock or housing prices collapse, household wealth falls and households cut spending to rebuild savings — known as deleveraging.
Confidence collapsesFinancial crises create uncertainty; firms and households postpone large decisions, reducing aggregate demand further.
Bank failures cascadeIf banks fail, depositors may panic and withdraw funds from other banks — a bank run — potentially triggering broader financial collapse.
Case Study
The 2007–2009 financial crisis

The most significant financial shock in recent U.S. history was the 2007–2009 financial crisis, associated with the collapse of the housing market and the near-failure of major financial institutions. Housing prices had risen sharply from the late 1990s through the mid-2000s, fueled by easy credit, relaxed lending standards, and the widespread packaging of risky mortgages into complex financial securities. When housing prices fell, the value of those securities collapsed. Major financial institutions suffered catastrophic losses. Credit markets froze. The resulting recession — the Great Recession — saw unemployment rise from about 4.5 percent in 2007 to 10 percent in October 2009, and real GDP fall by approximately 4.3 percent from its peak.

The Fragility of Confidence

Financial shocks teach a lesson about the fragility of confidence in market economies: expectations of stability and expectations of crisis can both be self-fulfilling. When everyone expects others to default, credit dries up and defaults rise. Financial regulation, deposit insurance, and central bank backstops exist in large part to prevent these dynamics from spiraling out of control.

Section 24.9

Expectations and Confidence

One of the most important — and often underappreciated — forces in business cycle fluctuations is expectations. What households, firms, and financial markets expect to happen in the future shapes what they do today, and what they do today shapes what actually happens.

Consumer expectations: When households expect future income to be strong and jobs to be plentiful, they spend more today. When households expect a recession, they save more and cut spending. Because consumption is the largest component of aggregate demand, shifts in consumer confidence can move the entire economy.

Business expectations: Firms invest based on expected future demand. When firms expect demand to grow, they hire workers, build factories, and purchase equipment. When firms expect demand to fall, they cut investment plans, freeze hiring, and reduce output.

Financial market expectations: Stock prices reflect expected future profits, bond prices reflect expected future interest rates, and exchange rates reflect expected future currency values. When financial markets expect deteriorating conditions, asset prices fall — reducing household wealth, consumption, and economic activity.

Self-Fulfilling Dynamics

If households and firms expect a recession, they cut spending. Falling spending reduces output and employment — the recession they expected arrives because they expected it. If households and firms expect expansion and rising incomes, they spend more, and the expansion they expected arrives. This self-fulfilling property of expectations is one reason confidence is a genuine economic force, not merely a psychological curiosity.

Concept
Keynesian animal spirits

Economist John Maynard Keynes, writing in the 1930s, used the phrase animal spirits to describe the spontaneous waves of optimism and pessimism that drive investment decisions when the future is genuinely uncertain. His insight — that investment depends partly on confidence and mood, not just rational calculation — remains influential in understanding why business cycles can be so volatile.

Section 24.10

Stabilization Policy at an Introductory Level

Because business cycles cause real harm — unemployment, lost income, business failures, poverty — governments and central banks attempt to stabilize the economy. Stabilization policy refers to the use of monetary policy and fiscal policy to reduce the severity of recessions and moderate inflationary booms.

Automatic Stabilizers

Automatic stabilizers are features of the tax and spending system that automatically increase government deficits when the economy contracts and reduce deficits when the economy expands, thereby dampening the cycle. Examples include unemployment insurance, which replaces some lost income during a recession, and progressive income taxes, which cushion the decline in after-tax income when earnings fall.

Discretionary Fiscal Policy

Discretionary fiscal policy refers to deliberate changes in government spending or taxes passed by legislatures and signed by executives to respond to economic conditions. Expansionary fiscal policy increases spending or cuts taxes to boost demand during a recession; contractionary fiscal policy reduces spending or raises taxes during an inflationary boom.

Challenges of Discretionary Fiscal Policy

Legislation takes time, so stimulus may arrive too late to help. It is politically easier to pass spending increases and tax cuts than spending cuts and tax increases, creating an asymmetry in how the tools are used. Running large deficits to stabilize the economy also increases public debt, creating long-run fiscal challenges.

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy refers to decisions by the central bank — in the United States, the Federal Reserve — about interest rates and credit conditions. Expansionary monetary policy lowers interest rates to encourage investment and spending; contractionary monetary policy raises rates to slow spending and reduce inflationary pressure. Monetary policy can act more quickly than fiscal policy since it does not require legislative approval, though it still works through the economy with a lag.

Goals and Limits

Stabilization policy aims at two broad goals: preventing severe recessions with mass unemployment, and preventing inflationary booms with rapidly rising prices. These goals sometimes conflict — a tension captured in the concept of the Phillips curve, a short-run empirical relationship suggesting that lower unemployment is often associated with higher inflation, and higher unemployment with lower inflation. Policymakers do not have perfect foresight; the goal of stabilization policy is not perfection but reducing the severity of downturns and the persistence of unemployment.

Worked Example Review

Worked Example 24.1
Identifying the phase of the business cycle
  • GivenOver the past three quarters, real GDP has fallen from $22.1 trillion to $21.8 trillion to $21.5 trillion. The unemployment rate has risen from 4.2 percent to 5.8 percent. Business investment has declined sharply. Consumer spending has fallen modestly.
  • Step 1Identify what is happening to real GDP: it is falling for multiple consecutive quarters.
  • Step 2Check other indicators: unemployment is rising, investment is falling, consumer spending is declining.
  • Step 3Classify the phase: all indicators confirm contraction. The economy is in a recession.
Follow-up: If the economy reaches a trough next quarter and then real GDP rises for three straight quarters, the phase that follows is recovery, followed by expansion.
Worked Example 24.2
Identifying the type of shock
  • Scenario AOil prices double overnight due to a conflict in a major oil-producing region. Production costs rise across many industries. Output begins to fall while price levels rise. Classification: negative supply shock — SRAS shifts left, causing falling output and rising prices simultaneously.
  • Scenario BA major government stimulus package increases government spending significantly. Aggregate demand rises. Output increases and price levels rise somewhat. Classification: positive demand shock — AD shifts right.
  • Scenario CA collapse in housing prices destroys trillions of dollars of household wealth. Consumer confidence plunges and households cut spending sharply. Classification: negative demand shock — AD shifts left as consumption falls.
Key takeaway: Identify the shock by asking whether output and prices move in the same direction (demand shock) or opposite directions (supply shock).

  7. Look at the table of U.S. recession dates in the Data Review section. Calculate the average duration of recessions (a) during 1980–2010 and (b) during the Great Depression. What does this comparison suggest about the severity of the Great Depression relative to postwar recessions?
  8. Explain the difference between discretionary fiscal policy and automatic stabilizers. What is one advantage and one disadvantage of discretionary fiscal policy relative to automatic stabilizers?

Economic Reasoning Questions

  1. A government economist argues that the economy is near a peak and that policymakers should begin contractionary measures now to prevent inflation. A critic argues that it is too early to act and that premature tightening could cause a recession. What economic information would help resolve this disagreement? What risks does each side face if it is wrong?
  2. A severe financial crisis destroys a large amount of household wealth and tightens credit sharply. Analyze this as an economic shock. What type of shock is it? What happens to aggregate demand? What policies might stabilize the economy, and what are their tradeoffs?
  3. During a recession, politicians debate whether to cut taxes or increase government spending to stimulate the economy. Both are expansionary fiscal policy tools. Are there reasons to prefer one over the other in a deep recession? Consider the speed of impact, who benefits, and what happens when the recession ends.
  4. Explain why the unemployment rate is a lagging indicator. During a recovery, why might firms take months or years to restore employment to pre-recession levels even as output rebounds?
  5. Some economists argue that business cycles are caused mainly by demand shocks, while others argue that supply shocks are more important. How would evidence about what happened to both output and the price level during a downturn help distinguish between these two explanations?

Policy Scenario Questions

Scenario 1
A Technology Supply Disruption

A global shortage of semiconductor chips — critical inputs in automobiles, electronics, and industrial equipment — causes production costs to rise sharply across many industries. Output at automobile manufacturers falls. Prices on consumer electronics rise. Workers at affected firms face temporary layoffs.

Identify the type of shock. What happens to real GDP and the price level in the short run? What are the policy options? What tradeoffs do policymakers face?
Scenario 2
A Consumer Confidence Collapse

A major news event causes consumer confidence to fall sharply. Surveys show that households plan to cut spending significantly over the next six months. Business investment plans are also cut as firms anticipate weaker demand.

Identify the type of shock. Draw the AD-AS shift. What phase of the business cycle might follow? What automatic stabilizers would activate? What discretionary policies might a government consider?
Scenario 3
A Prolonged Expansion

An economy has been in expansion for seven years. Unemployment is below 4 percent. Consumer spending is strong. Wage growth is accelerating. Inflation has risen from 2 percent to 3.5 percent.

Is this economy approaching a peak? What indicators would you look at? What concerns might a central bank have? What monetary policy action might it consider, and what are the risks?

Error Analysis

Identify the error in each statement and explain the correct reasoning.

  1. "A recession begins as soon as real GDP growth slows."
  2. "A leading indicator tells us what is happening to the economy right now."
  3. "A negative supply shock causes falling output AND falling prices."
  4. "Automatic stabilizers require Congress to vote on new spending bills each time a recession begins."
  5. "Because the unemployment rate fell last month, the economy must have passed its trough."
  6. "Financial shocks affect the financial sector but do not affect the broader economy very much, since most people do not own stocks."

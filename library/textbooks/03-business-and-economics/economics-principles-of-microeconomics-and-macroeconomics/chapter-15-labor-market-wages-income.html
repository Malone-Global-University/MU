Chapter 15 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When you take a job, accept a wage, decide whether to pursue more education, or consider moving to a new city for work, you are participating in one of the most important markets in any economy: the labor market. Labor markets bring together workers who supply their time and skills and firms that demand those skills to produce goods and services.

Labor markets determine wages, employment levels, hours worked, and ultimately the income that households use to buy food, housing, education, healthcare, and everything else they need. Because labor income is the primary source of income for most households, understanding how labor markets work is essential to understanding inequality, poverty, economic growth, and public policy.

This chapter introduces the economic analysis of labor markets. You will learn why firms demand labor, what determines how much workers are paid, how labor supply and labor demand interact to set wages, and why wages differ so much across occupations, industries, and individuals. You will also examine the role of human capital, unions, minimum wage laws, and discrimination in shaping labor market outcomes.

Opening Question

Think about your own future career, or a job you already hold. Why does that job pay what it pays? Is it because of the skills it requires, the risks it carries, the location where it is performed, or something else entirely? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to analyze why wages differ — and why some of those differences are easy to explain, while others raise harder questions about fairness and discrimination.

Section 15.1

Labor as a Factor of Production

Every firm that produces something — a loaf of bread, a legal brief, a software application, a medical procedure — needs inputs. Economists group the inputs used in production into factors of production: land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship.

Labor refers to the human work, effort, skills, and time that people contribute to production. Labor is unique among the factors of production because it is inseparable from the people who supply it. When a firm hires a worker, it does not simply purchase a quantity of hours. It enters into a relationship with a person who brings knowledge, effort, motivation, and judgment to the job.

Labor is used in virtually every stage of every production process. Farmers grow crops. Engineers design bridges. Teachers instruct students. Nurses care for patients. Even in highly automated industries, workers operate, maintain, program, and oversee machines.

Labor Markets

A labor market is a market in which workers sell their labor services and firms buy those services. Like other markets, labor markets have buyers and sellers, prices, and mechanisms of adjustment.

ComponentDescription
SellersWorkers — individuals who offer their time, effort, and skills in exchange for wages or salaries
BuyersFirms — organizations that hire workers to produce goods and services
PriceThe wage — the payment workers receive for their labor

Labor markets exist at every level of aggregation: local labor markets (restaurant workers in a city), regional labor markets (construction workers in a state), national labor markets (physicians across a country), and global labor markets (software engineers across multiple countries).

Why Labor Market Analysis Matters

Labor income accounts for roughly 60 to 70 percent of national income in most advanced economies. Wages signal where labor is most valued, and labor market outcomes shape household well-being, poverty rates, inequality, and economic mobility. Policies such as minimum wages, occupational licensing, anti-discrimination laws, and job training are among the most debated and consequential economic policies.

Section 15.2

Labor Demand

Firms demand labor not for its own sake but because labor helps them produce output. This is the key insight of derived demand: firms demand labor because they demand the output that labor helps produce. Labor demand is derived from the demand for the final good or service.

The Demand for Labor at the Firm Level

Each additional worker a firm hires produces additional output, called the marginal product of labor (MPL). As a firm hires more and more workers — holding other inputs like equipment and space constant — the marginal product of labor typically falls. This is the principle of diminishing marginal product.

From Marginal Product to Labor Demand

A firm hires workers to earn profit. The additional revenue generated by hiring one more worker is the marginal revenue product of labor (MRPL).

Key Formula

Marginal Revenue Product of Labor = Marginal Product of Labor × Output Price

A profit-maximizing firm hires workers up to the point where MRPL = Wage. The firm continues hiring as long as MRPL ≥ Wage and stops once MRPL < Wage.

If the wage is $15 per hour, the pizza shop hires workers whose marginal revenue product is at least $15. If the wage rises to $20 per hour, fewer workers meet that threshold and the firm demands fewer workers. This reasoning generates the law of labor demand: as the wage rises, the quantity of labor demanded falls; as the wage falls, the quantity of labor demanded rises — all else equal.

Section 15.3

Labor Supply

Workers decide how much labor to supply — how many hours to work, whether to take a job, and which jobs to accept — based on the tradeoff between work and other uses of their time.

The Individual Labor Supply Decision

When a worker decides how many hours to work, she weighs the benefit of working (the wage she earns) against the opportunity cost of working (the value of leisure time, household production, education, or other activities she gives up).

As wages rise, working becomes more attractive relative to leisure — the substitution effect. However, higher wages also make workers wealthier, which may cause some workers to want more leisure — the income effect. At very high wages, the income effect can outweigh the substitution effect, causing the labor supply curve to bend backward. For introductory purposes, this chapter focuses on the typical range where higher wages lead to a greater quantity of labor supplied.

Common Misconception

Students sometimes assume the labor supply curve always slopes upward without limit. In reality, at very high wages some workers choose more leisure rather than more work — the labor supply curve can bend backward. This chapter focuses on the typical, more relevant range for most policy analysis.

Section 15.4

Wages and Equilibrium in Labor Markets

The equilibrium wage and equilibrium employment level are determined by the interaction of labor demand and labor supply. The equilibrium wage is the wage at which the quantity of labor demanded equals the quantity of labor supplied.

Graph 15.3 — Labor Market Equilibrium
Wage
  |         LS
  |       /
  |      /
  | ----*---- Equilibrium (W*, L*)
  |      \
  |       \
  |        LD
  +------------------------
              Quantity of Labor

At the equilibrium wage (W*), the quantity of labor demanded equals the quantity of labor supplied at equilibrium employment (L*).

If the wage is above equilibrium, there is a surplus of labor: more workers want jobs than firms want to hire, which puts downward pressure on wages. If the wage is below equilibrium, there is a shortage of labor: firms want to hire more workers than are available, which puts upward pressure on wages. Adjustment can be slow and incomplete in practice, especially when wages are rigid downward, workers have limited mobility, or information is incomplete.

ShiftEffect on Equilibrium WageEffect on Equilibrium Employment
Labor demand increasesRisesRises
Labor demand decreasesFallsFalls
Labor supply increasesFallsRises
Labor supply decreasesRisesFalls

Section 15.5

Human Capital and Productivity

Why do surgeons earn more than hospital orderlies? A major reason is human capital — the knowledge, skills, training, experience, and capabilities that workers accumulate through education, training, and experience. Human capital raises a worker's productivity: a more skilled worker produces more output per hour than a less skilled worker.

Human Capital Theory

Human capital theory, developed extensively by economist Gary Becker, treats education and training as investments. Just as a firm invests in physical capital to increase future output, individuals invest in human capital to increase their future earning power. Like any investment, this involves upfront costs (tuition, fees, forgone wages during school) and future benefits (higher wages earned over the working life).

TypeDescriptionWho Typically Pays
General human capitalSkills valuable across many employers and industries — literacy, numeracy, communication, problem-solving, digital skillsUsually the worker (tuition, forgone wages)
Firm-specific human capitalSkills primarily valuable to a particular employer — knowledge of specific systems, processes, and cultureOften shared with the firm (training programs, on-the-job learning)
Human Capital and the Wage Premium

Workers with more education earn higher wages on average — the education wage premium. Some of this reflects higher productivity. Some may reflect signaling: a degree may signal traits employers value, such as discipline and perseverance, even apart from any direct increase in productivity. In practice, both channels likely operate together.

Section 15.6

Education, Training, and Skills

Human capital is accumulated through multiple channels. Formal education — schools, colleges, vocational programs — is strongly correlated with wages, though the quality and content of education matter as much as the quantity, and unequal access to quality education contributes to wage inequality.

On-the-job training builds human capital outside the classroom. Formal on-the-job training includes apprenticeships, mentoring programs, and structured job rotations. Informal on-the-job training happens through learning by doing — workers become more productive simply by performing their jobs repeatedly and observing experienced colleagues.

Skill-Biased Technological Change

Technology has increased demand for workers with cognitive skills (analysis, problem-solving, creativity), technical skills (programming, data analysis, engineering), and interpersonal skills (collaboration, teaching, caregiving). At the same time, it has reduced demand for workers performing routine manual or routine cognitive tasks. This pattern is one reason wage inequality has grown in many countries over recent decades.

Section 15.7

Compensating Differentials

Not all wage differences reflect differences in productivity or human capital. Some reflect differences in the attractiveness of the jobs themselves. Compensating differentials are wage differences that compensate workers for unpleasant, risky, or otherwise undesirable job characteristics.

Job CharacteristicExample OccupationsEffect on Wage
Physical dangerLogging, commercial fishing, mining, roofing, firefightingPremium for injury and death risk
Unhealthy conditionsChemical exposure, dust, noise, extreme temperaturesPremium for disamenities
Unpleasant workSewer cleaning, meat processing, night shifts, isolated locationsPremium to attract workers
Irregular hoursEvening or weekend shifts, unpredictable schedulesShift differentials and schedule premiums
Geographic isolationRemote facilities, undesirable locationsPremium to attract workers
Example 15.7.1
Comparing two otherwise similar jobs
  • Job AFlexible remote work, pleasant conditions, regular daytime hours.
  • Job BNight shifts at a remote facility with exposure to unpleasant conditions, similar skill requirements.
  • PredictionIf Job B did not pay a wage premium, few workers would choose it over Job A.
  • ConclusionThe wage premium Job B must pay to attract workers is the compensating differential — compensation for the difference in job attractiveness.
Key takeaway: Compensating differentials help explain wage variation among workers with similar skills and education.
The Reverse Case

Jobs with attractive characteristics — flexible hours, prestige, pleasant conditions, job security, desirable location — may pay less than productivity alone would predict, because workers accept a lower wage in exchange for these non-wage benefits.

Section 15.8

Unions and Collective Bargaining

Individual workers typically negotiate wages with employers from a position of unequal power. Labor unions are organizations of workers that bargain collectively with employers over wages, benefits, and working conditions.

What Unions Do

Unions represent their members in collective bargaining over wages and wage increases; benefits such as health insurance and retirement plans; working conditions; job security; and grievance procedures. A successful negotiation produces a labor contract. If bargaining fails, unions may call a strike — the credible threat of which is itself a source of union bargaining power.

The Union Wage Premium

Where unions succeed in raising wages above the competitive equilibrium, unionized workers tend to earn higher wages than comparable non-union workers — commonly estimated at 10 to 20 percent in the United States. However, higher union wages may lead firms to hire fewer workers, and displaced workers seeking jobs elsewhere can push down wages in non-union sectors.

Unions also affect non-wage outcomes: some research finds unionized workplaces have lower turnover, better safety records, and more systematic grievance procedures. Union membership has declined significantly in many advanced economies — in the United States, from roughly 35 percent of workers in the mid-20th century to under 11 percent in recent years — due to structural shifts away from manufacturing, increased international competition, changes in labor law, and growth of non-standard work arrangements.

Section 15.9

Minimum Wages at an Introductory Level

A minimum wage is a legal floor on the wage that employers may pay workers. Their effects are among the most studied and debated topics in labor economics.

Graph 15.4 — Minimum Wage Above Equilibrium
Wage
  |         LS
  |       /
  | --W_min------ Surplus (L_S - L_D)
  |    /      \
  | --W*---*------ Equilibrium
  |   /        \
  |  /           LD
  +------------------------
       L_D    L*    L_S
              Quantity of Labor

At a minimum wage above equilibrium (W_min > W*), quantity demanded falls to L_D, quantity supplied rises to L_S, and a labor surplus (L_S − L_D) results.

In a simple competitive labor market, a minimum wage set above the equilibrium wage raises pay for workers who keep their jobs but reduces the quantity of labor demanded and increases the quantity supplied, creating a surplus of labor — unemployment among those who want jobs but cannot find them.

ComplicationExplanation
Monopsony powerIf a local market has only one or a few employers, a minimum wage can raise both wages and employment by offsetting the employer's wage-setting power
Empirical findingsMany studies find little or no measurable employment decrease from moderate increases; others find modest negative effects, especially for younger or less experienced workers
Magnitude mattersA minimum wage modestly above the market wage likely has small employment effects; one set far above the market wage is more likely to cause significant unemployment
Policy Debate, Not Settled Science

Minimum wage policy involves genuine tradeoffs between higher incomes for workers who retain employment and possible reduced employment opportunities for others. Careful economic analysis weighs both potential benefits and potential costs rather than assuming one outcome automatically dominates.

Section 15.10

Income Differences and Labor Market Institutions

Workers' wages and incomes differ enormously across the economy. Wage differences arise from several interacting factors: human capital differences, occupational differences and compensating differentials, industry differences, geographic differences, experience and tenure, and discrimination — paying workers differently based on characteristics unrelated to productivity.

The Gender Wage Gap

The gender wage gap describes the tendency for women to earn less than men on average. In the United States, full-time working women have historically earned roughly 80 to 85 cents for every dollar earned by full-time working men. Part of this gap reflects differences in education, occupation, industry, experience, and hours worked, and the gap has narrowed as women's educational attainment has risen.

A significant portion of the remaining gap is difficult to explain with observable worker characteristics. Contributing factors researchers study include occupational segregation, the "motherhood penalty," gender differences in wage negotiation, and continued unexplained differentials that may reflect discrimination.

The Role of Institutions

Labor market outcomes are shaped not only by supply and demand but also by institutions: minimum wage laws, collective bargaining and unions, anti-discrimination laws, occupational licensing, employment protection laws, and social insurance programs. These institutions interact with market forces to produce the outcomes we observe.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 15.1Labor markets bring together workers (sellers) and firms (buyers), with the wage as the price. Labor income makes up most national income and shapes household well-being.
  • Sec 15.2Labor demand is derived from demand for output. Firms hire workers until MRPL equals the wage. Diminishing marginal product makes the labor demand curve slope downward. Output demand, productivity, input prices, and number of firms shift labor demand.
  • Sec 15.3Workers supply labor by weighing wages against the opportunity cost of time. In the typical range, higher wages increase quantity of labor supplied. Population, participation, alternative opportunities, non-wage income, and preferences shift labor supply.
  • Sec 15.4Equilibrium wage and employment occur where labor demand and supply intersect. Surpluses and shortages push wages toward equilibrium. Shifts in demand move wage and employment together; shifts in supply move them in opposite directions.
  • Sec 15.5Human capital — education, training, experience — raises productivity and wages. General human capital is usually paid for by workers; firm-specific human capital costs are often shared with employers. The education wage premium reflects both productivity gains and signaling.
  • Sec 15.6Human capital accumulates through formal education and on-the-job training. Skill-biased technological change has raised demand for cognitive, technical, and interpersonal skills while reducing demand for routine tasks, contributing to rising wage inequality.
  • Sec 15.7Compensating differentials explain wage differences across jobs with similar skill requirements: dangerous, unpleasant, or inconvenient jobs pay premiums, while jobs with attractive non-wage features may pay less.
  • Sec 15.8Unions bargain collectively over wages, benefits, and conditions. The union wage premium benefits members but may reduce employment among union workers or wages in non-union sectors. Union membership has declined substantially in recent decades.
  • Sec 15.9In a competitive market, a minimum wage above equilibrium raises pay for employed workers but can create a labor surplus. Monopsony power complicates this analysis, and empirical evidence on employment effects is mixed and depends on the size of the increase.
  • Sec 15.10Wage differences reflect human capital, occupation, industry, geography, experience, and discrimination. The gender wage gap has narrowed but an unexplained portion persists. Labor market institutions shape outcomes alongside supply and demand.

Key Terms Review

labor market
A market in which workers sell their labor services and firms buy those services, with wages as the price.
derived demand
Demand for a factor of production that arises from demand for the output that factor helps produce.
marginal product of labor (MPL)
The additional output produced by hiring one more worker, holding other inputs constant.
diminishing marginal product
The tendency for the marginal product of labor to fall as more workers are added to a fixed set of other inputs.
marginal revenue product of labor (MRPL)
The additional revenue a firm earns by hiring one more worker; equals MPL times output price for competitive firms.
labor demand curve
A curve showing the relationship between the wage and quantity of labor demanded; slopes downward.
labor supply curve
A curve showing the relationship between the wage and quantity of labor supplied; slopes upward in typical ranges.
equilibrium wage
The wage at which quantity of labor demanded equals quantity of labor supplied.
human capital
The knowledge, skills, training, and experience that raise a worker's productivity.
general human capital
Skills and knowledge valuable to many employers across industries.
firm-specific human capital
Skills and knowledge primarily valuable to a particular employer.
education wage premium
The difference in average wages between workers with more and less education.
compensating differentials
Wage differences that compensate workers for undesirable job characteristics such as risk or inconvenient schedules.
labor union
An organization of workers that bargains collectively with employers over wages, benefits, and conditions.
collective bargaining
Negotiation between a union and an employer over the terms of employment.
union wage premium
The wage advantage of unionized workers over comparable non-union workers.
minimum wage
A legal floor on the wage employers may pay workers.
monopsony
A labor market in which a single buyer (employer) has significant power over the wage.
gender wage gap
The observed difference in average wages between men and women.

Worked Example 2
Analyzing a minimum wage
  • SituationEquilibrium wage for restaurant workers is $11/hour at 5,000 workers. A minimum wage of $15 is enacted.
  • At $15/hourQuantity demanded falls to 4,200; quantity supplied rises to 5,500.
  • SurplusLabor surplus = 5,500 − 4,200 = 1,300 workers
  • Winners4,200 workers who keep or find jobs now earn $15 instead of $11 — a $4/hour increase.
  • LosersUp to 800 workers who previously had jobs at $11 may lose employment; 1,300 workers who want jobs at $15 cannot find them.
Key insight: The net welfare effect depends on which is larger — the wage gain for employed workers or the employment loss for displaced workers. This is empirically contested and varies with how high the minimum wage is relative to the market wage.

Economic Reasoning Questions

  1. A student is deciding whether to pursue a two-year associate's degree in medical imaging technology or enter the workforce immediately after high school. Apply human capital theory to explain how an economist would think about this decision. What information would be most important for making the decision?
  2. A city raises its minimum wage from $12 to $17 per hour. Economists study the local restaurant industry and find that employment fell by only 1 percent — much less than a simple supply-and-demand model would predict. What explanations might account for this smaller-than-expected employment effect?
  3. Explain how skill-biased technological change could increase wage inequality even if no discrimination is occurring and all workers are paid their marginal revenue product.
  4. A firm opens a new facility in a rural area where there are few employers. The firm pays wages well below what similar workers earn in the nearest city. Analyze this situation. What labor market concept might explain why workers accept these wages? What might cause wages to rise over time?
  5. Two workers with identical resumes, education, and experience apply for the same job. One is offered the position at $62,000 per year; the other is told the position is no longer available but is later found to have been filled at the same salary. If this reflects discrimination, explain the economic costs — not just the moral problem — of labor market discrimination. Who is harmed?

Real-World Applications

  1. Automation and labor demand. Some economists argue that advances in artificial intelligence and robotics could displace millions of workers in transportation, manufacturing, and services. Using labor demand analysis, explain how automation affects the labor market in the short run and what adjustments might occur in the long run.
  2. Immigration and wages. Critics of immigration argue that more immigrant workers reduce wages for native-born workers. Supporters argue immigrants fill labor shortages and increase economic output. Using labor supply and demand, analyze both arguments. Under what conditions would each argument be more valid?
  3. The gig economy. Platforms like rideshare and delivery apps use independent contractors rather than employees. These workers often lack benefits like health insurance, retirement contributions, and unemployment insurance coverage. What labor market concepts help explain the growth of gig work? What tradeoffs do gig workers face compared to traditional employees?
  4. The teacher shortage. Many school districts report difficulty filling teaching positions, especially in math, science, and special education. Using labor market analysis, identify possible explanations for teacher shortages and evaluate possible policy responses.
  5. International wage differences. Wages for similar work differ enormously across countries — a software developer might earn far more in one country than in another with similar educational levels. What economic factors explain international wage differences? How might these differences change as economies develop?

Looking Ahead
Chapter 16: Externalities and Public Goods

Chapter 15 explored how labor markets work — how wages are determined, why they differ across workers and occupations, and how institutions like unions and minimum wages shape outcomes. These insights focused on well-functioning labor markets. Chapter 16 turns to situations where markets may not produce efficient or socially desirable outcomes — market failures. The chapter introduces externalities (costs or benefits that fall on parties outside a market transaction) and public goods (goods that markets tend to underprovide because individuals cannot be excluded from using them). Understanding when and why markets fail, and how policy might respond, is one of the most important applications of microeconomic reasoning.

Chapter 14 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"Management is doing things right; leadership is doing the right things."
— Peter Drucker

Every business, from a two-person food cart to a multinational corporation, depends on someone making decisions, organizing work, motivating people, and steering the enterprise toward its goals. That someone — or that group of people — is management.

Management is often misunderstood. Students sometimes picture a manager as a person who sits in an office, gives orders, and checks off lists. That picture is incomplete. Real management is about coordination: aligning the work of many people toward a shared purpose, solving problems that arise along the way, and helping everyone involved do their best work.

This chapter introduces management and leadership as two essential but distinct forces in any organization. Management provides structure. Leadership provides direction and inspiration. The best business operators learn to do both.

Opening Question

Mireille founded a bakery supply company and handled everything herself at first. When the business grew, she promoted her most skilled employee to operations manager. Six months later, deliveries are delayed, customer complaints are up, and employees are frustrated. What went wrong — and what does it tell us about the difference between being good at a job and being good at managing people who do that job? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to answer that question.

Section 14.1

What Management Is

Management is the process of coordinating people, resources, information, and decisions to achieve organizational goals.

That definition contains several important ideas worth unpacking.

What Management CoordinatesWhat This MeansWhat Happens Without It
PeopleEnsuring individuals and teams understand their roles, work together, and avoid duplication or conflictOverlapping effort, confusion, and conflict
ResourcesAllocating money, equipment, materials, time, and information where they are needed mostWaste, shortages, and missed priorities
InformationEnsuring the right people have the knowledge they need to do their jobsPoor decisions made on incomplete or wrong information
DecisionsMaking timely choices and creating structures so routine decisions happen without constant management attentionBottlenecks, delays, and paralysis
Management and Entrepreneurship

Students sometimes assume that management only applies to large businesses. This is incorrect. Entrepreneurs manage from day one — even if they manage only themselves at first. A solo entrepreneur manages time, money, and attention. Poor management is one of the most consistent reasons businesses fail, regardless of how good the product is.

Section 14.2

Planning, Organizing, Leading, and Controlling

Management scholars have long described management in terms of four core functions. These functions are not always performed in strict sequence — in practice, managers juggle all four simultaneously — but understanding them separately helps clarify what management actually involves.

Planning

Core Function 1
Planning — Setting goals and deciding how to achieve them

Planning happens at multiple levels. Strategic planning sets the long-term direction for the entire organization. Operational planning translates that direction into shorter-term priorities. Tactical planning addresses specific initiatives and campaigns.

A common mistake in planning is confusing activity with progress. A plan that lists tasks without connecting them to outcomes is just a to-do list. A real plan defines goals, explains how the tasks lead to those goals, identifies resources required, and includes ways to measure success.

Organizing

Core Function 2
Organizing — Structuring work, roles, and resources to execute the plan

Organizing involves decisions such as: Who is responsible for which tasks? How will teams be structured? Who has authority to make which decisions? When a business is small, organizing is informal. As a business grows, informal organizing breaks down — roles overlap, responsibilities blur, and important work falls through gaps.

Organizational structure is the formal arrangement of roles and reporting relationships. A flat structure has few layers between the top and the front line. A hierarchical structure has many layers. Neither is universally better — the right structure depends on the size, type, and strategy of the business.

Leading

Core Function 3
Leading — Guiding, motivating, and influencing people to achieve goals

This is where management and leadership overlap most directly. Leading is not about issuing commands — it is about creating conditions in which people do excellent work willingly and with understanding. Effective leaders communicate clearly, set expectations, listen, give feedback, recognize contributions, and model the behavior they expect from others.

Controlling

Core Function 4
Controlling — Monitoring progress, comparing results to goals, and adjusting

Controlling is about accountability — making sure that work is moving in the right direction, catching problems early, and course-correcting before small issues become large ones. Controlling tools include reports, dashboards, meetings, performance reviews, financial statements, and customer feedback systems. The goal of controlling is not punishment — it is learning.

Common Misunderstanding

"Controlling" in management does not mean micromanaging or restricting people. It means measuring results against goals and adjusting when needed. When results fall short, the management question is: what does that tell us, and what should we change?

Leadership and Influence

Leadership is the ability to inspire, guide, and influence others toward a shared purpose. Leadership and management are related but not identical.

What Makes an Effective Leader?

Rather than memorizing a list of traits, it is more useful to understand the principles that consistently appear across effective leaders.

PrincipleWhat It Looks Like

Leadership Styles

Leadership style refers to how a leader tends to make decisions and interact with their team. Most effective leaders do not use only one style — they adapt their approach to the situation, the team's needs, and the nature of the task.

Section 14.4

Decision-Making in Organizations

Decision-making is one of management's most important and most difficult responsibilities. Every business depends on a continuous stream of decisions — what to produce, how to price, whom to hire, when to expand, and how to respond to problems.

Types of Decisions

TypeDescriptionManagement Response
RoutineRecurring situations with established answers; handled by policies and proceduresApply existing policy; do not consume management attention unnecessarily
Non-routineNovel situations requiring judgment, analysis, and often creative problem-solvingUse a structured decision-making process; do not apply old policies blindly

The Decision-Making Process

A structured approach to decision-making reduces errors and improves outcomes, especially for important non-routine decisions.

Six-Step Decision Process

Step 1: Define the decision. What exactly needs to be decided? Poorly framed decisions lead to solutions for the wrong problem.

Step 2: Gather relevant information. What do you know? What do you not know? What assumptions are you making?

Step 3: Identify options. What are the realistic alternatives? Effective decision-making requires generating multiple options rather than defaulting to the first idea.

Step 4: Evaluate options. What are the likely outcomes of each? What are the risks, costs, and benefits?

Step 5: Make the decision. Choose an option and commit to it. Indecision is itself a decision with consequences.

Step 6: Implement and monitor. Execute the decision and track results. Adjust if evidence suggests the decision needs revision.

Common Decision-Making Errors

ErrorWhat It IsHow to Counter It
Confirmation biasSeeking information that confirms existing beliefs and ignoring evidence that challenges themActively look for evidence that might prove you wrong
Sunk cost fallacyContinuing to invest in a failing course of action because of what has already been spentBase decisions on expected future outcomes, not past investment
GroupthinkTeam pressure toward conformity that suppresses dissent and reduces decision qualityCreate space for disagreement; actively invite alternative perspectives
OverconfidenceOverestimating the accuracy of one's own judgments and forecastsStress-test important decisions; seek out people who will challenge the plan
Entrepreneur Application

Entrepreneurs are especially vulnerable to confirmation bias — it is easy to fall in love with your own idea. Building the habit of seeking disconfirming evidence before making major decisions protects against costly mistakes. Customer discovery (Chapter 11) is partly a structured practice of testing assumptions rather than confirming them.

Section 14.5

Delegation and Accountability

Delegation is the transfer of responsibility and authority for a specific task or decision from a manager to a team member. Effective delegation is not simply assigning tasks — it involves transferring the authority needed to complete the task, providing required information and resources, defining the expected outcome clearly, and establishing how progress will be tracked.

Why Managers Resist Delegation

Resistance ReasonThe Problem with This Thinking
"I can do it faster myself."Probably true in the short term — but not over time. Delegating builds others' capacity while freeing the manager for higher-level work.
"They won't do it the way I would."Possibly true. The question is whether it must be done exactly that way, or whether a different approach is acceptable if the outcome is achieved.
"If something goes wrong, it reflects on me."True — which is why accountability structures matter. But accountability is not served by doing everything yourself.
The Bottleneck Problem

Managers who refuse to delegate become bottlenecks. Decisions queue up behind them, team members feel underused and disrespected, and the manager becomes exhausted doing work that others could handle. As a business grows, a manager who cannot delegate becomes a ceiling on the entire organization's capacity.

Delegation and Accountability Together

Delegation without accountability is abandonment — assigning a task with no follow-up or expectation of results.

Accountability without delegation is unfair — holding people responsible for outcomes they were never given the authority to influence.

Effective management pairs both: assign the task and the authority, define the expected outcome, agree on how progress will be tracked, and address problems honestly when they arise.

Section 14.6

Motivation and Morale

A fundamental management challenge is that people are not machines. People bring their full humanity to work — their needs, goals, emotions, relationships, and sense of meaning. Managers who ignore this reality create workplaces where people do the minimum required and nothing more.

Three Key Motivation Frameworks

Framework 1
Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Abraham Maslow proposed that human needs operate in layers: physiological needs first, then safety, then belonging, then esteem, and finally self-actualization — the desire to grow and achieve one's potential. Workers who are worried about basic security cannot fully engage with meaningful work. Managers must ensure foundational needs are met before expecting high-level engagement.

Framework 2
Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction — adequate pay, safe working conditions, job security. Motivators generate genuine engagement — meaningful work, recognition, autonomy, and growth opportunities. The critical insight: removing dissatisfaction is not the same as creating motivation. Hygiene factors are necessary but not sufficient.

Framework 3
Self-Determination Theory

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan identified three core psychological needs that support intrinsic motivation: competence (feeling capable and effective), autonomy (feeling a sense of choice and ownership), and relatedness (feeling connected to others). Work that supports these needs generates deeper, more sustained engagement than work that relies on external rewards alone.

Practical Implications for Managers

PracticeWhy It Works
Recognize contributions publicly and sincerelyPeople want to know their work matters; acknowledgment costs nothing and yields significant returns
Communicate purpose, not just tasksWhen people understand why their work matters, they tend to do it better; transforms compliance into contribution
Give people appropriate autonomyMicromanagement signals distrust and reduces ownership; latitude within clear boundaries increases engagement
Provide growth opportunitiesPeople learning and developing are more committed and less likely to disengage or leave
Address problems promptlyLow morale has identifiable causes; waiting for the situation to worsen is more costly than addressing it early
Section 14.7

Organizational Culture

Organizational culture is the set of shared values, norms, behaviors, and beliefs that shape how people in an organization act, make decisions, and treat each other. Culture is sometimes described as "the way things are done around here."

Culture is powerful precisely because much of it is unspoken. It does not live primarily in a mission statement or a list of values posted on the wall — it lives in daily behaviors, decisions, and habits. What actually happens when someone speaks up about a problem? What actually happens when a mistake is made? The answers to those questions describe the real culture.

Why Culture Matters

Area of ImpactHow Culture Shapes It
Hiring and retentionToxic cultures drive away talented people who have options; strong cultures attract people who share the organization's values
Customer experienceOrganizations that treat employees poorly tend to create employees who treat customers poorly — and vice versa
Ethics and riskStrong ethical cultures surface problems early; poor ethical cultures create environments where people look away or cover up
Adaptation and learningCultures that value learning and candor adapt well; cultures that punish mistakes become brittle over time
Culture Follows Behavior, Not Intention

Culture is not built by proclamation. When a leader says "we value honesty" and then punishes someone for delivering bad news, the message received is that honesty is not actually safe. Entrepreneurs and founders have significant influence over culture during a business's early years — the habits and norms established in the early stages tend to persist, which is a reason to be intentional about culture from the beginning.

Section 14.8

Managing Change

Businesses must change — markets shift, technology evolves, customer needs change, and competitive conditions change. Managing change is one of management's most demanding responsibilities. People resist change for understandable reasons: it creates uncertainty, disrupts established routines, and may threaten status, skills, or job security. These reactions are normal and human.

Principles for Leading Change Effectively

Build understanding first. People who understand why a change is necessary are more likely to support it. Explain the reasoning honestly and completely before asking for commitment.

Involve people in the process. Change that happens to people generates more resistance than change that people help design. Involve those who will be affected in planning and decision-making where possible.

Communicate repeatedly. During change, people need more information than usual. A single announcement is rarely enough. Consistent, transparent communication throughout the process reduces anxiety and rumor.

Address concerns directly. Acknowledging concerns honestly — even when the answer is uncertain — is more effective than dismissing them.

Recognize that change takes time. Behavior and culture change slowly. Effective change leaders set realistic timelines and celebrate progress rather than only measuring completion.

Concept Summary

  • Sec 14.1Management is the process of coordinating people, resources, information, and decisions to achieve organizational goals. It is relevant to businesses of every size, including solo ventures and early-stage startups.
  • Sec 14.2The four functions of management are planning (setting goals), organizing (structuring work and roles), leading (guiding and motivating people), and controlling (monitoring results and adjusting). All four are needed simultaneously.
  • Sec 14.3Leadership is the ability to inspire, guide, and influence others toward a shared purpose. Management provides structure; leadership provides direction. Organizations need both. Effective leaders adapt their style to the situation.
  • Sec 14.4Decisions are routine or non-routine. A structured six-step process improves decision quality. Common errors include confirmation bias, the sunk cost fallacy, groupthink, and overconfidence.
  • Sec 14.5Delegation transfers responsibility and authority together. Managers who refuse to delegate become bottlenecks. Accountability must accompany delegation — holding people responsible for outcomes they were never authorized to influence is unfair.
  • Sec 14.6People are motivated by more than money. Maslow, Herzberg, and self-determination theory each show that purpose, recognition, autonomy, growth, and belonging drive deep engagement. Practical management behaviors can support or undermine motivation.
  • Sec 14.7Organizational culture is the shared values, norms, and behaviors that shape how people act. Culture follows behavior, not intention, and powerfully shapes hiring, customer experience, ethics, and adaptation. Founders shape culture most in a business's early stages.
  • Sec 14.8Change is difficult because it creates uncertainty and disrupts routines. Effective change management requires honest communication, genuine involvement of affected people, repeated updates, direct engagement with concerns, and realistic timelines.
  • Sec 14.9Common management mistakes include avoiding difficult conversations, confusing activity with results, failing to develop others, deciding without adequate information, treating people as interchangeable, and failing to celebrate success.

Business Math and Data Practice

Scenario
A manager oversees a team of five employees. Each employee earns a salary of $42,000 per year. The team produces 1,200 completed units of work per month. The manager estimates that improving team communication and clarity could increase productivity by 15%. A one-day training session costs $600 per person. The business values each unit of output at $38.
  1. What is the current monthly output per employee?
  2. What would monthly output per employee be if productivity increased 15%?
  3. What is the total additional units produced per month from a 15% increase?
  4. At $38 per unit, what is the monthly revenue increase associated with the 15% improvement?
  5. What is the total training investment for the entire team?
  6. If the revenue increase occurs every month, how many months does it take to recover the training investment? Round to the nearest month.

Case Study

Case Study
The Promotion Problem

Mireille founded a bakery supply company four years ago. She handled everything herself at first — sales, orders, deliveries, and customer service. As the business grew, she hired three employees and promoted her most technically skilled employee, Dante, to operations manager.

Six months later, the team is struggling. Deliveries are delayed. Customer complaints have increased. Two employees have told Mireille privately that they find Dante difficult to work with. When Mireille asked Dante about it, he said, "I just need people to follow procedures. If they did what they were supposed to, there would be no problems."

Meanwhile, Mireille has noticed that Dante is working twelve-hour days and handling many tasks himself rather than delegating. When she asked why, he said, "It's faster if I do it."

Discussion Questions
  1. What management functions is Dante struggling with? Be specific about planning, organizing, leading, and controlling.
  2. What do Dante's behaviors suggest about his approach to delegation and accountability?
  3. What might explain the gap between Dante's technical skills and his management effectiveness?
  4. What would you advise Mireille to do? What would you advise Dante to do?
  5. What cultural signals is this situation sending to the rest of the team?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics Scenario
The High Performer

You are the manager of a customer service team. One of your best performers — the employee with the highest customer satisfaction scores — has been making disparaging comments about a colleague to other team members. You have received two complaints from other employees. The behavior is creating tension on the team, but the employee in question does not know you are aware of it.

Questions
  1. What are the ethical issues involved for you as the manager?
  2. What are the consequences — for the team and for the culture — of not addressing the behavior?
  3. How would you approach the conversation with the employee? What would you say?
  4. How does this situation relate to the concept of organizational culture from Section 14.7?
  5. Does the employee's high performance change how you handle the situation? Why or why not?

Chapter 5 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

When someone decides to start a business, one of the first real decisions they face is not what to name it or where to locate it. It is a quieter, more consequential question: How should this business legally exist in the world?

The answer — the ownership structure — shapes almost everything that follows. It determines who controls the business, who receives its profits, who is responsible if something goes wrong, how the business pays taxes, and whether it can survive the departure of its founders.

This chapter explains the major forms of business ownership: sole proprietorships, partnerships, corporations, limited liability companies, cooperatives, franchises, and nonprofits. For each, you will learn how it works, what advantages it offers, what risks or limitations it carries, and what kinds of businesses typically choose it. The chapter closes with a framework for choosing the right structure for a given situation.

Opening Question

Two friends open a bakery together without signing any paperwork. Six months later, the business is thriving — but the friends disagree about whether to expand. One partner wants to hire staff and open a second location; the other wants to stay small. Neither wrote down how decisions would be made. Who is right? Who owes what if the business borrows money and fails to repay it? By the end of this chapter, you will understand why ownership structure is not bureaucratic paperwork — it is the foundation of how a business governs itself and protects the people behind it.

Section 5.1

Why Ownership Structure Matters

Ownership structure is the legal framework governing the relationship between a business, its owners, its workers, its customers, and the broader world. Choosing the wrong structure can expose a founder's personal savings to a lawsuit. Choosing the right one can attract investors, reduce taxes, and allow a business to grow beyond what any single person could manage.

Three Core Questions

Who bears the risk? If the business loses money, owes a debt, or is sued, who is legally responsible? Can the owner's personal assets be taken to settle a business debt?

Who controls decisions? Is one person in charge, or do multiple owners share authority? Can outside investors buy in and gain influence?

How is money handled? How does the business get funded? How are profits distributed? How does it pay taxes?

No Single Best Answer

A freelance web designer has different needs than a restaurant, which has different needs than a hospital, which has different needs than a technology startup seeking venture capital. Understanding the options is the first step toward making a well-reasoned choice.

Section 5.2

Sole Proprietorships

A sole proprietorship is the simplest form of business — owned and operated by one person, with no legal separation between the owner and the business itself. In most places, it requires no formal registration. When a person starts providing a service and accepting payment, they have, in the eyes of most legal systems, formed a sole proprietorship.

FeatureHow It Works in a Sole Proprietorship
FormationNo registration required in most jurisdictions; begins when you start working for pay
LiabilityUnlimited personal liability — personal assets can be used to pay business debts or judgments
TaxationBusiness income reported on owner's personal tax return; no separate business tax filing
ControlComplete; the owner makes all decisions without consulting anyone
ContinuityEnds when the owner dies, retires, or stops working
Capital accessLimited; cannot easily sell ownership stakes to raise outside investment
Unlimited Personal Liability

Because the business and the owner are the same legal entity, there is no protection between business debts and personal assets. If the business is sued and loses, the owner's personal savings, home, and other property can be used to pay the judgment. For any business with meaningful risk of lawsuits or large debts, this is a critical concern.

Section 5.3

Partnerships

A partnership is a business owned by two or more people who share profits, responsibilities, and — in most cases — legal liability. Partnerships can be created informally, but a well-drafted partnership agreement is essential. This document specifies how much each partner contributes, how profits and losses are divided, who makes which decisions, what happens if a partner wants to leave, and how disputes will be resolved.

TypeManagement AuthorityLiabilityCommon Uses
General PartnershipAll partners share management; each can act for the businessUnlimited personal liability for all partners; joint and severalLaw firms, small professional practices
Limited PartnershipGeneral partners manage; limited partners are passive investors onlyGeneral partners: unlimited. Limited partners: limited to their investmentReal estate investment, some professional arrangements
Joint and Several Liability

In a general partnership, if one partner makes a bad decision — signs a risky contract, takes on a loan, causes an accident — the other partners can be held fully and personally responsible too. Each partner is liable for all of the business's obligations, not just their share.

Partnerships Are Like Marriages

Business partnerships require trust, communication, and shared values. Partnerships that begin with enthusiasm often end badly when partners disagree about money, direction, or effort. A formal partnership agreement does not prevent conflict, but it creates a structure for resolving it before it destroys the business.

Section 5.4

Corporations

A corporation is a legal entity that exists separately from its owners. It can own property, enter contracts, borrow money, hire employees, be sued, and pay taxes — all in its own name. The owners of a corporation are called shareholders, who own shares of stock representing their portion of ownership. This separation creates limited liability: shareholders can lose their investment, but their personal assets are generally protected from the corporation's debts and obligations.

How Corporations Are Governed

Shareholders elect a board of directors, which sets the overall direction and oversees major decisions. The board hires executive officers — such as a CEO — to run day-to-day operations. This structure allows corporations to separate ownership from management, making it possible for many people to own shares without running the business.

TypeTaxationShareholder RestrictionsBest For
C CorporationDouble taxation: corporate profits taxed, then dividends taxed again at shareholder levelUnlimited shareholders; foreign investors allowed; multiple share classesLarge businesses, public companies, venture-backed startups
S CorporationPass-through taxation: profits and losses flow to shareholders' personal returnsMax 100 shareholders; U.S. citizens/residents only; one class of stockSmall to mid-size businesses avoiding double taxation
Benefit Corporation (B Corp)Same as C corpLegally required to consider workers, community, environment — not only shareholdersBusinesses wanting social/environmental mission embedded in governance
Double Taxation Explained

A C corporation earns $100,000 in profit. It pays corporate income tax (say, 21%), leaving $79,000. When that $79,000 is distributed to shareholders as dividends, shareholders pay tax again on the dividend income. The same money is taxed twice — once at the corporate level, once at the individual level. S corps and LLCs avoid this by passing income directly to owners' personal tax returns.

Section 5.5

Limited Liability Companies

A limited liability company (LLC) is a hybrid structure combining the liability protection of a corporation with the tax simplicity of a partnership. The owners of an LLC are called members. The LLC exists as a separate legal entity, so members' personal assets are generally protected. Profits and losses pass through directly to members' personal tax returns, avoiding double taxation.

FeatureLLCCorporationSole Prop / Partnership
Personal liability protectionYesYesNo
Pass-through taxationYes (default)No (C corp); Yes (S corp)Yes
Ownership flexibilityHigh — any persons or entitiesLimited (S corp); broad (C corp)High
Management flexibilityHigh — member or manager managedLower — board governance requiredHigh
Administrative complexityLow to moderateModerate to highVery low
The Operating Agreement

LLCs are governed by an operating agreement that describes how the business will be run, how profits are divided, what happens when a member wants to leave, and how decisions are made. Even when the law does not require one, creating an operating agreement is strongly recommended — for the same reasons a partnership agreement matters.

Investor Preference

Some investors — particularly venture capital firms — prefer corporations because LLCs are less suited to the financing structures they use (preferred stock, convertible notes, etc.). A business planning to raise venture capital may need to convert from an LLC to a C corporation at some point.

Section 5.6

Cooperatives and Member-Owned Businesses

A cooperative (or co-op) is a business owned and controlled by its members, who share in its benefits and governance. Unlike a corporation whose goal is typically to maximize returns to shareholders, a cooperative exists to serve the needs of its members. Each member typically has one vote, regardless of investment amount. Profits — called surplus — are distributed based on how much members used or contributed, not how many shares they own.

TypeWho Owns ItPurposeExamples
Consumer CooperativeCustomers / usersProvide goods or services to members at lower cost or higher qualityGrocery co-ops, credit unions, REI
Worker CooperativeEmployeesWorkers own, manage, and share in profits of their workplaceManufacturing firms, legal services, bakeries
Producer CooperativeSmall producersPool resources, processing, and distribution to compete with large businessesAgricultural co-ops, dairy groups
Purchasing CooperativeSmall businessesBuy together to get lower prices than any one business could obtain aloneHardware store buying groups, independent retailers
Section 5.7

Franchises

A franchise is a licensing arrangement in which a business owner (the franchisee) pays for the right to operate under another company's established brand, systems, and support structure. The company granting those rights is the franchisor. When someone buys a franchise, they are buying a proven model, a recognizable name, standardized products or services, training, and an operational system that has already been tested in the market.

PaymentWhat It CoversTypical Amount
Franchise FeeUpfront right to open one location; access to brand and systemsVaries widely — $10,000 to $50,000+ depending on brand
RoyaltiesOngoing fee to use the brand and receive support; typically % of gross sales4–8% of monthly revenue is common
Marketing FeeContribution to the franchisor's national/regional advertising fund1–4% of monthly revenue
Read the Franchise Disclosure Document

Franchise agreements can be heavily tilted in the franchisor's favor. The franchise disclosure document (FDD) is a legally required document that provides detailed information about the franchise system, fees, obligations, and historical performance. Every prospective franchisee should have a lawyer review the FDD before signing anything.

Section 5.8

Nonprofits and Social Enterprises

Nonprofits — also called not-for-profit organizations — are formed to serve a social, educational, religious, scientific, or community purpose. Any financial surplus must be reinvested in the organization's mission rather than distributed to owners. In the U.S., nonprofits qualifying under section 501(c)(3) are exempt from federal income tax and can receive tax-deductible donations.

A social enterprise is an organization using business strategies to achieve a social, environmental, or community purpose. Social enterprises exist on a spectrum: some are nonprofits, others are for-profit businesses structured as LLCs, corporations, or benefit corporations. A coffee shop training people experiencing homelessness, a company selling affordable solar energy to rural households, a grocery store serving a neighborhood with no other food access — these are social enterprises.

FeatureTraditional NonprofitFor-Profit Social EnterpriseBenefit Corporation
Profit distributionNot allowed; surplus reinvested in missionAllowed to owners/investorsAllowed, but social/environmental mission is legally required
Tax statusOften tax-exempt (501c3)Pays taxesPays taxes
Funding sourcesDonations, grants, program revenueRevenue, investmentRevenue, investment
GovernanceVolunteer board accountable to missionOwners/investorsBoard required to consider stakeholders beyond shareholders
Nonprofits Can Pay Competitive Salaries

The "nonprofit" designation means profits are not distributed to owners — not that the organization cannot pay its staff market-rate salaries. Nonprofits do have paid employees, and executive compensation can be substantial in larger organizations.

Section 5.9

Choosing a Business Structure

After reviewing the major ownership forms, a question remains: How does someone actually choose? There is no single right answer. The best structure depends on a combination of factors specific to each business and its founders.

FactorKey Questions to AskImplications
Liability ExposureDoes the business involve lawsuits, injuries, or large debts? Does it employ workers or serve the public?Any significant risk → avoid sole proprietorship; choose LLC or corporation
Number of OwnersSolo? Two people? Large group? Outside investors?Solo → sole prop or single-member LLC. Multiple → partnership or LLC. Outside investors → often corporation
Tax ConsiderationsIs double taxation a concern? Are there reasons to prefer pass-through vs. corporate taxation?Pass-through → sole prop, partnership, LLC, S corp. Corporate tax → C corp. Complex; consult a tax professional.
Growth PlansWill the business hire staff, open multiple locations, or seek investment?Ambitious growth → build structure for the future, not just today. Venture capital → typically requires C corp.
Administrative ComplexityHow much paperwork, governance, and compliance is acceptable?Corporations most complex. LLCs moderate. Sole props and partnerships simplest.
Mission and ValuesIs social purpose central? Do you want shared worker ownership?Social purpose → consider nonprofit, social enterprise, or benefit corp. Shared ownership → cooperative.
A Practical Path

Most business advisors suggest three steps when starting a new venture:

Step 1. Talk to a business attorney and a tax professional early — before making a final decision.

Step 2. Start with the simplest structure providing adequate liability protection — often a single-member LLC.

Step 3. Revisit the structure as the business grows and its needs change. Switching later is possible, though it involves administrative and sometimes tax complexity.

Chapter 5 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 5.1Ownership structure answers three core questions: who bears risk, who controls decisions, and how money is handled. No single structure is best for every situation.
  • Sec 5.2Sole proprietorships are simple and give owners full control, but offer no personal liability protection and limit growth. They are the default starting point for individuals working independently.
  • Sec 5.3General partnerships expose all partners to unlimited personal liability and require a strong partnership agreement. Limited partnerships allow passive investors with limited liability alongside managing general partners.
  • Sec 5.4Corporations are separate legal entities with limited liability for shareholders. C corps face double taxation; S corps allow pass-through taxation with restrictions; benefit corps embed stakeholder accountability into governance.
  • Sec 5.5LLCs combine liability protection with pass-through taxation and flexible management. They are popular with small to medium-sized businesses and are governed by an operating agreement.
  • Sec 5.6Cooperatives are owned and governed by their members — consumers, workers, or producers. Democratic governance and surplus distribution make them distinct from investor-owned businesses.
  • Sec 5.7Franchises offer entrepreneurs a path to ownership through a proven brand and system, at the cost of independence and ongoing royalty payments. The franchise disclosure document must be reviewed carefully.
  • Sec 5.8Nonprofits serve social or community purposes and cannot distribute profit to owners. Social enterprises pursue both financial and social goals across a spectrum from nonprofit to benefit corporation.
  • Sec 5.9Choosing a structure requires weighing liability exposure, number of owners, tax treatment, growth plans, administrative complexity, and mission alignment. Early legal and tax advice is strongly recommended.

Key Terms Review

sole proprietorship
A business owned and operated by one person, with no legal distinction between the owner and the business.
unlimited personal liability
Legal exposure in which an owner's personal assets can be used to pay business debts or satisfy legal judgments.
partnership
A business owned by two or more people who share management, profits, and liability.
general partnership
A partnership in which all partners share management authority and full personal liability for business obligations.
limited partnership
A partnership with at least one general partner (full liability) and at least one limited partner (liability limited to their investment).
joint and several liability
Legal principle in which each general partner can be held fully responsible for all of the partnership's debts.
corporation
A legal entity that exists separately from its owners, providing limited liability to shareholders.
shareholders
The owners of a corporation, who hold shares of stock representing their portion of ownership.
board of directors
A group elected by shareholders to oversee a corporation's direction and governance.
limited liability
Legal protection that shields owners' personal assets from business debts and legal judgments.
double taxation
The situation in which corporate income is taxed at the corporate level and again when distributed to shareholders as dividends.
C corporation
A standard corporation subject to corporate income tax; can have unlimited shareholders of any type.
S corporation
A corporation with pass-through taxation and restrictions: max 100 U.S. shareholders, one class of stock.
benefit corporation
A corporation legally required to consider the interests of workers, customers, the community, and the environment, not only shareholders.
limited liability company
A hybrid business structure combining liability protection with pass-through taxation and flexible management.
members
The owners of an LLC.
operating agreement
A document governing the management, profit-sharing, and decision-making rules of an LLC.
cooperative
A business owned and governed by its members — consumers, workers, or producers — who share in its benefits and decision-making.
surplus
The cooperative equivalent of profit, distributed to members based on use or contribution rather than investment.
franchise
A licensing arrangement in which a franchisee pays for the right to operate under a franchisor's brand and systems.
franchisee
The individual or business that purchases a franchise license and operates the location.
franchisor
The company that grants franchise rights to franchisees in exchange for fees and adherence to its standards.
royalties
Ongoing fees paid by a franchisee to a franchisor, typically calculated as a percentage of monthly sales.
nonprofit
An organization that exists to serve a social, community, educational, or charitable purpose and does not distribute profits to owners.
social enterprise
An organization that uses business methods to pursue social or environmental goals, operating across the spectrum from nonprofit to for-profit.

Business Math Practice

Problem 1
Franchise Economics

A franchise agreement requires a $35,000 upfront franchise fee and ongoing royalties of 6% of gross monthly sales. If a franchisee generates $48,000 in monthly sales, what is the monthly royalty payment? Over a full year, how much will the franchisee have paid in royalties? What is the combined first-year cost of the franchise fee plus royalties?

Problem 2
Partnership Profit Distribution

Three partners in a general partnership agree to split profits 50% / 30% / 20% according to their relative contributions. In the first year, the business earns a net profit of $84,000. How much does each partner receive? If the business instead suffers a loss of $24,000, how much does each partner absorb under the same split?

Problem 3
Double Taxation Example

A C corporation earns $200,000 in profit. It pays a 21% corporate tax rate on that amount. It then distributes the remaining after-tax profit as dividends to its sole shareholder, who pays a 15% qualified dividend tax rate. Calculate: (a) the corporate tax owed; (b) the after-tax profit available for distribution; (c) the tax owed by the shareholder on the dividend; and (d) the total combined tax paid as a percentage of the original $200,000 profit.

Case Study

Case Study
Three Friends, Three Paths

Kenji, Marisol, and Dayo all decide to start businesses in the same year. Each makes a different ownership decision.

Kenji starts a freelance video editing service on his own. He does not register a business entity. Within a year, he has several steady clients and is earning enough to support himself. His tax preparation is simple, and he enjoys making all his own decisions.

Marisol and her college friend open a catering company together. They shake hands on a 50/50 split and get to work. Six months in, they have a successful business — but they also have a disagreement about whether to hire employees and expand. Neither of them wrote down what would happen if they wanted to go different directions.

Dayo forms a single-member LLC for her online shop selling handmade ceramics. She pays a registration fee and drafts a simple operating agreement. She knows that if something goes wrong — a customer slips at a craft fair, a package damages someone's property — her personal savings are protected.

DiscussionCase Questions
  1. What are the main risks Kenji faces by operating as a sole proprietor? What steps might he take as his business grows?
  2. What could Marisol and her partner have done before starting their business to protect both the business and their friendship? What document would have helped most?
  3. Why did Dayo choose an LLC rather than a sole proprietorship, even though she is the only owner? What did the registration fee and operating agreement accomplish?
  4. If all three businesses grew significantly over five years — Kenji to a ten-person agency, Marisol's catering company to multiple locations, and Dayo's ceramics shop to a wholesale brand — what ownership structures might they each consider at that point, and why?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics Scenario

A small software company is organized as an LLC. One of its two members has been contributing less and less to the business over the past year while drawing the same salary and share of profits as the other member. The more active member wants to change the profit distribution arrangement but discovers that the operating agreement they drafted two years ago does not address this situation clearly.

Consider: What ethical obligations do business partners have to one another regarding contribution and compensation? What practical lesson does this situation illustrate about operating agreements? If you were advising both members on how to resolve this situation, what would you suggest?

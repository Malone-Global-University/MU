Chapter 4 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every business makes choices — about how to treat customers, how to handle information, how to behave toward workers and communities, and how to respond when rules are unclear. Some of those choices are governed by law. Many are not. Ethics is the practice of making good choices when rules alone are not enough.

This chapter introduces ethical decision-making in business. By the end of the chapter, you should understand that legal behavior and ethical behavior are related but not identical, that trust and reputation are central to long-term business success, and that businesses operate within communities and ecosystems that they affect and depend upon. Entrepreneurship requires ethical judgment from the beginning — not as an afterthought.

Opening Question

A pharmacist discovers that a batch of a medication she has already sold to customers may have a defect. Contacting all 200 customers and replacing the product will cost $1,200. Doing nothing might never be discovered — or it might result in harm, lawsuits, and a destroyed reputation. No law at this moment requires her to act. What should she do? By the end of this chapter, you will have several frameworks for thinking through questions like this — and an understanding of why the answer matters far beyond any single decision.

Section 4.1

What Business Ethics Means

Ethics is the study of right and wrong conduct. In business, ethics asks a deceptively simple question: What is the right thing to do, even when no one is forcing you to do it?

Business decisions affect people. When a company prices a product, hires an employee, handles a complaint, or chooses a supplier, it is making choices that ripple outward. Ethical business behavior means making those choices with honesty, fairness, and genuine care for the people involved — not merely because doing so is required, but because it is right.

Business ethics is not a checklist. It is a habit of asking: Is this right? Who does this affect? Could this cause harm? Would I be comfortable if this decision were made public?

Key Distinction

Ethics is broader than legality. Something can be legal but still wrong. A practice can be technically permitted and still harm people, damage trust, or damage a community. Good business ethics means going beyond "Is it legal?" to ask "Is it right?"

Why Ethics Matters to Business

  • Customers trust businesses that treat them honestly — and return to them
  • Employees work harder and stay longer in ethical workplaces
  • Suppliers and partners prefer to work with businesses that keep their word
  • Communities support businesses that behave responsibly
  • Reputation, once damaged, is difficult and expensive to repair
A Practical Point

Ethics is not only a moral obligation — it is also practical. Over the long run, ethical businesses tend to outperform unethical ones. Trust, loyalty, and reputation are real assets. A business that routinely cuts ethical corners may gain in the short term; it tends to lose badly over time.

Section 4.2

Laws, Rules, and Ethical Judgment

Laws are rules that governments create and enforce. Regulations are rules set by government agencies. Business policies are rules created by companies themselves. All three shape business behavior — but none of them can cover every situation a business faces.

Legal and Ethical Legal but Unethical Illegal but Debated Illegal and Unethical
Delivering what you promise Charging hidden fees that are technically disclosed in fine print Whistleblowing in violation of an NDA Fraud, false advertising
Paying employees on time Exploiting legal loopholes to avoid fair obligations Civil disobedience Environmental dumping

Laws are written in advance and cannot anticipate every situation. New technologies create scenarios that existing laws do not cover. Laws vary across states, countries, and industries. And enforcement is imperfect — many harmful behaviors are never caught or prosecuted.

Businesses that wait to be told exactly what they must do are vulnerable. Businesses that develop genuine ethical judgment — an internal sense of what is right — are better positioned to handle new challenges, earn lasting trust, and avoid costly mistakes.

For Entrepreneurs

When you build a business from the beginning, you set the ethical tone. The practices, habits, and expectations you establish early become the culture of the organization. It is far easier to start with good habits than to correct bad ones after they have become ingrained.

Section 4.3

Honesty, Trust, and Reputation

Honesty in business means accurately representing your products, prices, practices, and intentions. It means telling customers what they need to know to make good decisions, following through on commitments, and admitting mistakes rather than hiding them.

Trust is the belief that a business will do what it says it will do. Trust takes a long time to build and a short time to destroy. Reputation is how a business is known and regarded by customers, employees, suppliers, investors, and the public — built through repeated behavior over time, not through a single statement.

Common Forms of Dishonesty in Business

  • Exaggerating the benefits of a product or service
  • Hiding fees, terms, or limitations in fine print
  • Making promises that cannot be kept
  • Misrepresenting qualifications or track record
  • Using misleading statistics or selective comparisons
  • Staying silent about known problems with a product
Important

None of these require active lying. Misleading someone through omission — by leaving out important information — is still dishonest. The standard for honesty is not "Did I technically say anything false?" but "Did I give this person what they needed to make a good decision?"

Case Principle

A customer asks a salesperson whether a product has a feature it does not have. The salesperson could deflect, mislead, or stay quiet. An honest salesperson says: "No, it doesn't — but here is what it does do." That answer may lose one sale. It often builds a long-term relationship worth far more.

Section 4.4

Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest occurs when a person's personal interests could interfere with their responsibilities to a business, customer, or other party. The danger is that personal benefit can distort judgment — sometimes without the person even realizing it.

Examples of Conflicts of Interest

  • A purchasing manager who selects a supplier owned by a family member
  • A financial advisor who recommends investments that pay higher commissions, regardless of whether they are best for the client
  • A hiring manager who favors friends or relatives for open positions
  • A contractor who steers a client toward services the client does not need, because the contractor profits from those services
  • An employee who starts a competing business while still employed elsewhere
Managing Conflicts of Interest

Disclose — When a conflict exists, tell the relevant parties before making any decision.

Recuse — Remove yourself from decisions where you have a personal stake in the outcome.

Establish clear policies — Businesses should have explicit rules about common conflict situations before they arise.

The test: Would this decision look different if my personal interest were not involved? If yes, the conflict needs to be addressed.

Section 4.5

Fair Treatment of Customers and Employees

Business ethics requires treating people fairly — not just following minimum rules, but genuinely respecting the dignity and interests of everyone a business touches.

Fair Treatment of CustomersFair Treatment of Employees
Providing accurate, complete information before a sale Paying agreed wages on time
Honoring stated prices, terms, and commitments Providing a safe working environment
Handling complaints promptly and honestly Not discriminating on the basis of protected characteristics
Offering refunds or solutions when things go wrong Communicating expectations clearly
Not targeting vulnerable populations with harmful practices Following through on commitments made during hiring

There is a well-documented connection between how a business treats its employees and how those employees treat customers. Workers who feel valued tend to provide better service. Workers who feel disrespected, underpaid, or mistreated often show those feelings in their work. Ethical treatment of employees is not just internally good — it affects customers, quality, and reputation too.

Section 4.6

Privacy, Data, and Responsible Use of Information

Modern businesses collect large amounts of information about customers, employees, and operations. That information creates both opportunities and responsibilities.

People share information with businesses in order to receive a product or service. They do not necessarily expect that information to be sold, shared with third parties, used in ways they did not anticipate, or stored indefinitely with inadequate protection.

What Ethical Data Practices Look Like

Collect only what you need for the purpose at hand.

Protect what you collect with appropriate security measures.

Use data only for purposes customers reasonably expect.

Be transparent about what you collect and how you use it.

Delete data when it is no longer needed.

Notify people promptly and honestly if a breach occurs.

Key Question

Would customers be comfortable if they knew exactly how their data is being collected, stored, and used? If the answer is no, the practice needs to change — not the communication about it.

For Entrepreneurs

Data practices established early tend to persist. Building responsible data habits from the beginning — clear privacy policies, minimal collection, strong security, honest communication — is far easier than retrofitting them later, especially after a breach or a regulatory action.

Section 4.7

Environmental and Community Responsibility

Businesses do not operate in isolation. They exist within communities, ecosystems, and physical environments. Their decisions — about what they produce, how they produce it, where they locate, and how they dispose of materials — affect people and places beyond their immediate customers and employees.

What Environmental Responsibility Includes

  • Managing waste, emissions, and pollution responsibly
  • Minimizing unnecessary resource consumption
  • Considering the environmental impact of sourcing and supply chain decisions
  • Being honest about environmental impacts rather than hiding or misrepresenting them

What Community Responsibility Includes

  • Treating local workers and vendors fairly
  • Being a good neighbor in terms of noise, traffic, and appearance
  • Considering the impact of decisions on local housing, wages, and quality of life
  • Engaging honestly with community concerns rather than dismissing them
Key Concept — Externality

An externality is a cost or benefit that falls on parties outside a transaction. When a factory produces goods and sells them to customers but also pollutes the air and water, the pollution is an externality — a cost borne by others who did not choose to bear it. Ethical businesses try to minimize negative externalities rather than simply externalizing costs onto others.

Section 4.8

Stakeholders and Shareholders

Shareholders are people who own stock in a corporation. As owners, they have a financial interest in the business's success. For many years, a common view held that the primary obligation of a business was to maximize returns to shareholders.

Stakeholders is a broader term. A stakeholder is anyone who has an interest in, or is affected by, a business's decisions.

Who Stakeholders AreTheir Interest
CustomersHonest products, fair prices, reliable service
EmployeesFair wages, safe conditions, dignity, development
Suppliers and vendorsReliable payment, fair contracts, honest dealing
Investors and ownersReturn on investment, transparency, sound management
Local communitiesJobs, safety, responsible operations, civic contribution
Government and regulatorsLegal compliance, tax payments, fair competition
The natural environmentReduced pollution, responsible resource use

The traditional shareholder-first view holds that a business's job is to generate profit for owners, and that this will naturally benefit others through jobs, taxes, and goods. Critics argue that this view can justify decisions that harm workers, communities, or the environment as long as profits increase.

The stakeholder view holds that businesses have genuine obligations to all the parties they affect, and that long-term success depends on satisfying multiple stakeholders — not just shareholders. In 2019, the Business Roundtable, a group of CEOs of major U.S. corporations, issued a statement redefining the purpose of a corporation to include delivering value to customers, investing in employees, dealing fairly with suppliers, supporting communities, and generating long-term value for shareholders.

Note on the 2019 Statement

The Business Roundtable statement represented a notable shift in official business language. Debate continues about whether the actual behavior of large corporations has changed as much as the language of the statement suggests. Watching the gap between what businesses say and what they do is an important analytical skill.

Section 4.9

Ethical Decision-Making Frameworks

When ethical questions arise, it helps to have frameworks for thinking through them carefully. No single framework is perfect. Used together, they provide multiple perspectives on a difficult decision — which is exactly when multiple perspectives are most valuable.

Framework 1
The Consequences Approach (Consequentialism)

Ask: What outcome will this decision produce? Who will benefit and who will be harmed? What is the likely effect on the greatest number of people?

This approach focuses on results. A decision is good if it produces more benefit than harm overall, and if that benefit is distributed reasonably widely.

Limitation: It can be difficult to predict consequences accurately. And focusing only on outcomes can sometimes justify harmful means to reach a desired end.

Framework 2
The Duties and Rights Approach (Deontological Ethics)

Ask: Does this decision respect the rights and dignity of the people involved? Are there duties or obligations that apply here, regardless of the outcome?

This approach holds that some actions are right or wrong independent of their consequences. People have rights — to honest information, fair treatment, and privacy — and those rights must be respected even when violating them might produce better aggregate outcomes.

Limitation: Duties can conflict with each other, and this framework does not always provide guidance when they do.

Framework 3
The Virtue Ethics Approach

Ask: What would a person of good character do? What kind of person or business does this decision reflect?

This approach focuses on character and integrity rather than rules or outcomes. It asks not just "Is this permitted?" but "Is this who we want to be?" A business with genuine virtues — honesty, fairness, courage, care — will tend to make better decisions over time than a business that simply complies with rules.

Limitation: What counts as virtuous can vary across cultures and individuals, and this framework offers less specific guidance in novel situations.

Framework 4
The Fairness Approach

Ask: If I did not know which role I would play in this situation — customer, employee, supplier, community member — would I still consider this decision fair?

This approach, inspired by philosopher John Rawls, encourages decision-makers to consider fairness from behind a "veil of ignorance" — imagining that you do not know your own position in the outcome before deciding whether the outcome is just.

Limitation: Real decisions are made by people who do know their position, so applying this framework requires deliberate effort and imagination.

A Practical Test — The Newspaper Test

Imagine that a journalist is writing a story about this decision. Would you be comfortable seeing it on the front page? A useful variation asks the opposite: Would you also be uncomfortable if a journalist wrote about your failure to act? Both directions of the test matter.

When Frameworks Conflict

When all frameworks point in the same direction, confidence in a decision is higher. When they conflict, that is a signal the situation is genuinely complex — and that careful consultation, transparency, and humility are needed before acting. Disagreement among frameworks is a reason to slow down, not a reason to pick whichever framework justifies what you already wanted to do.

The Eight-Step Ethical Decision Process

  1. Identify the decision that needs to be made and who is authorized to make it.
  2. Identify all the parties who will be affected by the decision — including parties who are not in the room.
  3. Gather all relevant information; resist the temptation to decide before you understand the situation.
  4. Apply multiple ethical frameworks to the decision, noting where they agree and where they conflict.
  5. Pay special attention to the most vulnerable parties — those with the least power to protect themselves.
  6. Consult with others who have relevant knowledge, perspective, or stake in the outcome.
  7. Make a decision and be prepared to explain your reasoning clearly and honestly.
  8. Reflect on the outcome afterward: What happened? What would you do differently? What did you learn?
For Entrepreneurs

Ethical decisions often need to be made quickly, under pressure, with incomplete information. Developing a habit of asking ethical questions — not just when a crisis arrives, but as part of daily operations — makes good ethical judgment more automatic and less dependent on having enough time to run through a full process.

Chapter 4 — Back Matter

Chapter Review

  • Sec 4.1Ethics asks what is right even when no rule requires it. Ethical behavior and legal behavior are related but not identical. Trust, loyalty, and reputation are genuine business assets built through consistent ethical conduct.
  • Sec 4.2Legal compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. Rules cannot anticipate every situation; genuine ethical judgment fills the gaps. Founders set ethical tone from the beginning, and early habits become lasting culture.
  • Sec 4.3Honesty means giving people what they need to make good decisions — not just avoiding literal falsehoods. Trust takes years to build and moments to destroy. For new ventures, early trust is a competitive advantage.
  • Sec 4.4A conflict of interest arises when personal interest could distort judgment about a business or client decision. Management requires disclosure, recusal, and clear policies — not just awareness.
  • Sec 4.5Fair treatment of customers and employees is both morally right and practically valuable. How workers are treated shapes how they serve customers. Retention of both customers and employees depends heavily on perceived fairness.
  • Sec 4.6Data privacy is a responsibility, not only a legal requirement. Ethical data practice means collecting minimally, protecting carefully, using transparently, and communicating honestly when things go wrong.
  • Sec 4.7Businesses produce externalities — costs or benefits borne by parties outside a transaction. Environmental and community responsibilities arise from the fact that business decisions affect more people than just customers and owners.
  • Sec 4.8Stakeholders are all parties affected by a business, not just shareholders. Long-term shareholder value tends to depend on the trust and cooperation of customers, employees, suppliers, and communities — making stakeholder thinking practically necessary.
  • Sec 4.9Four frameworks — consequentialism, deontological ethics, virtue ethics, and fairness — each provide a different lens. When they agree, confidence is higher. When they conflict, the decision deserves more caution. An eight-step process structures ethical reasoning in practice.

Key Terms Review

ethics
The study of right and wrong conduct; in business, the practice of making decisions with honesty, fairness, and care for those affected — not only when required by rules.
legal compliance
Meeting the minimum requirements set by law. The floor of acceptable behavior, not the ceiling; ethical behavior often requires more.
conflict of interest
A situation in which a person's personal interests could interfere with their responsibility to a business, customer, or other party — distorting judgment even without intent.
stakeholder
Anyone who has an interest in, or is affected by, a business's decisions: customers, employees, suppliers, communities, investors, regulators, and others.
shareholder
A person who owns stock in a corporation; one specific type of stakeholder with a financial ownership interest.
externality
A cost or benefit that falls on parties outside a transaction who did not choose to bear it — such as pollution borne by a community from a nearby factory.
privacy
The right of individuals to control what personal information is collected about them, how it is used, and who has access to it.
reputation
How a business is regarded by customers, employees, suppliers, investors, and the public; built through repeated behavior over time, not through statements alone.
consequentialism
An ethical framework that evaluates decisions based on their outcomes and the overall balance of benefit and harm produced.
deontological ethics
An ethical framework that evaluates decisions based on duties, rights, and obligations — independent of outcomes. Some things are right or wrong regardless of consequences.
virtue ethics
An ethical framework that focuses on the character and integrity of the decision-maker: not just "Is this permitted?" but "Is this who we want to be?"

Business Math Practice

Scenario — The Defective Batch

A small business discovers that a product batch may have a defect affecting approximately 200 units already sold. Proactively contacting all 200 customers and replacing the affected units will cost $800 in staff time and $400 in replacement parts — a total of $1,200.

If the business does nothing, there is an estimated 1-in-10 chance that a customer will be harmed. If a customer is harmed, the estimated cost of legal liability, settlement, and reputation damage is $45,000.

  1. What is the total cost of proactive notification and replacement?
  2. What is the expected cost of inaction? (Hint: multiply the probability of harm by the cost if harm occurs.)
  3. Based on the expected values, which choice is financially better for the business?
  4. What does this calculation suggest about the relationship between ethics and business outcomes?
  5. Are there reasons to act ethically even if the numbers pointed the other direction? Explain your reasoning.

Case Study

Case Study 4.1
The Pricing Decision — Miriam's Pharmacy

Miriam runs a small pharmacy in a rural town. She is the only pharmacy within 30 miles. A pharmaceutical supplier offers her a specialty medication needed by approximately 15 patients in the area. The medication costs her $18 per unit. There is no local competition.

She is deciding what price to charge. A 30% markup would put the price at $23.40. A 200% markup would put it at $54. The manufacturer's suggested retail price is $28.

  1. What are the competing considerations in Miriam's pricing decision?
  2. Which stakeholders are affected by each pricing option?
  3. How would each ethical framework — consequentialism, duties and rights, virtue ethics, and fairness — evaluate the three pricing options?
  4. What price would you recommend, and why?
  5. Does Miriam's status as the only local pharmacy change her ethical obligations compared to a business with many competitors? Explain your reasoning.

Ethics or Decision Point

Decision Point 4.1
The Misleading Advertisement

You are the owner of a small marketing agency. One of your top clients asks you to create advertising content that is technically accurate but designed to mislead customers about a competitor's product — using real but selectively chosen statistics to make the competitor look worse than it actually is. The client offers to pay a 25% premium for the work.

  1. Is this assignment legal? Does that settle the ethical question?
  2. Who could be harmed by this decision — and who bears those harms?
  3. What are the risks to your own business if you accept this assignment?
  4. What would you do, and how would you explain your decision to the client?

Applied Practice

Choose a business you are familiar with — a local business, a business you have worked for, or a well-known company. Research or observe one specific ethical decision it has made or currently faces, in any of the following areas: customer treatment, employee practices, data use, environmental impact, or community relationships.

Write a one-page analysis that addresses: what the ethical issue is; who the stakeholders are and how they are affected; what the business did or is doing; how you would evaluate that decision using at least two ethical frameworks; and what you would recommend, with reasons. Be honest and balanced. Most businesses will be strong in some areas and weaker in others.

Entrepreneurship Portfolio Checkpoint

Legal and Ethical Risk Checklist — Draft
Portfolio Item: Chapter 4 contribution — to be refined in Chapter 8 and Chapter 28
Instructions

For your business idea, identify at least five potential ethical risks or decisions your business might face. Use the table structure below for each item.

Checklist Structure
  1. Name the ethical issue or risk (e.g., "Pricing for customers with limited alternatives," "Handling customer data from online orders").
  2. Identify who is affected and how they could be harmed if the issue is handled poorly.
  3. Describe how you would handle this situation — what policies, practices, or habits would you put in place?
  4. Note any legal requirements that apply to this issue (even if you are not yet sure of the details).
  5. Identify which ethical framework(s) are most relevant to this particular issue and why.
Required Items to Include
  1. One issue related to how you will treat customers — including honest communication about your product or service.
  2. One issue related to how you will handle any personal data you collect from customers or users.
  3. One issue related to how you will treat any workers, contractors, or partners your business depends on.
  4. One issue related to environmental or community impact, if relevant to your business type.
  5. One issue specific to your business concept that does not fit neatly into the above categories.

Review Questions

  1. What is the difference between legal behavior and ethical behavior? Give an example of each.
  2. Why is trust considered a business asset? Describe how trust is built and how it is damaged.
  3. What is a conflict of interest? Give one specific example from a business context.
  4. Who are stakeholders? How does stakeholder thinking differ from shareholder-only thinking?
  5. What is an externality? Give one example of a negative externality a business might produce.

Short Answer Questions

  1. A customer gives your business a five-star review after you resolved a complaint. A staff member suggests asking that same customer to give identical reviews on three other review platforms. Is this ethical? Explain your reasoning using at least one framework.
  2. Your business collects customer email addresses for order confirmations. A partner business asks for your customer list for their own marketing campaigns. Using the privacy framework from Section 4.6, how would you evaluate this request?
  3. Describe a situation in which all three frameworks — consequentialism, deontological ethics, and virtue ethics — would point toward the same decision. Then describe a situation where they might conflict.

Discussion Questions

  1. Is it possible for a business to be "too ethical" — to apply ethical standards so strictly that it cannot survive or compete in its industry? What do you think, and why?
  2. Some businesses advertise that they are "committed to sustainability" or "socially responsible" without making meaningful changes to their practices. This is sometimes called "greenwashing." How should customers, competitors, and regulators respond to this behavior?
  3. The text argues that ethical businesses tend to outperform unethical ones over the long run. Do you believe this is true? What evidence or examples from your own observation support or challenge this claim?
Mini ProjectThe Ethics Audit

Select a real company of any size or industry. Using publicly available information — their website, news articles, company reports, and customer reviews — write a brief ethics audit covering the five categories below. For each category, give a rating (strong, adequate, concerning, or unknown) and a one- to two-sentence explanation. Be honest and balanced: most companies will be strong in some areas and weaker in others.

  1. Customer treatment: Does the company represent its products honestly? Does it handle complaints responsibly and openly?
  2. Employee treatment: What can you learn about wages, working conditions, and workplace culture from available sources?
  3. Data practices: What does the company's privacy policy say? Does it appear reasonable and understandable to a non-expert?
  4. Environmental practices: Does the company acknowledge its environmental impacts? What specific actions does it describe?
  5. Community engagement: Does the company interact responsibly with the communities it operates in? How do you know?
Chapter ReflectionPersonal Ethics Connection

Think about a time when you faced an ethical decision in your own life — not necessarily in business, but in any context. You may also think about a situation you observed. Write a short paragraph (three to five sentences) describing: what the situation was; what made it ethically difficult; what you did or what was done; and what you would do if you faced a similar situation now, having read this chapter.

You do not need to share this publicly if you choose not to. The purpose is to connect the ideas in this chapter to your own experience and judgment — the most durable foundation for ethical decision-making.

BM–1

Glossary of Business Terms

Terms are listed alphabetically. Each definition emphasizes the practical meaning used in this textbook. For further elaboration on any term, consult the chapter listed in the Index.

Accounts payable
Money a business owes to suppliers or vendors for goods and services already received but not yet paid for.
Accounts receivable
Money owed to a business by customers who have received goods or services but have not yet paid.
Assets
Everything a business owns or controls that has economic value, including cash, equipment, inventory, and property.
Balance sheet
A financial statement showing a business's assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time.
Bootstrapping
Starting and growing a business using only personal savings and operating revenue, without outside investors or loans.
Bottom line
A business's net profit or net loss after all revenues and expenses are accounted for.
Brand
The identity, reputation, and associations that customers connect to a business, product, or service.
Break-even point
The level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs, producing neither profit nor loss.
Business model
The plan for how a business creates value, delivers it to customers, and earns revenue from that value.
Business plan
A written document describing a business's goals, strategies, market, operations, team, and financial projections.
Capital
Money or assets available to start or grow a business.
Cash flow
The movement of money into and out of a business over a period of time.
Competitive advantage
A characteristic or capability that allows a business to outperform competitors in ways that are difficult to copy.
Contribution margin
The amount of revenue remaining after variable costs are subtracted; contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.
Cooperative (co-op)
A business owned and governed by its members, who share in the benefits and decision-making.
Corporation
A legal business structure that exists as a separate legal entity from its owners, offering limited liability and the ability to raise capital by selling shares.
Cost of goods sold
The direct costs of producing the goods or services a business sells. Abbreviated COGS.
Crowdfunding
Raising small amounts of money from many people, typically through online platforms, to fund a project or business.
Customer segment
A defined group of customers who share similar needs, behaviors, or characteristics.
Debt financing
Raising money by borrowing, with an obligation to repay the amount plus interest.
Differentiation
Making a product or service meaningfully distinct from competitors in ways that customers value.
Entrepreneur
A person who identifies an opportunity, takes on risk, and organizes resources to build a business or venture.
Equity
The ownership interest in a business; calculated as assets minus liabilities.
Equity financing
Raising money by selling ownership shares in a business.
Expenses
The costs a business incurs to operate and generate revenue.
Fixed costs
Costs that remain constant regardless of how many units a business produces or sells (e.g., rent, salaries).
Franchise
A business arrangement in which an owner pays for the right to operate under an established brand and system.
Gross margin
Revenue minus the cost of goods sold, expressed as a dollar amount or percentage.
Gross profit
Total revenue minus cost of goods sold.
Income statement
A financial statement showing revenue, expenses, and profit or loss over a period of time. Also called a P&L.
Intellectual property
Creations of the mind protected by law, including patents, trademarks, copyrights, and trade secrets.
Intrapreneurship
Entrepreneurial activity carried out within an existing organization by employees who identify and develop new opportunities.
Inventory
Goods held by a business for sale or use in production.
Key performance indicator
A measurable value used to evaluate how effectively a business is achieving its objectives. Abbreviated KPI.
Launch
The process of making a product, service, or business available to customers for the first time.
Liabilities
Amounts a business owes to others, including loans, accounts payable, and other financial obligations.
Limited liability company
A flexible business structure that combines limited personal liability with simplified tax treatment. Abbreviated LLC.
Logistics
The planning and management of the movement of goods, services, and information from origin to customer.
Market
A space, physical or virtual, where buyers and sellers exchange goods, services, or information.
Market research
The systematic process of gathering and analyzing information about customers, competitors, and market conditions.
Marketing mix
The combination of product, price, place, and promotion decisions used to reach and serve a target market.
Markup
The amount added to the cost of a product to arrive at its selling price.
Minimum viable product
The simplest version of a product that can be tested with real customers to gather useful feedback. Abbreviated MVP.
Mission statement
A brief declaration of a business's core purpose and the value it exists to create.
Net profit
The amount remaining after all expenses, including taxes, are subtracted from total revenue.
Nonprofit organization
An organization that uses surplus revenue to advance its mission rather than distributing it to owners or shareholders.
Operating costs
The ongoing expenses required to run a business day-to-day.
Operations
The systems and processes a business uses to produce and deliver its goods or services.
Opportunity cost
The value of the next best alternative given up when a choice is made.
Overhead
Indirect costs of running a business not directly tied to production (e.g., administrative salaries, utilities).
Partnership
A business owned by two or more people who share responsibilities, profits, and liabilities.
Persona
A detailed, semi-fictional profile of a target customer based on research and data.
Pivot
A deliberate change in a business's strategy, product, or market direction based on new information or feedback.
Positioning
How a business chooses to be perceived in customers' minds relative to competitors.
Profit
The financial gain remaining after all costs are subtracted from revenue.
Prototype
An early, testable version of a product or service used to gather feedback and inform further development.
Revenue
The total amount of money a business earns from selling goods or services before expenses are deducted.
Revenue model
The specific method a business uses to generate income from its customers.
Risk
The possibility of loss, failure, or an outcome that differs from what was expected or planned.
Scaling
Growing a business in a way that increases revenue faster than costs, often through repeatable systems and processes.
Scarcity
The condition in which resources are limited relative to unlimited wants, forcing tradeoffs and choices.
Social enterprise
A business or organization that pursues social, environmental, or community goals alongside or instead of profit.
Sole proprietorship
A business owned and operated by one person, with no legal separation between the owner and the business.
Startup costs
One-time expenses incurred to launch a new business before it begins generating revenue.
Supply chain
The network of suppliers, producers, distributors, and retailers involved in creating and delivering a product.
Target market
The specific group of customers a business intends to serve.
Value proposition
A clear statement of the specific benefit a product or service delivers to a particular customer and why it is better than alternatives.
Variable costs
Costs that change in direct proportion to production volume or sales activity.
Venture capital
Investment from professional investors who provide capital to high-growth startups in exchange for equity.
Vision statement
A description of the long-term future a business is working toward.
Working capital
The difference between current assets and current liabilities; a measure of short-term financial health.

Business Math Reference

The formulas below are used throughout this textbook, primarily in Chapters 22–25. Each formula is presented with a plain-language description and, where helpful, a worked example. Use this section as a quick reference during problem sets, checkpoints, and portfolio work.

Section A
Profit and Loss
Gross Profit
Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold
Net Profit
Net Profit = Revenue − Total Expenses
Profit Margin (%)
Profit Margin = (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
Section B
Markup and Margin
Markup (added to cost)
Selling Price = Cost × (1 + Markup %)

Example: Cost = $40, Markup = 50% → Selling Price = $40 × 1.50 = $60

Gross Margin (%)
Gross Margin = ((Selling Price − Cost) ÷ Selling Price) × 100

Example: Selling Price = $60, Cost = $40 → Gross Margin = ($20 ÷ $60) × 100 = 33.3%

Markup vs. Margin

Markup is calculated as a percentage of cost. Margin is calculated as a percentage of selling price. A 50% markup does not equal a 50% margin — always be clear which base you are using.

Section C
Break-Even Analysis
Contribution Margin per Unit
Contribution Margin = Selling Price − Variable Cost per Unit
Contribution Margin Ratio
CM Ratio = Contribution Margin per Unit ÷ Selling Price
Break-Even Point (Units)
Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin per Unit
Break-Even Point (Revenue)
Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ Gross Margin %
Section D
Cost Calculations
Total Cost
Total Cost = Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost per Unit × Units Produced)
Cost per Unit
Cost per Unit = Total Cost ÷ Units Produced
Section E
Cash Flow
Net Cash Flow
Net Cash Flow = Cash Inflows − Cash Outflows
Ending Cash Balance
Ending Cash = Beginning Cash + Net Cash Flow
Section F
Customer and Revenue Metrics
Average Revenue per Customer
Average Revenue = Total Revenue ÷ Number of Customers
Customer Retention Rate (%)
Retention Rate = ((Customers at End − New Customers) ÷ Customers at Start) × 100
Conversion Rate (%)
Conversion Rate = (Number of Sales ÷ Number of Leads or Visitors) × 100
Return on Investment (%)
ROI = ((Net Profit − Cost of Investment) ÷ Cost of Investment) × 100

How to Use This Canvas

Fill in Customer Segments first — everything else depends on who you serve. Then write the Value Proposition. Only after those two blocks are clear should you complete the remaining seven. Use sticky notes or pencil so you can revise freely as your understanding grows.

Sample Customer Interview Guide

The purpose of a customer interview is to understand a potential customer's real problems, current behaviors, and unmet needs — before building a solution. Use this guide as a starting point and adapt it to your specific venture concept. Each interview should run 20–30 minutes.

Before the Interview
  • Identify your riskiest assumptions about the customer's problem.
  • Prepare open-ended questions. Avoid yes/no questions.
  • Plan for 20–30 minutes per conversation.
  • Take notes or ask permission to record.
Opening (2–3 minutes)
  • "Thank you for your time. I'm working on understanding [problem area]. I'd love to learn from your experience."
  • "I'm not selling anything — I just want to understand how you currently deal with [topic]."
Core Questions (15–20 minutes)
  • "Can you tell me about the last time you experienced [problem]?"
  • "How do you currently handle that? What do you use or do?"
  • "What's frustrating or inefficient about that approach?"
  • "How much time or money does this cost you?"
  • "Have you ever looked for a better solution? What did you find?"
  • "What would an ideal solution look like for you?"
  • "Who else is affected by this problem?"
  • "Is this something you deal with often, or just occasionally?"
Closing (2–5 minutes)
  • "Is there anything I didn't ask that you think is important?"
  • "Would you be willing to talk again as I develop this further?"
  • "Can you refer me to anyone else who might have a similar experience?"
After the Interview
  • Write up your notes within 24 hours.
  • Identify any surprises, patterns, or quotes worth saving.
  • Update your assumptions based on what you learned.

Survey Best Practices

Keep surveys under 10 questions when possible. Use consistent rating scales throughout. Test your survey on one or two people before sending widely. Thank respondents and consider sharing what you learn with them.

Entrepreneurship Portfolio Rubric

The portfolio is assessed holistically. Each component is evaluated across four performance levels. The total portfolio score is 40 points. Use the component rubric below to assess individual deliverables and the summary table to calculate the final score.

Component Excellent (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Beginning (1)
Business Idea Clearly solves a real problem; specific and compelling Identifies a problem and solution; generally clear Idea is vague or solution is unclear Idea is missing or does not address a real problem
Customer Research Multiple methods used; rich findings that shaped the concept Research conducted; findings are relevant Research is minimal or not connected to decisions No meaningful research conducted
Value Proposition Clear, specific, customer-centered, and differentiated Addresses customer need and differentiates somewhat Vague or generic; not clearly differentiated Missing or does not address customer value
Business Model Canvas All nine blocks completed thoughtfully; internally consistent Most blocks completed; model is coherent Several blocks missing or inconsistent Canvas is incomplete or shows fundamental misunderstanding
Marketing Plan Target market well-defined; channels, message, and tactics are specific and realistic Target market and approach are identified; plan is workable Marketing plan is general or incomplete Missing or not connected to target customer
Operations Plan Clear, practical, and complete; shows understanding of delivery Operations are described and generally realistic Described at a surface level Missing or unclear
Financial Plan Startup costs, operating budget, and break-even are accurate and realistic Key financial figures are present and mostly accurate Incomplete or contains significant errors Financial plan is missing
Risk and Ethics Review Risks are specific, well-reasoned, and mitigation strategies are realistic; ethics statement is thoughtful Risks are identified with some mitigation; ethics addressed Risks or ethics are superficially addressed Risk and ethics review is missing

Further Reading and Study Pathways

The chapters in this textbook are starting points. Each study pathway below identifies the next questions worth asking, skills worth building, and topics worth exploring for students who want to go further in a particular area of business.

Entrepreneurship

Study lean startup methodology, customer development, and design thinking. Explore case studies of startups in industries that interest you. Begin keeping a problem and opportunity journal. Find a local small business owner willing to share their experience.

Topics: Customer discovery · Lean startup · Design thinking · Product-market fit · Business model innovation
Accounting and Finance

Study the accounting cycle and double-entry bookkeeping in depth. Practice building and reading real financial statements. Learn to use spreadsheet software for financial modeling. Explore introductory personal finance alongside business finance.

Topics: Financial accounting · Managerial accounting · Corporate finance · Financial modeling · Taxation
Marketing

Study consumer behavior and the psychology of decision-making. Explore digital marketing platforms, tools, and measurement. Build a personal or project brand and apply marketing principles. Practice writing clear, customer-centered copy and messaging.

Topics: Consumer behavior · Brand strategy · Content marketing · Digital advertising · Market segmentation
Management and Leadership

Study organizational behavior, motivation theory, and leadership styles. Explore how real organizations handle change, conflict, and culture. Practice giving and receiving feedback constructively. Take on leadership roles in student or community organizations.

Topics: Organizational behavior · Leadership theory · Human resources management · Change management
Operations and Supply Chains

Study process mapping, lean operations, and quality management. Explore how global supply chains are structured and disrupted. Learn project management tools and methodologies. Examine how operations differ across product and service businesses.

Topics: Operations management · Supply chain management · Quality management · Project management · Process improvement
Business Law and Ethics

Study contract law, employment law, and business entity law in more depth. Explore real business ethics cases and regulatory enforcement actions. Practice identifying ethical issues in everyday business scenarios. Research intellectual property law, especially as it applies to creative and technology businesses.

Topics: Business law · Contract law · Employment law · Intellectual property · Corporate governance · Business ethics
Economics

Study microeconomics: supply, demand, elasticity, market structures, and firm behavior. Study macroeconomics: GDP, inflation, interest rates, fiscal and monetary policy. Explore behavioral economics and how real decision-making differs from theory.

Topics: Microeconomics · Macroeconomics · Behavioral economics · International trade · Labor economics

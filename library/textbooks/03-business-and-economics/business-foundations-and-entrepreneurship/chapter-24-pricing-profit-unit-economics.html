Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Pricing is one of the most consequential decisions a business makes. Set a price too high and customers walk away. Set it too low and the business bleeds money even when sales are strong. Chapter 24 teaches students how pricing connects to costs, value, demand, competition, and profit. Students will learn the language and math of pricing — fixed costs, variable costs, margins, markup, contribution margin, and break-even — and understand why smart pricing requires judgment, not just calculation.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain three major pricing approaches, calculate gross margin and markup, determine contribution margin per unit, and conduct a break-even analysis for a real or hypothetical business. These tools will carry directly into the Financial Projections work of the chapters that follow.

Opening Question

Maya bakes sourdough bread and sells it at a weekend market for $9.00 a loaf. It costs her $3.60 per loaf to make, and she pays $1,080 per month to rent a commercial kitchen. Is she making money? How many loaves must she sell before the first dollar of profit appears? And what would happen if she dropped her price to $7.50 to undercut a competitor? By the end of this chapter, you will know how to answer all three questions — and why the answers matter more than the sales numbers alone.

Section 24.1

Why Pricing Is Difficult

Most students assume pricing is straightforward: figure out what something costs to make, add a profit on top, and charge that amount. In reality, pricing is one of the most complex and consequential decisions a business makes. Get it right and the business is healthy. Get it wrong — even with strong sales — and the business can fail.

Pricing is difficult for several interconnected reasons. First, a price signals value. A product priced too low may seem cheap or low quality, even if it is excellent. A product priced too high may drive customers to competitors, regardless of quality. Price shapes perception before anyone has experienced the product.

Second, customers do not evaluate prices in isolation. They compare prices to alternatives, to their own budgets, to what they paid last time, and to what they expect based on branding and context. A cup of coffee priced at three dollars feels reasonable at a gas station and inexpensive at a specialty café. Context matters.

Third, prices must work mathematically. A price that generates strong sales can still destroy a business if it does not cover costs. This is why pricing requires both market understanding and financial analysis. Entrepreneurs who set prices based only on what "feels right" often discover too late that their numbers do not add up.

Fourth, pricing decisions ripple through the entire business. The price affects how many units must be sold to survive, how much revenue is possible, who the target customer is, and how the business competes. A low-price strategy requires high volume and tight cost control. A high-price strategy requires strong differentiation and a customer who perceives and values that difference.

Key Idea

Pricing is not just math. It is a signal, a strategy, and a financial necessity all at once.

Section 24.2

Cost-Based Pricing

The most straightforward approach to pricing is cost-based pricing. A business calculates what it costs to produce a good or deliver a service and then adds a desired profit margin on top. The result is the selling price.

Formula — Cost-Based Pricing

Selling Price = Total Cost per Unit + Desired Profit per Unit

Example 24.2.1
Applying cost-based pricing
  • ProblemA student sells handmade candles. Each candle costs $4.00 to make (wax, wick, fragrance, container, and packaging). She wants to earn $6.00 profit per candle. What should the selling price be?
  • SolveSelling Price = $4.00 + $6.00 = $10.00
  • CheckRevenue of $10.00 minus cost of $4.00 leaves $6.00 profit. ✓
Answer: Selling price = $10.00 per candle.

Cost-based pricing has real advantages. It is easy to calculate, and it ensures the business does not sell below cost — as long as all costs are correctly identified. It gives a consistent method for pricing an entire product catalog.

However, cost-based pricing has serious limitations. It ignores what customers are willing to pay. A candle that costs $4.00 to make might be worth $25.00 to a customer shopping for a gift. Pricing it at $10.00 leaves money on the table. Conversely, if customers only expect to pay $6.00 for that type of candle, a $10.00 price is too high — regardless of costs.

Entrepreneur Warning

Cost-based pricing ensures survival math, but ignores what customers actually value. It is a useful starting point — not the final answer.

Section 24.3

Value-Based Pricing

Value-based pricing sets the price according to how much value the customer receives — not how much it costs the business to produce. The question is not "What did this cost us?" but "What is this worth to the customer?"

Consider a business consultant who helps a small company reorganize its inventory system. The consultant works for 20 hours and has out-of-pocket expenses of $200. Using cost-based pricing at $50 per hour, the total bill would be $1,200. But if the reorganization saves the client $50,000 per year in reduced waste and labor costs, the value delivered is enormous. Value-based pricing would charge far more — perhaps $8,000 or $12,000 — because the client captures tremendous value and the price reflects that.

Value-based pricing is most effective when the customer's gain is large and demonstrable, the product or service is differentiated and not easily replaced by a cheaper alternative, and the customer has the willingness and ability to pay. Premium brands use this logic consistently. Two garments with nearly identical production costs may sell for $20 and $90 respectively — the difference is brand perception, status, and identity, not materials.

The challenge with value-based pricing is that it requires customer research and confidence. Many first-time entrepreneurs underprice out of fear of rejection, leaving significant revenue and profit behind.

Key Idea

Value-based pricing captures what a product or service is actually worth to the customer, not just what it costs to produce. It requires understanding the customer deeply enough to know what value you are delivering.

Section 24.4

Competitive Pricing

Competitive pricing sets prices based on what competitors charge. In markets where customers can easily compare options, businesses must be aware of competitor prices and decide how to position themselves relative to them.

PositionDescriptionWhat It Requires
Below competitorsAttract price-sensitive customers; compete on costLower cost structure than rivals; high volume to compensate for thinner margin
At parityMatch the market price; compete on quality, service, or convenienceA clear reason for customers to choose you over an identically priced alternative
Above competitorsSignal premium quality or exclusivityMeaningful differentiation that customers perceive and value enough to pay more for
Caution — Price Wars

When businesses compete primarily by cutting prices, everyone's margins shrink. The business with the lowest costs wins; everyone else suffers. Competing on price alone is the easiest strategy for a larger, lower-cost competitor to copy and sustain. Small businesses almost always lose price wars.

Key Idea

Competitive pricing is context, not a formula. Know what competitors charge, understand where the business sits relative to them, and ensure the price reflects a coherent strategy — not just imitation.

Practice 24.4Competitive Positioning

  2. A small bookstore charges $15 for a paperback that Amazon sells for $11. Is this necessarily a mistake? What would the bookstore need to have in order to sustain the higher price?
  3. Explain in one paragraph why a small food truck should be cautious about starting a price war with a competing truck that has much lower operating costs.

Section 24.5

Fixed and Variable Costs

Before a business can price confidently, it must understand its cost structure. Costs generally fall into two categories: fixed and variable.

Two Types of Cost

Fixed Costs — Stay the same regardless of how many units are produced or sold. Rent, software subscriptions, insurance, full-time salaries, and loan payments are fixed costs. A bakery pays $2,000 per month in rent whether it sells 100 cakes or 1,000 cakes.

Variable Costs — Change in proportion to the volume of goods produced or services delivered. Ingredients, packaging, shipping, sales commissions, and hourly labor are typically variable costs. The bakery that makes more cakes uses more flour, butter, and boxes.

Total Cost = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs

Volume, Fixed Costs, and Profit — A Model

A tutoring business has $1,000 per month in fixed costs and $15 in variable costs per session. Sessions are priced at $60. The table below shows how profitability changes with volume.

Sessions / Month Revenue Variable Costs Fixed Costs Profit / Loss
10$600$150$1,000−$550
20$1,200$300$1,000−$100
23$1,380$345$1,000+$35
30$1,800$450$1,000+$350
40$2,400$600$1,000+$800
Key Insight

Notice how the business loses money at 10 and 20 sessions, barely breaks even at 23, and then becomes more profitable quickly beyond that. This pattern is typical for businesses with significant fixed costs: once fixed costs are covered, additional sales generate strong profit. Understanding this dynamic is essential before setting prices or forecasting financial results.

Section 24.6

Gross Margin and Markup

Two of the most commonly used pricing and profitability measures are gross margin and markup. They are related but calculated differently and communicate different things.

Gross Margin

Gross margin measures what percentage of revenue remains after covering the direct costs of producing a good or delivering a service. Those direct costs are called cost of goods sold (COGS) or cost of services.

Gross Margin Formula

Gross Margin (%) = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100

Example 24.6.1
Calculating gross margin
  • ProblemA clothing brand sells a shirt for $50.00. The shirt costs $20.00 to produce (fabric, labor, and manufacturing). What is the gross margin?
  • Solve Gross Margin = ($50.00 − $20.00) ÷ $50.00 × 100 = $30.00 ÷ $50.00 × 100 = 60%
  • Interpret60 cents of every revenue dollar remains after covering direct production costs. That 60 cents must cover fixed costs (rent, salaries, marketing) and leave profit behind.
Answer: Gross margin = 60%

Markup

Markup measures how much a business adds to its cost to arrive at the selling price. Markup is expressed as a percentage of cost — not of the selling price. This is the key distinction.

Markup Formula

Markup (%) = (Selling Price − Cost) ÷ Cost × 100

Example 24.6.2
Calculating markup — and comparing to gross margin
  • ProblemThe same shirt: cost $20.00, selling price $50.00. What is the markup?
  • Solve Markup = ($50.00 − $20.00) ÷ $20.00 × 100 = $30.00 ÷ $20.00 × 100 = 150%
  • CompareA 150% markup and a 60% gross margin describe the exact same transaction — measured from different angles. Markup is the premium over cost. Margin is the share of revenue that remains.
Answer: Markup = 150%. Same transaction as Example 24.6.1, different lens.
Common Confusion — Margin vs. Markup

A 50% markup on a $10.00 item yields a $15.00 price and a 33% gross margin. A 50% gross margin on a $10.00 item yields a $20.00 price and a 100% markup. These are completely different outcomes. Always clarify which measure you mean before using either in a financial conversation.

Industry Gross Margin Benchmarks

IndustryTypical Gross MarginWhy It Varies
Software / SaaS70–85%Near-zero cost to deliver one more copy
Retail clothing40–60%Sourcing, manufacturing, and returns
Restaurants60–70%Food cost is the primary COGS variable
Grocery stores20–30%High-volume, low-margin model; perishables
Construction / trades20–35%Labor-intensive; materials-heavy

Section 24.7

Contribution Margin

Contribution margin answers a specific and powerful question: after covering the variable costs of one unit, how much money is left over to contribute toward fixed costs and profit?

Contribution Margin Formulas

Contribution Margin per Unit = Selling Price − Variable Cost per Unit

Contribution Margin Ratio (%) = Contribution Margin per Unit ÷ Selling Price × 100

Example 24.7.1
Calculating contribution margin — food truck tacos
  • ProblemA food truck sells tacos for $4.00 each. Variable cost per taco (ingredients, packaging, condiments) is $1.50. What is the contribution margin per taco and the contribution margin ratio?
  • Solve Contribution Margin = $4.00 − $1.50 = $2.50 per taco Contribution Margin Ratio = $2.50 ÷ $4.00 × 100 = 62.5%
  • InterpretEvery taco sold generates $2.50 toward covering the truck's fixed costs (fuel, permits, equipment, insurance). Once those fixed costs are fully covered, each additional taco contributes $2.50 directly to profit.
Answer: Contribution margin = $2.50/taco. Contribution margin ratio = 62.5%.
Why Contribution Margin Matters for Product Mix

When a business sells multiple products, comparing contribution margins reveals which items are most profitable to sell. A business should be cautious about carrying products with very low or negative contribution margins unless they serve a specific strategic purpose — such as drawing customers who then buy higher-margin items.

Section 24.8

Break-Even Analysis

Break-even analysis answers the most fundamental question in business finance: how many units must be sold — or how much revenue must be generated — before the business starts making money? At the break-even point, total revenue exactly equals total costs. Below break-even, the business loses money. Above break-even, it generates profit.

Break-Even in Units

Break-Even Formula — Units

Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin per Unit

Example 24.8.1
Break-even in units — food truck
  • ProblemThe food truck from Section 24.7 has $1,500/month in fixed costs (truck payment, insurance, permits, helper wages). Each taco has a contribution margin of $2.50. How many tacos must it sell to break even each month?
  • Solve Break-Even Units = $1,500 ÷ $2.50 = 600 tacos/month
  • InterpretThe truck must sell 600 tacos before it makes a single dollar of profit. Every taco beyond 600 generates $2.50 in profit.
Answer: 600 tacos per month to break even.

Break-Even in Revenue

When a business sells multiple products at different prices, it is often more useful to express break-even as a revenue figure rather than a unit count.

Break-Even Formula — Revenue

Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio

Example 24.8.2
Break-even in revenue — food truck
  • ProblemUsing the same food truck: fixed costs $1,500/month, CM ratio 62.5%. What monthly revenue is needed to break even?
  • Solve Break-Even Revenue = $1,500 ÷ 0.625 = $2,400/month
  • CheckAt $4.00/taco, $2,400 ÷ $4.00 = 600 tacos. Matches our unit break-even. ✓
Answer: $2,400 in monthly revenue to break even.

How Price and Cost Changes Affect Break-Even

Break-even analysis becomes especially powerful as a planning tool when you use it to model "what if" scenarios. The table below shows the impact of pricing and cost changes for the food truck.

Scenario Price Variable Cost Contribution Margin Break-Even Units
Original$4.00$1.50$2.50600
Price cut$3.50$1.50$2.00750
Cost increase$4.00$2.00$2.00750
Price increase$5.00$1.50$3.50429
Critical Insight

Raising price (all else equal) lowers the break-even point and accelerates profitability. Cutting price or allowing costs to rise pushes break-even higher and makes profitability harder to achieve. A seemingly small price cut can require a dramatically larger volume increase just to stay even.

Section 24.9

Discounts, Bundles, and Pricing Mistakes

Even after a business sets a thoughtful base price, pricing decisions continue. Discounts, promotions, bundles, and adjustments all carry strategic consequences that entrepreneurs must understand before using them.

Discounts

Discounts reduce the selling price temporarily or for specific customers. Volume discounts reward customers who buy more. Promotional discounts attract new customers or move excess inventory. Loyalty discounts retain high-value customers.

The risk of discounts is that they train customers to wait for sales and erode perceived value. A business that discounts constantly teaches customers that the regular price is not the real price — undermining pricing integrity and reducing average revenue per sale.

Discounts also directly reduce contribution margin. A 20% discount on a product with a 30% gross margin can eliminate most of the profit on that sale. Entrepreneurs should calculate the margin impact of any discount before offering it.

Example 24.9.1
The break-even math on a discount
  • ProblemA product normally sells for $50 with a variable cost of $30, giving a contribution margin of $20. Monthly fixed costs are $2,000. The owner is considering a 20% promotional discount. What happens to break-even?
  • Original Break-Even = $2,000 ÷ $20.00 = 100 units/month
  • With Discount Discounted price = $50.00 × 0.80 = $40.00 New CM = $40.00 − $30.00 = $10.00 New Break-Even = $2,000 ÷ $10.00 = 200 units/month
  • InterpretA 20% discount doubled the break-even. The owner must sell twice as many units just to cover the same fixed costs.
Answer: Break-even doubles from 100 to 200 units when a 20% discount is applied.

Bundles

Bundling combines products or services into a package at a combined price. Bundles work well when they offer customers convenience or apparent savings while allowing the business to increase average transaction size. A hair salon might bundle a cut, color, and deep conditioning treatment at a combined price slightly below the sum of each service. Customers feel they are getting a deal; the salon earns more per visit and builds retention through routine. Effective bundles pair high-margin items with lower-margin ones so the bundle is profitable overall.

Common Pricing Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Underpricing out of fear
"I'll charge less so nobody rejects me."

Result: a business that works hard but earns little. Research market rates, understand value delivered, and price with confidence. Fear-based pricing punishes the business, not the customer.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring the full cost
Price set based on materials only, ignoring labor, overhead, and payment processing fees

Result: prices that appear profitable but are not. Every cost associated with the sale must be counted before setting a price.

Mistake 3 — Competing solely on low price
"We'll just be the cheapest option."

Result: a race to the bottom that large competitors can almost always win. Small businesses win by being better, faster, or more specialized — not cheaper.

Mistake 4 — Never revisiting prices
Same price set two years ago, never reconsidered

Result: a business gradually underfunded as costs rise and markets change. Pricing should be reviewed at least annually and after any significant cost or market shift.

Mistake 5 — Inconsistent pricing without rationale
Different customers charged different amounts for identical work, with no transparent system

Result: damaged trust and potential legal complications. If prices vary (as with negotiated contracts), the reasoning should be defensible and consistently applied.

Key Idea

Discounts and bundles are tools, not defaults. Used thoughtfully, they grow revenue and customer loyalty. Used carelessly, they erode the financial foundation of the business.

Key Terms

Break-Even Point
The volume of sales at which total revenue equals total costs; the point at which the business neither profits nor loses money.
Competitive Pricing
A pricing approach based on what competitors charge, adjusted for the business's own cost structure and strategic position.
Contribution Margin
The amount remaining from each sale after variable costs are subtracted; what each unit contributes toward fixed costs and profit.
CM Ratio
Contribution margin per unit expressed as a percentage of the selling price. Shows how much of every revenue dollar contributes beyond variable costs.
Cost of Goods Sold
The direct costs of producing a good or delivering a service, including materials and direct labor. Also called COGS or cost of services.
Cost-Based Pricing
Setting prices by calculating production cost per unit and adding a desired profit margin on top.
Discount
A reduction in the regular selling price, offered temporarily or to specific customer groups such as volume buyers or loyal customers.
Fixed Costs
Business costs that remain constant regardless of production volume — rent, insurance, salaried staff, software subscriptions.
Gross Margin
Revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage of revenue. Measures how much of each revenue dollar remains after direct costs.
Markup
The amount added to the cost of a product to arrive at the selling price, expressed as a percentage of cost — not of revenue.
Unit Economics
The financial analysis of revenue and costs at the level of a single unit — one sale, one session, one product — to understand profitability at scale.
Value-Based Pricing
Setting prices based on the value delivered to the customer rather than on production cost. Requires deep understanding of the customer's gain.
Variable Costs
Business costs that change in proportion to the volume of goods produced or services delivered — ingredients, packaging, commissions, hourly labor.

Ethics and Decision Point

A software company sells project management software to small businesses for $79 per month. The company discovers that two of its longest-running customers — both small nonprofits — are paying that same rate, even though larger business customers now pay $149 per month under a rate introduced after the nonprofits signed up.

The company is considering raising prices across the board. One team member suggests quietly raising the nonprofit rates to $149 without contacting them, since "they signed no price guarantee."

What are the ethical considerations here? What would you recommend the company do? How should they communicate with their longtime customers if a price change is appropriate?

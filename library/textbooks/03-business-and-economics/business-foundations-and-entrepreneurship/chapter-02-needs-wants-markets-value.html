Chapter 2 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every business in the world started with the same basic question: what do people need or want, and can anyone serve that need better than what currently exists? This chapter explores that question at the foundation. Before you can design a product, set a price, or write a marketing message, you need to understand why businesses exist in the first place — and that answer always traces back to a person with an unmet need.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand the difference between needs and wants, how markets form around them, how customers segment into distinct groups, what drives demand and substitution, how competition shapes opportunity, and how to craft a value proposition that gives customers a compelling reason to choose you. You will also begin applying these concepts to your own venture idea in the Entrepreneurship Portfolio Checkpoint.

Opening Question

Think of the last thing you purchased — food, a subscription, a piece of clothing, an app. What need or want did it meet? What would you have done if that product or service did not exist? What alternatives would you have used instead? The answers to these questions are the same questions every successful entrepreneur asks about their customers before building anything.

Needs, Problems, and Markets: Why Businesses Exist

Section 2.1

Needs and Wants

Every business in the world starts with the same basic question: What do people need or want? This question sounds simple, but it contains a great deal. Needs and wants are different things, and understanding the difference matters enormously in business.

A need is something a person must have to survive, function, or maintain a basic standard of living. Food, water, shelter, clothing, safety, and healthcare are classic examples. People do not choose whether to need these things. The need exists whether or not they have the money to meet it, and whether or not a business steps in to help.

A want is something a person desires but does not strictly require to survive. A person needs food, but wants a specific restaurant meal. A person needs clothing, but wants a particular brand of shoes. Wants are shaped by culture, experience, identity, income, social influence, and personal preference. They are not less real than needs — in many economies, the majority of business activity exists to serve wants, not survival needs.

TypeDefinitionExampleBusiness Implication
NeedRequired for survival or basic functioningFood, shelter, healthcareDemand tends to be stable even in hard times
WantDesired but not strictly requiredRestaurant meals, luxury goodsBusinesses must demonstrate value more actively
Evolved needA want that has become near-essential over timeElectricity, smartphones, internetDemand approaches the stability of a genuine need
Entrepreneur's Lens

A gap between what people need or want and what currently exists is the raw material of business opportunity. Entrepreneurs pay close attention to these gaps — situations where a need or want is not being served well, or at all.

Section 2.2

Problems as Business Opportunities

Most businesses begin not with a product idea, but with a problem someone experienced. The founder of a food delivery company noticed that people wanted restaurant meals but lacked the time or ability to go out. The founder of a translation service noticed that travelers struggled to communicate abroad. In each case, the business did not invent the need. The need already existed. What the business did was recognize it clearly and organize resources to meet it.

Problems are not the same as complaints. People complain about many things they tolerate easily. A business opportunity is a problem that meets all four of the following conditions:

The Four Conditions of a Real Business Opportunity

Real — People actually experience this problem, not just theoretically.

Significant — The problem causes genuine inconvenience, cost, frustration, or harm.

Underserved — No existing solution fully resolves it, or existing solutions are too expensive, complicated, or inaccessible.

Reachable — The people experiencing the problem can be found and served.

Entrepreneurial Habit

When most people encounter a frustrating experience, they move on. Entrepreneurs stop and ask: Why does this problem exist? What would it take to solve it? Who else has this problem? These questions are the beginning of a business.

Section 2.3

What a Market Is

A market is not a physical place. It is the set of all buyers and sellers of a particular good or service. More precisely, a market exists wherever three elements come together: people with a shared need or want, their willingness to exchange something of value to meet that need, and one or more providers offering a solution. When any one of these disappears, the market shrinks or collapses.

ElementDescriptionExample
Buyers with a needPeople who share a common unmet want or needCommuters who need transportation
Willingness to exchangeCustomers prepared to pay money or something of valueWillingness to pay for a ride
Sellers offering solutionsBusinesses or individuals who can meet the needTaxi companies, ride-hailing apps
Key Insight

You do not always need to find an existing market. Sometimes you can create one by making it easier for people with a need to connect with people who can meet it. The total addressable market (TAM) — the full pool of potential customers — helps businesses set realistic goals. Smaller markets can be highly profitable when they are well-served.

Section 2.4

Customers and Customer Segments

A market is made up of individual people and organizations — the customers. One of the most important realizations in business is that not all customers are alike. Even within the same market, different groups have different needs, budgets, preferences, behaviors, and expectations. These groups are called customer segments.

Consider the market for air travel. Every customer wants to get from one place to another by plane. But within that market, business travelers want reliability and flexibility; families want affordable fares; budget travelers want the lowest possible price. Each is a distinct segment. An airline that tries to serve all three identically will struggle.

Common Segmentation Approaches

TypeWhat It MeasuresExample Criteria
DemographicPopulation characteristicsAge, income, education, occupation, family status
GeographicWhere customers are locatedCity, region, urban vs. rural, climate
PsychographicValues, attitudes, and lifestyleInterests, beliefs, personality, social identity
BehavioralHow customers actPurchase frequency, brand loyalty, usage rate, decision triggers
Common Mistake — "Our customers are everyone"

Entrepreneurs who try to serve everyone often end up serving no one particularly well. A focused business almost always outperforms a business chasing a broad market. Identify the segment you can serve best, and design everything — product, price, message, channel — around that group.

Section 2.5

Demand, Choice, and Substitution

Demand describes how much of a product or service customers want to buy at a given price and time. Demand is not fixed — it changes as prices change, as customer circumstances change, and as alternatives appear.

The law of demand holds that when the price of something rises, the quantity customers want to buy generally falls. When the price falls, the quantity they want to buy generally rises. This creates a constant challenge: set prices too high and fewer people buy; set prices too low and revenue may not cover costs. How sensitive demand is to price changes is called price elasticity.

ConceptDefinitionBusiness Implication
Law of demandHigher prices generally reduce quantity demanded; lower prices generally increase itPrice changes directly affect sales volume
Price elasticityHow sensitive demand is to price changesElastic demand = small price rise causes large drop in sales; inelastic = customers buy regardless
SubstituteAn alternative product or service that meets the same underlying needMany substitutes = customers have power; few substitutes = business has pricing power
Practical Example

A local coffee shop does not just compete with other coffee shops. It competes with making coffee at home, buying coffee at the gas station, or any other way a customer could solve the same morning need. Understanding all substitutes — not only direct rivals — is essential to setting prices and designing offerings.

Section 2.6

Competition and Alternatives

Competition is a fact of business life. Wherever there is a market, others will try to serve it. Competition is not a sign that a market is bad — it is usually a sign that a market is real and valuable. Understanding your competition means understanding more than just the names of other companies. It means understanding what solutions already exist, how well they work, what customers like and dislike about them, and at what cost.

TypeDefinitionExample
Direct competitorOffers essentially the same product or service to the same customersTwo pizza restaurants on the same street
Indirect competitorOffers a different product that meets the same underlying needA pizza restaurant and a sandwich shop — both offer a quick dinner
Future competitorNot yet in the market but could enter if it proves valuableA large national chain watching a local market that is growing
Reframing Competition

Competition is not only a threat. It teaches customers that a category exists and validates demand. It raises quality across the board. Entrepreneurs who welcome competition as motivation to stay excellent often build stronger businesses than those who fear it.

Sections 2.7 – 2.9
Value Propositions, Buying Decisions, and Value Creation

Section 2.7

Value Propositions

At the heart of every business is a value proposition: a clear statement of the benefit a business delivers to a specific customer, and why that customer should choose this business over any available alternative. A value proposition is not a slogan. It is not a mission statement. It is a precise answer to the customer's most important question: Why should I buy from you?

A Strong Value Proposition Answers Three Questions

1. What do you offer? — Not in technical terms, but in terms of the problem solved or the outcome delivered.

2. Who is it for? — Which specific customer or segment benefits most from your offer?

3. Why is it better? — What makes your solution meaningfully different from or superior to the alternatives available?

Examples of Strong Value Propositions

BusinessValue PropositionWhat Makes It Strong
Local tax service"Accurate returns for self-employed tradespeople in under 48 hours, guaranteed — flat fee, no surprises."Names the customer, promises an outcome, specifies time and price transparency
Meal kit company"Restaurant-quality dinners at home in 30 minutes, with no shopping required."Addresses a real friction (shopping, cooking time) and promises a specific outcome
Project management software"One place for your team to see every task, deadline, and responsibility — so nothing gets missed."Describes a concrete benefit directly tied to a pain point
Avoid Vague Value Propositions

"We offer great service at a fair price" does not help customers decide, and it does not help the business focus. Vague propositions describe almost every business in the world. Value propositions must be specific — and they must be tested against real customers, not just stated by founders.

Section 2.8

Why Customers Buy

Understanding why customers buy is one of the most powerful capabilities a business can develop. It requires looking beyond the surface — beyond the product feature or price point — to the deeper motivation driving the purchase.

Customers buy outcomes, not products. A person buying a drill does not want the drill — they want a hole in the wall. A person buying a gym membership does not want access to equipment — they want to feel healthy, look better, or have more energy. A business that focuses only on features will lose customers to a business that understands and sells the outcome.

The Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework

Every product or service a customer purchases is being "hired" to do a job — to accomplish something in their life. When businesses understand what job their product is being hired to do, they can design and market it far more effectively.

Ask: What is the customer trying to accomplish? What would they hire to get that done if our product didn't exist?

FactorWhat It MeansBusiness Response
FunctionalCustomer needs the product to work reliablyDeliver on the core promise every time
EmotionalCustomer wants to feel confident, safe, or respectedReduce uncertainty; build trust through clarity
SocialCustomer is influenced by peers, reviews, and social proofTestimonials, referrals, and community build credibility
Section 2.9

Creating Value Instead of Merely Selling

There is a difference between a business that sells and a business that creates value. Selling focuses on moving product from the business to the customer — the goal is the transaction. Creating value focuses on genuinely improving the customer's situation — the transaction is the result of that improvement, not the goal itself.

Layer of ValueDescriptionExample
Functional valueThe product does what it promises, reliablySoftware that never crashes
Economic valueThe customer receives more benefit than they paidA repair that costs less than replacement
Emotional valueThe customer feels confident, respected, or cared forA doctor who explains options clearly and listens
Social valueThe purchase creates status, community, or identityA brand that connects customers to a community
The Long View

Businesses that deliver across all four layers of value build loyalty that is very difficult for competitors to copy. Businesses that push customers toward purchases that do not truly benefit them may generate short-term revenue but lose long-term trust. The most durable businesses build their reputation on one thing: they consistently make their customers better off.

Key Terms

need
Something a person requires to survive or function at a basic level. Demand for products serving genuine needs tends to be more stable.
want
Something a person desires but does not strictly require. Shaped by culture, identity, income, and social influence.
market
The set of all buyers and sellers of a particular good or service, defined by shared needs and the exchange of value.
customer segment
A group of customers who share similar characteristics, needs, and behaviors within a larger market.
demand
The quantity of a good or service customers want to buy at a given price and time.
price elasticity
A measure of how sensitive customer demand is to changes in price. Highly elastic demand falls sharply when prices rise.
substitute
An alternative product or service that meets the same underlying need as another. The existence of substitutes limits pricing power.
value proposition
A clear statement of the benefit delivered to a specific customer and why they should choose this business over every available alternative.
direct competitor
A business offering essentially the same product or service to the same customers.
indirect competitor
A business offering a different product that meets the same underlying need as your own offering.

Concept Summary

  • Sec 2.1Every business traces back to a need or want. Needs tend to generate stable demand; wants require more active value demonstration. Gaps between what people need and what exists are the raw material of opportunity.
  • Sec 2.2A problem becomes a business opportunity when it is real, significant, underserved, and reachable. Complaints alone are not opportunities. The entrepreneurial habit is asking why the problem exists and who else has it.
  • Sec 2.3A market is the set of all buyers and sellers of a particular good or service. It requires buyers with a need, willingness to exchange value, and sellers with a solution. Markets can be found or created.
  • Sec 2.4Customer segments are groups who share characteristics and needs. Segmenting by demographics, geography, psychographics, or behavior helps businesses focus. Trying to serve everyone usually means serving no one well.
  • Sec 2.5The law of demand holds that higher prices generally reduce quantity demanded. Price elasticity measures how sensitive demand is. Substitutes define what options customers have when evaluating any offer.
  • Sec 2.6Direct competitors offer the same product to the same customers; indirect competitors offer different products meeting the same need. Competition validates markets and raises quality — it is a source of information as much as a threat.
  • Sec 2.7A value proposition answers three questions: what you offer, who it is for, and why it is better. It must be specific, customer-focused, and tested against real buyers — not just declared by founders.
  • Sec 2.8Customers buy outcomes, not products. The jobs-to-be-done framework helps identify what customers are really hiring a product to accomplish. Emotion and social proof play large roles in buying decisions.
  • Sec 2.9Value creation means genuinely improving customers' situations. It has four layers: functional, economic, emotional, and social. Businesses that deliver across all four build loyalty that is difficult for competitors to copy.

Use what you have learned about demand, price, and substitution to work through these problems.

Problem SetPrice and Demand Scenarios
  1. Problem 1 — Coffee Shop Pricing
    A coffee shop sells 200 cups per day at $4.00 each.

    a) Calculate daily revenue.
    b) If the shop raises the price to $5.00 and demand falls to 160 cups per day, what happens to daily revenue? Is this a better or worse outcome for the business?
    c) If a competing coffee shop opens nearby and the original shop's daily sales fall to 140 cups at the $4.00 price, what is the new daily revenue? What options does the shop have?
  2. Problem 2 — Tutoring Business
    A tutoring business serves 20 students per month at $80 per session, with each student attending approximately 4 sessions per month.

    a) Calculate monthly revenue.
    b) The owner is considering raising prices to $100 per session. If 4 students decide this is too expensive and stop attending, is the price increase worth it? Show your calculation.
    c) What factors beyond the math should the owner consider before raising prices?

Case Study

Case Study — Chapter 2
The Problem with Perfect Coffee

Mara spent two years perfecting her cold brew coffee recipe. She used single-origin beans, a 24-hour steep, and a proprietary filtering method she was convinced produced the best cold brew available. She rented a commercial kitchen, bought professional equipment, and started selling at a local weekend market.

The coffee was genuinely excellent. Customers who tried it said so. But after three months, Mara was struggling to break even. Sales were modest, repeat visits were rare, and she was spending most of her time talking to curious passersby rather than selling to loyal buyers.

When a mentor suggested she interview her customers, Mara learned something she had not expected. Most of the people who bought her coffee at the weekend market were not buying because they craved premium cold brew. They were buying because they were already at the market, the booth looked appealing, and they wanted something refreshing. Several did not even know what cold brew was before trying it.

Her actual target customers — people who actively sought out high-quality cold brew and would pay a premium consistently — were not at the weekend market. They were buying subscriptions from coffee roasters online, picking up cold brew at specialty cafes before work, or making their own at home.

Mara's product was excellent, but she had been selling in the wrong market to the wrong customers. Once she understood her actual customer segment, she reconsidered her channel entirely.

Discussion Questions
  1. What assumptions did Mara make about her customers before she started selling? Which turned out to be wrong?
  2. Describe the difference between Mara's actual market (weekend market shoppers) and her target market (premium cold brew seekers) in terms of needs, willingness to pay, and buying behavior.
  3. What would a strong value proposition look like for Mara's cold brew business if she decided to sell directly to cold-brew-seeking customers? Write a one-sentence value proposition.
  4. What channels or markets might Mara explore to reach her true target customers?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics and Decision Point

A startup is developing an app to help elderly people manage their medications. The founders know that their core customers are less likely to comparison-shop, less likely to cancel once enrolled, and less likely to fully understand pricing structures. One founder suggests pricing the app higher than competitors and using complex subscription terms that are hard to cancel, because "our users probably won't notice."

Consider: Is this a legal business strategy? Is it an ethical one? How would this approach affect customer trust and reputation over time? What are the long-term business risks of designing a product that exploits rather than serves its customers? What would a value-creation approach look like instead?

Mini Project — Competitor Map

Choose a local business in your community — a restaurant, a retail store, or a service provider you can observe directly.

  1. Identify its three to five most important direct competitors.
  2. Identify two to three indirect competitors — businesses offering different products that meet the same underlying customer need.

  4. Based on your analysis, write a one-paragraph value proposition for the business you chose that emphasizes its genuine strengths relative to its competition.

Chapter Reflection

Business does not begin with a product or a price. It begins with a person — a customer — and a problem that person lives with every day. The most durable businesses in the world did not become successful by inventing something clever and hoping customers would come. They became successful by paying careful, sustained attention to what real people actually needed, and then organizing every decision around serving that need better than anyone else.

As you move through this book and develop your own venture idea, return to the questions in this chapter often: Who is my customer? What do they actually need? What alternatives do they have? Why would they choose me? These questions do not have permanent answers. Customer needs shift. Markets change. Competition evolves. The discipline of asking these questions continuously — and updating your answers honestly — is one of the most important habits you can develop as a business thinker and entrepreneur.

Chapter 10 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"An idea is just a beginning. An opportunity is an idea that meets a real need, a willing customer, and a realistic path forward."

Everyone has ideas. You have probably had dozens of them — a product you wish existed, a service that would make your life easier, a way to fix something that always seems broken. Ideas come quickly and feel exciting. But most ideas never become businesses. Many that do become businesses fail within a few years. Why?

The difference between an idea and a real business opportunity is evidence. An opportunity is not just something that sounds good in your head. It is a problem people genuinely have, that they want solved, that they would pay for, that you can realistically address, and that the timing supports. Moving from a raw idea to a serious opportunity requires work, honesty, and a willingness to test your assumptions against the real world.

This chapter teaches you that process. You will learn how to find problems worth solving, how to screen your ideas for feasibility, and how to select one opportunity to develop further. By the end, you will understand that entrepreneurship is not about dreaming the biggest dream — it is about identifying the most grounded, testable, buildable path forward.

Opening Question

A student notices that every time she visits the campus health center, she waits 45 minutes even for a minor concern — and the appointment booking system requires a phone call during business hours. She thinks: "There has to be a better way." Is that thought an idea or an opportunity? By the end of this chapter, you will have the tools to answer that question with evidence rather than intuition — and to know exactly what to do next.

Section 10.1

Ideas Versus Opportunities

An idea is a mental image of something new or different. It can emerge anywhere — while standing in a long line, dealing with a frustrating product, or listening to a complaint from a friend. Ideas are free, plentiful, and easy to generate. They require no proof. They only require imagination.

An opportunity is an idea that has been tested against reality and found to have enough merit to pursue seriously. Entrepreneurs who confuse ideas with opportunities make a common and costly mistake: they build things no one wanted. The habit of separating ideas from opportunities saves resources, reduces heartbreak, and points effort toward ventures with a genuine chance.

CharacteristicIdeaOpportunity
FoundationImagination aloneEvidence from the real world
ProblemAssumedConfirmed — real people experience it regularly
CustomerImaginedIdentified — willing to pay or exchange value
Path forwardVagueRealistic given available skills and resources
TimingNot evaluatedMarket, technology, and culture are ready now
FitNot consideredEntrepreneur has relevant skill, access, or motivation
Entrepreneur Habit

Whenever you generate an idea, immediately ask: "What would I need to prove to turn this into an opportunity?" That question reframes your thinking from dreaming to investigating — which is where real entrepreneurship lives.

Section 10.2

Finding Problems Worth Solving

Many beginning entrepreneurs start with a solution: "I want to build an app" or "I want to open a café." Strong entrepreneurs start with a problem. When you start with a solution, you risk building something that fits your vision but not your customer's need. When you start with a problem, you let the customer's experience guide what you build.

Qualities of a Problem Worth Solving
  • It is experienced frequently — daily or weekly, not once a year
  • It causes real pain, cost, or frustration, not merely mild inconvenience
  • People are already spending money on imperfect substitutes
  • A significant number of people share the problem — a market, not a personal quirk
  • No fully satisfactory solution already exists at an accessible price

Where to Find Problems

SourceWhat to Look ForExample Signal
Your own experiencePersonal frustrations and friction points you know firsthand"I spend an hour every week just finding parking near my job."
Other people's complaintsPatterns of frustration across multiple peopleThree friends complain about the same broken process
Underserved groupsPopulations whose needs are ignored or poorly servedSmall business owners priced out of enterprise software
Inefficient industriesSectors that changed slowly as needs evolvedPaper-based scheduling in healthcare offices
Environmental shiftsNew technology, laws, or demographics that make old solutions obsoleteRemote work creating demand for home office ergonomics
Section 10.3

Observing Customers and Daily Frictions

Reading about customer needs in reports and articles is useful. But some of the most valuable insight comes from direct observation — watching real people in real situations deal with real problems. Entrepreneurs call this getting out of the building: instead of imagining what customers experience, you go watch what they actually do.

Field Method
What to Watch For During Observation
  • WorkaroundsWhen people cannot find a good solution, they improvise. A restaurant owner tracking inventory on sticky notes is working around a problem. Workarounds reveal real unmet needs.
  • Wasted TimeWhere do people wait, search, redo, or repeat work? Unnecessary steps indicate a broken process that a better solution could fix.
  • ConfusionWhere do people make mistakes, get lost, give up, or ask for help? Confusion reveals gaps between what exists and what customers actually need.
  • EmotionFrustration, embarrassment, relief, and delight are data. Notice when people sigh, scowl, smile, or relax.
  • Behavior vs. WordsPeople do not always accurately report their own behavior. What people do is more reliable than what they say they do. Observation captures behavior directly.
The Problem Journal

Keep a simple notebook or note-taking app where you record problems you observe or experience each day. Over a week or two, patterns emerge. The journal forces you to look at the world through a problem-finding lens — a foundational entrepreneurial habit.

Practice 10.1–10.3Problem Identification
  1. A friend tells you: "Someone should build a better way to find used textbooks." Is this an idea or an opportunity? What additional evidence would you need to elevate it to an opportunity?
  2. List three problems you personally encountered this week. For each, identify whether it has the qualities of a problem worth solving. Explain your reasoning.
  3. Describe a workaround you have observed or used yourself. What does that workaround tell you about the unmet need underneath it?
  4. What is the difference between starting with a solution and starting with a problem? Give a real or hypothetical example of each approach and explain which is more likely to lead to a viable business.
  5. Identify one underserved group in your community and describe a problem they face that you believe is not well served by existing solutions.

Section 10.4

Trends and Changes as Sources of Opportunity

A trend is a sustained shift in behavior, technology, demographics, values, or the broader environment. Trends matter to entrepreneurs because they change what problems people face and what solutions become possible. A business built on a rising trend has the wind at its back. A business built against a trend faces constant headwinds.

Type of TrendDescriptionExample Opportunity
DemographicChanges in population age, size, composition, or geographyAging population → accessibility technology, retirement planning tools
TechnologyNew capabilities that change what is possible or affordableSpread of smartphones → mobile banking, food delivery, navigation apps
Cultural / BehavioralShifts in what people value, prioritize, or expectWellness interest → ergonomic products, mental health apps, organic food
EconomicChanges in income, spending priorities, or financial conditionsEconomic pressure → affordable subscription alternatives to high-cost services
Regulatory / PolicyNew laws, rules, or lifted restrictions that open or close marketsNew environmental regulations → compliance consulting, cleaner-tech products
Trend vs. Fad

Not everything that gets attention is a genuine trend. Fads are short-term spikes that fade quickly. Ask: Is this growing steadily over years, or was it a sudden spike? Is the underlying driver — demographic shift, technological development — durable? Are major institutions adapting to it? If the answers point to durability, it is more likely a trend.

Section 10.5

Brainstorming Business Ideas

Brainstorming is the process of generating many ideas without judging them immediately. The key rule of effective brainstorming is to separate generation from evaluation. During brainstorming, ideas are not criticized, dismissed, or ranked — they are simply collected. Criticism during brainstorming causes self-censorship and reduces the range of ideas produced. Evaluation comes later.

Technique Guide
Five Brainstorming Approaches for Entrepreneurs
  • Problem-FirstStart with a specific problem and generate as many solutions as possible. Ask: "In a perfect world, how might this be solved?" Produce 20–30 answers without filtering.
  • CombinationTake two existing things and ask what would happen if you combined them. What if you combined a gym and a café? A tutoring service and a video game? Combinations sometimes reveal new value.
  • Role-StormingImagine being a different kind of person — a child, an elderly person, a professional you know nothing about — and ask: "What problems would I face? What would I wish existed?" This breaks you out of your own perspective.
  • Trend + ProblemTake a trend you have observed and list the new problems it creates and amplifies. Remote work trends created home office, childcare, and internet-reliability problems. Each is a potential opportunity.
  • "What Bothers Me"List 10–20 things that bother you in daily life. Then ask: Is anyone else bothered by this? Could a business solve it? Simple lists can surface ideas that systematic methods miss.
After the Brainstorm

After generating many ideas, remove only those that are clearly not worth pursuing — illegal, technically impossible, already perfectly solved, or far beyond any realistic resource access. Keep the rest for the screening process in Section 10.6. Do not over-filter at this stage.

Practice 10.4–10.5Trends and Brainstorming
  1. Identify one demographic trend, one technology trend, and one cultural trend you have personally observed. For each, describe one business opportunity it might create.
  2. How would you determine whether growing interest in plant-based food is a trend or a fad? What evidence would you look for?
  3. Use the "Trend + Problem" technique. Select one trend from Question 1 and generate five problems that trend creates or amplifies. Then identify which of those problems seems most worth pursuing as a business opportunity and explain why.
  4. Conduct a 10-minute problem-first brainstorm on the following: "People frequently find it difficult to manage their personal finances." Generate at least 15 ideas without filtering them. Then identify the three that seem most promising.
  5. Apply role-storming. Imagine you are a 70-year-old person living alone. List five problems you would likely face in daily life. Which of those problems does the current market serve poorly?

Section 10.6

Screening Ideas for Feasibility

Screening is the process of evaluating ideas against practical criteria to identify which ones deserve serious investigation. Not every idea deserves equal time. Screening helps you direct effort toward ideas with the most potential. It is not the same as final evaluation — you are deciding which ideas deserve closer examination, not whether to build a business yet.

Question 1
Is the problem real?
Can you identify specific people who genuinely experience this problem regularly and find it meaningful? If not, the business has no foundation.
Question 2
Is there a customer willing to pay?
A real problem can still fail to generate a business if people expect the solution to be free or if the pain is not strong enough to motivate spending.
Question 3
Can it be built or delivered?
Do you have — or could you reasonably acquire — the skills, materials, technology, and infrastructure needed to produce and deliver this solution?
Question 4
Is the market large enough?
Is the number of potential customers sufficient to support a sustainable business? Market size does not need to be massive, but it must match the business you want to build.
Question 5
Is there a path to differentiation?
Can you identify a way to serve customers meaningfully better, cheaper, faster, or differently than existing alternatives? Without differentiation, competing is extremely difficult.
Scoring Your Ideas

Rate each idea on a 1–5 scale for each screening question (5 = most favorable). Add the scores. Ideas with the highest totals are candidates for deeper investigation. This scoring is rough and subjective — its purpose is to structure thinking and enable comparison, not to produce precise results.

Screening in Practice
Comparing Two Ideas Side by Side
  • Idea AAn app that helps students find affordable rides home during holidays. Real problem? Yes — confirmed through observation. Willing to pay? Likely, if priced under ride-share. Buildable? Yes, with existing mapping APIs. Market? Large on campuses. Differentiation? Peer-to-peer model is distinct. Score: 4+4+3+4+4 = 19/25
  • Idea BA service delivering specialty coffee to remote cabins. Problem real? Niche. Willing to pay? Uncertain. Buildable? Logistics are very difficult. Market? Small. Differentiation? Limited. Score: 2+2+2+2+3 = 11/25
  • DecisionIdea A scores significantly higher across all five criteria and deserves deeper investigation. Idea B has too many critical weaknesses at this stage.
Screening does not finalize your decision — it prioritizes your investigation.
Section 10.7

Customer Need and Willingness to Pay

The single most important early test of a business opportunity is whether a real customer will pay for it. This sounds obvious. In practice, it is one of the things beginning entrepreneurs most often skip. Many invest months of work into products that fail not because of poor execution but because no one was truly willing to pay.

What Genuine Willingness to Pay Looks Like
  • A customer exchanges real money for your product or service
  • A customer commits to a future purchase, even informally ("I'd buy that for $30")
  • A customer is currently spending money on an inferior substitute
  • A customer invests real time or effort trying to find or use existing solutions
Polite Encouragement Is Not Evidence

When someone says "that sounds useful" or "I'd definitely use that," they are being polite — not committing. People do not want to discourage you. Genuine willingness to pay requires an actual or committed exchange of value. Push always toward the strongest evidence you can get.

ConceptThe CustomerThe User
DefinitionThe person or organization that paysThe person who uses the product or service
May differ?Yes — parents buy for children; employers buy software employees use; schools buy textbooks students read
Why it mattersYour pricing, pitch, and distribution must address the payerYour product design must address the user's experience
Pricing as a Signal

Think about what customers might pay before you know how to price your product. A solution customers would pay $5 for looks very different economically than one they'd pay $500 for. Even a rough estimate of willingness to pay helps you understand whether the economics of the business can work.

Practice 10.6–10.7Screening and Willingness to Pay
  1. Apply the five screening questions to the following idea: a subscription service that curates and delivers locally made candles to subscribers each month. Rate each criterion 1–5 and explain each score briefly.
  2. A student asks 25 people about a business idea. 20 say "that's a great idea." 3 say they would pay $10/month. 1 person has already paid $10 for a prototype. Rank these three signals from weakest to strongest evidence of willingness to pay, and explain your ranking.
  3. For the candle subscription from Question 1: who is the customer and who is the user? Are they the same person? Does this distinction affect your analysis?
  4. Identify a business idea where the customer and user are different people. Explain how that distinction would affect the product design and the sales approach.
  5. You are evaluating two similar ideas. Idea A shows people saying they would pay $20 but no actual purchases. Idea B has 10 actual customers each paying $8. Which represents stronger evidence of a viable business? Why?

Section 10.8

Timing, Resources, and Fit

An idea can be real, a customer can be willing to pay, and a product can be feasible — and the venture can still fail because the timing is wrong. Timing refers to whether the world is ready for your solution at this moment. Tablet computers existed conceptually decades before tablets succeeded commercially. The concept did not fail; the timing did.

Timing Checklist
Questions to Evaluate Whether the Moment Is Right
  • TechnologyIs the technology needed to deliver this solution available and affordable today?
  • BehaviorHave customer behaviors or expectations recently shifted in a way that makes this more appealing now than before?
  • RegulationIs the regulatory or legal environment currently permissive? Or are rules about to change in a favorable direction?
  • CompetitionAre early signs of competitor movement present? This can signal good timing — or a warning that you may be too late to differentiate.

Resource Inventory

Resources include money, time, skills, knowledge, equipment, connections, and credibility. Every business requires some starting resources. Understanding what you actually have — not what you hope to acquire someday — is essential for selecting a realistic opportunity.

Resource TypeWhat to Inventory
FinancialPersonal savings or access to early-stage funds
SkillsWriting, design, coding, selling, cooking, teaching, and other practical abilities
TimeHours per week genuinely available after school, work, and other commitments
NetworkPeople who might be early customers, advisors, partners, or feedback sources
KnowledgeIndustry familiarity from jobs, internships, family experience, or personal interest

Personal Fit

Fit refers to the match between an opportunity and the person pursuing it. Personal fit does not mean only pursuing opportunities where you already know everything. Learning is part of entrepreneurship. But it means honestly evaluating where you start and what gaps you would need to close.

Dimension of FitThe Question to Ask
Skill fitDo you have the skills this opportunity requires, or can you learn or hire them?
Passion fitAre you genuinely interested in this problem and this customer? Entrepreneurship involves long periods of unrewarding work. Passion helps sustain effort.
Knowledge fitDo you understand the industry, customer, and problem well enough to make good decisions?
Lifestyle fitDoes this opportunity fit with how you want to live? A restaurant requires physical presence and long hours. A software business might allow remote work. Contradiction between opportunity and values is difficult to sustain.
Section 10.9

Selecting an Idea to Develop

By now you have observed problems, brainstormed ideas, screened for feasibility, tested for customer need, and evaluated timing and fit. You likely have a handful of ideas that survived the process. Now you need to select one to develop further — at least for now.

Many aspiring entrepreneurs resist committing to a single idea. They want to keep options open or wait for the "perfect" opportunity. This resistance is understandable but counterproductive. Developing an opportunity seriously requires focused effort. Divided attention produces shallow results. Choosing one idea does not mean abandoning the others forever — it means directing your best effort into one path so that you can generate real evidence about whether it works.

Selection QuestionWhy It Matters
Which problem is most clearly real?Real problems have paying customers. Everything else follows.
Which has the clearest path to a first customer?Early customers validate the idea and generate learning faster than any other source.
Which fits best with your current skills and resources?Fit reduces the time and cost to get started and lowers early-stage risk.
Which are you most willing to work on for a long time?Persistence requires genuine motivation. Forced interest collapses under pressure.
Which has the fewest critical uncertainties?Fewer unknowns means lower early-stage risk and faster progress toward evidence.
The Next Step Is Not Building

Selecting an idea does not mean starting to build a product. The next step is customer discovery — going out to talk with real potential customers, gather evidence, test assumptions, and refine your understanding of the problem and the opportunity. Chapter 11 covers that process in detail. Selecting an idea means committing to investigate it seriously. The investigation will either confirm the opportunity or reveal that it needs to change — which is valuable information either way.

Practice 10.8–10.9Timing, Fit, and Selection
  1. A student wants to build a virtual reality fitness class platform. Using the timing checklist from Section 10.8, evaluate whether the current moment favors or disfavors this idea. What factors would you investigate?
  2. Conduct a personal resource inventory. List what you actually have in each of the five resource categories. Then identify two business ideas that would be realistic given your current resources, and two that would not be.
  3. You are choosing between two opportunities. Idea A is highly feasible and low-risk but connects to a problem you find only mildly interesting. Idea B is riskier but addresses a problem you care deeply about. How should passion fit weigh against feasibility in your selection?
  4. Why is it counterproductive to pursue multiple ideas simultaneously during the early development stage? When, if ever, is it appropriate to develop more than one idea at once?
  5. Using the five-question selection framework, compare two real or hypothetical business ideas and select the stronger one. Write a paragraph explaining your reasoning.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 10.1An idea is a mental image requiring no proof. An opportunity is an idea confirmed to have a real problem, a willing customer, a realistic path, appropriate timing, and reasonable fit with the entrepreneur.
  • Sec 10.2Problem-first thinking guides entrepreneurs toward viable opportunities. Problems worth solving are frequent, painful, unsolved by existing options, and shared by enough people to support a sustainable business.
  • Sec 10.3Direct observation reveals workarounds, wasted time, points of confusion, and emotional responses that tell entrepreneurs more than surveys alone. The problem journal builds the habit of systematic observation.
  • Sec 10.4Trends — demographic, technological, cultural, economic, and regulatory — shift what problems exist and what solutions become possible. Trends must be distinguished from fads, which lack durable underlying drivers.
  • Sec 10.5Brainstorming generates many diverse ideas by separating generation from evaluation. Techniques include problem-first, combination, role-storming, trend-plus-problem, and the "what bothers me" list.
  • Sec 10.6Screening compares ideas against five criteria: problem reality, customer willingness to pay, feasibility, market size, and path to differentiation. Scoring structures comparison without replacing judgment.
  • Sec 10.7Willingness to pay means an actual or committed exchange of value — not polite encouragement. The customer who pays and the user who benefits may be different people, with distinct motivations and decision processes.
  • Sec 10.8Timing, available resources, and personal fit — across skill, passion, knowledge, and lifestyle — shape which opportunities are right for a particular entrepreneur at a particular moment.
  • Sec 10.9Selecting one idea to develop requires focused commitment. The selection framework compares ideas on problem clarity, path to first customer, fit, motivation, and uncertainty. The next step after selection is investigation, not building.

Key Terms Review

idea
A mental image of something new or different, generated without reference to external evidence or validation. Ideas are plentiful and require no proof.
opportunity
An idea tested against reality and found to have a real problem, a willing customer, a realistic path, appropriate timing, and reasonable fit with the entrepreneur's skills and resources.
problem-first thinking
The entrepreneurial practice of starting with an observed or experienced problem before generating solutions, to ensure the solution addresses a genuine need.
workaround
An improvised substitute solution created by customers who cannot find a satisfactory product or service. Workarounds signal real unmet needs.
problem journal
A notebook or note-taking app used to record problems observed or experienced daily, building the habit of systematic problem-finding over time.
trend
A sustained, directional shift in behavior, technology, demographics, values, or the environment that changes what problems people face and what solutions are possible.
fad
A short-term spike in interest or behavior that fades quickly and lacks a durable underlying driver. Distinguishing fads from trends matters because businesses need to outlast the conditions that create them.
brainstorming
A technique for generating many ideas by separating idea generation from idea evaluation, producing a large and diverse set of possibilities before any filtering occurs.
feasibility
The practical question of whether an opportunity can be pursued given realistic constraints of skill, resources, time, technology, and market conditions.
willingness to pay
Evidence that customers would exchange real money or equivalent value for a solution. Distinct from saying an idea seems useful or seems nice.
timing
The degree to which the current environment — technology, regulation, culture, and customer behavior — supports the success of a proposed solution.
personal fit
The match between an entrepreneurial opportunity and the entrepreneur's skills, knowledge, motivations, lifestyle preferences, and available resources.

Case Study

Case Study — Chapter 10
The Parking Problem: Marcus and the Campus Shuttle

Marcus is a college junior who noticed that students on his campus consistently complained about two things: there were not enough parking spaces near the academic buildings, and the shuttle from the remote parking lot ran infrequently and unpredictably.

He found that approximately 800 students used campus parking regularly. The shuttle was supposed to run every 20 minutes during peak hours but frequently arrived late or not at all because of a single aging van. Students paid $90 per semester for parking passes. In observation over one week, Marcus documented 14 separate instances of students waiting more than 25 minutes for a shuttle that showed no sign of arriving.

Marcus brainstormed several ideas: a ride-matching app for students to share rides from remote lots, a private shuttle service using a van he could lease, a real-time tracker app for the existing shuttle, and a service selling reserved spots in a privately owned lot two blocks from campus.

He screened each idea using the five questions and found that the real-time tracker app could be built quickly, required the least capital, and solved the most directly stated frustration — students reported that not knowing when the shuttle would arrive was worse than the wait itself.

  1. What evidence did Marcus gather that confirmed this was a real problem, not just a complaint?
  2. Apply the five screening questions to all four of Marcus's ideas. Which scores highest overall? Does your ranking match Marcus's conclusion?
  3. What questions would Marcus still need to answer before concluding this is a genuine opportunity?
  4. Who is the customer for the real-time tracker app — the student, the university, or both? How does the answer affect Marcus's approach?
  5. What resources would Marcus need to develop the real-time tracker app idea? What does he likely already have?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics Discussion

A student named Priya has been developing a business idea to help elderly residents of her neighborhood with grocery shopping and errands. In her research, she discovers that a well-funded startup based in another city is planning to launch the same kind of service in her area in approximately six months. Priya is discouraged and thinks her idea is no longer worth pursuing.

Discuss: Does the existence of a larger, well-funded competitor necessarily mean Priya's idea is not viable? What advantages might Priya have that a national startup does not? What information would help Priya decide whether to proceed, modify her idea, or move on? Is there an ethical dimension to how Priya should think about competing with or collaborating with the other company?

Applied Practice

Applied PracticeOpportunity Audit

Over the next three days, keep a simple problem journal. Every time you experience or observe a problem, friction, workaround, or complaint — no matter how small — write it down. Include: what the problem was, who experienced it, how they were dealing with it (if at all), and your initial reaction to whether it might be a business opportunity. At the end of three days, review your list. Identify the three problems that seem most significant. For each, answer the five screening questions from Section 10.6.

  7. Why is it important to commit to one idea before beginning customer discovery, rather than investigating multiple ideas simultaneously?
Section 2 — Short Answer
  1. A friend says: "I have a great idea — an app that reminds you to drink water throughout the day." Using concepts from this chapter, walk through whether this sounds more like an idea or an opportunity. What additional information would you need?
  2. Describe three specific situations where observing customer behavior would reveal more useful information than simply asking customers what they want.
  3. You are screening two ideas. Idea A scores high on feasibility and personal fit but low on market size. Idea B scores high on market size and willingness to pay but low on your personal fit. Which would you pursue, and why?
Section 3 — Discussion
  1. Entrepreneurs are often told to "follow their passion." Based on this chapter, is passion alone a sufficient basis for selecting a business opportunity? What should passion be balanced with?
  2. Is it possible for the same idea to be a strong opportunity for one person and a poor opportunity for another, even if the market conditions are identical? Explain.
  3. Some of the best-known businesses were built on problems the founders personally experienced. What are the advantages of solving a problem you know firsthand? What are the risks?

Mini Project: Opportunity Comparison

Working individually or in a small group, identify two business ideas using any of the techniques from this chapter. For each idea:

  1. Write a one-paragraph problem statement describing who experiences the problem, how often, and how significantly.
  2. Complete the five-question screening evaluation with scores and brief explanations for each criterion.
  3. Identify the timing factors — positive and negative — affecting each idea right now.
  4. Evaluate personal fit for each idea given your actual skills and resources.
  5. Select the stronger of the two ideas and write a paragraph explaining your reasoning.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 11: Customer Discovery and Market Research

Chapter 10 gave you the tools to move from raw ideas to screened, selected opportunities. The next step is not to build — it is to investigate. Chapter 11 teaches you how to gather evidence before launching: how to conduct customer interviews, design surveys, observe customer behavior in the field, study competitors, and identify your true target market. You will learn that assumptions are the enemy of successful ventures, and that the fastest way to build a successful business is to challenge your assumptions as early as possible — before you invest significant time or money.

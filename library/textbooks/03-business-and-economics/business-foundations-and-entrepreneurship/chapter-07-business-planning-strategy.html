Chapter 7 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every business faces the same fundamental challenge: there is never enough time, money, or energy to do everything. Resources are limited. Opportunities are many. Risks are real. Planning is how businesses decide where to focus.

This chapter introduces planning not as a bureaucratic exercise but as a thinking tool — a way to clarify what a business is trying to accomplish, how it intends to compete, and what trade-offs it is willing to make. You will learn how mission, vision, goals, and strategy connect, and how tools like SWOT analysis help businesses make smarter choices.

By the end of this chapter, you will have the foundation to write your own strategy statement as part of your entrepreneurship portfolio.

Opening Question

Two bakeries open in the same city within a year of each other. One focuses on speed and low prices for morning commuters. The other opens late, charges premium prices, and sources local ingredients. After three years, both are still operating. How can two businesses in the same industry with completely opposite strategies both succeed? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question — and apply the same kind of strategic thinking to your own venture idea.

Section 7.1

Why Businesses Plan

When a new business opens without a plan, it often struggles not because the idea is bad but because no one has thought carefully about the decisions ahead. Where will customers come from? What will be charged? Who will handle which responsibilities? What happens if sales are slow in the first month? Planning forces these questions into the open before they become emergencies.

A business plan is a written document that describes what a business intends to do, how it intends to do it, and what it needs to succeed. But planning is more than a document. It is a discipline — the habit of thinking ahead, anticipating obstacles, and aligning people around shared goals.

What Planning Does

FunctionWhat It Means in Practice
Clarifies directionForces owners and teams to answer: What are we actually trying to build? Without a shared answer, different people pull in different directions.
Guides decisionsWhen a business faces a choice — add a new product, hire staff, enter a new market — a clear plan provides a reference point for whether that move fits the goals.
Allocates resourcesTime, money, and people are finite. A plan helps businesses decide what to prioritize and what to postpone.
Attracts supportLenders, investors, partners, and employees want to understand how a business thinks about its future. A thoughtful plan signals seriousness.
Creates accountabilityWhen goals are written down and shared, it becomes possible to track progress and identify problems early.
Planning Is Not Predicting

No plan survives contact with the real world perfectly intact. Markets shift. Customers behave unexpectedly. Competitors make moves no one anticipated. A good plan is not a rigid script — it is a structured starting point that helps a business make better decisions as conditions change. Businesses that plan well tend to adapt better than those that never planned, because they have thought through their goals, assumptions, and trade-offs.

Key Idea

Planning is not paperwork. It is the discipline of thinking clearly about what a business wants to accomplish and how it will get there.

Section 7.2

Mission, Vision, and Values

Beneath every business strategy lies a set of foundational commitments. Three of the most important are the mission statement, the vision statement, and the values statement. These are often short, but they carry significant weight in shaping how a business operates, communicates, and makes decisions.

StatementCore Question It AnswersOrientationExample
MissionWhat do we do, who do we serve, and why do we exist?Present"We help middle school students build the math skills they need to succeed in high school and beyond."
VisionWhat do we aspire to become?Future"To become the most trusted neighborhood bakery in the region, known for quality, community, and consistency."
ValuesHow will we behave — especially in hard situations?OngoingHonesty: We refund customers when something goes wrong, even when not legally required to.
Mission Statements Work Best When They Are Specific

A vague mission like "We deliver excellence" tells no one anything useful. A specific mission names a customer, a need, and a standard. Compare: "We deliver excellence" vs. "We provide affordable, nutritious school lunches for working families in underserved communities." The second version could actually guide a decision.

Values Only Matter When They Influence Decisions

A business that lists "integrity" as a value should be able to point to specific behaviors: refunding customers proactively, being transparent about delays rather than making false promises. If the value is not connected to a real behavior, it is decoration, not a guide.

Section 7.3

Goals and Objectives

Mission and vision tell a business where it is and where it wants to go. Goals and objectives describe what it will accomplish along the way.

A goal is a broad statement of desired outcome. Goals tend to be qualitative and medium-to-long range. An objective is a specific, measurable, time-bound target that supports a goal. A goal without objectives is a wish. Objectives without a goal are a list of tasks without meaning.

SMART Objectives Framework

S — Specific: Names the exact metric or outcome to change.

M — Measurable: Can be tracked with a number or observable evidence.

A — Achievable: Realistic given current resources and conditions.

R — Relevant: Directly supports the broader goal or mission.

T — Time-bound: Has a deadline or defined time period.

Example 7.3.1
Turning a vague goal into a SMART objective
  • GoalGrow the online presence of our bakery.
  • ProblemThis goal is directional but not actionable. It does not say what to measure, by how much, or by when.
  • SMART Obj. Increase monthly website visitors from 500 to 1,000 unique visitors within six months by publishing two new blog posts per week.
  • CheckSpecific (website visitors), Measurable (500 → 1,000), Achievable (based on current trends), Relevant (supports online presence goal), Time-bound (six months). ✓
The SMART objective gives the team something to track and a deadline that creates accountability.
Align Goals with Mission

Goals should connect logically to the mission. A business whose mission focuses on customer service should have goals related to satisfaction, complaint resolution time, and repeat purchases — not only revenue growth. When goals align with mission, the business acts consistently rather than chasing whatever seems most urgent.

Section 7.4

Strategy and Strategic Choices

Strategy is the set of choices a business makes about how to compete and create value. It answers the question: Given our goals and resources, how will we win in our market?

Strategy is not the same as tactics. A tactic is a specific action taken to execute a strategy. Running a social media advertisement is a tactic. Deciding to build the business primarily through digital marketing rather than a physical storefront is a strategy. Strategy operates at a higher level — it shapes which customers a business pursues, what it offers, how it prices, where it sells, and what it chooses not to do.

Strategy Requires Trade-offs

Doing everything is itself a strategy — and usually a losing one. When a business tries to appeal to every customer, offer every product, and compete on every dimension simultaneously, it often ends up doing nothing particularly well. Good strategy requires deliberate choices about what not to do. Choosing what to leave out is as important as choosing what to include.

Three Generic Competitive Strategies

StrategyHow It CompetesDepends OnTypical Customer
Cost LeadershipOffers the lowest prices in the marketEfficiency, scale, process discipline, low operating costsPrice-sensitive buyers; customers who prioritize value over brand or customization
DifferentiationOffers something meaningfully different — quality, design, service, unique features, distinctive experienceInnovation, brand-building, quality control, customer experienceBuyers who value what makes this business different and will pay a premium for it
Focus (Niche)Concentrates on one specific customer segment, geography, or product category and serves it better than broader competitorsDeep customer knowledge, specialized capabilities, narrow scopeA specific, well-defined group that broader competitors underserve
Think About It

Think of a local business you use regularly. What strategy does it appear to be using? Is it competing on price, on differentiation, or on serving a specific niche? What evidence supports your answer? Can you identify any trade-offs it has made?

Section 7.5

Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats

SWOT analysis is one of the most widely used planning tools in business. It provides a structured way to evaluate a business's current situation by examining four categories. The key distinction: Strengths and Weaknesses are internal — they describe the business itself. Opportunities and Threats are external — they describe the environment outside the business.

Helpful Harmful
Internal
Strengths
Internal advantages: skilled team, exclusive supplier, strong brand, low costs, loyal customers, proprietary process.
Weaknesses
Internal limitations: no online presence, high overhead, limited capital, narrow product line, untrained staff.
External
Opportunities
External conditions that could help: new development nearby, competitor closing, favorable regulation, emerging trend, underserved segment.
Threats
External conditions that could harm: new competitor, economic downturn, rising costs, changing customer preferences, new regulation.
Make SWOT Entries Specific

Vague entries — "our team is talented" or "the market is competitive" — are true of nearly every business and offer little guidance. Useful SWOT entries are specific: "We have an exclusive supplier relationship that gives us lower ingredient costs than our competitors" is a strength that could shape real decisions. Vague entries waste the exercise.

Example 7.5.1
SWOT analysis — neighborhood coffee shop
  • StrengthWe have been in the same location for eleven years and have a loyal regular customer base that accounts for 60% of daily revenue.
  • WeaknessWe have no established online presence and currently reach new customers only through walk-in traffic and word of mouth.
  • OpportunityA new 200-unit apartment complex is opening two blocks away, adding potential daily foot traffic to our area within the next year.
  • ThreatA national specialty coffee chain recently opened a location one block away and is running introductory discounts for the next three months.
Notice each entry is specific, names a real condition, and could directly inform a decision about what to do next.
SWOT as a Conversation Starter

SWOT analysis does not make decisions. Its value is in surfacing important information and prompting questions: How do we use our strengths to capture opportunities? How do we address our weaknesses before they become serious problems? Which threats require immediate action?

Portfolio Connection

Complete a SWOT analysis for your venture idea. List at least three specific entries in each category. Avoid vague entries — each one should be specific enough to influence a real decision.

Section 7.6

Competitive Advantage

A business has a competitive advantage when it consistently delivers more value to customers than its competitors can — and does so in a way that is difficult to copy. The critical question is not just "are we better?" but "why can't competitors easily do what we do?"

Sources of Competitive Advantage

SourceDescriptionExample
Cost advantageProducing goods or services at a lower cost than competitors, enabling lower prices or higher marginsA bakery with a high-volume oven and standardized recipes keeps ingredient waste under 5% while competitors run at 15%
DifferentiationOffering something customers genuinely value and cannot easily get elsewhereA coffee shop sources a single-origin bean from one farm and trains baristas to explain the flavor profile
Network effectsThe product or service becomes more valuable as more people use itA local vendor marketplace becomes more useful to buyers when more sellers join, and vice versa
Switching costsCustomers have invested time, data, or money such that changing providers is costly or inconvenientA bookkeeping service that has set up all of a client's accounts over three years is hard to replace quickly
Relationships and reputationTrust built over time is difficult for new entrants to replicate quicklyA contractor known for completing jobs on time without surprises gets referrals that no advertisement can buy
LocationFor businesses serving local customers, being in the right place is itself an advantageThe only coffee shop inside a commuter train station faces no meaningful competition during rush hour
Temporary vs. Sustainable Advantage

Some advantages are temporary. A new product feature can be copied. A promotional price can be matched. A talented employee can be hired away. Sustainable competitive advantage comes from combinations that are harder to replicate — a unique culture, deeply embedded customer relationships, proprietary processes, or a brand built over years of consistent delivery. Early-stage businesses often compete on hustle and founder relationships; as they grow, they need to develop more systematic advantages.

Key Idea

Competitive advantage means offering value in a way that is better than alternatives and difficult to copy. Ask not just "are we better?" but "why can't competitors easily do what we do?"

Section 7.7

Positioning in a Market

Positioning describes how a business wants to be perceived in the minds of its target customers relative to competitors. It answers the question: In a crowded market, why should customers choose us?

Positioning is not just about marketing messages. It reflects real choices about customers, products, pricing, and service. If a business positions itself as the premium, high-service option, its operations, pricing, and communication must all reflect that position consistently.

The Positioning Statement Template
For [target customer], [business name] is the [category] that [key benefit or difference] because [reason to believe].

Example: For first-generation college students, Launchpad Tutoring is the academic support service that combines test prep with college application coaching because our staff are themselves first-generation graduates who understand the challenges from personal experience.

Example 7.7.1
Writing a positioning statement
  • BusinessA neighborhood hardware store that specializes in helping DIY homeowners (not contractors) with weekend renovation projects.
  • CustomerFirst-time homeowners who feel intimidated by large hardware chain stores and need guidance, not just products.
  • Statement For first-time homeowners tackling renovation projects, Cornerstone Hardware is the hardware store that provides hands-on project guidance alongside the right tools and materials because our staff are experienced tradespeople who speak plain English and never make you feel like you should already know this.
  • CheckNames a specific customer. Identifies a real benefit. Gives a credible reason to believe. Differentiates from big-box competitors. ✓
A strong positioning statement is specific enough to exclude some customers — and that is intentional.

Positioning Maps

A positioning map (sometimes called a perceptual map) is a simple two-axis diagram showing where different competitors sit relative to each other along two important dimensions — for example, price and quality, or specialization and convenience. Positioning maps help businesses identify gaps in the market and clarify where they want to compete. If every competitor clusters in one corner, there may be an opportunity in the empty space — if customers exist who want what that space represents.

Portfolio Connection

Draw a simple positioning map for your venture idea. Choose two dimensions that matter to your target customers and place known competitors on the map. Where does your business fit? Is there a gap you can occupy?

Section 7.8

Short-Term and Long-Term Planning

Business planning operates on different time horizons, and each serves a different purpose. Short-term planning (next few weeks to twelve months) focuses on immediate decisions, operational execution, and near-term goals — it is specific and actionable. Long-term planning (one to five years or more) focuses on direction, major investments, and strategic positioning — it is less precise but more directional. Both horizons are necessary. A business that only thinks short-term may be efficient today but strategically lost. A business that only thinks long-term may have a beautiful vision but no idea what to do on Monday morning.

The Planning Cascade

  1. 1Mission & VisionWhy the business exists and where it is ultimately going. Sets the direction for everything below.
  2. 2Long-Term GoalsMajor outcomes the business aims to achieve over three to five years. Broad and qualitative.
  3. 3Annual PrioritiesThe most important things to accomplish this year. Informed by long-term goals; specific enough to guide resource allocation.
  4. 4Quarterly ObjectivesSMART targets for the next ninety days. Concrete, measurable, assigned to owners.
  5. 5Weekly & Monthly ActionsThe specific tasks and activities that move quarterly objectives forward. Daily work.
Rolling Plans

Because conditions change, many businesses use rolling plans — planning documents that are updated regularly rather than written once and filed away. A rolling twelve-month plan, updated quarterly, keeps the business's thinking current without requiring constant from-scratch planning efforts.

Section 7.9

When Plans Need to Change

Every business plan is built on assumptions about customers, markets, competitors, costs, and conditions. Some assumptions turn out to be correct. Many do not. When a plan is built on assumptions that turn out to be wrong, the plan needs to change. Holding stubbornly to a plan after the evidence has changed is not discipline — it is denial.

Signals That a Plan Needs Revisiting

SignalWhat to Ask
Sales or customer behavior differ significantly from projectionsAre our assumptions about demand still valid?
A major competitor enters or exits the marketDoes our competitive strategy still make sense?
Costs turn out higher or lower than anticipatedDoes our pricing and profitability model need adjustment?
A new technology changes customer expectations or economicsIs our delivery method or product still competitive?
Regulatory changes affect what is legal or requiredDo we need to change our operations or offering?
The founding assumptions about the customer problem were wrongAre we solving the right problem for the right customer?
What Is a Pivot?

In entrepreneurship, a pivot is a deliberate change in strategy based on evidence. A pivot is not giving up — it is updating the approach based on what has been learned. A business might pivot by changing its target customer, its pricing model, its delivery channel, or even its core product.

Successful pivots are driven by evidence, not frustration. They preserve what has been learned while changing what is no longer working. The judgment to know when to pivot — as opposed to simply giving a plan more time — comes from staying close to customers, tracking the right data, and being honest about results.

Key Idea

Plans are living documents. Revisiting and updating a plan based on evidence is not weakness — it is smart management.

Concept Summary

  • Sec 7.1Businesses plan to clarify direction, guide decisions, allocate resources, attract support, and create accountability. Planning is a thinking tool, not an end in itself. Plans are not predictions — they are structured starting points.
  • Sec 7.2Mission statements describe what a business does and why (present tense). Vision statements describe where it aims to go (future). Values describe how it will behave — they matter only when specific enough to influence real decisions.
  • Sec 7.3Goals are broad desired outcomes. Objectives are specific, measurable, and time-bound. SMART objectives make goals actionable. Goals should align with mission, not just with what seems urgent.
  • Sec 7.4Strategy is the set of choices a business makes about how to compete. Good strategy requires deliberate trade-offs. Three broad directions: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus (niche).
  • Sec 7.5SWOT analysis examines internal strengths and weaknesses alongside external opportunities and threats. Specific entries are useful; vague entries waste the exercise. SWOT is a conversation starter, not a decision-maker.
  • Sec 7.6Competitive advantage means delivering value in ways competitors cannot easily copy. Sources include cost, differentiation, network effects, switching costs, relationships, and location. Sustainable advantage comes from combinations that are hard to replicate.
  • Sec 7.7Positioning describes how a business wants to be seen by target customers relative to competitors. A positioning statement names the customer, the benefit, and the reason to believe. Positioning maps visualize the competitive landscape.
  • Sec 7.8Businesses need both short-term and long-term plans. The planning cascade connects vision to daily action: mission and vision → long-term goals → annual priorities → quarterly objectives → weekly actions. Rolling plans stay current.
  • Sec 7.9Plans are built on assumptions. When assumptions change, plans must be revisited. A pivot is a deliberate, evidence-based strategic change — not giving up. The judgment to know when to pivot vs. when to wait requires staying close to customers and being honest about results.

Business Math Practice

Break-Even Thinking in Strategy

Suppose your business plans to sell a product for $40. The variable cost per unit is $15. Your fixed monthly costs (rent, software, utilities) total $3,750.

  1. What is the contribution margin per unit sold?
  2. How many units must you sell per month to break even?
  3. If you lower your price to $35 to gain a competitive advantage on cost, how many units would you now need to sell to break even?
  4. What does this calculation tell you about the trade-off between a price-based competitive strategy and profitability?
  5. A competitor charges $38 for a comparable product. What strategy options does this price gap suggest for your business?

Hint: Contribution margin = Price − Variable Cost.   Break-even units = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin.

Case Study — Chapter 7
Two Bakeries, Two Strategies

Sunrise Bakery and The Craft Loaf are both bakeries that opened in the same mid-sized city within a year of each other. After three years, both are still operating — but they serve very different customers and have taken very different strategic paths.

Sunrise Bakery

Sunrise focuses on speed and value. It opens at 5:30 a.m. for the morning commuter crowd. Its menu is short: drip coffee, a few pastry types, bagels, and sliced bread loaves. Prices are low; turnover is high. The owner standardized every recipe to reduce training time and waste. Profit comes from volume. Mission: "Fresh, affordable baked goods for busy people, every morning."

The Craft Loaf

The Craft Loaf opens at 8 a.m. and closes by 2 p.m. It offers a rotating menu of artisan breads, seasonal pastries, and small-batch roasted coffees. Prices are significantly higher than Sunrise. The two bakers take pride in sourcing local flour and organic ingredients. Lines form on weekend mornings. Vision: "To become the most respected artisan bakery in the region, known for craft, ingredients, and community."

Ethics and Decision Point — The Overpromised Vision

She uses this vision in her pitch deck, her website, and conversations with early investors. After two years, the business is growing but remains a small regional player.   3. How can businesses write ambitious vision statements without misleading stakeholders?
  4. What role do values play in this scenario?

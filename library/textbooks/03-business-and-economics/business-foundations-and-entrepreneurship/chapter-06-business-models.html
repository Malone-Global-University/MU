Chapter 6 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"A business model describes the rationale of how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value."
— Alexander Osterwalder

Every business, from a neighborhood barbershop to a global software company, runs on a business model. The model is the underlying logic that explains how the business works: who it serves, what it offers, how it reaches customers, and how it earns enough money to survive.

Many people confuse a business idea with a business model. An idea says what you want to do. A model explains how it actually works as a functioning operation. "I want to sell healthy lunches near my school" is an idea. The business model answers the harder questions: Who exactly will buy? What will you charge? How will you make it? What will it cost? How will customers find you? What happens when sales are slow?

This chapter teaches you to think in business models. You will learn to analyze how businesses earn money, manage costs, deliver value, and organize their resources and activities. You will also explore the many different ways businesses can be structured to generate revenue — because the same basic idea can be built in very different ways depending on the model chosen.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to describe how a real business works at a structural level, draft a simple business model for your own venture idea, and recognize why model design is often more important than the original idea.

Opening Question

Two students both want to offer tutoring services. One charges $30 per session. The other charges $120 per month for unlimited access. Both offer tutoring. Their business models are entirely different. Which model would you choose as a business owner? Which would you choose as a customer? By the end of this chapter, you will know how to analyze that question — and why the answer matters more than the service itself.

Section 6.1

What a Business Model Is

A business model is the plan that describes how a business creates value, delivers it to customers, and earns money in return. It is not just a financial plan or a marketing plan. It is the whole logic of the business — the design of how all the pieces fit together.

Think of a business model the way you might think of the rules of a game. The rules do not tell you every move you will make, but they define how the game is played, who can participate, how points are scored, and what winning looks like. Similarly, a business model defines how the business plays in the market.

QuestionWhat It Addresses
Who are the customers?The specific people or organizations the business serves
What value does it offer?The benefit the business delivers that customers care about
How is it delivered?The channels, processes, and activities that get value to customers
How does it earn money?The revenue model — what customers pay for and how
What does it cost?The cost structure required to operate the model
What makes it work?Key resources and activities the business must have and do
Who does it rely on?Key partners and suppliers the business depends on externally
Real-World Comparison
Two coffee shops. Same product. Very different models.

One rents space in a high-foot-traffic shopping center, sells individual cups at $5 each, and pays a large staff. The other operates a small roasting operation, sells bags of coffee directly to subscribers online, and operates with two employees. Both sell coffee. Their business models are entirely different, and each carries different risks, costs, and opportunities.

Why Models Matter More Than Products

A business with a weak product but a clever model can outperform a business with an excellent product and a broken model. Understanding models helps you evaluate whether an idea can survive, identify the most critical parts of a business to protect, and spot what might go wrong before investing time and money.

Business Models Are Hypotheses

Business models are not set in stone. Many successful companies have changed their models dramatically over time — a software company shifting from selling licenses to selling monthly subscriptions, a retailer moving from stores to e-commerce. The model is an educated guess about how the business will work. Like any hypothesis, it should be tested and revised based on evidence.

Section 6.2

The Difference Between an Idea and a Model

A common mistake among new entrepreneurs is falling in love with an idea without thinking through the model. Ideas feel exciting. Models require hard thinking. But an idea without a model is not yet a business.

The Idea Trap

Suppose someone has the idea: "I want to create an app that connects dog owners with dog walkers." That is an idea. But it answers almost none of the critical questions: Will dog owners pay, or will dog walkers pay, or both? How much will each party pay? What percentage does the platform keep? How will trust and safety be established? How will the platform attract both sides at the same time, especially at launch? Two entrepreneurs answering these questions differently would build two very different businesses with very different chances of success.

Turning an Idea Into a Model

Moving from idea to model requires discipline — forcing yourself to answer uncomfortable questions, especially about money and costs. A useful completeness test is to fill in this sentence with as much specificity as possible:

The Model Completeness Test

"My business will help [specific customer] who has [specific problem] by providing [specific solution], which they will pay for by [payment method and amount], and we will deliver it through [channel], while our main costs will be [key costs], and we will be different from competitors because [differentiation]."

If you cannot complete that sentence clearly, you have an idea but not yet a model.

Practice 6.2Idea to Model
  1. Take one of the following ideas and list at least five unanswered questions that must be resolved before it becomes a business model: (a) a food truck selling tacos, (b) an online tutoring platform, (c) a clothing resale shop.
  2. Apply the Model Completeness Test to a business idea of your own. Write out the full sentence. Identify the part of the sentence that is hardest to complete — and explain why.
  3. Why do you think entrepreneurs often resist thinking through the difficult model questions? What are the costs of waiting too long to do this work?
Section 6.3

Customers, Value, and Revenue

Every business model starts with a clear picture of who the customer is and what value the business creates for them. Revenue — the money the business earns — flows from customers who believe the value is worth paying for.

Defining the Customer

A customer is the person or organization that pays for the product or service. In some businesses, the person who uses the product is different from the person who pays. A children's educational software company must design its product for the child but its marketing and pricing for the parent. In a business-to-business (B2B) sale, the person who uses the product (an employee) is often not the person who approves the purchase (a manager or finance department).

Customer segments are groups of customers who share similar characteristics, needs, or behaviors. A business may serve multiple segments with different priorities, price sensitivities, and expectations — which may require different products, services, or pricing structures for each.

The Value Proposition

A value proposition is the specific combination of benefits a business promises to deliver to a customer. It answers the question: Why should this customer choose us over the alternatives?

CharacteristicWhat It MeansWeak ExampleStrong Example
SpecificNames a particular problem or need"Great quality""Birthday cakes delivered same-day"
CredibleCustomers can believe the promise"Best in the world""Within 10 miles, in 3 hours"
DifferentiatedExplains why this is a better choice"Excellent service""Starting at $45, no minimum order"
RelevantAddresses what customers actually care about"Award-winning recipes""For customers who forgot a birthday"
Revenue Is Not Profit

Revenue is the total money a business receives from customers. Profit is what remains after all costs are subtracted. A business can generate significant revenue and still lose money if its costs exceed its revenue. For a business to survive long-term, revenue must eventually exceed costs and leave a margin for reinvestment and growth.

Section 6.4

Common Revenue Models

A revenue model is the specific mechanism by which a business earns money. Revenue model design is one of the most creative and consequential decisions an entrepreneur makes. The same basic service can be monetized in very different ways, and the choice of model dramatically affects customer behavior, business economics, and competitive positioning.

Model 1
Transaction Revenue
A customer pays once in exchange for a product or service. Revenue is earned each time a sale occurs. Simple and easy to understand, but requires continual customer attraction.
Examples: shoe store, barbershop, used car dealer
Model 2
Subscription Revenue
A customer pays a recurring fee — weekly, monthly, or annually — for ongoing access. Creates predictable recurring revenue. High churn can undermine an otherwise strong model.
Examples: streaming services, gym memberships, SaaS software
Model 3
Service Revenue
Charges for labor, expertise, or time. Often lower startup costs. Faces a key constraint: time is finite. Scaling usually requires hiring or productizing the service.
Examples: lawyers, plumbers, consultants, tutors
Model 4
Licensing Revenue
Grants another party the right to use intellectual property in exchange for a fee or royalty. Highly scalable once IP is created, but depends on the IP being genuinely valuable and protected.
Examples: franchises, patents, brand licensing, software APIs
Model 5
Advertising Revenue
Offers a platform or content for free to users; earns money by charging advertisers to reach those users. Depends on attracting and retaining a large, engaged audience.
Examples: search engines, social media, free news websites
Model 6
Commission / Marketplace
Facilitates transactions between buyers and sellers, earning a percentage of each transaction. Does not own the goods exchanged. Scales well as transaction volume grows.
Examples: real estate agents, online marketplaces, stock brokers
Model 7
Data Revenue
Collects, organizes, or analyzes information valuable to others and sells access. Depends on unique or superior data. Often grows through network effects as more users generate more data.
Examples: credit bureaus, market research firms, financial data providers
Model 8
Asset Rental / Sale
Sells or rents physical assets repeatedly over time. The asset must generate enough revenue over its useful life to justify acquisition and maintenance costs. Often requires significant upfront capital.
Examples: equipment rental, real estate leasing, vehicle fleets

Section 6.5

Products, Services, Subscriptions, and Licensing

CategoryEconomicsKey AdvantageKey Challenge
Physical Products Gross margin = price minus unit cost. Must cover fixed overhead. Tangible, can be inventoried and distributed widely Inventory risk, supply chain complexity, logistics costs
Digital Products Near-zero reproduction cost once built. Very high margins at scale. Scales without proportional cost increase Piracy, competition, constant update pressure

CAC and LTV — The Subscription Health Check

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is the total cost of convincing a new customer to buy — including marketing, sales, and onboarding. Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) is the total revenue a business expects to earn from that customer over the full period they remain a subscriber.

A healthy subscription business has LTV significantly higher than CAC. If acquiring a customer costs more than that customer will ever pay, the model loses money no matter how many subscribers it adds.

Practice 6.5Business Category Analysis
  1. A freelance graphic designer charges $80 per hour. She works 30 billable hours per week and has no employees. What is her maximum weekly revenue? What would she need to do to double her income without working more hours?
  2. A subscription meal-kit service spends $60 on average to acquire each new subscriber through advertising. The average subscriber pays $80 per month and cancels after 4 months. Calculate LTV and compare it to CAC. Is this model healthy? What must improve?
  3. A software developer creates a mobile app that sells for $4.99. After the app is built, each download costs the developer essentially nothing. If she sells 10,000 copies, what is her gross revenue? Why do digital products have such different economics than physical ones?
Section 6.6

Platforms and Marketplaces

One of the most powerful and complex business models of the past two decades is the platform model. Platforms create value not by producing goods or services directly, but by enabling interactions between two or more groups of users.

Platform vs. Traditional Business
A taxi company versus a ridesharing app

The taxi company owns vehicles, employs drivers, and provides rides directly to passengers. The ridesharing platform owns no vehicles. It connects drivers who have cars and time to sell with passengers who need rides — and earns a commission on each transaction.

This is a two-sided market: the platform serves two distinct groups whose participation depends on each other. More drivers make the platform more valuable to passengers. More passengers make the platform more attractive to drivers. This creates network effects — the platform becomes more valuable as more people use it.

Platform TypeTwo Sides ServedHow It Earns
E-commerce MarketplaceBuyers and sellersCommission on transactions; listing fees
App StoreDevelopers and usersPercentage of app sales; developer fees
Social MediaContent creators and advertisersAdvertising revenue; sponsored content
Gig Economy PlatformWorkers and customersService fee on each transaction
Payment NetworkBuyers, sellers, and banksTransaction fees; interchange fees
The Cold Start Problem

A platform with no users on one side has no value to users on the other side. Getting both sides of a market active simultaneously is one of the hardest challenges in platform entrepreneurship. Successful platforms typically solve this by subsidizing one side early on with free or discounted services, or by launching in a small, specific market where critical mass is easier to achieve before expanding.

Section 6.7

Cost Structures

Understanding how a business earns revenue is only half of the model. The other half is understanding what it costs to operate. Cost structure refers to all of the costs the business incurs in running its operations.

Cost TypeDefinitionExamplesBehavior as Volume Changes
Fixed Costs Remain constant regardless of output volume Rent, insurance, full-time salaries, loan payments Do not change; create operating leverage
Variable Costs Change in direct proportion to output or sales Raw materials, packaging, shipping, sales commissions Increase with every additional unit produced or sold
Semi-Variable Costs Have both fixed and variable components Utility bills, part-time labor, cloud computing Fixed up to a threshold; variable above it
Operating Leverage: Fixed Costs Cut Both Ways

Fixed costs create operating leverage. When revenue grows, fixed costs do not grow proportionally, so profit margins improve as volume increases. This is why scaling up feels profitable — the same overhead spreads over more units.

But fixed costs also create risk. If revenue falls, fixed costs remain, and losses mount quickly. A business with very high fixed costs and low revenue is in serious danger, because it cannot reduce its cost base fast enough to match the drop in income.

Cost-Driven vs. Value-Driven
Two different philosophies of cost structure

Cost-driven models compete primarily on price and efficiency. Every operational decision aims to minimize spending. Budget airlines, discount retailers, and commodity manufacturers use this approach. Operational efficiency is the primary competitive advantage.

Value-driven models deliver maximum quality, exclusivity, or expertise and charge premium prices to match. Luxury brands, specialized consultancies, and custom makers operate this way. Higher costs are accepted because the value delivered justifies the price.

Practice 6.7Cost Structure Analysis
  1. A small bakery has the following monthly costs: rent ($1,500), one full-time employee ($2,200), flour and ingredients ($0.80 per loaf), and packaging ($0.15 per loaf). Identify which costs are fixed and which are variable. If the bakery produces 800 loaves in one month, what is the total variable cost? What are total costs?
  2. A tutoring business has almost no fixed costs — just a laptop and a phone. Is this an advantage or a disadvantage? In what circumstances might it be each?
  3. A software company has $400,000 in fixed monthly costs (engineers, offices, servers) and earns $8 per user per month in subscription revenue. How many subscribers does it need to break even? What happens to profit margin once it passes that number?
Section 6.8

Key Resources and Key Activities

Every business depends on a set of key resources — the assets required to create and deliver its value proposition — and key activities — the most critical things the business must do well in order to function.

Types of Key Resources

Resource TypeDescriptionExamples
PhysicalTangible assets needed for operationsEquipment, vehicles, facilities, inventory
IntellectualIntangible assets that are hard to replicatePatents, trademarks, proprietary software, customer data, brand recognition
HumanPeople whose skills, knowledge, and relationships drive the businessSpecialized engineers, experienced sales team, creative designers
FinancialCash, credit, and capital available to fund operationsCash reserves, credit lines, investor funding
Key Activities Vary Dramatically by Business Type

A manufacturing company's key activities might be production, quality control, and supply chain management. A consulting firm's are relationship development, research, and analysis. A platform's are platform development, community management, and trust and safety enforcement. Identifying key activities matters because it tells you where to allocate attention and resources — a business that loses focus on its most critical work is likely to underperform.

Key Partnerships

Most businesses cannot do everything themselves. Key partnerships are the external organizations and suppliers a business relies on to make its model work. Partnerships reduce the need to own or build every capability internally, but they also create dependencies. If a key partner fails, raises prices, or changes terms, the business is affected.

Practice 6.8Resources, Activities, and Partnerships

  2. What are the key activities that a ridesharing platform must do well to maintain user trust and grow? List at least four and briefly explain each.
  3. A small e-commerce clothing brand relies on a manufacturer in another country for all of its products. What partnership risk does this create? How might the brand manage or reduce that risk?

Business Model Canvas Foundations

The Business Model Canvas is a widely used visual tool for designing, describing, and analyzing business models. Developed by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur, the canvas organizes all the key elements of a business model onto a single page, making it easy to see how the pieces connect and where tensions exist.

Use the Canvas to Spot Tensions

When filling in the canvas, look for misalignments: Does the revenue model match what the customer segments are actually willing to pay? Are key activities aligned with the value proposition? Is the cost structure sustainable given realistic revenue expectations? Are the channels appropriate for reaching target customers?

The canvas is most useful as a thinking tool, not just a documentation exercise. It is a hypothesis — a summary of your current best thinking — and the point of completing it is to identify which assumptions need testing most urgently.

  3. Compare the Business Model Canvas of a subscription music streaming service to that of a traditional CD store. Using the nine blocks, identify at least four significant differences between the two models.

Case Study — Chapter 6
Same Idea, Different Models: Two Student Tutoring Businesses

Two students both want to offer tutoring services to high school students in their city. Both are equally qualified. Their models, however, are very different — and so are their economics.

Kezia
Transaction Model

Charges $30 per hour for in-person tutoring sessions. Finds clients through flyers and word of mouth. Earns money session by session with no guaranteed income from week to week. Low startup costs, complete flexibility. Main challenges: unpredictable demand and a ceiling on income (only so many hours available per week).

Marcus
Subscription Model

Charges $120 per month for a membership that includes four one-hour sessions, access to a shared study group, and unlimited questions via text. Customers pay on the first of each month. Marcus knows his income in advance. Fixed monthly commitment makes retention critical, but provides stable revenue to plan around.

Same idea. Different models. Different economics. Different challenges.

DiscussionCase Questions
  1. What are the risks and advantages of Kezia's model compared to Marcus's?
  2. At what number of regular clients does each model become more or less attractive for the business owner?
  3. How would the Business Model Canvas look different for each student's business?
  4. Which model would you choose if you were starting today, and why?
Ethics and Decision Point — Chapter 6
When the Revenue Model Conflicts with User Wellbeing

A social media company offers its platform for free to users and earns all of its revenue by selling advertising. The company knows that users are more likely to stay engaged — and therefore more valuable to advertisers — when they are shown content that triggers strong emotional reactions, including outrage, fear, and conflict.

Should the company optimize its platform for user engagement if doing so increases emotional distress among users? Does the fact that the platform is free change the ethical calculus? Who are the company's true customers — the users or the advertisers?

This scenario reflects a real tension in advertising-based business models. The revenue model can create incentives that conflict with the wellbeing of the people the product serves. When designing a business model, ask not only "does this work economically?" but "is this model one we can defend as fair and honest to everyone it affects?"

Applied Practice

Choose a business you interact with regularly — a restaurant, an app, a store, or a service you use at home.

  1. What customer segment does it primarily serve?
  2. What is its core value proposition?
  3. What revenue model does it use? (Transaction, subscription, advertising, etc.)
  4. What do you think its largest costs are? Are they mostly fixed or variable?
  5. What key resources does it depend on most?
  6. What key activities must it do well to deliver its value proposition?
  7. Who are likely its most important external partners?
Extension

Compare your answers with a classmate who analyzed a different business. What patterns do you notice across different types of businesses? What is the single most important driver of success in each model you studied?

Block 2 — Value Proposition
  1. What problem do you solve, or what need do you meet? Why would customers choose you over existing alternatives? Write a value proposition that is specific, credible, differentiated, and relevant.
Block 3 — Channels
  1. How will customers find you? How will you deliver your product or service to them? List the channels you plan to use and explain why each is appropriate for your customer segment.
Block 4 — Customer Relationships
  1. How will you interact with and support customers? Will you offer personal service, self-service, automated support, or a community? How will you keep customers coming back?
Block 5 — Revenue Streams
  1. How will you earn money? Identify your revenue model (transaction, subscription, commission, etc.). What will you charge, how often, and why is that price appropriate for your segment?
Blocks 6–9 — Operations Side
  1. Key Resources: What physical, intellectual, human, and financial resources do you need?
  2. Key Activities: What must you do consistently and well to deliver your value proposition?
  3. Key Partnerships: Who will you rely on externally? Suppliers, distributors, technology partners?
  4. Cost Structure: What are your most significant anticipated costs? Identify at least three and label each as fixed or variable.
Reflection
  1. After completing the canvas, write two or three sentences answering: What is the biggest assumption in your model that you would need to test before committing to this approach?

Short Answer Questions

Discussion Questions

  1. Is it ethical to offer a product for free and earn money by selling users' attention and data to advertisers? Where should the line be drawn? Does your answer change depending on the type of users (children vs. adults)?

Chapter Reflection

Business models are designs. Like any design, they can be strong or weak, and the same underlying goal can be achieved through many different structural choices. The best entrepreneurs treat their business model as a hypothesis to be tested rather than a plan to be defended.

As you continue building your venture portfolio, resist the temptation to settle on the first model that comes to mind. Ask: Are there other ways to deliver this value? Are there other ways to charge for it? What model would make this business most sustainable and most fair to everyone involved?

The most important skill is not knowing which model to choose. It is developing the habit of asking the right questions before committing to an answer.

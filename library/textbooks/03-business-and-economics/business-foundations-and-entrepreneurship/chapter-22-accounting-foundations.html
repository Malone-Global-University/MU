Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every business generates financial activity — money coming in, money going out, things owned, things owed. Accounting is the system that captures that activity, organizes it into meaningful categories, and turns raw numbers into useful information. Without accounting, business owners cannot know whether they are profitable, whether they can pay their bills, whether they are growing, or whether someone is stealing from them.

This chapter introduces accounting as a practical tool for decision-making. Students do not need to become bookkeepers or accountants, but they do need to understand the language of business finance. The concepts introduced here will support every other financial topic in this book and prepare students for more advanced study in accounting, finance, and business management.

Opening Question

Two bakeries in the same town each report $180,000 in sales at the end of Year 1. One expands successfully and secures a bank loan. The other closes. How is that possible? By the end of this chapter, you will understand the accounting tools that separate a business owner who truly knows their numbers from one who is guessing — and why that difference determines survival.

The Language of Business Finance

Section 22.1

What Accounting Is

Accounting is the process of recording, organizing, summarizing, and reporting a business's financial transactions. It answers basic but critical questions: How much money did the business bring in this month? How much did it spend? What does the business own? What does the business owe? Is the business making a profit or losing money?

These questions matter to many different audiences. Owners want to know if the business is healthy. Managers need financial data to make good decisions. Banks want to assess whether a borrower can repay a loan. Investors want to evaluate potential returns. Tax authorities require accurate reporting of income and expenses.

Two Branches of Accounting

BranchAudiencePurposeFormat
Financial AccountingOutside parties — investors, lenders, regulatorsProduce standardized reports for comparison and trustFollows GAAP; standardized rules
Managerial AccountingInside the business — managers and ownersSupport planning, control, and operational decisionsFlexible; detailed by product, department, or period
Accounting vs. Bookkeeping

Bookkeeping is the day-to-day recording of transactions — logging a sale, recording an expense, tracking a payment. Accounting is the broader practice of organizing, analyzing, and reporting that recorded data. In small businesses, the same person may do both. In larger organizations, these roles are typically separate.

Section 22.2

Revenue, Expenses, and Profit

Three of the most fundamental concepts in accounting are revenue, expenses, and profit. Understanding the relationship among these three is the foundation of reading any financial report.

TermDefinitionExample
RevenueTotal money earned from selling products or services during a period. Also called sales. Recorded when earned, not necessarily when cash is received.A bakery earns $12,000 from bread, pastries, and coffee sales in March
ExpensesCosts incurred to generate revenue and operate during a period. Includes rent, wages, utilities, materials, marketing, insurance, and depreciation.The bakery pays $3,200 in rent, $4,100 in wages, and $800 in supplies
ProfitWhat remains after subtracting expenses from revenue. Also called net income or earnings. Can be negative (a loss).$12,000 − $8,100 in total expenses = $3,900 profit
The Core Profit Formula

Profit = Revenue − Expenses

Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Net Profit = Revenue − All Expenses (COGS + Operating + Interest + Taxes)

Example 22.2.1
Calculating gross profit and net profit
  • GivenA retail store earns $85,000 in quarterly sales. Products cost $34,000 to purchase. Operating expenses total $28,000. Interest expense is $1,200. Estimated taxes are 22% of profit before taxes.
  • Gross Profit Gross Profit = $85,000 − $34,000 = $51,000 Gross Profit Margin = $51,000 ÷ $85,000 = 60%
  • Net Profit Operating Profit = $51,000 − $28,000 = $23,000 Profit Before Taxes = $23,000 − $1,200 = $21,800 Tax = $21,800 × 22% = $4,796 Net Profit = $21,800 − $4,796 = $17,004
Gross Profit: $51,000 (60% margin)  ·  Net Profit: $17,004
Profit is not cash

A business can show profit on paper while running short of actual money. Profit is calculated based on when revenue is earned and expenses are incurred — not necessarily when cash moves. This critical distinction is explored in Section 22.4.

Section 22.3

Assets, Liabilities, and Equity

Beyond the income picture, a business needs to understand its financial position — what it owns, what it owes, and what belongs to the owners. These three categories are the building blocks of the balance sheet.

Assets

TypeDescriptionExamples
Current AssetsExpected to be converted to cash or used within one yearCash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses
Non-Current AssetsExpected to provide value for more than one yearEquipment, buildings, vehicles, computers, patents, trademarks

Liabilities

TypeDescriptionExamples
Current LiabilitiesObligations due within one yearAccounts payable, short-term loans, wages payable, taxes owed
Long-Term LiabilitiesObligations due after one yearBusiness loans, mortgages, long-term lease obligations, bonds payable

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Equity
Understanding Equity

Equity = Assets − Liabilities

Equity represents what belongs to the owners after all debts are paid. It increases when the business earns profit and decreases when it incurs losses. Owner investments increase equity; distributions (dividends) decrease it.

This equation must always balance — it is the foundation of every balance sheet ever produced and the reason the system is called "double-entry bookkeeping."

Example 22.3.1
Calculating owner's equity from the accounting equation
  • GivenA business has total assets of $92,000 and total liabilities of $41,000.
  • Solve Equity = Assets − Liabilities Equity = $92,000 − $41,000 = $51,000
  • CheckAssets = Liabilities + Equity → $92,000 = $41,000 + $51,000 = $92,000. ✓
Owner's equity: $51,000
Section 22.4

Cash Flow

A business can be profitable and still fail. This seems paradoxical, but it happens regularly. The reason is cash flow — the movement of actual money in and out of a business.

Consider a consulting firm that completes a project in January and invoices the client for $20,000. The client pays in March. In the meantime, the firm still owes rent, salaries, and software subscriptions in January and February. The firm recognized $20,000 of revenue in January — but cash did not arrive until March. If it runs short in February, it may not be able to pay its bills even though the business is profitable.

Type of Cash FlowWhat It TracksWhat It Signals
OperatingCash from core business activities — selling products, paying employees, buying suppliesHow much actual cash the business generates from its work
InvestingCash used to buy long-term assets or received from selling themNegative often signals growth (buying equipment); positive may signal asset sales
FinancingCash from loans or investors; cash paid to repay debt or distribute dividendsPositive may indicate borrowing; watch if operating cash flow is also negative
Key Principle

A healthy business generates strong operating cash flow. Negative investing cash flow often reflects growth investments. A business that needs to borrow continuously just to cover operations — positive financing, weak operating — is at risk.

Example 22.4.1
Why profit and cash flow differ
  • ScenarioA photographer earns $6,000 in revenue in October but collects only $2,500 in cash (the rest is owed by clients). She incurs $1,800 in expenses, all paid in cash.
  • Profit Profit = Revenue − Expenses = $6,000 − $1,800 = $4,200
  • Cash Flow Operating Cash Flow = Cash Received − Cash Paid = $2,500 − $1,800 = $700
  • AnalysisShe shows $4,200 in profit — but only $700 in cash came in. The $3,500 gap is money owed by clients that has not yet arrived. If she needs to pay rent or buy equipment this month, she may not have enough cash even though the business is profitable.
Profit: $4,200  ·  Operating cash flow: $700  ·  Gap: $3,500 in uncollected receivables
Section 22.5

Records and Transactions

Accounting begins with transactions — any event with a financial impact on the business. Every purchase, sale, payment, and receipt is a transaction that must be recorded. Businesses organize these using a chart of accounts — a categorized list of every type of financial activity tracked, covering assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, and expenses.

Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Every transaction is recorded in at least two accounts — as a debit and a credit — ensuring the accounting equation always stays in balance.

Sell a product for $500 cash: Cash (asset) increases — debit. Revenue increases — credit.

Pay $200 in rent: Cash (asset) decreases — credit. Rent expense increases — debit.

Cash Basis vs. Accrual Basis

MethodRevenue Recorded WhenExpenses Recorded WhenBest For
Cash BasisCash is receivedCash is paidSmall businesses, sole proprietors; simpler and more intuitive
Accrual BasisRevenue is earned (regardless of payment)Expense is incurred (regardless of payment)Larger businesses; required under GAAP; more accurate long-term picture

Section 22.6

Income Statements

The income statement (also called the profit and loss statement or P&L) summarizes a business's revenues, expenses, and profit over a specific period of time — typically a month, quarter, or year. It moves from the top (revenue) downward through layers of expense to arrive at net profit — often called "the bottom line."

Sample Income Statement — Quarter Ending March 31
Revenue

Less: Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)− $60,000

Operating Expenses

Rent− $12,000
Marketing− $5,000
Utilities− $2,000
Insurance− $1,500
Depreciation− $3,000
Other Operating Expenses− $1,500
Total Operating Expenses− $60,000
Operating Profit (EBIT)$30,000
Below Operating

Profit Before Taxes$28,000

Reading the Layers

Gross profit reveals production efficiency. Operating profit reveals day-to-day operational efficiency. Net profit is what the owners actually earned after every obligation. A business with strong gross profit but weak net profit likely has high overhead costs — a target for management attention.

Section 22.7

Balance Sheets

The balance sheet is a snapshot of a business's financial position at a single point in time. While the income statement covers a period ("What happened this quarter?"), the balance sheet captures a moment ("What does the business look like today?"). It always displays the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

What the Balance Sheet Reveals

Liquidity — Compare current assets ($40,000) to current liabilities ($15,000). The business can comfortably meet short-term obligations. Leverage — Total liabilities ($35,000) versus total equity ($30,000) shows how much of the business is debt-financed. Growth — Comparing balance sheets over time shows whether assets and equity are building.

Section 22.8

Cash Flow Statements

The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of cash into and out of the business during a period. It reconciles the difference between profit (from the income statement) and the change in cash (from the balance sheet). Investors and lenders pay close attention to operating cash flow because it reflects how much actual cash the core business generates.

Sample Cash Flow Statement — Quarter Ending March 31
Operating Activities
Net Income$22,400
Adjustments to reconcile net income to cash:
Depreciation (non-cash expense)+ $3,000
Increase in Accounts Receivable− $4,000
Decrease in Inventory+ $2,000
Increase in Accounts Payable+ $1,500
Net Cash from Operating Activities$24,900
Investing Activities
Purchase of Equipment− $10,000
Net Cash from Investing Activities− $10,000
Financing Activities
Repayment of Business Loan− $5,000
Net Cash from Financing Activities− $5,000
Net Increase in Cash+ $9,900
Cash at Beginning of Period$8,100
Cash at End of Period$18,000
Reading the Cash Flow Statement

Net income is $22,400 but operating cash flow is $24,900. The $2,500 difference is largely explained by depreciation — a non-cash expense that reduces income but does not reduce cash. Strong operating cash flow confirms the business is self-sustaining. Negative investing cash flow (−$10,000) reflects an equipment purchase — a sign of investment in growth, not distress.

Section 22.9

Why Accurate Records Matter

Accurate accounting records are not just about satisfying tax authorities. They are a survival tool for the business itself. Business owners who rely on memory or gut feel instead of records consistently overestimate profits and underestimate costs.

ReasonWhat Happens Without Good Records
Decision-MakingDecisions about hiring, pricing, expansion, and cost-cutting are made blindly. Owners cannot tell whether pricing is working, which products are profitable, or whether growth is creating cash strain.
Tax ComplianceInaccurate records lead to incorrect tax filings — penalties, interest, audits, or legal consequences. Good records also support legitimate deductions that reduce the tax burden.
Loan ApplicationsBanks require financial statements before approving loans. Businesses without clean records may be unable to secure financing even when fundamentally healthy.
Fraud DetectionMany small business losses from employee theft go undetected for years simply because the owner never reconciled accounts or reviewed records carefully.
Investor ConfidenceInvestors conduct due diligence — reviewing financial records to verify the health of the business. Disorganized or inaccurate records destroy credibility and can kill deals.
Business ValuationThe sale price of a business depends heavily on documented financial performance. A business with clean, credible records is worth significantly more than an equivalent business with messy finances.

Chapter Summary and Review

  • Sec 22.2Revenue is money earned from sales. Expenses are costs incurred to operate. Gross profit equals revenue minus COGS. Net profit equals revenue minus all expenses. Profit is not the same as cash.

  • Sec 22.6The income statement shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a time period. It reveals gross profit, operating profit, and net profit — each layer identifying a different efficiency question.
  • Sec 22.7The balance sheet is a point-in-time snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity. It must balance per the accounting equation and reveals liquidity, leverage, and growth over time.
  • Sec 22.8The cash flow statement reconciles profit and actual cash movement across operating, investing, and financing activities. Strong operating cash flow signals a self-sustaining business.
  • Sec 22.9Accurate records support decision-making, tax compliance, loan applications, fraud detection, investor confidence, and business valuation. They are a survival tool, not merely a compliance requirement.

Key Terms Review

accounting
The process of recording, organizing, summarizing, and reporting a business's financial transactions.
revenue
Money earned from the sale of goods or services. Recorded when earned, not necessarily when cash is received.
expenses
Costs incurred to generate revenue and operate the business during a given period.
profit
Revenue minus all expenses. Also called net income. Can be negative (a loss).
gross profit
Revenue minus the direct cost of goods sold. Reveals production efficiency before overhead is considered.
assets
Everything a business owns that has economic value, divided into current (under one year) and non-current.
liabilities
Everything a business owes to others, divided into current (due within one year) and long-term.
equity
What belongs to the owners after all liabilities are subtracted from assets. Also called owner's equity or net worth.
accounting equation
Assets = Liabilities + Equity. This equation must always balance and is the foundation of every balance sheet.
cash flow
The actual movement of money in and out of a business. Distinct from profit, which is based on accrual timing.
income statement
A financial report showing revenue, expenses, and profit over a period of time. Also called the P&L statement.
balance sheet
A financial report showing assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time. Always balances.
cash flow statement
A financial report tracking the actual movement of cash across operating, investing, and financing activities.
COGS
Cost of Goods Sold — the direct costs of producing the goods or services sold during a period.
double-entry bookkeeping
A recording system where every transaction is entered as both a debit and a credit, keeping the books balanced.
chart of accounts
A categorized list of all account types a business tracks — assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, and expenses.
cash basis
Accounting method that records revenue when cash is received and expenses when cash is paid. Simpler; used by small businesses.
accrual basis
Accounting method that records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of cash timing. Required under GAAP for larger businesses.
accounts receivable
Money owed to the business by customers for goods or services already delivered. A current asset.
accounts payable
Money the business owes to suppliers for goods or services already received. A current liability.

Case Study

Case Study — Chapter 22
Two Bakeries, Two Outcomes

Maya and Devon each opened a bakery in the same town in the same year. Both reported similar revenues at the end of Year 1 — approximately $180,000 each.

Maya had taken a bookkeeping course and tracked every transaction using accounting software from day one. She could tell at any moment how much cash she had, what she owed suppliers, and what her profit margin was per product. When her flour costs increased by 15%, she spotted it in her cost of goods sold within two weeks and adjusted her pricing.

Devon ran his bakery from intuition. He checked his bank account balance regularly and felt comfortable as long as he saw money there. He did not track inventory costs separately or monitor individual expenses closely. He hired a part-time employee without calculating whether the revenue increase would cover the wage cost.

At the end of Year 2, Maya secured a small business loan and opened a second location. Devon's bakery closed — not because of poor sales, but because Devon could not explain to his bank why, despite steady revenue, his bank balance kept declining. The answer, discovered only after closure: rising ingredient costs had eroded his margins to nearly zero while his expenses had grown steadily. Devon had no records to detect the shift.

Discussion Questions
  1. What specific accounting tools could Devon have used to catch the problem earlier?
  2. Why is a rising bank balance not a reliable measure of business health?
  3. What information would a monthly income statement have revealed to Devon within 60–90 days?
  4. If you were Devon's advisor at the start of Year 1, what record-keeping system would you recommend and why?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics Scenario — Financial Reporting

A small business owner is preparing financial statements to show a potential investor. The business had a difficult year and net profit was low. The owner is considering recording some January revenue in December to make the current year look stronger.

Questions to consider: Is this legal under accrual accounting rules? Under cash basis? Is it ethical regardless of legality? What are the potential consequences if the investor discovers the adjustment later? What standards should guide financial reporting even in difficult periods?

Chapter Reflection

Think about a business you interact with regularly — a restaurant, a store, a service provider. Consider all the financial transactions that business must track: every sale, every supplier payment, every payroll cycle, every utility bill, every piece of equipment purchased.

What would happen to that business if no one kept accurate records? What decisions would go wrong? What opportunities would be missed? What risks would go undetected? Accounting is not glamorous, but it is the foundation on which every other business decision rests. The entrepreneurs and managers who understand their numbers make better decisions, survive tougher conditions, and build stronger enterprises than those who do not.

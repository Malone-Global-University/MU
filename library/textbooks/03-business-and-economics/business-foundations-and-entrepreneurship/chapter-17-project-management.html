Chapter 17 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

"A goal without a plan is just a wish."

— Antoine de Saint-Exupéry

Ideas are plentiful. Plans are useful. But execution is what actually creates results. Many business students learn to write mission statements, analyze markets, and design business models. Fewer learn how to take a plan and turn it into finished, delivered work. That gap — between having a plan and completing real work — is what project management is designed to close.

Every business runs projects. A restaurant launches a new menu. A nonprofit organizes a fundraising event. A startup builds its first product. A marketing team runs a campaign. A student team creates a business plan for a competition. All of these involve people, tasks, time, money, and the constant pressure to deliver something useful by a specific date.

This chapter introduces project management as a practical skill. You will learn how to break a large goal into tasks, organize those tasks into a timeline, assign responsibilities, track progress, manage risks, communicate with your team, and review results when the work is done. These skills apply directly to the capstone venture project at the end of this course — and to every job, freelance engagement, or entrepreneurial venture you pursue afterward.

The Foundation: Why Execution Is Different from Planning
Why Execution Matters · Projects vs. Ongoing Operations
Section 17.1

Why Execution Matters

Most business failures are not caused by bad ideas. They are caused by poor execution: tasks that never get finished, deadlines that slip, budgets that run over, communication that breaks down, and decisions that get delayed until the opportunity is gone. Execution means doing the work — converting strategy into action, action into deliverables, and deliverables into results.

What Gets in the Way of Execution

ObstacleWhat It Looks LikeWhy It Matters
Unclear ownershipEveryone thinks someone else is responsible for a taskTasks fall through the cracks; no one can be held accountable
Vague deliverables"Improve the website" vs. "Add contact form and compress images by Friday"The team works in circles; no one agrees on what "done" looks like
Underestimated effortThree-day task estimated as one dayDeadlines slip; the team is chronically rushed
Poor communicationDecisions not documented; no agenda in meetings; emails without action itemsMassive time wasted reconstructing what should have been shared clearly
Ignored warning signsSmall delays and unclear requirements dismissed earlyMinor issues compound into crises that cannot be resolved before deadline
The Planning Fallacy

Researchers have documented a reliable human tendency to underestimate how long tasks take — even when past experience suggests longer timelines. Building buffer time into a project schedule is not pessimism; it is accuracy. A plan that assumes everything goes right is a plan that assumes the unusual.

Project management is not only for construction managers or software engineers. Every entrepreneur, manager, marketer, operations coordinator, and team leader uses project management skills daily. The difference between a person who "stays busy" and a person who "gets things done" often comes down to how deliberately they manage their work.

Section 17.2

Projects Versus Ongoing Operations

Key Distinction

A project is a temporary effort with a defined beginning, end, and goal. It produces a specific deliverable or outcome that did not exist before.

An ongoing operation is work that repeats continuously to keep the business functioning — processing orders, restocking inventory, answering customer service messages, running payroll.

Projects need planning, timelines, and focused execution. Operations need consistent systems and procedures. Both matter; both require different tools.

CharacteristicProjectOngoing Operation
DurationTemporary — defined start and endContinuous — no planned end date
GoalProduces something new (product, event, plan)Maintains existing functions (orders, payroll, service)
Team structureOften assembled for the project; disbands afterStable team with defined ongoing roles
Primary toolProject plan, timeline, risk registerStandard operating procedures, process documentation
ExampleLaunching a new product lineProcessing customer orders daily
When Projects Become Operations

A startup builds its first e-commerce website (project), and then the site must be maintained, updated, and monitored continuously (operation). Recognizing this transition helps businesses staff appropriately and document the processes created during the project phase — so the operation does not depend on one person's memory.

Section 17.3

Tasks, Milestones, and Deliverables

The first step in project management is breaking a large goal into smaller, manageable pieces called tasks. A task should be specific, actionable, assignable to one person, and completable — meaning there is a clear point at which it is done.

Example 17.3.1
Vague vs. Well-Defined Tasks
  • Vague"Work on the marketing plan" — impossible to assign, impossible to mark complete, no clear output.
  • Better Draft the target customer profile for the marketing plan Research three competitor pricing strategies Write the promotional channels section (500 words) Review and revise the draft based on teammate feedback
  • WhyEach task is specific, owned by one person, and has a clear completion point. Progress is visible.

Three Core Planning Concepts

ConceptDefinitionExample
TaskA specific, assignable, completable unit of work"Write the executive summary (250 words)"
MilestoneA significant checkpoint marking the completion of a phase or important outcome — not ongoing work"Customer research complete"; "Website live"
DeliverableA specific, tangible output produced by the project — something you can point toFinished business plan; working prototype; launch event
Start with Deliverables

Begin every project plan by listing deliverables first. Then ask: what tasks must happen in order to produce each deliverable? Tasks that do not trace back to a deliverable are often unnecessary work.

Section 17.4

Timelines and Deadlines

A timeline turns a list of tasks into a schedule. Without a timeline, work drifts. With a timeline, the team knows what needs to happen and when. Timelines also reveal conflicts early — if a project requires twelve weeks but only eight are available, that problem surfaces immediately rather than becoming a crisis.

Five Steps to Build a Timeline

Step 1 — List all tasks. Use deliverables as anchors.

Step 2 — Estimate time for each task. Be honest; add buffer time, especially for tasks depending on other people.

Step 3 — Identify dependencies. Some tasks cannot start until others finish. Map these to prevent scheduling conflicts.

Step 4 — Assign start and end dates. Work backward from the final deadline if needed.

Step 5 — Visualize the schedule. A table, calendar, or Gantt chart makes the timeline easy to understand and share.

The Deadline Trap

Teams often treat a deadline as the moment to start working, not the moment to finish. A project due in three weeks feels distant for two weeks and then suddenly urgent in the final days. Build in review time before the final submission. The goal is to be done a few days early — not to finish at midnight the night before.

Example 17.4.1
Sample Task Dependency Map — Business Plan Project
  • No depsComplete customer research (can begin immediately)
  • Depends on 1Analyze competitors; Draft business model canvas (require customer research first)
  • Depends on 2Write marketing plan section; Create startup budget (require business model canvas first)
  • Depends on 3Build financial projections (requires startup budget first)
  • LastWrite executive summary; Design pitch deck; Review full plan (require all sections complete)
Key insight: mapping dependencies reveals which tasks are on the critical path — the sequence that determines the minimum time the project can take.
Section 17.5

Roles and Responsibility Charts

Confusion about who is responsible for what causes more project delays than almost any other single factor. When everyone assumes someone else is handling a task, it does not get done. When two people both try to own the same decision, conflict and rework follow. RACI charts are a simple, proven tool for preventing both problems.

The RACI Framework

R — Responsible   Who does the work on this task?

A — Accountable   Who owns the outcome and makes the final call? (Only one person per task.)

C — Consulted   Who should be asked for input before decisions are made?

I — Informed   Who should be kept updated on progress and outcomes?

TaskTeam LeadResearcherWriterClient
Define project goalsACCR
Complete market researchIRCI
Write business plan draftICRI
Review and approve final planACCR
RACI Rules

Every task should have exactly one A (accountable). Multiple A's create conflict; zero A's create gaps. If a task has no R, it will not get done. If it has no A, no one owns the outcome.

Section 17.6

Budgets and Resource Planning

When people hear "resources," they often think only of money. But a project budget includes time, money, equipment, and people. Failing to account for any of these leads to shortages in the middle of a project — which is one of the worst times to discover you lack what you need.

Resource TypeQuestions to AskCommon Oversight
TimeHow many hours will this require? Whose hours? Are those people available?Not accounting for competing demands on key team members
MoneyWhat are the licensing, material, printing, advertising costs?Forgetting small recurring costs (subscriptions, hosting)
Equipment & toolsWhat software, hardware, or physical equipment is needed?Assuming tools are available when they require purchase or training
PeopleDoes the project require skills the team has? If not, who provides them?Realizing mid-project that a needed skill is missing from the team

Scope Creep

Scope creep is the gradual, uncontrolled expansion of a project beyond its original boundaries. It usually arrives through small, individually reasonable additions: "Can we add one more feature?" Each feels minor; together they can double the project's time and cost.

Managing Scope

Have a clear agreement about what the project includes — and enforce that boundary. If new work is genuinely worth adding, treat it as a formal change: evaluate its impact on time and budget, then get approval before any work begins. Never let "just one more thing" go undocumented.

Section 17.7

Risk Tracking

Every project faces uncertainty. A supplier might be delayed. A key team member might get sick. A tool might not work as expected. Risk tracking does not mean assuming everything will go wrong — it means identifying the most likely and most serious threats early, so the team can prepare.

Four Risk Response Strategies

Avoid — Change the plan to eliminate the risk entirely.

Mitigate — Take action to reduce the likelihood or impact of the risk.

Transfer — Shift the risk to another party (insurance, contract clauses).

Accept — Acknowledge the risk but take no specific action, especially if likelihood or impact is low.

Sample Risk Register

RiskLikelihoodImpactResponse StrategyResponse Plan
Key team member unavailable for one weekMediumHighMitigateCross-train another member on their tasks in advance
Supplier delivers materials lateLowHighMitigateOrder materials two weeks before they are needed
Survey response rate too low for reliable dataHighMediumMitigateSend reminder emails; distribute through two channels
Software tool has technical problems on deadline dayLowMediumMitigateIdentify a backup tool and test it in advance
Scope expands due to stakeholder requestsHighHighAvoidDocument scope agreement; require formal approval for all changes
The Risk Register Principle

A risk that is identified and planned for rarely derails a project. A risk that is ignored often does. The value is not in predicting the future — it is in forcing the team to think through "what could go wrong" before it happens.

Section 17.8

Project Communication

Poor communication is one of the top causes of project failure — but it rarely looks like a single catastrophic breakdown. It accumulates through small failures: an email not sent, a decision not documented, a status update that never happened, a meeting where no one agreed on next steps. Good project communication is deliberate, consistent, and structured.

What a Good Status Update Covers

Status Update Components

Completed — What has been finished since the last update?

In progress — What is currently underway and on track?

Blocked — What is behind schedule or waiting on a dependency?

Decisions needed — What does the team need to decide or act on before the next check-in?

Meeting Discipline

Meetings are expensive. When five people spend an hour in a meeting that could have been a ten-minute email, the organization loses five person-hours of productive time. Effective project meetings share a few features: a written agenda distributed beforehand, a clear purpose (decision-making or problem-solving — not general discussion), a designated facilitator, and a written record of decisions and next steps.

If It Did Not Produce Action Items, It Was Not a Meeting — It Was a Conversation

If a meeting ends without specific tasks assigned to specific people with specific deadlines, the time was likely wasted. Every project meeting should end with: Who is doing what? By when?

Documentation Essentials

DocumentWhat It ContainsWhy It Matters
Project planTasks, timeline, responsibilitiesThe single source of truth for what the project includes and who owns what
Meeting notesDecisions made, action items, attendeesPrevents disputes about what was agreed; creates accountability
Decision logKey decisions and reasoningPrevents the team from re-debating solved problems
Status logWeekly progress, issues, changesEnables anyone to understand project health at a glance
Deliverables recordCompleted outputs with version and dateConfirms what was produced and when
Section 17.9

Reviewing Results

When a project ends, the natural impulse is to move immediately to the next thing. But skipping the review is a missed opportunity. Every project generates useful information: what worked, what did not, what took longer than expected, what surprised the team. A project review — sometimes called a retrospective or post-mortem — captures that information while it is still fresh.

Five Questions for a Strong Project Review

What did we accomplish? — Review deliverables and outcomes against original goals. Where was the gap?

What went well? — Identify practices and decisions that produced good results. Repeat these.

What should we do differently? — Identify problems, delays, and miscommunications. Address these next time.

What did we learn? — Knowledge about customers, suppliers, tools, or team dynamics that is valuable beyond this project.

What would we tell a future team starting this same project? — This framing surfaces the most practical insights.

Applying Lessons Learned

A review that produces insights but no action is only partly useful. The most effective teams update their templates and checklists, share lessons with other teams facing similar projects, and revise time and cost estimates for similar future work. In entrepreneurship, learning from each project is a competitive advantage — teams that improve become faster and more capable over time.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 17.1Execution fails when ownership is unclear, deliverables are vague, effort is underestimated, communication breaks down, or early warning signs are ignored. Project management is a practical skill used in every business role.
  • Sec 17.2Projects are temporary, goal-directed efforts that produce something new. Ongoing operations are continuous, repeating activities that keep the business running. Both matter; both need different approaches.
  • Sec 17.3Tasks should be specific, assignable, and completable. Milestones mark major checkpoints — the completion of a phase. Deliverables are the tangible outputs the project must produce. Start planning from deliverables and work backward.
  • Sec 17.4Timelines convert task lists into schedules. Build them by estimating effort, mapping dependencies, and working backward from the deadline. Buffer time prevents the last-minute rush. Gantt charts visualize the schedule effectively.
  • Sec 17.5RACI charts assign Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed roles to each task. Every task needs exactly one Accountable owner. Role clarity prevents both gaps (no owner) and conflicts (too many owners).
  • Sec 17.6Project budgets cover time, money, equipment, and people. Scope creep is the gradual expansion of a project beyond its original definition — manage it by documenting scope and requiring formal approval for changes.
  • Sec 17.7A risk register lists identified risks with likelihood, impact, and a planned response. The four response strategies are avoid, mitigate, transfer, and accept. A planned risk rarely derails a project; an ignored one often does.
  • Sec 17.8Good project communication requires regular status updates (completed, in progress, blocked, decisions needed), disciplined meetings with agendas and action items, and consistent documentation of plans, decisions, and progress.
  • Sec 17.9Project reviews capture what was accomplished, what went well, what should change, and what was learned. Applied lessons — updated templates, revised estimates, shared knowledge — turn individual project experience into organizational capability.

Key Terms Review

project
A temporary effort with a defined beginning, end, and goal that produces a specific deliverable or outcome not previously existing.
ongoing operation
Work that repeats continuously to keep a business functioning, as distinct from a time-limited project.
task
A specific, assignable, completable unit of work within a project — small enough for one person to own and mark as done.
milestone
A significant checkpoint in a project marking the completion of a phase or important outcome. Not ongoing work — a moment of arrival.
deliverable
A specific, tangible output produced by a project — a document, product, presentation, system, or event.
timeline
A schedule that assigns start and end dates to project tasks, revealing conflicts and preventing last-minute crises.
Gantt chart
A horizontal bar chart that visualizes project tasks against a time scale, showing duration, sequence, and overlap.
dependency
A relationship between tasks in which one task cannot begin until another is complete.
RACI chart
A responsibility assignment tool in which each task is assigned roles: Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed.
scope creep
The gradual, uncontrolled expansion of a project's boundaries beyond its original definition, usually through small additions.
risk register
A documented list of identified project risks with notes on likelihood, impact, and planned responses.
status update
A regular communication summarizing what has been completed, what is in progress, what is blocked, and what needs a decision.
documentation
The practice of recording important project information, decisions, and outcomes so the team can access them later.
project review
A structured reflection conducted after a project ends to capture lessons learned and improve future work. Also called a retrospective or post-mortem.
planning fallacy
The documented human tendency to underestimate how long tasks and projects will take, even when past experience suggests longer timelines.

Case Study

Case Study — Chapter 17
The Launch That Almost Wasn't

Maya and her two partners had spent six weeks preparing their food truck launch. They had a menu, a truck, permits, and a social media following of over 400 people. Their launch date was set for the first Saturday of the month.

Five days before the launch, their commercial kitchen supplier called to say the certified kitchen space they had reserved would not be available until the following week — a permit issue the supplier had not disclosed earlier.

The partners had two choices: delay the launch, disappointing the followers they had already promoted to, or find an alternative solution fast.

Maya pulled up the project plan she had maintained throughout the preparation. The plan had a risk register that included "kitchen access delayed" as a medium-likelihood risk. Her planned response: identify backup kitchen options in advance.

She had never actually identified those backup options.

Within 48 hours, Maya found a church with a licensed commercial kitchen that rented weekend time to small food businesses. The fee was $120 — not in the original budget, but manageable. They launched on schedule.

  1. What did Maya do right in her project management that helped her navigate this problem? Be specific.
  2. What did she fail to do, and what was the consequence of that gap? How did she end up exposed despite having a risk register?
  3. If you were advising the team before the launch, what other risks would you have added to their risk register?
  4. What lesson does this case illustrate about risk registers beyond simply "write down the risks"?

Ethics and Decision Point

Ethics and Decision Point — Chapter 17
The Uneven Team

A student team is working on a group business plan project worth 30% of their final grade. Two of the six members have been consistently missing deadlines, submitting incomplete work, and not responding to messages. The project is due in one week. The team lead, Jordan, must decide how to handle the situation.

  • Option A — Absorb the workDo the missing members' work yourself with one reliable teammate to protect the group grade. Say nothing to the instructor.
  • Option B — Confront and cutGive the underperforming members a final deadline in a group meeting. If they miss it, submit the project without their sections and explain the situation to the instructor.
  • Option C — Document and escalateEmail the instructor now to document the problem, ask for guidance, and continue pushing the underperforming members to contribute.
Ethics DiscussionAnalysis Questions
  1. What are the short-term and long-term consequences of each option for Jordan, the underperforming members, and the rest of the team?
  2. Which option would you choose? Explain your reasoning using an ethical framework introduced in Chapter 4 of this course.
  3. How might a clear RACI chart and documented accountability check-ins earlier in the project have prevented this situation from reaching a crisis point?

Applied Practice

Activity 1Task Breakdown

Choose a project you are currently working on — the class capstone, a personal goal, a job, or an event you are planning. Break it into at least twelve specific tasks.

  1. For each task, write a task name (specific and completable), an estimated number of hours, the name of the person responsible, and whether the task depends on another task.
  2. Order the tasks by dependency to create a logical sequence.
  3. Identify which task is on the critical path — the one whose delay would delay the whole project.
Activity 2One-Week Timeline

Using your tasks from Activity 1 (or a shorter list of 6–8 items), create a simple weekly timeline. Assign each task to a day and a person using a table or calendar grid.

  1. Are there any days where one person is overloaded?
  2. Are there any tasks that were not accounted for initially?
  3. How much buffer time do you have before the final deadline?
Activity 3Project Communication Simulation

Imagine you are the project lead on a team of four people preparing a 10-minute business pitch for a competition in three weeks. Write a 150-word status update for your team after the first week of work.

  1. Your update must include: what has been completed, what is in progress, what is behind or blocked, what decisions need to be made, and what each person should accomplish before the next check-in.

Mini Project

Mini Project
The Event Project Plan

With a partner or small group, plan a hypothetical business event — a product launch party, a pop-up market, a customer appreciation day, or a community fundraiser. Your plan must include:

  1. Project overview: One paragraph describing the event, its purpose, and its target audience.
  2. Deliverables list: At least five specific deliverables the project must produce.
  3. Task list: At least fifteen tasks broken down from the deliverables, each with an owner and estimated time.
  4. Timeline: A visual representation (table, calendar, or chart) showing tasks over a four-week period.
  5. Budget summary: Estimated costs in at least four resource categories.
  6. Risk register: At least four risks with response plans.
  7. Communication plan: How will the team stay coordinated throughout the project?

Present your plan in a ten-minute team presentation. Be prepared to explain your prioritization choices and risk responses.

Chapter 5 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces the financial institutions that hold, protect, and move money for individuals and households. Banks and credit unions are not just convenient places to keep cash. They are regulated institutions with specific rules, products, costs, and protections that directly affect how much money a person pays in fees, earns in interest, and keeps safe from loss.

Students who understand financial institutions can open the right accounts, avoid unnecessary fees, choose between competing options, and know what protections apply when something goes wrong. Students who do not understand these institutions often pay more, earn less, and carry more risk than necessary.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain what banks and credit unions are and how they differ, describe the main types of financial accounts and how they work, calculate basic account interest and fee scenarios, compare accounts using cost and benefit criteria, and identify the protections that apply to federally insured deposits.

Opening Question

You receive your first paycheck and need to decide where to keep the money. A large national bank has a branch nearby with a $12 monthly fee (waived with direct deposit). A credit union two miles away offers free checking and a savings account earning 4.50% APY. An online bank offers no fees and the same high rate — but has no physical locations. Which do you choose? By the end of this chapter, you will have the framework to answer this question for yourself — based on your actual needs, costs, and priorities.

Section 5.1

Why Financial Accounts Matter

Most people interact with the financial system through a financial account. A financial account is a record-keeping relationship between a person and a financial institution. The institution holds your money, tracks your transactions, and provides access to your funds through various payment tools.

Without a financial account, everyday financial life becomes harder and more expensive. People without accounts often rely on check-cashing services, which charge fees to convert paper checks into cash. They may pay to buy money orders for bills instead of writing a check or initiating a free transfer. They cannot easily receive direct deposit of a paycheck. They have no record of their saving. They may keep cash at home, where it earns nothing and is vulnerable to theft or loss.

A basic financial account does several things at once. It keeps money safe. It creates a transaction record. It provides access to the payment system — the network of electronic transfers, debit cards, bill payments, and automated transactions that most modern financial life runs on. It may earn a small amount of interest. And it provides legal protections that cash kept at home does not.

The Banked and the Unbanked

Economists and researchers use the term banked to describe individuals and households with at least one account at a financial institution. The term unbanked describes those without any such account. Millions of households are unbanked or underbanked — they have some account access but rely heavily on alternative financial services such as check cashing, payday lending, or money orders.

Being unbanked is often expensive. The fees paid to cash checks, purchase money orders, and access financial services outside the banking system can add up to hundreds of dollars per year — money that a person with a free or low-cost checking account would keep. Understanding financial accounts and how to access them is one of the most practical financial skills a person can develop.

Section 5.2

Banks and Credit Unions

Two major types of depository institutions accept deposits from the public and offer financial accounts: commercial banks and credit unions. A depository institution is any financial institution that accepts deposits — money placed by customers — and holds those deposits in accounts.

Commercial Banks

A commercial bank is a for-profit financial institution that accepts deposits, makes loans, and provides financial services to individuals, businesses, and organizations. Commercial banks are owned by shareholders. Their goal is to earn profit for those shareholders by charging for loans, earning interest on assets, and collecting fees on accounts and services.

Commercial banks are regulated at both the federal and state level. National banks are chartered by the federal government and regulated primarily by the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC). State-chartered banks that are members of the Federal Reserve System are regulated by the Federal Reserve. The most important consumer protection for bank customers is FDIC insurance, which is covered in detail in Section 5.7.

Credit Unions

A credit union is a not-for-profit financial cooperative owned by its members. Every person who has an account at a credit union is a member and a partial owner of that institution. Credit unions exist to serve their members rather than to earn profit for outside shareholders.

Credit unions are organized around a field of membership — a defined group of people eligible to join. Today, many credit unions have expanded their fields of membership, and some serve anyone who lives or works in a certain geographic area. Because credit unions do not distribute profit to outside shareholders, they often return value to members in the form of lower loan rates, higher savings rates, and lower fees. Credit unions are regulated primarily by the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA).

Neither Is Universally Better

The decision between a bank and a credit union is not about which type is always superior. It is about which institution best matches your needs, situation, and priorities. Many households have accounts at both a bank and a credit union, using each for different purposes.

Online Banks

Online banks are banks that operate primarily or entirely through digital channels — no physical branches. Because they have lower operating costs, they often offer fewer fees and higher interest rates on savings accounts than traditional banks. The tradeoff is the absence of in-person service and, sometimes, fewer ATM options. Online banks are FDIC-insured if they are chartered banks — their lack of branches does not reduce depositor protections.

Section 5.3

Checking Accounts

A checking account is a deposit account designed for frequent transactions. Today checking accounts support electronic transfers, debit card purchases, online bill payment, and mobile payments. When you deposit money into a checking account, the institution holds those funds on your behalf and records the balance. When you make a payment, the institution reduces your balance by the payment amount and sends those funds through the payment network to the recipient.

Balance vs. Available Balance

Your account balance is the total amount of money currently in your account. Your available balance may differ — it reflects funds available to spend after accounting for pending transactions, holds, or deposits that have not yet fully cleared. Always check your available balance before spending. A balance that looks sufficient may have pending deductions not yet reflected.

Overdrafts and Overdraft Protection

An overdraft occurs when you spend more than your available balance. Banks and credit unions handle overdrafts in several ways. The institution may simply decline the transaction. It may approve the transaction and charge an overdraft fee — historically $25 to $35 per transaction, though many institutions have reduced or eliminated these fees under regulatory and competitive pressure. Or it may offer overdraft protection, linking your checking account to a savings account or line of credit that automatically covers shortfalls — typically for a much smaller fee than a standard overdraft charge.

Waiver Conditions Matter

Many institutions waive monthly maintenance fees if you maintain a minimum balance, set up direct deposit, or make a minimum number of transactions per month. A fee that appears unavoidable may be easy to avoid if you understand the conditions. Always read the waiver conditions before opening an account.

Section 5.4

Savings Accounts

A savings account is a deposit account designed for storing money over time rather than for frequent daily transactions. Savings accounts typically earn interest on the balance and have fewer transaction features than checking accounts — no debit card, limited check writing, and possible restrictions on how often you can make withdrawals.

How Savings Account Interest Works

Savings accounts earn interest — a periodic payment made by the institution to the account holder in exchange for the use of the deposited funds. Banks take customer deposits and use them to fund loans. The interest paid to depositors is part of the cost of that funding.

Example 5.4.1
Simple Interest on a Savings Deposit
  • ProblemYou deposit $1,000 into a savings account earning 2.00% annual interest. You leave it there for one year with no additional deposits or withdrawals. How much interest do you earn? What is your ending balance?
  • FormulaInterest = Principal × Rate × Time
  • Calculate Interest = $1,000 × 0.02 × 1 = $20.00 Ending balance = $1,000 + $20 = $1,020.00
  • Check2% of $1,000 is $20. That is 2 cents per dollar — reasonable for one year at 2%.
  • InterpretLeaving $1,000 in a 2% savings account for one year earns $20 in interest at no risk to your principal.
Answer: $20.00 interest earned. Ending balance: $1,020.00.

High-Yield Savings Accounts

A high-yield savings account is a savings account that offers a higher interest rate than a standard savings account — particularly common at online banks with lower overhead costs. A high-yield savings account at an FDIC- or NCUA-insured institution is just as safe as any other insured savings account. The higher rate reflects the institution's business model, not higher risk to the depositor.

Section 5.5

Account Fees

Fees are among the most important factors to evaluate when comparing financial accounts. Fees reduce the effective value of holding an account. A checking account with a $12 monthly fee costs $144 per year — money that leaves your account without providing financial benefit. Before choosing an account, calculate the realistic annual cost of fees based on how you actually use a bank account.

Example 5.5.1
Annual Fee Cost Comparison — Three Scenarios
  • Scenario AMaria opens a checking account with a $10 monthly fee, waived if she sets up direct deposit. She earns $2,200/month and sets up direct deposit.

    Annual maintenance fee: $0 (waiver met)
  • Scenario BJames opens the same account but does not set up direct deposit. He pays $10/month.

    Annual maintenance fee: $10 × 12 = $120
  • Scenario CJames also uses out-of-network ATMs twice per month. His bank charges $3 per transaction.

    Annual ATM fees: $3 × 2 × 12 = $72 Total annual fees: $120 + $72 = $192
  • InterpretJames is paying $192 per year for a checking account. A no-fee account at a credit union or online bank would save him that entire amount — $960 over five years, before considering what the money could have earned if saved.
Key insight: Calculating total annual fee cost — not just the monthly fee — reveals the real price of an account.

Strategies for Avoiding Fees

The most effective strategies for avoiding common account fees include: setting up direct deposit if required for fee waiver; maintaining the minimum balance if feasible; using only in-network ATMs or choosing an account that reimburses ATM fees; opting for electronic statements instead of paper; monitoring balances to avoid overdrafts; and choosing accounts with no minimum balance requirements from the start.

Section 5.6

Interest on Deposit Accounts

Annual Percentage Yield (APY)

The annual percentage yield (APY) is the standardized measure of interest earned on a deposit account over one year, taking into account the effect of compounding. When comparing savings accounts, always compare APY — not the stated interest rate alone. APY is calculated and disclosed using a standard method, making comparisons across institutions meaningful.

A savings account with an APY of 4.00% will earn more over a year than one with an APY of 3.50%, regardless of how the two institutions compound interest internally.

Approximate Annual Interest Formula
Annual Interest ≈ Balance × APY (expressed as a decimal)

This is an approximation suitable for introductory comparisons. The exact amount depends on compounding frequency and balance changes during the year. For precise calculations, see Chapter 10.

Example 5.6.1
Comparing APY Across Three Savings Accounts
  • ProblemYou have $5,000 to deposit. Three accounts offer different APYs. Which earns the most interest in one year?
  • Calculate Community Bank Savings (0.50% APY): $5,000 × 0.0050 = $25.00 Credit Union Savings (1.25% APY): $5,000 × 0.0125 = $62.50 Online Bank HY Savings (4.50% APY): $5,000 × 0.0450 = $225.00
  • InterpretThe difference between the lowest and highest rate is $200 per year on a $5,000 balance — simply from choosing a different account for the same amount of money.
The online high-yield account earns $225 — nine times more than the community bank at 0.50% APY. The only tradeoff is the absence of in-person service.
APY Rates Change Over Time

The interest rates offered on deposit accounts are not fixed permanently. They change as general economic conditions and Federal Reserve policy change. An account offering 4.50% APY today may offer 2.00% APY in two years if interest rates fall. Keeping an eye on your account's current APY — and being willing to switch if better options become available — is part of active account management.

Section 5.7

Deposit Insurance

One of the most important and least understood consumer protections in personal finance is deposit insurance — a government-backed guarantee that protects depositors when a bank or credit union fails.

When a bank or credit union becomes insolvent and cannot meet its obligations, deposit insurance prevents depositors from losing their money up to specified limits. The government steps in and makes depositors whole, even if the institution cannot. This protection is why you do not need to worry about whether your bank is having a bad quarter before you deposit your paycheck — as long as your institution is insured and your deposits are within coverage limits, your money is safe from institutional failure.

What Deposit Insurance Does Not Cover

Deposit insurance does not protect against fraud, theft, or losses in investment accounts. It covers only the failure of the institution itself. Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, cryptocurrency, life insurance policies, annuities, and the contents of safe deposit boxes are not covered by FDIC or NCUA insurance.

FDIC Insurance (Banks)

The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) is an independent federal agency that insures deposits at banks and savings institutions. FDIC coverage is $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category. The ownership category distinction matters: a single account in your name alone, a joint account with another person, and a retirement account (IRA) at the same bank fall into different ownership categories — meaning a single person could have more than $250,000 protected at one institution if accounts are held under different categories.

For most individuals: if you have less than $250,000 on deposit at a single FDIC-insured bank across all accounts in your name, your deposits are fully protected.

NCUA Insurance (Credit Unions)

The National Credit Union Administration (NCUA) provides equivalent protection for credit union members through the National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund (NCUSIF). Coverage is $250,000 per member, per insured credit union, per ownership category — essentially identical to FDIC coverage in structure and limits. Federal credit unions are required to be NCUA-insured. Most state-chartered credit unions are also NCUA-insured.

Deposit Insurance vs. SIPC Protection

When you see brokerage or investment advertisements mentioning "SIPC protection," that refers to the Securities Investor Protection Corporation, which protects customers if a brokerage firm fails — but it does not protect against investment losses. Deposit insurance (FDIC/NCUA) and SIPC protection are entirely different things covering entirely different types of accounts and risks.

Opening and Managing Accounts

What You Need to Open an Account

Current addressA current mailing or residential address
Initial depositSome accounts require a minimum opening deposit; others can be opened with any amount, including zero

Some institutions may check your banking history using a service called ChexSystems, a consumer reporting agency that tracks negative banking history such as unpaid overdrafts, fraud flags, and forced account closures. If your ChexSystems report shows negative history, some institutions will decline to open an account. However, many credit unions and banks offer second-chance checking accounts specifically for people with negative banking history who are working to establish or rebuild a banking relationship.

The Account Agreement

When you open an account, you agree to an account agreement (also called deposit agreement or account disclosures). This document describes all fees and when they are charged, interest rates and how they are calculated, overdraft policies and options, how the institution can change account terms, and your rights and responsibilities as an account holder. Account agreements can be long and written in dense language — but knowing where to find yours matters. When a fee surprises you, the account agreement is usually where the answer is.

Managing an Account Day to Day

Once your account is open, ongoing management involves monitoring your balance regularly through the mobile app, online portal, or account alerts; reviewing statements monthly to catch errors, unauthorized transactions, or unexpected fees; keeping contact information current so the institution can reach you about account issues or suspicious activity; and responding promptly to fraud alerts when the institution contacts you about unusual transactions.

Section 5.10

Choosing Accounts for Real Needs

Financial accounts are tools. The right tool depends on your specific situation, and your situation will change over time. The following scenarios illustrate how people at different life stages and financial situations might think about account selection.

Scenario A
First Checking Account — Young Adult, Starting Work

Anya is 18 and starting her first part-time job. She will receive direct deposit. She does not have much money saved, so she cannot reliably maintain a $1,500 minimum balance. She uses her phone for everything and has no strong preference for in-person banking.

Key priorities: No monthly fee (with direct deposit waiver or no fee at all), good mobile app, accessible ATMs.

Good options to explore: Online bank with no fee requirements and a large ATM network, or a credit union with a student account or low-fee checking.

Less ideal: A traditional bank with a $1,500 minimum balance requirement, since she may not consistently meet it and would pay the monthly fee frequently.

Scenario B
Savings Account for Emergency Fund — Adult, Working Full-Time

Marcus has a checking account and wants to open a separate savings account for his emergency fund. He wants the money to be safe and accessible, but does not plan to touch it for routine spending. He wants to earn as much interest as possible without risk.

Key priorities: High APY, FDIC/NCUA insurance, easy transfer to checking when needed, no fees.

Good options to explore: High-yield online savings account, or a credit union savings account with a competitive rate.

Less ideal: A savings account at the same large bank where he has his checking account if that bank's savings APY is 0.01% — he is giving up significant interest earnings for no reason.

Scenario C
Joint Account — Partners Managing Shared Expenses

Daniela and Erica are partners sharing an apartment and household expenses. They want a joint checking account to pay shared bills — rent, utilities, groceries — while keeping separate accounts for personal spending.

Key priorities: Joint account access for two account holders, direct deposit from both employers if possible, easy bill payment, no fees.

Note on joint accounts: In a joint account, both account holders typically have equal access to all funds and equal responsibility for any negative balance. This arrangement requires trust and clear communication about spending.

Scenario D
Banking Without a Social Security Number

Samuel recently moved to the United States and does not yet have a Social Security Number. He has an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) issued by the IRS.

Key priorities: An institution that accepts ITIN for account opening.

Good options to explore: Many credit unions explicitly welcome members with ITINs. Some large banks also accept ITINs. Community development financial institutions (CDFIs) are another option — they are mission-driven institutions often serving underserved communities.

Scenario E
Rebuilding After a Banking Problem

Tyler had a checking account that went negative two years ago. He was unable to pay the overdraft, the bank closed the account and reported it to ChexSystems. He now has difficulty opening a standard account.

Key priorities: An institution that offers second-chance checking, or a prepaid debit card as a transitional tool.

Good options to explore: Second-chance checking accounts at credit unions or community banks that do not rely solely on ChexSystems. After using the account responsibly for a period of time (typically 12–24 months), the ChexSystems record may no longer disqualify him from standard accounts.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 5.1Financial accounts hold money safely, create transaction records, and provide access to the payment system. Being unbanked often means paying hundreds of dollars per year in fees for basic financial services that account holders receive at low or no cost.

  • Sec 5.3Checking accounts support frequent transactions. The available balance — not the account balance — is the correct number to check before spending.   • Sec 5.5Fees reduce the real value of holding an account. Calculating the total annual cost — including maintenance fees, ATM fees, and overdraft fees — reveals the true price of an account and enables honest comparisons. Many fees can be avoided by meeting waiver conditions or choosing no-fee institutions.
  • Sec 5.6Annual percentage yield (APY) is the standardized measure for comparing interest on deposit accounts. Approximate annual interest equals Balance × APY. Small differences in APY can mean hundreds of dollars per year on typical savings balances. APY rates change as economic conditions change.
  • Sec 5.7FDIC insurance (banks) and NCUA/NCUSIF insurance (credit unions) protect depositors up to $250,000 per depositor per insured institution per ownership category when an institution fails. Deposit insurance covers checking accounts, savings accounts, money market deposit accounts, and CDs — not investment accounts, stocks, bonds, or cryptocurrency.
  • Sec 5.8Opening an account requires government-issued ID, SSN or ITIN, a current address, and sometimes an initial deposit. ChexSystems may affect approval; second-chance accounts are available at many institutions. The account agreement defines all fees, rates, and terms — read it when something is unclear.

  • Sec 5.10Account selection depends on individual circumstances: income timing, balance levels, preferences for digital versus in-person service, identification documents available, and banking history. The best account is not the most advertised — it is the one that fits your real life at the lowest total cost.

Calculation Review

Formula: Simple Interest on a Deposit
Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Where Rate is expressed as a decimal and Time is in years. Example: $2,000 × 0.035 × 1 = $70.00 interest earned in one year at 3.5%.

Formula: Approximate Annual Interest Using APY
Annual Interest ≈ Balance × APY (as a decimal)

This approximation is useful for comparing accounts. For exact calculations accounting for compounding frequency and balance changes, see Chapter 10.

Formula: Annual Fee Cost
Annual Fee Cost = Monthly Fee × 12 + (Per-Transaction Fee × Monthly Frequency × 12)

Apply to each fee type separately, then sum. Compare total annual cost across account options before choosing.

Practice Problems

Financial Systems Connection

Personal financial accounts do not exist in isolation. When you deposit money into a checking or savings account, that money enters a larger financial system. The institution uses those deposits — pooled with deposits from many other customers — to fund loans to other customers and businesses, to hold in reserve as required by regulation, and to invest in other financial assets. The interest you earn on your savings account is one output of this process: the institution pays you a portion of what it earns from using your deposit.

This flow of money — from savers to institutions to borrowers and back again — is one of the most fundamental functions of the financial system. Banks and credit unions act as financial intermediaries: they stand between those with excess funds (depositors) and those who need funds (borrowers), providing a more efficient and safer system than if every saver had to find and directly lend to every borrower.

Deposit insurance reinforces this system by maintaining public confidence. If depositors feared that any bank failure would wipe out their savings, they might withdraw funds at the first sign of trouble — causing exactly the kind of bank failure they feared. Deposit insurance breaks this cycle by assuring depositors that their money is safe regardless of the institution's short-term condition.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "All banks are basically the same."

Banks vary significantly in their fees, interest rates, customer service, technology, ATM access, and mission. Choosing a bank based on name recognition or habit rather than comparison can cost hundreds of dollars per year.

Misconception: "Credit unions are only for people in certain professions."

While some credit unions do have specific membership requirements, many have broad fields of membership and accept nearly anyone who lives or works in their geographic area. It is always worth checking eligibility before assuming you cannot join.

Misconception: "Online banks are not as safe as traditional banks."

FDIC-insured online banks carry exactly the same deposit protection as brick-and-mortar banks. The safety of your deposits does not depend on whether the institution has physical branches.

Misconception: "Overdraft protection means I can spend whatever I want."

Overdraft protection prevents declined transactions, but it does not eliminate the cost of overspending. Overdraft protection typically involves a fee, and if it transfers from a linked credit card, it creates debt that must be repaid with interest.

Misconception: "High-yield savings accounts are risky because of the high rate."

A high-yield savings account at an FDIC- or NCUA-insured institution carries the same depositor protection as any other insured savings account. The rate reflects the institution's business model (often lower overhead for online banks), not higher risk to the depositor.

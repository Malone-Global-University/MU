Appendix A

Mathematics for Personal Finance

This appendix reviews the arithmetic and quantitative skills used throughout the textbook. Students who need a refresher on any of these tools should work through the relevant section before or alongside the chapters where each skill appears.

A.1 Percentages

A percentage expresses a part of a whole as a fraction of 100. To convert a percentage to a decimal, divide by 100. To convert a decimal to a percentage, multiply by 100.

FormExample
Percentage6%
Decimal0.06
Fraction6/100 or 3/50
Example A.1
Finding a percentage of a number
  • ProblemWhat is 8% of $1,500?
  • Calculate$1,500 × 0.08 = $120
Answer: $120
Common Percentage Applications

Interest rate · Savings rate · Tax rate · Credit utilization · Down payment percentage · Expense ratio

A.2 Ratios

A ratio compares two quantities of the same kind and can be written as 3:1, 3/1, or "3 to 1."

Example A.2
Debt-to-income ratio
  • GivenMonthly debt payments = $500; gross monthly income = $2,500
  • Calculate$500 ÷ $2,500 = 0.20, or 20%
DTI = 20%

A.3 Averages

The mean (arithmetic average) is the sum of all values divided by the number of values. The median is the middle value when data is arranged in order and is often more useful when extreme values would distort the average.

Example A.3
Monthly spending average
  • GivenMonthly spending: $1,200 / $1,450 / $1,100 / $1,250
  • CalculateSum = $5,000 ÷ 4 months = $1,250 per month
Mean monthly spending: $1,250

A.4 Simple Interest

Simple Interest Formula

I = P × r × t

A = P + I = P(1 + r × t)

Where P = principal, r = annual rate (decimal), t = time in years.

Example A.4
Simple interest on a loan
  • Given$2,000 loan at 5% annual interest for 3 years
  • CalculateI = $2,000 × 0.05 × 3 = $300 Total repaid = $2,000 + $300 = $2,300
Interest: $300 · Total: $2,300

A.5 Compound Interest

Compound Interest Formulas

Annual compounding: A = P × (1 + r)^t

n periods per year: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Compounding Frequencyn
Annually1
Semi-annually2
Quarterly4
Monthly12
Daily365
Example A.5
Compound growth
  • Given$1,000 at 6% per year, 10 years, annual compounding
  • CalculateA = $1,000 × (1.06)^10 = $1,000 × 1.7908 ≈ $1,790.85
Future value: $1,790.85

A.7 Inflation Adjustment

Real Value and Real Return

Real Value = Nominal Value ÷ (1 + inflation rate)^t

Real Return ≈ Nominal Return − Inflation Rate

Example A.7
Purchasing power over time
  • Given$10,000 today; 3% annual inflation; 10 years
  • CalculateReal Value = $10,000 ÷ (1.03)^10 = $10,000 ÷ 1.344 ≈ $7,441
  • Interpret$10,000 today buys what $7,441 buys after 10 years of 3% inflation.

A.8 Payment Comparison

When comparing loan options, always calculate total cost — not just the monthly payment.

Total Cost Formulas

Total Cost = Monthly Payment × Number of Payments

Total Interest = Total Cost − Original Principal

LoanMonthly PaymentMonthsTotal Cost
Loan A$20036$7,200
Loan B$15048$7,200
Loan C$13060$7,800
Watch Out

Loan C has the lowest monthly payment but the highest total cost. The monthly payment is not the full picture of what you pay.

A.9 Debt-to-Income Ratio

DTI Formula

DTI = Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income

Lenders typically prefer DTI below 36%. Above 43% is considered high risk for mortgage lending.

A.10 Savings Rate

Savings Rate Formula

Savings Rate = Amount Saved ÷ Gross Income × 100

Example: $280 saved ÷ $2,800 income × 100 = 10%

C.5 Deposit Insurance

FDIC and NCUA Coverage

The FDIC insures deposits at member banks up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per account ownership category.

The NCUA provides equivalent protection at federally insured credit unions.

Deposit insurance protects against bank failure — not investment losses or fraud.

C.6 Account Safety Checklist

  • Use a strong, unique password for each financial account
  • Enable multifactor authentication (MFA) on all accounts
  • Review account statements monthly
  • Set up alerts for large transactions, low balances, and logins
  • Report unauthorized transactions promptly
  • Never share account credentials by phone, text, or email
  • Keep contact information current with financial institutions
  • Avoid accessing accounts on public or unsecured Wi-Fi networks

D.6 Minimum Payment Warning

The Cost of Minimums Only

$3,000 credit card balance at 20% APR, paying only the minimum (2% of balance or $25): repayment takes approximately 20+ years and more than $3,000 in interest — more than the original balance.

D.7 Debt Repayment Strategies

Strategy 1
Debt Avalanche — Highest Interest Rate First
Mathematically optimal · Pays least total interest

List all debts with balances and interest rates. Make minimum payments on all. Direct all extra money to the highest-rate debt first. When that debt is paid off, redirect its payment to the next highest rate. Repeat until all debt is paid.

Strategy 2
Debt Snowball — Smallest Balance First
Builds momentum · Many find it easier to maintain

List all debts by balance. Make minimum payments on all. Direct all extra money to the smallest-balance debt. When it is paid off, redirect its payment to the next smallest balance. Repeat until all debt is paid.

G.3 Premium-Deductible Tradeoff

Decision Rule

Higher deductibles generally mean lower premiums. Lower deductibles generally mean higher premiums.

Choose a deductible you could realistically pay out-of-pocket in an emergency. If a $2,000 deductible would cause financial hardship, the lower-deductible option may be safer despite the higher monthly cost.

G.4 Policy Comparison Checklist

  • What is the monthly or annual premium?
  • What is the deductible?
  • What is the coverage limit?
  • What is excluded from coverage?
  • What is the claims process?
  • Is the insurer financially stable and well-reviewed?
  • Does the policy meet required minimums (e.g., state auto insurance minimums)?
  • Are there discounts available (bundling, safe driver, good student)?
  • Does the coverage match actual risk exposure?

J.3 How a Debit Card Payment Moves

Step-by-Step Payment Flow

1 · Consumer swipes or taps card at a merchant.

2 · Merchant's terminal contacts the payment network (Visa, Mastercard, etc.).

3 · Payment network contacts the consumer's issuing bank.

4 · Issuing bank approves or declines based on available balance.

5 · Merchant's acquiring bank receives authorization.

6 · Transaction settles within 1–3 business days.

7 · Funds move from consumer's account to merchant's account.

8 · Fees are distributed to the payment network and issuing bank.

J.4 Trust and System Risk

Financial systems depend on trust. Depositors trust that banks will hold money safely. Borrowers trust that loan terms will remain as agreed. Investors trust that markets are reasonably fair and transparent. When trust breaks down — through fraud, bank failures, or market crises — the effects spread beyond the original problem. Regulation, deposit insurance, consumer protection laws, and transparency requirements exist to maintain systemic trust.

K.3 Identity Theft Protection Checklist

  • Review credit reports from all three bureaus annually (free at AnnualCreditReport.com)
  • Place a fraud alert or credit freeze if identity theft is suspected
  • Use strong, unique passwords and a password manager
  • Enable multifactor authentication on all financial accounts
  • Shred documents with personal financial information before discarding
  • Do not carry your Social Security card
  • Monitor account statements regularly for unauthorized transactions

Financial Ethics and Public Policy Reference

L.1 Key Ethical Concepts

ConceptDefinition
DisclosureThe obligation to provide truthful, complete information about financial products and risks
Conflict of interestA situation where a financial professional's personal interest may conflict with a client's best interest
Fiduciary dutyA legal and ethical obligation to act in the best interest of another person
Suitability standardA requirement that a product be appropriate for a client (a lower standard than fiduciary)
Predatory lendingLoan practices that exploit borrowers through deceptive terms, excessive fees, or targeting vulnerable populations
RedliningHistorical and illegal practice of denying financial services based on race or neighborhood
Credit discriminationDenying or restricting credit based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, age, or other protected characteristics

L.2 Fiduciary vs. Suitability Standard

Why This Distinction Matters

A financial advisor under a fiduciary standard must act in the client's best interest at all times.

A professional under a suitability standard must recommend appropriate products but is not required to choose the best available option for the client.

When selecting a financial advisor, ask whether they operate as a fiduciary. This matters most for investment accounts, retirement plans, and insurance products.

L.4 Public Policy Tradeoffs

Financial regulation involves genuine tradeoffs that reasonable people debate. Students should be able to identify competing interests without necessarily reaching a single conclusion.

Appendix N

Glossary

Alphabetical glossary of personal finance and financial systems terms used throughout the textbook. Terms are defined in student-friendly language while remaining financially accurate.

A
  • Annual percentage rate (APR)The annual cost of borrowing expressed as a percentage of the loan amount, not including compounding within the year.
  • Annual percentage yield (APY)The effective annual rate of return on a deposit account, reflecting compounding within the year.
  • AssetSomething of economic value owned by an individual, household, or institution.
  • Asset allocationThe percentage of an investment portfolio divided among different asset classes such as stocks, bonds, and cash.
  • Automatic paymentA pre-authorized recurring withdrawal or charge used to pay a bill or loan on a set schedule.
B
  • BalanceThe amount of money in an account at a given time.
  • BeneficiaryA person or entity designated to receive money, property, or insurance proceeds.
  • BondA debt security representing a loan made to a company or government in exchange for periodic interest payments and return of principal at maturity.
  • BudgetA plan that estimates income and expenses over a period of time, used to guide financial decisions.
C
  • Capital gainThe profit earned when an investment is sold for more than its purchase price.
  • Cash flowThe difference between money coming in (income) and money going out (expenses) over a period.
  • Certificate of deposit (CD)A savings product that pays a fixed interest rate for a fixed term. Withdrawing early typically incurs a penalty.
  • Checking accountA deposit account used for everyday spending, accessible by debit card, check, and electronic transfer.
  • ClaimA formal request to an insurance company for payment following a covered loss.
  • Closing costsFees and expenses paid at the completion of a real estate transaction, typically 2–5% of the purchase price.
  • CoinsuranceThe percentage of covered healthcare costs a policyholder pays after meeting the deductible.
  • Compound interestInterest calculated on both the principal and the interest already accumulated.
  • CopayA fixed amount a patient pays for a healthcare service, separate from the deductible.
  • CreditThe ability to borrow money with the promise to repay it, typically with interest.
  • Credit bureauA company that collects and maintains consumer credit history (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion).
  • Credit limitThe maximum balance allowed on a revolving credit account.
  • Credit reportA record of a consumer's credit history maintained by credit bureaus.
  • Credit scoreA number representing creditworthiness, calculated from information in a credit report. Typically ranges from 300 to 850.
  • Credit utilizationThe percentage of available revolving credit currently in use.
D
  • Debit cardA card linked to a checking account that deducts funds directly at the time of purchase.
  • Debt avalancheA debt repayment strategy that prioritizes the debt with the highest interest rate first.
  • Debt snowballA debt repayment strategy that prioritizes the debt with the smallest balance first.
  • Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)Total monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. Used by lenders to assess borrowing capacity.
  • DeductibleThe amount a policyholder pays out-of-pocket before insurance coverage begins.
  • DefaultFailure to repay a debt according to its terms.
  • Deposit insuranceGovernment-backed protection of deposits at qualifying financial institutions up to a specified limit.
  • DepreciationThe decrease in value of an asset over time, commonly applied to vehicles and equipment.
  • DiversificationSpreading investments across different assets, industries, and geographies to reduce the impact of any single loss.
  • DividendA payment made by a company to its shareholders, typically from profits.
  • Dollar-cost averagingInvesting a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of market price, which can reduce the impact of market volatility over time.
  • Down paymentA percentage of a purchase price paid upfront, with the remainder financed.
E
  • Emergency fundSavings set aside to cover unexpected expenses or income disruption without incurring debt. Typically 3–6 months of essential expenses.
  • Employer matchA contribution made by an employer to an employee's retirement account, typically up to a percentage of the employee's own contribution.
  • EquityThe value of an owned asset minus any debt owed on it (e.g., home equity = home value minus mortgage balance).
  • Expense ratioThe annual fee charged by a mutual fund or ETF, expressed as a percentage of assets under management.
F
  • FDICFederal Deposit Insurance Corporation. Insures bank deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category.
  • Federal ReserveThe central bank of the United States; responsible for monetary policy, bank oversight, and maintaining financial stability.
  • FiduciaryA person or institution legally and ethically obligated to act in another party's best interest.
  • Filing statusA tax category (single, married filing jointly, etc.) that determines tax rates and standard deduction amounts.
  • Fixed expenseA cost that remains the same from period to period, such as rent or a loan payment.
  • FraudIntentional deception for financial gain.
G–H
  • Grace periodA period after a credit card billing cycle during which the full balance can be paid without incurring interest.
  • Gross incomeTotal income before taxes, deductions, or withholding.
  • Hard inquiryA review of a credit report triggered by a credit application. May lower the credit score slightly and remains on the report for two years.
  • Human capitalThe knowledge, skills, experience, and health that enable a person to earn income over time.
I
  • Identity theftThe unauthorized use of another person's personal information to commit fraud, open accounts, or file false tax returns.
  • Index fundA mutual fund or ETF designed to replicate the performance of a market index such as the S&P 500.
  • InflationA general increase in prices over time, which reduces the purchasing power of money.
  • Installment loanA loan repaid in fixed payments over a set period (e.g., car loan, student loan, mortgage).
  • InsuranceA contract in which a policyholder pays premiums in exchange for financial protection against specified losses.
  • InterestThe cost of borrowing money (charged to the borrower) or the return earned on savings or investments (paid to the saver).
L–M
  • LiabilityA financial obligation or debt; in insurance, financial responsibility for harm or damage to others.
  • LiquidityHow quickly and easily an asset can be converted to cash without significant loss of value.
  • Minimum paymentThe smallest required payment on a credit account each billing cycle. Paying only the minimum results in significant interest costs over time.
  • Mutual fundAn investment vehicle that pools money from many investors to purchase a portfolio of securities managed by a fund manager.
N–O
  • NCUANational Credit Union Administration. Insures deposits at federally insured credit unions up to $250,000.
  • Net incomeIncome remaining after taxes and deductions; take-home pay.
  • Net worthTotal assets minus total liabilities. A measure of financial position at a point in time.
  • Opportunity costThe value of the best alternative given up when a choice is made. Every financial decision involves an opportunity cost.
  • Out-of-pocket maximumThe most a policyholder pays for covered healthcare in a plan year. After this amount is reached, insurance covers 100% of covered costs.
  • OverdraftA situation where spending exceeds the available balance in an account, resulting in a negative balance.
P–R
  • Payroll taxTaxes withheld from wages to fund Social Security and Medicare programs.
  • PhishingAn attempt to steal personal or financial information through deceptive emails, texts, or websites.
  • PremiumThe amount paid for an insurance policy, typically monthly or annually.
  • Present valueThe current worth of a future amount of money, given a specified rate of return. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future.
  • PrincipalThe original amount borrowed or invested, before interest is applied.
  • Real returnInvestment return adjusted for inflation. Real return ≈ nominal return − inflation rate.
  • ReconciliationThe process of verifying that personal financial records match official account statements.
  • Revolving creditA credit account with a flexible balance and credit limit that can be used, repaid, and used again (e.g., credit card).
  • RiskThe possibility that an outcome will differ from what was expected, including the possibility of loss.
  • Risk poolingThe insurance principle of spreading risk across many policyholders so no single policyholder bears the full cost of a loss alone.
S
  • Savings accountA deposit account designed to hold money not needed for immediate spending. Pays interest and is FDIC or NCUA insured.
  • Savings rateThe percentage of income saved during a given period. Savings rate = amount saved ÷ gross income × 100.
  • Secured debtDebt backed by collateral that the lender can claim if the borrower defaults (e.g., a mortgage backed by a home).
  • Simple interestInterest calculated on the original principal only, not on accumulated interest. Formula: I = P × r × t.
  • Sinking fundSavings set aside regularly for a specific anticipated future expense (e.g., a new car, vacation, holiday gifts).
  • Soft inquiryA credit report review that does not affect the credit score (e.g., self-checks, preapproval reviews, background checks).
  • StockA share of ownership in a company. Stock value fluctuates with company and market performance.
T–Z
  • Tax creditA direct reduction in the amount of tax owed. More valuable dollar for dollar than a tax deduction.
  • Tax deductionA reduction in taxable income that indirectly reduces taxes owed. Value depends on the taxpayer's marginal rate.
  • Time horizonThe length of time an investment is expected to be held before the money is needed. Longer horizons generally allow more risk.
  • Time value of moneyThe principle that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future due to its earning potential and the effect of inflation.
  • Total cost of ownershipAll costs associated with owning an asset over its useful life, including purchase price, financing, operating costs, maintenance, and depreciation.
  • Unsecured debtDebt not backed by collateral. If the borrower defaults, the lender has no specific asset to claim.
  • Variable expenseA cost that changes from period to period, such as groceries, fuel, or utility usage.
  • VestingThe process by which an employee earns the right to keep employer-contributed retirement funds over time. Unvested funds may be forfeited if the employee leaves before the vesting schedule is complete.
  • VolatilityThe degree to which an investment's value fluctuates over time. Higher volatility means larger price swings in both directions.
  • W-2A tax document provided by employers showing annual wages earned and taxes withheld during the year.
  • W-4A form employees complete to direct their employer on how much income tax to withhold from each paycheck.
  • WithholdingIncome tax amounts deducted from each paycheck and sent to the government on the employee's behalf throughout the year.

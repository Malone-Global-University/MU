Chapter 24 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Financial plans do not stay the same. A plan that works for a single person renting an apartment will not look the same after that person moves in with a partner, has a child, supports an aging parent, or loses a job. Life changes, and financial plans must change with it.

Chapter 24 introduces the financial dimensions of changing household circumstances. Students will learn how to plan for major purchases, manage shared financial responsibilities, understand what dependents and caregiving mean for a budget, navigate the financial effects of marriage and partnership, prepare for emergencies, respond to disruptions like job loss or illness, update financial documents and accounts, and revise a financial plan when circumstances shift.

Throughout this chapter, one principle applies: the goal is not a perfect plan. The goal is a plan that is honest, flexible, and ready to adapt. Households that can recognize a financial change and respond to it thoughtfully are better prepared than households that assume their current plan will always hold.

Opening Question

A person has a working budget as a single renter. Their partner moves in; they have a child two years later; a job loss follows two years after that. The same plan cannot serve all of these situations. How does a household recognize when a financial plan needs revision — and what does that revision actually look like?

Section 24.1

Financial Plans Change Over Time

A financial plan is not a permanent document. It is a working tool that reflects a person's current circumstances, goals, and constraints — and those things change throughout life.

Some changes are expected. A person finishes school and begins working. A household moves from renting to owning. A couple decides to have children. A worker retires. These are the kinds of changes that can be anticipated and planned for, at least in general terms.

Other changes are unexpected. A job ends. A medical emergency arises. A relationship ends. A parent needs help. A car breaks down beyond repair. These changes cannot always be predicted, but they can be prepared for through emergency funds, insurance, and flexible financial habits.

Understanding that financial plans change is not pessimistic — it is practical. A person who expects their plan to evolve is much better positioned than someone who assumes that what works today will always work. The skill is not writing a perfect plan once. It is knowing how to revisit and revise a plan when life requires it.

The Central Question at Every Stage

Given my current income, expenses, obligations, and goals, what does a realistic financial plan look like right now?

Important — Households Vary Widely

People do not all follow the same path. Some remain single. Some have large families. Some care for parents and children simultaneously. Some start over after illness, job loss, or economic disruption. A useful financial education prepares students for many kinds of households, not just one idealized pattern.

Section 24.2

Major Purchases

A major purchase is a spending decision large enough to significantly affect a household's budget, savings, or debt load. Major purchases include appliances, furniture, electronics, vehicles, jewelry, and similar durable goods. There is no universal dollar threshold — what is major depends on a household's income, savings, and financial situation.

What Makes a Purchase "Major"

A purchase is major when it requires drawing down savings that took time to accumulate, requires taking on new debt, creates a recurring payment that changes the monthly budget, or involves significant comparison before buying. A refrigerator might be a minor expense for a high-income household and a major financial event for a household with limited savings.

The Major Purchase Decision Questions

Five Questions Before Any Major Purchase

1. Is this purchase necessary now, or can it be delayed?

2. What is the total cost — not just the price tag?

3. Is this the right time financially?

4. What is the opportunity cost?

5. New, used, or refurbished?

Worked Example 24.2.1
Total cost of a major purchase — washing machine comparison
ItemMachine AMachine B
Purchase price$520$720
Delivery and installation$80Included
Extended warranty (3 years)$75$60

  • ThinkCompare total cost over the same 8-year period, not just the purchase price.
  • Calculate A $520 + $80 + $75 + ($40 × 8) = $520 + $80 + $75 + $320 = $995

Financing Major Purchases

When a major purchase is financed — paid for over time through a loan or credit card — the borrowing cost adds to the total price. Financing is not always wrong, but students should understand that using credit for major purchases increases the total cost.

Worked Example 24.2.2
Cost of financing a sofa on a credit card
  • SituationA $1,200 sofa. Option 1: pay cash. Option 2: credit card at 22% APR, $80/month payment.
  • Option 1Total cost: $1,200
  • Option 2At $80/month → approximately 18 months to pay off. Total interest paid: approximately $195. Total cost: $1,200 + $195 = $1,395
  • InterpretFinancing costs about $195 more than paying cash. That $195 is the price of borrowing. The question is whether convenience now is worth that cost.
Answer: Cash purchase = $1,200. Financed purchase ≈ $1,395. Financing adds approximately $195.
Student Warning — Deferred Interest Is Not 0% Interest

Retailers often advertise "0% financing for 24 months." If the promotion involves deferred interest, any unpaid balance at the end of the promotional period is charged interest retroactively on the original balance — often at 25% or more. Missing the payoff deadline by even one payment can trigger this charge. Always confirm whether a promotional offer is genuine 0% or deferred interest before committing.

Worked Example 24.2.3
Deferred interest trap — $2,400 furniture purchase
  • SituationA retailer offers "18 months no interest" on a $2,400 furniture purchase. Deferred interest at 26.99% applies if the balance is not paid in full by month 18.
  • At $120/mo$120 × 18 = $2,160. Less than $2,400. Balance not cleared. Deferred interest triggered.
  • Deferred interest$2,400 × 26.99% = approximately $648 applied retroactively.
  • Needed payment$2,400 ÷ 18 = $133.34/month to clear balance before deadline.
  • Interpret$120/month sounds close to $133, but the shortfall triggers a large retroactive charge. A buyer who does not read the terms carefully faces an unexpected $600+ bill.
Answer: $120/month is not enough to avoid the deferred interest charge. The required monthly payment is at least $133.34.

Section 24.3

Household Budgeting

A household budget is more complex than an individual budget because more people means more categories of spending, more decisions to coordinate, and more potential for miscommunication. The word "household" refers to the people who share a living space and, often, financial resources.

Shared and Separate Expenses

TypeDescriptionExamples
Shared expensesCosts that benefit all members; paid collectivelyRent, utilities, groceries, internet, household supplies
Separate expensesCosts that primarily benefit one personClothing, personal care, individual subscriptions, personal transportation

Income-based split — two adults sharing a household

  • Shared costs
    Shared ExpenseMonthly
    Rent$1,400
    Utilities$180
    Internet$60

Household Budget Communication

A regular monthly or quarterly household money check-in — where both people review shared accounts, upcoming expenses, and financial concerns — prevents most household budget conflicts. Money conversations are easier when they happen routinely than when they only happen during a crisis.

Section 24.4

Shared Financial Responsibilities

Sharing a household means sharing financial responsibilities, not just expenses. The division of financial tasks matters as much as the division of costs. In many households, financial tasks fall unevenly — often to one person by default rather than by agreement. This creates a serious risk: if the person managing finances becomes unavailable due to illness, travel, or a relationship change, the other household member may not know where accounts are, how bills are paid, or what documents exist.

What Both Household Members Should Know

CategoryInformation Both Members Should Have
AccountsWhat accounts exist, where they are held, how to access online banking
BillsHow bills are paid, when they are due, which are automatic
DebtsWhat debts exist, balances, monthly payments
InsuranceWhat policies exist, policy numbers, beneficiary designations
DocumentsWhere financial documents are stored, including physical and digital
Emergency fundWhere it is held and how to access it
Section 24.5

Dependents and Caregiving

A dependent is a person who relies on another for financial support. Having dependents significantly affects a household's budget, income, taxes, and insurance needs.

Planning Questions Before Adding a Dependent

Financial Planning Questions — New Dependent

What will childcare cost, and how does that fit in the current budget?

Will either parent need to reduce work hours, and what does that mean for income?

What insurance changes are needed — health coverage for the child, life insurance for parents?

Is the emergency fund large enough to cover an unexpected medical expense?

Do legal documents need updating — beneficiary designations, will, guardianship?

Costs for children include childcare, food, clothing, medical care, school supplies, activities, and potentially education costs later. These vary enormously by location and circumstances. In many areas, childcare costs alone equal or exceed a car payment or rent contribution.

Caregiving for adult family members carries both direct costs — medical supplies, transportation, paid help — and indirect costs: reduced work hours for the caregiver, which can reduce current income and long-term retirement contributions. Planning for caregiving means having honest conversations early about what support may be needed and what the household can realistically provide.

Section 24.6

Marriage, Partnership, and Shared Finances

When two people enter a long-term partnership, their financial lives become interconnected in ways that have significant practical and legal consequences. Financial conversations before combining lives are not just helpful — they are essential.

Key Areas to Discuss Before Combining Finances

TopicWhat to Discuss
IncomeWhat does each person earn? Is either income variable or uncertain?
DebtsStudent loans, credit cards, car loans — how much and what are the payments?
AssetsWhat savings, investments, and property does each person have?
CreditWhat is each person's credit history and score?
Financial goalsAre the goals compatible? What are the timelines and priorities?
Financial valuesWhat does each person believe about debt, saving, and helping family financially?

Options for Organizing Shared Finances

ApproachDescriptionConsideration
Fully combinedAll income and expenses flow through shared accountsSimple; requires high trust and shared values
Fully separateEach person maintains their own accounts; shared expenses are splitPreserves independence; requires more coordination
HybridShared account for shared expenses and savings; separate personal accountsIncreasingly common; balances autonomy with shared obligations
Worked Example 24.6.1
Hybrid household finance structure
  • SetupPartner 1 earns $4,000/month net. Partner 2 earns $2,800/month net. Shared expenses: $3,200/month. Combined: $6,800/month.
  • Calculate Partner 1 share: $3,200 × (4,000 ÷ 6,800) ≈ $3,200 × 58.8% ≈ $1,882/month Partner 2 share: $3,200 × (2,800 ÷ 6,800) ≈ $3,200 × 41.2% ≈ $1,318/month
  • Personal funds Partner 1 retains: $4,000 − $1,882 = $2,118/month (personal spending, saving, debt) Partner 2 retains: $2,800 − $1,318 = $1,482/month (personal spending, saving, debt)
  • InterpretEach person has autonomy over personal funds while the shared account handles all shared obligations. The income-based formula reflects relative earning power.
Answer: Partner 1 contributes ≈$1,882/month; retains ≈$2,118. Partner 2 contributes ≈$1,318/month; retains ≈$1,482.
Student Warning — Beneficiary Designations and Relationship Changes

Getting married, divorced, or entering a new partnership requires updating beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and bank accounts. These designations are legally binding and override a will. A person who forgets to update their beneficiary designation after a divorce may leave life insurance benefits to an ex-spouse regardless of what their will says. These updates must be done actively — they do not happen automatically.

Emergency Planning

An emergency plan is a household's preparation for financial disruptions that are unexpected but not impossible. It has two components: financial preparation (the emergency fund and insurance) and practical preparation (organized documents and contacts).

Section 24.8

Job Loss, Moving, Illness, and Other Transitions

Job Loss

Job loss is one of the most financially stressful transitions a household can experience. Its financial effects begin immediately: income drops from the first missed paycheck. Several urgent tasks need prompt attention.

Decision Checklist — Immediate Steps After Job Loss
Complete within the first two weeks
  • File for unemployment insurance as soon as eligible — deadlines apply
  • Review the household budget; identify essential versus non-essential expenses
  • Contact lenders about hardship programs before missing a payment
  • Address health insurance coverage within the COBRA or marketplace deadline (typically 30–60 days)
  • Pause or cancel recurring subscriptions and non-essential automatic payments
  • Begin active job search; document activity for unemployment eligibility requirements
  • Set a timeline for reassessment if the search is not producing results
Unemployment Insurance — Key Facts

Most workers who lose their job through no fault of their own are eligible for unemployment insurance. Benefits typically replace 50–60% of prior wages, up to a state-set maximum. Benefits are taxable income and last a limited number of weeks. Workers must actively search for new employment to remain eligible.

Worked Example 24.8.1
Calculating unemployment benefit and income gap
  • SetupA worker's prior monthly take-home pay was $3,100. Unemployment pays 55% of prior wages, up to a $520/week maximum.
  • Calculate 55%$3,100 × 55% = $1,705/month
  • Weekly max$520/week × 4.33 weeks/month = $2,252/month maximum
  • Applicable benefit$1,705 is less than $2,252. The 55% calculation applies. Benefit: approximately $1,705/month.
  • Income gap$3,100 − $1,705 = $1,395/month shortfall
  • InterpretThe household must cover a $1,395/month gap through budget cuts and emergency fund use while the job search continues.
Answer: Unemployment benefit ≈ $1,705/month. Monthly income gap ≈ $1,395/month.

Moving

Moving involves both one-time costs — truck rental, packing supplies, security deposit, utility setup fees, immediate purchases for the new space — and ongoing changes to the monthly budget. Before committing to a new housing arrangement, update the budget to reflect the expected ongoing costs in the new location, not just the cost of moving.

Illness and Medical Events

Serious illness or injury creates both direct costs (deductibles, copays, uncovered services) and indirect costs (lost income, reduced long-term work capacity). Most hospitals have financial assistance programs for patients who cannot pay in full — but patients often need to ask for them, as they are not always offered automatically.

Critical — Beneficiary Designations Override Wills

A beneficiary designation is legally binding and determines who receives assets from a retirement account, life insurance policy, or payable-on-death bank account — regardless of what a will says. A person who names a former spouse as beneficiary and never updates after divorce will have those assets go to the ex-spouse even if a will leaves everything to a new spouse. Beneficiary updates must be done actively after every significant life change.

Section 24.10

Revising a Financial Plan

When to Review a Financial Plan

Review Triggers

Annually, at minimum, as a scheduled check-in.

After any significant income change — raise, job loss, new job, income reduction.

After any significant expense change — new housing, new dependent, end of a large debt.

After any significant life event — marriage, divorce, birth, death, major illness, graduation, retirement.

Seven-Step Framework for Revising a Financial Plan

Framework
How to revise a financial plan after a major life change
  • Step 1Describe the change. Write down what changed specifically: "My income increased by $400/month" or "We now have a childcare expense of $1,100/month." Naming the change clearly makes it easier to analyze.
  • Step 2Recalculate income and essential expenses. Update the budget with current income and all essential expenses. Calculate new cash flow. Is there a surplus or a gap?
  • Step 3Identify what no longer fits. Are there expenses that made sense before but should now be cut? Subscriptions, memberships, or discretionary spending that was affordable before may no longer be.
  • Step 4Update savings targets. Recalculate emergency fund targets based on new monthly essential expenses. Adjust retirement contributions if employment has changed. Update sinking funds.
  • Step 5Review debt strategy. Has the change affected the ability to make regular debt payments? Does a new income level allow accelerated payoff? Has a disruption made minimum payments difficult?
  • Step 6Check insurance, beneficiaries, and documents. Use the checklist from Section 24.9 to confirm that insurance is current, beneficiary designations are up to date, and legal documents are appropriate.
  • Step 7Set a new timeline. When is the next review scheduled? What needs to happen before then?
Financial Systems Connection

Revising a financial plan is not only a personal process. When a household's income drops, lenders may offer hardship programs. Employers may have employee assistance programs. Government programs may provide housing assistance, food assistance, childcare subsidies, health coverage, or unemployment benefits. Financial systems provide resources during transitions that individual planning cannot always cover alone. Knowing what institutions exist — and being willing to use them when needed — is financial systems literacy, not a failure of personal responsibility.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 24.1Financial plans change because life changes. Expected changes can be planned for; unexpected ones can be partially prepared for through emergency funds, insurance, and flexible habits. The central question at every stage: what does a realistic plan look like right now?

  • Sec 24.4Both household members should understand the full financial picture — accounts, bills, debts, insurance, and documents. Financial dependency on one person creates vulnerability. Shared financial literacy is practical protection for both parties.
  • Sec 24.5Having dependents — children, aging parents, or others — significantly affects a household's budget, insurance needs, and taxes. Planning before adding a dependent includes childcare costs, insurance updates, and emergency fund review. Caregiving for adult family members carries both direct and indirect financial costs.
  • Sec 24.6Entering a partnership requires financial transparency about income, debts, assets, credit, and goals. Hybrid approaches — shared accounts for shared expenses plus separate personal accounts — are common and can work well. Significant relationship changes require updating beneficiary designations and other documents.
  • Sec 24.7Emergency planning has two components: financial preparation (emergency fund and insurance) and practical preparation (organized documents and contacts). A household emergency fund should target three to six months of essential expenses. The fund should be held separately, remain liquid, and be replenished after any withdrawal.
  • Sec 24.8Job loss requires filing for unemployment insurance, addressing health insurance within the deadline, cutting non-essential expenses, and communicating with lenders before missing payments. Moving, illness, divorce, and death each create specific financial tasks that need prompt attention.
  • Sec 24.9Every major life change should trigger a review of insurance coverage, savings targets, and key documents. Beneficiary designations are legally binding and override wills — they must be updated actively after marriage, divorce, birth, or death.   • Sec 24.10A seven-step framework for financial plan revision: describe the change; recalculate income and expenses; identify what no longer fits; update savings targets; review debt strategy; check insurance and documents; set a new review timeline. Key Terms Review

beneficiary
A person named to receive assets from an account, insurance policy, or retirement plan upon the account holder's death. Beneficiary designations are legally binding and override wills.
COBRA
A law allowing departing employees to continue employer-sponsored health insurance by paying the full premium themselves, typically for up to 18 months.
deferred interest
A financing arrangement where interest is not charged during a promotional period but is applied retroactively to the original balance if not paid in full by the deadline.
dependent
A person who relies on another for financial support. Most commonly refers to children, but may include aging parents or other family members.
durable power of attorney
A legal document authorizing another person to make financial decisions on behalf of the account holder if the account holder becomes incapacitated.
emergency fund
Money held in a liquid, accessible account to cover unexpected financial disruptions. Standard guidance targets three to six months of essential expenses.
essential expenses
Expenses that must be paid to maintain basic stability: housing, utilities, food, insurance, and required minimum debt payments.
hybrid structure
A household finance arrangement combining a shared account for shared expenses with separate personal accounts for individual spending and autonomy.
income-based split
A method of dividing shared household expenses where each person contributes a percentage proportional to their share of total household income.
life transition
A significant personal change — marriage, job loss, the birth of a child, moving, illness, retirement — that typically requires revisions to a financial plan.
major purchase
A spending decision large enough to significantly affect a household's budget, savings, or debt load. Significance depends on household context, not a universal dollar threshold.
promotional financing
A retailer offer of deferred or reduced interest payments for a set period. May involve retroactive deferred interest if the balance is not paid off by the deadline.
sinking fund
Money saved in advance for a predictable irregular expense — an annual insurance premium, a planned trip, a major purchase — to avoid debt when the expense arrives.
total cost of ownership
The full cost of owning or using something over time, including purchase price, financing charges, insurance, maintenance, repairs, and eventual replacement.
unemployment insurance
A government program providing partial income replacement to workers who lose jobs through no fault of their own. Benefits are temporary, taxable, and require active job searching.
will
A legal document specifying how a person's assets are to be distributed after death and, for parents of minor children, who should serve as guardian.

Calculation Review

24-A: Total Cost of a Major Purchase
Total cost = Purchase price + Delivery and installation + Warranty + (Annual maintenance × Expected years)
A dishwasher costs $680. Delivery and installation: $95. Extended warranty: $90. Annual maintenance: $25/year for 10 years.
$680 + $95 + $90 + ($25 × 10) = $680 + $95 + $90 + $250 = $1,115
Total cost: $1,115
24-B: Total Cost of a Financed Purchase
Total financed cost = Purchase price + Total interest paid
A $1,500 appliance is financed at 18% APR with $100/month payments. Approximately 18 months to repay. Total interest paid: approximately $180.
$1,500 + $180 = $1,680
Total financed cost: $1,680
24-C: Income-Based Expense Split
Person A's share = Total shared expenses × (Person A's income ÷ Combined household income)
Combined income: $6,200/month. Shared expenses: $2,800/month. Person A earns $3,600/month.
$2,800 × (3,600 ÷ 6,200) = $2,800 × 0.581 ≈ $1,626/month
Person A's share: ≈$1,626/month
24-D: Emergency Fund Target
Emergency fund target = Monthly essential expenses × Number of months targeted (3 to 6)
Monthly essential expenses: $3,400. Target: 4 months.
$3,400 × 4 = $13,600
Emergency fund target: $13,600
24-E: Remaining Personal Budget After Shared Contribution
Personal remaining = Net income − Shared contribution
Net income: $2,600/month. Shared contribution from 24-C example (Person B): $2,800 × (2,600 ÷ 6,200) ≈ $1,174/month.
$2,600 − $1,174 = $1,426/month
Person B retains: $1,426/month for personal spending, saving, and debt

Major Purchase Decision Practice

For each scenario, identify: (a) the total cost of each option, (b) the lower-total-cost option, and (c) any non-cost factors that might affect the decision.

  1. Refrigerator comparison. A household needs a new refrigerator. Option A: $750 with $80 delivery and $50 service warranty, expected to last 7 years with $30/year maintenance. Option B: $950 with free delivery and a $45 warranty, expected to last 12 years with $15/year maintenance. Calculate total cost for each over 7 years. Which option has the lower total cost over that period?
  2. Laptop — cash versus financed. A person wants a laptop. Option A: new model for $1,100 purchased on a credit card at 21% APR with minimum payments of $55/month (approximately 26 months to pay off; estimated interest: $200). Option B: refurbished model for $600 paid in cash. Calculate the total cost of each option. What is the cost difference?
  3. Deferred interest — furniture purchase. A retailer offers "18 months, no interest" on a $2,400 furniture purchase. Fine print: if the balance is not paid in full within 18 months, deferred interest of 26.99% is charged retroactively on the original balance.

    (a) If the household pays $120/month, how much have they paid after 18 months?
    (b) Is that enough to clear the $2,400 balance?
    (c) If deferred interest is triggered, approximately how much interest is charged on the original balance?
    (d) What monthly payment is needed to pay off $2,400 in exactly 18 months?

Life Transition Analysis

For each transition, identify: (a) the immediate financial tasks required, (b) the expected budget changes, and (c) the insurance, savings, or document updates needed.

  1. Finishing school and starting a first full-time job. Consider: income (now starting), student loan repayment (may begin), health insurance (through employer or marketplace), retirement enrollment (first opportunity), budget revision (from student to employed), and emergency fund (now able to begin building).
  2. A couple moves in together and combines households. Consider: shared expense agreement, account structure decision, budget revision, renters insurance update, review of both partners' debts and financial obligations, and beneficiary discussion.
  3. A household member is hospitalized unexpectedly for two weeks. Consider: emergency fund activation, health insurance deductible and out-of-pocket maximum, short-term disability benefits if available, employer sick leave, income gap if the person is self-employed, hospital financial assistance programs, and bill negotiation.
  4. A household member is laid off. Consider: unemployment insurance application, health insurance address within COBRA deadline, budget revision, subscription and discretionary spending cuts, emergency fund use, debt payment communication with lenders, and active job search documentation.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"My will controls everything that happens to my money when I die."

Not quite. Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, and payable-on-death bank accounts are legally binding and override a will. A will controls only assets without a designated beneficiary or joint owner. Keeping beneficiary designations current is essential.

Misconception 2
"Deferred interest financing is the same as 0% interest."

Deferred interest means interest is postponed, not eliminated. If the balance is not paid in full by the deadline, interest is charged retroactively on the original balance at the full rate — often 25% or more. This can result in a very large unexpected charge on what appeared to be a 0% deal.

Misconception 3
"If my partner had the debt before we got together, it's not my problem."

Debts brought into a relationship generally remain the legal responsibility of the person who incurred them. However, debts incurred jointly during a marriage may be the legal responsibility of both partners depending on location. Any debt that reduces available household income affects both people regardless of whose name is on it.

Misconception 4
"I'll update my beneficiaries eventually."

Eventually becomes never. Life changes happen quickly, and failing to update a beneficiary designation after a divorce, death, or new family member is a common and consequential error. Beneficiary updates should happen promptly — within weeks, not months — after any major life event.

Misconception 5
"Using the emergency fund means I failed financially."

The emergency fund exists to be used during genuine emergencies. A household that draws on an emergency fund appropriately and then replenishes it is managing money well. The failure would be not having an emergency fund and being forced to borrow at high interest rates when a disruption occurs.

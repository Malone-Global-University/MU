Chapter 16 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 15 introduced insurance as a tool for managing financial risk — the concepts of risk pooling, premiums, deductibles, coverage limits, exclusions, claims, and beneficiaries. Chapter 16 applies those concepts to the specific types of insurance that most households will actually use.

This chapter surveys six major insurance categories: health insurance, auto insurance, renters insurance, homeowners insurance, disability insurance, and life insurance. For each type, students will learn what the insurance covers, what key terms and decisions apply, and how to think about comparing policies. The chapter ends with a framework for reviewing a household's overall insurance needs.

Chapter Purpose

Insurance is not one product — it is a category of tools, and different tools cover different risks. No single policy covers all of them. Financially literate households think about insurance as a portfolio of coverage types, each matched to a specific category of risk. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to explain what each insurance type protects against, identify key terms specific to each category, compare basic policies using cost and coverage information, recognize common tradeoffs between premiums and out-of-pocket costs, and apply an insurance review checklist to realistic household scenarios.

Section 16.1

Why Households Use Different Insurance Types

In Chapter 15, you learned that insurance is a financial tool that transfers certain financial risks from individuals to a larger pool of policyholders. You pay a premium — a regular fee — and in return, the insurer agrees to pay covered costs if a qualifying event occurs.

But insurance is not one product. It is a category of tools, and different tools cover different risks. Consider what can go wrong financially in a typical household: a person gets sick or injured and needs expensive medical care; a car is in a collision; an apartment fire destroys a tenant's belongings; a homeowner's roof is damaged in a storm; a worker becomes too ill or injured to work; a parent dies unexpectedly. Each of these risks is real, and each has a corresponding insurance product designed to address it.

The Goal Is Not Maximum Coverage

The goal of insurance is not to buy as much coverage as possible. The goal is to cover risks that would cause serious financial harm if they occurred uninsured, while not spending more than necessary on risks you can self-insure or that are unlikely to affect you significantly. Premiums cost money — every dollar spent on insurance is a dollar not available for savings, investing, or other expenses. The decision is always a tradeoff: how much risk are you transferring, and what does that transfer cost?

Section 16.2

Health Insurance

Health insurance helps pay for medical care, including doctor visits, hospital stays, emergency care, surgery, prescription drugs, preventive care, mental health services, and other healthcare costs. In the United States, health insurance is one of the most consequential financial products a household manages, because healthcare costs without coverage can be catastrophic. Medical expenses are among the leading causes of financial hardship — a single hospital stay can cost tens of thousands of dollars, and a serious illness can produce bills that exceed a person's annual income many times over.

Section 16.3

Copays, Coinsurance, Deductibles, and Out-of-Pocket Maximums

These four concepts work together to determine how you and your insurer share healthcare costs. Think of your annual healthcare costs as flowing through three layers: first the deductible (you pay 100%), then coinsurance or copays (you share costs with the insurer), then the out-of-pocket maximum (after which the insurer pays 100% for the rest of the year).

Worked Example 16.3.1
Following one person's healthcare costs through the cost-sharing system
  • SetupMarcus has: monthly premium $280 · annual deductible $1,800 · coinsurance 20% · out-of-pocket maximum $6,500
  • JanuaryMarcus visits his primary care doctor. Cost: $200. He has not met his deductible yet, so Marcus pays the full $200 out of pocket. Running out-of-pocket total: $200
  • MarchMarcus has outpatient surgery. Allowed cost: $4,000. At this point, $200 has been applied to his deductible. Remaining deductible: $1,800 − $200 = $1,600 → Marcus pays $1,600 Amount above deductible: $4,000 − $1,600 = $2,400 Marcus's 20% share: $2,400 × 0.20 = $480 Insurer's 80% share: $2,400 × 0.80 = $1,920 Surgery cost to Marcus: $1,600 + $480 = $2,080
  • Running totalMarcus has paid $200 + $2,080 = $2,280 out of pocket. Remaining to out-of-pocket max: $6,500 − $2,280 = $4,220. If a later hospitalization produces $25,000 in covered costs, Marcus pays 20% coinsurance until total reaches $6,500, then the insurer pays everything.
Out-of-pocket maximum = critical protection against catastrophic medical bills

The High-Deductible, Low-Premium Tradeoff

Plans generally trade off premium cost against deductible. A high-deductible health plan (HDHP) has lower monthly premiums but a higher deductible (often $1,500+). A low-deductible plan has higher monthly premiums but lower out-of-pocket costs when you use care. Which is better depends on how much healthcare you use, whether you have savings to cover the deductible, and your risk tolerance. HDHPs may be paired with a Health Savings Account (HSA) — a tax-advantaged account for qualified medical expenses.

Financial Tip

A high-deductible plan is only financially sound if you have accessible savings to cover the deductible when you need care. Choosing a very high deductible without emergency savings creates a dangerous gap: you technically have insurance, but you cannot afford to use it.

Section 16.4

Health Insurance Networks

A network is the group of providers that have contracted with your insurer at negotiated rates. Using in-network providers dramatically lowers your cost. Out-of-network providers may receive little or no payment from the insurer, leaving you responsible for a much larger bill — sometimes the entire amount. Network selection is therefore a significant financial decision, not merely a convenience preference.

Before Enrolling in Any Health Plan

Check whether your current doctors, specialists, and preferred hospitals are in-network. Switching plans may mean switching providers, which matters for continuity of care — especially if you have an ongoing condition or a specialist you rely on. Verifying network status before selecting a plan can prevent costly surprises.

Section 16.5

Auto Insurance

Auto insurance protects against the financial consequences of vehicle accidents, theft, damage, and certain other events. In most U.S. states, some level of auto insurance is legally required to drive. The specific requirements vary by state, but the goal is consistent: ensure that people who cause accidents can pay for the harm they cause.

Coverage Components

Coverage TypeWhat It Pays ForRequired?
Liability — Bodily injuryMedical bills, lost wages, and other costs for people you injureYes, in most states
Liability — Property damageRepairs or replacement of property you damage (e.g., another vehicle)Yes, in most states
CollisionDamage to your own vehicle in a collision, regardless of faultOptional (required by lenders)
ComprehensiveVehicle damage from theft, fire, weather, vandalism, or animal strikesOptional (required by lenders)
Uninsured/underinsured motoristYour losses when the at-fault driver has no or insufficient insuranceOptional (required in some states)
MedPay / PIPYour medical expenses (and sometimes lost wages) after an accident, regardless of faultRequired in no-fault states
Understanding Liability Limits

Liability coverage is expressed as three numbers — for example, 100/300/100 — meaning $100,000 per person for bodily injury, $300,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $100,000 for property damage. State minimums are often far lower than recommended. If you cause a serious accident and your liability is exhausted, you can be sued personally for the remainder.

Worked Example 16.5.1
Comparing two auto insurance policies
  • SetupCamille, age 22, drives a 4-year-old sedan worth approximately $12,000. She is choosing between two policies:
  • Comparison Policy A Policy B Monthly premium $95 $138 Liability limits 25/50/25 100/300/100 Collision deductible $1,000 $500 Comprehensive deduct. $1,000 $500 Uninsured motorist Not included Included
  • AnalysisPolicy A saves $43/month ($516/year). But Policy B offers substantially higher liability limits, lower deductibles, and UM coverage. If Camille causes a serious accident, Policy A's $25,000 limit may be far too low, exposing her to personal liability for the difference.
  • ConclusionThe lower premium is not automatically the better choice. The decision depends on her financial situation, her emergency fund, and how much personal liability risk she can absorb.
A lower premium always means higher exposure — to deductibles, liability gaps, or uncovered events
Section 16.6

Renters Insurance

Renters insurance is property and liability insurance for tenants. It does not cover the building — that is the landlord's responsibility — but it covers the tenant's personal belongings, provides liability protection, and often covers additional living expenses if the rental becomes uninhabitable due to a covered event.

What Renters Insurance Covers

CoverageWhat It PaysExample
Personal propertyLoss or damage to belongings from covered perils (fire, theft, burst pipes, wind)Laptop stolen → replacement paid
LiabilityInjuries to others in your rental or accidental damage to their propertyGuest slips and falls → medical bills covered
Additional living expenses (ALE)Temporary housing, meals, and extra costs if covered damage makes your rental uninhabitableApartment fire → hotel costs covered

What Renters Insurance Does NOT Cover

Standard renters insurance does not cover: flooding from external sources (requires separate flood insurance); earthquakes (requires separate coverage); your roommate's belongings unless listed on the policy; high-value items like jewelry or art above standard limits (may need a rider); vehicle damage; or damage you intentionally cause.

Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost

Actual cash value (ACV): Pays what your belongings were worth at the time of the loss, accounting for depreciation. A laptop bought for $1,200 three years ago might have an ACV of $400.

Replacement cost value (RCV): Pays what it would cost to replace the item with a new, comparable item today — perhaps $1,100 for that same laptop. RCV policies cost more but provide significantly better protection.

Worked Example 16.6.1
Estimating personal property value for renters insurance
  • Inventory Electronics (laptop, phone, tablet, TV) $2,800 Clothing and shoes $1,500 Furniture (bed, desk, couch, dresser) $1,800 Kitchen items $600 Books, sporting goods, other $700 Total estimated value $7,400
  • Policy selected$15,000 personal property (replacement cost) · $500 deductible · $100,000 liability · $3,000 ALE
  • CostApproximately $18–$25 per month, or $216–$300 per year — to protect $7,400 in property plus gain liability and ALE coverage.
Renters insurance is typically one of the most cost-effective household insurance purchases
Why Renters Often Skip It

Many renters underestimate the value of their belongings and believe "I don't have much stuff." But clothing, electronics, furniture, and kitchen items add up quickly. Even modest possessions may total several thousand dollars. Many landlords now require renters insurance as a lease condition.

Section 16.7

Homeowners Insurance

Homeowners insurance protects property owners against financial losses related to their home and its contents. It is more comprehensive and more expensive than renters insurance, reflecting the much higher value of the asset. Mortgage lenders almost always require homeowners insurance — if you stop carrying it, the lender may force-place insurance on your behalf, typically at a much higher cost with less favorable coverage.

What Homeowners Insurance Covers

Coverage ComponentWhat It Pays For
Dwelling coverageRepairs or rebuilding of the home structure from covered perils (fire, windstorm, hail, lightning, vandalism)
Other structuresDetached structures on the property (garage, fence, shed)
Personal propertyHomeowner's belongings inside the home (similar to renters insurance)
LiabilityLawsuits if someone is injured on the property or you damage someone else's property
Additional living expenses (ALE)Temporary housing and related costs if the home is uninhabitable from a covered loss
Reconstruction Cost ≠ Market Value

Dwelling coverage should be sufficient to rebuild the home at current construction costs — not the market value and not the original purchase price. These figures are often very different. A home in a lower-cost real estate market may still be expensive to rebuild per square foot. Review dwelling coverage periodically and after major renovations.

What Homeowners Insurance Does NOT Cover

Standard homeowners insurance excludes: flooding (requires separate flood insurance, often through the National Flood Insurance Program); earthquakes; routine maintenance and wear; intentional damage; certain high-value items above standard limits; home-based business losses; and sewer backup (usually requires a separate endorsement).

Section 16.8

Liability Coverage

Liability coverage appears in auto, renters, and homeowners insurance because the underlying risk — being held legally responsible for causing harm to another person or their property — can arise in multiple contexts. When you are legally liable, liability insurance pays legal defense costs, court judgments, and settlements.

Worked Example 16.8.1
Liability coverage in action
  • SituationSophia owns a home and hosts a party. A guest trips on a step, breaks their wrist, and requires surgery. Medical bills: $22,000. Guest sues for additional $30,000 in lost wages and damages. Total claim: $52,000.
  • Scenario ASophia's homeowners policy has $100,000 in liability coverage. The insurer covers the $52,000 claim, including her legal defense. Sophia pays nothing beyond her normal premium.
  • Scenario BIf Sophia had only $25,000 in liability coverage, the insurer pays $25,000 and Sophia is personally responsible for the remaining $27,000.
Liability limits should reflect your assets — people with more assets at risk need higher limits
Personal Umbrella Insurance

An umbrella policy sits above your other liability coverage and provides additional protection — often $1 million or more — at a relatively affordable premium, typically $150–$350 per year. Worth considering for homeowners, people with significant assets, or situations that carry elevated liability exposure.

Section 16.9

Disability Insurance

Disability insurance replaces a portion of your income if you become unable to work due to illness or injury. It is one of the most overlooked but financially important types of insurance. Many people insure their car and their home — but not their income, even though their income is what makes it possible to pay for everything else. Actuarial data shows that a significant percentage of workers will experience a disability lasting 90 days or more during their working years.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term Disability

FeatureShort-Term Disability (STD)Long-Term Disability (LTD)
Benefit periodWeeks to months (typically 3–6 months)Years to retirement age
Elimination periodDays to weeks60–180 days (emergency fund or STD covers the gap)
Typical benefit amount50–70% of pre-disability income50–70% of pre-disability income
Primary purposeCover short gaps in income from illness or injuryCover extended or permanent inability to work

Key Disability Insurance Terms

TermDefinitionWhy It Matters
Elimination periodWaiting period before benefits beginLonger period = lower premium but more financial exposure during the wait
Benefit periodHow long benefits are paidLonger period = more protection but higher premium
Benefit amountPercentage of pre-disability income replaced (typically 50–70%)Determines whether benefits cover essential expenses
Own-occupationPays benefits if you cannot perform the duties of your specific occupationBroader protection — e.g., a surgeon who loses the use of a hand still qualifies even if they can teach
Any-occupationPays benefits only if you cannot perform any occupation you are reasonably suited forNarrower protection — harder to qualify; lower premiums
Sources of Disability Coverage

Many employers offer short-term and long-term disability coverage as employee benefits, sometimes at no cost. Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) is available for workers who qualify, though the application process can be lengthy and approval is not guaranteed. Individual disability policies can also be purchased privately — more expensive, but portable when you change jobs.

Section 16.10

Life Insurance

Life insurance provides a financial benefit — the death benefit — to designated beneficiaries when the insured person dies. It is not for you; it is for the people who depend on you financially. Life insurance is most important when others rely on your income: a parent with dependent children, a spouse or partner whose standard of living depends on your earnings, or a household where your income pays shared debts. It is generally not necessary for someone with no financial dependents.

Term vs. Permanent Life Insurance

FeatureTerm LifePermanent Life (Whole/Universal)

Estimating life insurance needs — simplified income replacement
  • SetupDarius earns $72,000/year. His household would need income replacement for approximately 15 years if he died. He has $18,000 in outstanding debt and $145,000 remaining on his mortgage.
  • Calculate Income replacement: $72,000 × 15 years = $1,080,000 Outstanding debt: + $18,000 Mortgage payoff: + $145,000 Total estimated need: $1,243,000

  • NoteThis is a simplified estimate. A financial planner would also consider investment returns on the lump sum, current savings and assets, Social Security survivor benefits, and other factors specific to the household.

Section 16.11

Comparing Policies Carefully

The premium is only part of the cost of insurance. The true cost depends on premiums, deductibles, copays and coinsurance, coverage limits, exclusions, and the out-of-pocket maximum for health coverage. A policy with a $50 lower monthly premium may have a $500 higher deductible — if you file two claims per year, the "cheaper" policy could cost you $1,000 more.

General Insurance Comparison Framework

QuestionWhy It Matters
What does this policy cover?Understand exactly what events trigger coverage
What does this policy exclude?Know what you are still exposed to
What is the premium and how often is it paid?Understand the ongoing cost
What is the deductible?Know your first-dollar exposure when a claim occurs
What are coverage limits?Know the maximum the insurer will pay
Is this replacement cost or actual cash value?Affects how much you actually receive for a loss
Are my preferred providers in-network?Relevant for health insurance specifically
What happens at renewal?Can premiums change? Can coverage be canceled?
What is the insurer's claims reputation?A policy from an insurer that delays or denies claims has less real value
Is there an out-of-pocket maximum?Limits catastrophic exposure for health insurance

Comparing two renters insurance policies
  • Comparison Policy A Policy B Monthly premium $14 $22 Personal property coverage $15,000 $25,000 Valuation method Actual cash value Replacement cost Deductible $500 $500 Liability coverage $50,000 $100,000 Additional living expenses $1,500 $5,000

The right policy depends on your specific belongings, savings, and risk tolerance — not just the premium

Insurance Review Checklist

Review your insurance at least once a year and after any major life change — new job, marriage, divorce, new dependent, move, new vehicle, or major change in income or assets.

Annual Insurance Review Checklist
Health Insurance
  • Is my deductible affordable if I need significant medical care?
  • Are my doctors and specialists in-network?
  • Does the formulary cover my prescription medications?
  • Is the out-of-pocket maximum within a manageable range?
Auto Insurance
  • Are my liability limits high enough to protect my assets?
  • Is my deductible affordable if I have a collision?
  • Do I need comprehensive and collision on my current vehicle?
  • Do I have uninsured motorist coverage?
Renters or Homeowners Insurance
  • Is my personal property coverage sufficient for my actual belongings?
  • Am I covered for replacement cost, not just actual cash value?
  • Is my liability limit high enough?
  • Do I need flood, earthquake, or other supplemental coverage for my area?
  • Does my policy account for any high-value items requiring riders?
Disability Insurance
  • Do I have short-term and long-term disability coverage through my employer?
  • Is the benefit amount sufficient to cover my essential monthly expenses?
  • What is the elimination period, and do I have savings to cover that gap?
  • Is my definition of disability own-occupation or any-occupation?
Life Insurance
  • Do other people depend on my income?
  • Is my death benefit sufficient to replace my income for the appropriate period?
  • Are my beneficiary designations current and correct?
  • If my term policy is nearing expiration, do I still have dependents who need coverage?

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 16.1Different insurance types cover different risks. No single policy covers all household exposures. The goal is not maximum coverage but appropriate coverage for risks that would cause serious financial harm — purchased at a cost that does not crowd out saving and investing.
  • Sec 16.2Health insurance protects against catastrophic medical costs. Key terms — premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, out-of-pocket maximum — interact to determine how costs are shared between you and the insurer. Sources include employer plans, government programs, and the ACA marketplace.
  • Sec 16.3Cost-sharing moves through three layers: deductible (you pay 100%), then coinsurance or copays (shared), then the out-of-pocket maximum (insurer pays 100%). High-deductible plans lower premiums but require accessible savings to cover the deductible when care is needed.
  • Sec 16.4Networks determine which providers are covered at negotiated rates. HMOs require a primary care physician and in-network use; PPOs allow more flexibility at higher premiums; EPOs and POS plans fall between. Verify your providers are in-network before enrolling.
  • Sec 16.5Auto insurance includes legally required liability coverage plus optional collision, comprehensive, and uninsured motorist coverage. Liability limits should protect your assets. A lower premium always means higher exposure somewhere — through higher deductibles, lower limits, or fewer coverage types.
  • Sec 16.6Renters insurance covers personal property, liability, and additional living expenses for tenants. Replacement cost coverage provides better protection than actual cash value. It is typically affordable and often required by landlords.
  • Sec 16.7Homeowners insurance covers the dwelling structure, other structures, personal property, liability, and additional living expenses. Dwelling coverage should reflect reconstruction cost, not market value. Standard policies exclude flooding and earthquakes.
  • Sec 16.8Liability coverage appears in multiple policy types because the risk — being held legally responsible for another's injury or property damage — can arise in multiple contexts. Limits should reflect your asset exposure. Umbrella policies provide additional protection above standard limits.
  • Sec 16.9Disability insurance replaces income when illness or injury prevents work. Short-term disability covers shorter gaps; long-term disability covers extended periods. Key variables are the elimination period, benefit amount, benefit period, and whether the definition is own-occupation or any-occupation.
  • Sec 16.10Life insurance provides a death benefit to dependents. Term life is affordable coverage for a specific period; permanent life provides lifelong coverage with a cash value component but at much higher premiums. Coverage amounts should reflect income replacement needs, outstanding debts, and household obligations.
  • Sec 16.11The true cost of insurance includes premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, coverage limits, and exclusions. Use a structured comparison framework and an annual insurance review checklist to ensure your coverage matches your current financial situation and risk exposure.

Key Terms Review

actual cash value (ACV)
Value of property at the time of a claim, accounting for depreciation. Lower than replacement cost.
additional living expenses (ALE)
Coverage for temporary housing and related costs when a covered loss makes a home uninhabitable. Also called loss of use coverage.
any-occupation disability
Policy definition paying benefits only if the policyholder cannot perform any occupation for which they are reasonably qualified. Narrower protection than own-occupation.
beneficiary
The person or entity designated to receive an insurance benefit, such as a life insurance death benefit. Should be reviewed after major life changes.
coinsurance
The percentage of covered costs the policyholder pays after the deductible is met; the insurer pays the remaining percentage.
collision coverage
Auto insurance paying for damage to the policyholder's vehicle in a collision, regardless of fault.
comprehensive coverage
Auto insurance paying for vehicle damage from causes other than collision — theft, weather, vandalism, or animal strikes.
copay
A fixed dollar amount paid for a specific healthcare service, such as $30 for a primary care visit.
death benefit
The amount paid to beneficiaries by a life insurance policy when the insured person dies. Generally not taxable income to beneficiaries.
deductible
The amount a policyholder pays out of pocket before insurance begins covering costs for a covered claim.
disability insurance
Insurance that replaces a portion of income — typically 50–70% — when illness or injury prevents the policyholder from working.
dwelling coverage
The component of homeowners insurance that pays to repair or rebuild the home structure from covered perils.
elimination period
The waiting period in a disability insurance policy before benefits begin. A longer elimination period lowers the premium but requires more self-coverage during the wait.
formulary
A list of prescription drugs covered by a health insurance plan, typically organized in cost tiers.
HMO
Health Maintenance Organization. A plan type requiring in-network providers and typically a primary care physician referral for specialists. Lower premiums, less flexibility.
HDHP
High-Deductible Health Plan. A plan with lower premiums and higher deductibles, often paired with a Health Savings Account (HSA).
homeowners insurance
Property and liability insurance covering a home's structure, contents, liability exposure, and additional living expenses.
in-network
Healthcare providers that have contracted with an insurer to provide services at negotiated rates. Using in-network providers substantially lowers cost.
liability coverage
Insurance paying for damages and legal costs when the policyholder is legally responsible for injuring another person or damaging their property.
long-term disability (LTD)
Disability coverage paying benefits for extended periods — potentially years to retirement age — after a qualifying elimination period.
network
The group of healthcare providers contracted with an insurer. Using out-of-network providers typically results in significantly higher costs.
out-of-pocket maximum
The annual cap on a policyholder's cost-sharing in health insurance; once reached, the insurer pays 100% of remaining covered costs for the year.
own-occupation disability
A policy definition paying benefits if the policyholder cannot perform the duties of their specific occupation — broader protection than any-occupation.
permanent life insurance
Life insurance providing coverage for the insured's entire life with a cash value component. More expensive and complex than term life.
PIP (Personal Injury Protection)
Auto insurance covering the policyholder's medical expenses and sometimes lost wages after an accident, regardless of fault. Required in no-fault states.
PPO
Preferred Provider Organization. A plan type allowing in-network and out-of-network providers, with lower costs for in-network use. Higher premiums, more flexibility.
premium
The regular payment made to maintain insurance coverage, paid whether or not any claims are filed.
renters insurance
Property and liability insurance for tenants covering personal belongings, liability exposure, and additional living expenses.
replacement cost value (RCV)
Coverage that pays to replace property with new comparable items, without depreciation. Superior to actual cash value coverage.
short-term disability (STD)
Disability coverage providing benefits for periods typically ranging from weeks to several months.
term life insurance
Life insurance providing coverage for a specific period (10, 20, or 30 years), paying a death benefit only if the insured dies during the term.
umbrella policy
Liability insurance providing additional coverage above the limits of other policies, such as homeowners and auto. Often $1 million+ at $150–$350/year.
underwriting
The process by which an insurer assesses risk to determine whether to issue coverage and at what premium. Younger, healthier individuals qualify for lower rates.
uninsured/underinsured motorist
Auto coverage paying for the policyholder's losses when the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage.

Calculation Review

16.C.1Health Insurance Out-of-Pocket Cost

Renata has health insurance with a $1,200 annual deductible, 20% coinsurance after the deductible, and an out-of-pocket maximum of $5,500. She incurs $9,000 in covered medical expenses. How much does she pay? How much does the insurer pay?

Solution — 16.C.1
  • Step 1Apply the deductible: Renata pays the first $1,200. Remaining: $9,000 − $1,200 = $7,800.
  • Step 2Apply coinsurance: Renata's 20%: $7,800 × 0.20 = $1,560 · Insurer's 80%: $7,800 × 0.80 = $6,240
  • Step 3Total out-of-pocket: $1,200 + $1,560 = $2,760. This is below the $5,500 OOP maximum. Renata pays: $2,760 | Insurer pays: $6,240
16.C.2Out-of-Pocket Maximum Scenario

Using the same plan from 16.C.1, suppose Renata's total covered expenses were $40,000 instead of $9,000. How much does she pay?

Solution — 16.C.2
  • Calculate Deductible: $1,200 Remaining amount: $40,000 − $1,200 = $38,800 20% coinsurance would be: $38,800 × 0.20 = $7,760 Deductible + coinsurance: $1,200 + $7,760 = $8,960
  • Apply OOP maximum$8,960 exceeds the $5,500 out-of-pocket maximum. Once Renata's spending reaches $5,500, the insurer covers 100% of remaining costs. Renata pays: $5,500 | Insurer pays: $34,500
  • InterpretationThis is why the out-of-pocket maximum is the critical protection against catastrophic medical expenses. Without it, Renata's liability would be $8,960; with it, it is capped at $5,500.
16.C.3Auto Insurance Deductible Tradeoff

Theo chooses between: Option A — $500 deductible, $1,020 annual premium; Option B — $1,000 deductible, $840 annual premium. (a) Annual savings from Option B? (b) Which costs less if he files one claim per year? (c) How many claim-free years for Option B to break even?

Solution — 16.C.3
  • Part aAnnual savings: $1,020 − $840 = $180/year
  • Part b Option A total (1 claim): $1,020 + $500 = $1,520 Option B total (1 claim): $840 + $1,000 = $1,840 Option A costs less if Theo files one claim per year.
  • Part cExtra deductible cost for Option B: $500 more per claim. Break-even: $500 ÷ $180 ≈ 2.8 years. If Theo expects fewer than one claim every three years, Option B saves money overall.
16.C.4Life Insurance Income Replacement Estimate

Darius earns $72,000/year. His household needs income replacement for 15 years. He has $18,000 in outstanding debt and $145,000 remaining on his mortgage. Estimate his life insurance need.

Solution — 16.C.4
  • Calculate Income replacement: $72,000 × 15 = $1,080,000 Outstanding debt: + $18,000 Mortgage payoff: + $145,000 Total estimated need: $1,243,000
  • RecommendationA term life policy of approximately $1.25–$1.5 million. A financial planner would also factor in investment returns on the lump sum, existing savings and assets, and Social Security survivor benefits.

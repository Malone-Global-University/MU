Chapter 8 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every financial system that moves money also creates opportunities for people to steal it. This chapter introduces the threats that target financial accounts, personal information, and consumer trust — and it introduces the legal protections, technical tools, consumer rights, and personal habits that reduce those risks.

Students who finish this chapter will be able to recognize common fraud and scams, protect their accounts and identity, respond effectively when something goes wrong, and build a personal financial safety plan that stays practical without making them afraid of everyday financial technology.

Opening Question

You receive a phone call. The caller says he is from the Social Security Administration and that your Social Security number has been suspended due to suspicious criminal activity. To clear your name, you must pay $500 in gift cards immediately or you will be arrested. He tells you not to tell anyone. What do you do — and how did you know what to do? By the end of this chapter, recognizing this threat and knowing exactly how to respond will be second nature.

The Stakes and the Threats: Fraud and Scams

Section 8.1

Why Financial Safety Matters

Financial safety is not a topic reserved for wealthy people, elderly people, or careless people. Fraud, scams, and identity theft affect people of every income level, age, background, and level of education. The Federal Trade Commission receives millions of consumer fraud reports every year. Billions of dollars are lost annually to financial crime in the United States alone — and that figure undercounts what actually happens, because many victims never report what happened to them.

For someone with a small savings account and limited income, losing even a few hundred dollars to fraud can mean missing rent, skipping a car payment, or draining an emergency fund it took months to build. Financial crime does not only threaten wealthy people with large investment portfolios. It threatens anyone who has a bank account, a debit card, a Social Security number, or a phone number.

A Common Mistake

It is tempting to assume that careful, educated people never get scammed and that victims must have been careless. This is not accurate. Financial criminals are often highly sophisticated. They study human psychology, exploit trust, and adapt constantly. Scams that would seem obvious in calm analysis can be deeply convincing in the moment — especially when they target urgency, fear, excitement, grief, or love.

Recognizing that fraud is a systemic problem — not only a personal failure — is the first step toward understanding it clearly. At the same time, individuals have meaningful power to reduce their own exposure. Good habits, account monitoring, strong authentication, and rapid response when problems appear make a significant difference. The goal is not to become fearful of all financial technology. It is to understand risk accurately and respond to it proportionately.

Section 8.2

Fraud and Scams

Fraud is the intentional deception of a person or institution for financial gain — misrepresenting facts, concealing information, or creating false impressions to take money or property. Fraud is a crime under both state and federal law. A scam is a fraudulent scheme: a specific organized method of committing fraud. The words are often used interchangeably in everyday language.

Common Scam Categories

Impersonation Scams
Criminal pretends to be a bank, IRS, Social Security Administration, or other trusted institution. Claims something is wrong and demands immediate payment or personal information.
⚠ Urgent demands · Gift card payments · "Don't call the bank"
Prize & Lottery Scams
Victim is told they won a contest they never entered. A fee must be paid upfront to claim the prize. The prize never arrives once payment is sent.
⚠ Win without entering · Pay to receive prize · Expiring deadline
Romance Scams
Criminal builds a false relationship online over weeks or months, then introduces a financial crisis requiring the victim to send money — repeatedly.
⚠ Never meets in person · Rapid emotional attachment · Recurring money requests
Investment Scams
Promises unusually high returns with little or no risk. Includes Ponzi schemes, fraudulent cryptocurrency deals, fake stock tips, and phony real estate investments.
⚠ Guaranteed returns · Pressure to act fast · Unclear how returns are generated
Employment Scams
Fake job offers with high pay and minimal requirements. Victim provides personal information or receives a fraudulent check and is asked to return the "excess" before it bounces.
⚠ Pay upfront for training · Check for more than agreed · Unusually easy high-pay work
Charity Scams
Fake or misleading charitable causes — often mimicking legitimate organizations during disasters. Donations go to the scammer, not to those in need.
⚠ High-pressure donation requests · Names similar to real charities · Gift card / wire payment requests

The Anatomy of a Scam

Most scams share a recognizable structure. Understanding the pattern makes individual scams easier to identify before harm is done.

  1. Establish Contact and CredibilityThe scammer reaches the victim by phone, email, text, or social media. They present themselves as trustworthy — impersonating an official, offering something attractive, or building a relationship.
  2. Create Urgency or EmotionScammers rarely allow time for calm reflection. Urgency ("You must respond in the next hour"), fear ("Your account will be closed"), or emotional connection ("I need your help") disrupts careful thinking. This pressure is intentional.
  3. Request Money or InformationPayment in hard-to-reverse forms: gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or money orders. Or personal information: Social Security number, account numbers, passwords, or verification codes.
  4. Resist VerificationScammers actively discourage victims from verifying through official channels. "Don't call the bank — they can't help with this." "This is confidential — don't tell family members." These instructions exist because verification would reveal the scam.
The Hang-Up Rule

If someone contacts you claiming to be from a bank, government agency, or other institution, and asks for money or personal information — do not respond to that contact directly. End the call or interaction. Look up the institution's official phone number independently from their official website or the back of your card, and call it yourself. Scammers depend on controlling the communication channel. Cutting that channel defeats a major part of their approach.

Worked Example 8.2.1
Recognizing a Government Impersonation Scam
  • SituationMaya receives a phone call. The caller says he is from the Social Security Administration. He tells Maya that her Social Security number has been suspended due to suspicious activity linked to a drug smuggling case. To clear her name, she must pay $500 in gift cards immediately. If she does not pay today, she will be arrested. He tells her not to tell anyone or the case will be escalated.
  • Red FlagsGovernment agencies do not call to demand immediate gift card payment. The threat of arrest for not paying by phone is not how law enforcement works. The instruction to keep the call secret is designed to isolate the victim. "Suspended" Social Security numbers are not a real government action.
  • ResponseMaya should end the call immediately without providing any information or payment. If concerned, she can call the SSA directly using the number from ssa.gov — not any number the caller provided. She can report the call to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
Answer: This is a classic government impersonation scam. Hang up, verify independently, and report.

Section 8.3

Identity Theft

Identity theft occurs when someone uses another person's personal information — Social Security number, name, date of birth, account numbers, or other identifying data — without permission, typically to commit financial fraud or access financial accounts. Identity theft is not one single crime; it is a category that includes many different schemes.

Type of Identity TheftWhat the Criminal Does
New account fraudOpens new credit cards or loans in the victim's name
Tax identity theftFiles a fraudulent tax return to steal the victim's refund
Account takeoverTakes control of existing bank or investment accounts
Medical identity theftUses stolen health insurance information to receive medical care
Benefits fraudApplies for government benefits using the victim's identity
Synthetic identity fraudCombines real and fake information to create a new false identity, often using a real SSN

How Personal Information Is Stolen

Data breaches occur when criminals gain unauthorized access to databases held by companies, hospitals, government agencies, or financial institutions. An individual can be a victim of a data breach without ever making a mistake, because the failure happened at the institution that held their data.

Physical theft includes stolen mail (bank statements, tax documents, credit card offers), stolen wallets, and information taken from trash or mailboxes. Skimming devices are hardware attached to ATMs or gas pump card readers that capture card data when a card is swiped. Account takeover uses stolen or guessed passwords to access existing accounts and lock the real owner out.

Warning Signs of Identity Theft

  • Unexpected bills or collection notices for debts you do not recognize
  • Unfamiliar accounts appearing on your credit report
  • Denial of a loan or credit application due to unexpected negative information
  • Tax return rejected because a return was already filed using your Social Security number
  • Missing expected mail — bills, statements, or government correspondence
  • Unauthorized charges on bank or credit card statements
  • Account login failures when your password has not changed
Worked Example 8.3.1
Responding to Discovered Identity Theft
  • SituationDiego applies for a car loan and is told his application was denied due to multiple unpaid credit card accounts he does not recognize. He pulls his free credit report and finds three credit cards opened in his name over the past 18 months — all with large balances sent to collections — that he never applied for.
  • Step 1Place a fraud alert with one credit bureau (they are required to notify the other two). An initial alert lasts one year; victims can request a seven-year extended alert.
  • Step 2Place credit freezes with all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) separately. A freeze prevents any new credit from being opened in Diego's name.
  • Step 3File an identity theft report at IdentityTheft.gov. The FTC provides a personalized recovery plan and official documentation needed for disputes.
  • Step 4Dispute the fraudulent accounts in writing with each credit bureau, attaching the FTC identity theft report. Contact the credit card companies directly to report the fraud.
  • CheckThis process takes time — disputes can take 30 to 45 days or longer. Diego should keep copies of all correspondence and follow up if deadlines are not met.
Next steps: Fraud alert → Credit freeze → IdentityTheft.gov → Dispute fraudulent accounts → Monitor for further activity.
Section 8.4

Phishing and Social Engineering

Phishing is a type of attack in which a criminal sends a fraudulent communication — typically an email, text message, or social media message — that appears to come from a legitimate source in order to trick the recipient into revealing sensitive information, clicking a malicious link, or downloading harmful software.

Attack TypeChannelDescription
PhishingEmailBroad fraudulent emails impersonating banks, retailers, government agencies, or tech companies
Spear phishingEmailTargeted attack using personal details to appear more convincing to a specific individual
SmishingSMS textPhishing via text message — fake delivery alerts, bank warnings, prize notifications
VishingPhone callPhishing via voice call — impersonating bank fraud departments, IRS, or tech support
PretextingAnyFabricated scenario designed to establish credibility before extracting information
Worked Example 8.4.1
Identifying a Phishing Email
  • Email From: security-alerts@chase-bank-verification.com Subject: URGENT: Your Chase account has been locked Dear Valued Customer, We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Your account has been temporarily locked. You must verify your information within 24 hours or your account will be permanently closed. [Click Here to Verify Your Account]
  • Red FlagsSender domain is chase-bank-verification.com, not chase.com. "Dear Valued Customer" — not the account holder's name. 24-hour deadline creates panic. "Click Here" link destination is unknown. Threat of permanent closure prevents calm evaluation.
  • ResponseDo not click the link. Log into Chase directly by typing chase.com into the browser, or call the number on the back of your card. If the account is fine, delete the email and report it as phishing.
Answer: Five distinct red flags. The correct response is to close the email and contact the institution through a verified channel.
How to Evaluate a Suspicious Message

1. Pause. Urgency is the attacker's tool. Pausing defeats it. 2. Do not click links or open attachments. 3. Evaluate the sender's actual email address — is it genuinely from the institution it claims? 4. Look at the URL before clicking — hover to reveal the destination. 5. Contact the institution independently using their official phone number or website. 6. When in doubt, delete. If the message is legitimate, the institution will contact you again.

Section 8.5

Passwords and Multifactor Authentication

Passwords are the most common method of protecting access to financial accounts, email, and personal information. A weak or reused password is one of the most common pathways through which accounts are compromised. In a credential stuffing attack, criminals take username-password combinations exposed in a data breach and test them across many other websites — because people frequently reuse the same password in multiple places.

Principles of Strong Passwords

PrincipleWhy It MattersExample
LengthLonger passwords are dramatically harder to crack. A 16-character passphrase is typically stronger than a short password with symbols."BlueRiverTableGlass" vs. "P@ss1!"
UniquenessEvery important account needs a different password. A breach at one service should not compromise all others.Different password for bank, email, each card
Avoid obvious infoNames, birth dates, phone numbers, and addresses can often be found on social media and used to guess passwords.Not "Max2007" or "JohnSmith1985"
RandomnessPredictable patterns (Summer2024!, Password1!) offer less protection than they appear to.Use a password manager to generate truly random passwords
Password Managers

Remembering dozens of unique, strong passwords is not realistic for most people. Password managers are applications that generate, store, and fill strong passwords securely. They require only one strong master password to access all others. Using a password manager is significantly more secure than reusing passwords or writing them in a note on your phone.

Multifactor Authentication (MFA)

Multifactor authentication (MFA), also called two-factor authentication (2FA), adds a second verification step beyond the password. Even if an attacker obtains the correct password, MFA requires something additional before access is granted. MFA should be enabled on every financial account that offers it — and also on email accounts, since email is often used to reset passwords for financial accounts.

MFA MethodHow It WorksSecurity Level
SMS text codeA one-time code is sent to a registered phone numberBetter than none; can be intercepted via SIM swapping
Authenticator appApp generates time-limited codes on the device without a phone networkMore secure than SMS; recommended
Hardware security keyPhysical device (USB or tap) that must be present to complete loginHighly secure; less common in consumer use
BiometricsFingerprint or face recognition as device login layerConvenient; as secure as the device itself
Decision Checklist 8.5.1 — Password and Authentication Audit

For each important account (bank, investment, email, tax, Social Security), verify:

  • Is my password at least 12 characters long?
  • Is this password unique — not used anywhere else?
  • Does this password avoid my name, birthdate, phone number, or other obvious information?
  • Am I using a password manager to store this securely?
  • Is multifactor authentication enabled on this account?
  • If MFA is available, am I using an authenticator app rather than only SMS?
  • Is MFA also enabled on my email account (since email can reset financial account passwords)?
  • When did I last review whether this password appeared in a known data breach?
Section 8.6

Secure Websites and Safe Transactions

When entering any sensitive information online — a password, a credit card number, a Social Security number — the connection between the browser and the website should be encrypted. The indicator of this encryption is the HTTPS prefix in the web address and a padlock icon in the address bar.

Important Limitation of HTTPS

HTTPS tells you the connection is encrypted. It does not tell you that the website itself is legitimate. A phishing website can have HTTPS. The padlock means the connection to that website is secure — it does not mean the website is who it claims to be. Always verify the domain name carefully before entering any credentials or payment information.

Recognizing a Legitimate Domain

URLLegitimate?Why
www.bankofamerica.com✓ RealGenuine domain with correct spelling
www.bankofamerica-login.com✗ FakeHyphenated domain — the real domain is bankofamerica-login.com, not bankofamerica.com
www.bankofamerica.com.secure-login.net✗ FakeThe actual domain is secure-login.net — bankofamerica.com is just a subdomain
www.bank0famerica.com✗ FakeZero substituted for the letter "o"
Tip — Public Wi-Fi and Financial Transactions

Open public Wi-Fi allows other users on the same network to potentially observe network traffic. Avoid conducting financial transactions on public Wi-Fi if possible. Use a personal mobile data connection or a VPN (Virtual Private Network) if public Wi-Fi cannot be avoided. Never conduct sensitive financial transactions on shared or public computers.

Section 8.7

Account Alerts and Monitoring

The fastest response to unauthorized activity in a financial account comes when the account holder notices it quickly. Every day that passes before unauthorized charges or withdrawals are reported is another day the attacker may continue — and, in some cases, a day that affects the legal protections available to the account holder. Active monitoring also catches errors that are not fraud: a merchant charging twice, an automatic subscription that was cancelled, or a bank fee applied incorrectly.

Account Alert Types

Alert TypeWhat It Catches
Transaction over a threshold (e.g., any purchase over $50)Large unauthorized charges
Login to the accountUnauthorized account access
Password or contact information changeAccount takeover in progress
New payee added for bill payRedirected payments
Balance below a minimum (e.g., below $100)Overdraft risk; large unauthorized withdrawals
Card used online or internationallyCard number theft
Failed login attemptsPassword guessing attempt underway

Credit Report Monitoring

Credit reports show whether new accounts, inquiries, or public records have appeared in the consumer's name. Reviewing credit reports regularly catches identity theft that has already opened new accounts. Under federal law, every consumer is entitled to one free credit report per year from each of the three major credit bureaus through AnnualCreditReport.com. Reviewing one bureau's report every four months — rotating among the three — provides year-round monitoring at no cost.

FrequencyAction
Real-timeReceive and review account alerts via text or email
WeeklyQuick check of account balances and recent transactions
MonthlyFull statement review for all financial accounts
Every 4 monthsPull one credit bureau report (rotating across Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
AnnuallyFull review of all three credit reports, insurance policies, and account permissions
When something seems wrongInvestigate immediately — do not wait for the next scheduled review
Section 8.8

Consumer Rights and Dispute Processes

Consumers in the United States are protected by a significant body of federal law governing financial accounts, payment disputes, credit reporting, and lending practices. These protections establish rights, procedures, and timelines that consumers can use to recover from financial losses caused by fraud, unauthorized transactions, and errors.

Key Federal Laws and Agencies

Law or AgencyWhat It Covers
EFTA — Electronic Fund Transfer ActConsumer rights regarding electronic transactions — debit card charges, ATM withdrawals, electronic bill payments. Limits liability for unauthorized electronic transactions.
FCBA — Fair Credit Billing ActCredit card billing disputes. Gives consumers the right to dispute charges and limits liability for unauthorized credit card charges to $50.
FCRA — Fair Credit Reporting ActCredit reporting accuracy. Gives consumers the right to access credit reports, dispute inaccurate information, and require investigations.
CFPB — Consumer Financial Protection BureauFederal agency that supervises financial institutions and accepts consumer complaints about banks, credit card companies, debt collectors, and credit bureaus.

Unauthorized Debit Card Charges — Liability Limits Under the EFTA

Timing of ReportConsumer Liability
Before any unauthorized transactions occur$0
Within 2 business days of discovering the lossUp to $50
3 to 60 days after discovering the lossUp to $500
More than 60 days after statement containing the unauthorized chargePotentially unlimited — may lose all funds taken
Why Timing Matters

The same fraud can cost $50 or thousands of dollars depending solely on when it is reported. This is why monitoring accounts and reporting problems quickly is so important — the liability limits under the EFTA are tied directly to how fast the consumer acts.

Credit Cards Offer Stronger Protections

The FCBA caps credit card liability for unauthorized charges at $50 regardless of when reported — and most major card issuers offer zero liability. More importantly: the consumer does not have to pay the disputed charge during the investigation period, meaning the consumer is not out of money while the dispute is resolved. With a debit card, the money has already left the account. This difference is one reason financial professionals often recommend using a credit card (paid in full monthly to avoid interest) for everyday purchases rather than a debit card.

Worked Example 8.8.1
Debit Card Unauthorized Charges — Steps and Liability
  • SituationPriya reviews her bank account on the 12th of the month and notices three charges totaling $340 from a retailer she has never heard of. The charges appeared between the 8th and 10th. Her debit card is still in her possession, but the card number must have been stolen — possibly through a skimming device at a gas pump she used last week.
  • Step 1Call the bank immediately to report unauthorized charges. Ask for a dispute claim number and the name of the representative. End the call with written confirmation of the dispute in case follow-up is needed.
  • Step 2Follow up in writing within two business days of discovering the charges. Keep a copy of the letter and the certified mail receipt.
  • Step 3Request a replacement debit card with a new card number. Change her PIN and review any automatic payments linked to the old card number so those can be updated.
  • LiabilityBecause she reported within 2 business days of discovering the charges, her maximum liability under the EFTA is $50. The $340 should be credited back to her account once the bank confirms the charges were unauthorized.
Outcome: Report quickly → liability capped at $50 → replacement card → update autopay links.
Section 8.9

Reporting Financial Problems

Reporting financial crimes and problems serves two purposes: it helps the individual resolve their specific situation, and it provides information to agencies that investigate fraud, protect consumers, and build cases against criminal enterprises. Many victims feel embarrassed and do not report what happened — but this means criminals continue operating without consequences, and consumer protection agencies have less data with which to identify patterns and warn others.

Reporting a Compromised Account to the Financial Institution

Call the number on the back of the card or on the official account statement — not a number found in a suspicious email or through an internet search (which might lead to a fake support number). Ask to speak with the fraud department specifically. Request a case or reference number for every call. Follow up in writing through the institution's official secure message system or by certified mail.

Section 8.10

Building a Personal Financial Safety Plan

A personal financial safety plan is a set of proactive habits, decisions, and records that reduce exposure to fraud and scams and enable a fast, organized response when problems occur. It is not a complicated document — it is a checklist of protections put in place, and a record of the accounts, contacts, and resources to use if something goes wrong.

Component 1 — Account Inventory

Know what financial accounts exist — checking, savings, credit cards, investment accounts, retirement accounts — and where each one is held. This allows quick identification of unauthorized accounts, ensures all accounts are monitored, and provides contact information needed for rapid response.

Keeping Safety in Perspective

Financial safety habits can sound overwhelming when listed together. In practice, most require setup time once and then become automatic. Setting up account alerts takes five to fifteen minutes per account — once. Installing a password manager and generating new passwords takes an hour or two — once. Enabling multifactor authentication takes a few minutes per account — once. After setup, the ongoing habits — checking statements, reviewing credit reports, recognizing suspicious messages — take modest time and become routine. The goal is not constant anxiety about financial threats. It is informed, calm vigilance.

Mini-Project: Personal Financial Safety Checklist

Using the components in this section, build a personal financial safety checklist. Include: (1) a list of at least four financial accounts with institution name, account type, and alert status; (2) an authentication audit for each account; (3) a monitoring schedule; (4) an emergency contact list; (5) a brief document security plan. For privacy — do not include actual account numbers, passwords, or PINs. Use institution names and account types only.

Chapter 8 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 8.1Financial safety is not optional and not a specialty concern for wealthy people. Fraud targets anyone who participates in the financial system. Safety requires both personal habits and system awareness.
  • Sec 8.2Fraud and scams follow a recognizable structure: establish credibility, create urgency or emotion, request money or information, and discourage verification. The hang-up rule — ending contact and calling back independently — defeats a major part of most scams.
  • Sec 8.3Identity theft includes new account fraud, tax fraud, account takeover, medical fraud, benefits fraud, and synthetic identity fraud. Recovery requires fraud alerts, credit freezes, an FTC identity theft report, and formal disputes with credit bureaus and creditors.
  • Sec 8.4Phishing — by email, text (smishing), or phone (vishing) — impersonates trusted institutions to steal credentials or install malware. Pausing, evaluating the sender carefully, and contacting institutions through independently verified channels are the most reliable defenses.
  • Sec 8.5Strong, unique passwords managed through a password manager combined with MFA on all financial and email accounts significantly reduce the risk of unauthorized account access. An authenticator app is more secure than SMS-based codes for MFA.
  • Sec 8.6HTTPS indicates an encrypted connection, not a legitimate website. Domain names must be verified carefully before entering credentials. Public Wi-Fi poses risks for financial transactions; a VPN adds protection when a secure connection is unavailable.
  • Sec 8.7Account alerts and regular statement review provide early detection of unauthorized activity. Credit reports should be reviewed at minimum every four months (rotating among the three bureaus) to catch new accounts opened fraudulently.
  • Sec 8.8The EFTA limits debit card liability to $50 if reported within two business days of discovery — but liability grows sharply with delay. Credit cards offer stronger protection: $50 maximum by law, zero liability by most issuers' policies, and no out-of-pocket loss during dispute investigation.
  • Sec 8.9Reporting fraud to the FTC, CFPB, and relevant agencies helps both the individual and the broader consumer protection system. Reporting quickly preserves the strongest legal protections and gives the best chance of fund recovery.
  • Sec 8.10A personal financial safety plan — account inventory, authentication checklist, monitoring routine, emergency contact list, and document security plan — brings all protections together in a form that can be acted on when something goes wrong.

Key Terms Review

account alert
An automated notification sent when a specified activity occurs — a large transaction, a login, or a password change.
credential stuffing
An attack testing username-password combinations from data breaches across multiple sites, exploiting password reuse.
credit bureau
One of the three major agencies (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) that collect consumer credit information and produce credit reports.
credit freeze
A restriction on a credit file that prevents new credit from being opened without the freeze being temporarily lifted.
data breach
An incident where unauthorized parties access protected data — passwords, Social Security numbers, or financial account information — held by a company or institution.
EFTA

FCBA
Fair Credit Billing Act — federal law giving credit card holders the right to dispute billing errors and capping liability for unauthorized charges at $50.
FCRA
Fair Credit Reporting Act — federal law governing credit report accuracy and giving consumers the right to dispute inaccurate information.
fraud
Intentional deception for financial gain through misrepresentation of facts, concealment of information, or creation of false impressions.
fraud alert
A notice on a credit file requiring creditors to take extra steps to verify identity before opening new accounts in the consumer's name.
HTTPS
Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure — indicates the connection between browser and website is encrypted. Does not confirm the website is legitimate.
identity theft
The unauthorized use of another person's personal information, typically to commit financial fraud or access financial accounts.
multifactor authentication
A security method requiring two or more verification steps — such as a password plus a one-time code — before granting account access.
password manager
An application that generates, stores, and fills strong unique passwords, requiring the user to remember only one master password.
phishing
A fraudulent communication — typically by email — designed to appear from a legitimate source in order to steal credentials, personal information, or money.
pretexting
A social engineering technique in which a fabricated scenario is created to gain a victim's trust and extract information or access.
scam
An organized fraudulent scheme designed to deceive victims into providing money or personal information.
SIM swapping
An attack in which a criminal convinces a phone carrier to transfer the victim's phone number to a device the criminal controls, allowing interception of SMS-based authentication codes.
skimming device
Hardware attached to a card reader at an ATM or gas pump that captures card data when a physical card is swiped.
smishing
Phishing conducted via SMS text message.
social engineering
The use of psychological manipulation to influence people into taking actions or revealing information that serves an attacker's goals.
spear phishing
A targeted phishing attack using personal details about a specific individual to make the fraudulent message more convincing.
synthetic identity fraud
Identity theft using a combination of real and fabricated information — often a real Social Security number with a false name — to create a new false identity.
VPN
Virtual Private Network — a service that encrypts internet traffic and routes it through a secure server, providing privacy protection on public or untrusted networks.
vishing
Phishing conducted via voice phone call.

Calculation Review

8.1 — Debit Card Liability Under the EFTA

Consumer liability depends on how quickly unauthorized charges are reported after discovery.

Example A — Fast Report
Jordan notices a $600 unauthorized charge on a Monday morning and calls her bank that same day.
  • AnalysisShe reported on the same day she discovered the charge — within 2 business days of discovery.
  • AnswerMaximum liability: $50
Example B — Delayed Report
Jordan does not notice the charge until reviewing a monthly statement 65 days after it appeared.
  • AnalysisMore than 60 days have passed since the statement containing the unauthorized charge was sent.
  • AnswerMaximum liability: potentially the full $600 — and any additional unauthorized charges after day 60
Lesson: The same fraud can cost $50 or the full amount, depending entirely on how quickly it is reported.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1
"Smart people don't fall for scams." Scammers study psychology and adapt continuously. Highly educated, financially sophisticated people fall victim to fraud regularly. The emotional manipulation in many scams — urgency, fear, romance, grief — bypasses rational evaluation regardless of intelligence. Recognizing scams is a learned skill, not evidence of intelligence.
Misconception 2
"Identity theft only affects people with good credit or high incomes." Criminals target anyone with a usable identity. Children's Social Security numbers are used in synthetic identity fraud precisely because children have no credit history. People with lower incomes who have limited reason to check their credit may not discover theft for months or years.
Misconception 3
"Fraud is always committed by strangers." A significant portion of identity theft involves someone known to the victim — a family member, romantic partner, caregiver, or colleague with access to personal documents or account information. This makes it particularly difficult to recognize and report.
Misconception 4
"If I've been scammed, there's nothing I can do to get my money back." In some cases money can be recovered through bank dispute processes, credit card chargebacks, or law enforcement action. Recovery is not guaranteed, especially when money was sent via wire transfer, gift card, or cryptocurrency — but reporting quickly and following the correct steps gives the best chance.

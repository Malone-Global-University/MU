Chapter 13 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

This chapter introduces taxes through the one document that almost every working person encounters before any other tax form: the paycheck. Before students learn to file taxes, prepare tax returns, or understand the full tax code, they need to understand why money disappears between what an employer pays and what arrives in a bank account. This chapter explains that gap — every line of it — so that any student who picks up a pay stub can read it completely and verify it accurately.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to calculate gross pay for hourly and salaried workers, identify every major deduction category on a pay stub, explain the difference between income taxes and payroll taxes, calculate Social Security and Medicare withholding, and distinguish pre-tax from post-tax deductions. These skills carry directly into Chapter 14, where the tax return reconciles what was withheld with what is actually owed.

Opening Question

A job offer says you will earn $22 per hour. After your first paycheck, you notice the deposit is noticeably smaller than you expected. Where did the money go? Is something wrong? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to answer that question for any paycheck — and verify that every number on your pay stub is exactly what it should be.

Section 13.1

Why Taxes Appear on Paychecks

Most people encounter taxes for the first time not in a government office and not in tax preparation software — but in a paycheck. They work a job, expect to be paid, and discover that the deposit is noticeably smaller than what the employer said they would earn. The gap between those two numbers is not a mistake. It is the result of several taxes and deductions that federal law, state law, and sometimes local law require employers to collect on behalf of their employees.

Understanding this is one of the most practically important lessons in personal finance. The paycheck is where most financial plans begin. A person who cannot read their pay stub cannot accurately estimate spendable income, cannot build a reliable budget, and cannot catch errors in withholding — which happen more often than most people realize.

Key Insight

Taxes on paychecks are not punishments or surprises for workers who understand the system. They are advance collections of obligations the worker already has. The tax return filed later in the year reconciles those advance payments against the actual amount owed.

Section 13.2

Gross Pay and Net Pay

Core Formula

Net Pay = Gross Pay − All Taxes − All Deductions

Gross Pay — total compensation earned before any deductions

Net Pay (Take-Home Pay) — the amount actually received; the number that drives financial planning

Calculating Gross Pay for Hourly Workers

Example 13.2a
Hourly gross pay — no overtime
  • ProblemMarcus earns $18.50 per hour and works 40 hours in a week.
  • FormulaGross Pay = Hourly Rate × Hours Worked
  • Solve$18.50 × 40 = $740.00
Gross Pay: $740.00
Overtime Rule

Federal law (the Fair Labor Standards Act) requires at least 1.5× the regular hourly rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. Some states have stricter rules. Always verify your employer's overtime policy and applicable state law.

Example 13.2b
Hourly gross pay — with overtime
  • ProblemMarcus works 46 hours. Overtime rate is 1.5× regular rate.
  • Regular40 × $18.50 = $740.00
  • Overtime6 × ($18.50 × 1.5) = 6 × $27.75 = $166.50
  • Solve$740.00 + $166.50 = $906.50
Gross Pay with Overtime: $906.50

Student Warning — Planning with the Wrong Number

The gap between gross and net pay can be 20–25% or more. A worker who budgets based on gross pay will consistently overestimate available money. Always plan with net pay — the actual amount deposited.

Section 13.3

Pay Stubs

A pay stub (also called a paycheck stub or earnings statement) is the document that accompanies each paycheck and explains how gross pay was calculated and how deductions reduced it to net pay. Pay stubs vary in format between employers but contain the same essential information. Review your pay stub every pay period — errors in payroll systems happen, and the worker who catches them promptly avoids compounding problems.

Why Review Every Period?

Pay stubs are verification tools, not just records. Benefits elections are sometimes processed incorrectly. Hours can be miscounted. Tax withholding can be miscalculated. A worker who reviews their stub every period catches errors while they are small; one who never reads it may discover a year's worth of problems at tax filing time.

Section 13.4

Income Tax Withholding

Income tax withholding is the process by which an employer deducts a portion of each paycheck and sends it to the federal government (and state government, where applicable) as an advance payment toward the employee's income tax obligation. Withholding is not the tax itself — it is a prepayment. The actual tax obligation is determined when the worker files a tax return. If more was withheld than owed, the worker receives a refund. If less was withheld, the worker owes the balance due.

Form W-4 — How Employers Determine Withholding

Employers calculate withholding using the employee's Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate). This form captures filing status, multiple-job situations, dependents, and additional withholding requests. The employer applies W-4 information to IRS withholding tables to calculate each paycheck's withholding amount.

Employees can update their W-4 at any time — after marriage, a new child, a second job, or any change that affects annual tax liability.

Filing StatusEffect on Withholding
SingleGenerally the highest withholding rate; tax brackets are narrower
Married Filing JointlyLower withholding rate; wider tax brackets than Single
Head of HouseholdIntermediate withholding; available to qualifying single parents
Large Refund vs. Balance Due

A large refund means you overpaid throughout the year — effectively lending the government money interest-free. A balance due means you underpaid. Neither is inherently better; the goal is accurate withholding that matches actual liability. Workers consistently over-withheld may consider submitting a new W-4 to reclaim that money throughout the year instead.

Example 13.4a
Annualizing withholding from a pay stub
  • ProblemJordan's pay stub shows $168.44 in federal income tax withheld for one semimonthly period (24 per year). Estimate annual withholding.
  • Solve$168.44 × 24 = $4,042.56 estimated annual withholding
  • NoteWhether this matches Jordan's actual liability depends on total annual income, credits, and deductions — determined at filing.
Estimated Annual Federal Withholding: $4,042.56
Student Warning — States Without Income Tax

Workers in Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming do not have state income tax withheld. New Hampshire and Tennessee historically taxed only investment income, not wages. Workers in states with income tax will see a separate state withholding line on every pay stub.

Section 13.5

Payroll Taxes

Payroll taxes are distinct from income taxes. They fund Social Security and Medicare specifically and are calculated as fixed percentages of wages regardless of filing status, deductions, or credits. Unlike income taxes, payroll taxes apply at the same rate to all workers up to certain limits.

Medicare (HI)1.45%1.45%2.9%No cap — all wages subject

Example 13.5a
Verifying payroll tax calculations — Jordan's pay stub
  • ProblemGross pay: $1,892.00. Verify Social Security and Medicare shown on the stub.
  • Social Security$1,892.00 × 0.062 = $117.30 ✓
  • Medicare$1,892.00 × 0.0145 = $27.43 ✓
  • Employer Match$117.30 + $27.43 = $144.73 (employer pays this additionally)
Total payroll taxes from this one period: $289.46 combined (employee + employer)
Self-Employed Workers

Self-employed workers pay both the employee and employer share — the full 15.3% — because they are both parties at once. However, they may deduct the employer-equivalent half when calculating income taxes, partially offsetting this higher rate.

Section 13.6

Social Security and Medicare at an Introductory Level

Every paycheck connects directly to two of the largest federal programs in the United States through payroll taxes. Understanding what these programs do helps workers understand what their contributions fund — and what they may eventually receive.

Social Security

Benefit TypeWho QualifiesKey Details
Retirement BenefitsWorkers with sufficient credits who reach qualifying agesReduced benefits from age 62; full at full retirement age; maximum at 70
Disability Benefits (SSDI)Workers unable to work due to qualifying disabilityBased on earnings record; requires sufficient credits
Survivors' BenefitsSpouses, children, and sometimes parents of deceased workersAmount depends on deceased worker's earnings record
The Three-Legged Stool of Retirement

Social Security was designed as one part of retirement income, not the sole source. Financial planners often describe a "three-legged stool": (1) Social Security, (2) employer retirement plans or pensions, and (3) personal savings. Workers who rely on Social Security alone typically face significant financial hardship in retirement, as it replaces only a portion of pre-retirement income.

Medicare

Part AHospital InsuranceHospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, some home health care; funded by the 1.45% Medicare payroll tax

Connection to Your Paycheck

The 1.45% Medicare tax on every paycheck funds Part A — Hospital Insurance. Workers who pay Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters) generally qualify for Part A with no monthly premium when they reach age 65. Workers should periodically verify their Social Security earnings record through the SSA website to confirm that reported wages are accurate — errors can affect future benefit calculations.

Section 13.7

Pre-Tax Deductions

Pre-tax deductions are subtracted from gross pay before income taxes are calculated. Because they reduce taxable income, they reduce the income tax a worker owes. This makes pre-tax deductions more valuable, dollar for dollar, than the same amount deducted after taxes. Pre-tax deductions generally do not reduce Social Security and Medicare taxes — payroll taxes are typically calculated on gross wages.

Common Pre-Tax DeductionDescription
Employer-sponsored health insurance premiumEmployee share of group health plan premium; typically runs through a Section 125 Cafeteria Plan
Traditional 401(k) / 403(b) contributionRetirement contribution that reduces taxable income in the contribution year
Health Flexible Spending Account (FSA)Pre-tax dollars for eligible medical expenses; copays, prescriptions, eyeglasses
Dependent Care FSAPre-tax dollars for qualifying childcare or adult dependent care
Health Savings Account (HSA)Pre-tax contributions for workers enrolled in qualifying high-deductible health plans
Dental and vision insurance premiumsEmployee share of employer-sponsored dental/vision coverage
How Pre-Tax Deductions Reduce Tax

Taxable Wage = Gross Pay − Pre-Tax Deductions

Income tax withholding is calculated on the taxable wage, not on gross pay. The lower the taxable wage, the lower the income tax withheld.

Example 13.7a
Calculating the tax benefit of pre-tax deductions
  • SetupGross pay: $2,000. Pre-tax health insurance: $200. Pre-tax 401(k): $100. Assumed combined income tax rate: 20%.
  • WithoutTaxable Wage: $2,000.00 Income Tax (20%): $400.00
  • WithPre-Tax Deductions: $300.00 Taxable Wage: $2,000 − $300 = $1,700.00 Income Tax (20%): $340.00
  • SavingsIncome Tax Savings This Period: $60.00 Annualized (24 periods): $1,440.00 saved per year
Pre-tax elections saved $60 in income tax this period — $1,440 across a full year.
Example 13.7b
Jordan's taxable wage — applying pre-tax deductions
  • Gross Pay$1,892.00
  • Pre-TaxHealth Insurance: $145.00 401(k) Contribution: $94.60 Total Pre-Tax: $239.60

Section 13.8

Post-Tax Deductions

Post-tax deductions are amounts subtracted from the paycheck after income taxes have already been calculated. They do not reduce taxable income and therefore provide no immediate income tax benefit. Workers still choose post-tax deductions voluntarily for other reasons — or, in the case of garnishments, are required to pay them.

Common Post-Tax DeductionDescription
Roth 401(k) contributionPost-tax retirement savings; qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free (opposite trade-off from traditional 401(k))
Supplemental life insurance premiumCoverage above employer-provided basic level; employee pays the additional premium post-tax
Short- and long-term disability premiums (employee-paid)When paid post-tax, resulting disability benefits are typically tax-free
Wage garnishmentsCourt-ordered withholding to satisfy legal obligations (child support, defaulted loans, civil judgments)
Union duesRequired membership fees in unionized workplaces; typically post-tax
Charitable contributionsVoluntary payroll giving to designated organizations; post-tax
Student Warning — Unrecognized Post-Tax Deductions

Any post-tax deduction you do not recognize should be investigated immediately. Wage garnishments appear without advance notice on a pay stub. Contact your payroll department and, if a garnishment is unexpected, seek legal guidance to understand your options.

Section 13.9

Benefits and Take-Home Pay

Employee benefits are forms of compensation beyond wages. Many benefits are paid entirely by the employer and do not appear as deductions on the employee's pay stub — which means they are invisible to the worker who never looks beyond the stub. Understanding the full value of a compensation package requires looking beyond net pay.

Example 13.9a
Comparing total compensation — two job offers

Student Warning — Benefits Are Not Free

Declining health insurance to preserve take-home pay saves $145 per month ($1,740/year) — but one unexpected hospitalization can cost tens of thousands of dollars. Open enrollment decisions involve real risk, not just arithmetic. Consider both the cost and the risk before declining coverage.

Section 13.10

Reading a Paycheck Carefully

Every skill in this chapter serves one practical goal: the ability to pick up any pay stub and read it completely, verify it accurately, and act on any errors you find. This section synthesizes the chapter into a systematic review process and applies it to a full worked example.

Eight-Step Pay Stub Review Process

Step 1 — Confirm the basics: your name, employee ID, pay period dates, and pay date.

Step 2 — Verify gross pay: hours, hourly rate, overtime at 1.5×, salary per period, any bonuses.

Step 3 — Check federal and state income tax withholding: reasonable given W-4 elections? Updated after life changes?

Step 4 — Verify payroll taxes: Social Security at 6.2%, Medicare at 1.45% of gross. Calculate yourself if anything looks unusual.

Step 5 — Verify pre-tax deductions: match elected amounts for health, dental, retirement, FSA, HSA.

Step 6 — Verify post-tax deductions: recognize and authorize every line.

Step 7 — Calculate net pay yourself: gross minus total deductions. Does it match the stated figure and the deposited amount?

Step 8 — Review YTD figures: do cumulative totals match the number of elapsed pay periods × the current period's amounts?

What to Do When You Find an Error

Gather documentation: pay stubs, employment agreement, benefits enrollment confirmation, time records. Contact the payroll department promptly and specifically — identify the exact line, the error, and the correct amount. Request written confirmation of when and how the correction will appear. Review the corrected paycheck when it arrives to verify the fix. If the error involved taxes — particularly if it persisted over multiple periods — ask how corrections will be handled at year-end.

Chapter 13 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 13.1Taxes appear on paychecks because employers are legally required collection agents for federal and state governments. Withholding is an advance payment against year-end tax liability — not a punishment. The five deduction categories are federal income tax, state income tax, payroll taxes, pre-tax deductions, and post-tax deductions.
  • Sec 13.2Gross pay is total compensation before deductions. Net pay is what the worker receives. Hourly gross pay: rate × hours, plus overtime at 1.5× for hours over 40 per week. Salaried gross pay: annual salary ÷ pay periods per year. Always budget from net pay, not gross.
  • Sec 13.3A pay stub documents the journey from gross to net. It shows earnings, each deduction line, and year-to-date totals. Review every pay period to catch errors before they compound. YTD figures allow verification against the number of elapsed pay periods.
  • Sec 13.4Income tax withholding is an advance payment toward annual tax liability — not the tax itself. Employers use Form W-4 and IRS tables to calculate withholding. Over-withholding produces a refund; under-withholding produces a balance due. Workers can update Form W-4 at any time.
  • Sec 13.5Payroll taxes fund Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%). Both employee and employer pay equal shares. Self-employed workers pay both sides (15.3%) but may deduct the employer-equivalent half. Payroll taxes are flat-rate, unlike graduated income taxes.
  • Sec 13.6Social Security provides retirement, disability, and survivors' benefits based on lifetime earnings. Medicare provides health coverage primarily for those 65 and older. The 1.45% payroll tax funds Part A (Hospital Insurance). Social Security alone is insufficient for retirement — the three-legged stool also requires employer plans and personal savings.
  • Sec 13.7Pre-tax deductions reduce taxable income and lower income tax withholding. Common examples: health insurance premiums, 401(k) contributions, FSA and HSA contributions. Taxable Wage = Gross Pay − Pre-Tax Deductions. Income taxes are calculated on taxable wage, not gross pay.
  • Sec 13.8Post-tax deductions come out after income taxes are calculated. They reduce take-home pay but not taxable income. Examples: Roth 401(k), supplemental life insurance, wage garnishments, union dues. Any unrecognized post-tax deduction should be investigated with payroll immediately.
  • Sec 13.9Many employer benefits are invisible on the pay stub but represent real compensation: employer health insurance contributions, 401(k) matching, paid disability insurance. Total compensation = wages + value of all benefits. Job comparisons require looking beyond salary.
  • Sec 13.10A systematic eight-step pay stub review verifies identity information, gross pay, tax withholding, payroll taxes, pre-tax deductions, post-tax deductions, net pay, and YTD totals. Errors must be reported to payroll promptly with documentation, and corrections must be verified when they appear.

Key Terms Review

gross pay
Total compensation earned in a pay period before any taxes or deductions are subtracted.
net pay
The amount received after all taxes and deductions are subtracted from gross pay; the number that drives financial planning.
income tax withholding
An advance payment of income taxes deducted from each paycheck and sent to the government; reconciled at tax filing.
payroll taxes
Taxes specifically funding Social Security and Medicare, calculated as fixed percentages of wages shared equally between employee and employer.
Social Security tax
A payroll tax of 6.2% of wages (employee share) up to the annual wage base, funding retirement, disability, and survivors' benefits.
Medicare tax
A payroll tax of 1.45% of all wages (employee share, no cap), funding Part A hospital insurance for those 65 and older.
pre-tax deduction
A deduction subtracted before income taxes are calculated; reduces taxable income and lowers income tax withholding.
post-tax deduction
A deduction subtracted after income taxes are calculated; reduces take-home pay but not taxable income.
taxable wage
Gross pay minus all pre-tax deductions; the amount on which income tax withholding is calculated.
pay stub
The document showing earnings, all deductions, net pay, and year-to-date totals for each pay period.
Form W-4
The Employee's Withholding Certificate; used by employers to calculate how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck.
overtime pay
Compensation at a minimum of 1.5× the regular hourly rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek, as required by federal law.
year to date (YTD)
Cumulative totals from the first pay period of the calendar year through the current period.
refund
Money returned to a taxpayer when annual withholding exceeded actual tax liability; indicates over-withholding during the year.
balance due
The amount owed when annual tax liability exceeded withholding; indicates under-withholding during the year.
total compensation
The full value of a worker's pay package: wages plus the value of all employer-provided benefits.
employer matching
Employer contributions to a retirement account that match, in whole or in part, the employee's own contributions.
wage garnishment
A court-ordered post-tax deduction from wages to satisfy a legal obligation such as child support or a civil judgment.
401(k) / 403(b)
Employer-sponsored retirement savings accounts; traditional contributions are pre-tax, reducing taxable income in the contribution year.
Section 125 Cafeteria Plan
A tax code provision allowing certain employee benefit premiums to be paid on a pre-tax basis through payroll deductions.

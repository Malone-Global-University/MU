Chapter 22 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Housing is one of the largest financial decisions most people make. Whether renting an apartment or buying a home, the choice shapes a household's budget, mobility, savings, risk exposure, and long-term financial health for years at a time. Unlike most purchases, housing decisions involve contracts, legal obligations, large upfront costs, ongoing expenses, and market forces that are often difficult to predict.

This chapter introduces students to housing as a financial decision. Students will learn how renting works, what leases and security deposits involve, how mortgages function, what homeownership actually costs beyond the monthly payment, how to measure housing affordability, and how to compare total housing costs honestly. The chapter treats both renting and owning as legitimate choices with different tradeoffs depending on income, savings, mobility, market conditions, and personal goals.

A central theme throughout this chapter is the difference between what housing appears to cost and what housing actually costs. A rent payment is not the total cost of renting. A mortgage payment is not the total cost of owning. Understanding the full picture — upfront costs, ongoing expenses, opportunity costs, and risks — is how students learn to make housing decisions with open eyes.

Opening Question

A friend tells you that renting is "throwing money away" and that buying a home is always the smarter financial choice. Is this true? What costs does a homeowner pay that a renter does not? What advantages does renting offer that ownership does not? By the end of this chapter, you will be able to calculate the true total monthly cost of both renting and owning — and make a reasoned comparison rather than relying on a slogan.

Section 22.1

Why Housing Decisions Matter

Housing is not like other expenses. It is the largest single category in most household budgets. According to consistent data from government surveys, housing typically consumes between 25 and 35 percent of household income for most families — and significantly more for lower-income households and those living in high-cost cities.

But housing is also different from other expenses in a deeper way: housing decisions are rarely reversible in the short term. When you rent an apartment, you sign a lease that legally commits you for months or years. When you buy a home, you take on a mortgage that may span 15 or 30 years, along with legal ownership of a physical asset that changes in value, requires maintenance, and cannot quickly be converted to cash in an emergency.

How Housing Connects to Everything Else

Housing decisions interact with nearly every other part of a financial plan. Where you live affects:

  • Commuting costs and transportation expenses
  • Access to schools, employment, and services
  • Income tax obligations — property taxes, mortgage interest in certain circumstances
  • Insurance needs — renters insurance versus homeowners insurance
  • Savings capacity — housing costs either leave more or less room for saving
  • Credit — mortgage applications depend heavily on credit scores and debt-to-income ratios
  • Emergency resilience — a housing payment that stretches a budget leaves little room for unexpected costs
The Rent vs. Own Question Is Not Simple

Many students have heard that buying a home is always better than renting, or that renting is "throwing money away." Neither statement is reliably true. Both renting and owning involve costs. The right choice depends on how long a person expects to stay in one location, whether they have savings for upfront costs, local housing market conditions, income stability, and personal goals. This chapter gives students the tools to analyze both options honestly.

Decision Spine — Housing

Throughout this chapter, apply these questions to every housing scenario:

  1. What is the goal? Stable housing, flexibility to move, building equity, minimizing monthly cost?
  2. What are the short-term and long-term costs?
  3. What are the risks?
  4. What happens if income changes or an emergency occurs?
  5. What information is missing?
Section 22.2

Renting

Renting means paying for the right to occupy a property — usually an apartment, house, or other dwelling — for a defined period of time in exchange for regular payments called rent. The person who rents is called a tenant or renter. The person or organization that owns the property is called a landlord or property owner.

Renting does not build ownership. When a tenant pays rent, those payments compensate the landlord for the use of the property, the landlord's costs of ownership (including mortgage, property taxes, insurance, and maintenance), and in many cases a profit. The tenant gains housing but not equity.

Advantages of RentingDisadvantages of Renting
Flexibility — can move at end of lease without selling a property or paying real estate transaction costsNo equity accumulation — rent payments do not build ownership value
Limited maintenance responsibility — landlord is typically responsible for major repairs and structural maintenanceLess control over the space — tenants generally cannot make major modifications without permission
Lower upfront cost — security deposit plus first month's rent is far less than a home down paymentInstability — landlords can decline to renew leases; rent can increase significantly at renewal
No exposure to home price declines — if property values fall, the renter is unaffectedNo long-term price lock — unlike a fixed-rate mortgage, rent can rise over time with the market
Worked Example 22.2.1
Estimating true monthly rental cost
  • SituationRaquel is looking at an apartment listed at $975 per month. The lease states that water, sewer, and trash are included. Electric and internet are not included.
  • Estimate Rent: $975 Electric: $ 60 (based on similar units) Internet: $ 55 Renters insurance: $ 18 (estimated)
  • CalculateTotal monthly cost = $975 + $60 + $55 + $18 = $1,108
  • InterpretThe advertised price is $975. The realistic monthly cost is $1,108 — a $133 difference. This gap matters when comparing apartments or evaluating whether a rental fits the budget.
True monthly cost: $1,108 — not $975
Section 22.3

Leases and Security Deposits

A lease is a legally binding contract between a tenant and a landlord that defines the terms of a rental arrangement. Before signing a lease, tenants should read it carefully. A lease is a legal document, and tenants are bound by its terms once signed.

Lease ElementWhat It Covers
Lease termLength of the agreement — commonly 12 months, 6 months, or month-to-month
Rent amount and due dateThe agreed monthly rent and when it is due each month, often with a grace period
Late feesA penalty charged when rent is not paid by the due date — flat fee or percentage of rent
Security depositUpfront payment held by landlord to cover unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear and tear
Pet policyWhether pets are allowed, what types and sizes, and any pet deposit or monthly pet fee
Maintenance and repairsWho is responsible for what types of repairs — typically landlord maintains structure, plumbing, heating, electrical
Early terminationPenalties or conditions if tenant needs to leave before the lease ends
Renewal termsWhether the lease automatically renews and under what conditions
Notice requirementsHow much advance notice tenant must give before moving out — commonly 30 or 60 days
Critical Warning — Read the Lease

A lease is a contract. Not reading it is not a defense if problems arise. Students should read every lease before signing and ask questions about anything they do not understand. An early termination clause can mean owing months of rent even after moving out.

Security Deposits

A security deposit is a sum of money — typically equal to one or two months' rent — paid by the tenant to the landlord before moving in. The landlord holds this money during the tenancy and returns it when the tenant moves out, minus any legitimate deductions.

Landlords Can DeductLandlords Generally Cannot Deduct
Unpaid rentNormal wear and tear (minor scuffs, carpet wear from regular use)
Damage beyond normal wear and tear (holes in walls, broken fixtures, stains)Repairs that were the landlord's responsibility to begin with
Cleaning costs if unit is left significantly dirtier than receivedCosts not supported by proper documentation
Security Deposit Best Practices

At move-in: Walk through the unit and complete a written checklist noting all existing damage. Take dated photographs. Keep a copy.

At move-out: Repeat the walkthrough with the landlord if possible. Take dated photographs of every room.

Know your state's laws: Many states specify maximum deposit amounts, return deadlines (often 14–30 days), required documentation for deductions, and penalties for wrongful withholding.

Worked Example 22.3.1
Security deposit documentation saves money
  • SituationMarcus moved in with a $1,000 security deposit. When he moved out, the landlord returned $640 and kept $360 for "cleaning and repairs."
  • ResearchMarcus had kept his move-in photos. He found: (1) a cracked bathroom tile that was present at move-in — documented in his photos; (2) a small wall scuff from furniture — normal wear and tear; (3) a broken kitchen cabinet door he accidentally broke — legitimate damage.
  • ActionMarcus checked his state's security deposit law: landlords must provide itemized written receipts for deductions within 21 days. He had received no itemized statement. He sent a written request and received a revised refund of $840, with $160 for the cabinet repair only.
  • LessonMove-in documentation and knowledge of tenant rights made a $200 difference.
Documentation + tenant rights knowledge = $200 recovered

Older Buildings and Energy Costs

Older apartments with poor insulation or older appliances may have significantly higher utility costs than newer units. A lower rent on an old apartment may not be a better deal after utilities. Always ask about average utility bills before signing a lease.

Renters Insurance

Renters insurance is a policy that protects a tenant's personal belongings and provides liability coverage. It does not cover the building itself — that is the landlord's responsibility.

Renters Insurance CoversRenters Insurance Does Not Cover
Personal property damaged or destroyed by fire, theft, vandalism, or certain weather eventsThe building or structure (that is the landlord's coverage)
Liability if a guest is injured in your apartment or you accidentally damage someone else's propertyFloods (requires separate flood insurance in most cases)
Additional living expenses if your unit becomes uninhabitableEarthquakes (requires separate coverage in most cases)
 High-value items like jewelry or instruments may need additional coverage
Renters Insurance Cost vs. Protection

Renters insurance typically costs $15–$30 per month for a standard policy. This is one of the best value insurance products available. A fire, flood, or theft that destroys $5,000–$10,000 in belongings would be financially devastating for most households. Many landlords now require renters insurance as a lease condition.

Section 22.5

Homeownership

Homeownership means legally owning the property in which you live. Owners have rights that renters do not: the ability to modify their home, freedom from a landlord's decisions, and the potential to build equity — ownership value — as the home is paid off and if its market value increases.

Homeownership is a major life and financial milestone for many people. It can build long-term wealth, provide stability, and create permanence. But it also involves substantial costs, risks, and responsibilities that renters do not face.

How Equity Builds

Equity Building Example
How homeowner equity grows over time
  • At PurchaseA household buys a home for $250,000 with a $200,000 mortgage and $50,000 down payment. Initial equity = $250,000 − $200,000 = $50,000
  • Several Years LaterAfter regular payments, the loan balance has fallen to $185,000. The home's market value is now $280,000. Equity = $280,000 − $185,000 = $95,000
  • The Two SourcesEquity grew because (1) mortgage payments reduced the loan balance from $200,000 to $185,000, and (2) the home appreciated in value from $250,000 to $280,000.
Equity grew from $50,000 to $95,000 through payments and appreciation
The Underwater Scenario — Home Values Can Fall

If a household buys a $250,000 home with a $200,000 mortgage and the home's value falls to $190,000, they are "underwater" — owing more than the home is worth. This happened to millions of homeowners during the housing market crash of 2007–2009. Home values are not guaranteed to rise.

Homeownership May Be More Appropriate WhenHomeownership May Be Less Appropriate When
Buyer expects to stay at least 5–7 yearsBuyer expects to relocate within a few years
Buyer has stable income and emergency savingsBuyer's income is unstable or insufficient
Local market is reasonably priced relative to rentingDown payment would deplete savings entirely
Savings cover down payment, closing costs, AND emergency fundLocal market is significantly overpriced relative to renting
Section 22.6

Mortgages

Most people who buy a home borrow most of the purchase price from a lender — typically a bank, credit union, or mortgage company — and repay that loan over time. This type of loan is called a mortgage. A mortgage is a secured loan, meaning the home itself serves as collateral. If the borrower fails to make payments, the lender has the legal right to take the home through a process called foreclosure.

Key Mortgage Terms

Principal — The amount borrowed: home price minus the down payment.

Interest rate — The annual cost of borrowing, expressed as a percentage of the remaining balance.

Loan term — The repayment period; most common are 30 years and 15 years.

Amortization — The gradual payoff of the loan through regular payments that cover both principal and interest.

Foreclosure — The lender's legal remedy when a borrower fails to make required payments.

How Interest Rate and Loan Term Affect Monthly Payment

Three Key Relationships

Higher interest rate → higher monthly payment and much higher total interest paid over the life of the loan.

Shorter loan term → higher monthly payment, but far less total interest paid and faster equity building.

Larger loan amount → higher monthly payment. The monthly figures above cover only principal and interest — not taxes, insurance, or maintenance.

Worked Example 22.6.1
Total interest on a 30-year mortgage
  • SituationA household takes out a $220,000 mortgage at 6.5% for 30 years. Monthly P+I payment: approximately $1,390.
  • Calculate Total payments: $1,390 × 360 months = $500,400 Total interest: $500,400 − $220,000 = $280,400
  • CompareOn a 15-year mortgage at 6.0%, monthly payment ≈ $1,858 (higher), but total interest ≈ $114,400 — less than half the 30-year total interest.
  • InterpretThe household pays $280,400 in interest on top of the $220,000 borrowed. Choosing the 15-year loan costs more each month but saves approximately $166,000 in interest over the life of the loan.
30-year total interest: $280,400  |  15-year total interest: ~$114,400

Down Payments and Closing Costs

Down Payments

The down payment is the portion of the home's purchase price that the buyer pays upfront from their own savings. The mortgage covers the remainder.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

When a buyer makes a down payment of less than 20%, most conventional lenders require private mortgage insurance (PMI). PMI protects the lender — not the buyer — in case of default. PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount per year. On a $240,000 mortgage at 1%, PMI adds $200 per month. PMI payments continue until the borrower has accumulated 20% equity.

Closing Costs

Closing costs are the fees and expenses paid to complete a home purchase, beyond the down payment. They are paid at "closing" — when ownership officially transfers. Closing costs typically range from 2% to 5% of the home's purchase price.

Worked Example 22.7.1
True upfront cost of buying a home
  • SituationElena is buying a home priced at $275,000. She plans a 10% down payment.
  • Calculate Down payment (10% of $275,000): $27,500 Estimated closing costs (3%): $ 8,250 Home inspection fee: $ 450 Moving expenses: $ 1,500 Total estimated upfront cost: $37,700

Common Misconception — Down Payment ≠ Full Upfront Cost

Many first-time buyers save exactly the down payment amount but underestimate closing costs and moving expenses. The true upfront cost of buying a home is typically 2–5% higher than the down payment alone. Saving for closing costs separately is essential.

Section 22.8

Property Taxes and Maintenance

Property Taxes

Property taxes are annual taxes charged by local governments on the assessed value of real property. They fund schools, roads, emergency services, parks, and local government operations. Property taxes are calculated as a percentage of the home's assessed value.

Property Tax Calculation
Annual and monthly property tax from assessed value and tax rate
  • GivenHome assessed value: $260,000  |  Annual tax rate: 1.2%
  • Annual Tax$260,000 × 0.012 = $3,120 per year
  • Monthly$3,120 ÷ 12 = $260 per month
Annual tax: $3,120  |  Monthly equivalent: $260
Escrow Accounts

Many lenders collect property taxes and homeowners insurance as part of the monthly mortgage payment, depositing them into an escrow account. The lender pays the tax bill and insurance premium on the homeowner's behalf when due. This prevents large lump-sum surprise bills — but it does mean the true monthly housing payment is higher than principal and interest alone.

Maintenance and Repairs

Unlike renters, homeowners bear full financial responsibility for maintaining and repairing their property. These are not if costs — they are when costs. Every home requires ongoing maintenance and eventual replacement of major systems.

ItemTypical Cost Range
Roof replacement$8,000–$20,000+
HVAC system replacement$5,000–$15,000
Water heater replacement$700–$2,000
Plumbing repairs$500–$5,000+
Electrical repairs$500–$3,000+
Foundation repairs$5,000–$50,000+
The 1–2% Maintenance Rule

A commonly used guideline is to budget 1%–2% of the home's value per year for maintenance and repairs. For a $275,000 home, this means budgeting $2,750–$5,500 per year — roughly $230–$460 per month — just for ongoing maintenance. This is a rough estimate; older homes may cost more.

Worked Example 22.8.1
True monthly cost of homeownership beyond the mortgage payment
  • SituationAndres has a mortgage payment of $1,420 per month (P+I) on a $240,000 loan. His home is worth $280,000 in a county with a 1.1% property tax rate. Homeowners insurance is $1,500 per year. He budgets 1.5% of home value for maintenance.
  • Calculate Principal + interest: $1,420 Property taxes ($280,000 × 1.1% ÷ 12): $ 257 Homeowners insurance ($1,500 ÷ 12): $ 125 Maintenance budget (1.5% × $280,000 ÷ 12): $ 350 Total monthly housing cost: $2,152
  • InterpretThe advertised mortgage payment is $1,420. The realistic total monthly cost is $2,152 — approximately 52% higher.
True monthly cost: $2,152 — not $1,420

Section 22.9

Housing Affordability

Housing affordability is the relationship between housing costs and household income. Understanding affordability is essential before committing to either a rent payment or a mortgage.

The 28/36 Rule

28% — Total housing costs (PITI: principal, interest, taxes, insurance) should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income.

36% — Total debt payments (housing plus all other debt: auto, student loans, credit cards) should not exceed 36% of gross monthly income.

These are guidelines used widely in mortgage lending, not absolute limits. A household with variable income or no savings may find even these percentages stressful.

Worked Example 22.9.1
Applying the 28% guideline to find an affordable loan size
  • GivenRosa earns $58,000 per year gross.
  • Step 1Gross monthly income = $58,000 ÷ 12 = $4,833
  • Step 228% of $4,833 = $1,353 maximum monthly PITI
  • Step 3If property taxes and insurance add $430 per month: $1,353 − $430 = $923 available for principal + interest
  • InterpretAt 7% for 30 years, a $923 monthly P+I payment corresponds to a loan of approximately $139,000. This tells Rosa the maximum affordable loan given her income and local tax/insurance costs.
Maximum affordable mortgage at these conditions: ~$139,000

Cost-Burdened Households

Households spending more than 30% of gross income on housing are described as cost-burdened. Households spending more than 50% are severely cost-burdened. These situations leave very little room for saving, debt repayment, or absorbing unexpected expenses.

What Affordability Ratios Do Not Tell You

Affordability ratios are a starting point, not a complete picture. They do not account for: emergency fund status, retirement savings goals, income stability, other large financial obligations, or local cost of living beyond housing. A household within the 28% guideline but with no emergency savings is still financially fragile.

Section 22.10

Total Housing Cost and Tradeoffs

The most important skill in housing decision-making is calculating and comparing total housing cost — not just the advertised rent or mortgage payment.

Full Monthly Cost Comparison: Renting vs. Owning

Renter's Monthly CostsAmountHomeowner's Monthly CostsAmount
Rent$1,400Mortgage principal + interest$1,390
Electric$75Property taxes$280
Internet$55Homeowners insurance$140
Renters insurance$20Maintenance budget$350
  PMI (less than 20% down)$130
Total$1,550Total$2,290
What the Comparison Shows

In this example, the homeowner's total monthly housing cost is approximately $740 more than the renter's. Over one year, this is $8,880 more. The homeowner is building equity and may benefit from appreciation, but these are long-term, uncertain gains. The renter keeps $740 per month — which could be saved and invested. This does not mean renting is always better. It means the comparison must be made carefully with real numbers, not assumptions.

The Break-Even Horizon

Buying a home involves large transaction costs when buying (closing costs) and again when selling (real estate agent fees and additional closing costs — often 6–8% of the sale price). These costs must be recouped through equity building and appreciation before homeownership becomes financially advantageous over renting. As a rough guideline, most financial analysts suggest homeownership begins to show financial advantages only after 5 to 7 years of occupancy, and sometimes longer.

The Decision Spine Applied to Housing

Before making any housing decision — whether to rent or buy, which rental to choose, or which home to purchase — work through these eight questions:

  1. What is the goal? Stable housing, flexibility to move, building equity, minimizing monthly cost?
  2. What are the tradeoffs? Renting offers flexibility; owning offers equity but ties up capital and requires maintenance capacity.
  3. What are the short-term and long-term costs? Total monthly cost and total 5-year cost including upfront expenses?
  4. What is the opportunity cost? Down payment money is unavailable for other investments. Is the return on homeownership competitive?
  5. What are the risks? Job loss, income reduction, home value decline, unexpected repairs, inability to sell quickly?
  6. What information is missing? Local property tax rates? Comparable rental costs? Realistic maintenance cost on this specific property?
  7. What happens if income changes or an emergency occurs? Can this household make the mortgage through a job loss? Is the emergency fund preserved after purchase?
  8. What is the safest reasonable choice given current circumstances?
Worked Example 22.10.1
Complete housing decision analysis
  • ProfileCamille is 24, earns $52,000/year, has $18,000 in savings. She is considering whether to rent or buy in her city.
  • Rental OptionA 1-bedroom apartment for $1,100/month. With utilities and renters insurance, total monthly cost: approximately $1,250.
  • Purchase OptionA condo listed at $215,000. With 5% down ($10,750) and estimated closing costs of $5,500, upfront cost = $16,250 — nearly all her savings. Remaining savings: ~$1,750 (far below a recommended emergency fund). Mortgage (7%, 30yr, $204,250): ~$1,359 Property taxes: $195 Homeowners insurance: $110 Maintenance: $270 PMI: $143 Total monthly ownership: $2,077
  • Affordability Check$52,000 ÷ 12 × 0.28 = $1,213 guideline maximum $2,077 actual ownership cost — significantly exceeds guideline
  • AnalysisBuying would deplete Camille's emergency fund, push her well above the affordability guideline, and leave her financially fragile. Continuing to rent, building savings aggressively, and revisiting in 2–3 years when income and savings have grown is likely the sounder choice.
Recommendation: Continue renting and build savings — readiness, not preference, drives this analysis

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 22.1Housing is the largest single budget category for most households and affects transportation, taxes, insurance, savings, credit, and financial resilience. Both renting and owning involve real costs; the right choice depends on income, savings, mobility, and market conditions — not on slogans.
  • Sec 22.2Renting provides flexibility, lower upfront cost, and limited maintenance responsibility but does not build equity. The true monthly cost of renting includes rent plus utilities and renters insurance — not just the advertised price.

Security deposits are refundable minus legitimate deductions — tenants should document their unit carefully at move-in and know their state's laws.
  • Sec 22.4Utilities (electricity, gas, water, internet) and renters insurance must be added to rent to calculate true monthly housing cost. Renters insurance typically costs $15–$30 per month and provides essential protection for personal belongings and liability.
  • Sec 22.5Homeownership can build equity through loan paydown and appreciation, but home values can also fall. Homeownership is more appropriate when buyers have stable income, adequate savings for purchase and emergencies, and plan to stay at least 5–7 years.
  • Sec 22.6A mortgage is a secured loan for real estate. Interest rate, loan amount, and loan term together determine the monthly payment and total interest paid. A 30-year loan has lower monthly payments but far higher total interest than a 15-year loan on the same amount.
  • Sec 22.7The upfront cost of buying a home includes the down payment (typically 3%–20% of the purchase price) plus closing costs (2%–5%), inspection fees, and moving expenses. Down payments below 20% require PMI, adding to monthly costs until 20% equity is reached.
  • Sec 22.8Ongoing homeownership costs beyond the mortgage include property taxes, homeowners insurance, and a maintenance budget of 1%–2% of home value per year. The true monthly housing cost is typically 30%–60% higher than the mortgage payment alone.
  • Sec 22.9The 28/36 rule provides a starting affordability benchmark: total housing costs should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income, and total debt payments should not exceed 36%. Households above 30% for rent are considered cost-burdened.
  • Sec 22.10Total housing cost — not the advertised payment — is the correct basis for comparison. Homeownership typically becomes financially advantageous over comparable renting only after 5–7 years due to transaction costs. The decision spine provides a systematic framework for making housing decisions given real financial circumstances.

Key Terms Review

amortization
The gradual repayment of a loan through scheduled payments covering both principal and interest; early payments are mostly interest, later payments mostly principal.
assessed value
The value assigned to a property by a local government for calculating property taxes; may differ from market value.
closing costs
Fees and expenses paid at the completion of a real estate transaction, beyond the down payment; typically 2–5% of the purchase price.
cost-burdened
A household spending more than 30% of gross income on housing. Households above 50% are severely cost-burdened.
down payment
The portion of a home's purchase price paid upfront by the buyer from their own savings; the remainder is financed through a mortgage.
equity
The portion of a home's value that the owner actually owns: market value minus any outstanding loan balance. Builds through payments and appreciation.
escrow account
An account held by a mortgage lender that collects monthly funds for property taxes and homeowners insurance and pays those bills on the homeowner's behalf.
fixed-rate mortgage
A mortgage with an interest rate that does not change for the life of the loan; provides payment certainty and eliminates interest rate risk.
foreclosure
The legal process by which a lender takes ownership of a mortgaged property when the borrower fails to make required payments.
homeowners insurance
An insurance policy covering the structure of a home and its contents against damage, and providing liability coverage; required by mortgage lenders.
landlord
A property owner who rents that property to tenants in exchange for rent payments.
lease
A legally binding contract between a landlord and tenant specifying the terms of the rental arrangement, including rent, term, and responsibilities.
mortgage
A secured loan used to finance the purchase of real estate, with the property serving as collateral; failure to pay can result in foreclosure.
PMI
Private Mortgage Insurance — required when a buyer's down payment is less than 20%; protects the lender (not the buyer) in case of default.
principal
The original amount borrowed on a loan, before interest; reduces with each payment.
property taxes
Annual taxes charged by local governments based on a property's assessed value; fund schools, roads, emergency services, and other public services.
renters insurance
An insurance policy protecting a tenant's personal belongings and providing liability coverage; does not cover the building itself.
security deposit
A sum paid by the tenant before move-in, held by the landlord to cover unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear and tear; must be returned with documentation of deductions.
tenant
A person who pays rent to occupy a property owned by another party.
total housing cost
All costs of occupying a home: rent or mortgage, property taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance — not just the advertised monthly payment.
underwater
When a homeowner owes more on their mortgage than the home is currently worth; also called "negative equity."
28/36 rule
A mortgage lending guideline: total housing costs ≤ 28% of gross monthly income; total debt payments ≤ 36% of gross monthly income.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1 — "Renting is throwing money away."

Rent pays for housing — a real good with real value. Mortgage payments in the early years also consist largely of interest, not equity building. Both renting and owning involve money that does not accumulate as personal savings. The comparison should be based on total costs, flexibility, risks, and financial readiness — not on the idea that one form of payment is wasted and another is not.

Misconception 2 — "My mortgage payment is my total housing cost."

Property taxes, homeowners insurance, maintenance, and possibly PMI add substantially to the true monthly cost of homeownership. Calculating only the mortgage payment is a systematic underestimate — often by 30%–60% or more.

Misconception 3 — "I should buy as much house as the lender will approve."

Lender approval is based on maximum qualifying ratios, not on what is financially comfortable or wise for a specific household's goals, savings, or income stability. Borrowing the maximum the lender allows is rarely the right financial choice.

Misconception 4 — "Homeowners insurance and renters insurance cover the same things."

They are different products. Homeowners insurance covers the building structure and its contents. Renters insurance covers only the tenant's belongings and liability — not the building. A renter whose landlord has insurance is not personally protected by that policy.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 23: Transportation, Vehicles, and Ownership Costs

Chapter 22 examined housing — the largest category of household spending for most families. Chapter 23 turns to transportation, which is typically the second-largest expense category for households. Like housing, transportation decisions involve both obvious costs — the purchase price or monthly payment — and hidden costs: fuel, insurance, maintenance, repairs, registration fees, and depreciation. Like housing, transportation decisions also involve tradeoffs between ownership and access: owning a vehicle versus using public transportation, rideshare services, or other alternatives. Chapter 23 will introduce the concept of total cost of ownership for vehicles and apply the same decision-making framework used here to a different major life expense. Students who understand both housing costs and vehicle costs will have a clear picture of the two expenses that most shape a household's financial flexibility and savings capacity.

Chapter 18 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Chapter 17 introduced investing as putting money to work for future growth or income. It defined assets, explained the difference between saving and investing, explored risk and return, and introduced the idea that time horizon shapes investment decisions. Chapter 18 builds on that foundation by examining the major categories of investment vehicles most households actually encounter: stocks, bonds, mutual funds, exchange-traded funds, and index funds.

Students will learn what each type of investment is, what it represents legally and economically, how it earns money, what risks it carries, and how combining different investment types through diversification reduces overall portfolio risk without eliminating all risk. By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain the difference between owning stock and owning a bond, understand why mutual funds and ETFs exist, read a basic investment account statement, compare expense ratios, understand what diversification means and why it matters, and apply these concepts to beginner investment scenarios.

Opening Question

Imagine you have saved $2,000 and you want to begin investing. A friend tells you to put all of it into one company's stock because the company "is definitely going to grow." Another friend tells you to spread it across dozens of companies and bonds using a single mutual fund. A third friend says to just find an index fund with low fees and leave it alone. Who is right? What would you need to know to decide? By the end of this chapter, you will have the vocabulary and framework to think through that question seriously.

Section 18.1

Why Investment Types Differ

Not all investments work the same way. The differences among stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs are not cosmetic. They reflect different legal relationships, different sources of return, different levels of risk, and different roles in a portfolio.

When you invest, you are doing one of two fundamental things: owning something or lending something. These two activities generate different kinds of returns and expose you to different kinds of risk.

Investment ActivityWhat It MeansPrimary ExampleReturn Source
OwnershipYou hold a stake in an asset's value. You gain if it rises; you lose if it falls.StockPrice appreciation; dividends
LendingYou give money to a borrower who promises to repay principal plus interest over a defined period.BondInterest payments; return of principal
Fund (pooled)A container holding stocks, bonds, or other assets on behalf of many investors. Not a separate economic category.Mutual fund; ETFDepends on what the fund holds
Key Distinction

Funds — mutual funds, ETFs, and index funds — are containers. They hold stocks, bonds, or other assets on behalf of a group of investors. Understanding what the fund holds is what matters. A stock fund behaves like a stock. A bond fund behaves like a bond. A balanced fund combines both.

Section 18.2

Stocks

What a Stock Is

A stock is a unit of ownership in a corporation. When a company wants to raise money, it can sell pieces of itself to the public. Each piece is called a share. A person who buys shares becomes a shareholder and owns a small fraction of the company.

The technical term for this kind of ownership is equity. Stocks are sometimes called equities for this reason. If a company has issued 1,000,000 shares and you own 1,000 of them, you own 0.1% of that company — a proportional claim on the company's profits, its assets if it is ever dissolved, and sometimes a vote in major company decisions.

How Stocks Generate Returns

Return TypeHow It WorksWhen You Receive It
Price appreciationThe share price rises as the company grows in value. Buying at $20 and selling at $35 produces a $15 per share capital gain.When shares are sold
DividendsSome companies distribute a portion of profits to shareholders on a regular schedule, usually quarterly.On the dividend payment date; automatically deposited
Not All Stocks Pay Dividends

Companies that are growing rapidly often reinvest all profits rather than paying dividends. Many well-known technology companies paid no dividends for years while their share prices rose significantly. Dividend payments are not guaranteed and can be reduced or eliminated at any time.

Risks of Stock Ownership

Market risk is the possibility that stock prices fall across the market due to economic conditions, investor sentiment, or external events that have nothing to do with a particular company's performance. Company risk is the possibility that a single company fails — shareholders are among the last to be paid back in a bankruptcy and often receive nothing. Volatility refers to the wide swings in stock prices over time. A stock can rise 30% in a year and fall 40% the next, and both movements can happen for reasons that are difficult to predict.

Example 18.2.1
Reading Basic Stock Information
  • ScenarioA student's brokerage account shows: NoraCo Industries — 20 shares owned, purchased at $40/share, current price $48.50/share.
  • QuestionWhat is the total original cost? What is the unrealized capital gain per share and in total?

Section 18.3

Bonds

What a Bond Is

A bond is a lending instrument. When you buy a bond, you are lending money to the bond's issuer — which may be a corporation, the federal government, a state or local government, or another institution. In return, the issuer promises to pay you interest on a regular schedule and return the original amount at a specified future date.

Bond TermDefinitionExample
Face value (par value)The amount the issuer will repay at maturity. Bonds typically have face values of $1,000.$1,000
Coupon rateThe annual interest rate paid on the face value.4% coupon on $1,000 = $40/year
Maturity dateThe date when the principal is returned to the bondholder.10 years from issue date
YieldThe return an investor earns based on what they paid and the interest received.May differ from coupon rate if purchased at a discount or premium
IssuerThe borrower — a corporation, the U.S. Treasury, a municipality, or another entity.U.S. government; Apple Inc.; City of Chicago

Common Types of Bonds

Bond TypeIssued ByRisk LevelTax Notes
Government bondsU.S. federal government (Treasuries)Lowest — backed by the federal governmentSubject to federal tax; exempt from state/local
Municipal bondsState and local governmentsLow to moderateOften exempt from federal income tax
Corporate bondsCompaniesModerate to high — companies can defaultFully taxable; higher yields reflect higher risk
Bond Prices and Interest Rates Move in Opposite Directions

When market interest rates rise, existing bonds with lower coupon rates become less attractive — their prices fall. When interest rates fall, existing bonds become more attractive and their prices rise. This inverse relationship between bond prices and interest rates is one of the most important concepts in fixed-income investing.

Example 18.3.1
Bond Interest Calculation
  • ScenarioA student buys a $1,000 corporate bond with a 5% annual coupon rate and a maturity date 10 years away.
  • Solve Annual interest: $1,000 × 5% = $50.00 per year Total interest (10 yrs): $50 × 10 = $500.00 Principal at maturity: $1,000.00 Total received: $500 + $1,000 = $1,500.00
  • CheckThe investor lends $1,000, receives $50/year for 10 years, and gets the $1,000 back at the end. Total inflows of $1,500 on a $1,000 investment.
Annual interest: $50.00  |  Total interest: $500.00  |  Total return at maturity: $1,500.00
Credit Ratings

Rating agencies such as Moody's, S&P, and Fitch assess the creditworthiness of bond issuers. Bonds rated AAA to BBB are generally considered investment grade — relatively low default risk. Bonds rated below BBB are called high-yield bonds (informally "junk bonds") — they offer higher interest rates to compensate investors for higher default risk.

Section 18.4

Mutual Funds

What a Mutual Fund Is

A mutual fund is a pooled investment vehicle. Many investors contribute money to the fund, and the fund uses those combined assets to purchase a portfolio of securities — stocks, bonds, or a mixture of both — according to a stated investment strategy. When you invest in a mutual fund, you own shares of the fund, not shares of the individual securities the fund holds.

Mutual funds exist because individual investors face two significant challenges: buying a truly diversified portfolio of individual stocks requires substantial capital and many transactions, and researching individual companies takes time, expertise, and information access that most individuals do not have. Funds solve both problems by pooling investor money and delegating investment decisions to professional managers.

Net Asset Value (NAV)

NAV Formula

NAV = (Total Fund Assets − Liabilities) ÷ Number of Shares Outstanding

Mutual fund shares are priced once per day, after markets close, at the NAV. If a fund holds $10,000,000 in assets, has $100,000 in liabilities, and 500,000 shares outstanding: NAV = $9,900,000 ÷ 500,000 = $19.80 per share. When you buy mutual fund shares, you pay the NAV calculated at the end of that trading day — you do not know the exact price when you place your order.

Actively vs. Passively Managed

Actively managed funds employ portfolio managers who select investments and attempt to outperform a market benchmark. They tend to charge higher fees. Passively managed funds simply replicate the holdings of a specific market index without trying to beat it, and typically charge much lower fees. Research consistently shows that most actively managed funds fail to outperform their benchmark indexes over long periods, especially after fees.

Section 18.5

Exchange-Traded Funds

An exchange-traded fund (ETF) is similar to a mutual fund in that it holds a collection of securities. The key difference is how shares are bought and sold: ETF shares trade on stock exchanges throughout the trading day, just like shares of individual companies. You can buy or sell an ETF at any moment during market hours at the current market price.

FeatureMutual FundETF
TradingOnce per day, at NAV after market closeThroughout the trading day on exchanges
Minimum investmentOften set minimums (e.g., $1,000)Price of one share (or fractional shares)
Expense ratiosOften higher for active fundsOften very low, especially index ETFs
Tax efficiencyCan distribute capital gains to investorsGenerally more tax-efficient
Management styleActive or passiveMostly passive (index-tracking)
Example 18.5.1
Comparing Three Approaches to a $1,000 Investment
  • ScenarioThree students each have $1,000 to invest. Student A buys shares of one technology company. Student B buys a mutual fund holding 200 companies. Student C buys an ETF tracking an index of 3,000+ companies.
  • RiskStudent A has the highest company-specific and sector-specific risk — all $1,000 is concentrated in one company. If that company fails, Student A loses everything. Students B and C have spread risk across many companies.
  • DiversificationStudent C's ETF covers the broadest range of companies across many sectors. If the technology sector declines, Students B and C are partially insulated because their funds hold companies across healthcare, consumer goods, financial services, energy, and more.
  • ConclusionThis comparison does not mean Student A is wrong — it depends on goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. But it illustrates why funds were created: to provide diversification that a single-stock investment cannot.
Section 18.6

Index Funds

What a Market Index Is

A market index is a statistical measure that tracks the performance of a defined group of securities, constructed using specific rules about which securities to include and how to weight them. An index fund is a mutual fund or ETF designed to replicate the holdings and performance of a specific market index. The fund buys the same securities in approximately the same proportions as the index, with no fund manager making judgment calls about which companies to favor.

Example 18.6.1
The Long-Term Cost of Expense Ratios
  • ScenarioTwo investors each contribute $5,000 to start and add $200 per month for 30 years. Both earn a gross annual return of 7% before fees. Fund A charges 1.00%/year. Fund B charges 0.05%/year.
  • Net Returns Fund A net annual return: 7.00% − 1.00% = 6.00% Fund B net annual return: 7.00% − 0.05% = 6.95%
  • Result Fund A (6.00%) — approximate ending value after 30 years: ~$200,900 Fund B (6.95%) — approximate ending value after 30 years: ~$242,600 Approximate difference: ~$41,700
  • Key InsightExpense ratios are silent. They do not appear as charges on your statement — they reduce the rate at which your money grows. Over decades, small fee differences compound into large dollar differences.
The 0.95% annual fee difference costs the Fund A investor approximately $41,700 over 30 years — from compounding, not from any single large charge.

Section 18.7

Diversification

What Diversification Means

Diversification means spreading investments across different assets, companies, industries, and sometimes countries, so that no single investment can cause catastrophic damage to the whole portfolio. The concept rests on a straightforward observation: different investments do not all move in the same direction at the same time. Combining assets that do not move perfectly together reduces the overall volatility of the portfolio without necessarily reducing the expected return.

Types of Diversification

Diversification TypeWhat It ReducesExample
Across companiesCompany-specific risk: any one company performing very badly or failingHolding 50 companies instead of 1
Across sectorsSector-specific downturns affecting all companies in one industryHolding technology, healthcare, energy, consumer goods, utilities
Across asset classesPartial protection when stocks and bonds move differently during economic stressHolding both stock funds and bond funds
Across geographiesExposure to conditions in any single country's economy or marketsHolding U.S. and international stock funds
Across timeTiming risk of investing all at once at a market peakInvesting a fixed amount each month (dollar-cost averaging)
What Diversification Cannot Do

Diversification reduces unsystematic risk — risk specific to a single company, industry, or sector. It does not eliminate systematic risk — risk that affects the whole economy or market. When a severe recession hits or global financial markets experience a crisis, nearly all assets tend to fall simultaneously. Diversification protects against losing everything in one investment, not against broad market declines.

Example 18.7.1
Concentration vs. Diversification
  • ScenarioInvestor A puts $10,000 in one retail company. Investor B puts $500 each into 20 different companies across five industries. The retail company has an especially bad year and falls 60% in value.
  • Investor A $10,000 × (1 − 0.60) = $4,000 remaining Loss: $6,000 (60% of total portfolio)
  • Investor B Retail position: $500 × (1 − 0.60) = $200 remaining (loss: $300) Other 19 positions (assume flat): $9,500 unchanged Total portfolio: ~$9,700 (loss: $300, or 3% of total portfolio)
  • ConclusionThe same 60% retail decline cost Investor A $6,000 and Investor B $300. Diversification does not eliminate loss, but it dramatically limits the damage any single investment can cause.
Investor A lost $6,000 (60%). Investor B lost $300 (3%). Same event; dramatically different outcomes.
Section 18.8

Asset Allocation

Asset allocation is the decision about how to divide a portfolio among different asset classes — most commonly stocks, bonds, and cash equivalents — according to an investor's goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. Diversification is about spreading within asset classes. Asset allocation is about dividing among asset classes. Both matter.

Stocks have historically delivered higher long-term returns than bonds, but with considerably more short-term volatility. Bonds typically deliver lower returns but with more stability and predictable income. Choosing an allocation is about balancing time horizon, psychological tolerance for loss, and income needs from the portfolio.

Sample Asset Allocation Ranges

Important Note

The table below shows illustrative ranges only — not recommendations. Actual allocations should reflect individual circumstances, financial goals, income needs, and risk tolerance. A financial professional can help tailor an allocation.

Investor StageStocksBondsCash
Young investor, 30+ years to retirement80–90%10–20%0–5%
Mid-career, 15–30 years to retirement60–80%20–40%0–5%
Near retirement, 5–15 years out40–60%40–60%5–10%
In retirement, drawing income20–50%50–70%10–20%

Rebalancing

Over time, market movements change a portfolio's actual allocation. If stocks rise strongly, the portfolio may drift from a 70% stock / 30% bond target to 80% / 20%. Rebalancing means periodically selling some of the overweighted assets and buying the underweighted ones to return to the target allocation. It enforces the discipline of buying low and selling high — you sell assets that have grown and buy assets that have not yet recovered.

Target-Date Funds

Target-date funds automate asset allocation by holding a mix of stocks and bonds that automatically becomes more conservative as the target retirement date approaches. A fund labeled "Target Date 2055" is designed for investors who plan to retire around 2055 and gradually shifts from higher stock exposure to higher bond exposure over the coming decades. They are widely used in 401(k) plans because they provide simple, automatic diversification and rebalancing.

Section 18.9

Fees and Expense Ratios

An expense ratio is the annual fee charged by a fund, expressed as a percentage of assets under management. It covers the fund's operating costs — management salaries, administrative costs, legal fees, and other expenses. The fee is not billed to you directly; it is subtracted from the fund's assets, which reduces your returns. Expense ratios are paid every year, regardless of whether the fund made money. They compound silently against your returns.

Fund TypeTypical Expense Ratio Range
Actively managed stock mutual fund0.50% – 1.50% or higher
Actively managed bond mutual fund0.40% – 1.00%
Passively managed index mutual fund0.03% – 0.20%
Index ETF0.03% – 0.25%
Target-date fund (passive)0.10% – 0.20%

Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Tax-advantaged accounts such as 401(k) plans and IRAs allow investments to grow without annual taxation. Taxes are either deferred until withdrawal (traditional accounts) or avoided entirely on qualified withdrawals (Roth accounts), depending on the account type. This is a major reason why these accounts are powerful tools for long-term investing. Chapter 20 covers retirement accounts in depth.

Chapter 18 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 18.1Investment types differ because they represent fundamentally different economic activities: owning an asset versus lending money. Stocks are ownership; bonds are lending. Mutual funds and ETFs are pooled containers that can hold either.
  • Sec 18.2A stock is a unit of equity ownership in a corporation. Stocks generate returns through price appreciation and dividends. They carry significant risk, especially when concentrated in a single company or sector.
  • Sec 18.3A bond is a lending instrument with a fixed face value, coupon rate, and maturity date. Bonds generate returns through regular interest payments and return of principal. Bond prices move inversely with interest rates.
  • Sec 18.4Mutual funds pool investor money to purchase a portfolio of securities. Shares are priced once daily at NAV. Funds may be actively or passively managed; research consistently shows most active funds underperform their benchmarks after fees.
  • Sec 18.5ETFs are similar to mutual funds but trade on stock exchanges throughout the day. They are generally more tax-efficient and often carry lower expense ratios than actively managed mutual funds.
  • Sec 18.6Index funds replicate a specific market index passively, with minimal management. Because index funds require minimal active management, they typically carry very low expense ratios — a significant advantage over decades.
  • Sec 18.7Diversification reduces unsystematic (company-specific and sector-specific) risk by spreading investments across assets that do not move in perfect lockstep. It does not eliminate systematic (market-wide) risk.
  • Sec 18.8Asset allocation divides a portfolio among stocks, bonds, and cash based on goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. Younger investors can typically hold more stocks; investors near retirement shift toward more stable assets. Rebalancing periodically restores the target allocation.
  • Sec 18.9Expense ratios are silent annual fees that reduce the rate of portfolio growth. Small differences in expense ratios compound to large dollar differences over decades. Index funds and ETFs typically carry much lower expense ratios than actively managed funds.
  • Sec 18.10Investments generate returns through dividends, interest, and capital gains. Unrealized gains become realized — and potentially taxable — when investments are sold. Tax-advantaged retirement accounts allow investments to grow without annual taxation.

Key Terms Review

asset allocation
Dividing a portfolio among different asset classes (stocks, bonds, cash) based on goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance.
bear market
A sustained period of declining stock prices, typically defined as a drop of 20% or more from a recent high.
bond
A debt instrument in which the investor lends money to an issuer in exchange for regular interest payments and return of principal at maturity.
bull market
A sustained period of rising stock prices, often characterized by investor optimism and economic growth.
capital gain
Profit from selling an investment for more than its purchase price. Unrealized until the investment is sold.
capital loss
A loss from selling an investment for less than its purchase price.
diversification
Spreading investments across different assets, companies, sectors, or countries to reduce the impact of any single investment's poor performance.
dividend
A cash payment distributed by a company to its shareholders, representing a share of profits, typically on a quarterly schedule.
equity
Ownership. In investing, stocks are equity investments because they represent ownership in a company.
ETF
Exchange-traded fund. A fund that holds a collection of securities and whose shares trade on a stock exchange throughout the trading day.
expense ratio
The annual fee charged by a fund, expressed as a percentage of assets, covering the fund's operating costs. Subtracted from fund assets, not billed separately.
face value
The amount a bond issuer will repay to the bondholder at maturity. Also called par value. Typically $1,000 per bond.
index fund
A fund designed to replicate the holdings and performance of a specific market index, without active stock selection by a manager.
maturity date
The date on which a bond's issuer returns the principal (face value) to the bondholder.
mutual fund
A pooled investment vehicle in which many investors' money is combined and managed according to a stated strategy. Shares are priced once daily at NAV.
NAV
Net asset value. The per-share value of a mutual fund: (total fund assets minus liabilities) ÷ shares outstanding.
portfolio
The total collection of investments held by an investor — across all accounts and asset types.
prospectus
A formal legal document a fund must provide to investors describing its investment objectives, strategies, fees, risks, and performance history.
rebalancing
Periodically adjusting a portfolio back to its target asset allocation by selling overweighted assets and buying underweighted ones.
shareholder
A person who owns shares of a company's stock or shares of a fund.
stock
A unit of ownership (equity) in a corporation. Also called a share. Shareholders have a proportional claim on company profits and assets.
systematic risk
Risk that affects the whole market or economy. Cannot be eliminated through diversification. Examples: recessions, financial crises.
unsystematic risk
Risk specific to a single company, industry, or sector. Can be reduced through diversification.
yield (bond)
The return an investor earns on a bond based on the price paid and interest received. May differ from the coupon rate if the bond was purchased at a discount or premium.

Financial Systems Connection

Investment markets are not just places where individuals grow savings. They are systems that channel capital from savers to businesses, governments, and institutions that need money to operate, grow, and invest. When you buy stock in a company, you are providing capital that can fund hiring, research, and expansion. When you buy a government bond, you are lending to the government to fund public services, infrastructure, or deficit spending.

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) oversees investment markets in the United States to require disclosure, prevent fraud, and maintain market integrity. The SEC requires mutual funds and ETFs to publish prospectuses — formal documents disclosing investment strategy, fees, risks, and performance history. Students should always read a fund's prospectus before investing.

Stock exchanges such as the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and Nasdaq are organized marketplaces where stocks and ETFs trade. They maintain trading rules and infrastructure that make buying and selling securities fast and transparent. Brokerage firms serve as intermediaries between investors and the markets, executing trades, holding investment accounts, and providing tools. The rise of online discount brokerages and zero-commission trading has reduced the cost of investing significantly for ordinary investors.

Error 3
"A fund returned 18% last year, so I know it will do well next year."

Error 4
A student ignores an expense ratio because "0.75% is such a tiny number it doesn't matter."

Error 5
"Bonds are safe. You can't lose money in bonds."

Identify at least three specific risks that bond investors face. Under what conditions might a bond investor lose money?

Chapter 18 introduced the types of investments that form most household portfolios. Chapter 19 deepens the analysis of risk.

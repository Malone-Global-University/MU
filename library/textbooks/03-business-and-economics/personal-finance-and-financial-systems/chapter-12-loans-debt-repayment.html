Chapter 12 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Borrowing money is one of the most consequential financial decisions people make. Loans fund education, cars, homes, and emergencies. Used carefully, credit can expand opportunity. Used carelessly, debt can become a serious burden that costs far more than the original purchase and limits future financial choices for years.

This chapter explains how borrowing works, what debt really costs, how lenders structure loans, how minimum payments trap borrowers in long repayment cycles, and how to compare and repay debt strategically. Students will learn to read the true cost of borrowing — not just the monthly payment — and to recognize the warning signs of debt stress before a manageable situation becomes a crisis.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to distinguish types of debt, calculate total borrowing costs, compare repayment strategies, evaluate the real impact of minimum payments, and identify resources for getting help with debt.

Opening Question

A car dealership advertises "Only $259 per month!" for a new vehicle. Is that a good deal? The answer depends on how long you pay, what interest rate applies, what fees are included, and what the total amount repaid actually is. A $259 monthly payment over 72 months costs more than $18,600 before interest — even if the sticker price was lower. By the end of this chapter, you will know how to read past the monthly payment and evaluate the true cost of any loan.

Section 12.1

Why People Borrow

Most people borrow money at some point in their lives. Understanding why people borrow — and what tradeoffs borrowing involves — is the starting point for responsible debt management.

The reasons people borrow

ReasonExplanationExample
Spreading cost over timePurchase price exceeds what savings can cover in a short period$18,000 car loan repaid over 48 months
Investing in human capitalEducation costs must be paid before future earnings arriveStudent loan repaid from post-graduation income
Covering emergenciesSudden expenses exceed available savingsMedical bill or car repair on a credit card
Building credit historyResponsible repayment creates a credit record for future borrowingA secured credit card used and paid monthly
Purchasing assetsMost people cannot buy a home from savings aloneMortgage with home as collateral
The Fundamental Tradeoff

Borrowing is not free. Every loan involves interest — making the total repaid greater than the amount borrowed. The core question every borrower must ask: Is what I am buying worth the total cost I will pay, including interest?

When someone makes loan payments, those dollars cannot be saved, invested, or spent elsewhere. Monthly debt payments represent an ongoing opportunity cost. A household spending $800 per month on loan payments cannot put that $800 toward retirement contributions, an emergency fund, or other goals during those repayment years.

Key Principle

Borrowing accelerates purchasing power but reduces future financial flexibility. Every loan is a tradeoff between access now and cost over time.

Section 12.2

Installment Loans and Revolving Credit

Debt comes in two fundamental structures. Understanding the difference shapes how borrowers manage repayment.

Installment Loans

An installment loan is a loan for a fixed amount, repaid in regular payments over a set period of time. The loan has a defined end date. When the final payment is made, the debt is retired.

  • Fixed loan amount borrowed at the start
  • Fixed repayment schedule (usually monthly)
  • Fixed number of payments and a clear end date
  • Often used for large purchases: cars, homes, education, personal loans

Revolving Credit

Revolving credit is a credit limit that the borrower can use, repay, and use again — repeatedly, over time. There is no fixed payoff date. The borrower's payment obligation each month depends on how much of the credit limit has been used.

  • Credit limit assigned by the lender; borrower chooses how much to use
  • Minimum payment required each month based on the current balance
  • Unused credit stays available; balance can grow if new charges exceed payments
  • No automatic end date
FeatureInstallment LoanRevolving Credit
Loan amountFixed at startVariable, up to credit limit
Payment scheduleFixedVariable (based on balance)
End dateYes, fixedNo fixed end date
Interest chargedOn remaining balanceOn unpaid balance monthly
Common examplesAuto loan, mortgage, student loanCredit card, line of credit
Risk of open-ended debtLowHigh if minimum payments only
Revolving Credit Risk

The flexibility of revolving credit is useful but creates risk: balances that are not paid in full accrue interest, often at high rates. Credit cards carrying balances month to month are one of the most expensive forms of consumer borrowing.

Section 12.3

Secured and Unsecured Debt

Lenders face the risk that borrowers will not repay. To manage that risk, lenders either require collateral or charge higher interest rates — or both.

TypeDefinitionExamplesDefault Consequence
Secured Backed by collateral the lender may seize if the borrower fails to repay Mortgage (home), auto loan (vehicle), secured credit card (cash deposit) Lender can foreclose on home or repossess vehicle
Unsecured Not backed by collateral; lender relies on creditworthiness and legal remedies Most credit cards, personal loans, medical debt, most student loans Credit damage, collections, possible court judgment — no direct asset seizure
Why the Distinction Matters

Because secured loans carry less lender risk, they typically carry lower interest rates. A borrower benefits from lower cost — but risks losing the collateral asset if they cannot repay. Default on a mortgage means potential foreclosure. Default on a credit card means credit damage and collections, but no immediate loss of property. Both outcomes are serious.

Section 12.4

Principal, Interest, and Fees

Understanding how lenders charge for borrowing requires separating the three components of loan cost.

The Three Components

ComponentDefinitionEffect on Borrower
PrincipalThe original amount borrowed — the actual dollars received or purchase price financedEvery payment partially reduces the principal; lower principal = less interest accumulates
InterestThe cost of borrowing, expressed as a percentage of the outstanding balance over timeHigher rate = more of each payment goes to interest instead of reducing the balance
FeesCharges beyond interest: origination, application, annual, late payment, prepayment penalty, balance transfer, cash advanceFees increase the true cost of a loan beyond the stated interest rate

How Interest Accrues Monthly

Example 12.4.1
Calculating the first month's interest on a loan
  • ProblemA borrower takes a $5,000 loan at 8% annual interest with monthly payments of $156.22. How much of the first payment is interest? How much reduces the principal?
  • Monthly Rate Monthly interest rate = 8% ÷ 12 = 0.667% = 0.00667
  • Interest Month 1 interest = $5,000 × 0.00667 = $33.33
  • Principal Principal reduction = $156.22 − $33.33 = $122.89 New balance = $5,000 − $122.89 = $4,877.11
  • InterpretMonth 2 interest will be calculated on $4,877.11 — slightly less than month 1. As the balance falls, the interest portion of each payment shrinks and the principal portion grows.
First month: $33.33 interest, $122.89 principal reduction. New balance: $4,877.11.
Annual Percentage Rate (APR)

APR is a standardized disclosure that captures interest plus certain fees, expressed as a yearly percentage. Lenders are required by law to disclose APR, making loan comparison easier. When comparing loans with the same stated rate but different fees, the loan with the higher APR is more expensive.

Always compare APR — not just the advertised rate — when evaluating borrowing options.

Important distinction: APR measures the cost of borrowing. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) measures the return on saving. These are different calculations used in different contexts.

Section 12.5

Monthly Payments and Total Cost

Many borrowers focus on the monthly payment when deciding whether to take a loan. Monthly payment matters — a payment must fit within the budget — but it is only part of the picture. Total cost over the life of the loan is equally important.

Three variables determine the monthly payment and total cost of an installment loan: principal (amount borrowed), interest rate (annual cost of borrowing), and loan term (number of months to repay). Changing any one changes both the monthly payment and the total amount repaid.

Worked Example: A $15,000 Auto Loan

Loan Term Interest Rate Monthly Payment Total Paid Total Interest Paid
36 months5.5%$453.48$16,325.28$1,325.28
48 months5.5%$347.79$16,694.12$1,694.12
60 months5.5%$286.96$17,217.38$2,217.38
60 months9.0%$311.38$18,682.80$3,682.80
72 months9.0%$269.62$19,412.64$4,412.64

What this table reveals:

  • Extending from 36 to 60 months at the same rate reduces the monthly payment by $167 but costs an additional $892 in interest.
  • Increasing the rate from 5.5% to 9.0% on a 60-month loan costs an additional $1,465 in interest — even though the monthly payment increases by only $24.
  • The longest, highest-rate option costs $4,412 in interest — more than 29% of the original loan amount paid in interest alone.
The Monthly Payment Illusion

A lower monthly payment achieved by extending the loan term nearly always means paying more in total. Borrowers should weigh the budget benefit of smaller payments against the higher total cost. The question is not only "Can I afford this payment?" but also "Can I afford what this loan actually costs?"

Comparison-Shopping for Loans

Before accepting any loan, compare: APR (not just the advertised rate), loan term, total amount to be repaid, all fees, prepayment flexibility, and lender reputation. Getting quotes from multiple lenders — banks, credit unions, and online lenders — gives borrowers the information to negotiate or choose the best option.

Calculating Total Cost and Total Interest
Total Cost = Monthly Payment × Number of Payments Total Interest Paid = Total Cost − Original Principal
Example: $286.96 × 60 = $17,217.60 total paid. $17,217.60 − $15,000 = $2,217.60 in interest.

Section 12.6

Minimum Payments

For revolving credit — especially credit cards — the minimum payment is the smallest payment the lender will accept each billing cycle without penalizing the account. Paying only the minimum is legal and keeps the account in good standing, but it creates a prolonged and expensive repayment cycle.

Credit card companies typically calculate minimum payments as: a flat dollar amount (e.g., $25 or $35), a percentage of the outstanding balance (e.g., 1%–3%), or the greater of a flat amount or a percentage. When the balance is small, the minimum might be $25. As the balance grows, the minimum rises somewhat — but often not proportionally.

Worked Example: A $3,000 Credit Card Balance at 21% APR

Minimum payment = 2% of balance, $25 floor. No new purchases.

Payment StrategyMonthly PaymentTime to Pay OffTotal Interest Paid
Minimum only (declining)~$60 at start, decreasingOver 17 years~$4,200
Fixed $100/month$100~4 years~$1,500
Fixed $200/month$200~18 months~$630
Fixed $500/month$500~7 months~$210

Paying only the minimum on a $3,000 balance at 21% interest can take more than seventeen years to repay and cost more in interest than the original balance. A borrower who charges $3,000 and pays only minimums may ultimately pay more than $7,000 total.

Why Minimum Payments Create Such Long Cycles

High interest rates eat most of each payment. The principal falls very slowly. On the large remaining balance, interest continues to compound. As the balance barely shrinks, the minimum payment also barely shrinks. The cycle repeats for years or decades.

What to Do Instead

Always pay more than the minimum on credit cards. Paying the full balance each month eliminates interest entirely. If the full balance is not possible, pay as much above the minimum as the budget allows. Even an extra $25 or $50 per month can save hundreds of dollars in interest and shave years off repayment.

Section 12.7

Amortization at an Introductory Level

Amortization is the process by which a loan is gradually repaid through regular payments that cover both interest and principal over a fixed schedule. Each payment is split between two components: the interest payment (calculated on the current remaining balance) and the principal payment (the remainder, which reduces what is owed).

At the start of the loan, the balance is large, so interest is large. Most of the early payment goes to interest, and relatively little reduces the principal. Over time, as the principal declines, the interest portion shrinks and the principal portion grows — even though the total payment stays the same each month. This structure is sometimes called front-loaded interest.

Why Amortization Matters for Borrowers

Early payoff saves money. If a borrower makes extra principal payments early, they reduce the balance sooner. The next month's interest is calculated on a lower balance, so less goes to interest and more goes to principal — accelerating payoff further. Longer loans cost more in total because the balance remains larger for longer, accumulating interest throughout.

Section 12.8

Debt-to-Income Ratio

Lenders and financial planners use the debt-to-income ratio (DTI) to measure how much of a borrower's gross monthly income is committed to debt payments. DTI helps assess whether a borrower can afford additional debt.

Debt-to-Income Ratio Formula
DTI = (Total Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income) × 100
Monthly debt payments include: mortgage or rent, car loan, student loan, credit card minimums, and other regular loan payments.
Example 12.8.1
Calculating a debt-to-income ratio
  • ProblemMaria earns $4,800 per month (gross). Monthly debt payments: Rent $1,100 · Car loan $285 · Student loan $175 · Credit card minimum $45. Calculate her DTI.
  • Total $1,100 + $285 + $175 + $45 = $1,605 total monthly debt payments
  • DTI DTI = ($1,605 ÷ $4,800) × 100 = 33.4%
  • InterpretMaria's DTI of 33.4% is within the manageable range. She has some borrowing capacity remaining but should be cautious about taking on additional large obligations.
Maria's debt-to-income ratio is 33.4%.
DTI RangeGeneral Meaning
Below 20%Strong — low debt burden relative to income
20%–35%Manageable — within acceptable range for most lenders
36%–49%Elevated — lenders may be cautious; borrower has limited flexibility
50% or aboveHigh — significant financial stress; many lenders will decline new credit
Limitations of DTI

DTI is useful but incomplete. Two borrowers with identical DTI ratios may have very different financial situations if their incomes, expense levels, savings, and risk exposure differ. DTI does not account for emergency savings, irregular income, family obligations, or cost of living variations.

Section 12.9

Debt Repayment Strategies

When a borrower carries multiple debts — several credit cards, a car loan, a student loan — they must decide which debt to prioritize. Two well-known strategies offer different advantages.

The Debt Avalanche (Highest-Interest-First)

The debt avalanche directs extra payments to the debt with the highest interest rate first, while making minimum payments on all other debts. Once the highest-rate debt is paid off, those freed funds move to the next-highest-rate debt.

Why it works: High-interest debt costs the most over time. Eliminating the highest-rate debt first minimizes total interest paid. The avalanche is the mathematically optimal strategy.

The Debt Snowball (Lowest-Balance-First)

The debt snowball directs extra payments to the debt with the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. Once the smallest debt is eliminated, those freed funds roll to the next-smallest debt.

Why it works: Paying off a debt completely — even a small one — creates a sense of progress and momentum. Research suggests that psychological wins help many people stay committed to long repayment plans.

Example setup: borrower has $200/month extra to put toward debt.

The Honest Answer: Which Is Better?

The best strategy is the one the borrower will actually follow. A mathematically superior plan abandoned after three months produces worse outcomes than a slightly less efficient plan sustained for three years. Both strategies produce dramatically better results than paying only minimums.

Once a debt is paid off, do not increase spending by the freed amount. Roll the payment toward the next target. This is what gives both strategies their power.

Section 12.10

Debt Stress and Getting Help

Debt becomes a problem when the burden exceeds what a household can reasonably manage. Recognizing debt stress early — before it becomes a crisis — gives borrowers more options and more time to act.

Warning Signs of Debt Stress

Warning SignWhat It Signals
Carrying a credit card balance every month; paying only minimumsRevolving debt is accumulating; interest costs are compounding
Using credit cards to pay for recurring expenses (groceries, utilities)Income is insufficient to cover basic costs without borrowing
Missing or delaying payments on one debt to cover anotherCash flow is failing; debt is becoming unmanageable
Receiving collection calls or noticesAccounts are seriously delinquent
DTI above 40%–45%Large share of income committed to debt, leaving little flexibility
No emergency fund; all income consumed by debt paymentsAny income disruption will trigger immediate default
Using payday loans to cover obligationsShort-term, extremely high-cost borrowing — often a debt trap
Recognizing the Signs Is Not Failure

Debt stress affects millions of households and can result from job loss, medical emergencies, family changes, and economic conditions — not only poor decisions. Early recognition opens more options. Waiting until a crisis point closes many of them.

Options When Debt Becomes Unmanageable

OptionWhat It IsBest For
Contact lenders proactively Lenders often have hardship programs — temporary payment reductions, deferred payments, or interest rate reductions for borrowers who reach out before missing payments Early-stage financial difficulty; before missing any payments
Nonprofit credit counseling Free or low-cost budget review, debt counseling, and Debt Management Plans (DMPs); look for NFCC- or FCAA-affiliated agencies Borrowers with significant unsecured debt at high interest rates
Debt consolidation loan Combines multiple debts into one new loan at a lower interest rate; only beneficial if the rate is genuinely lower and the borrower avoids new debt Borrowers with good enough credit to qualify for a meaningfully lower rate
Bankruptcy (Chapter 7 or 13) Legal process that provides relief from overwhelming debt; Chapter 7 discharges most unsecured debt; Chapter 13 restructures repayment; serious long-term credit consequences Borrowers with genuinely unmanageable debt and no realistic repayment path
What to Avoid

For-profit debt settlement companies (high fees, may leave borrowers worse off), payday loans to cover existing debt (extremely high APRs; create new more expensive debt), "guaranteed debt elimination" claims (no legitimate service can guarantee elimination of valid debts), and credit repair companies that charge fees for services borrowers can do themselves. Legitimate help is available without paying large upfront fees to commercial services.

Chapter 12 — Back Matter

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 12.1Borrowing is a tradeoff between purchasing power now and total cost over time. People borrow for five main reasons: spreading large costs, investing in human capital, covering emergencies, building credit, and purchasing assets. Every loan is also an opportunity cost — debt payments cannot be saved or invested elsewhere.
  • Sec 12.2Installment loans are fixed-amount, fixed-term loans with a defined payoff date. Revolving credit is a reusable limit with variable balances, no fixed end date, and risk of open-ended debt. Revolving balances that are not paid in full accrue interest, often at high rates.
  • Sec 12.3Secured debt is backed by collateral; default can result in asset seizure. Unsecured debt carries no collateral; default results in credit damage, collections, and possible legal judgment. Because secured lending carries less lender risk, it typically comes with lower interest rates.
  • Sec 12.4Loan cost has three components: principal (the amount borrowed), interest (the ongoing charge on the outstanding balance), and fees (origination, annual, late, and others). APR standardizes interest plus fees into a single annual percentage, enabling accurate loan comparisons.
  • Sec 12.5Total loan cost depends on principal, interest rate, and loan term. Extending the term lowers monthly payments but always increases total interest paid. Borrowers must evaluate total cost — not just affordability of the monthly payment — when comparing loan offers.
  • Sec 12.6Minimum payments on revolving credit keep accounts current but result in prolonged, expensive repayment cycles. High interest rates consume most of each minimum payment, leaving little to reduce the principal. Paying more than the minimum — even modestly more — dramatically reduces cost and timeline.
  • Sec 12.7Amortization is the process by which regular loan payments are split between interest and principal. Early payments are front-loaded with interest; over time, the principal portion grows. Making extra principal payments early reduces the balance faster and cuts total interest cost.
  • Sec 12.8The debt-to-income ratio (DTI) = total monthly debt payments ÷ gross monthly income × 100. DTI above 35%–40% signals elevated financial strain. Lenders use DTI to assess borrowing capacity. DTI is useful but does not capture savings, expenses, or other financial realities.
  • Sec 12.9The debt avalanche targets the highest-rate debt first, minimizing total interest paid. The debt snowball targets the smallest balance first, providing psychological wins. Both outperform minimum payments. The best strategy is the one the borrower will sustain. Freed payments should always roll to the next target.
  • Sec 12.10Warning signs of debt stress include carrying revolving balances, missing payments, high DTI, no emergency fund, and payday loan use. Options include lender hardship programs, nonprofit credit counseling, DMPs, consolidation loans, and — as a last resort — bankruptcy. For-profit debt settlement and payday borrowing to cover debt should be avoided.

Key Terms Review

Amortization
Gradual loan repayment through regular payments split between interest and principal reduction; early payments are interest-heavy, later payments reduce principal faster.
Annual Percentage Rate (APR)
The yearly cost of borrowing including interest and certain fees, standardized for loan comparison. Always compare APR, not just the stated rate.
Collateral
An asset pledged by a borrower to secure a loan; the lender may seize the collateral if the borrower defaults.
Debt Avalanche
Repayment strategy directing extra payments to the highest-interest-rate debt first. Minimizes total interest paid — the mathematically optimal method.
Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)
Total monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income, expressed as a percentage. Used to assess borrowing capacity and financial health.
Debt Management Plan (DMP)
A structured repayment arrangement negotiated by a nonprofit credit counselor, often reducing interest rates and consolidating payments into one monthly amount.
Debt Snowball
Repayment strategy directing extra payments to the smallest balance first. Provides early psychological wins to support long-term commitment.
Default
Failure to repay a loan per agreed terms. Can trigger collection, credit damage, and asset seizure for secured debt.
Front-Loaded Interest
The amortization structure in which early loan payments are heavily weighted toward interest, with principal reduction accelerating over time.
Installment Loan
A loan for a fixed amount repaid in regular payments over a set number of months, with a defined payoff date. Examples: auto loan, mortgage, student loan.
Minimum Payment
The smallest payment a lender will accept on a revolving account in a billing cycle. Paying only the minimum leads to prolonged repayment and high total interest cost.
Origination Fee
A fee charged at loan opening, often a percentage of the loan amount, that increases the effective cost of borrowing beyond the stated interest rate.
Principal
The original amount borrowed. Each loan payment reduces principal; a lower principal means less interest accumulates in future periods.
Revolving Credit
A credit arrangement with a set limit that can be used, repaid, and reused over time. Includes credit cards and lines of credit. No fixed payoff date.
Secured Debt
Debt backed by collateral; lenders may seize the asset upon default. Lower interest rates than unsecured debt because of reduced lender risk.
Total Cost of Borrowing
The sum of all payments over the life of a loan, including principal, interest, and fees. Contrasted with the face amount originally borrowed.
Unsecured Debt
Debt not backed by collateral. Includes most credit cards and personal loans. Typically carries higher interest rates than secured debt.

Financial Systems Connection

Debt is not simply a private transaction between a borrower and a lender. It flows through interconnected financial systems.

Lenders and underwriting. When a borrower applies for a loan, the lender evaluates credit reports, credit scores, income, existing debt, collateral, and other factors to decide whether to lend and at what rate. This process is called underwriting. The lender draws on data supplied by credit bureaus (covered in Chapter 11) and makes decisions within regulatory boundaries.

The secondary market. Many loans — especially mortgages and student loans — are sold by the originating lender to investors through a secondary market. A bank may originate a mortgage, then sell it to investors, freeing capital to make new loans. The borrower typically continues making payments to a loan servicer, which may or may not be the original lender.

Interest rates and monetary policy. The interest rates borrowers face are influenced by decisions made by the Federal Reserve. When the Federal Reserve raises the benchmark interest rate, borrowing costs throughout the economy tend to rise — including rates on credit cards, auto loans, and mortgages. This connection is covered further in Chapter 26.

Credit bureaus and reporting. Every payment on a loan — on time or late — is reported to one or more of the major credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion). This data shapes the borrower's credit report and score, affecting future access to credit and the rates charged. Debt management is therefore not just a current financial concern — it affects future financial options for years.

Debt collection systems. When loans default, they enter collection systems. Borrowers have rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), including the right to request written verification of debt, the right to dispute the debt, and limitations on when and how collectors may contact them.

Error Analysis

Each statement contains a financial error or misconception. Identify the error and explain the correct understanding.

Statement 1
"A longer loan term always saves me money because my monthly payment is lower."

What is wrong? A longer term reduces the monthly payment but increases total interest paid. Lower monthly payments achieved by extending the term cost more overall — not less.

Statement 2
"As long as I make the minimum payment every month, I'm handling my credit card responsibly."

What is wrong? Minimum payments keep accounts current but allow high-interest balances to persist for years. A $3,000 balance at 21% paid by minimums only can take 17+ years and cost more than $4,200 in interest alone.

Statement 3
"The monthly payment is the most important number to compare when choosing between two loans."

What is wrong? APR and total cost are more important. A lower monthly payment often reflects a longer term, which means more total interest paid. Compare APR, total repayment, and all fees.

Statement 4
"Using the debt snowball means I will definitely pay less interest than the debt avalanche."

What is wrong? The opposite is true. The debt snowball costs more in total interest because it does not prioritize the highest-rate balances first. Its advantage is psychological, not mathematical.

Statement 5
"If I consolidate all my debts into one loan, my debt problem is solved."

What is wrong? Consolidation restructures debt — it does not reduce the principal owed. If the borrower accumulates new debt after consolidating, they now have both the consolidation loan and new balances. Consolidation only helps if the rate is genuinely lower and new debt is avoided.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1

"I can't afford to pay more than the minimum, so minimum payments are fine." Minimum payments keep an account current, but they do not represent responsible debt management for high-interest revolving balances. Even a small amount above the minimum accelerates repayment significantly. The goal should be to increase payments whenever possible.

Misconception 2

"Paying off a loan early will hurt my credit score." Paying off an installment loan early may cause a slight, temporary reduction in score due to reduced account diversity. However, the long-term financial benefit of eliminating interest charges almost always outweighs any minor, temporary score effect.

Misconception 3

"If I ignore collection notices, the debt will disappear after a while." Unpaid debts can remain on a credit report for up to seven years. The statute of limitations on collecting a debt varies by type and state. Ignoring debt does not eliminate it — it can resurface, affect credit, and lead to legal judgment. Addressing debt through legitimate channels is nearly always better than avoidance.

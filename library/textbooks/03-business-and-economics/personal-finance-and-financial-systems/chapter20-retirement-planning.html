Chapter 20 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Retirement can feel like a distant abstraction when you are young, new to a career, or simply focused on getting through the month. But retirement planning is one of the most time-sensitive financial decisions a person makes — not because the decisions are complicated, but because the clock starts the moment you begin earning income.

This chapter introduces retirement as a long-term financial planning challenge. You will learn what retirement goals look like, how much income people typically need in retirement, how employer-sponsored retirement plans work, what individual retirement accounts are, how contributions and employer matching function, what tax advantages retirement accounts carry, how vesting affects the money your employer contributes, and how retirement plans should be adjusted over time.

The chapter does not require tax law expertise. It does not assume you are close to retirement. It is designed to give you a clear and honest picture of how the retirement system works so you can make informed decisions now — even if "now" is your first year of work.

Central Idea

The earlier you start, the less you have to contribute to reach the same retirement goal. Compound growth does significant work over decades, but only if you give it time. Waiting is the most expensive retirement mistake most people make — and it is also the easiest mistake to avoid.

Section 20.1

Why Retirement Planning Starts Early

Retirement is the period of life when a person stops working for income, or significantly reduces their work, and lives primarily on savings, investments, and income from retirement accounts or government programs. For most people, retirement lasts between fifteen and thirty years. That is a long time to live without a paycheck.

Because retirement lasts so long and begins at a specific point in time, preparation must happen over decades. You cannot save enough in the last five years before retirement to fund twenty-five years of living expenses. The math simply does not work. This is why financial planners consistently emphasize that retirement planning is a young person's priority, not only an old person's concern.

The Power of Starting Early

If you save money and it grows at a reasonable rate each year, the total amount grows not just from what you add, but from what the existing balance earns. Over ten or twenty years, the returns on previous returns become substantial. The following illustrative comparison shows what a ten-year head start is worth.

Comparison FactorPerson APerson B
Monthly contribution$200$400
Starting age2535
Ending age6565
Years of contributions4030
Total dollars contributed$96,000$144,000
Approximate ending balance (7% hypothetical growth)~$525,000~$453,000
Reading the Table

Person A contributes $48,000 less than Person B and still ends with more money. Person B contributed more dollars but gave compound growth thirty years to work instead of forty. The missing ten years cannot be bought back. Actual investment returns vary and are not guaranteed; this illustration shows how time affects compound growth, not specific outcomes.

The Key Principle

Starting early reduces how much you need to contribute to reach a retirement goal. Starting late forces you to contribute much more to reach the same goal — and you may still fall short. Every year you delay retirement savings is a year of compound growth that cannot be recovered.

Section 20.2

Retirement Goals

Retirement is not a single thing. For some people, retirement means completely stopping work at a specific age. For others, it means shifting from full-time to part-time work. Regardless of how you define it, the financial challenge is the same: at some point, your income from work will reduce or stop. A retirement goal helps you plan for that future.

Elements of a Retirement Goal

ElementDescriptionPlanning Note
Retirement ageWhen you expect to stop or significantly reduce working incomeCommon planning ages: 62, 65, 67 — a personal decision based on health, career, and finances
Years in retirementHow long you will need to fund living expenses without a paycheckPlan for a longer retirement rather than shorter — no one knows their lifespan
Annual living expensesHow much you will spend each year in retirementSome costs fall (commuting, work clothing); others rise (healthcare)
Income sourcesGuaranteed or expected income in retirementSocial Security, pensions, part-time work, spouse or partner income
Savings gapWhat personal savings must cover after guaranteed incomeThis is the amount your retirement accounts must provide
Goals Change Over Time

A retirement goal you set at age 22 will look different at 35, 45, and 55. Income changes. Life circumstances change. Review your retirement goal regularly and update it as your life evolves. Chapter 28 includes a full planning guide for updating financial goals over time.

Section 20.3

Replacement Income at an Introductory Level

Replacement income is the amount of money you need in retirement to maintain a reasonable standard of living. Financial planners often express this as a percentage of your pre-retirement income. A common guideline is that retirees need approximately 70 to 90 percent of their pre-retirement annual income to maintain a similar lifestyle. This range exists because some expenses typically fall in retirement while others, particularly healthcare, may rise.

Sources of Retirement Income

SourceWhat It IsTypical Role
Social SecurityFederal program providing monthly income to eligible retired workers based on earnings history and claiming ageTypically replaces 30–40% of pre-retirement income; claiming earlier reduces the monthly benefit permanently
PensionEmployer promise to pay a specific monthly income for life in retirement, based on years of service and salaryLess common in private sector; still important in government, education, and some union employment
Personal retirement savingsMoney you contribute to retirement accounts through an employer or on your ownCovers the gap between guaranteed income and total income need
Other incomePart-time work, consulting, rental income, non-retirement investmentsVaries widely; difficult to plan around with certainty

Estimating Your Personal Savings Requirement

The 25x Rule — Savings Gap Estimate
A simplified approach to estimating total retirement savings needed
  • Step 1Estimate your annual retirement income need. Example: $50,000 per year.
  • Step 2Estimate guaranteed income sources. Example: $20,000/year from Social Security and pension combined.
  • Step 3Calculate the annual gap from personal savings: $50,000 − $20,000 = $30,000 per year.
  • Step 4Apply the 25x rule (based on a ~4% sustainable annual withdrawal rate): $30,000 × 25 = $750,000 needed in retirement savings.
Result: $750,000 target in personal retirement savings. This is a simplified planning estimate — actual needs depend on inflation, investment returns, healthcare costs, and other factors.
Important Limitation

The 25x rule is a planning starting point, not a guarantee. Healthcare costs in retirement can be substantial. Long-term care needs — assisted living, nursing care, in-home care — can add significant expenses that many people underestimate. Use this estimate to start planning, then refine it as your situation becomes clearer.

Section 20.4

Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plans

Many employers offer retirement savings plans as part of their employee benefits package. These plans allow employees to contribute a portion of their pay directly into a retirement account, often with significant tax advantages and sometimes with employer contributions added on top.

Plan TypeWho Uses ItKey Feature
401(k)Private-sector employersMost common employer-sponsored plan; pre-tax or Roth contribution option
403(b)Nonprofits, schools, hospitalsSimilar structure to 401(k); used in mission-driven and public service organizations
457(b)State and local government employeesNo early withdrawal penalty when leaving employment before age 59½

How the Plan Works

When you participate, you instruct your employer how much money to deduct from each paycheck and deposit into your retirement account. This is called a salary deferral or elective deferral. The money goes directly into your retirement account before you ever see it in your bank account — making saving automatic and reducing the temptation to spend it.

Your account is invested in options — typically mutual funds or index funds — selected from a menu offered by the plan. You generally cannot access the money without penalty until age 59½. Withdrawing early typically triggers both income taxes and an additional penalty.

Target-Date Funds

A target-date fund is a single all-in-one fund designed for a specific retirement year. You choose the fund closest to your expected retirement — for example, a "2055 Fund" if you plan to retire around 2055. The fund automatically shifts from a higher proportion of stocks (for growth) toward bonds (for stability) as the target date approaches. They are a reasonable starting point for anyone uncertain how to allocate their retirement investments.

Section 20.5

Individual Retirement Accounts at an Introductory Level

An individual retirement account (IRA) is a retirement savings account you open and manage yourself, independent of any employer. IRAs allow people without employer plans to save for retirement with tax advantages, and allow people who do have employer plans to save additional money with more control over investment options.

Traditional IRA versus Roth IRA

FeatureTraditional IRARoth IRA
Contribution tax treatmentPre-tax (may be deductible from income)After-tax (not deductible)
GrowthTax-deferred — no annual tax on earningsTax-free — no annual tax on earnings
Withdrawals in retirementTaxed as ordinary incomeTax-free if qualified
Required minimum distributionsYes, beginning at a certain ageGenerally not required during owner's lifetime
Best potential fitExpect lower tax rate in retirementExpect same or higher tax rate in retirement
Using Both an Employer Plan and an IRA

Many people contribute to both. A common priority order: (1) Contribute to employer plan up to the full match. (2) Contribute to a Roth IRA if eligible. (3) Return to the employer plan for additional contributions. This order maximizes the employer match first, then adds tax-free Roth growth, then captures remaining tax-deferred capacity. Tax rules are detailed and change over time — consulting current IRS guidance for specific situations is important.

Section 20.6

Contributions

How much should you contribute? There is no single correct answer — it depends on income, expenses, debt, emergency savings, and goals. But practical frameworks exist for getting started.

Contribution Guidance

Minimum: Contribute at least enough to receive the full employer match. Failing to do so is forfeiting part of your compensation.

General guideline: Save 10–15% of gross income across all retirement accounts combined.

Higher-savings approach: 20% or more, for people starting late or with ambitious goals.

Starting point: Any amount. Starting small and building the habit beats waiting for a "right" moment.

Worked Example 1
Monthly contribution from gross pay
  • SituationMaria earns $52,000/year and wants to contribute 6% to her 401(k).
  • Annual$52,000 × 0.06 = $3,120 per year
  • Monthly$3,120 ÷ 12 = $260 per month
Answer: $260 will be deducted from Maria's paycheck each month before it reaches her bank account.
Worked Example 2
Increasing a contribution percentage
  • SituationMarco earns $48,000 and currently contributes 3%. He wants to increase to 8%.
  • Current$48,000 × 0.03 = $1,440 per year
  • New$48,000 × 0.08 = $3,840 per year
  • Increase$3,840 − $1,440 = $2,400 more per year ($200/month)
  • NoteIn a traditional pre-tax plan, retirement contributions reduce taxable income — so take-home pay falls by less than $200/month, depending on Marco's tax situation.
Answer: Marco's annual retirement saving increases by $2,400 at a cost of less than $200/month in take-home pay.

Section 20.7

Employer Matching

Employer matching is when your employer adds money to your retirement account based on the amount you contribute. It is one of the most valuable benefits an employer can offer. Employer matching is effectively additional compensation — when the employer adds funds, the total going into your retirement account exceeds what came out of your paycheck.

Common Matching Structures

StructureHow It WorksExample
Full match up to %Employer matches 100% of your contributions up to a set % of salary"Match 100% up to 4%" — if you contribute 4%, employer also contributes 4%
Partial match up to %Employer matches a portion of contributions up to a set % of salary"Match 50% up to 6%" — if you contribute 6%, employer adds 3%
No matchEmployer offers no matching; plan may still have tax advantagesContribution still worthwhile for tax benefits, but no immediate return
Why Capturing the Full Match Is the First Priority

Failing to contribute enough to receive the full employer match means forfeiting money your employer has agreed to give you as part of your compensation. This is often described as "leaving money on the table." Capturing the full match is typically the highest-priority step in retirement saving — it is an immediate 50% to 100% return on your contribution before any investment growth occurs.

Worked Example 4
The cost of missing the full match
  • SituationPriya earns $55,000. Employer matches 100% of contributions up to 5% of salary. Priya only contributes 2%.
  • Priya contributes$55,000 × 0.02 = $1,100/year
  • Employer match (on 2%)$55,000 × 0.02 = $1,100/year from employer
  • If Priya contributed 5%$55,000 × 0.05 = $2,750 from Priya + $2,750 from employer = $5,500/year total
  • Forfeited per year$2,750 − $1,100 = $1,650/year in employer contributions forfeited
By contributing only 2%, Priya forfeits $1,650 per year in employer contributions. Over a 30-year career with compound growth, this difference would be very substantial.
Worked Example 5
Match calculation with a partial match formula
  • FormulaEmployer matches 50% of employee contributions up to 6% of salary.
  • Employee AEarns $45,000 and contributes 6%: Employee: $45,000 × 0.06 = $2,700 Employer: $2,700 × 0.50 = $1,350 Total: $2,700 + $1,350 = $4,050/year (9% of salary)
  • Employee BEarns $45,000 but only contributes 3%: Employee: $45,000 × 0.03 = $1,350 Employer: $1,350 × 0.50 = $675 Total: $1,350 + $675 = $2,025/year (4.5% of salary)
By contributing 3% less, Employee B also loses $675/year in employer contributions — and only gets 4.5% total into their account instead of 9%.
Section 20.8

Tax Advantages at an Introductory Level

Retirement accounts receive special tax treatment from the government — a deliberate policy designed to encourage long-term saving. Understanding the basic structure helps you see why retirement accounts are more powerful than ordinary savings accounts, even when the underlying investments are the same.

Two Basic Tax Structures

Account TypeContribution StatusGrowthWithdrawal in RetirementBest Fit
Pre-Tax
Traditional 401(k), Traditional IRA		 Reduces taxable income now Tax-deferred — no annual tax on gains Taxed as ordinary income Expect lower tax rate in retirement
Post-Tax (Roth)
Roth 401(k), Roth IRA		 No deduction — already taxed Tax-free — no annual tax on gains Tax-free if qualified Expect same or higher tax rate in retirement
Worked Example 6
Tax deferral in a pre-tax account
  • Situation$5,000 invested in a pre-tax retirement account growing at a hypothetical 7% per year for 30 years without annual taxation.
  • FormulaFV = $5,000 × (1.07)^30 ≈ $5,000 × 7.61 ≈ $38,000
  • Key pointIn a taxable account, gains would be taxed each year, reducing the amount available to compound. The retirement account allows the full $5,000 to compound year after year — the tax deferral itself generates significant additional growth over decades.
The absence of annual taxation on investment gains is itself a meaningful financial advantage — independent of the contribution deduction.
Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Traditional pre-tax retirement accounts require you to begin withdrawing money at a certain age (currently 73 in the U.S., though this has changed before and may change again). These required minimum distributions ensure that the government eventually collects taxes on deferred money. Roth IRAs do not have RMDs during the owner's lifetime. RMDs are a planning consideration for later in life, not something to manage when you are first contributing.

Section 20.9

Vesting

Vesting refers to your right to keep the money your employer has contributed to your retirement account. Money you contribute is always yours. Employer matching contributions, however, are often subject to a vesting schedule — a timeline that determines when those employer contributions become fully yours. Leaving before full vesting means forfeiting some or all of the employer's contributions.

Types of Vesting Schedules

TypeHow It WorksRisk to Employee
ImmediateEmployer contributions are yours right awayNone — always fully vested from day one
Cliff0% vested until a set year, then 100% all at once (e.g., 3-year cliff)Leave before the cliff and you forfeit all employer contributions
GradedVesting increases gradually each year (e.g., 20% per year over 5 years)Partial forfeiture if you leave before reaching 100%

Example Graded Vesting Schedule

Years of ServicePercent Vested
Less than 1 year0%
1 year20%
2 years40%
3 years60%
4 years80%
5 years or more100%
Worked Example 7
The cost of leaving before full vesting
  • SituationDiego has worked 2 years under the graded schedule above. His employer has contributed $8,000 to his retirement account. He is 40% vested.
  • Vested$8,000 × 0.40 = $3,200 Diego keeps
  • Forfeited$8,000 × 0.60 = $4,800 returned to employer
  • DecisionIf Diego waits one more year (3 years of service → 60% vested): he would keep $4,800 instead of $3,200 — a difference of $1,600. Whether staying is worth it depends on the value of the new opportunity versus the additional vesting.
Vesting status is an important factor in any job change decision. Know your schedule before you leave.

What Happens to Your Account When You Leave a Job

OptionHow It WorksKey Consideration
Leave in placeKeep the money in the former employer's planSimple, but you can no longer contribute; must manage an inactive account
Roll to new employer planTransfer to your new 401(k) or equivalent, if acceptedConsolidates accounts; preserves tax-deferred status
Roll to an IRAOpen or use an existing IRA and move the money there (rollover IRA)Preserves tax status; gives more investment choices
Cash outWithdraw the money directlyTriggers income taxes plus early withdrawal penalty if under 59½ — generally the most costly option
Student Warning — Cashing Out

Cashing out a retirement account when leaving a job is one of the most expensive retirement mistakes people make. Income taxes plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty can reduce your balance by 20–40% immediately. Money withdrawn also loses all future compound growth. Rollover options preserve your retirement savings without triggering taxes or penalties and should almost always be preferred.

Section 20.10

Adjusting Retirement Plans Over Time

A retirement plan is a living document. Life changes — income rises or falls, expenses shift, employers change, markets move — and your plan must respond. Reviewing and adjusting your retirement plan regularly is as important as starting one.

When to Review and Adjust

TriggerWhat to Review
AnnuallyConfirm contribution rate is appropriate; verify investment choices match time horizon; review overall retirement picture
Major life eventsNew job, marriage or divorce, having a child, buying a house, significant income change, inheritance
Significant market changeRebalance if your asset allocation has drifted from your target mix
As you ageGradually shift toward more stable investments (bonds) as retirement approaches; reduce exposure to market volatility
Worked Example 8
Increasing contributions with a raise
  • SituationSofia earns $50,000 and contributes 5%. She receives a raise to $55,000.
  • Old$50,000 × 0.05 = $2,500/year
  • If unchanged$2,500 ÷ $55,000 = 4.5% of new salary — her savings rate has effectively declined
  • If increased to 7%$55,000 × 0.07 = $3,850/year — $1,350 more per year into retirement
Directing part of every raise into retirement savings is one of the most effective long-term retirement strategies. Sofia's take-home pay also increased from the raise — the additional contribution reduces but does not eliminate the income increase she receives.

Chapter Summary

  • Sec 20.1Retirement is a long-horizon challenge that rewards early action more than any other single financial behavior. Compound growth over decades means smaller early contributions can outperform larger later ones. Every year of delay costs future retirement security.
  • Sec 20.2A retirement goal includes retirement age, years in retirement, expected expenses, income sources, and the savings gap that personal accounts must fill. Goals should be revisited regularly as income, family, and circumstances change.
  • Sec 20.3Replacement income — typically 70–90% of pre-retirement income — comes from Social Security, pensions, personal savings, and other sources. The 25x rule gives a simplified starting estimate of total savings needed: multiply the annual savings gap by 25.
  • Sec 20.4Employer-sponsored plans (401(k), 403(b), 457(b)) allow salary deferrals into tax-advantaged investment accounts. Money grows until retirement. Target-date funds provide automatic asset allocation adjustment as the retirement year approaches.
  • Sec 20.5IRAs are personal retirement accounts independent of any employer. Traditional IRAs offer potential tax deductions now with taxes due in retirement. Roth IRAs offer no deduction now but tax-free growth and withdrawals. Income eligibility rules apply to both.
  • Sec 20.6The minimum contribution is enough to capture the full employer match. A general guideline is 10–15% of gross income. Auto-escalation features increase contributions automatically. Starting any amount is better than waiting.
  • Sec 20.7Employer matching is additional compensation deposited directly into your retirement account. Capturing the full match should be the first retirement saving priority. Partial contributions that fail to reach the match cap forfeit employer money annually.
  • Sec 20.8Pre-tax accounts reduce taxable income now; withdrawals are taxed in retirement. Roth accounts provide tax-free growth and withdrawals. The absence of annual taxes on investment gains accelerates compound growth significantly over decades.
  • Sec 20.9Vesting governs when employer contributions become fully yours. Cliff vesting gives 0% until a threshold year, then 100%. Graded vesting increases gradually. Leaving a job before full vesting means forfeiting unvested employer contributions. Rollover options preserve savings when changing jobs.
  • Sec 20.10Retirement plans should be reviewed annually, after major life events, and as income grows. Increasing contributions with raises accelerates progress. Retirement readiness benchmarks help identify whether savings are on track for the target retirement date.

Employer-sponsored retirement plan allowing employees to contribute salary on a pre-tax or Roth basis, with money invested and growing until retirement.

pension

Moving money from one retirement account to another without triggering taxes or penalties — for example, from a former employer's 401(k) to an IRA.

An IRA funded with after-tax contributions where qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

traditional IRA

The process by which an employee earns the right to keep employer-contributed retirement funds, either immediately or over a defined schedule.

Calculation Review

Formula 1 — Annual Retirement Contribution
Annual contribution = Gross annual salary × Contribution percentage Monthly contribution = Annual contribution ÷ 12
Example: Salary = $54,000; Rate = 6% → $54,000 × 0.06 = $3,240/year → $270/month
Formula 2 — Employer Match Calculation
Employer match = Employee contribution × Match rate (up to the cap)
Example: Salary = $48,000; Employee contributes 5%; Employer matches 50% up to 6% → $48,000 × 0.05 = $2,400; Employer: $2,400 × 0.50 = $1,200; Total: $3,600
Formula 3 — Vested Amount
Vested amount = Total employer contributions × Vesting percentage Forfeited amount = Total employer contributions × (1 − Vesting percentage)
Example: $6,500 in employer contributions; 60% vested → Keep $3,900; Forfeit $2,600
Formula 4 — Replacement Income Estimate
Annual retirement income need = Pre-retirement income × Replacement rate (e.g., 0.80)
Example: $65,000 income × 0.80 = $52,000/year needed in retirement
Formula 5 — Savings Gap and 25x Rule
Annual savings gap = Annual retirement income need − Expected guaranteed income Total savings target = Annual savings gap × 25
Example: Need $52,000/year; Social Security provides $18,000 → Gap = $34,000 → Target: $34,000 × 25 = $850,000
Formula 6 — Compound Growth (Illustrative)
FV = PV × (1 + r)^n
FV = future value; PV = present value; r = annual growth rate; n = years. Example: $3,000 at 7% for 30 years → $3,000 × (1.07)^30 ≈ $3,000 × 7.61 ≈ $22,800. Actual investment returns vary significantly and are not guaranteed.

Conceptual PracticeUnderstanding Time and Compound Growth
  1. If you contribute $150/month ($1,800/year) starting at age 22 and use a simplified hypothetical 7% annual growth rate, approximately how much would you have at age 62? Use FV = $1,800 × [(1.07)^40 − 1] / 0.07 for an approximation. (Note: actual monthly compounding would produce a somewhat different result.)
  2. Why does delaying retirement savings by 10 years cost more than 10 years' worth of contributions? Explain compound growth in your own words and describe what "lost time" means mathematically.

  4. The 25x rule estimates how much total savings you need at retirement. What assumptions does this rule make? In what situations might it underestimate or overestimate the amount needed?

Error Analysis

Each statement contains a mistake in reasoning or calculation. Identify and correct each error.

Error 1 — Match Cap Misunderstanding
"My employer matches 100% up to 4% of salary. I earn $60,000 and contribute 6%, so my employer matches 6% of $60,000 = $3,600."

What is wrong with this reasoning? What is the correct employer match amount?

Error 2 — Cliff Vesting Misunderstood as Proportional
"I've been here 2 years under a cliff vesting schedule with a 3-year cliff. My employer put $5,000 in my account. If I leave now, I get to keep $3,333 — two-thirds of the total."

What is wrong with this reasoning? What would actually happen?

Error 3 — Waiting for Higher Income
"I only earn $28,000 a year, so there's no point saving for retirement until I earn more. I'll start when I'm 35."

Identify at least two problems with this reasoning.

Error 4 — Cashing Out a 401(k)
"Cashing out my old 401(k) from my last job is fine — I'll just put the money in a savings account for now and reinvest later."

What risks or costs does this plan overlook?

Error 5 — Roth Is "Always Better"
"A Roth IRA is always better than a traditional IRA because you never pay taxes on withdrawals."

Why is "always better" too strong a claim? What would need to be true for the traditional IRA to be more advantageous?

Retirement Planning Checklist

  • I know whether my employer offers a retirement plan (401(k), 403(b), 457(b), or other).
  • I know my employer's matching formula and the contribution rate required to receive the full match.
  • I am contributing at least enough to receive the full employer match.
  • I understand my employer's vesting schedule and know my current vesting percentage.
  • I have considered whether a traditional or Roth account structure is more appropriate for my situation.
  • I know what investment options are available in my employer plan and have selected options appropriate for my time horizon.
  • I know whether I am eligible to contribute to a traditional IRA or Roth IRA in addition to my employer plan.
  • I have estimated roughly how much income I may need in retirement and what my primary income sources might be.
  • I know what to do with my retirement account if I change jobs (rollover options).
  • I have set a date to review my retirement plan and contribution rate annually.

Looking Ahead
Chapter 21: Education, Training, and Student Loans

Chapter 20 answered the question: "How do I prepare for the end of my working years?" Chapter 21 answers the question: "How do I prepare for — and finance — the beginning of my career?"

Chapter 21 explores tuition, fees, and living costs across different education pathways; the opportunity cost of education; scholarships, grants, and work-study options; student loans and how their terms affect long-term repayment; and how to evaluate the return on investment of a credential relative to the career path it supports. Education decisions and retirement decisions are deeply connected: every dollar borrowed for education reduces money available for emergency savings, housing, and retirement contributions. Understanding how education financing fits into the larger financial plan is essential for making informed choices at any age.

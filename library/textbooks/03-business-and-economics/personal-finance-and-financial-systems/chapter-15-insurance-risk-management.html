Chapter 15 — Front Matter

Purpose and Overview

Every financial plan carries risk. Income can stop. A car can be totaled. A medical emergency can arrive without warning. A home can flood. The question is never whether risks exist — they always do — but how to manage them thoughtfully and affordably.

Chapter 15 introduces insurance as one of the most important tools in personal financial planning. Insurance does not eliminate risk. It transfers or shares certain financial consequences so that one large, unpredictable event does not destroy a plan built over years. Understanding insurance — what it does, how it works, what it costs, and how to choose it — is essential financial literacy.

This chapter covers the nature of financial risk, strategies for managing risk, how insurance works as a system, and the key concepts that appear in every insurance decision: premiums, deductibles, coverage limits, exclusions, claims, and beneficiaries. Chapter 16 surveys the major types of insurance that households use. Together, these two chapters build the foundation for a complete personal risk management plan.

Opening Question

A household has $6,000 in savings and earns $52,000 per year. A serious car accident causes $43,000 in medical bills and liability costs. Without insurance, what happens to this household's financial plan? With adequate insurance, what happens instead? By the end of this chapter, you will understand not only the answer to that question, but why transferring financial risk is one of the most important decisions any household can make.

Section 15.1

What Financial Risk Is

Risk is the possibility that something uncertain will happen and that its consequences will affect your finances. Financial risk is everywhere in daily life. The uncertainty is not the enemy — it is simply the condition of being alive and making plans in an unpredictable world.

Types of Financial Risk

Loss of income. A job ends unexpectedly. A business loses its largest customer. An illness prevents work for weeks or months. Income risk is often the most serious financial risk a person faces, because most financial plans depend on regular income continuing.

Property loss or damage. A vehicle is stolen or wrecked. A fire damages a home. A pipe bursts and floods an apartment. Property risks can be sudden, expensive, and emotionally difficult. The financial damage can reach tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Liability risk. Someone is injured in your car accident. A visitor is hurt in your home. You are held legally responsible for damage or injury to another person. Liability risk can result in lawsuits and financial losses far beyond what most households can absorb.

Health risk. An unexpected illness, injury, or surgery generates medical bills that can be enormous. Medical debt is one of the most common causes of severe financial hardship and personal bankruptcy filings in the United States.

Death and disability risk. A person dies earlier than expected, leaving dependents without financial support. A person becomes too disabled to work. Both risks can have devastating effects on a household's financial security.

Catastrophic risk. A flood, wildfire, earthquake, or severe storm causes large-scale losses. These events can damage or destroy property and disrupt economic life in ways that no individual can prevent.

Risk Characteristics: Probability and Severity

Risks differ in two important dimensions. Probability is how likely the event is to happen. Severity is how costly it would be if it did. Risk management decisions depend on thinking about both dimensions together. High-probability, low-severity risks are annoying but manageable. Low-probability, high-severity risks are exactly what insurance is designed for.

Major car accidentLow to moderateModerate to highCollision, totaled vehicle

Key Idea — Your Risk Profile

Understanding your own risk profile — the combination of risks you face based on your life, health, property, job, and family situation — is the foundation of a good risk management plan. Two people in different circumstances can have very different risk profiles even at the same income level.

Section 15.2

Risk Management

Risk management is the process of identifying, evaluating, and deciding how to handle financial risks. There are four basic strategies, and a well-designed plan uses all four working together.

StrategyWhat You DoBest Used ForLimitation
AvoidanceEliminate the risk by not engaging in the activity that creates itSpecific discretionary risksCannot avoid all risks; not always practical
ReductionLower the probability or severity through prevention and safe habitsMost risks — works as a complementDoes not eliminate risk; must be combined with other strategies
Retention (self-insurance)Accept and absorb the financial consequences yourselfLow-severity risks within your savings capacityDangerous for high-severity or catastrophic risks
TransferShift financial consequences to an insurer via a premiumHigh-severity, low-probability risks; catastrophic eventsCosts money; does not prevent the event itself
All Four Strategies — A Car Risk Example

Avoidance: Drives less; uses public transit when possible.

Reduction: Maintains brakes, tires, and lights; drives within speed limits; avoids distracted driving.

Retention: Carries a $1,000 deductible, accepting that amount out-of-pocket if a claim occurs.

Transfer: Purchases collision, comprehensive, and liability coverage through an auto insurer.

Student Warning — Self-Insurance Has Limits

Self-insurance works for small, manageable losses you can cover from savings. It does not work for catastrophic risks. If your home burns down and you have $8,000 in savings but $240,000 of uninsured property value, you have retained a risk far beyond your ability to absorb. The emergency fund covers the manageable shocks; insurance covers the catastrophic ones.

Section 15.3

What Insurance Does

Insurance is a contract between a person (or household, or business) and an insurance company. The person agrees to pay regular premiums. The insurance company agrees to pay for covered financial losses according to the terms of the policy.

Insurance converts an unpredictable, potentially devastating financial event into a predictable, manageable expense. Instead of facing the possibility that a single event will cost $80,000 — money almost no one keeps in savings — a person pays $900 per year in premiums. If the event happens, the insurance company covers the loss (minus the deductible). If the event does not happen, the person has paid premiums but avoided the large loss.

What an Insurance Policy Contains

An insurance policy is a legal contract. It specifies what losses are covered, what is excluded, the maximum the insurer will pay (coverage limits), what you must pay first (the deductible), your regular payment to maintain coverage (the premium), conditions for cancellation, and how to file a claim.

What Insurance Does Not Do

Insurance is not a savings account. Premiums are not held for you — if you never file a claim, they are not returned. You paid for protection; the protection worked by being available.

Insurance does not remove risk. A car accident can still happen. Insurance changes who bears the financial cost if it does.

Insurance does not pay for everything. Coverage has limits, exclusions, deductibles, and conditions. Reading your policy before a loss — not after — is essential.

Perspective — Paying Premiums Without Claims

It is tempting to feel that paying insurance premiums and never filing a claim means the money was "wasted." This misunderstands what insurance is. You paid for financial protection that was available to you throughout the coverage period. Not needing it is the good outcome, not a bad one.

Section 15.4

Risk Pooling

Risk pooling is the principle that makes insurance economically possible. An insurance company collects premiums from a large number of people who all face similar types of risk. In any given year, most of them will not experience a major loss. A small percentage will. The premiums from the many who do not suffer losses fund the payouts to the few who do.

Example 15.4.1
A simple risk pooling model
  • Situation10,000 people each own a home worth $250,000. Historical data shows roughly 1 in 100 homes suffers a major fire in a given year. The average major fire loss is $120,000.
  • Calculate Expected fires per year: 10,000 × 1/100 = 100 homes Total expected payout: 100 × $120,000 = $12,000,000 Cost per household: $12,000,000 ÷ 10,000 = $1,200 per year
  • InterpretEach person can afford $1,200 per year. Almost none could individually absorb $120,000. The pool makes protection affordable by spreading expected costs across all participants.
The risk pool converts a rare but catastrophic individual loss into a manageable annual cost shared by all members.

Adverse Selection and Moral Hazard

Adverse selection occurs when only higher-risk people join a pool, raising average costs, driving away lower-risk participants, and destabilizing the pool. Insurance companies manage this through underwriting, requiring disclosures of risk factors, and setting individual premiums. Government-run programs often use mandatory participation to keep pools stable.

Moral hazard occurs when having insurance changes behavior in ways that increase risk — being less careful about parking because the car is fully insured, for example. Deductibles reduce moral hazard by ensuring the insured person still bears some financial cost from any loss.

Section 15.5

Premiums

A premium is the amount you pay for insurance coverage. Premiums can be paid monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or annually. They are the cost of transferring risk to an insurance company. Premiums are calculated by actuaries — specialists who use statistical data and probability models to estimate how much a given risk pool will cost to cover.

What Determines Premiums?

FactorHow It Affects PremiumExample
Your risk factorsHigher individual risk → higher premiumDriver with three speeding tickets pays more than a driver with a clean record
Coverage choicesHigher limits or lower deductible → higher premium$500 deductible costs more than $1,500 deductible
Type of insuranceEach type priced on its own risk categoryHealth, auto, and homeowners all priced differently
LocationRegional risk, costs, and regulations affect ratesAuto rates vary by state and city; coastal homes cost more to insure
Credit history (some states)Some insurers use credit-based insurance scoresVaries by state law; practice is regulated and prohibited in some states
Example 15.5.1
Comparing annual vs. monthly premium costs
  • SituationRowan is comparing two renters insurance policies. Policy A: $18/month or $210/year. Policy B: $22/month or $252/year.
  • Calculate Policy A — monthly annual cost: $18 × 12 = $216 Policy A — annual savings if paid yearly: $216 − $210 = $6 Policy B — monthly annual cost: $22 × 12 = $264 Policy B — annual savings if paid yearly: $264 − $252 = $12
  • InterpretPolicy A costs less in both monthly and annual terms. But before choosing on price alone, Rowan should compare coverage limits, deductibles, and exclusions. A slightly higher premium might buy meaningfully better protection.
Policy A is less expensive, but the right choice requires comparing coverage — not only price.
Student Warning — Cheapest Is Not Always Best

The goal is not to minimize premiums at all costs. The goal is adequate protection at a reasonable and affordable price. An inexpensive policy with very low coverage limits or many exclusions may provide far less real protection than a slightly more expensive policy. Compare what you actually get, not only what you pay.

Section 15.6

Deductibles

A deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance coverage begins paying a claim. If you have a $500 deductible on auto collision coverage and your car sustains $3,200 in damage, you pay $500 and your insurance company pays $2,700. If the loss is only $400 — less than your deductible — you pay the full $400 yourself and the insurer pays nothing.

Why Deductibles Exist

Deductibles lower the insurer's cost, which lowers premiums for everyone in the pool. They also reduce moral hazard: when you bear part of any loss, you have an incentive to prevent losses in the first place. They also filter out small claims that would cost more to administer than they are worth.

Example 15.6.1
Deductible decision analysis
  • SituationMaya is choosing auto insurance. Option 1: $500 deductible, $1,440/year premium. Option 2: $1,500 deductible, $1,080/year premium.
  • Calculate Annual premium savings with Option 2: $1,440 − $1,080 = $360/year Additional out-of-pocket if claim occurs: $1,500 − $500 = $1,000 more Break-even years: $1,000 ÷ $360 = 2.8 years
  • InterpretIf Maya goes more than 2.8 years without a claim, Option 2 saves money overall. If she files a claim within that time, Option 1 was the better financial choice. Key question: Does Maya have $1,500 in savings she could use comfortably if a claim occurs?
The higher deductible saves $360/year but requires $1,500 in savings available to cover a potential claim. Financial capacity determines whether the tradeoff is appropriate.
Tip — Per-Occurrence vs. Annual Deductibles

Auto and homeowners insurance typically use per-occurrence deductibles: you pay the deductible each time a covered event happens. Health insurance often uses an annual deductible: once you have paid the deductible amount across all claims in a year, your coverage pays in full for the rest of that year.

Section 15.7

Coverage Limits and Exclusions

Even with insurance, not every loss is covered, and not every dollar of a covered loss is paid. Coverage limits and exclusions define the boundaries of what your insurance will actually do.

A coverage limit is the maximum amount an insurance company will pay for a covered loss. If your loss exceeds your limit, you are responsible for the difference. Limits are choices you make when purchasing a policy — buying inadequate coverage to save on premiums creates exactly the gap you were trying to avoid.

Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

This is a critical distinction in property insurance. Replacement cost coverage pays to replace damaged property at current prices. Actual cash value (ACV) coverage pays only what the property was worth at the time of loss — accounting for depreciation. ACV settlements can be significantly lower than what it costs to replace what was lost.

Example 15.7.1
Replacement cost vs. actual cash value
  • SituationA five-year-old laptop originally worth $1,200 is stolen. It has depreciated 60% over five years. Replacing it today costs $1,100. The policy has a $250 deductible.
  • ACV settlement Depreciation: $1,200 × 60% = $720 ACV at time of loss: $1,200 − $720 = $480 Insurer pays (ACV): $480 − $250 deductible = $230
  • Replacement cost settlement Replacement cost today: $1,100 Insurer pays (replacement): $1,100 − $250 deductible = $850
  • DifferenceReplacement cost coverage pays $850; ACV coverage pays only $230 — a $620 difference on a single claim. Replacement cost coverage costs more in premiums, but for many people the difference in coverage is worth it.
ACV: $230 payout. Replacement cost: $850 payout. The $620 difference illustrates why coverage type — not only coverage limit — matters.

Student Warning — Read Before You Need It

Many people discover exclusions only after a loss occurs — and discover too late that what they assumed was covered was not. Reading the declarations page, coverage section, and exclusions section of any policy before you need it is one of the most important financial habits you can build. You have every right to ask your agent to explain anything you do not understand in plain language.

Section 15.8

Claims

A claim is a formal request for your insurance company to pay for a covered loss under your policy. Filing a claim correctly — and knowing when to file versus when to pay out of pocket — is an important financial skill.

The Claims Process

StepWhat HappensYour Responsibility
1. Loss occursAn accident, theft, fire, or other covered event takes placeEnsure safety first; begin documenting the loss
2. Notify insurerReport the event promptly — most policies require thisContact your insurer or agent as soon as reasonably possible
3. Document the lossPhotograph damage; list affected items with values; gather receiptsSupport your claim with thorough, honest documentation
4. Complete claim formsInsurance company provides forms for the event detailsFill out accurately and completely
5. Adjuster evaluatesAn adjuster assesses the loss and determines what the policy coversCooperate with the adjuster; provide requested documentation
6. SettlementInsurer offers a settlement amountPay deductible; receive payment; appeal if settlement seems unfair
Tip — When Not to File a Claim

Filing a claim can raise your premium at renewal. For small losses close to your deductible amount, the premium increase may cost more over time than simply paying the loss yourself. Always weigh the immediate settlement benefit against the longer-term premium impact before filing. For large, serious losses, filing is almost always the right choice — that is exactly what insurance is for.

Student Warning — Insurance Fraud Is a Crime

Filing false or inflated claims is insurance fraud. It is illegal, and it raises premiums for everyone in the risk pool. Document what actually happened and what was actually lost. Consequences of fraud include policy cancellation, being dropped by your insurer, civil liability, and criminal prosecution.

Section 15.9

Beneficiaries

A beneficiary is a person or entity designated to receive the proceeds of an insurance policy when a covered event occurs — typically, when the insured person dies. Beneficiary designations are most commonly associated with life insurance, but also appear in annuities and retirement accounts.

A primary beneficiary is the first-named recipient. A contingent (secondary) beneficiary receives the proceeds if the primary beneficiary cannot — for example, if they have also died.

Life Insurance Death Benefits Pass Outside Probate

Life insurance death benefits pass directly to named beneficiaries without going through the legal probate process. This means money can reach beneficiaries quickly. But it also means beneficiary designations override your will — an outdated designation can direct money to the wrong person regardless of your current wishes.

Common Beneficiary Mistakes

MistakeWhat Can HappenHow to Prevent It
Outdated designation (e.g., former spouse still named)Ex-spouse legally receives the death benefit regardless of divorce or new willUpdate after every major life event
No named beneficiaryProceeds pass through estate; subject to probate delays and creditors' claimsAlways name at least a primary beneficiary
Minor children named directlyInsurer cannot pay minors directly; funds held in court-supervised guardianshipUse a trust or UTMA arrangement for minor beneficiaries
Never reviewing after life changesPolicy does not reflect current family structure or wishesReview beneficiaries annually and after any major event
Tip — When to Review Beneficiary Designations

Review after: marriage, divorce, the birth or adoption of a child, the death of a named beneficiary, significant changes in a dependent's circumstances, or any major financial or family change. Updating is usually as simple as completing a form from the insurance company — it does not require a new policy.

Section 15.10

Choosing Coverage Based on Risk

Choosing insurance is a financial decision, not a routine purchase. It requires analyzing your risk exposures, your financial resources, and what would happen if a loss occurred without adequate coverage.

Six-Step Insurance Decision Framework

Step 1 — Identify your risks. What risks do you face based on your life, property, job, and family?

Step 2 — Assess severity. For each major risk, estimate the worst-case financial loss. What would it cost?

Step 3 — Assess your ability to absorb the loss. Could you manage this from savings or income? For large losses, the honest answer is almost always no.

Step 4 — Research options. Compare policies, coverage limits, deductibles, exclusions, and premiums across multiple insurers.

Step 5 — Choose coverage that matches your actual risk. Avoid both underinsurance and overinsurance.

Step 6 — Review regularly. Risks change. Review coverage annually and after major life events.

Common Insurance Mistakes

Choosing based on price alone. The cheapest policy is not always the best policy. Limits, deductibles, and exclusions matter at least as much as the premium.

Failing to read the policy. Most post-loss surprises are preventable by reading the policy before the loss.

Underinsuring property. Insuring a home for less than its replacement cost saves money on premiums but creates a dangerous gap if serious damage occurs.

Forgetting to update after major changes. A new car, new home, marriage, or new child all change your insurance needs.

Dropping insurance when finances are tight. The periods when cash is tight are often when financial resilience matters most. Dropping coverage precisely when you have less cushion increases catastrophic risk.

Insurance and the Emergency Fund

Insurance and an emergency fund serve different but complementary roles. They are not substitutes — they cover different things and together form a two-layer financial safety net.

Emergency FundInsurance
CoversSmall, manageable financial shocksLarge, potentially catastrophic events
ExamplesUnexpected car repair, reduced hours, appliance replacementSerious accident, major illness, house fire, disability, death
MechanismSavings you accumulate and draw on yourselfPremium paid to a risk pool; insurer pays covered losses

Types include income, property, liability, health, death and disability, and catastrophic risk. Your risk profile depends on your specific life circumstances.
  • Sec 15.2Risk management uses four strategies: avoidance (eliminate the activity), reduction (lower probability or severity), retention (self-insure manageable losses), and transfer (use insurance for high-severity risks). A complete plan uses all four together.
  • Sec 15.3Insurance converts unpredictable large losses into predictable manageable costs. A policy is a legal contract specifying coverage, exclusions, limits, deductibles, premiums, and claims procedures. Insurance does not prevent risk, does not return premiums, and does not cover everything.
  • Sec 15.4Risk pooling spreads the financial cost of rare but expensive events across many participants. It works through large numbers and predictable loss rates. Adverse selection and moral hazard are challenges that insurers and regulators manage through underwriting, deductibles, and mandatory participation.
  • Sec 15.5Premiums are the regular cost of insurance. They reflect individual risk factors, coverage choices, location, and market conditions. The goal is adequate protection at a reasonable price — not simply the lowest premium. Paying annually often saves a small amount over paying monthly.
  • Sec 15.6A deductible is the out-of-pocket amount you pay before coverage begins. Higher deductibles lower premiums but increase exposure if a claim occurs. A break-even analysis shows how many claim-free years are needed to justify a higher deductible. Financial capacity to cover the deductible matters.
  • Sec 15.7Coverage limits cap what the insurer pays; losses beyond the limit are uninsured. Replacement cost pays to replace at current prices; actual cash value pays only the depreciated worth. Exclusions are types of losses the policy does not cover. Reading the policy before a loss prevents surprises.
  • Sec 15.8A claim is a formal request for payment after a covered loss. Document thoroughly, notify the insurer promptly, and fill out forms accurately. Small losses near the deductible may not be worth filing if the premium impact exceeds the settlement. Insurance fraud is a crime with serious consequences.
  • Sec 15.9Beneficiaries receive life insurance proceeds and must be kept current. Death benefits pass outside probate directly to named beneficiaries. Outdated designations, no named beneficiary, or naming minor children directly all create serious problems. Review after every major life event.
  • Sec 15.10Choosing insurance requires identifying risks, assessing severity, evaluating your capacity to absorb losses, researching options, and reviewing coverage regularly. The emergency fund and insurance together form a two-layer safety net: one for manageable shocks, one for catastrophic events.

Key Terms Review

financial risk
The possibility that an uncertain event will create financial loss or hardship.
probability
The likelihood that a given risk event will occur.
severity
How costly or damaging a risk event would be if it occurred.
risk management
The process of identifying, evaluating, and deciding how to handle financial risks.
risk avoidance
Eliminating a risk by not engaging in the activity that creates it.
risk reduction
Taking steps to lower the probability or severity of a potential loss.
risk retention
Accepting the financial consequences of a risk yourself; appropriate for small, manageable losses.
risk transfer
Shifting financial consequences of a risk to an insurance company in exchange for a premium.
risk pooling
The mechanism by which insurance spreads financial risk across a large group so individual losses become collectively manageable.
adverse selection
The tendency for higher-risk individuals to seek insurance more than lower-risk individuals, which can destabilize a risk pool.
moral hazard
The tendency for having insurance to reduce incentives to prevent losses.
premium
The amount paid periodically to maintain insurance coverage.
deductible
The amount the insured person must pay out of pocket before insurance coverage begins paying a claim.
coverage limit
The maximum amount an insurance policy will pay for a covered loss.
exclusion
A specific event, cause, or type of loss that an insurance policy does not cover.
replacement cost
The cost of replacing damaged or destroyed property with a similar item at current prices, without adjusting for depreciation.
actual cash value (ACV)
The value of property at the time of loss, accounting for depreciation. Usually lower than replacement cost.
claim
A formal request submitted to an insurance company for payment of a covered loss.
policy
A written insurance contract specifying coverage, exclusions, limits, deductibles, premiums, and claim procedures.
beneficiary
A person or organization designated to receive the proceeds of an insurance policy upon a covered event, typically death.
primary beneficiary
The first-named person or organization designated to receive insurance proceeds.
contingent beneficiary
A backup beneficiary who receives insurance proceeds if the primary beneficiary cannot.
underwriting
The process by which an insurance company evaluates an applicant's risk and determines whether and at what price to offer coverage.
underinsurance
Having coverage limits too low to meaningfully protect against serious loss; creates dangerous gaps in financial protection.

Calculation Review

15-C1: Annual vs. Monthly Premium Cost

Annual cost (monthly payments) = Monthly premium × 12

Annual savings from lump sum = (Monthly premium × 12) − Annual lump-sum premium

Calculation 15-C1 — Practice
An auto insurance policy costs $134 per month or $1,548 if paid annually
  • Monthly cost$134 × 12 = $1,608 per year
  • Annual savings$1,608 − $1,548 = $60 saved by paying annually
15-C2: Claim Payment Calculation

If loss ≥ deductible: Insurance pays = Total covered loss − Deductible

If loss < deductible: Insurance pays $0 (insured pays the full loss)

Calculation 15-C2 — Practice
Three claim scenarios, all with a $500 deductible
  • Scenario ALoss = $4,200: $4,200 − $500 = $3,700 insurance pays
  • Scenario BLoss = $380: Loss below deductible; insurance pays $0
  • Scenario CLoss = $18,750: $18,750 − $500 = $17,750 insurance pays
15-C3: Deductible Break-Even Analysis

Break-even years = (Difference in deductibles) ÷ (Annual premium savings)

Interpretation: if you go more than this many years without a claim, the higher deductible saves money overall.

Calculation 15-C3 — Practice
Policy A: $250 deductible, $1,200 premium. Policy B: $1,000 deductible, $960 premium.
  • Deductible diff$1,000 − $250 = $750 more out of pocket if a claim occurs
  • Premium savings$1,200 − $960 = $240 saved per year
  • Break-even$750 ÷ $240 = 3.125 years
  • InterpretIf more than ~3 years pass without a claim, Policy B saves money overall. If a claim occurs within 3 years, Policy A was the better financial choice.
15-C4: Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

ACV = Original value − (Original value × Depreciation rate)

ACV insurance payment = ACV − Deductible

Replacement cost payment = Replacement price today − Deductible

Calculation 15-C4 — Practice
A sofa originally costing $900, depreciated 40%; replacement cost today = $1,050; deductible = $200
  • ACV$900 − (40% × $900) = $900 − $360 = $540
  • ACV payout$540 − $200 = $340 insurance pays
  • RC payout$1,050 − $200 = $850 insurance pays
  • Difference$850 − $340 = $510 more received under replacement cost coverage

Financial Systems Connection

Insurance is not only a personal product — it is a system connecting individuals, insurance companies, actuaries, state regulators, employers, lenders, courts, and governments. When you pay an auto insurance premium, your money joins a pool managed by the company. Actuaries analyze loss data to set rates that keep the company solvent. State insurance departments approve rate changes, license companies, and protect consumers from unfair practices.

Lenders require homeowners insurance before issuing mortgages because the home is their collateral. Employers often offer group health insurance as part of compensation, negotiating rates for large employee pools that individuals could not access on their own. After a serious accident or disaster, insurance payments flow back through the economy to repair shops, hospitals, contractors, and emergency services.

Government programs also play a role in risk transfer: the National Flood Insurance Program provides flood coverage in high-risk areas where private insurers often will not. Medicare and Medicaid provide health coverage for elderly and low-income Americans. State unemployment systems share the risk of job loss across employers and workers.

  3. How does your driving record or credit history affect your access to insurance and its cost? Do you think this is a fair system? Why or why not?

Checklists and Planning Tools

Personal Risk Assessment Checklist
Income Risks
  • My income comes from a single employer or source — I have considered what would happen if it stopped.
  • I could maintain my financial plan if I were unable to work for three months due to illness or injury.
  • I have disability coverage or a plan for extended income interruption.
Property Risks
  • My rented or owned home is insured at an appropriate coverage level.
  • I know whether my policy covers replacement cost or only actual cash value.
  • I have checked for flood or earthquake risk in my area and know whether standard coverage applies.
  • My vehicle is insured for its appropriate value and use.
Health and Liability Risks
  • I have health insurance and know my deductible, out-of-pocket maximum, and which services require prior authorization.
  • My auto insurance includes liability coverage sufficient to protect my assets.
  • I know what my homeowners or renters policy covers for visitor injuries.
Life and Dependency Risks
  • I have identified whether others depend on my income financially.
  • If I have dependents, I have life insurance coverage appropriate for their needs.
  • My beneficiary designations are current and reflect my actual wishes.
Emergency Fund and Coverage
  • I have 3–6 months of expenses in an accessible emergency fund.
  • My emergency fund is large enough to cover my insurance deductibles without being fully depleted.
  • I review my coverage at least annually and after any major life event.
Policy Review Checklist
  • I have read the declarations page and know my coverage limits, deductibles, and premium.
  • I have read the coverage section and understand what is covered.
  • I have read the exclusions section and know what is not covered by this policy.
  • I know how to file a claim and the deadline for notifying my insurer after a loss.
  • I have documented my insured property (photos, receipts, serial numbers stored securely).
  • My premium payment dates are scheduled and I will not have a lapse in coverage.
  • My beneficiary designations are current (for policies where this applies).
  • I will review this policy annually or after any major life change.

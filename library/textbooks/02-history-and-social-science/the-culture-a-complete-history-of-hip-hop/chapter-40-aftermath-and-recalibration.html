The funerals were over. The investigations were not. The industry had to decide what kind of business it intended to be going forward, and it made that decision largely in silence.

On the hip-hop industry, spring 1997
Historical Overview

Two Deaths, One Industry

By the spring of 1997, hip-hop had lost its two most commercially significant and culturally resonant young artists within a span of seven months. Tupac Shakur was shot in Las Vegas on September 7, 1996, and died six days later. The Notorious B.I.G. was shot in Los Angeles on March 9, 1997, and died within the hour. Neither murder was solved at the time, and neither would be solved for decades, if indeed either case can be said to be fully resolved even now. The two killings, arriving in such close proximity and framed by the press as opposite poles of an East Coast–West Coast war, produced a kind of institutional shock that the hip-hop industry had not previously experienced and was not obviously prepared to absorb.

This chapter is not primarily about the killings themselves, which the two preceding chapters have already examined in connection with the lives and careers of the men who died. It is about what happened to the institutions around them in the months and years that followed: the rapid disintegration of Death Row Records, the label that had built its identity on exactly the menace that now looked less like marketing and more like prophecy; the equally rapid, and in some ways equally startling, expansion of Bad Boy Entertainment, which absorbed the loss of its signature artist and converted that loss into one of the most commercially successful periods any hip-hop label had yet produced; the quiet, partial, and ultimately incomplete effort by industry figures to formally end a rivalry that had outlived the men who were supposed to be feuding; and the beginning of a long process of public mourning, mythmaking, and historical reckoning that continues, in altered form, into the present day.

None of these processes happened cleanly, and none of them happened in isolation from the others. Death Row's collapse and Bad Boy's expansion were, in some respects, mirror images of the same underlying event: the market's verdict on which approach to hip-hop business — and which approach to the relationship between violence, image, and commerce — would be allowed to continue. Understanding how that verdict was rendered, and at what cost, is essential to understanding the industry that hip-hop would become over the following decade.

Origins & Background

The State of the Industry, Early 1997

To understand what happened to Death Row and Bad Boy after March 1997, it is necessary to recall what each label had been in the years immediately prior. Death Row Records, founded in 1991 by Marion "Suge" Knight together with producer Dr. Dre, had by the middle of the decade become the dominant force in commercial rap, built on the G-funk sound that Dre had pioneered on The Chronic and extended on Snoop Doggy Dogg's Doggystyle, and on a business culture that fused genuine creative talent with an atmosphere of intimidation that the label's executives did little to discourage and, by most accounts, actively cultivated. Knight's reputation for physical menace was not incidental to Death Row's commercial identity. It was, in the eyes of much of the industry and a significant portion of the label's audience, part of the product.

Bad Boy Entertainment, founded in 1993 by Sean "Puffy" Combs after his departure from Uptown Records, had built a different kind of commercial identity, oriented around radio-friendly production, interpolated samples of familiar pop and R&B records, and a roster built around the singular talent of the Notorious B.I.G., whose 1994 debut Ready to Die had established Bad Boy as a serious commercial force without requiring the label to match Death Row's atmosphere of menace, even as Bad Boy's own affiliations and conflicts, particularly with Death Row itself, were hardly free of real danger.

The signing of Tupac Shakur to Death Row in October 1995, after Knight paid his bail pending appeal of a sexual assault conviction, had intensified the perceived rivalry between the two camps into something the music press treated as an open war, complete with public accusations, diss records, and an atmosphere in which industry insiders on both coasts reported genuine fear about attending events where rivals might be present. By late 1996, with Shakur's murder unsolved and the war of words still active in interviews and lyrics, the industry as a whole existed in a state that can fairly be described as crisis, even if much of the public-facing business of record sales, video rotation, and award shows continued as though nothing extraordinary were happening.

A Note on Sourcing

Much of what is known about the internal collapse of Death Row in 1996 and 1997 comes from later journalism, court filings related to royalty and ownership disputes, and the recollections of former label associates published in the years that followed. Contemporary coverage was often speculative or incomplete, and this chapter treats claims about internal label dynamics with appropriate caution where independent confirmation is limited.

It was into this already volatile environment that the Notorious B.I.G. was killed in March 1997, an event that did not so much begin a crisis as confirm one that had already been building for a year and a half. What followed was not a single moment of reckoning but a prolonged, uneven process of institutional unraveling on one side of the rivalry and institutional consolidation on the other, conducted largely through ordinary business decisions — contracts signed, executives hired and fired, albums released and shelved — rather than through any dramatic public reckoning with what had happened.

Main Narrative

The Collapse of Death Row

Death Row Records did not collapse because of a single event. It collapsed through the accumulation of several distinct crises that converged in the year following Tupac Shakur's death, each of which alone might have been survivable, but which together proved fatal to the label as it had existed under Suge Knight's direct control.

Suge Knight's Incarceration

The most immediate and legally consequential of these crises had nothing directly to do with Shakur's death. On the night of the shooting in Las Vegas, Knight had been driving the car in which Shakur was a passenger, and earlier that evening surveillance video had captured Knight and members of Shakur's entourage assaulting a man named Orlando Anderson in the lobby of the MGM Grand, an altercation that investigators would later connect, with varying degrees of confidence, to the shooting that followed hours later. The assault itself constituted a violation of the probation Knight was serving following an earlier conviction, and in February 1997, a Los Angeles judge sentenced Knight to nine years in state prison for that probation violation, a sentence handed down before the Notorious B.I.G. had even been killed.

Knight's incarceration removed Death Row's central figure, its primary deal-maker, and the source of the intimidating presence that had structured much of the label's business culture, all at the precise moment the label most needed stable leadership. Without Knight present day to day, control of Death Row's operations became contested among remaining executives, attorneys, and family members, none of whom commanded the same authority, and the label's ability to retain artists, negotiate distribution, and manage its own legal exposure deteriorated rapidly over the following two years.

The Departure of Dr. Dre

Compounding Knight's legal troubles was the loss of the producer whose musical vision had defined Death Row's sound from the beginning. Dr. Dre had already begun distancing himself from the label before Shakur's murder, frustrated by what he described as Death Row's business practices and its escalating culture of violence, concerns that friends and colleagues said he had voiced privately for some time. In March 1996, months before Shakur's death, Dre formally left Death Row and founded Aftermath Entertainment, securing a new distribution arrangement through Interscope Records, the same company that had distributed Death Row's catalog.

Dre's exit deprived Death Row of the producer most directly responsible for the sound that had made the label commercially dominant, and his departure proved, in retrospect, remarkably well timed: Aftermath would spend its first two years struggling to establish a clear identity, but by the end of the decade Dre's new label would become the platform for the most commercially successful phase of his career, built around artists he developed largely outside the culture of intimidation that had characterized Death Row. The contrast between Dre's post-Death Row trajectory and the trajectory of the artists who remained on the label became, for many observers, a kind of natural experiment in what hip-hop business might look like with the same musical talent applied under different organizational conditions.

Roster Fragmentation and Legal Disputes

With Knight imprisoned and Dre gone, Death Row's remaining roster scattered over the following several years. Snoop Dogg, whose 1993 debut had been central to the label's commercial identity, departed for No Limit Records in 1998 after his contract expired, citing dissatisfaction with how the label was being run in Knight's absence. Other affiliated acts drifted away through a combination of expired contracts, royalty disputes, and simple attrition, as the label's ability to promote and develop new talent withered without Knight's deal-making presence or Dre's production leadership.

Tupac Shakur's posthumous catalog became a particular point of contention. Death Row continued to release material from Shakur's substantial backlog of recorded but unreleased songs, beginning with The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory, released under the pseudonym Makaveli less than two months after his death and widely understood within the industry to have been largely complete or near complete at the time of the shooting. Subsequent posthumous releases, drawn from an enormous archive of recorded material, would continue for years, eventually exceeding the volume of music Shakur had released during his lifetime, a pattern that raised persistent questions, voiced by journalists, fellow artists, and members of Shakur's own family, about the degree to which the artistic and editorial decisions behind these releases reflected Shakur's own intentions versus the commercial interests of whoever controlled the masters. Control of that catalog, and the royalties it generated, became the subject of extended litigation between Death Row, Shakur's estate — managed by his mother, Afeni Shakur — and various other claimants, litigation that continued well after the label itself had ceased to function as a meaningful commercial entity.

By the early 2000s, Death Row Records existed in little more than name, its assets eventually sold off and its catalog passing through a succession of owners. The label that had defined the commercial sound of West Coast hip-hop for half a decade had effectively ceased to operate as a creative enterprise within roughly three years of the event that the press had treated as confirmation of its menace.

The Expansion of Bad Boy

Where Death Row contracted, Bad Boy Entertainment expanded, and it did so with a speed that struck many observers at the time as difficult to reconcile with the scale of the loss the label had just suffered. The Notorious B.I.G. had been Bad Boy's most important and most talented artist, and his death might reasonably have been expected to cripple a label still in only its fourth year of operation. Instead, the period immediately following his death became, commercially, the most successful in Bad Boy's history.

Life After Death and the Posthumous Catalog

The Notorious B.I.G.'s second album, Life After Death, had been completed before his death and was released sixteen days later, on March 25, 1997. The album's title, chosen well before the shooting, took on an unintended and widely commented-upon resonance, and the record debuted at number one on the Billboard 200, eventually selling several million copies and producing multiple hit singles, including "Hypnotize" and "Mo Money Mo Problems." The commercial success of Life After Death demonstrated that the audience for the Notorious B.I.G.'s music had not diminished with his death and had, if anything, been amplified by the surrounding tragedy and the press coverage it generated, a pattern that would recur, in various forms, throughout hip-hop's later history whenever a prominent artist died young.

Puff Daddy's Pivot to the Foreground

Sean Combs, who had built his reputation primarily as a producer and executive rather than as a recording artist in his own right, made the decision in the months following the Notorious B.I.G.'s death to step forward as a performer under the name Puff Daddy, releasing his debut album, No Way Out, in July 1997. The album's lead single, "Can't Nobody Hold Me Down," became a commercial hit, but it was the second single, "I'll Be Missing You," built around a sample of the Police's "Every Breath You Take" and featuring vocals from Faith Evans, the Notorious B.I.G.'s widow, and the vocal group 112, that became the defining record of Bad Boy's 1997.

"I'll Be Missing You"

Released as an explicit tribute to the Notorious B.I.G., the single spent eleven weeks at number one on the Billboard Hot 100, an extraordinary commercial run for a hip-hop record at the time. Its success illustrated both the genuine scale of public mourning for Biggie and the degree to which Bad Boy, under Combs's direction, had become skilled at converting personal and commercial material into mass-market pop product, a skill that would define the label's approach through the remainder of the decade.

No Way Out itself went on to sell several million copies and won the Grammy Award for Best Rap Album, cementing Combs's transformation from behind-the-scenes producer into one of hip-hop's most visible commercial figures. The album's sound, built heavily on interpolated samples of recognizable pop, rock, and R&B hits from the 1970s and 1980s, represented a further development of the production approach Bad Boy had used on the Notorious B.I.G.'s own records, one that prioritized immediate recognizability and crossover radio appeal over the more sample-obscure, beat-driven aesthetic that had characterized much of the preceding decade's most acclaimed hip-hop production.

Mase and the Bad Boy Roster

Bad Boy's commercial expansion in this period was not built on Combs alone. The rapper Mase, who had signed to the label in 1996, released his debut album Harlem World in October 1997 to substantial commercial success, his laid-back, melodic delivery offering a notably different register from the dense, narrative-driven style the Notorious B.I.G. had made central to Bad Boy's identity. Mase's success, alongside continued output from other Bad Boy-affiliated acts, demonstrated that the label's commercial model, built around glossy production, sample-heavy hooks, and an emphasis on celebration and luxury over street narrative, could sustain a roster beyond its founding star, even as critics within hip-hop's press and among some artists began to describe this period, somewhat derisively, as the era of the "shiny suit," a reference to the matching metallic outfits Combs and his collaborators favored in music videos of the period.

That criticism, voiced most prominently in outlets like The Source, centered on a perceived shift away from hip-hop's earlier emphasis on lyrical complexity, sampling craft, and social or street-level authenticity toward a more openly commercial, pop-oriented aesthic that some critics regarded as a dilution of the genre's artistic ambitions. The debate was not new — versions of it had recurred since rap's earliest commercial recordings in 1979 — but it acquired particular intensity in 1997 because it was occurring in the immediate aftermath of a tragedy that many in the industry felt should have prompted reflection rather than further commercial expansion. Combs and his defenders argued, in response, that Bad Boy's success represented hip-hop's continued ability to dominate mainstream popular music on its own terms, and that the label's commercial triumph in the face of devastating loss was itself a form of resilience worth honoring rather than condemning.

An Unfinished Peace

The Notorious B.I.G.'s death in March 1997 produced, almost immediately, a wave of public statements from figures across the hip-hop industry calling for an end to the East Coast–West Coast hostilities that the press, and to some extent the artists and executives themselves, had allowed to escalate over the preceding eighteen months. The most visible of these efforts came in April 1997, when the activist and entrepreneur Russell Simmons, alongside Minister Louis Farrakhan of the Nation of Islam, convened a gathering of hip-hop executives, artists, and community figures in Chicago explicitly intended to formally call a truce between East Coast and West Coast factions of the industry.

The Chicago summit produced public statements of reconciliation and was covered extensively by music and mainstream press as a symbolically important moment, one that suggested the industry was prepared to take collective responsibility for an atmosphere that had, by most accounts, contributed to an environment in which two young men had been killed. Yet the practical effects of the gathering were limited and difficult to measure. No mechanism existed to enforce the truce, no individual or institution held authority over the industry as a whole, and the underlying business incentives that had rewarded confrontational marketing throughout the preceding years did not disappear simply because executives had stood together and called for peace.

De-Escalation Without Resolution

What did occur, in the years following 1997, was a gradual de-escalation driven less by any formal peace process than by the simple removal of the conditions that had sustained the conflict. With Shakur dead, the Notorious B.I.G. dead, and Suge Knight imprisoned, the specific individuals whose public feuding had defined the rivalry were no longer in a position to continue it. Death Row's commercial decline removed the institutional rival against which Bad Boy had defined itself, and the broader hip-hop industry, chastened by two unsolved murders that remained subjects of ongoing investigation, press scrutiny, and lawsuits from grieving families, became noticeably more cautious about explicitly coastal or label-based feuding as a marketing strategy, at least for a time.

This should not be mistaken for genuine resolution. Both murders remained officially unsolved for years. Investigations by the Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department into Shakur's killing and by the Los Angeles Police Department into the Notorious B.I.G.'s killing proceeded slowly and were repeatedly criticized, including in lawsuits filed by Voletta Wallace, the Notorious B.I.G.'s mother, who alleged that the LAPD had failed to adequately investigate possible connections between the killing and corrupt officers, allegations that became entangled with the broader Rampart corruption scandal that engulfed the department in the years that followed. The lack of resolution in either case left a persistent vacuum that journalists, documentarians, and amateur investigators would continue to fill with competing theories for decades, few of which could be definitively confirmed or ruled out using publicly available evidence.

The industry could call a truce between living executives. It could not produce an answer to the question that mattered most, which was who had pulled the trigger, and why.

Historical Significance

What the Aftermath Revealed

The diverging fates of Death Row and Bad Boy in the years following 1997 carried significance well beyond the fortunes of two record labels. They constituted, in effect, a real-time market test of two distinct models for how a hip-hop label could relate to violence, image, and commercial strategy, and the test's outcome shaped the industry's behavior for years afterward, even if its lessons were rarely articulated as explicitly as this.

Death Row's model had depended on the genuine and perceived menace of its leadership as an inseparable part of its commercial identity, an approach that had generated extraordinary profits through the first half of the decade but that proved catastrophically fragile once its central figure was removed from day-to-day operation and once the violence the label had cultivated as image ceased to remain safely contained within image. Bad Boy's model, by contrast, depended on Combs's ability to absorb shock and convert it into product with remarkable speed, an approach that drew real criticism for its perceived opportunism but that demonstrated, in commercial terms, a far more resilient organizational structure, one not dependent on the continued presence of any single artist, however central.

The Industry's Preference for Silence

Perhaps the most consequential, and least discussed, feature of this period was the industry's general preference for moving forward commercially rather than reckoning publicly with what had enabled two killings to occur. Award shows continued. Album release schedules continued. The business press covered Bad Boy's 1997 sales figures with little sustained attention to the unresolved investigations running in parallel. This was not a uniquely cynical feature of hip-hop as a genre; the broader American entertainment industry has rarely paused its commercial operations to conduct sustained public reckonings with violence connected to its products. But it meant that the formal "end" of the East Coast–West Coast rivalry, as marked by the April 1997 Chicago summit, functioned more as a public relations milestone than as a genuine resolution of the conditions that had produced the violence in the first place.

A Shift in Marketing Strategy

In the years immediately following 1997, several major hip-hop labels and artists became noticeably more cautious about deploying explicit regional or label-based rivalry as a marketing tool, a caution that would erode gradually over the following decade as new feuds, generally lower in stakes and conducted with greater self-awareness about the commercial function of conflict, became a recurring feature of hip-hop promotion. The memory of 1996 and 1997 did not eliminate rivalry from hip-hop's commercial culture, but it did, for a period, change the texture of that rivalry, pushing public conflict further toward the performative and rhetorical and further away from anything resembling the genuinely volatile atmosphere that had surrounded Death Row and its rivals in the mid-1990s.

Legacy and Continuity

Memory, Mourning, and Historiography

The deaths of Tupac Shakur and the Notorious B.I.G. became, almost immediately, subjects of a mythmaking process that has continued, in various forms, for decades. Both artists' posthumous catalogs grew to rival or exceed their lifetime output, raising persistent and largely unresolved questions about authorship, consent, and the ethics of releasing material an artist never approved for public release. Both men became subjects of documentary films, biographies, museum exhibitions, and academic study, their lives reconstructed and reinterpreted by writers and filmmakers with varying degrees of access to verified fact and varying degrees of interest in resisting the pull toward simplified narrative.

The unsolved nature of both murders fed a substantial body of speculative journalism, amateur investigation, and conspiracy theory, much of it organized around competing claims about police involvement, label involvement, or connections between the two killings that have never been established through evidence that would satisfy a court of law. This chapter does not attempt to adjudicate those theories, in part because the historical record does not support confident resolution, and in part because the proliferation of unverified claims is itself a notable historical phenomenon, illustrative of how cultural memory fills the vacuum left by unresolved fact.

Renewed Investigation

The Shakur case saw a significant development decades after the killing: in September 2023, Las Vegas authorities arrested Duane "Keffe D" Davis and charged him with murder in connection with the 1996 shooting, marking the first criminal charge filed in the case. The development illustrated both that investigative interest in the killing had never fully disappeared and that, even with new legal action, full public clarity about the events of that night remained elusive more than a quarter century later.

What can be said with confidence is that the institutional history traced in this chapter, the collapse of Death Row and the expansion of Bad Boy, became part of how the industry itself narrated the meaning of 1996 and 1997, often in ways that served the commercial interests of whichever party was doing the narrating. Death Row's decline was frequently presented, in later retrospectives, as a form of moral consequence, a label undone by the very culture of violence it had cultivated. Bad Boy's expansion was frequently presented, by its own promotional apparatus, as a story of resilience and triumph over tragedy. Both framings contain genuine truth and both also serve obvious institutional interests, a tension that the careful historian must hold rather than resolve.

Hip-Hop's Long Memory of 1997

The events covered in this chapter also marked a turning point in how hip-hop as a culture related to its own history. Prior to 1997, hip-hop's historical self-understanding had been organized primarily around innovation: who had invented which technique, which region had developed which sound, which artist had pushed the form forward. After 1997, hip-hop's historical self-understanding increasingly had to accommodate loss as a central category, a recognition that the culture's history included casualties as well as innovations, and that the industry's commercial machinery had not always protected the people whose talent it depended on. This recognition would resurface repeatedly in the decades that followed, as hip-hop lost other prominent figures to violence, and each time, something of the unfinished business of 1996 and 1997 would be invoked, explicitly or implicitly, as precedent and warning alike.

Chapter Summary

Conclusion

This chapter has traced the institutional aftermath of the two killings examined in the preceding chapters: the rapid disintegration of Death Row Records, driven by Suge Knight's imprisonment, Dr. Dre's earlier departure, and the slow scattering of the label's roster; the equally rapid commercial expansion of Bad Boy Entertainment under Sean Combs, built on the posthumous success of Life After Death and the calculated pivot of No Way Out; the formal but practically limited effort by industry figures to declare an end to East Coast–West Coast hostilities; and the beginning of a public mourning and mythmaking process around Tupac Shakur and the Notorious B.I.G. that has never fully concluded.

None of this resolved the central, unanswered question that had hung over the industry since September 1996: who had killed these two men, and why. That question would remain substantially open for the rest of the period this book covers, surfacing periodically in lawsuits, journalism, and eventually new criminal proceedings, without ever receiving the kind of full public accounting that might have allowed the industry, or the families involved, genuine closure.

What did change, decisively, was the shape of the commercial hip-hop industry going forward. With Death Row neutralized as a commercial force and Bad Boy ascendant, the late 1990s would be defined less by coastal rivalry than by an accelerating wave of commercial expansion, corporate consolidation, and crossover success that carried hip-hop, for the first time, to the unambiguous center of American popular music. The chapters that follow examine that expansion in detail, beginning with the broader market forces that turned hip-hop, by the end of the decade, into the best-selling genre in the country.

The rivalry did not end because anyone resolved it. It ended because the market moved on, carrying its survivors forward and leaving its dead to become, gradually and unevenly, history.

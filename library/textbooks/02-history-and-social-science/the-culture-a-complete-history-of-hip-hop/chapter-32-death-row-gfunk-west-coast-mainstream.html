Death Row, G-Funk,
and the West Coast Mainstream

How a producer's exit from Ruthless Records, a label built around one man's control of every contract, and a sound assembled from slowed funk grooves and synthesizer whine carried gangsta rap from regional controversy to the center of the national pop chart

The Culture: A Complete History of Hip-Hop
Part VI  ·  Gangsta Rap and Regional Reorientation

By the end of 1993, the most controversial subgenre in American popular music was also its most commercially reliable one. The two facts were not in tension. They were the same fact.

On the commercial ascent of West Coast rap, 1992 to 1996
Historical Overview

From Compton's Studios to the National Top Ten

The previous two chapters traced how Los Angeles rap, having emerged from the city's street gang geography and its independent record economy, broke into national consciousness through N.W.A's confrontational realism and then through the political backlash that followed Ice-T's brief, much-publicized detour into thrash metal. Both stories were defined as much by controversy as by sales. Straight Outta Compton had alarmed federal law enforcement and most of the national press before it had sold a fraction of what it would eventually sell. Cop Killer had triggered a corporate crisis at Time Warner before most Americans had ever heard the song. Gangsta rap, by 1992, was nationally famous. It was not yet, in any sustained way, nationally profitable on the scale that major labels required to treat it as a core business rather than a controversial sideline.

That changed within the space of about eighteen months, and it changed because of one record label, one producer, and one musical innovation that together restructured what mainstream rap sounded like for the rest of the decade. The label was Death Row Records, founded in 1991 by the producer Dr. Dre and the music industry entrepreneur Suge Knight. The producer was Dre himself, whose work on N.W.A's records had already established him as the most technically accomplished beatmaker on the West Coast. The innovation was a sound that came to be known as G-funk, a slowed, synthesizer-driven reworking of 1970s Parliament-Funkadelic style funk that traded the dense, chaotic sample collage of earlier rap production for something smoother, more spacious, and considerably easier for radio programmers to put on the air.

This chapter follows that transformation from its origins in a contract dispute at Ruthless Records through the release of Dr. Dre's solo debut, The Chronic, in December 1992, and Snoop Doggy Dogg's Doggystyle a year later, to the commercial empire Death Row had built by the middle of the decade. It is, in part, a story about music: about a specific set of production techniques and a specific vocal style that together reshaped the sonic vocabulary of rap nationally. It is also a story about business: about a joint venture between an independent gangsta rap label and a major label distribution arm that became a template other labels would study and imitate, and about a company whose internal culture of control and intimidation would, within a few years, contribute directly to its own unraveling. The chapters that follow this one, on the East Coast revival and the rivalry that would eventually claim two of the era's most significant artists, cannot be understood without the commercial foundation that Death Row laid in these years.

Origins & Background

The Breakup at Ruthless and the Making of a New Label

Dr. Dre, born Andre Young, had been the principal architect of N.W.A's sound since the mid-1980s, when he was producing local mixtapes and electro records as a member of the World Class Wreckin' Cru before Eazy-E recruited him to build beats for Ruthless Records. By 1991, Dre had produced the group's two studio albums along with a string of successful solo and side projects for Ruthless artists, work that had made the label and its owner, Eazy-E, extraordinarily profitable. Dre's own compensation from that work, by his own account and by the account of several people close to the label, did not reflect the centrality of his contribution. Like many producers and writers working under the contracts that independent rap labels offered in this period, he had signed agreements early in his career that gave Ruthless and its manager, Jerry Heller, control over publishing and royalty structures that left the label's creative engine earning a small fraction of the wealth his work was generating.

This was not, in the rap industry of the late 1980s, an unusual arrangement. It was, however, an increasingly intolerable one to a producer who understood exactly how much of N.W.A's commercial success depended on his specific musical choices, and who had watched Ice Cube leave the group two years earlier over almost identical grievances. The catalyst that finally pushed Dre toward departure came through a friend and collaborator named The D.O.C., a Dallas-born rapper and ghostwriter who had written significant portions of N.W.A's and Eazy-E's most successful material before a near-fatal 1989 car accident damaged his vocal cords and effectively ended his career as a performer. The D.O.C. remained closely connected to the West Coast rap scene as a behind-the-scenes collaborator, and it was through him, according to multiple accounts of the period, that Dre was introduced to a former college football player and concert promoter named Marion "Suge" Knight, who had begun working in the music business as a bodyguard and artist representative and who was looking for an opportunity to build a label of his own.

On the Founding Capital

The precise financing behind Death Row's 1991 launch has never been fully settled in the public record. Industry accounts and subsequent journalism have pointed to a mix of sources, including the entertainment executive Dick Griffey, whose Solar Records provided early distribution support, and individuals whose business activities outside the music industry were later the subject of federal investigation.

This chapter treats the label's founding capital as a documented point of historical dispute rather than a settled fact, since the available sourcing is contested and the underlying allegations were never resolved through a definitive court finding naming Death Row's investors.

Knight's method for extracting Dre from his Ruthless contract became, in the retelling that followed over the subsequent decade, one of the most frequently repeated stories in rap industry lore: that Knight and associates confronted Eazy-E and Jerry Heller directly, applying pressure that went beyond ordinary contract negotiation, until Heller agreed to release Dre and The D.O.C. from their Ruthless obligations. The specifics of that confrontation are difficult to verify with precision, and accounts from people present at the time differ in important details. What is not in dispute is the outcome: by the second half of 1991, Dre and The D.O.C. were free of their Ruthless contracts, and Death Row Records existed as a functioning label, with Knight as chief executive, Dre as the company's musical director, and a small founding roster built around the talent the two men had assembled in the preceding months.

The label needed a distribution partner capable of pressing, marketing, and shipping records on a national scale, and it found one in Interscope Records, a label founded in 1990 by the record executive Jimmy Iovine and the entertainment financier Ted Field. Interscope's arrangement with Death Row, formalized in 1992, took the shape of a joint venture rather than a conventional signing: Death Row retained ownership of its masters and creative control over its records, while Interscope provided major label marketing muscle, radio promotion infrastructure, and access to the formal retail and chart-reporting systems that an independent label could not reach on its own. This structure, novel at the time, would prove enormously profitable for both parties and would become an influential model for how major labels engaged with rap labels through the rest of the decade, a point this chapter returns to in its discussion of the period's broader historical significance.

The Sound and the Catalog

The Chronic and the Invention of G-Funk

Dr. Dre's solo debut, The Chronic, was released in December 1992, and it remains one of the most consequential production albums in the history of American popular music, not because its individual songs were unprecedented in subject matter, but because of the specific sonic palette Dre constructed to carry them. The dominant rap production style of the preceding several years, exemplified by the Bomb Squad's work for Public Enemy and discussed in earlier chapters, had favored dense, layered collages of short, chopped samples stacked into a wall of controlled chaos. Dre's approach on The Chronic moved in nearly the opposite direction. The tracks were spacious rather than dense. The tempo sat in a slow, rolling pocket, often well under the brisker tempos common in East Coast production of the period. The dominant melodic element was not a sample loop but a high, whining synthesizer lead, frequently played live by session musicians rather than lifted directly from a record, set against a deep, sustained bassline and a drum pattern built for low-end weight rather than rhythmic density.

The musical lineage of this sound ran directly back to the 1970s funk of George Clinton's Parliament-Funkadelic collective, whose synthesizer textures, vocal chants, and bass-heavy grooves had already shaped West Coast rap in earlier records, including work by Above the Law and the South Central duo DJ Quik had been developing in parallel. What Dre did on The Chronic was formalize and refine that influence into a coherent, repeatable production language, one distinctive enough that critics and fans alike soon needed a name for it. The term that stuck, G-funk, captured both halves of the formula: the funk source material and the gangsta subject matter layered over it.

The legal environment of the early 1990s shaped this production choice as much as any purely aesthetic preference did. A 1991 federal court ruling in a sampling dispute involving the rapper Biz Markie had established, in stark terms, that unlicensed sampling exposed labels to serious legal and financial liability, and the rap industry as a whole spent the years immediately following that decision adjusting its production practices accordingly. Rather than looping a Parliament-Funkadelic record directly and absorbing the cost and risk of clearing the sample, Dre frequently had musicians replay the relevant bassline, synthesizer figure, or guitar lick in the studio, a process known in the industry as interpolation. This reduced clearance costs and legal exposure while preserving the sonic character that made the source material valuable, and it gave G-funk a slightly cleaner, more deliberately arranged quality than the sample-collage records that had preceded it. The technique would become a standard part of hip-hop production practice well beyond Death Row's catalog.

The Chronic also introduced national audiences to a twenty-one-year-old rapper from Long Beach, California, whose unhurried, melodically loose vocal style would become as central to G-funk's identity as Dre's production. That rapper, performing under the name Snoop Doggy Dogg, appeared throughout the album, most prominently on the single "Nuthin' but a 'G' Thang," which became an inescapable presence on radio and MTV through the first half of 1993 and introduced a vocal cadence, drawn out, conversational, built around internal rhyme delivered without apparent strain, that a generation of West Coast and eventually national rappers would study and imitate. The Chronic entered the upper tier of the Billboard 200 within weeks of release and remained on the chart for well over a year, eventually selling several million copies. It established Death Row, in the span of a single record, as a commercial and creative force the rest of the industry could not ignore.

The Sound and the Catalog

Snoop Doggy Dogg and the Sound of Doggystyle

Calvin Broadus had grown up in Long Beach, a port city south of Los Angeles with its own distinct gang geography and its own, somewhat separate, hip-hop scene from the one centered in Compton. He had been performing locally and recording demo tapes with a small circle of collaborators, among them Warren G, a rapper and producer who happened to be Dr. Dre's stepbrother. It was Warren G, by most accounts of the period, who first brought Snoop's tape to Dre's attention, leading to Snoop's appearance throughout The Chronic and setting up what became, by late 1993, one of the most anticipated debut albums in the genre's history.

Doggystyle, released in November 1993, continued and deepened the G-funk formula Dre had established on his own album, building entire tracks around Snoop's voice in a way The Chronic, as a producer's showcase featuring many guests, had not been structured to do. The album debuted at number one on the Billboard 200, the first time a hip-hop artist's debut album had opened at the top of the chart, and it sold several hundred thousand copies in its first week, a figure without real precedent for a new rap artist at the time. The achievement confirmed what The Chronic's success had already suggested: that gangsta rap, far from being a niche or regional phenomenon, commanded a national audience large enough to compete with any other genre of American popular music for chart position.

The album's release was shadowed by a serious legal matter that had begun months earlier, when Snoop and his bodyguard, McKinley Lee, were charged in connection with the shooting death of a young man named Philip Woldemariam in a Los Angeles park. The case proceeded through 1994 and into 1995 even as Doggystyle climbed the charts and Snoop became one of the most visible figures in American popular culture, and it became, alongside the broader political backlash against gangsta rap discussed in the previous chapter, a central exhibit for critics who argued that the genre glamorized the violence its artists had lived through. Both Snoop and Lee were ultimately acquitted in 1996, the jury accepting a self-defense argument, but the trial's two-year run alongside the album's commercial triumph illustrated, in unusually stark terms, the central paradox of mid-1990s gangsta rap: the genre's commercial appeal and its surrounding controversy were not separate phenomena working against each other. They were, to a significant degree, feeding the same audience's fascination with the same material.

Gangsta rap's mainstream success was never a story of controversy being overcome. It was a story of controversy becoming, for a brief and historically specific window, a reliable engine of sales.

On the commercial logic of the G-funk era
Business and Organization

An Empire Built In-House

Death Row's commercial dominance through the middle of the decade rested on more than two breakout albums. The label built a tightly integrated roster and production system that allowed it to release a steady stream of records sharing the same musical identity, the same in-house producers, and overlapping casts of guest performers across nearly every project. Tha Dogg Pound, the duo of Daz Dillinger and Kurupt, anchored much of the label's second tier of releases. The singer Nate Dogg, whose smoothed, melodic hooks became a fixture of West Coast rap across the decade and well beyond it, appeared on records by Dre, Snoop, Warren G, and numerous other artists both inside and outside Death Row's roster. Lesser-known but musically significant contributors, including the rapper RBX and the vocalist Daz collaborator Lady of Rage, filled out a catalog whose internal consistency made Death Row records instantly recognizable on the radio, often within the first few bars.

This in-house approach gave the label a degree of creative coherence that few rap companies before it had achieved, and it allowed Death Row to function, for several years, almost as a self-contained production house, generating hit singles, guest appearances, and full-length albums from a relatively small and stable group of collaborators working primarily out of Dre's studio sessions. The arrangement also concentrated an enormous amount of creative and financial control in very few hands, principally Suge Knight's. Knight's management of the label's business affairs became, almost as quickly as the label's music did, a subject of national attention. Multiple artists, producers, and industry figures who dealt with Death Row during these years later described a business culture defined by aggressive, sometimes openly intimidating negotiating tactics, and Knight's personal reputation for using physical presence and implied threat as a negotiating tool became, by the middle of the decade, nearly as well known in entertainment industry circles as the label's music was to the record-buying public.

That reputation did not, in the short term, slow the label's commercial momentum. If anything, Death Row's mystique, the sense that the label operated by its own rules and answered to no one, became part of its appeal to an audience that had embraced gangsta rap precisely because of its outlaw posture. In the longer term, however, the same culture of control and confrontation would prove corrosive to the label's stability, contributing directly to the conflicts and departures that unraveled Death Row within a few years of its commercial peak, a story this book takes up fully in its chapter on the rivalry era's aftermath.

Reception and Reach

Crossover, Controversy, and the Reach of Radio

G-funk's commercial breakthrough depended heavily on a media environment that, by the early 1990s, had become considerably more receptive to rap than it had been even five years earlier. Top 40 and urban contemporary radio stations, which had been cautious about playing explicit gangsta rap records, found in G-funk a sound smooth and melodic enough to program alongside conventional R&B and pop, provided the lyrics were edited for broadcast. Record labels became increasingly sophisticated at producing radio edits and instrumental versions that allowed a song's hook and groove to circulate widely even where its full lyrical content could not. MTV, which had been a comparatively late and cautious adopter of rap programming earlier in the genre's history, gave heavy rotation to videos from The Chronic and Doggystyle, introducing the visual vocabulary of Death Row's catalog, lowriders, palm trees, backyard barbecues, the loose, sun-bleached imagery of Southern California street life, to a national and increasingly suburban audience far removed from Compton or Long Beach.

This crossover success arrived alongside an intensifying political backlash. The activist C. Delores Tucker, a former Pennsylvania secretary of state who had become one of gangsta rap's most prominent national critics, led a sustained campaign against the genre's lyrical content, directing particular pressure at Time Warner over its minority ownership stake in Interscope Records and, by extension, its indirect financial relationship to Death Row's catalog. That pressure, combined with shareholder concerns about reputational risk, contributed to Time Warner's 1995 decision to sell its stake in Interscope, a move that generated extensive press coverage and reinforced gangsta rap's position at the center of a broader national argument over lyrical content, free expression, and corporate responsibility, an argument this book examines more fully in its later chapter on censorship and public debate.

None of this controversy meaningfully slowed Death Row's sales. By 1995, the label was one of the most commercially successful enterprises in American recorded music, and Suge Knight extended its reach further that year by posting bail, reported at roughly 1.4 million dollars, to secure the release of the incarcerated rapper Tupac Shakur in exchange for Tupac signing to Death Row and recording for the label. That arrangement, which brought one of the era's most famous and most volatile artists directly into Death Row's orbit, set in motion the events covered in this book's chapters on the East Coast-West Coast conflict and its tragic aftermath. By the time those events unfolded, however, Death Row had already accomplished something of lasting historical significance independent of the rivalry that would follow: it had proven, conclusively and at enormous commercial scale, that gangsta rap was not a passing controversy at the margins of the music industry but a permanent and central part of it.

Key Figures and Institutions

The People Who Built the Label

Death Row's rise cannot be told as the story of a single genius producer or a single charismatic executive, though both figures mattered enormously. It was the product of a specific small group of collaborators, musicians, and businesspeople whose individual talents and individual flaws together produced one of the most commercially successful and organizationally volatile companies in the history of American recorded music.

Dr. Dre and the Architecture of G-Funk

Dre's significance to this period of hip-hop history rests less on any single performance than on a body of production decisions that, taken together, reorganized the sonic expectations of mainstream rap. His command of arrangement, his insistence on live instrumentation alongside electronic programming, and his exacting standards in the mixing process gave Death Row's catalog a sonic polish that distinguished it from both the rawer East Coast production of the period and from the rougher independent West Coast records that had preceded it. That polish was not incidental to the music's commercial success. It made the records sound expensive, and it made them sound, crucially, like they belonged on the same radio stations and the same record store shelves as the era's biggest pop and R&B releases, even when their lyrical content remained explicitly tied to gang life, violence, and street commerce. Dre's influence would extend far beyond Death Row itself; his subsequent career as the founder of Aftermath Entertainment and as a mentor to a new generation of producers and artists is a thread this book picks up in later chapters covering the late 1990s and the 2000s.

Suge Knight and the Business of Intimidation

Knight's role in Death Row's history resists easy characterization. He was, by any measure, an extraordinarily effective executive in narrowly commercial terms, capable of securing favorable contracts, commanding loyalty from his roster, and building an organization that generated sustained profitability during a period when many rap labels struggled to translate cultural relevance into financial stability. He was also, by the consistent testimony of numerous artists, producers, and industry figures who worked with or against him, an executive whose methods relied substantially on physical intimidation and implied threat, methods that several scholars and journalists covering the period have argued were themselves drawn from, and reinforced, the gang-affiliated street culture that much of Death Row's music depicted. That combination, commercial brilliance paired with an organizational culture built on fear, would prove unsustainable. The same tactics that helped build Death Row's early dominance generated the internal instability, legal exposure, and external conflict that would unravel the label within a few years of its commercial peak, a development examined in this book's later chapter on the rivalry era's collapse.

Snoop Doggy Dogg and the Voice of Long Beach

If Dre supplied G-funk's instrumental architecture, Snoop supplied its voice, and the importance of that voice to the genre's success is difficult to overstate. Snoop's delivery favored a relaxed, almost melodic cadence built around extended internal rhyme schemes delivered without apparent effort, a marked contrast to the more clipped, aggressive vocal styles that had characterized much of the gangsta rap that preceded him. That delivery made violent and frequently explicit subject matter sound, to many listeners, less menacing than it might otherwise have, an effect that almost certainly contributed to the music's crossover appeal even as critics like C. Delores Tucker argued that the smoothness of the presentation made the content more dangerous, not less, by rendering it more palatable to young listeners. Snoop's subsequent decades-long career as one of the most recognizable and commercially durable figures in American popular culture, a trajectory well beyond the scope of this chapter, began with the specific vocal innovations he brought to Dre's productions in 1992 and 1993.

Historical Significance

What Death Row Changed

The significance of this period extends well beyond the commercial success of two albums, however historic that success was at the time. Death Row's rise reorganized several structural features of the rap industry that persisted long after the label's own collapse. The Death Row and Interscope joint venture model, in which a major label provided distribution and marketing capital while the originating label retained ownership of masters and creative control, became an influential template that other major labels studied closely and, in various forms, adopted in their own dealings with independent rap labels through the rest of the decade. It offered a middle path between full major label signing, which typically stripped artists and independent labels of ownership and control, and pure independence, which limited a label's ability to reach national retail and radio infrastructure.

The shift toward interpolation rather than direct sampling, driven initially by the legal pressures following the 1991 Biz Markie ruling, also had lasting consequences for how rap records were made. Dre's approach demonstrated that a producer could achieve the harmonic and textural richness of classic funk and soul without the legal exposure of uncleared sampling, by hiring musicians to replay the relevant parts. This practice spread well beyond Death Row's catalog over the following years, becoming a standard production option alongside sample clearance and original composition, and it gave rap production a path forward at a moment when the legal environment around sampling had become considerably more hostile than it had been during the genre's first decade.

Perhaps most significantly, Death Row's commercial success settled, in purely financial terms, an argument that the previous several chapters of this book have traced through controversy after controversy: whether gangsta rap could sustain itself as a permanent, central part of the American music industry or whether it would remain a regional and periodically controversial subgenre, profitable in bursts but never fully legitimized by the industry's mainstream institutions. By 1994, with two of the best-selling albums in the country built almost entirely around explicit depictions of gang life, drug commerce, and street violence, that question had been answered. Gangsta rap was not a phase the industry was passing through. It had become one of the industry's most reliable sources of revenue, and every major label in the country adjusted its strategy accordingly.

Legacy and Continuity

After the Mainstream Breakthrough

G-funk's influence did not remain confined to Los Angeles and Long Beach. Through the middle of the 1990s, producers in other regions, particularly in the Midwest and the South, absorbed elements of the sound, its synthesizer textures, its slowed tempos, its emphasis on melodic hooks layered over funk-derived grooves, and adapted them to local production traditions, a process this book traces in detail in its chapters on Southern commercial power and the rise of Atlanta as a hip-hop capital. The sung hook, popularized nationally through Nate Dogg's appearances across Death Row's catalog, became a standard structural element of mainstream rap songs well beyond the 1990s, persisting through the melodic and Auto-Tuned rap styles that would emerge more than a decade later.

Death Row itself did not sustain its dominance for long. The organizational culture that had helped the label rise so quickly, built around Suge Knight's centralized and often coercive control, generated escalating internal conflict and external entanglements through 1995 and 1996, entanglements that intersected directly with the East Coast-West Coast rivalry and the deaths of Tupac Shakur and The Notorious B.I.G., events this book treats in full in its chapters on that period. Dr. Dre left Death Row in 1996 to found Aftermath Entertainment, taking with him the production sensibility that had defined the label's sound and applying it, over the following years, to a new roster of artists that would extend his influence well into the next decade. Snoop Doggy Dogg departed not long after, beginning a recording and public career that would prove, across the following three decades, to be one of the most durable and adaptable in American popular music. Suge Knight remained associated with the Death Row name through a long series of subsequent legal and financial troubles that progressively diminished the label's standing, until the company that had once dominated the Billboard charts existed largely as a cautionary case study in industry retrospectives.

What survived, regardless of the label's later decline, was the sound and the commercial proof of concept. G-funk's synthesizer textures and unhurried tempos remain audible in West Coast production for decades after Death Row's commercial peak, and the broader lesson of the period, that explicit, regionally specific street narrative could be packaged into smooth, radio-friendly production and sold at a national scale, shaped the strategic thinking of record labels working with rap artists from every region of the country for years afterward. The Southern and Midwest scenes covered in the chapters that follow inherited not only specific musical techniques from this period but a commercial template: that a regional sound, marketed correctly and distributed through the right partnerships, could become the dominant sound of the moment.

Chapter Summary

Conclusion

This chapter has traced how a contract dispute at Ruthless Records, a new label built on an unusually centralized and confrontational management style, and a specific set of production choices grounded in 1970s funk together produced one of the most commercially significant developments in the history of American rap music. Dr. Dre's departure from Ruthless and his partnership with Suge Knight created Death Row Records in 1991. The joint venture between Death Row and Interscope, finalized the following year, gave the label major label reach while preserving its creative independence. And the release of The Chronic in December 1992, followed a year later by Doggystyle, introduced a sound, slow, melodic, built from interpolated funk rather than dense sample collage, and a voice, Snoop Doggy Dogg's unhurried cadence, that together carried gangsta rap from regional controversy into the unambiguous commercial center of American popular music.

That achievement came bound up with the same controversies that had defined gangsta rap's earlier history: a murder trial that shadowed Snoop's rise to stardom, a political campaign that pressured Time Warner into divesting from Interscope, and an organizational culture inside Death Row itself that mixed extraordinary commercial success with a pattern of intimidation that would, within a few years, contribute to the label's own unraveling. The chapters that follow turn to the Southern scenes that would absorb G-funk's commercial lessons and develop their own regional sound, and then to the East Coast revival that would set the stage for the rivalry, the tragedy, and the national consolidation that closed out hip-hop's first quarter century.

A Note on Sources

The history of Death Row Records and the G-funk era draws on a substantial body of music journalism and subsequent historical scholarship. Ronin Ro's Have Gun Will Travel: The Spectacular Rise and Violent Fall of Death Row Records (1998) remains the most detailed contemporaneous account of the label's founding and business culture, though its sourcing on some disputed events has itself been the subject of later scrutiny. Dan Charnas's The Big Payback: The History of the Business of Hip-Hop (2010) situates Death Row's joint venture with Interscope within the broader industry history of major label and independent label relationships. Jeff Chang's Can't Stop Won't Stop (2005) and Brian Coleman's interview-based oral histories provide additional context on the period's musical and cultural developments, and contemporaneous reporting in The Source and Billboard documents the chart performance and radio reception discussed in this chapter.

Several events described in this chapter, including the precise circumstances of Death Row's founding capital and the details of Suge Knight's negotiations with Ruthless Records, remain points of historical dispute. Where sourcing conflicts or relies substantially on secondhand industry accounts, this chapter has attempted to flag that uncertainty rather than present a single contested version as settled fact.

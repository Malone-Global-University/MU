Part I — Migration, Labor, and Early Chinese America

Race, Violence, and the Anti-Chinese Movement

The Making of Chinatown: Chinese Immigration, Exclusion, and Community Power in America

Chapter Opening

In October 1871, a mob numbering in the hundreds descended on the small Chinese quarter of Los Angeles, dragged Chinese residents from their homes and places of business, and killed at least seventeen of them in a single night — one of the deadliest incidents of racial violence in nineteenth-century Los Angeles, and one episode in a much longer pattern this chapter traces.

Chapter 2 examined how Chinese labor became indispensable to the economic development of the American West — building the railroad, reclaiming farmland, staffing canneries, laundries, and workshops — and how that very indispensability generated organized political opposition among white workers who framed Chinese labor as an existential threat to their livelihoods. This chapter follows that opposition as it moved beyond the specific grievances of craft unions and railroad workers into a broader, more diffuse, and increasingly violent movement: a movement that drew on economic anxiety but that cannot be reduced to economics alone, because it drew just as heavily on ideas about race, civilization, gender, disease, and national belonging that had little direct connection to any particular labor dispute.

This chapter asks students to examine anti-Chinese politics and violence as a coherent historical phenomenon with its own internal logic, chronology, and consequences — not simply as background noise preceding the federal exclusion law examined in Chapter 5, but as a subject worthy of study in its own right. The anti-Chinese movement built formal political organizations, contested and won local and state elections, shaped major party platforms, and normalized forms of mob violence, arson, and forced expulsion that killed an unknown number of Chinese immigrants and drove thousands more from the towns and camps where they had built homes, businesses, and communities. Understanding how this movement developed — who built it, what ideas it deployed, and how it operated at the local level — is essential to understanding why the federal government would ultimately respond with the most comprehensive immigration restriction law in American history to that point.

The chapter is organized around four interlocking dimensions of this movement. It begins with the political organizations and campaigns through which anti-Chinese sentiment was built into an organized force capable of shaping law and policy. It then turns to the violence — mob attacks, arson, and forced removals — that accompanied and reinforced this political organizing, particularly in mining camps and smaller cities where local government was often complicit or simply absent. The chapter then examines the racialized stereotypes — concerning disease, vice, and gender — that gave anti-Chinese politics its ideological content and made Chinese immigrants legible to white Americans as a categorical threat rather than as individual workers, neighbors, or competitors. Finally, the chapter turns to Chinese communities' own responses: the petitions, lawsuits, diplomatic appeals, and merchant-led advocacy through which Chinese immigrants and their leaders resisted, wherever possible, the politics and violence examined in the chapter's earlier sections.

Holding these four dimensions together is essential to understanding this period accurately. Anti-Chinese politics was not simply spontaneous violence, nor was it simply cynical political calculation, nor was it simply inherited racial prejudice operating independent of material conditions. It was all of these things interacting with and reinforcing one another — economic anxiety providing energy that racial ideology channeled into an organized political movement, and that movement providing cover and, at times, active encouragement for violence that swept up individuals who may have had no direct economic stake in anti-Chinese politics at all. This chapter's task is to trace how that combination formed, spread, and, ultimately, prepared the ground for the federal exclusion regime examined later in this textbook.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the rise of organized anti-Chinese political clubs and parties in California during the 1870s, and explain their relationship to the labor politics examined in Chapter 2.
  2. Explain how local political campaigns translated anti-Chinese sentiment into electoral success and policy outcomes at the municipal and state level.
  3. Analyze patterns of mob violence, arson, and forced removal directed against Chinese communities in mining camps and cities, and assess the role of local government complicity and police neglect.
  4. Identify the major racialized stereotypes — concerning disease, vice, and gender — through which Chinese immigrants were represented in nineteenth-century American political and popular discourse.
  5. Evaluate how these stereotypes functioned ideologically, distinguishing their political and rhetorical purposes from the actual conditions of Chinese immigrant life examined in earlier chapters.
  6. Describe the strategies — petitions, lawsuits, diplomatic appeals, and merchant leadership — through which Chinese communities resisted anti-Chinese politics and violence.
  7. Assess competing historical interpretations of what primarily drove the anti-Chinese movement: economic competition, racial ideology, or a combination of both operating together.
  8. Connect the political organizing and violence examined in this chapter to the gendered and federal exclusion laws examined in Chapters 4 and 5.
  9. Interpret primary sources such as political cartoons and campaign speeches as historical evidence of how anti-Chinese ideology was constructed and disseminated.
  10. Explain how the pattern of economic reliance followed by racialized backlash, traced in Chapters 1 and 2, escalated in this chapter into organized political movements and physical violence.

Historical Context

The anti-Chinese movement that this chapter examines did not emerge suddenly in the 1870s. As Chapters 1 and 2 have shown, anti-Chinese sentiment and discriminatory policy had accompanied Chinese immigration to California from its earliest years, expressed through the Foreign Miners' License Tax of the early 1850s, informal exclusion from many of the state's most productive mining claims, and the localized labor politics that intensified as Chinese workers moved into railroad construction, agriculture, and manufacturing during the 1860s. What changed over the course of the 1870s was scale and organization: anti-Chinese sentiment, previously expressed largely through local, often informal mechanisms, became the basis for sustained statewide and eventually national political mobilization.

Several developments converged to produce this shift. The completion of the transcontinental railroad in 1869, examined in Chapter 2, released a large Chinese workforce into an already contracting California labor market, intensifying competition for employment across multiple industries at precisely the moment that a national economic downturn — the Panic of 1873 and the prolonged depression that followed — sharply reduced the number of jobs available to workers of all backgrounds. This combination of contracting opportunity and heightened competition provided fertile ground for political organizers who argued that Chinese labor, rather than broader economic conditions, was principally responsible for white workers' economic distress.

California's political system, particularly its loosely regulated system of local and municipal politics, provided an accessible venue for this organizing. Anti-Chinese clubs, often organized initially around specific trades or neighborhoods, proliferated across San Francisco and other California cities during the early and middle 1870s, coordinating boycotts of Chinese-made goods and Chinese-operated businesses, lobbying municipal governments for discriminatory ordinances, and building the organizational infrastructure that would culminate, by the end of the decade, in explicitly anti-Chinese political parties capable of contesting and winning state elections. This organizing occurred alongside, and was reinforced by, a wave of mob violence against Chinese communities that swept mining camps, small cities, and, on occasion, larger urban centers throughout the decade — violence that this chapter's second section examines in detail, and that frequently proceeded with minimal intervention, and at times active participation, from local law enforcement and government officials.

The ideological content of this movement drew on a set of racialized stereotypes that had circulated in American popular culture since Chinese immigration began, but that anti-Chinese political organizing systematized and amplified during the 1870s into a coherent, politically weaponized ideology. Stereotypes concerning disease framed Chinese immigrants and Chinese neighborhoods as inherent public health threats, often invoked to justify municipal health ordinances that singled out Chinese residents and businesses for inspection, quarantine, or closure. Stereotypes concerning vice associated Chinese communities with gambling, opium use, and prostitution, frequently exaggerating the scale of these activities and ignoring their presence among non-Chinese Californians, in order to portray Chinese immigration as a moral as well as an economic threat. Stereotypes concerning gender, intertwined with the Page Act examined in Chapter 4, cast Chinese women in particular as agents of vice and Chinese men as a threat to white womanhood and to conventional gender and family norms, reinforcing the broader case for exclusion on grounds that extended well beyond labor competition alone.

Chinese communities did not respond to this escalating political and physical assault passively. The merchant leadership and district associations examined in greater detail in Chapter 8 organized petitions to municipal, state, and federal officials protesting discriminatory legislation and violence; pursued lawsuits challenging discriminatory ordinances, several of which reached the United States Supreme Court in the following decades; and worked through Chinese diplomatic representatives to press the Chinese government to raise formal protests with American officials regarding the treatment of Chinese nationals. These efforts achieved only limited success against a political movement with such broad popular and institutional support, but they represent an important and often underexamined dimension of Chinese American history in this period — one that this chapter's final section addresses directly, and that recurs throughout the rest of this textbook as Chinese communities continued to develop strategies of legal and political resistance under increasingly adverse conditions.

Section 3.1

The Rise of Anti-Chinese Politics

White Labor Resentment

As Chapter 2 examined, the wage competition narratives and craft union organizing of the 1870s provided much of the initial energy behind organized anti-Chinese politics. The dispersal of the Central Pacific Railroad's large Chinese workforce after 1869, combined with the sharp national economic contraction that followed the Panic of 1873, intensified competition for a shrinking pool of jobs across California's economy at precisely the moment when white workers were most economically vulnerable and most receptive to arguments that Chinese labor, rather than broader structural conditions, bore primary responsibility for their distress. This resentment, genuine in its economic origins even where its political expression became increasingly disconnected from the specific facts of Chinese labor conditions, supplied anti-Chinese organizers with a durable base of popular grievance to build upon.

Anti-Chinese Clubs

Beginning in the early 1870s and accelerating through the middle of the decade, anti-Chinese clubs proliferated across San Francisco and other California cities, typically organized initially around specific trades — cigar-making, boot and shoe manufacturing, and similar crafts where Chinese labor competition was most directly felt — before expanding into broader neighborhood- and citywide organizations. These clubs coordinated boycotts of Chinese-made goods and Chinese-operated businesses, circulated petitions demanding restrictive legislation, and organized public meetings and rallies at which anti-Chinese speakers combined economic grievance with increasingly explicit racial rhetoric. The organizational infrastructure these clubs built — membership networks, meeting halls, and experience coordinating collective public action — would prove directly transferable to the more formal party politics that emerged later in the decade.

Political Parties and Local Campaigns

By the late 1870s, the anti-Chinese club movement had matured into explicitly anti-Chinese political parties capable of contesting and, in some cases, winning local and state elections. The Workingmen's Party of California, which emerged from San Francisco labor politics in 1877, represented the fullest expression of this development, uniting labor organizing, economic populism, and racial exclusion into a coherent political platform under the widely circulated slogan "The Chinese Must Go." The party's rapid rise — including significant success in San Francisco municipal elections and influence over California's 1879 state constitutional convention — demonstrated the extent to which anti-Chinese politics had moved from marginal agitation to a central and electorally consequential feature of California political life.

Racial Scapegoating

Central to this political mobilization was a consistent pattern of racial scapegoating, in which broader economic and social anxieties — unemployment, wage stagnation, urban crowding, and public health concerns among them — were attributed disproportionately or exclusively to Chinese immigration, even where the underlying conditions had causes extending well beyond, or entirely independent of, Chinese labor or Chinese communities. This scapegoating served a specific political function: it offered a clear, racially defined target around which diverse grievances could be unified into a single political movement, and it displaced attention from the structural economic conditions — including the national depression following the Panic of 1873 — that anti-Chinese political leaders had little capacity to address directly.

Historical Vocabulary
Nativism

Nativism refers to a political orientation that favors the interests of established or "native" inhabitants of a nation over those of immigrants, often expressed through hostility toward specific immigrant groups perceived as culturally, racially, or economically threatening. In the American context, nativist movements have recurred across multiple historical periods, targeting different immigrant populations under varying ideological justifications.

The anti-Chinese movement examined in this chapter represents one of the most consequential nativist movements in American history, distinguished both by the scale of political organization it achieved and by the fact that it culminated in comprehensive federal legislation — the Chinese Exclusion Act examined in Chapter 5 — that would remain in force for over sixty years. Historians identify the anti-Chinese movement's nativism as combining economic grievance, examined throughout this chapter's early sections, with the racialized stereotypes concerning disease, vice, and gender examined in Section 3.3, producing an unusually durable and politically effective form of nativist mobilization.

Understanding nativism as a recurring feature of American political history, rather than as a phenomenon unique to Chinese immigration, helps situate the anti-Chinese movement within a broader comparative context that later chapters of this textbook, particularly Chapter 14's discussion of public memory, revisit directly.

Section 3.2

Violence in Mining Camps and Cities

Mob Attacks

Organized political agitation against Chinese immigrants was accompanied, throughout the 1870s and into the 1880s, by a sustained pattern of mob violence directed against Chinese communities across California and the broader American West. Mining camps and smaller cities, where Chinese residents were often numerically significant but politically powerless and where local law enforcement was frequently thin, sympathetic to anti-Chinese agitation, or altogether absent, proved especially vulnerable to this violence. The October 1871 attack on Los Angeles's small Chinese quarter, in which a mob numbering in the hundreds killed at least seventeen Chinese residents in a single night, stands as one of the deadliest single incidents of racial violence in nineteenth-century American history and illustrates the scale of lethal violence anti-Chinese mobs proved capable of organizing even outside the mining camps most commonly associated with this period's anti-Chinese violence.

Arson

Arson was a recurring tactic through which anti-Chinese mobs sought to destroy Chinese-occupied buildings and, in some cases, entire Chinese quarters, frequently under circumstances in which local fire departments either failed to respond promptly or actively declined to extinguish fires understood to be targeting Chinese property. This tactic served both a destructive and a coercive function: it inflicted direct economic loss on Chinese residents and business owners, and it communicated, often unmistakably, that continued Chinese presence in a given town or neighborhood would not be tolerated, reinforcing the forced removals examined below and anticipating the more organized expulsion campaigns, including the Tacoma Method, that Chapter 6 examines in detail.

Forced Removals

Beyond individual acts of mob violence and arson, numerous California and western towns during this period experienced organized campaigns to forcibly remove their entire Chinese population, often orchestrated by local anti-Chinese clubs or committees operating with minimal resistance from municipal authorities. These removal campaigns varied in their specific mechanisms — some relying primarily on the threat or reality of violence, others on more bureaucratic tools such as selective enforcement of municipal ordinances — but shared a common goal of achieving what anti-Chinese organizers frequently termed a town's complete "riddance" of its Chinese residents. This chapter's discussion of forced removal previews the more extensively documented and more thoroughly organized expulsion campaigns of the middle 1880s, including the Rock Springs Massacre and the Tacoma Method, examined in full in Chapter 6.

Police Neglect

A recurring feature of the violence this section examines is the frequent failure of local law enforcement either to prevent anti-Chinese violence or to prosecute its perpetrators after the fact. In numerous documented instances, local police forces failed to intervene during mob attacks already in progress, and prosecutors and juries proved reluctant to pursue meaningful legal consequences against participants in anti-Chinese violence, reflecting both the broader popularity of anti-Chinese sentiment among the white electorate from which juries were drawn and the practical reality that Chinese immigrants, barred from testifying against white defendants in many California courts during earlier decades and generally excluded from full legal standing and political representation, had limited capacity to compel more vigorous law enforcement response. This pattern of impunity, examined further in this chapter's primary source materials, is essential to understanding both the scale and the persistence of anti-Chinese violence across this period.

Historical Vocabulary
Racial Scapegoating

Racial scapegoating describes the political and social process by which a racialized minority group is blamed for broader social or economic problems disproportionately to, or entirely independent of, that group's actual role in causing those problems. In the context of the anti-Chinese movement, racial scapegoating operated by attributing wage stagnation, unemployment, urban crowding, and public health concerns to Chinese immigration, even where these conditions had causes — including the national economic depression following the Panic of 1873 — extending well beyond any specific Chinese economic activity.

Historians regard racial scapegoating as functioning, in this period as in others, to unify diverse and sometimes unrelated popular grievances around a single, racially defined target, thereby building broader political coalitions than narrower, more specific economic complaints could achieve on their own. The effectiveness of this scapegoating in the anti-Chinese movement depended substantially on the racialized stereotypes examined in Section 3.3, which provided the specific ideological content — concerning disease, vice, and gender — that made Chinese immigrants legible to much of the white public as a categorical threat rather than as individual economic competitors.

Section 3.3

The Racialization of Chinese Immigrants

Disease Stereotypes

Anti-Chinese rhetoric of the 1870s and beyond routinely characterized Chinese immigrants and Chinese neighborhoods as inherent sources of disease and public health danger, a framing that municipal governments, particularly in San Francisco, translated into discriminatory health ordinances subjecting Chinese residences and businesses to inspection, quarantine, and closure at rates disproportionate to actual public health conditions. This disease framing served a specific rhetorical function distinct from, though reinforcing, the labor competition narratives examined in Chapter 2: it recast Chinese immigration as a danger to the physical safety and health of the broader population, rather than merely an economic threat to specific trades, and it provided a public health rationale that municipal officials could invoke even where explicit racial discrimination might have faced greater legal or political resistance.

Vice Stereotypes

Chinese communities, particularly Chinatown neighborhoods in San Francisco and other cities, were also persistently associated in anti-Chinese and broader popular discourse with gambling, opium use, and prostitution, an association that anti-Chinese political rhetoric exaggerated in scale and frequently detached from the presence of these same activities among non-Chinese Californians. This vice framing, examined further in Chapter 11's discussion of the vice economies that did develop within Chinatown neighborhoods under the pressures of exclusion and bachelor-society demographics, functioned during the 1870s primarily as a moral rather than economic argument for restriction, casting Chinese immigration as a threat to public morality alongside its supposed threats to public health and white employment.

Gender Stereotypes

Gender occupied a distinctive and especially consequential place within the racialized ideology this section examines. Chinese women, present in far smaller numbers than Chinese men as a direct consequence of the migration patterns Chapter 1 examined and the bachelor-society demographics Chapter 4 explores in detail, were frequently characterized in anti-Chinese rhetoric and in immigration enforcement practice as presumptively engaged in prostitution, a characterization that would find direct legislative expression in the Page Act of 1875 examined in the following chapter. Chinese men, conversely, were frequently cast as a sexual and social threat to white women and to conventional American gender norms, a framing that drew on, and reinforced, broader American anxieties about racial mixing and the boundaries of white domestic and social life. These gendered stereotypes, though analytically distinguishable from the disease and vice stereotypes examined above, operated together to construct a comprehensive image of Chinese immigration as a threat extending into the most intimate dimensions of American domestic and social life.

Legal and Social Exclusion

The racialized stereotypes this section has examined translated, over the course of the 1870s, into both formal legal exclusion — including the municipal ordinances discussed above and the state and federal legislation examined in Part II of this textbook — and informal social exclusion that limited Chinese immigrants' access to housing, employment, public accommodations, and legal protection well beyond what any specific statute required. This combination of formal and informal exclusion is essential to understanding the urban enclosure that Chapter 7 examines as central to Chinatown's formation: Chinese immigrants concentrated in defined urban neighborhoods not only because of the mutual aid and cultural familiarity such neighborhoods provided, but because the racialized exclusion this chapter has traced left few alternatives.

Multiple Perspectives

The Anti-Chinese Movement of the 1870s: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of white workers and labor organizers: Many participants in anti-Chinese political organizing understood their activity as a legitimate defense of white workers' economic livelihood during a period of severe national economic depression, framing Chinese labor competition as an unfair threat that legitimate political organizing was entitled, and indeed obligated, to resist.
From the perspective of Chinese immigrants and community leaders: Chinese residents experienced the developments this chapter examines as an escalating and increasingly organized threat to their safety, livelihoods, and legal standing, one that merchant and association leaders sought to address through the petitions, lawsuits, and diplomatic appeals examined in Section 3.4, even as the scale and popularity of anti-Chinese sentiment left these efforts with limited practical effect.
From the perspective of California municipal and state officials: Many elected officials, whatever their private views, found significant political advantage in accommodating or actively supporting anti-Chinese sentiment, given its broad popularity among the white electorate, and municipal ordinances targeting Chinese neighborhoods on ostensible public health or vice grounds often reflected this political calculation as much as any independent policy judgment.
From the perspective of later historians: Historians examining this period have generally concluded that the anti-Chinese movement cannot be reduced to a single cause, but instead reflected the interaction of genuine economic grievance, opportunistic political organizing, and a racialized ideology whose disease, vice, and gender stereotypes bore only a loose relationship to the actual conditions of Chinese immigrant life documented in this textbook's earlier chapters.
Section 3.4

Chinese Resistance and Legal Strategy

Community Petitions

Chinese community leaders, working substantially through the district association network examined in greater detail in Chapter 8, organized formal petitions to municipal, state, and federal officials throughout the 1870s protesting discriminatory legislation, documenting instances of mob violence, and requesting government protection for Chinese residents and property. These petitions, while rarely achieving their immediate objectives given the broader political climate this chapter has examined, created a documentary record of Chinese communities' own testimony regarding the conditions they faced — a record that later historians, including the authors of the primary source materials examined below, have found essential to reconstructing Chinese immigrants' own perspective on this period, a perspective that Chapter 2's discussion of the documentary record noted was similarly underrepresented in sources concerning railroad labor.

Lawsuits

Chinese residents and their legal representatives pursued litigation challenging discriminatory municipal ordinances and enforcement practices throughout this period, building a body of legal strategy and precedent that would culminate, in the following decades, in significant United States Supreme Court decisions, including the 1886 case Yick Wo v. Hopkins referenced in Chapter 2's discussion of laundry regulation. While the most significant of these legal victories fall chronologically after this chapter's primary period, the litigation strategy and legal organizing this section examines had its origins in the 1870s, as Chinese communities and their legal counsel developed the arguments and evidentiary approaches that later, more successful challenges would build upon.

Diplomatic Appeals

Beyond domestic legal and political channels, Chinese community leaders worked through Chinese diplomatic representatives in the United States to press the Chinese government to raise formal protests with American officials regarding the treatment of Chinese nationals, including instances of mob violence and discriminatory legislation. These diplomatic channels reflected both the transnational character of Chinese immigrant identity examined in Chapter 1 and the practical recognition among Chinese community leaders that domestic political and legal avenues alone were unlikely to secure adequate protection, given the scale of popular anti-Chinese sentiment this chapter has documented.

Merchant Leadership

Throughout the resistance strategies this section examines, merchant leadership played a consistently central organizing role, reflecting the class structure of Chinese American community life that Chapter 8 examines in greater detail. Merchants, generally possessing greater capital, English-language capacity, and standing with American officials than the laboring majority of Chinese immigrants, took primary responsibility for organizing petitions, funding legal challenges, and communicating with diplomatic representatives — a leadership role that provided crucial resources and legitimacy for Chinese community resistance during this period, even as it reflected internal class dynamics within Chinese America that Chapter 8's discussion of leadership, class, and community power addresses directly.

Cause and Effect

From Labor Politics to Organized Racial Movement: Structural Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The wage competition narratives and craft union organizing traced in Chapter 2; the persistence of racialized disease, vice, and gender stereotypes circulating in American popular culture since the earliest years of Chinese immigration.

Short-Term and Proximate Causes

The dispersal of the Central Pacific Railroad's large Chinese workforce after 1869; the Panic of 1873 and the severe national economic depression that followed; the proliferation of anti-Chinese clubs across California cities during the early and middle 1870s.

The Development

The consolidation of anti-Chinese clubs into explicitly anti-Chinese political parties, most significantly the Workingmen's Party of California; a sustained pattern of mob violence, arson, and forced removal across mining camps and cities; the systematization of racialized stereotypes into a coherent political ideology.

Early Consequences

Growing state and federal political pressure for restrictive immigration legislation; the gendered exclusion embodied in the Page Act examined in Chapter 4; the direct political groundwork for the Chinese Exclusion Act examined in Chapter 5.

Historical complexity: The anti-Chinese movement did not develop uniformly across California and the American West; the intensity of political organizing, the frequency and lethality of violence, and the effectiveness of Chinese community resistance varied considerably by locality. Understanding this variation, alongside the broader statewide and national pattern, is essential to understanding both the movement's scale and the specific vulnerabilities that shaped its most violent episodes.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living Under Threat: Chinese Community Life During the 1870s Agitation

For Chinese immigrants living in California's cities and mining camps during the 1870s, the political organizing and violence this chapter examines were not abstract developments but immediate, daily realities that shaped decisions about where to live, how to travel, which businesses to patronize, and how visibly to participate in public life. Chinese residents of smaller towns and mining camps, in particular, frequently faced the practical calculation of whether continued residence in a given location remained safe, a calculation that anti-Chinese clubs and, later, organized expulsion campaigns were specifically designed to make as difficult as possible.

Within larger Chinese neighborhoods, particularly San Francisco's Chinatown, the associational infrastructure examined in Part III of this textbook — merchant associations, district associations, and mutual aid networks — provided practical resources for navigating this hostile environment, including legal assistance, financial support for those affected by violence or discriminatory enforcement, and organized channels for petitioning officials and communicating with Chinese diplomatic representatives. This associational infrastructure, developed initially to address the practical needs of migration and labor examined in Chapters 1 and 2, took on an increasingly protective and defensive function during this period, a development that anticipated the fuller institutional response examined in Chapter 7's discussion of Chinatown as refuge.

Daily commercial and social life continued, of necessity, alongside this climate of threat: Chinese-run laundries, restaurants, and shops examined in Chapter 2 remained open, Chinese workers continued to seek employment across the industries this textbook has traced, and community institutions continued to function. But this continuity occurred under conditions of sustained uncertainty, in which the boycotts, discriminatory ordinances, and periodic outbreaks of mob violence this chapter has documented could, and frequently did, disrupt or destroy in a single day businesses and communities built over years.

The generation of Chinese immigrants who lived through the 1870s agitation examined in this chapter would go on, in many cases, to become the community leaders, association officers, and legal advocates whose work Part III of this textbook examines in detail — a continuity of experience and institutional development that connects the resistance strategies traced in this chapter's final section directly to the fuller associational world of Chinatown that emerged in the following decades.

  • Smaller California cities and towns that experienced organized anti-Chinese club activity and, in numerous documented cases, forced removal campaigns during the 1870s

Primary Source Window

Anti-Chinese Political Cartoons and Speeches

Creator: Contemporary American newspapers, political cartoonists, and anti-Chinese political organizers  ·  Dates: 1870s  ·  Type: Political cartoons, campaign speeches, newspaper editorials

Context: The political cartoons and campaign speeches produced by anti-Chinese organizers and sympathetic newspapers during the 1870s constitute some of the richest surviving evidence of how anti-Chinese ideology was constructed and disseminated to a broad public audience. These sources combined the disease, vice, and gender stereotypes examined in Section 3.3 with more directly economic imagery depicting Chinese labor as an overwhelming, dehumanized mass threatening white employment, frequently through caricature techniques common to nineteenth-century American political cartooning more broadly.

Campaign speeches associated with the Workingmen's Party of California and similar organizations combined this visual and rhetorical repertoire with more explicit political argument, framing Chinese exclusion as necessary to the defense of white working-class livelihood, American civilization, and, at times, the biological integrity of the white race. These sources reached large public audiences through mass-circulation newspapers and public rallies, making them essential evidence for understanding how anti-Chinese ideology moved from specific local grievances into the broad-based political movement this chapter has traced.

Significance and limits: Because these sources were produced by anti-Chinese organizers and sympathetic publications, they provide direct evidence of anti-Chinese ideology and rhetoric but offer little insight into Chinese immigrants' own experience or perspective, which survives primarily through the petitions and other documents examined in Section 3.4. Historians must also read these sources with attention to their persuasive intent, recognizing that their exaggerations and distortions were often deliberate rhetorical strategies rather than good-faith factual claims.

Historical Debate

What Drove the Anti-Chinese Movement? Competing Interpretations

The question: Historians have long debated the relative weight of economic and racial-ideological factors in explaining the rise and success of the anti-Chinese movement examined in this chapter — whether the movement is best understood primarily as a response to genuine economic competition and dislocation, primarily as an expression of racial ideology largely independent of economic conditions, or as a phenomenon that cannot be adequately explained by either factor in isolation.

Interpretation A — The Economic Competition Model: This interpretation emphasizes the genuine economic dislocation of the 1870s, particularly the severe national depression following the Panic of 1873, as the primary driver of anti-Chinese political mobilization, treating racialized rhetoric as largely instrumental — a vocabulary through which real economic grievance was expressed rather than an independent causal force. This interpretation draws support from the clear chronological association between the depression's onset and the acceleration of anti-Chinese organizing, and from the concentration of the earliest and most intense anti-Chinese organizing in trades, such as cigar-making, where direct labor competition was most acute.
Interpretation B — The Racial Ideology Model: This interpretation emphasizes the depth, persistence, and specific content of the racialized stereotypes examined in Section 3.3 — stereotypes concerning disease, vice, and gender that bore little direct relationship to labor competition — as evidence that anti-Chinese sentiment reflected a racial ideology with its own independent force, one that predated the 1870s economic depression and that would have found political expression regardless of specific economic conditions. This interpretation draws support from the presence of anti-Chinese sentiment and discriminatory policy well before the 1870s, examined in Chapters 1 and 2, and from the movement's continued strength even in trades and communities with minimal direct Chinese labor competition.
Interpretation C — A Synthesis: Interacting and Mutually Reinforcing Causes: A third position, increasingly favored by historians, treats economic grievance and racial ideology not as competing explanations but as mutually reinforcing elements of a single historical process: economic anxiety provided energy and popular urgency that racial ideology channeled into an organized, broadly appealing political movement, while pre-existing racial stereotypes shaped how that economic anxiety was interpreted and directed specifically against Chinese immigrants rather than toward other, non-racialized explanations for economic hardship. Under this view, neither factor alone adequately explains the movement's scale, durability, or specific ideological content.

Why it matters: How this debate is resolved shapes not only historical understanding of the anti-Chinese movement itself, but the broader interpretive frameworks through which historians and the public understand later episodes of nativist and racially exclusionary politics in American history — a connection this chapter's discussion questions and Chapter 14's treatment of public memory both invite students to consider further.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Chinese Resistance Strategies: Continuity and Change

What Changed

  • Chinese community resistance moved from the relatively informal mutual aid and camp-based cooperation examined in Chapters 1 and 2 toward more formally organized petitioning, litigation, and diplomatic engagement over the course of the 1870s
  • The scale and severity of threats facing Chinese communities escalated substantially, from localized labor competition and discriminatory taxation toward organized political movements and lethal mob violence
  • Chinese community leadership increasingly engaged formal American legal and political institutions directly, rather than relying primarily on internal community mechanisms, anticipating the fuller institutional development examined in Part III of this textbook

What Stayed the Same

  • Merchant leadership continued to play the central organizing role in Chinese community response, as it had in the labor contracting and camp organization examined in Chapters 1 and 2
  • The broader pattern, introduced in Chapter 1 and traced through Chapter 2, of economic welcome giving way to organized racialized opposition, recurred and intensified in this chapter, expanding from labor-specific grievance into a comprehensive political and ideological movement
  • Chinese communities' capacity for organized collective response, evident in the 1867 railroad strike examined in Chapter 2, persisted and developed further through the petitions, lawsuits, and diplomatic appeals this chapter has examined

Why the pattern matters: The resistance strategies this chapter identifies connect directly to the associational world examined in Part III of this textbook, whose family, district, and merchant associations would continue and expand the petitioning, legal, and mutual aid functions this chapter's Chinese community leaders began developing under the acute pressures of 1870s anti-Chinese agitation.

Structural Connection

Anti-Chinese Politics and the Coming Structure of Federal Exclusion

The organized political movement this chapter has traced — anti-Chinese clubs, the Workingmen's Party of California, and the sustained pattern of mob violence and forced removal accompanying this organizing — was the direct political precondition for the gendered and federal exclusion laws examined in Chapters 4 and 5. The racialized disease, vice, and gender stereotypes this chapter has examined would find direct legislative expression in the Page Act of 1875, which specifically targeted Chinese women through the presumption of prostitution this chapter's discussion of gender stereotypes has traced, and in the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, which drew heavily on the labor competition and racial-threat arguments this chapter's earlier sections have examined.

The Workingmen's Party of California's influence over the 1879 state constitutional convention, noted in this chapter's chronology, illustrates how thoroughly anti-Chinese politics had moved from marginal agitation into formal governmental structure by the end of the decade this chapter examines — a development that provided crucial momentum for the federal legislative campaigns examined in Part II of this textbook.

This chapter's account of anti-Chinese politics and violence, in other words, should be read not only as a discrete episode of nineteenth-century American history, but as the direct institutional and ideological bridge between the labor history examined in Chapter 2 and the formal legal architecture of exclusion that the remainder of Part II traces in full: from the gendered exclusion of the Page Act, through the federal exclusion regime of the Chinese Exclusion Act, to the organized mass violence and expulsions examined in Chapter 6.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the rise of organized anti-Chinese politics and violence in California between the early 1870s and the early 1880s, following the labor competition and craft union organizing examined in Chapter 2 as it developed into a broader, more diffuse, and increasingly violent political movement. The chapter began with the political organizations — anti-Chinese clubs and, eventually, the Workingmen's Party of California — through which this movement built its organizational and electoral infrastructure, before turning to the sustained pattern of mob violence, arson, and forced removal that accompanied and reinforced this political organizing across mining camps, small towns, and, at times, larger cities including Los Angeles and San Francisco.

The chapter then examined the racialized stereotypes concerning disease, vice, and gender that gave anti-Chinese politics its specific ideological content, distinguishing these stereotypes' rhetorical and political functions from the actual conditions of Chinese immigrant life this textbook's earlier chapters have documented. These stereotypes, this chapter has argued, functioned to recast Chinese immigration as a comprehensive threat — to public health, to public morality, and to the integrity of white domestic and social life — extending well beyond the specific labor competition narratives examined in Chapter 2, and providing crucial ideological ammunition for the political organizing this chapter's first section traced.

Finally, the chapter examined Chinese communities' own responses to this escalating threat: the petitions, lawsuits, diplomatic appeals, and merchant leadership through which Chinese immigrants and their leaders sought to resist discriminatory legislation and violence, achieving only limited success against a political movement with such broad popular and institutional support, but establishing organizational patterns and legal strategies that would continue to develop throughout the exclusion era this textbook traces.

The significance of this chapter for the larger history this textbook traces lies in its demonstration of how economic grievance and racial ideology interacted to produce a durable, broadly popular, and ultimately successful political movement. The pattern introduced in Chapter 1 and intensified in Chapter 2 — economic welcome giving way, as competition intensified, to organized racialized opposition — reached, in this chapter, a new level of organization and violence, moving from localized labor disputes and informal discrimination toward statewide political parties, systematic mob violence, and the direct legislative groundwork for the federal exclusion regime examined in Part II of this textbook.

Finally, this chapter has extended this textbook's central argument that Chinese America's history should be understood through agency and resistance as well as victimization. Even as anti-Chinese clubs, political parties, and mobs organized violence and legislation against them, Chinese community leaders organized their own sustained campaign of petitions, litigation, and diplomatic engagement — a campaign that achieved only partial success in this period, but that laid essential groundwork for the associational and legal strategies examined throughout the remainder of this textbook.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Nativism
A political orientation favoring the interests of established inhabitants of a nation over those of immigrants, expressed in this period through the organized hostility toward Chinese immigration this chapter examines.
Racial scapegoating
The process by which a racialized minority group is blamed for broader social or economic problems disproportionately to its actual role in causing them, used to unify diverse grievances against Chinese immigrants during the 1870s.
Anti-Chinese movement
The organized political movement of the 1870s and beyond that combined labor grievance, racial ideology, and political organizing to advocate for restriction and exclusion of Chinese immigration.
Mob violence
Organized or spontaneous collective violence directed against Chinese communities, including the 1871 Los Angeles Massacre, frequently occurring with minimal law enforcement intervention or prosecution.
Workingmen's Party of California
An explicitly anti-Chinese political party that emerged in San Francisco in 1877, uniting labor organizing and racial exclusion into a coherent and electorally successful political platform.
Racialization
The process by which Chinese immigrants were constructed, through disease, vice, and gender stereotypes, as a categorical racial threat rather than as individual workers, neighbors, or economic competitors.
Disease stereotype
The characterization of Chinese immigrants and neighborhoods as inherent public health threats, used to justify discriminatory municipal health ordinances during this period.
Vice stereotype
The exaggerated association of Chinese communities with gambling, opium use, and prostitution, deployed as a moral argument for restriction alongside economic and public health arguments.
Gender stereotype
The characterization of Chinese women as presumptively engaged in prostitution and Chinese men as a threat to white womanhood, which would find direct legislative expression in the Page Act examined in Chapter 4.
Merchant leadership
The central organizing role played by Chinese merchants, who possessed greater capital and standing with American officials, in coordinating petitions, legal challenges, and diplomatic appeals during this period.
Diplomatic appeal
The strategy through which Chinese community leaders worked through Chinese diplomatic representatives to press the Chinese government to formally protest the treatment of Chinese nationals in the United States.
Forced removal
Organized campaigns to expel entire Chinese populations from towns and cities, often through violence or selective enforcement of municipal ordinances, anticipating the more thoroughly organized expulsions examined in Chapter 6.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the third chapter of a long and consequential story — the story of how economic grievance, organized political mobilization, and racial ideology combined during the 1870s to produce a movement capable of shaping law, policy, and, at its most extreme, taking the lives of Chinese immigrants with near-total impunity. The labor competition examined in Chapter 2 gave way, in this chapter, to organized clubs, political parties, mob violence, and a comprehensive racialized ideology that recast Chinese immigration as a threat extending into public health, public morality, and the most intimate dimensions of American domestic life.

The Workingmen's Party of California stands, in this chapter's telling, as both the clearest single institutional expression of anti-Chinese politics' organizational success and a warning about how thoroughly economic anxiety, channeled through racial ideology, can reshape a political system's priorities and outcomes. The 1871 Los Angeles Massacre stands, similarly, as a stark reminder that this political organizing was never merely rhetorical: it accompanied, enabled, and at times directly encouraged violence that killed an unknown but substantial number of Chinese immigrants across California and the broader American West.

The resistance strategies examined at this chapter's close also matter deeply for what follows. The petitions, lawsuits, diplomatic appeals, and merchant leadership Chinese communities developed during this period were not a late or separate development from the political and physical violence this chapter traces; they emerged directly in response to it, establishing organizational patterns and legal strategies that Chinese American communities would continue to develop and rely upon throughout the exclusion era examined in the remainder of this textbook.

The final lesson of this chapter, as of the two before it, is that the people whose history it describes were not merely subjects of political and physical violence beyond their control. They were community members who organized, petitioned, litigated, and appealed to diplomatic channels in defense of their safety and their standing, even as an increasingly powerful political movement worked to deny them both. That organized resistance, and the organized hostility it responded to, together set the stage for the gendered and federal exclusion regimes that Chapters 4 and 5 now take up in full.

Part I — Migration, Labor, and Early Chinese America

Labor, Railroads, and the Building of the West

The Making of Chinatown: Chinese Immigration, Exclusion, and Community Power in America

Chapter Opening

By the time the golden spike joined the rails at Promontory Summit in May 1869, some ten to fifteen thousand Chinese laborers had cut, blasted, graded, and laid the most difficult stretch of track in the transcontinental line — and stood almost entirely absent from the photograph taken to commemorate the achievement.

Chapter 1 traced the migration of Chinese laborers to California during the Gold Rush era and the earliest settlements they established in mining camps and port cities. This chapter follows those laborers as their work expanded far beyond the diggings of the Sierra Nevada foothills — into the construction crews of the Central Pacific Railroad, the fields and canneries of California agriculture, the laundries and kitchens of its growing cities, and the factories of its emerging manufacturing economy. It is, in one sense, a chapter about economic history: about the specific industries in which Chinese labor became indispensable to the development of the American West. But it is also, and just as importantly, a chapter about the political consequences of that indispensability — about how Chinese workers' very success in meeting the West's labor needs generated the organized, racialized opposition that would culminate, within a few decades, in the most comprehensive exclusion regime in American immigration history.

The pattern that Chapter 1 identified in the mining camps of the early 1850s — economic welcome giving way, as competition intensified, to racialized hostility — recurs and intensifies throughout the history this chapter traces. Chinese labor was, at every turn, sought after by the employers who needed it and resented by the white workers who competed with it or feared they might. Central Pacific Railroad executives actively recruited Chinese laborers because they performed difficult, dangerous work reliably and at a cost the company found advantageous; farm owners, cannery operators, and manufacturers made similar calculations across the 1860s and 1870s. At the same time, white laborers and the labor organizations that represented them increasingly framed Chinese workers not as fellow wage earners but as a racial threat to the white working class's economic future — a framing that would provide much of the popular energy behind the anti-Chinese movement examined in Chapter 3 and the federal exclusion law examined in Chapter 5.

This chapter asks students to hold in view both the scale of Chinese workers' contribution to the physical and economic construction of the American West and the specific mechanisms — wage competition narratives, labor union organizing, the "coolie labor" stereotype, and eventually formal political mobilization — through which that contribution was reinterpreted as a danger to be contained. Understanding Chinese labor in this period requires attention to concrete working conditions: the wages Chinese laborers earned, the tasks they performed, the risks they assumed, and the strategies of cooperation and endurance that allowed them to survive brutally demanding work. It also requires attention to the political discourse that grew up around that labor — a discourse that had only a loose relationship to the actual conditions of Chinese employment, but that proved extraordinarily effective at mobilizing white working-class support for exclusionary politics.

The railroad is this chapter's central case study, and for good reason. The Central Pacific Railroad's Chinese workforce undertook some of the most dangerous construction work in American history, blasting tunnels through Sierra Nevada granite and laying track across the high desert of Nevada and Utah under conditions that killed an unknown but substantial number of workers. Their labor was essential to the completion of the first transcontinental railroad, an infrastructure project that transformed the American economy and bound the Pacific coast to the rest of the nation. Yet Chinese railroad workers received systematically lower wages than white workers performing comparable labor, were excluded from the ceremonial recognition that attended the railroad's completion, and would, within little more than a decade of finishing this work, find themselves the targets of a federal law barring further immigration of Chinese laborers altogether.

This chapter also broadens the story beyond the railroad to examine the full range of industries in which Chinese labor became significant during the 1850s through the 1870s — agriculture, canning, domestic service, laundry work, and small manufacturing — and to trace how the same economic logic that shaped Chinese employment in mining and railroad construction operated across this broader occupational landscape. Understanding this breadth is essential to understanding why anti-Chinese politics, examined in the following chapter, became a statewide and eventually national political movement rather than a localized dispute confined to a single industry or region.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the conditions of Chinese labor in the California mining economy of the 1850s, including camp life, discriminatory taxation, and economic competition with white miners.
  2. Explain the Central Pacific Railroad's recruitment of Chinese laborers and analyze the working and living conditions Chinese crews experienced during the construction of the transcontinental railroad.
  3. Identify the range of industries — agriculture, manufacturing, domestic service, laundries, and restaurants — in which Chinese labor became significant during the 1850s through 1870s.
  4. Analyze the political mobilization of white working-class opposition to Chinese labor, including the role of labor unions and organized political campaigns.
  5. Evaluate the "coolie labor" stereotype as a political construction and distinguish it from the actual contractual and economic conditions of Chinese labor migration described in Chapter 1.
  6. Compare wages, working conditions, and risks faced by Chinese and white laborers performing similar work in the railroad, mining, and agricultural economies of this period.
  7. Assess competing historical interpretations of Chinese railroad workers' role in the construction and completion of the transcontinental railroad.
  8. Connect the labor history examined in this chapter to the anti-Chinese political movement and exclusionary legislation examined in Chapters 3 through 5.
  9. Interpret primary sources documenting railroad labor, including company records, worker accounts, and contemporary reporting, as historical evidence with specific limits and perspectives.
  10. Explain how the pattern of economic reliance followed by racialized backlash, introduced in Chapter 1, deepened and expanded across the industries this chapter examines.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  2. Why did the Central Pacific Railroad turn to Chinese labor for the construction of the transcontinental line, and what specific tasks did Chinese work crews perform?
  3. What risks did Chinese railroad workers face, and how did their wages and working conditions compare to those of white workers performing similar labor?
  4. Beyond mining and railroad construction, in what other industries did Chinese labor become significant during this period, and why?

  6. What was the "coolie labor" stereotype, and how did it differ from the actual contractual structure of Chinese labor migration described in Chapter 1?

  8. What does the pattern traced in this chapter — economic reliance on Chinese labor across multiple industries, met by escalating organized opposition — suggest about the deeper structure of Chinese American history that this textbook traces?

Historical Context

The labor history this chapter traces unfolds against the backdrop of one of the most rapid economic transformations in American history. California's admission to the Union in September 1850 was followed, over the ensuing two decades, by an extraordinary expansion and diversification of the state's economy, from a Gold Rush society organized almost entirely around mineral extraction to a diversified regional economy encompassing large-scale agriculture, industrial manufacturing, and, after 1869, direct rail connection to the national market. Chinese labor was present, and in many sectors indispensable, at every stage of this transformation.

As Chapter 1 explained, Chinese miners entered the California gold fields in substantial numbers beginning in 1849 and 1850, working claims through cooperative, labor-intensive methods well suited to placer mining. Over the course of the 1850s, however, mining opportunities available to Chinese workers narrowed considerably. The most productive and accessible claims had generally been depleted or claimed by earlier arrivals, and organized efforts by white miners — reinforced by discriminatory taxation, most significantly the Foreign Miners' License Tax — increasingly restricted Chinese access to remaining opportunities. By the early 1860s, growing numbers of Chinese workers who had begun their California careers in mining were seeking employment in other sectors of the state's rapidly diversifying economy.

The most consequential of these new opportunities arrived with the chartering of the Central Pacific Railroad in 1861 and the subsequent decision, following the outbreak of the Civil War and the passage of the Pacific Railway Act of 1862, to construct a transcontinental line connecting California to the existing rail network of the eastern United States. The Central Pacific, responsible for building eastward from Sacramento across the Sierra Nevada and the Great Basin, faced a chronic and worsening shortage of laborers willing to perform the grueling, dangerous work of grading roadbed, blasting tunnels, and laying track through some of the most difficult terrain the American railroad industry had yet encountered. Company leadership, after initial skepticism about whether Chinese laborers — widely stereotyped by contemporary Americans as physically unsuited to heavy labor — could perform this work, began recruiting Chinese crews in substantial numbers beginning in 1865, first from the existing California Chinese population and increasingly, as demand outstripped local supply, through direct recruitment in China itself.

The results of this recruitment transformed the Central Pacific's labor force. By 1867 and 1868, Chinese workers constituted the overwhelming majority — by most estimates, roughly ninety percent — of the Central Pacific's construction crews, numbering at peak construction periods somewhere between ten and fifteen thousand men. These crews performed the most demanding and dangerous portions of the entire transcontinental project, including the series of tunnels blasted through solid granite at the Sierra Nevada summit, work that proceeded through brutal winter conditions at high altitude and that killed an unknown but almost certainly substantial number of workers through accidents, avalanches, and exposure. The precise number of Chinese worker deaths during the railroad's construction remains a subject of historical investigation and debate, in significant part because the Central Pacific kept incomplete records of Chinese worker casualties relative to the records it maintained for white employees — itself a telling indication of the differential value the company's own record-keeping practices assigned to Chinese and white lives.

Beyond the railroad, Chinese labor became increasingly significant across a widening range of California industries during the 1860s and 1870s. Agricultural employers, particularly in the expanding wheat, fruit, and vegetable operations of California's Central Valley, relied heavily on Chinese field labor, and Chinese workers played a central role in the reclamation and drainage of agricultural land in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, work that transformed vast areas of wetland into some of the state's most productive farmland. The canning industry that developed alongside California's expanding fruit and vegetable agriculture employed large numbers of Chinese workers, as did domestic service, laundry operations — an industry in which Chinese-run businesses came to hold a distinctive and, in the public imagination, closely associated position — and a range of small-scale manufacturing enterprises, including cigar-making, boot and shoe production, and woolen textile manufacturing.

This economic breadth mattered enormously for the political history that followed. Because Chinese labor was present across so many sectors of the California economy, opposition to Chinese labor was never confined to a single industry or a single group of aggrieved workers. Miners, railroad laborers displaced or undercut by Chinese crews, agricultural workers, and, especially, urban craft workers in trades such as cigar-making, boot and shoe manufacturing, and laundry work where Chinese competition was most directly and immediately felt, all developed grievances — some grounded in genuine economic competition, others amplified well beyond their economic substance by racial ideology — that would converge, over the course of the 1860s and 1870s, into an increasingly organized, increasingly political anti-Chinese labor movement. That movement's early development, traced at the end of this chapter, sets the stage directly for Chapter 3's fuller examination of anti-Chinese politics and violence.

Section 2.1

Chinese Labor in the Gold Rush Economy

Mining Labor

As Chapter 1 described, Chinese miners entered the California gold fields in growing numbers from 1849 onward, organizing themselves into cooperative work groups that pooled labor, capital, and equipment to work claims systematically, including claims that earlier arrivals had abandoned as insufficiently profitable for individual prospectors. This pattern of disciplined, group-based labor allowed Chinese miners to extract meaningful returns from marginal claims throughout the 1850s, even as the most productive and accessible deposits were claimed and depleted by earlier arrivals.

By the early 1860s, however, the mining economy that had drawn the first generation of Chinese migrants to California had changed considerably. Placer mining, the relatively low-capital, labor-intensive technique best suited to the cooperative work groups Chinese miners typically organized, had given way in many districts to hydraulic and hard-rock mining methods requiring substantially greater capital investment — methods generally controlled by larger, capital-intensive operations from which Chinese labor was frequently excluded, whether through direct discrimination or through the practical barriers of raising the capital such operations required. Chinese miners who remained in the mining economy through this period increasingly worked claims and districts that had already been passed over by this shift toward large-scale operations, continuing the pattern, established in the 1850s, of Chinese labor entering an economic niche after the most lucrative opportunities had already been captured by others.

Camp Life

Mining camp life for Chinese workers in this period continued to combine demanding physical labor with the maintenance of communal and cultural practices adapted to frontier conditions, much as Chapter 1 described for the 1850s. Camps organized around kinship and shared county of origin persisted, as did the connections to merchant firms in San Francisco and Sacramento that supplied goods, transmitted remittances and correspondence, and provided a link to the broader migration and mutual aid networks examined throughout this textbook. As mining opportunities narrowed, however, camp populations increasingly included men who had already begun supplementing or replacing mining income with other forms of labor, including seasonal agricultural work and, from the mid-1860s onward, railroad construction — a transitional pattern that reflects the broader occupational diversification this chapter traces.

Racial Taxes and Restrictions

The discriminatory taxation that Chapter 1 identified as an early formalization of anti-Chinese policy in the mining economy continued and, in some districts, intensified through the 1860s. Beyond the Foreign Miners' License Tax itself, Chinese miners faced a range of informal and formal restrictions on which claims and districts they could work, restrictions enforced through local mining district rules, through organized harassment by white miners' associations, and, with increasing frequency, through violence — a pattern examined in greater detail in Chapter 3's discussion of anti-Chinese violence in mining camps and cities. These restrictions, compounding the capital barriers created by the shift toward hydraulic and hard-rock mining, steadily narrowed the range of mining opportunities available to Chinese workers across the decade, accelerating the movement of Chinese labor into the railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing sectors this chapter goes on to examine.

Economic Competition

The economic competition between Chinese and white miners that intensified over the course of the 1850s and into the 1860s established a pattern of grievance and political mobilization that would recur, in different forms, across every industry this chapter examines. White miners who framed Chinese labor as an unfair competitive threat — whether because Chinese miners' willingness to work marginal claims at modest returns was perceived as depressing wages and opportunities generally, or because of more directly racial hostility toward Chinese presence in the mining districts — developed political and organizational tools, including exclusionary mining codes and discriminatory taxation, that would be adapted and expanded as Chinese labor moved into new sectors of the California economy. Understanding this mining-camp origin of anti-Chinese labor politics is essential to understanding why the railroad, agricultural, and craft-union conflicts examined later in this chapter followed such a familiar and well-rehearsed pattern.

Historical Vocabulary
The "Coolie Labor" Stereotype

The term "coolie" was used throughout the nineteenth-century United States, with considerable imprecision and hostility, to describe Chinese laborers as a degraded, quasi-servile class of workers, implicitly or explicitly incapable of the free, independent wage bargaining that Americans of the period associated with legitimate labor. As Chapter 1 explained, the credit-ticket system that financed the great majority of Chinese labor migration to America was a voluntary contractual debt arrangement, generally repaid from wages the migrant continued to control — a system meaningfully different from the coercive indentured labor systems that supplied "coolie" labor to plantations in Latin America and the Caribbean during the same period.

Despite this distinction, American nativist rhetoric of the 1850s through 1870s routinely and inaccurately conflated all Chinese labor migration with the coercive "coolie trade," using the term to suggest that Chinese workers were fundamentally unfree, and therefore inherently unfair competitors against free white labor. This framing served a specific political function: it allowed opponents of Chinese immigration to cast their opposition not as simple racial hostility but as a defense of free labor principles against a supposedly servile and degraded competing workforce.

Historians regard the "coolie labor" stereotype as a political construction that had only a loose relationship to the actual conditions of Chinese employment in California. Chinese railroad workers, as this chapter documents, engaged in organized strikes for higher wages, and Chinese laborers across the mining, agricultural, and manufacturing economies operated within contractual wage relationships, not systems of bonded servitude. The persistence and political effectiveness of the "coolie" stereotype, despite its inaccuracy, illustrates how racialized economic narratives could shape public perception and policy independent of the specific facts they claimed to describe.

Section 2.2

The Transcontinental Railroad

Central Pacific Recruitment

The Pacific Railway Act of 1862 authorized construction of a transcontinental railroad connecting California to the existing rail network of the eastern United States, with the Central Pacific Railroad responsible for building eastward from Sacramento across the Sierra Nevada and the Great Basin to a meeting point with the Union Pacific, which was building westward from Iowa. From the outset, the Central Pacific faced a severe labor shortage. California's relatively small and highly mobile population, drawn disproportionately toward the ongoing lure of mining opportunity, provided an unreliable and insufficient workforce for the grueling, dangerous, and comparatively low-paid work of railroad grading and construction.

Company leadership, including construction superintendent Charles Crocker, initially expressed considerable skepticism about whether Chinese laborers, widely stereotyped by contemporary Americans as physically frail and unsuited to heavy manual labor, could perform the demanding work the railroad required. This skepticism was overcome through a combination of labor necessity and direct experience: an initial, small-scale trial of Chinese work crews in early 1865 demonstrated that Chinese laborers performed the required grading and construction work capably and reliably, and the company moved rapidly toward large-scale recruitment. By 1867, Chinese workers constituted roughly ninety percent of the Central Pacific's construction workforce, numbering at peak periods somewhere between ten and fifteen thousand men — a workforce drawn initially from the existing Chinese population of California and increasingly, as demand outstripped local supply, through direct recruitment efforts in Guangdong itself, operating through the same emigration broker and credit-ticket networks that Chapter 1 examined.

Work Crews and Labor Discipline

Chinese railroad crews were typically organized under a system of subcontracting in which a Chinese labor contractor, often himself a member of the merchant or association leadership examined in greater detail in Part III of this textbook, assumed responsibility for recruiting, organizing, and, in significant part, supervising a crew of workers on behalf of the railroad company. This arrangement gave Chinese work crews a substantial degree of internal organization and self-management, with work assignments, camp provisioning, and daily discipline managed substantially through Chinese contractors and crew leaders rather than through direct company supervision of individual workers — an arrangement that provided Chinese laborers considerably more collective bargaining leverage, and more capacity for coordinated action such as the 1867 wage strike discussed later in this section, than the popular image of an undifferentiated, passively obedient Chinese labor force would suggest.

Mountain Labor and Explosives

The most dangerous and technically demanding portion of the Central Pacific's construction project was the series of tunnels required to carry the line through the granite summit of the Sierra Nevada. Chinese work crews performed the overwhelming majority of this tunnel construction, employing black powder and, as the project progressed, the newly developed and considerably more volatile explosive nitroglycerin to blast through solid rock, often working simultaneously from both ends of a planned tunnel and, in the case of the summit tunnel, from a vertical shaft sunk to allow blasting to proceed from the tunnel's middle as well as its ends. This work proceeded through brutal Sierra Nevada winters, with crews laboring in tunnels dug beneath snowpack that in some years exceeded thirty feet, housed in camps that were themselves periodically struck by avalanches. The precise number of Chinese worker deaths during this phase of construction remains a subject of ongoing historical investigation, complicated by the Central Pacific's incomplete record-keeping regarding Chinese casualties relative to the more thorough records the company maintained for white employees.

Wages, Strikes, and Dangerous Work

Chinese railroad workers were paid systematically lower wages than white workers performing comparable labor, and were typically required to provide and pay for their own food and lodging out of their wages, an expense the company generally covered for white workers. In June 1867, several thousand Chinese workers on the Central Pacific line organized a strike, demanding wage increases, a reduction in the workday from twelve to ten hours, and an end to the practice of company foremen physically beating workers to enforce labor discipline. The strike, involving an estimated three thousand or more workers and sustained for approximately a week before economic pressure — the company's suspension of food supplies to strikers' camps — forced its end, demonstrated a degree of coordinated labor organization among Chinese workers that directly contradicted contemporary stereotypes of Chinese labor as inherently passive or incapable of collective action. The strike produced only modest concessions from the company, but it stands as significant evidence, examined further in this chapter's primary source materials, of Chinese railroad workers' agency and capacity for organized resistance to exploitative conditions.

Historical Vocabulary
The Central Pacific Railroad

The Central Pacific Railroad was chartered in 1861 to construct the western portion of the first transcontinental railroad, building eastward from Sacramento across the Sierra Nevada and the Great Basin. Authorized under the Pacific Railway Act of 1862 and financed through a combination of federal land grants and government bonds, the Central Pacific faced construction challenges of extraordinary difficulty, including the granite tunnels of the Sierra Nevada summit.

The company's decision, beginning in 1865, to recruit Chinese laborers at scale transformed both the pace of construction and the demographic composition of its workforce, which by 1867 was roughly ninety percent Chinese. The Central Pacific's reliance on Chinese labor makes it the single most significant employer of Chinese workers in nineteenth-century America and the central case study through which this chapter examines the working conditions, wages, and political position of Chinese labor in the broader economy of the American West.

Despite this central role, Chinese railroad workers were largely excluded from the public commemoration of the transcontinental railroad's completion at Promontory Summit in May 1869, a discrepancy between contribution and recognition that later chapters of this textbook, and the broader field of Chinese American public history examined in Chapter 14, have worked to address.

Completion and Its Aftermath

The Central Pacific and Union Pacific lines met at Promontory Summit, Utah Territory, on May 10, 1869, completing the first transcontinental railroad and marking a transformative moment in American economic history, binding California and the broader Pacific coast to the national rail network and dramatically reducing the time and cost of transcontinental travel and commerce. The ceremony held to mark the occasion, including the driving of a ceremonial golden spike, produced photographs and accounts that became iconic images in American popular memory — images from which Chinese workers, despite having performed the overwhelming majority of the Central Pacific's construction labor, were conspicuously absent. Following the railroad's completion, the large Chinese workforce that had built it dispersed into other sectors of the western economy, including the agricultural, manufacturing, and railroad-maintenance and expansion work examined in the following section, carrying with them skills, savings, and organizational experience that would shape Chinese labor history in the American West for decades to come.

Section 2.3

Agriculture, Manufacturing, and Service Work

Farm Labor

As California's agricultural economy expanded rapidly during the 1860s and 1870s, driven by the growth of large-scale wheat cultivation and, increasingly, the fruit and vegetable production that would come to define the state's agricultural reputation, Chinese labor became an increasingly significant component of the state's farm workforce. Chinese workers performed the labor-intensive tasks of planting, cultivating, and harvesting that this expanding agricultural economy required, often organized, as in mining and railroad work, into cooperative labor groups managed through Chinese labor contractors. Chinese laborers also played a central and often underrecognized role in the reclamation of agricultural land in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, constructing the extensive system of levees that drained wetland areas and transformed them into some of the state's most productive farmland — physically demanding and dangerous work performed under conditions that, as in railroad construction, received limited contemporary documentation relative to its scale and significance.

Canneries

The growth of California's fruit and vegetable canning industry during the 1870s and 1880s, itself substantially dependent on the agricultural expansion Chinese field labor helped make possible, created significant additional employment for Chinese workers, who performed much of the labor-intensive processing work canning required. Cannery labor, like agricultural and railroad work, was frequently organized through Chinese labor contractors who supplied and managed crews on behalf of cannery operators, extending the contracting system this chapter has examined across multiple sectors of the California economy.

Domestic Service

Chinese workers, particularly in the gender-imbalanced bachelor society that Chapter 4 examines in greater detail, found significant employment in domestic service throughout California's growing cities, working as cooks, house servants, and general domestic laborers in the households of the state's expanding middle and upper classes. This employment, while frequently low-paid and precarious, provided an important occupational alternative for Chinese workers as mining and, eventually, railroad opportunities contracted, and it placed Chinese workers in direct, sustained daily contact with white American households in a manner that shaped broader public perceptions of Chinese labor and character.

Laundries

The laundry trade became, over the course of the 1860s and 1870s, one of the most distinctive and, in the public imagination, most closely associated occupations of Chinese America. Chinese-run laundries required relatively modest startup capital, relied on skills that were readily transferable and did not depend on English fluency or extensive local social connections, and occupied an economic niche — commercial laundry service — that faced relatively limited competition from other California industries during this period. By the 1870s, Chinese-operated laundries were a common feature of California's urban commercial landscape, and the laundry trade would remain a significant occupation for Chinese Americans for decades, eventually becoming subject to a distinctive body of discriminatory municipal regulation that the U.S. Supreme Court would address in the 1886 case Yick Wo v. Hopkins, a landmark equal protection decision falling outside this chapter's period but significant to the broader legal history this textbook traces.

Restaurants

Chinese restaurants, serving both a Chinese immigrant clientele and, increasingly over time, a broader American public, developed alongside the other service industries this section examines, providing both a source of employment for Chinese workers and an early and durable point of cultural contact between Chinese and non-Chinese Californians. Restaurant work, like laundry operation, offered an occupational path that did not depend on the capital-intensive investment required in manufacturing or the physical risks of mining and railroad labor, and it would remain, alongside laundry work, one of the most persistent and visible occupational niches of Chinese America across the following century.

Historical Vocabulary
Labor Competition

Labor competition refers to the economic dynamic in which workers from different populations compete for the same jobs, potentially affecting wages and employment opportunities for both groups. In the context of nineteenth-century California, the concept of labor competition became a central organizing framework through which white workers, labor unions, and political organizations understood and opposed Chinese employment across mining, railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing industries.

Historians distinguish between the genuine economic dimensions of labor competition — the real effects that a large, low-wage labor pool could have on wages and employment opportunities in specific trades — and the exaggerated or racially motivated political rhetoric that frequently accompanied invocations of labor competition in nineteenth-century American political discourse. The "wage competition narrative" examined later in this chapter drew on genuine economic concerns among some workers while substantially amplifying and generalizing those concerns into a broader racial politics that extended well beyond the specific trades in which direct economic competition existed.

Section 2.4

Chinese Labor and White Working-Class Politics

Wage Competition Narratives

As Chinese labor became increasingly visible across the mining, railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing sectors of the California economy during the 1860s and 1870s, a political narrative developed among white workers and their organizations holding that Chinese labor systematically depressed wages and displaced white workers from employment opportunities they would otherwise have held. This wage competition narrative drew, in specific trades where direct competition for the same jobs genuinely existed — cigar-making, boot and shoe manufacturing, and certain forms of agricultural and railroad labor prominent among them — on real economic grievances. At the same time, the narrative was substantially amplified beyond its specific economic basis into a generalized claim that Chinese labor as such, across the entire California economy, threatened the livelihood and living standards of white workers, a claim that found receptive audiences even among workers with little direct occupational competition with Chinese labor.

Labor Unions and Racial Exclusion

Craft unions in trades where Chinese competition was most directly felt became early and vocal centers of organized opposition to Chinese labor during the 1870s. Cigar makers' unions, boot and shoe workers' organizations, and similar craft-based labor organizations increasingly incorporated explicit anti-Chinese positions into their organizing platforms, campaigning for boycotts of Chinese-made goods, for legislative restriction of Chinese labor, and, eventually, for the federal exclusion of Chinese immigration altogether. These unions' opposition combined genuine economic grievance with an increasingly explicit racial ideology holding that Chinese workers were fundamentally incompatible with an American labor movement organized around free white labor — an ideology that would find its fullest political expression in the anti-Chinese movement examined in Chapter 3 and the exclusionary legislation examined in Part II of this textbook.

Political Mobilization Against Chinese Workers

By the mid-1870s, opposition to Chinese labor had moved decisively beyond individual trade unions into broader, more organized political mobilization, laying the groundwork for the explicitly anti-Chinese political parties and campaigns that Chapter 3 examines in detail. Labor organizations increasingly coordinated across trades, built alliances with broader nativist and racial-exclusionist political currents, and began directing sustained pressure toward state and federal legislators for restriction of Chinese immigration and labor. This political mobilization, emerging directly out of the specific labor conditions and grievances this chapter has traced across mining, railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing work, represents the crucial hinge between the labor history of this chapter and the political and legal history of exclusion that occupies the remainder of Part I and all of Part II of this textbook.

The "Coolie Labor" Stereotype

The "coolie labor" stereotype introduced earlier in this chapter played a central role in this political mobilization, providing white labor organizations with a framework for opposing Chinese labor that presented itself as a defense of free labor principles rather than simple racial exclusion. By characterizing Chinese workers as inherently unfree — bound, in this telling, to exploitative labor contractors and emigration firms in a manner scarcely distinguishable from indentured servitude — labor organizations and their political allies could frame opposition to Chinese immigration as consistent with, rather than contrary to, broader American ideals of free labor and fair competition. As this chapter has demonstrated, this framing bore only a loose relationship to the actual contractual structure of Chinese labor migration, the organizational autonomy of Chinese work crews under the labor contracting system, and the documented instances of organized Chinese labor action such as the 1867 railroad strike. Its political effectiveness, however, depended little on its factual accuracy, and it would remain a central rhetorical tool of anti-Chinese politics through the passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 and beyond.

Multiple Perspectives

Chinese Labor in the 1860s and 1870s Economy: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of Central Pacific Railroad management: Company executives, having overcome initial skepticism about Chinese workers' capacity for heavy labor, came to regard Chinese crews as reliable, disciplined, and essential to completing a construction project of unprecedented difficulty on a competitive timeline. The wage differential between Chinese and white workers, and the practice of requiring Chinese workers to cover their own food and lodging, reflected the company's assessment of what the labor market would bear rather than any acknowledgment of equivalent contribution or risk.
From the perspective of Chinese railroad workers: Surviving evidence, including the coordinated 1867 strike for higher wages, shorter hours, and an end to physical abuse by foremen, suggests that Chinese workers understood their labor in instrumental, wage-earning terms consistent with the sojourning framework examined in Chapter 1, while also demonstrating a clear capacity for collective organization and resistance when conditions became sufficiently intolerable. The internal organization of work crews under Chinese contractors provided both economic structure and, evidently, an infrastructure capable of supporting coordinated labor action.
From the perspective of white craft union members: Workers in trades such as cigar-making and boot and shoe manufacturing, where Chinese labor competition was most directly felt, experienced genuine economic pressure on wages and employment opportunities, and organized politically to resist that pressure. Their opposition to Chinese labor combined this genuine economic grievance with racial ideologies, inherited from broader American discourse, that cast Chinese workers as fundamentally incompatible with an American labor movement and an American national community defined in racially exclusive terms.
From the perspective of California agricultural and manufacturing employers: Farm owners, cannery operators, and manufacturers who employed Chinese labor generally valued its reliability and cost-effectiveness, and had economic incentives to resist the restrictive labor legislation that organized white labor increasingly demanded during the 1870s. This employer interest in continued access to Chinese labor would become an important, if ultimately unsuccessful, counterweight to the political momentum toward exclusion examined in the chapters that follow.
Cause and Effect

From Mining Decline to Railroad Recruitment: Structural Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The shift of California mining toward capital-intensive hydraulic and hard-rock methods that reduced opportunities for the cooperative, labor-intensive mining Chinese workers had organized around; the continuing operation of the credit-ticket migration and labor contracting networks established in Chapter 1's discussion of early Chinese settlement.

Short-Term and Proximate Causes

The Pacific Railway Act's authorization of transcontinental railroad construction in 1862; the Central Pacific's severe labor shortage and its 1865 decision, following a successful trial, to recruit Chinese workers at scale; the discriminatory taxation and organized exclusion narrowing Chinese mining opportunities across the 1850s and into the 1860s.

The Development

Ten to fifteen thousand Chinese workers, organized through a labor contracting system into disciplined work crews, performed the majority of Central Pacific construction labor between 1865 and 1869, including the extraordinarily dangerous work of blasting tunnels through the Sierra Nevada granite summit under brutal winter conditions.

Early Consequences

The completion of the transcontinental railroad in 1869 and the dispersal of its Chinese workforce into agricultural, manufacturing, and service industries across California, combined with escalating white labor union organizing against Chinese employment, that together produced the organized anti-Chinese political movement examined in Chapter 3.

Historical complexity: The labor history this chapter traces did not proceed uniformly across every industry or region; wages, working conditions, and the intensity of white labor opposition varied considerably by trade and locality. Understanding this variation — rather than treating "Chinese labor" as a single undifferentiated category — is essential to understanding both the diversity of Chinese occupational experience in this period and the uneven, industry-by-industry development of the anti-Chinese labor politics this chapter's final section introduces.

Everyday Life and Social History

The World of the Railroad Work Crew: Ordinary Life During Construction

For the thousands of Chinese men who spent the years between 1865 and 1869 building the Central Pacific Railroad, daily life combined extraordinarily demanding physical labor with a degree of communal and organizational structure that allowed workers to endure conditions of considerable hardship and danger. A typical work crew, organized under a Chinese contractor, lived in camps that moved forward along the line as construction progressed, camps that included cooks preparing food according to familiar patterns, supply arrangements that brought goods — including tea, rice, and dried foods — from merchant firms in San Francisco and Sacramento, and, where terrain and season permitted, arrangements for bathing and hygiene that contemporary observers frequently noted compared favorably to the practices of white work crews on the same project.

The labor itself varied by task and terrain: grading roadbed across the relatively open country of the Sacramento Valley and the Nevada desert; blasting and clearing rock in the Sierra Nevada foothills; and, in the most demanding and dangerous phase of the project, tunneling through solid granite at the summit, work that proceeded around the clock in shifts, using black powder and, increasingly, nitroglycerin, through winters in which snow accumulation at the work site could exceed thirty feet. Workers in the summit tunnels lived, for extended periods during the worst winter months, in quarters dug beneath the snowpack, connected by tunnels that allowed movement between work sites and living quarters without exposure to surface conditions.

Danger was a constant feature of this labor. Explosive accidents, tunnel collapses, avalanches, and the general hazards of construction work in extreme terrain and weather killed an unknown but substantial number of Chinese workers over the course of the project — losses that the Central Pacific's own incomplete record-keeping makes difficult to quantify with precision, but that surviving accounts and subsequent historical research suggest were considerable. The 1867 strike, in which thousands of Chinese workers coordinated a work stoppage demanding better wages, shorter hours, and an end to physical abuse by foremen, reflects both the severity of conditions crews endured and the organizational capacity — rooted in the same contractor-based crew structure that managed daily work — that allowed Chinese workers to act collectively in response to those conditions.

Following the railroad's completion in 1869, the skills, savings, and organizational networks that Chinese railroad workers had built over four years of construction dispersed across the American West, as former railroad workers moved into agricultural labor, into further railroad construction and maintenance work on the expanding western rail network, and into the growing range of manufacturing and service occupations examined earlier in this chapter — carrying with them both the hard-won experience of the railroad's construction and, increasingly, direct exposure to the escalating anti-Chinese political mobilization that this chapter's final section has traced.

Primary Source Window

Railroad Labor Accounts

Creator: Central Pacific Railroad company records; contemporary journalists; surviving worker testimony  ·  Dates: 1865–1869  ·  Type: Company records, correspondence, newspaper accounts

Context: The documentary record of Chinese railroad labor during the construction of the transcontinental railroad is shaped overwhelmingly by sources produced by the Central Pacific Railroad itself and by contemporary American journalists and observers, rather than by Chinese workers' own direct testimony, which survives only in fragmentary form. Company payroll records, correspondence among Central Pacific executives, and newspaper coverage of construction progress and, occasionally, of incidents such as the 1867 strike together provide historians with the primary evidentiary basis for reconstructing the conditions of Chinese railroad labor.

These sources allow historians to establish basic facts about wages, the scale of the Chinese workforce, and the general character of construction work, but they are limited in their capacity to convey the direct experience and perspective of Chinese workers themselves. The 1867 strike is particularly significant in this respect, since company and newspaper accounts of the strike's demands — for wage increases, shorter hours, and an end to physical abuse by foremen — provide rare, if indirect, evidence of Chinese workers' own assessment of their conditions and their capacity for organized collective response.

Significance and limits: Because the surviving record is so heavily weighted toward company and outside observer perspectives, historians must read these sources critically, attentive to the interests and assumptions of the sources' creators. Company records, for instance, likely undercounted Chinese worker casualties relative to the more careful records kept for white employees, and contemporary newspaper coverage frequently reflected the same racialized stereotypes this chapter has examined, including skepticism about Chinese workers' capacities that the actual construction record decisively contradicted.

Historical Debate

Who Built the Transcontinental Railroad? Competing Interpretations

The question: Historians and public commentators have long debated how the transcontinental railroad's construction should be understood and credited — as an achievement primarily of American engineering, financing, and corporate organization, in which Chinese labor played a supporting though substantial role, or as a project that would not have been possible without Chinese labor and that should be understood, first and foremost, as a Chinese American achievement obscured by decades of selective public memory.

Interpretation A — The Corporate Achievement Model: Traditional accounts of the transcontinental railroad's construction, dominant in American popular memory through much of the twentieth century, emphasized the achievement of the Central Pacific and Union Pacific companies, their executives, engineers, and financiers, treating the railroad's enormous workforce, Chinese and otherwise, as an important but secondary element of a story centered on corporate organization, engineering ingenuity, and national ambition. This interpretation draws support from the fact that the railroad's financing, route planning, and executive decision-making were indeed controlled by the companies' owners and managers, and it reflects the framing evident in contemporary commemorations such as the Promontory Summit ceremony itself.
Interpretation B — The Chinese Labor Achievement Model: A significant body of more recent historical scholarship, along with public history and commemorative projects examined in Chapter 14, argues that the traditional corporate-achievement framing has obscured the actual physical and organizational contribution of Chinese labor to the railroad's completion, and that a fuller and more accurate account must center Chinese workers as the primary builders of the Central Pacific's line, particularly its most difficult sections. This interpretation draws on the demographic reality that Chinese workers constituted roughly ninety percent of the Central Pacific's construction workforce, the documented evidence of Chinese workers' skill and organizational capacity, and the historical injustice of Chinese workers' exclusion from contemporary commemoration despite this central role.
Interpretation C — A Synthesis: Corporate Structure and Chinese Labor as Interdependent Achievements: A third position, increasingly favored by historians, treats the corporate-achievement and Chinese-labor-achievement framings not as competing claims but as accounts of interdependent and inseparable dimensions of the same historical accomplishment: the Central Pacific's financing, engineering, and corporate organization made large-scale railroad construction possible in the first place, while the skill, endurance, and organizational capacity of its overwhelmingly Chinese workforce made the specific achievement of completing the line, particularly through the Sierra Nevada, actually possible in practice. Under this view, crediting the railroad's completion accurately requires acknowledging both dimensions without allowing either to obscure the other.

Why it matters: How this debate is resolved shapes not only historical understanding of a specific nineteenth-century construction project but also the broader questions of memory, recognition, and public history that Chapter 14 examines in detail — questions about whose labor and whose achievement count as "American" history, and about how historical commemoration can perpetuate or correct the selective memory reflected in sources like the Promontory Summit photograph.

Continuity and Change Over Time

From Mining Camp to Work Crew: Continuity and Change in Chinese Labor Organization

What Changed

  • Chinese labor moved from the relatively decentralized, self-directed mining camps of the 1850s toward the more formally organized, contractor-managed work crews of the railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing economy of the 1860s and 1870s
  • The scale of Chinese employment under a single employer grew enormously, from small cooperative mining groups to the ten-to-fifteen-thousand-worker Central Pacific construction force
  • The specific tasks Chinese laborers performed diversified substantially, from mining alone to railroad construction, land reclamation, cannery work, domestic service, laundry operation, and small manufacturing
  • White labor opposition to Chinese employment moved from localized, informal harassment and discriminatory local taxation toward more organized, multi-trade, and eventually explicitly political union organizing

What Stayed the Same

  • The reliance on cooperative, network-based labor organization — grounded in kinship, village origin, and shared credit and mutual aid arrangements — persisted from the mining camps into the railroad work crews and beyond
  • The pattern of Chinese labor entering an economic niche after the most lucrative opportunities had already been claimed or restricted by others recurred across mining, agriculture, and manufacturing
  • The broader pattern, introduced in Chapter 1, of economic welcome for Chinese labor giving way, as competition or visibility intensified, to organized racialized opposition, recurred and intensified across every industry this chapter has examined
  • Chinese workers' capacity for organized collective action, evident in mining-camp mutual aid and again in the 1867 railroad strike, persisted as a recurring feature of Chinese labor history despite contemporary stereotypes suggesting otherwise

Why the pattern matters: The continuities this feature identifies — cooperative labor organization, niche entry into constrained opportunities, and the recurring cycle of economic reliance and racialized backlash — connect the labor history of this chapter directly to the associational world examined in Part III of this textbook, whose family, district, and merchant associations trace their organizational logic to exactly the same kinship and mutual aid networks that structured Chinese labor in the mining camps, railroad crews, and agricultural and manufacturing workplaces this chapter has traced.

Structural Connection

Labor History and the Coming Politics of Exclusion

The labor history this chapter has traced — across mining, railroad construction, agriculture, and manufacturing — was not merely an economic development in its own right. It was the direct precondition for the anti-Chinese political movement examined in Chapter 3 and the exclusionary federal legislation examined in Part II of this textbook. The wage competition narratives and craft union organizing that emerged from specific, genuine instances of labor competition in trades such as cigar-making and boot and shoe manufacturing provided the organizational infrastructure and political energy that anti-Chinese political parties and campaigns would harness and expand in the following decade.

The "coolie labor" stereotype examined throughout this chapter, initially developed as a rhetorical tool within specific labor disputes, would become a central justification cited by proponents of the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, discussed in Chapter 5, who argued that Chinese immigration constituted an inherently unfair and degraded form of labor competition incompatible with American free-labor principles — a claim this chapter's evidence, including the organizational autonomy of Chinese work crews and the documented 1867 strike, substantially undermines.

This chapter's account of Chinese labor, in other words, should be read not only as a story of economic contribution and hardship in its own right, but as the direct institutional and political origin point for the escalating anti-Chinese political mobilization that later chapters of this textbook will trace in full: from the anti-Chinese clubs and political campaigns examined in Chapter 3, through the gendered exclusion of the Page Act examined in Chapter 4, to the federal exclusion regime examined in Chapter 5 and the mob violence and forced expulsions examined in Chapter 6.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the expansion of Chinese labor across the mining, railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing economies of California and the American West between the early 1850s and the 1870s. It began with the continued but increasingly constrained mining labor examined in Chapter 1, followed the movement of Chinese workers into the massive construction effort of the Central Pacific Railroad, and extended the story into the diversified agricultural, manufacturing, and service economy that absorbed Chinese labor following the railroad's 1869 completion.

The Central Pacific Railroad's Chinese workforce, numbering at peak periods between ten and fifteen thousand workers and constituting roughly ninety percent of the company's construction crews, performed the most demanding and dangerous portions of the transcontinental line's construction, including the Sierra Nevada summit tunnels blasted through granite under brutal winter conditions. This workforce, organized through a system of Chinese labor contracting that provided both economic structure and organizational capacity, demonstrated its capacity for coordinated collective action in the 1867 strike for higher wages, shorter hours, and an end to physical abuse — evidence that directly contradicts the passive, servile stereotypes that anti-Chinese political rhetoric increasingly deployed during this same period.

Beyond the railroad, Chinese labor became significant across a widening range of California industries, including agricultural field labor and land reclamation, cannery work, domestic service, laundry operation, and small-scale manufacturing. This occupational breadth meant that Chinese labor's economic significance, and the political opposition it generated, were never confined to a single industry, but instead developed across the full range of trades in which Chinese and white workers might compete or interact.

That political opposition, examined in this chapter's final section, emerged directly from specific instances of labor competition in trades such as cigar-making and boot and shoe manufacturing, and was substantially amplified through the "coolie labor" stereotype, a political construction that inaccurately conflated the voluntary contractual credit-ticket migration examined in Chapter 1 with genuinely coercive indentured labor systems. This stereotype, despite its inaccuracy, proved highly effective in mobilizing white working-class opposition to Chinese labor across multiple industries and in building the organizational and political infrastructure that would drive the anti-Chinese movement examined in Chapter 3.

The significance of this chapter for the larger history this textbook traces lies precisely in this connection between labor history and political history. The pattern introduced in Chapter 1 — economic welcome for Chinese labor giving way, as competition intensified, to organized racialized opposition — recurred and intensified across every industry this chapter has examined, from the mining camps through the railroad construction crews to the craft workshops of California's growing cities. Understanding the actual conditions of Chinese labor in this period — the wages, the risks, the organizational structures, and the documented instances of workers' own agency and resistance — is essential to recognizing how thoroughly the political rhetoric of exclusion misrepresented that labor for political purposes, and to understanding the labor and economic history from which the exclusion era, examined throughout Part II of this textbook, directly grew.

Finally, this chapter has extended this textbook's central argument that Chinese America's institutions were built through agency and adaptation, not mere victimization. The Chinese workers who built the transcontinental railroad, drained the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, staffed the canneries and laundries of California's growing cities, and organized a coordinated strike against exploitative railroad conditions were, at every point, active agents navigating and shaping the economic opportunities available to them — even as those same opportunities generated, through the political mobilization examined in this chapter's final section, the organized hostility that would culminate in exclusion.

Key Terms

Coolie labor stereotype
A political construction of the 1850s–1870s that inaccurately conflated voluntary, contractual Chinese labor migration with coercive indentured "coolie" labor systems, used to frame opposition to Chinese immigration as a defense of free labor principles.
Central Pacific Railroad
The company chartered in 1861 to build the western portion of the transcontinental railroad, whose workforce was roughly ninety percent Chinese at peak construction and which stands as the single largest employer of Chinese labor in nineteenth-century America.
Labor competition
The economic dynamic in which Chinese and white workers competed for employment in the same trades, a genuine phenomenon in specific industries that was substantially amplified into a broader racialized political narrative.
Transcontinental Railroad
The rail line connecting California to the eastern United States, completed at Promontory Summit in May 1869 through the joint construction efforts of the Central Pacific and Union Pacific railroads.
Pacific Railway Act
The 1862 federal law authorizing construction of the transcontinental railroad and creating the enormous labor demand that drove the Central Pacific's large-scale recruitment of Chinese workers beginning in 1865.
Labor contracting system
The system through which Chinese labor contractors recruited, organized, and substantially managed Chinese work crews on behalf of railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing employers, providing both economic structure and organizational capacity for collective action.
Wage competition narrative
The political framing, developed by white workers and labor organizations, holding that Chinese labor systematically depressed wages and displaced white workers, a narrative that drew on genuine grievances in specific trades while being substantially amplified beyond its economic basis.
Craft union
A labor organization representing workers in a specific skilled trade, such as cigar-making or boot and shoe manufacturing, that became an early and vocal center of organized opposition to Chinese labor during the 1870s.
Land reclamation
The process of draining and converting wetland into arable farmland, substantially accomplished in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta through Chinese levee-building labor during the 1860s and 1870s.
Foreign Miners' License Tax
The discriminatory California state tax, discussed in Chapter 1, whose burden on Chinese miners contributed to the movement of Chinese labor into the railroad, agricultural, and manufacturing sectors examined in this chapter.
1867 railroad strike
A coordinated work stoppage by several thousand Chinese railroad workers demanding higher wages, shorter hours, and an end to physical abuse by foremen, demonstrating organizational capacity that contradicted contemporary stereotypes of passive Chinese labor.
Racial wage differential
The systematic practice of paying Chinese workers lower wages than white workers for comparable labor, and requiring Chinese workers, unlike white workers, to cover their own food and lodging expenses.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told the second chapter of a long and consequential story — the story of how Chinese labor became indispensable to the economic development of the American West even as that very indispensability generated the organized political hostility that would, within little more than a decade, culminate in federal exclusion law. The mining camps of Chapter 1 gave way, in this chapter, to railroad construction crews, agricultural fields, canneries, laundries, and workshops — a widening landscape of labor that carried Chinese workers into nearly every corner of the California and western economy.

The Central Pacific Railroad's Chinese workforce stands, in this chapter's telling, as both the most dramatic single instance of Chinese labor's contribution to the American West and the clearest illustration of the gap between that contribution and its public recognition. Ten to fifteen thousand men blasted tunnels through granite, worked through winters of unprecedented severity, organized a coordinated strike for basic dignity and fair wages, and completed one of the signature infrastructure achievements of nineteenth-century America — and then stood almost entirely absent from the photograph taken to commemorate that achievement's completion. That absence, this chapter has argued, is itself a historical document, revealing as much about the racial politics of recognition and memory as any written source could.

The escalating labor union organizing and political mobilization examined at this chapter's close also matter deeply for what follows. The wage competition narratives and craft union campaigns of the 1870s were not a late or separate development from the labor history this chapter traces; they emerged directly from it, as workers in specific trades translated genuine economic grievance into an increasingly organized, increasingly racialized political movement. The anti-Chinese movement examined in Chapter 3, the exclusionary legislation examined in Part II, and the violence and expulsions examined in Chapter 6 all extend and formalize a political mobilization whose origins are visible already in the craft union halls and labor disputes this chapter describes.

The final lesson of this chapter, as of the one before it, is that the people whose history it describes were not merely subjects of economic forces beyond their control. They were workers who organized, who struck, who built financial and organizational networks across the demanding industries examined in this chapter, and who, in doing so, physically constructed a substantial portion of the infrastructure — the railroad, the reclaimed farmland, the canneries and laundries and workshops — on which the modern American West was built. That contribution, and the organized political hostility it generated, together set the stage for the anti-Chinese movement that Chapter 3 now takes up in full.

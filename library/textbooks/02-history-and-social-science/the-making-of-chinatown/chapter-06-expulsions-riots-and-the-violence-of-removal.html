Part II — Exclusion, Expulsion, and Urban Enclosure
6

Expulsions, Riots, and the Violence of Removal

The Making of Chinatown: Chinese Immigration, Exclusion, and Community Power in America

Chapter Opening

On the morning of November 3, 1885, a committee of white citizens in Tacoma, Washington Territory, went door to door through the town's Chinese quarter, ordering every Chinese resident to gather their belongings and leave the city within hours. By nightfall, Tacoma's Chinese community — several hundred people who had lived, worked, and built lives in the town — had been marched to the railroad depot at Lake View and put on trains bound for Portland, while what remained of their homes and shops was left to be destroyed. No one was formally charged with a crime. No court had ordered anyone's removal. The expulsion had been carried out in broad daylight, by ordinary residents, with the tacit or open cooperation of local officials.

Chapter 5 examined how the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 built a formal federal architecture of documentation, certification, and deportation, a bureaucratic system that determined, on paper, who could lawfully enter and remain in the United States. This chapter turns to a different, and in many respects more immediate, form of exclusion: the organized, often extralegal violence through which white communities across the American West physically drove Chinese residents from towns and cities where federal law, however restrictive, had not barred their presence. Where Chapter 5's exclusion regime operated through certificates, inspectors, and courts, the violence this chapter examines operated through mobs, arson, and armed expulsion committees — a form of exclusion enacted not by federal statute but by local and regional collective action.

This chapter traces that violence across four sections. It begins with the broader phenomenon historians have called the "era of driving out," the pattern of anti-Chinese mob action, labor conflict, and local government complicity that swept the American West during the 1870s and 1880s. It then examines the single deadliest incident of anti-Chinese violence in American history, the Rock Springs Massacre of 1885, in which white miners killed dozens of Chinese miners and destroyed the town's Chinese settlement. The chapter turns next to the Tacoma Method, the organized, non-lethal expulsion carried out in Tacoma that same year, and the way other West Coast cities, including Seattle, looked to Tacoma's example, sometimes emulating it and sometimes resisting it through federal intervention. The chapter closes by surveying the wider regional pattern of expulsions, from Eureka, California, to towns across the Pacific Northwest and interior West, and asks what this violence meant for the communities the exclusion era's remaining chapters, and this textbook's Part III, examine in fuller detail.

Understanding this violence as this chapter presents it — not as scattered incidents of local disorder but as an organized, often civic-sanctioned mechanism of racial removal operating alongside the federal exclusion regime Chapter 5 examined — is essential to understanding why Chinese Americans built the defensive, self-reliant urban institutions that Part III of this textbook traces in detail.

  3. Analyze the Tacoma Method as a distinct model of organized civic expulsion, and compare it to the mob violence examined earlier in the chapter.
  4. Compare Tacoma's expulsion to Seattle's, and assess the significance of martial law and federal intervention in shaping different outcomes.

Historical Context

The expulsion violence this chapter examines did not emerge in isolation from the broader currents this textbook's earlier chapters have traced. Chapter 2 examined how Chinese labor competition, real and perceived, generated sustained resentment among white workers in the mining, railroad, and agricultural economies of the American West. Chapter 3 examined how this resentment converted into organized anti-Chinese political movements during the 1870s, movements that combined racial scapegoating with genuine labor grievance and that increasingly treated Chinese immigrants as a population whose removal, rather than mere legal restriction, was the appropriate political goal. Chapter 5 examined how this same political energy produced the federal Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, a law that barred future immigration but did nothing to remove the Chinese residents already living in towns and cities across the West.

This gap between federal exclusion and existing Chinese communities proved significant. The Exclusion Act, for all its bureaucratic reach, could not by itself remove Chinese laborers, merchants, and their families already residing in mining towns, railroad camps, and West Coast cities. For many white residents of these communities, whose anti-Chinese political mobilization had aimed at removal rather than merely restriction, the 1882 Act's passage did not resolve the "Chinese question" so much as redirect attention toward the Chinese communities that remained. The years immediately following the Act's passage saw an intensification, rather than a diminishment, of local anti-Chinese agitation across the West, as communities increasingly organized to accomplish through direct action what the exclusion law's forward-looking restrictions could not.

Economic conditions sharpened this dynamic. The mid-1880s brought labor unrest and economic hardship to much of the American West, particularly in mining regions where white and Chinese laborers competed directly for increasingly scarce work. Labor organizations, including the Knights of Labor, incorporated anti-Chinese agitation directly into their organizing efforts during this period, providing organizational infrastructure that in several documented instances channeled economic grievance into coordinated expulsion campaigns. The violence at Rock Springs in September 1885, examined in this chapter's second section, erupted directly out of this combination of labor competition, economic anxiety, and organized anti-Chinese sentiment, while the expulsion at Tacoma two months later demonstrated that organized civic action, rather than spontaneous mob violence, could achieve comparable ends of racial removal without comparable bloodshed.

The pattern that resulted — a wave of anti-Chinese expulsions extending from Rock Springs, Wyoming Territory, through Tacoma and Seattle, Washington Territory, to Eureka, California, and numerous smaller communities across the West during the mid-1880s — represents, as this chapter argues, an essential companion to the federal exclusion architecture examined in Chapter 5. Where federal law restricted future entry, local and regional violence addressed the presence of Chinese communities already established, and the combination of these two forces did much to determine where, and under what conditions, Chinese Americans could safely live during the decades this textbook examines.

Section 6.1

The Era of Driving Out

Anti-Chinese Mobs

Beginning in the 1870s and intensifying through the 1880s, mining towns, railroad camps, and small cities across the American West experienced repeated episodes of organized mob action aimed at removing Chinese residents entirely, a pattern historians have come to call the "era of driving out." These mobs drew on the anti-Chinese political organizing examined in Chapter 3, but they pursued a more direct and immediate goal than the legislative restriction that movement had also sought: the physical removal of Chinese residents from a specific town or district, regardless of whether those residents' presence violated any federal or territorial law.

Labor Conflict

Much of this mob violence erupted directly out of labor conflict, particularly in mining regions where Chinese and white laborers competed for increasingly scarce employment during the economic downturns of the mid-1880s. The labor competition narratives and "coolie labor" stereotype examined in Chapter 2 provided much of the ideological framework through which white miners and workers understood this competition, transforming what was often a straightforward economic conflict over wages and jobs into a racialized narrative of Chinese workers as an existential threat to white livelihood.

Local Government Complicity

A recurring feature of the expulsions this chapter examines was the participation, or at minimum the tacit acceptance, of local government officials — mayors, sheriffs, and city councils — in organizing or permitting the removal of Chinese residents. This complicity distinguished the "driving out" pattern from purely spontaneous mob violence, since in numerous documented cases, including the Tacoma expulsion examined later in this chapter, local officials played leading organizational roles, using the authority and legitimacy of civic office to coordinate what amounted to an extralegal mass removal.

Forced Migration

The cumulative effect of this violence was a pattern of forced migration that displaced Chinese residents from numerous Western towns and cities, pushing many toward the larger, more defensible Chinese communities in cities such as San Francisco and Portland, where greater numbers and more developed community institutions, examined in Chapter 8, offered relatively greater protection than smaller, more isolated settlements could provide. This displacement pattern, as this chapter's closing sections will argue, directly shaped the urban concentration of Chinese America into defensible Chinatown enclaves that Chapter 7 examines as a central subject of this textbook.

Historical Vocabulary
Driving Out

Historians use the term driving out to describe the pattern of organized anti-Chinese violence and forced expulsion that swept mining towns, railroad camps, and small cities across the American West during the 1870s and 1880s. The term captures both the mob violence and the more organized civic expulsions this chapter examines, distinguishing this pattern from the federal exclusion architecture Chapter 5 examined, since driving out targeted Chinese residents already present in a community rather than restricting future immigration.

The pattern combined labor conflict, racial ideology, and, in many documented cases, local government complicity, producing outcomes that ranged from mass killing, as at Rock Springs, to organized non-lethal removal, as at Tacoma, both examined later in this chapter.

Section 6.2

Rock Springs and Anti-Chinese Mass Violence

Mining Conflict

Rock Springs, a coal-mining town in Wyoming Territory, had employed both white and Chinese miners for the Union Pacific Coal Company for over a decade before September 1885, with Chinese miners making up a substantial portion of the workforce. Tension between white and Chinese miners had built steadily over disputes concerning access to the most productive mining locations within the company's coal seams, disputes that combined genuine competition over piece-rate earnings with the broader anti-Chinese labor politics examined in Chapter 2.

Racial Violence

On the morning of September 2, 1885, a dispute over access to a coal seam escalated rapidly into organized mob violence, as armed white miners attacked Chinese miners throughout the town, killing at least twenty-eight Chinese residents and wounding many more. The violence continued through much of the day, with white miners pursuing fleeing Chinese residents into the surrounding hills and preventing many from receiving aid, producing a death toll that later investigation suggested may have exceeded the number initially confirmed.

Destruction of Chinese Settlements

Alongside the killing, white miners systematically burned the Chinese section of Rock Springs, destroying homes, possessions, and the physical infrastructure of the Chinese mining community entirely. This destruction, extending well beyond the immediate violence against individuals, ensured that the Chinese community's return to Rock Springs would require rebuilding not merely a workforce but an entire physical settlement, a burden that shaped the community's uncertain and contested return to the town in the massacre's aftermath.

National Response

News of the Rock Springs Massacre produced significant national attention and, notably, direct diplomatic concern from the Qing government, which pressed the United States for both protection of Chinese residents and compensation for the destruction and loss of life. President Grover Cleveland ordered federal troops to Rock Springs to restore order and, eventually, to escort surviving Chinese miners back to the town, while Congress ultimately approved an indemnity payment to the Chinese government, though no perpetrators of the killing were ever successfully prosecuted despite a grand jury investigation.

Historical Vocabulary
The Rock Springs Massacre

The Rock Springs Massacre refers to the mob killing of at least twenty-eight Chinese coal miners by white miners in Rock Springs, Wyoming Territory, on September 2, 1885, an event that also involved the destruction of the town's Chinese settlement by fire. The massacre is widely regarded by historians as the single deadliest incident of anti-Chinese violence in American history.

The event prompted diplomatic protest from the Qing government, federal troop deployment, and an eventual congressional indemnity payment, though no participant in the killing was ever convicted of a crime. The massacre stands, in this chapter's account, as the most extreme expression of the broader "driving out" pattern examined in this chapter's first section.

Section 6.3

The Tacoma Method

Organized Civic Expulsion

Two months after the Rock Springs Massacre, Tacoma's anti-Chinese movement, led in substantial part by the city's own mayor, organized a different kind of removal: a coordinated, non-lethal expulsion of Tacoma's entire Chinese community, planned in advance and carried out with the participation of civic leaders rather than through spontaneous mob violence. This organized character, distinguishing the Tacoma expulsion from the sudden eruption of killing at Rock Springs, led contemporaries and later historians alike to treat the Tacoma expulsion as a model, one that other Pacific Northwest communities would consider adopting or explicitly resisting in the months that followed.

Forced Removal by Train

On November 3, 1885, hundreds of white residents, organized in advance by a citizens' committee, moved through Tacoma's Chinese quarter, ordering residents to gather their belongings and marching the community's several hundred Chinese residents to the Lake View railroad station, from which they were transported out of the city, many toward Portland. The expulsion was carried out without the mass killing that had characterized Rock Springs, though residents faced coercion, property loss, and the immediate physical danger inherent in a forced mass removal carried out by an organized crowd.

Destruction of Chinatown

In the expulsion's aftermath, much of Tacoma's Chinese quarter was burned, ensuring, as at Rock Springs, that the removal was not merely a temporary displacement but an attempt at permanent exclusion, one intended to prevent Tacoma's Chinese community from rebuilding or returning to the city. Unlike Rock Springs, where the Chinese mining community eventually returned under federal military protection, Tacoma's Chinese community was effectively barred from the city for decades following the 1885 expulsion.

Local Memory and Later Apology

For much of the twentieth century, the Tacoma expulsion received relatively little civic acknowledgment within the city itself, a pattern of historical silence that Chapter 14's discussion of public memory and historical recovery examines in fuller depth. Beginning in the early 1990s, Tacoma undertook a sustained process of public reckoning with the 1885 expulsion, including the establishment of a reconciliation park and formal civic acknowledgment of the expulsion's history, efforts that this textbook's final chapter treats as part of a broader pattern of local communities confronting previously unacknowledged anti-Chinese violence.

Historical Vocabulary
The Tacoma Method

The Tacoma Method refers to the organized, non-lethal expulsion of Tacoma, Washington Territory's entire Chinese community on November 3, 1885, carried out by a citizens' committee with the leading participation of the city's mayor and other civic officials. Unlike the Rock Springs Massacre examined earlier in this chapter, the Tacoma Method avoided mass killing while still achieving comprehensive, and in Tacoma's case lasting, removal of the city's Chinese residents.

Contemporaries in other Pacific Northwest cities, including Seattle, explicitly debated whether to follow Tacoma's example, and the term has since come to represent a specific model of organized civic expulsion distinguished from the spontaneous mob violence examined in this chapter's discussion of Rock Springs.

Section 6.4

Seattle, Eureka, and Other Expulsions

Urban Mobs

In February 1886, anti-Chinese organizers in Seattle attempted an expulsion modeled explicitly on Tacoma's example three months earlier, forcibly gathering Chinese residents and attempting to place them aboard a departing steamship. Unlike Tacoma, however, Seattle's expulsion attempt met organized resistance from residents and officials who opposed the removal, producing violent clashes between pro-expulsion and anti-expulsion factions within the city itself, a division that distinguished Seattle's experience from the comparatively unified civic action that had characterized Tacoma's expulsion.

Regional Copycat Actions

Beyond Tacoma and Seattle, communities throughout the Pacific Northwest and the broader American West debated and, in numerous documented instances, carried out similar expulsions during the mid-1880s, a regional pattern that reflected the widespread reach of the anti-Chinese political mobilization examined in Chapter 3. Eureka, California, expelled its Chinese residents in February 1885 following the killing of a white city councilman, an expulsion carried out with sufficient organization and permanence that Eureka's Chinese community did not meaningfully return to the city for decades.

Martial Law and State Response

The violence surrounding Seattle's expulsion attempt in February 1886 grew serious enough that President Cleveland declared martial law and deployed federal troops to the city, a response that stands in notable contrast to the absence of comparable federal intervention at Tacoma three months earlier. This inconsistency in federal response, this chapter argues, reflects less a clear federal policy toward anti-Chinese violence than the particular political and civic circumstances of each individual case, including the degree of local resistance to expulsion and the scale of violence involved.

Long-Term Displacement

Taken together, the expulsions this chapter has examined produced a lasting pattern of Chinese displacement from numerous smaller Western communities, concentrating Chinese American population increasingly within a smaller number of larger cities where community institutions, examined in Chapter 8, and greater numbers offered relatively more effective protection against the kind of organized violence this chapter has traced. This concentration, as the chapters that follow in Part III of this textbook examine, did much to shape the urban form of Chinatown as it developed across the following decades.

Cause and Effect

From Local Grievance to Organized Removal: Structural Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The labor competition narratives examined in Chapter 2; the organized anti-Chinese political movement examined in Chapter 3; the federal exclusion architecture examined in Chapter 5, which left existing Chinese communities exposed.

Short-Term and Proximate Causes

Economic downturn and mining labor conflict in the mid-1880s; Knights of Labor anti-Chinese organizing; specific local disputes, such as the Rock Springs coal-seam conflict.

The Development

Mob violence and mass killing at Rock Springs; organized civic expulsion at Tacoma; contested expulsion attempts at Seattle and Eureka; regional variation in local government complicity and federal response.

Early Consequences

Long-term displacement from smaller Western communities; concentration of Chinese American population in larger cities; the urban enclosure and Chinatown formation examined in Chapter 7.

Historical complexity: The outcomes this feature traces were not uniform; Seattle's contested expulsion attempt and the eventual return of Chinese miners to Rock Springs under military protection demonstrate that the "driving out" pattern did not proceed identically in every community.

Everyday Life and Social History

After the Wagons and the Trains: Displacement and Survival

For the Chinese residents forced from Rock Springs, Tacoma, Eureka, and the many smaller communities this chapter has examined, expulsion meant more than the loss of a home. It meant the sudden loss of possessions left behind or destroyed, the disruption of work and wages that many families depended upon to support relatives in China, and the immediate practical problem of finding shelter, work, and safety in an unfamiliar city, often with little advance warning and few resources beyond what could be carried in the hours before removal.

Many displaced Chinese residents made their way to Portland, San Francisco, and other cities with established Chinese communities, where the associations examined in Chapter 8 provided housing assistance, job placement, and material support to new arrivals fleeing expulsion violence. This assistance was not always sufficient to fully absorb the sudden influx of displaced residents, and expelled communities sometimes faced overcrowding and strained resources in the cities that received them.

The Rock Springs miners' eventual return to the town, escorted by federal troops, illustrates a further dimension of this displacement: return, where it occurred, did not restore what had been lost. Miners returned to a settlement that had been burned, to jobs that in some cases had been filled by other workers during their absence, and to a community whose sense of safety had been permanently altered by the violence it had experienced.

For communities like Tacoma and Eureka, where expulsion proved effectively permanent, the displacement this chapter examines meant a lasting absence of Chinese residents from civic life for decades, a gap in the historical record and in the built environment that the reconciliation efforts examined later in this textbook have attempted, imperfectly, to address.

Primary Source Window

Newspaper Accounts of Anti-Chinese Expulsions

Creator: Regional and national newspapers  ·  Date: 1885–1886  ·  Type: Contemporary press coverage

Context: Newspaper coverage of the Rock Springs Massacre, the Tacoma expulsion, and the Seattle disturbances constitutes a central body of contemporary evidence for the events this chapter examines, while also reflecting the racial assumptions and editorial sympathies of the period. Coverage varied considerably by region and by a paper's political alignment, with some accounts openly sympathetic to the expulsion committees and others, particularly papers reporting for Eastern or national audiences, more critical of the violence involved.

Students examining this coverage should attend closely to the language reporters used to describe Chinese residents, the degree to which coverage treated local officials' participation in organizing expulsions as newsworthy or unremarkable, and the differences between coverage produced close to the events and coverage produced for distant audiences less invested in local anti-Chinese politics.

Significance and limits: Newspaper accounts offer detailed, often eyewitness-level description of the events this chapter examines, but they reflect the perspective and racial assumptions of predominantly white reporters and editors, and they rarely convey the perspective of Chinese residents themselves, whose direct testimony from this period survives far less completely than the newspaper record. Students should read this coverage critically, attending to what it reveals and what it omits, rather than treating it as a neutral or complete account.

Questions for students:

  1. How did newspaper coverage of Rock Springs differ from coverage of the Tacoma expulsion, and what might explain these differences?
  2. What language do these accounts use to describe Chinese residents, and what does this language suggest about the racial assumptions of the period?
  3. How did coverage treat the involvement of local officials, such as Tacoma's mayor, in organizing expulsions?
  4. What perspectives are missing from this newspaper coverage, and how might historians recover them through other sources?

Historical Debate

Riot, Massacre, or Ethnic Cleansing? The Language of Anti-Chinese Violence

The question: Historians have debated which terms most accurately describe the violence this chapter examines, a debate with real interpretive stakes for how students and scholars understand the scale, intent, and significance of events like Rock Springs and the Tacoma expulsion.

Interpretation A — "Riot": This older terminology, common in period sources and some earlier historical accounts, frames the violence as spontaneous disorder arising from local tension, a framing critics argue understates the organized planning evident in events like the Tacoma expulsion and obscures the participation of civic officials.
Interpretation B — "Massacre" and "Expulsion": This terminology, more common in recent scholarship, distinguishes between the mass killing at Rock Springs, appropriately termed a massacre, and the organized, non-lethal removal at Tacoma, more precisely termed an expulsion, treating these as related but analytically distinct forms of anti-Chinese violence.
Interpretation C — "Ethnic Cleansing": Some historians argue that the systematic, organized character of events like the Tacoma Method, aimed explicitly at the permanent removal of an entire ethnic community from a defined territory, meets scholarly definitions of ethnic cleansing, a term that emphasizes the coordinated and comprehensive intent behind the violence rather than treating each incident as an isolated local disturbance.

Why it matters: The terminology chosen shapes whether these events are understood as isolated local disorders or as a coordinated regional pattern of racial removal, a distinction that bears directly on how this violence should be weighed alongside the federal exclusion architecture examined in Chapter 5 within the larger history of American racial immigration policy.

Student task: Based on the evidence in this chapter, evaluate which terminology, or combination of terms, best describes the Rock Springs Massacre and the Tacoma Method respectively. Does the same term apply equally well to both events, or do they warrant different classification?

Multiple Perspectives

The Violence of Removal: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of white miners and workers involved in expulsion: Participants in events like Rock Springs and Tacoma frequently framed their actions as a defense of white labor against unfair economic competition, drawing directly on the "coolie labor" stereotype and labor competition narratives examined in Chapter 2 to justify violence and removal as a response to perceived economic injustice.
From the perspective of Chinese miners and residents facing expulsion: Chinese residents of towns like Rock Springs and Tacoma experienced this violence as a sudden and often unpredictable threat to lives and livelihoods they had built over years, frequently with little effective recourse to local law enforcement, which in many documented cases either failed to intervene or actively participated in the expulsion.
From the perspective of local civic officials, such as Tacoma's mayor: Officials who organized or participated in expulsions like Tacoma's frequently presented their actions as an orderly, even civic-minded, resolution to what they characterized as an intractable social problem, framing organized removal as preferable to the disorder and violence that had occurred at Rock Springs two months earlier.
From the perspective of the Qing government and Chinese diplomatic officials: Chinese diplomatic officials protested the violence at Rock Springs and the expulsions that followed as a fundamental failure of the United States government to protect Chinese subjects, pressing for both indemnity and more effective federal protection, with only partial and inconsistent success.
Continuity and Change Over Time

Patterns of Anti-Chinese Violence: Continuity and Change

What Changed

  • Anti-Chinese violence shifted from the more diffuse mob attacks examined in Chapter 3's discussion of earlier mining-camp and urban violence toward the highly organized civic expulsion model the Tacoma Method represented
  • Federal and territorial response grew somewhat more consistent by early 1886, as the martial law declaration at Seattle demonstrated a willingness to intervene that had been largely absent at Tacoma three months earlier
  • Public memory and civic acknowledgment of this violence shifted substantially by the late twentieth century, as Tacoma's 1990s reconciliation process demonstrates

What Stayed the Same

  • The underlying combination of labor competition, racial ideology, and local political complicity persisted across the entire period this chapter examines, from the earliest mining-camp violence through the 1886 Seattle disturbances
  • Chinese residents' limited recourse to effective legal protection persisted throughout this period, whether the violence took the form of mass killing or organized non-lethal expulsion
  • The pattern of displacement toward larger, better-established Chinese communities persisted across every expulsion this chapter examines, reinforcing the urban concentration examined further in Chapter 7

Why the pattern matters: The continuities and changes this chapter identifies connect directly to the urban enclosure and community institution-building examined in Part III of this textbook, whose Chinatown formations developed in substantial part as a defensive response to the violence this chapter has traced.

Structural Connection

Expulsion Violence and the Coming Structure of Chinatown

The violence this chapter has traced — the mass killing at Rock Springs, the organized removal at Tacoma, and the broader regional pattern of expulsion these events represent — created powerful incentives for Chinese Americans to concentrate their communities in cities large enough, and institutionally developed enough, to offer meaningful protection against the kind of organized violence this chapter examines. A Chinese American population that had already experienced the documentary vulnerability and legal precarity Chapter 5 traced now faced an additional, more immediate threat: physical removal by organized local violence, often with the complicity of local government itself.

This combination of legal vulnerability and physical danger, this chapter argues, did much to determine the specific urban form Chinatown would take in cities like San Francisco, examined in Chapter 7, where sufficient population density, mutual aid institutions, and physical concentration offered Chinese Americans a degree of collective security that smaller, more isolated communities like Rock Springs and Tacoma had proven unable to provide. The associations examined in Chapter 8, several of which trace their expanded roles directly to this period, took on increasing responsibility for the practical work of receiving and resettling residents displaced by expulsion violence, work that built directly on the mutual aid functions those associations had already begun to perform under the documentary pressures Chapter 5 examined.

This chapter's account of expulsion violence, in other words, should be read not as a discrete or separate episode from the legal exclusion architecture examined in Chapter 5, but as its physical, extralegal complement, one that shaped where, and under what conditions of collective self-defense, Chinese American community life could survive for the six decades this textbook's remaining chapters continue to trace.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the organized violence of anti-Chinese expulsion during the 1870s and 1880s, situating this violence within the "era of driving out" that swept mining towns, railroad camps, and small cities across the American West and examining how this pattern intensified in the years immediately following the Chinese Exclusion Act's 1882 passage, examined in Chapter 5. The chapter began by tracing the broader pattern of anti-Chinese mobs, labor conflict, and local government complicity that characterized this era, building directly on the labor competition and political mobilization examined in Chapters 2 and 3.

The chapter then examined the Rock Springs Massacre of September 1885 in detail, tracing how mining conflict escalated into the deadliest incident of anti-Chinese violence in American history, and the limited national and diplomatic response that followed. The chapter turned next to the Tacoma Method, the organized, non-lethal expulsion carried out in Tacoma two months later, examining how this model of civic-organized removal differed from the mob violence at Rock Springs while achieving comparably comprehensive results. Finally, the chapter surveyed the wider regional pattern of expulsion, from the contested attempt at Seattle to the expulsion at Eureka, California, and examined the inconsistent federal and territorial responses these events produced.

The significance of this chapter for the larger history this textbook traces lies in its demonstration that federal exclusion law, examined in Chapter 5, operated alongside, rather than instead of, organized local and regional violence aimed at removing Chinese communities already established in towns and cities across the West. This violence, this chapter has argued, did much to determine the geography of Chinese American settlement during the decades this textbook examines, displacing residents from smaller and more isolated communities toward the larger cities whose urban form Part III of this textbook traces in detail.

Finally, this chapter has connected the violence it examines to the institutional and urban consequences this textbook's later chapters trace in fuller detail: the urban enclosure and Chinatown formation examined in Chapter 7, which developed in substantial part as a defensive response to the violence this chapter has traced, and the associational mutual aid examined in Chapter 8, which took on expanded responsibility for resettling communities displaced by expulsion.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Driving out
The pattern of organized anti-Chinese violence and forced expulsion that swept mining towns, railroad camps, and small cities across the American West during the 1870s and 1880s.
Rock Springs Massacre
The mob killing of at least twenty-eight Chinese coal miners and destruction of their settlement in Rock Springs, Wyoming Territory, on September 2, 1885.
Tacoma Method
The organized, non-lethal expulsion of Tacoma's entire Chinese community on November 3, 1885, carried out by a citizens' committee with the leading participation of the city's mayor.
Anti-Chinese riot
A term used, with some interpretive limitations examined in this chapter, to describe organized or spontaneous mob violence directed at Chinese communities during this period.
Expulsion committee
An organized group of civic leaders and residents responsible for planning and carrying out the forced removal of a town's Chinese population, as at Tacoma.
Martial law
The declaration, ordered by President Cleveland at Seattle in 1886, placing a city under military rather than civilian authority in response to anti-Chinese violence.
Forced removal
The physical expulsion of Chinese residents from a town or city, whether through mob violence or organized civic action.
Civic complicity
The participation of local government officials in organizing, enabling, or tacitly permitting anti-Chinese expulsion violence.
Ethnic cleansing
A term some historians apply to the systematic, organized removal of an entire ethnic community from a defined territory, as examined in this chapter's historical debate feature.
Local government complicity
The direct or indirect involvement of city and territorial officials in organizing or permitting anti-Chinese expulsions.
Knights of Labor
The labor organization whose anti-Chinese organizing provided infrastructure that channeled economic grievance into coordinated expulsion campaigns during the mid-1880s.
Reconciliation
The later civic process, exemplified by Tacoma's 1993 initiative, through which communities have publicly acknowledged and sought to address the history of anti-Chinese expulsion violence.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has told a story that federal statute alone, examined in Chapter 5, cannot fully explain: the organized, often civic-sanctioned violence through which white communities across the American West physically removed Chinese residents from towns where federal exclusion law had not barred their presence. The mob killing at Rock Springs and the organized removal at Tacoma, distinct as they were in method and mortality, shared a common purpose and a common outcome, the forcible erasure of Chinese communities from towns their residents had helped to build.

The inconsistent federal response this chapter has traced — troops sent to Rock Springs and Seattle, but no comparable intervention at Tacoma — reveals the limits of federal protection available to Chinese residents during this period, limits that stand in some tension with the elaborate federal bureaucracy of documentation and surveillance Chapter 5 examined. A government capable of building an extensive apparatus to restrict who could enter the country proved far less capable, or far less willing, to protect those already lawfully present from organized local violence.

This chapter's account, read alongside Chapter 5's treatment of federal exclusion law, insists that the history of Chinese exclusion cannot be told through statute alone. The violence this chapter has traced operated as exclusion's physical complement, shaping not merely who could enter the United States but where, within the United States, Chinese Americans could safely remain, a question that would determine the urban geography of Chinese America for decades to come.

The final lesson of this chapter, as of the one before it, is that the mechanics of exclusion — legal and extralegal alike — translated directly into human consequences: sudden displacement, lost homes and livelihoods, and a hard-won recognition, on the part of Chinese American communities, that safety would depend on the kind of collective, institutional self-reliance this textbook's Part III examines in full. That lesson, learned at Rock Springs and Tacoma, would shape the making of Chinatown as thoroughly as any federal statute this textbook examines.

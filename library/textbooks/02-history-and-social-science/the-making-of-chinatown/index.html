The Making of Chinatown

This textbook examines the history of Chinese immigration to the United States, the development of Chinatowns as resilient urban communities, and the legal, political, economic, and cultural forces that shaped Chinese American life from the nineteenth century through the modern era.

Overview

Students explore the origins of Chinese migration, labor on the American frontier, exclusion laws, racial violence, family separation, community organizations, self-governance, urban development, and the lasting legacy of Chinatown in American history. The book integrates immigration history, urban history, legal history, race relations, and cultural studies to explain how Chinatown emerged as both a refuge and a symbol of resilience.

Learning Outcomes

  • Explain the historical causes of Chinese migration to North America.
  • Analyze the relationship between immigration, labor, race, and urban development.
  • Describe the impact of exclusion laws on Chinese American communities.
  • Evaluate the role of Chinatown as both a protective community and a contested urban space.
  • Understand the evolution of Chinese American identity from the nineteenth century to the present.
  • Connect Chinatown's history to broader themes in immigration, civil rights, and American history.

Part VII — The Early Modern World, 1450–1750

Global Trade, Mercantilism, and Early Capitalism

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"In 1601, a small fleet of English ships departed from the Thames estuary, carrying investors' hopes and the formal authority of a royal charter — the institutional expression of a new way of organizing economic life that would, over the following two centuries, reshape the world."

In 1601, a small fleet of English ships departed from the Thames estuary, carrying investors' hopes and the formal authority of a royal charter. The company behind the voyage — the newly incorporated English East India Company — had raised capital from London merchants, aristocrats, and minor gentry, pooling their money against the enormous risk of an oceanic trading voyage to Asia. The ships would be gone for two years or more. They might sink in Atlantic storms, encounter hostile Portuguese or Dutch vessels in the Indian Ocean, or return with holds full of spice and cloth worth ten times the initial investment. The East India Company did not know exactly which outcome awaited. What it did know was that the potential rewards of the Asian trade justified the risk — and that by pooling capital across many investors rather than relying on a single wealthy patron, the company could survive the loss of a single ship without financial ruin. This was not simply a business trip. It was the institutional expression of a new way of organizing economic life — one that would, over the following two centuries, reshape the world.

Chapter 39 examines the growth of global trade and early capitalism between approximately 1450 and 1750. These three centuries witnessed a dramatic expansion and reorganization of the world's commercial networks. The Silk Roads, Indian Ocean routes, and trans-Saharan networks that had connected Afro-Eurasia for centuries did not disappear. But they were transformed by new actors, new technologies, new commodities, and new institutional structures. European mariners opened direct oceanic connections between the Americas, Africa, Asia, and Europe. Silver mined in the mountains of Peru and Mexico flowed across two oceans to China, lubricating a global economy of unprecedented scale. Enslaved Africans labored on plantation estates in the Caribbean and Brazil, producing sugar, tobacco, and cotton consumed by growing European middle classes. Merchants in Amsterdam, London, Lisbon, Seville, and later Bombay, Canton, and Nagasaki navigated a world of price differentials, currency exchange, commercial credit, and contractual risk that would have been unrecognizable to their grandparents.

This chapter is organized around several interlocking themes. The first is the expansion and transformation of global trade networks, including the integration of the Americas into world commerce for the first time and the intensification of connections between Asia, Africa, and Europe. The second is the theory and practice of mercantilism — the dominant framework through which early modern states understood and managed commerce. The third is the rise of new commercial institutions, especially the joint-stock company, which allowed large amounts of capital to be pooled and deployed across oceanic distances. The fourth is the role of silver — particularly American silver — in binding together a genuinely global monetary economy. The fifth is the brutal logic of plantation economies and the Atlantic slave system, which generated immense wealth for some while imposing catastrophic suffering on millions.

The sixth theme is the continuing vitality of Indian Ocean commerce and the challenges it faced from European commercial competition. The seventh is the growth of consumer culture, as new commodities from sugar to porcelain to cotton cloth transformed daily life and social meaning across the world. The eighth is the development of financial instruments — insurance, credit, bills of exchange, joint-stock shares — that allowed merchants to manage risk and accumulate capital at new scales. And the ninth, encompassing all the others, is the question of early capitalism: what it was, how it developed, what drove its growth, and what inequalities it produced.

The regions central to this chapter span the entire globe. Western Europe — especially Portugal, Spain, the Dutch Republic, England, and France — supplied many of the institutional innovations and maritime technologies that reshaped world commerce. But the story is emphatically not only European. The Mughal Empire, the Ming and Qing dynasties of China, the Ottoman Empire, the Safavid Empire of Persia, and the kingdoms of Southeast Asia were active participants in, and shapers of, the early modern global economy. African societies supplied both labor and commodities — and paid an enormous human cost in the Atlantic slave trade. The peoples of the Americas, whose silver mines and plantation labor drove Atlantic commerce, experienced the integration of their lands into a global economy as catastrophe: conquest, disease, forced labor, and the destruction of indigenous political and cultural systems.

The historical stakes of this chapter are high. The period from 1450 to 1750 witnessed the emergence of patterns of global inequality, commercial organization, and economic exploitation that shaped the modern world. The wealth accumulated through colonial extraction, plantation slavery, and oceanic trade provided capital that later funded industrialization. The institutional forms developed during this period — joint-stock companies, stock exchanges, insurance markets, commercial banking — became the building blocks of modern capitalism. The ideological frameworks of mercantilism gave way to classical political economy, which in turn shaped modern economic thought. Understanding this period is essential not only for understanding world history between 1450 and 1750, but for understanding how the modern global economy came to be structured as it is — and why its benefits and burdens have been distributed so unequally.

What kinds of evidence help historians reconstruct the early modern global economy? The sources are remarkably diverse. Account books and cargo manifests from trading companies record the movement of goods across oceans. Government records — tariff accounts, mint reports, trade legislation — document the policy frameworks within which commerce operated. Travelers' accounts and merchants' diaries describe the experience of trading across cultural and linguistic frontiers. Economic and political pamphlets debated the theory and practice of mercantilism in real time. Archaeological evidence — shipwrecks, warehouse ruins, plantation remains — preserves material traces of commercial activity. And the records of enslaved people, though far scarcer, offer glimpses of experience at the sharpest edge of the early modern global economy.

Historical Context

The global trading world of the early modern period — roughly 1450 to 1750 — grew from foundations that were already ancient by the time of Columbus's first voyage in 1492. For centuries, merchants had connected East Africa to the Arabian Peninsula, the Arabian Peninsula to South Asia, South Asia to Southeast Asia, and Southeast Asia to China along the maritime routes of the Indian Ocean. Camel caravans had crossed the Sahara Desert, linking West African gold fields to North African and Mediterranean markets. Silk Roads across Inner Asia had carried luxury goods and ideas between China, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe. The Mediterranean had sustained an intricate web of commercial exchange connecting southern Europe, North Africa, and the Levant. By 1400, the world already had long-distance trade networks of great sophistication, and the peoples who participated in them — Swahili merchants on the East African coast, Arab and Indian traders in the Indian Ocean, Genoese and Venetian merchants in the Mediterranean, Chinese maritime entrepreneurs in Southeast Asia — had developed refined techniques of commercial organization, credit, and cultural exchange.

What changed between 1450 and 1750 was not the existence of long-distance trade, but its geographic scope, institutional organization, volume, and power dynamics. Three developments above all reshaped world commerce during this period. First, European mariners — beginning with the Portuguese in the 1400s, followed by the Spanish, Dutch, English, and French — extended oceanic navigation to include the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans as commercially integrated spaces. The Portuguese rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488 and reached India by sea in 1498, opening a direct oceanic route between Europe and Asia that bypassed the overland routes through the Middle East. The Spanish reached the Americas in 1492, initiating a process of conquest and integration that eventually brought the Western Hemisphere into contact with Afro-Eurasian trading networks for the first time.

Second, the conquest of the Americas produced an enormous infusion of silver into world trade. The great silver mines of Potosí in the Andes (opened 1545) and Zacatecas in Mexico (opened 1546) produced quantities of precious metal unprecedented in world history. This silver — extracted by the forced labor of indigenous Americans under the Spanish mita system — flowed across the Atlantic to Spain and then dispersed across Europe and ultimately to Asia. China in particular absorbed vast quantities of American silver because the Ming government had established silver as the standard for tax payment, generating structural demand that pulled silver eastward across the Pacific through the Manila Galleon trade and westward across the Indian Ocean via European merchants. This global silver circulation created, for the first time, a genuinely worldwide monetary economy in which price signals could travel from the mines of Peru to the markets of Beijing.

Third, new institutional forms emerged to organize commerce at oceanic scale. The individual merchant or family firm, adequate for the trade of a single city or region, was poorly suited to the risks and capital requirements of multi-year oceanic voyages. The joint-stock company — in which many investors pooled capital, shared risk, and participated in profits proportional to their investment — provided a solution. The Dutch East India Company (VOC), founded in 1602, and the English East India Company (EIC), founded in 1600, were the most powerful examples, but similar institutions emerged across the early modern commercial world. These companies were not merely commercial entities; they held governmental powers — the right to make war, sign treaties, govern territories, and administer justice — that blurred the line between private commerce and state power.

The world economy of 1450–1750 was not, however, simply a European creation imposed on passive non-European societies. Asian merchants — Chinese, Indian, Arab, Malay, Japanese — remained major actors in long-distance trade throughout this period. The Ottoman Empire, the Mughal Empire, and the Qing dynasty were powerful states that could negotiate with European companies from positions of strength. The story of early modern global commerce is a story of interaction, negotiation, competition, and coercion involving peoples across the entire world — not a simple narrative of European triumph.

Regional Orientation

Western Europe — particularly Portugal, Spain, the Dutch Republic, England, and France — provides the institutional and navigational innovations most often associated with early modern global commerce. Atlantic-facing geography gave these states incentives to develop oceanic navigation. Atlantic ports — Lisbon, Seville, Amsterdam, London, Bordeaux — became global commercial hubs.

The Americas — both the Spanish colonial empire centered on the silver-rich Andes and Mexico, and the Atlantic plantation zones of Brazil, the Caribbean, and coastal North America — were essential as sources of silver and tropical commodities. The indigenous populations bore the catastrophic costs of this role; enslaved Africans supplied the plantation labor force.

West and Central Africa — especially the kingdoms of the Kongo, Dahomey, Ashanti, and Oyo — were active participants in the Atlantic trading system, though their participation was shaped by the catastrophic demand for enslaved people. African merchants and rulers exercised real agency in negotiating terms of trade, but the slave system imposed devastating demographic and political costs.

The Indian Ocean world — encompassing East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, South Asia, and Southeast Asia — continued to be one of the world's most commercially dynamic zones throughout this period. Arab, Indian (especially Gujarati), Chinese, Malay, and Swahili merchants remained major actors even as European commercial companies inserted themselves with growing force.

South Asia — particularly the Mughal Empire and the commercial cities of Gujarat, Bengal, and the Coromandel Coast — was a major producer of cotton textiles traded across the Indian Ocean, the Atlantic, and eventually the world. South Asian merchants operated sophisticated credit and commercial networks extending from East Africa to Southeast Asia.

East Asia — China above all, but also Japan and maritime Southeast Asia — was central to early modern global trade. Chinese demand for silver structured the global flow of American precious metal. Chinese porcelain, silk, and tea were among the most sought-after commodities in world markets.

The Ottoman and Safavid Empires — controlling the overland routes between Europe and Asia and the major ports of the eastern Mediterranean, Red Sea, and Persian Gulf — were important commercial actors who used tariffs, monopolies, and commercial regulation in ways that paralleled European mercantilism.

Section 39.1

Early Modern Global Trade

Before examining any specific institution, commodity, or theory, it is essential to understand what made the global trading world of 1450–1750 distinctive. Long-distance trade was, of course, not new. The Indian Ocean had sustained regular maritime commerce for two thousand years. The Silk Roads had linked China to the Mediterranean since the Han dynasty. Trans-Saharan caravans had moved gold, salt, enslaved people, and luxury goods across the desert for over a millennium. The Mediterranean had been a continuous space of commercial exchange since antiquity. What changed between 1450 and 1750 was the integration of all these networks — and the addition of the Americas — into a single, globally interconnected system.

The trigger for this integration was European oceanic expansion. Beginning in the early fifteenth century, Portuguese mariners systematically explored the Atlantic coast of Africa, motivated by commercial ambition, religious crusading ideology, and state support from the Portuguese crown. By 1488, Bartolomeu Dias had rounded the Cape of Good Hope, proving that an oceanic route from Europe to the Indian Ocean was viable. In 1498, Vasco da Gama completed the first such voyage, arriving at the port of Calicut on the Malabar Coast of India. He returned to Portugal with a cargo of spices that yielded a profit of approximately 3,000 percent — a figure that immediately demonstrated the commercial potential of direct oceanic access to Asian markets.

What the Portuguese had discovered was not a new trading world but an existing and sophisticated one. The Indian Ocean in 1498 was already a dense commercial network connecting East African ports like Malindi, Mombasa, and Kilwa to Arabian ports like Aden and Muscat, to Indian ports like Calicut, Surat, and Cochin, to Southeast Asian entrepôts like Malacca, and to Chinese and Japanese markets. Arab, Indian (especially Gujarati), Malay, Swahili, and Chinese merchants all participated in this network. They carried spices — pepper, cloves, nutmeg, cinnamon — from the islands of Southeast Asia westward to the Middle East and Europe. They transported cotton textiles from India across the ocean, and moved gold from East Africa to Arabia and India, and silver and copper from the Middle East into Asian markets. They operated sophisticated systems of credit, commercial partnership, and maritime insurance that had evolved over centuries.

The Portuguese inserted themselves into this world by force. They could not compete economically with established Indian Ocean merchants who had lower costs and better commercial intelligence. What the Portuguese had was superior naval firepower — specifically, cannon-armed warships that could outgun any vessel in the Indian Ocean. The Portuguese Estado da India (State of India), centered on Goa, used this firepower to seize key nodes in the Indian Ocean trading system: Hormuz (1515), Goa (1510), and Malacca (1511). From these strongholds, the Portuguese attempted to impose a system of licensed trading (the cartaz system), by which Asian merchants were required to purchase passes from Portuguese authorities. The system was never fully effective — the Indian Ocean was too large to police — but it established the template of commercial-military power that European states would employ throughout the early modern period.

The integration of the Americas into world trade created a qualitatively different kind of global economy. Before 1492, the Americas had been entirely separate from Afro-Eurasian commercial networks. The Columbian Exchange — the transfer of crops, animals, diseases, and peoples between the hemispheres — was the most dramatic biological event in human history since the end of the Ice Age. But the Americas' integration also took a specifically commercial form. Silver from the Andes and Mexico, produced by the forced labor of indigenous peoples and later enslaved Africans, became the lubricant of global trade. Sugar, produced by enslaved Africans on Atlantic plantation estates, became one of the most traded commodities in world history. Cotton, tobacco, indigo, and cacao followed similar paths: American or African origins, forced labor, oceanic transport, and European or Asian consumption.

By 1600, world trade had acquired a structure that was genuinely global in a way it had never been before. American silver flowed eastward across the Atlantic and westward across the Pacific. Asian spices, textiles, and porcelain flowed westward to Europe and the Americas. African enslaved labor powered Atlantic plantation economies that produced commodities consumed across the world. European ships — and eventually European commercial companies with governmental powers — connected all these flows, taking a percentage of the value at each node. The world economy was now, for the first time, a single interconnected system. But it was also, from the beginning, a system characterized by profound inequality: between those who controlled ships, firearms, and commercial institutions, and those whose labor and land were extracted to feed the system.

The volume of this commerce grew dramatically across the early modern period. Estimates of global trade volume are uncertain, but the broad trajectory is clear: the sixteenth century saw explosive growth in Atlantic trade following Spanish conquest; the seventeenth century saw the Dutch commercial hegemony and the maturation of the VOC and EIC systems; the eighteenth century saw further growth in plantation commerce, the peak of the Atlantic slave trade, and the emergence of Britain as the leading commercial power. By 1750, the world economy had been transformed from a set of regional networks into an integrated global system — one whose benefits were distributed very unequally and whose costs were borne disproportionately by the peoples of Africa, the Americas, and eventually Asia.

Section 39.2

Mercantilism and State Power

The theory that most clearly shaped early modern European commercial policy was mercantilism. Mercantilism was not a single unified doctrine but a family of related ideas, elaborated by merchants, government officials, and political writers across the sixteenth, seventeenth, and eighteenth centuries. Its core premises were simple: national wealth depends on the accumulation of precious metal (gold and silver); precious metal is accumulated by exporting more than a country imports (a favorable balance of trade); and the state has both the right and the duty to manage commerce through policy in order to achieve this favorable balance.

The mercantilist understanding of the world economy was fundamentally competitive and zero-sum. Wealth, in the mercantilist view, was finite: what one nation gained, another lost. Commerce was therefore a form of competition — an economic expression of the same zero-sum rivalry that states pursued through warfare. This worldview had important implications for policy. If trade was competition, then governments should actively support their own merchants and manufacturers, exclude foreign competition through tariffs and monopolies, promote exports of manufactured goods, discourage imports of manufactured goods, and use colonies as sources of raw materials and as captive markets for domestic manufacturers.

The most systematic practitioners of mercantilist policy in the seventeenth century were the English and the French. In England, the Navigation Acts (beginning 1651) required that goods imported to England or its colonies be carried in English ships, effectively excluding Dutch commercial dominance from English trade. In France, Jean-Baptiste Colbert, finance minister under Louis XIV from 1665 to 1683, implemented the most comprehensive mercantilist program in European history. Colbert established state-sponsored manufacturing enterprises (royal manufactures), imposed high tariffs on foreign manufactured goods, subsidized exports, built roads and canals, reorganized French guilds, and used colonial trade to maximize the inflow of precious metal. Colbertism — the French version of mercantilism — became the model that other states attempted to imitate.

Mercantilism had deep contradictions. The most fundamental was logical: if all nations pursued a favorable balance of trade simultaneously, not all could succeed — someone had to run a deficit. The Dutch Republic, which became the dominant commercial power of the seventeenth century, actually violated many mercantilist prescriptions: it was an entrepôt economy that thrived on carrying other nations' goods. The Spanish Empire, which accumulated the largest quantity of American silver, found that this bullion quickly drained away to pay for imports from more productive economies. The sixteenth-century "price revolution" — a sustained rise in prices across Europe associated with the influx of American silver — showed that too much money chasing too few goods was economically destabilizing rather than enriching.

Critics of mercantilist doctrine emerged throughout the early modern period, but the most systematic and influential critique was Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776). Smith argued that national wealth consisted not in accumulated precious metal but in productive capacity — the ability to produce goods and services efficiently. He argued that free trade, rather than mercantilist regulation, maximized productive efficiency by allowing each region to specialize in what it produced best. He attacked monopolies, tariffs, and colonial trade restrictions as devices that benefited a few merchants and manufacturers at the expense of consumers and of overall economic efficiency. Smith's critique was historically situated — it reflected the specific moment when British merchants and manufacturers found the restrictions of the old colonial system to be obstacles rather than advantages — but its influence was enormous.

Despite its theoretical problems, mercantilism as practice was enormously consequential. The state regulation, colonial systems, monopoly companies, and military-commercial power it justified helped to create the conditions for European commercial dominance of global trade during the early modern period. Whether or not the mercantilist theory of wealth was correct, the policies it generated — Navigation Acts, colonial monopolies, chartered trading companies — helped European states and merchants gain and maintain privileged positions in global commercial networks.

Historical Vocabulary
Mercantilism

Mercantilism refers to the economic doctrine and set of policies dominant in early modern Europe (roughly 1500–1800), according to which national wealth depended on accumulating precious metal through a favorable balance of trade — exporting more than a country imported. Mercantilist states used tariffs, monopolies, colonial systems, and chartered companies to manage commerce in the national interest. The theory treated trade as zero-sum competition between states and assumed an active role for government in shaping economic outcomes.

Critics — especially Adam Smith — argued that mercantilism confused the accumulation of money with genuine wealth and that free trade would produce better economic outcomes than state management. Mercantilism is important in world history because it shaped the commercial policies of early modern European states, justified colonial extraction, and informed the institutional frameworks through which European commercial power was organized. Its contradictions laid the intellectual groundwork for the emergence of classical liberal political economy in the late eighteenth century.

Section 39.3

Joint-Stock Companies

The single most important institutional innovation of early modern global commerce was the joint-stock company. To understand why, it is necessary to understand the problem that joint-stock organization solved: the problem of capital mobilization and risk distribution for large-scale, long-duration, geographically distant commercial ventures.

Before the joint-stock form, oceanic trade was typically organized through temporary partnerships, family firms, or royal licenses. Each voyage was a separate commercial venture, with investors pooling funds for a specific trip and dissolving the partnership on the voyage's return. This worked adequately for shorter voyages with predictable risks and returns. But for multi-year voyages to Asia, with enormous capital requirements — ships, cargo, provisioning, armaments — unpredictable risks, and distant markets requiring local expertise and sustained relationships, the temporary partnership model was inadequate. An investor who put money into a single voyage faced the loss of everything if that voyage failed. And the capital required for a properly equipped Asian voyage exceeded what any individual or small partnership could typically supply.

The joint-stock solution was to pool capital permanently across many investors, issuing shares proportional to investment and distributing profits (dividends) as they accrued. Unlike a temporary partnership, a joint-stock company had a permanent legal existence independent of its shareholders. Individual investors could sell their shares without dissolving the company. Because risk was spread across many investors, the failure of a single voyage did not mean financial ruin for anyone. And because the company had permanent capital, it could make long-term investments in warehouses, forts, local knowledge, and political relationships that paid off over years or decades.

The Dutch East India Company (VOC), founded in 1602 by the merger of competing Dutch trading ventures, was the most fully developed early modern joint-stock company. It was the first joint-stock company to issue shares that could be freely bought and sold on a secondary market — the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, also founded in 1602. It had a permanent capital base, a professional management structure, and a formal governance system (the Heeren XVII, or Seventeen Lords) that made decisions on behalf of thousands of shareholders. It was also, crucially, granted by the Dutch state the powers of a quasi-sovereign: the right to make war, sign treaties, build forts, and govern territories. The VOC was simultaneously a commercial firm and a political-military institution — a characteristic that defined the major chartered trading companies of the early modern period.

The VOC's commercial record was remarkable. At its height in the mid-seventeenth century, it dominated European trade in Asian spices, operated the only European trading post in Japan (at Dejima in Nagasaki), controlled the key ports of the East Indies (modern Indonesia), and maintained a fleet larger than most European navies. It also committed extraordinary violence: the massacre of the Bandanese people in 1621 to seize control of the nutmeg monopoly is one of the most notorious early examples of commercial genocide in world history. VOC profits financed the Dutch Golden Age — the extraordinary efflorescence of art, science, and commercial culture that made Amsterdam the world's leading city in the seventeenth century.

The English East India Company followed a different path. Rather than focusing on spice monopolies in the East Indies, the EIC concentrated on the massive market for Indian cotton textiles — calicoes, muslins, chintzes — of extraordinary quality and variety that was in demand across the Indian Ocean world and increasingly in Europe. By the late seventeenth century, Indian cotton textiles were being imported to England in such quantities that they provoked political resistance from English wool and silk manufacturers, eventually leading to import restrictions (the Calico Acts of 1690 and 1721). Over the eighteenth century, the EIC acquired territorial power in India — first through alliances with Indian rulers, then through military conquest. By 1757, after the Battle of Plassey, the EIC effectively governed Bengal. By 1800, it was the dominant political and military power on the Indian subcontinent.

The joint-stock company was historically significant not only for what it did — organize early modern global trade — but for what it represented: a new form of relationship between capital, law, and political power. It created a legal entity (the corporation) that had rights and could act independently of any individual person. It created a market for ownership claims (shares) that could be bought and sold independently of the underlying assets. It distributed risk across a broad investor base, allowing participation in long-distance commerce by people who could contribute capital but not labor. These features — perpetual legal existence, tradeable shares, limited liability, professional management — are the defining features of the modern business corporation, a form that still dominates global commerce.

Section 39.4

Silver, Currency, and Global Markets

No single commodity was more important to the early modern global economy than silver. Understanding why requires understanding the monetary context of the sixteenth century. In the medieval and early modern world, international trade was hampered by the absence of universally accepted currencies. Different states issued different coins of varying purity, denomination, and reliability. Bills of exchange and commercial credit partially solved this problem for merchants who trusted each other, but for trade across long distances and cultural frontiers, precious metal — especially silver — served as the universal medium of exchange.

The discovery and conquest of the great silver deposits of the Americas fundamentally changed the world's monetary supply. The silver mountain of Potosí, in what is now Bolivia, was discovered in 1545. Together with the Zacatecas mines opened in Mexico in 1546, these deposits produced quantities of silver that dwarfed anything previously known. Between 1500 and 1800, the Americas produced approximately 150,000 metric tons of silver — perhaps 85 percent of the world's total silver production during that period. This silver was minted into pesos (pieces of eight), which became the world's first truly global currency: recognized and accepted from the markets of Seville to the bazaars of Istanbul to the commercial houses of Canton.

The Spanish crown took approximately one-fifth of all silver production (the quinto real, or royal fifth) as a tax. This revenue funded the enormous costs of Spanish imperial governance, warfare, and administration — and also fueled a dramatic expansion of European purchasing power. Spanish merchants used this silver to purchase Asian commodities through intermediaries, and eventually directly through the Manila Galleon trade. The rest of Europe obtained access to American silver through trade with Spain, through piracy, or through direct involvement in Atlantic trade.

The effects of American silver on Europe were mixed and destabilizing. The influx of silver caused a sustained rise in prices — the "price revolution" of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries — that favored debtors (who repaid loans in cheaper money) and harmed creditors and wage earners. Landlords with fixed rents found their real incomes declining; merchants with access to trade goods found their real incomes rising. The price revolution was not caused solely by silver; population growth and harvest fluctuations also contributed. But the silver influx was a major driver of monetary instability.

The most consequential effect of American silver was on Asian markets, particularly China. The Ming dynasty had struggled for a century to maintain a workable monetary system, and settled on silver as the basis for taxation in the mid-sixteenth century. This created enormous demand: China was a huge economy with millions of taxpayers, and all of them now needed silver to pay the government. Chinese silver demand was structural and persistent, and it pulled American silver eastward through two routes. The overland route went via Europe and the Middle East and India and eventually into China through commercial exchange. The trans-Pacific route went directly via the Manila Galleon: Spanish ships carried American silver from Acapulco to Manila, where Chinese merchants (operating through Fujianese commercial networks) exchanged it for silk, porcelain, and other goods.

The global silver trade connected the mines of Peru and Mexico to the markets of China via the Pacific, to the markets of Asia and Africa via Europe and the Indian Ocean, and to the plantation economies of the Atlantic via Spanish and later Dutch, English, and French merchants. It created, for the first time, a genuinely worldwide price system in which price signals could travel from the mines of Peru to the markets of Beijing. This kind of global price interdependence was qualitatively new — and it meant that disruptions in one part of the system could send ripples across the entire world economy.

The dependence of Spain on American silver also created vulnerability. As silver production peaked in the early seventeenth century and then began a long decline, the costs of deeper mining rose and the richest ore was exhausted. The decline of Spanish imperial power — its bankruptcy, military defeats, and loss of commercial initiative to the Dutch and English — was partly a story of silver: the resource that had made Spain the world's dominant power in the sixteenth century could not be sustained indefinitely. Meanwhile, the economies of the Dutch Republic and England, which had used silver to build commercial and manufacturing capacity rather than to fund warfare and courtly display, proved more durable.

Section 39.5

Plantation Economies and Atlantic Commerce

If silver was the monetary spine of the early modern global economy, plantation agriculture was its muscular heart — at least within the Atlantic world. The plantation was a specific form of agricultural organization: a large estate devoted to the intensive production of a single cash crop for export, using a disciplined, coerced labor force. In the early modern Atlantic world, that labor force was predominantly enslaved people of African descent, imported across the Middle Passage from West and Central Africa. The plantation system, the Atlantic slave trade, and the commodities it produced — above all sugar, but also tobacco, cotton, indigo, and rice — were inextricably linked.

The plantation had roots in the medieval Mediterranean, where Italian merchants and the Crusader states had used enslaved labor on sugar estates in Cyprus and Sicily. Portuguese colonizers brought this model to the Atlantic islands of Madeira and São Tomé in the fifteenth century, and then to Brazil in the sixteenth. The key innovation was the substitution of enslaved Africans for the Mediterranean labor force. West and Central Africa became the labor supply for the Atlantic plantation system — initially because indigenous American populations had been catastrophically reduced by epidemic disease and conquest, and because African laborers proved resistant to certain tropical diseases that killed European workers, and because the transatlantic slave trade was a profitable enterprise in its own right that connected to existing African commercial networks.

Sugar was the first great plantation commodity of the Atlantic system. Produced first in Brazil (which remained the world's largest sugar producer through the early seventeenth century) and then in the Caribbean islands seized or developed by the Dutch, English, and French, sugar transformed the diets, economics, and cultures of the Atlantic world. Europeans who had once used honey as a sweetener or sugar as a rare luxury found sugar increasingly affordable and addictive. Demand expanded steadily from the sixteenth through the eighteenth centuries. Meeting that demand required more enslaved labor, more land brought under cultivation, more ships to carry raw sugar to European refineries, and more European manufactured goods to sell in Atlantic ports.

The scale of the Atlantic slave trade was enormous. Historians estimate that between 1500 and 1900, approximately 12.5 million Africans were forcibly transported to the Americas, of whom roughly 1.8 million died during the Middle Passage — the oceanic crossing from Africa to the Americas — due to disease, violence, and the brutal conditions of the slave ships. The peak of the trade was in the eighteenth century, when the Caribbean plantation boom was at its height. The enslaved people who survived the Middle Passage faced lives of brutal exploitation on plantation estates where mortality rates were so high that populations could only be maintained by continuous importation rather than natural increase.

The economic returns to plantation slavery were enormous — for slaveholders, merchants, refiners, and the states that taxed the trade. The ports that dominated the Atlantic slave trade — Bristol, Liverpool, Nantes, Bordeaux — were also centers of commercial and eventually industrial development. The profits of the sugar trade financed credit that flowed into agriculture, manufacturing, and transport. Tobacco, cultivated in Virginia and Maryland from the early seventeenth century, was the second major plantation commodity of the Anglo-American world. Rice cultivation in South Carolina and Georgia, and eventually cotton in the American South, followed similar trajectories. In each case, the plantation model — concentrated land ownership, coerced labor, single-crop production for export — shaped the social, economic, and political structures of the regions where it took root.

The Atlantic slave system was not a passive backdrop to early modern global commerce; it was one of its engines. The triangular trade — European manufactured goods to Africa, enslaved Africans to the Americas, American commodities to Europe — was one of the most commercially dynamic systems in the early modern world. It generated profits at every node: merchants who sold guns and cloth in West Africa, captains who transported enslaved people across the Atlantic, plantation owners who extracted labor from enslaved workers, and refiners and merchants who processed and sold plantation commodities in European markets. The costs — suffering, death, community destruction, demographic loss, political destabilization — were borne entirely by enslaved Africans and by the African communities from which they were taken.

Historical Vocabulary
Plantation Economy

A plantation economy is an economic system based on large agricultural estates (plantations) that produce a single cash crop for export, using a concentrated, coerced labor force. In the early modern Atlantic world, plantation economies relied on enslaved African labor and produced commodities such as sugar, tobacco, cotton, rice, and indigo. Plantation economies were characterized by concentrated land and capital ownership (typically by a small planter class), absence of economic diversification, dependence on external markets and credit, and structural reliance on coercion.

Historians study plantation economies both as economic systems — examining their productivity, profitability, and commercial linkages — and as social systems — examining the experiences of enslaved workers, the culture of planter elites, and the long-term effects of plantation labor on social structure, family life, and regional development. Understanding plantation economies is essential for understanding early modern Atlantic commerce, the origins of racial slavery, and the long-term economic legacies of colonialism.

Section 39.6

Indian Ocean Trade Under European Pressure

While the Atlantic world was being reorganized around plantation slavery and American silver, the Indian Ocean continued to function as one of the world's most commercially dynamic zones. But it was no longer outside European influence: the arrival of Portuguese, then Dutch, then English and French commercial-military power transformed the Indian Ocean's commercial geography, power dynamics, and institutional structures.

The Indian Ocean in 1500 was a mature commercial world. Its merchants — Arab, Gujarati, Swahili, Malay, Chinese — had developed over centuries a sophisticated set of commercial institutions: the dhow trade, the monsoon sailing calendar, systems of commercial partnerships, and a dense set of port cities that served as nodes of exchange, cultural mixing, and commercial intelligence. The commodities of the Indian Ocean trade were among the most valuable in the world: spices from the Maluku Islands (nutmeg, cloves, mace), pepper from the Malabar Coast, cotton textiles from India, silk from China and Persia, porcelain from China, and gold from East Africa and Southeast Asia.

The Portuguese arrived in this world in 1498 with a specific commercial strategy: to seize control of the key nodes of the spice trade by military force. Albuquerque's capture of Goa (1510), Malacca (1511), and Hormuz (1515) gave Portugal control over three of the most important commercial hubs in the Indian Ocean. The cartaz licensing system, enforced by Portuguese warships, attempted to extract revenue from all shipping passing through these zones. The system generated revenue but never achieved total control: the Indian Ocean was too large, the alternative routes too numerous, and the established merchants too commercially sophisticated to be simply displaced.

The arrival of the Dutch in the late sixteenth century intensified European commercial-military pressure. The VOC — with better capitalization, more disciplined military organization, and more sophisticated commercial intelligence than the Portuguese — moved quickly to displace Portugal from its key positions. By the 1620s, the VOC had established its headquarters at Batavia (modern Jakarta) in Java, expelled the Portuguese from key spice islands in the Maluku, and developed a network of trading posts across the Indian Ocean from Capetown to Nagasaki. Unlike the Portuguese, who had attempted to dominate existing trade routes, the VOC constructed a more systematic commercial empire, using inter-Asian trade (carrying goods between Asian ports) to generate profits that subsidized the expensive enterprise of oceanic commerce.

The English East India Company concentrated on the massive market for Indian cotton textiles. By the late seventeenth century, Indian cotton textiles were being imported to England in such quantities that they provoked political resistance from English wool and silk manufacturers — revealing the enormous demand for Indian cloth but also the political power of competing domestic producers. The EIC established trading posts (factories) at Surat, Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta, and built commercial relationships with Indian merchants and manufacturers.

What distinguished the Indian Ocean world from the Atlantic world during this period was not the absence of European commercial presence, but the continuing vitality of Asian commercial institutions alongside European ones. Gujarati merchants operated credit and commercial networks across the Indian Ocean that the VOC and EIC could not replicate or displace. The Mughal Empire could negotiate with European companies from a position of strength. Chinese commercial networks, operating through the ports of Fujian and Guangdong and spreading across Southeast Asia, were autonomous from European control. The Indian Ocean was not simply taken over by European companies; rather, it was restructured through the interaction of Asian and European commercial powers.

The shift came in the eighteenth century, when the weakening of the Mughal Empire after Aurangzeb's death (1707) opened space for the EIC to expand its territorial ambitions. The Battle of Plassey in 1757, in which the EIC's forces defeated the Nawab of Bengal, was a decisive turning point. By 1800, the relationship between European companies and Indian Ocean commerce had been fundamentally transformed: the EIC was no longer a commercial firm inserting itself into a pre-existing trade network, but an imperial power reshaping the economic structures of the Indian subcontinent.

Section 39.7

Consumer Goods and Cultural Change

One of the most striking features of early modern global commerce was its effect on the material culture and daily life of ordinary people — not only in Europe but across the world. The commodities that moved through early modern trade networks were not simply luxury goods consumed by elites. Many of them — sugar, tea, coffee, cotton cloth, tobacco, porcelain — became mass consumption goods that transformed the rhythms of daily life, the social meaning of objects, and the economic calculations of households across the world.

Sugar is the best-documented case. In 1600, refined sugar was a luxury, consumed mainly by wealthy European households and used in small quantities as a spice, medicine, and preservative. By 1700, it had become a regular household commodity for the growing European middle classes. By 1800, it was available to all but the very poorest European consumers, and British per capita sugar consumption had risen to levels that would have been inconceivable two centuries earlier. The social uses of sugar multiplied: it sweetened tea and coffee (themselves newly global commodities), preserved jams and jellies, appeared in baked goods, and became a marker of both comfort and status.

Tea and coffee underwent parallel transformations. Tea, originally a Chinese luxury, began to arrive in European markets in significant quantities in the early seventeenth century, imported by the VOC and EIC. Coffee, originating in Ethiopia and the Arabian Peninsula, spread first through the Ottoman Empire — where coffeehouses became major sites of social and commercial life by the late sixteenth century — and then to Europe, where coffeehouses multiplied rapidly in the seventeenth century. Both beverages were stimulants that changed the rhythm of the working day; both required sugar to be widely palatable to European consumers; both stimulated demand for Asian goods and created commercial incentives that shaped the structure of world trade.

Cotton cloth underwent a similar transformation. Before the early modern period, European consumers primarily wore wool and linen. Indian cotton cloth — lightweight, washable, colorfully dyed, and relatively inexpensive — began arriving in European markets through the EIC in the late seventeenth century. The demand was explosive. Indian calicoes and chintzes became fashionable across European society. So great was the demand that European manufacturers organized politically to restrict Indian cloth imports — leading to the English Calico Acts and their French equivalents. This attempt to protect domestic industry ultimately redirected European commercial and technological energy toward producing cotton cloth domestically, a process that became one of the drivers of the Industrial Revolution.

Porcelain — Chinese blue-and-white and later polychrome ceramic — became one of the most sought-after luxury goods in Europe during the seventeenth century. The VOC transported millions of pieces of Chinese porcelain to European markets. European potters attempted for decades to replicate the formula for hard-paste porcelain, finally succeeding at Meissen in Saxony around 1710. The passion for Chinese and then European porcelain — "china" — shaped interior design, table culture, and the social rituals of tea and coffee drinking across European society.

Tobacco — cultivated in Virginia and Maryland by enslaved and indentured labor, and also produced in Brazil and the Caribbean — spread across the world with remarkable speed after the sixteenth century. By 1600, tobacco smoking had reached not only Europe but also the Ottoman Empire, Persia, Mughal India, Japan, and West Africa. It became one of the most globally consumed commodities in world history within a century of European contact with the Americas.

The global flow of consumer goods created new social meanings and social distinctions. The ability to display fine porcelain, to serve sugar in one's tea, to wear fashionably printed cotton — these became markers of social status, taste, and membership in an emerging "polite" commercial culture. The desire for these commodities — the market demand they generated — was one of the primary engines driving the expansion of plantation agriculture, the Atlantic slave trade, and the commercial empires of the VOC and EIC. The material culture of European middle-class households was profoundly shaped by colonial commodities produced by workers they would never see, in conditions they rarely knew.

Section 39.8

Finance, Insurance, and Commercial Risk

Long-distance oceanic trade was inherently risky. Ships could sink, be captured by pirates or hostile navies, or arrive to find that the commodity they carried had glutted the market. Commercial partners in distant ports could default on debts. Governments could change their policies. Currencies could be debased. The institutional challenge of early modern commerce was not only to organize the physical movement of goods but to manage the financial risks associated with that movement — and to do so in ways that kept capital flowing into commercial ventures despite the ever-present possibility of catastrophic loss.

Early modern merchants developed three main financial instruments to manage commercial risk: insurance, credit (especially the bill of exchange), and equity shares in trading companies.

Marine insurance was the most direct form of risk management. The basic concept was ancient: a merchant or ship owner paid a premium to an underwriter who agreed to compensate the insured for losses due to maritime disasters. In the early modern period, marine insurance became more institutionalized and widespread. Genoa and Venice were early centers of insurance practice in the Mediterranean. By the seventeenth century, Amsterdam had become the world's leading insurance market. London's insurance market emerged in the late seventeenth century, centered on Lloyd's Coffee House (established around 1688), where ship captains and merchants posted information about voyages and underwriters offered coverage. Lloyd's evolved into a formal insurance market and eventually into the Lloyd's of London that still operates today.

The bill of exchange was a credit instrument that allowed merchants to conduct long-distance trade without physically transporting precious metal for every transaction. A merchant in London could draw a bill on a correspondent in Amsterdam, payable in thirty days, in exchange for a shipment of goods. The correspondent would accept the bill, guaranteeing payment, and the London merchant could sell or discount the bill to a third party who wanted funds in Amsterdam. Through this mechanism, a single stock of capital could support a much larger volume of trade than would otherwise be possible. The bill of exchange was the foundation of early modern commercial credit and a precursor of modern financial instruments.

Joint-stock shares, as discussed in section 39.3, were the third major financial innovation. By allowing ownership of a commercial enterprise to be divided into tradeable units, joint-stock companies created a new kind of financial asset — one whose value could rise and fall based on the company's commercial performance and investors' expectations. The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, opened in 1602 with the VOC's shares as its primary instrument, was the world's first secondary market for equity shares.

The convergence of insurance, credit, and equity markets in Amsterdam in the early seventeenth century created the world's first recognizably modern financial system. The Amsterdam Exchange Bank (Wisselbank), founded in 1609 to facilitate the clearing of commercial bills and to provide stable currency for trade, added a central banking function to this institutional complex. Together, these institutions — stock exchange, insurance market, commercial bank — gave Amsterdam a financial infrastructure that dramatically reduced the transaction costs of long-distance trade and made the Dutch Republic the world's most commercially efficient economy in the seventeenth century.

England developed its own financial infrastructure during the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, partly inspired by and competing with the Dutch model. The Bank of England, founded in 1694, provided a similar function to the Amsterdam Exchange Bank. These institutions gave England the financial capacity to sustain expensive wars of the eighteenth century through government borrowing backed by stable credit markets, and eventually to fund the capital investments of the Industrial Revolution.

The early eighteenth century also witnessed the first great financial bubbles and crashes associated with joint-stock speculation. The South Sea Bubble (England, 1720) and the Mississippi Company bubble (France, 1720) were both catastrophic collapses of speculative enthusiasm for joint-stock companies with implausible commercial prospects. Both involved governments using joint-stock speculation to manage national debt, creating perverse incentives for promotion and fraud. Both ended in financial disaster for thousands of investors. These crises revealed the speculative dangers inherent in financial markets where information was asymmetric, regulation was minimal, and investor expectations could become detached from commercial reality — a recurring pattern in the history of capitalism.

Section 39.9

Early Capitalism and Global Inequality

The economic world of 1450–1750 was the seedbed of capitalism — but historians disagree sharply about precisely when capitalism began, what it essentially consisted of, and how to characterize the relationship between early modern global commerce and the capitalism that became dominant in the nineteenth century.

At the most basic level, capitalism can be defined as an economic system characterized by private ownership of the means of production, commodity production for competitive markets, the use of wage labor, capital accumulation as a primary motive for economic activity, and market-determined prices for goods, labor, and capital. By this definition, capitalism in its fullest form was not yet fully present in the early modern world. Many forms of production were organized through coerced labor (slavery, serfdom, the mita) rather than wage labor. Many markets were shaped by monopoly privileges, guild regulations, and state intervention that limited competition. Capital accumulation was often reinvested in land, status goods, and military power rather than productive investment.

Yet clearly, something new was happening. Merchants in Amsterdam, London, and Seville were organizing production, managing risk, deploying capital, and responding to price signals across oceanic distances in ways that look recognizably modern. Joint-stock companies were accumulating capital at unprecedented scales and deploying it in pursuit of profit. Markets for equity shares, insurance, and commercial credit were functioning with a degree of sophistication that had no earlier parallel. Commercial profit — not military conquest, religious donation, or aristocratic display — was becoming an acceptable and even admirable motivation for economic activity.

The historian Immanuel Wallerstein, in his influential but contested Modern World-System (1974), argued that a capitalist world-system emerged in the sixteenth century, centered on the growing economies of Western Europe and encompassing a core, a semiperiphery, and a periphery. In Wallerstein's framework, capitalism was from the beginning a global system defined by the exploitation of peripheral regions by core ones. The plantation economies of the Americas, the silver mines worked by mita labor, and the slave-raided societies of West Africa were not pre-capitalist survivals: they were integral components of an emerging capitalist world economy, supplying the raw materials and forced labor that made core accumulation possible.

Critics of Wallerstein argue that his framework is too sweeping — that it conflates capitalism with market exchange and obscures the genuine differences between the commercial capitalism of the early modern period and the industrial capitalism of the nineteenth century. Some historians reserve the term "capitalism" for the period after industrial production transformed the labor process and created the fully developed wage labor relationship. Others locate capitalism's origins not in the sixteenth century but in agricultural changes in fifteenth-century England, where enclosure and commercialization of farming created a population dependent on wage labor and market access.

Whatever the theoretical resolution of these debates, a few historical conclusions seem clear. First, the early modern global economy was structured by profound inequality — between those who owned ships, companies, and plantation estates, and those whose labor (free or coerced) produced the commodities that circulated through the system. Second, this inequality had a geographic dimension: the regions that supplied raw materials and coerced labor — the Americas, West and Central Africa, and eventually South and Southeast Asia — were systematically disadvantaged relative to the regions that organized, transported, and commercially processed their products. Third, the institutions, practices, and accumulated capital of early modern global commerce provided the material and organizational foundations on which the Industrial Revolution was built. Understanding how the world got from 1450 to 1750 is essential for understanding how it got from 1750 to the present.

Historical Vocabulary
Capital Accumulation

Capital accumulation refers to the process by which surplus value generated through production or exchange is reinvested to expand productive capacity, rather than being consumed or dissipated. In economic history, capital accumulation is one of the defining features of capitalism: merchants, manufacturers, and eventually industrial firms reinvest profits into ships, tools, factories, and trained labor in order to produce more goods, generate more profit, and accumulate more capital in an expanding cycle.

In the early modern period, capital accumulated through trade was often reinvested in more ships, more trading voyages, and larger commercial enterprises, and eventually in manufacturing. The joint-stock company was a major vehicle for capital accumulation at scale. Understanding capital accumulation is important for understanding both the dynamism of early modern commerce and the inequalities it produced, since capital accumulated in some regions — especially Western Europe — often at the expense of labor and resources extracted from others.

Section 39.10

Chapter Review and Global Trade System Checkpoint

The period from 1450 to 1750 witnessed the creation of the world's first truly global trading system. American silver connected the mines of Peru and Mexico to the markets of China. Atlantic plantation agriculture, powered by enslaved African labor, produced commodities that transformed European consumption. Indian Ocean commerce, reorganized and partly controlled by European joint-stock companies, continued to be one of the world's most commercially dynamic zones. New financial institutions — stock exchanges, insurance markets, commercial banks — gave European merchants tools for managing risk and accumulating capital that had no earlier parallel.

The organizing ideology of this commercial world was mercantilism: the belief that national wealth depended on a favorable balance of trade and that states had the duty to manage commerce in the national interest. Mercantilist policy generated Navigation Acts, colonial monopolies, and chartered trading companies. It also generated theoretical critiques, culminating in Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations, which articulated the alternative vision of free trade and market efficiency that would shape economic policy for the next two centuries.

The institutional innovations of early modern global commerce — joint-stock companies, stock exchanges, commercial banks, marine insurance markets — were not merely adaptations to the needs of trade. They were the foundations of modern capitalism. The early modern global economy was also a system of profound and structurally generated inequality. The plantation system was built on slavery, and its profits came from the exploitation of millions of enslaved Africans. The silver economy was built on indigenous forced labor. The global trading system of 1450–1750 created the conditions for later economic development in some parts of the world precisely by extracting labor and resources from others.

Regional Lens

The Dutch Republic and the Commercial Revolution

The Dutch Republic — the United Provinces of the Netherlands, which achieved independence from Spain in a revolt beginning in 1568 — was the world's dominant commercial power in the seventeenth century, despite being a small, densely populated, and resource-poor state. Its commercial success was not an accident of geography or natural endowment; it was the product of specific institutional innovations, political arrangements, and commercial practices that made it the most efficient trading economy in the world.

The Dutch Republic's geography gave it one significant natural advantage: the Rhine, Maas, and Schelde rivers converged in the Low Countries, making Amsterdam the natural entrepôt for trade between the North Sea and the interior of Europe. Dutch merchants had dominated the Baltic grain trade since the medieval period, developing efficient bulk-cargo shipping (the fluyt, a cheap, capacious vessel requiring small crews) that gave them lower transportation costs than any competitor.

What distinguished the Dutch Republic was its political structure. Unlike France, Spain, or England — monarchies in which commercial policy was ultimately determined by royal court politics — the Republic was governed by a merchant oligarchy. The regents (regenten) who controlled city governments were themselves merchants, and commercial policy reflected their interests directly. Religious tolerance — the Republic accepted Jewish, Huguenot, and Catholic merchants expelled from less tolerant neighbors — concentrated commercial talent and capital in Amsterdam.

The VOC was the institutional expression of Dutch commercial ambition at oceanic scale. The Amsterdam Exchange Bank (1609) provided a stable clearing currency that facilitated international trade without the uncertainty associated with the multiple, often debased coins circulating in European commerce. These innovations — the modern corporation, the stock market, the commercial bank, marine insurance — became the foundations of modern financial capitalism, and they were first systematically developed in the Dutch Republic.

Dutch commercial hegemony peaked in the mid-seventeenth century and then declined, as larger and more powerful states — England and France — combined commercial sophistication with greater military and demographic resources. By 1700, England had absorbed many of the institutional lessons of Dutch commercial capitalism and combined them with a larger territorial base and more powerful navy. Yet the Dutch legacy was permanent: the institutional forms developed in Amsterdam defined the shape of the global commercial economy for centuries to come.

Global Connection

American Silver and the Chinese Economy

One of the most striking examples of early modern global economic integration is the flow of American silver to China. This connection linked the silver mines of the Andes and Mexico to the cotton textile producers of Fujian, the porcelain workshops of Jingdezhen, and the tea plantations of Guangdong — and it did so through two distinct oceanic routes, creating a global circuit that tied together the Americas, Europe, Africa, and Asia.

The connection began with Chinese monetary policy. The Ming dynasty had struggled for over a century with an unstable monetary system. In the sixteenth century, the government shifted to collecting taxes in silver — a decision that created structural demand for the metal across a vast empire. But China had relatively limited domestic silver deposits. American silver, primarily from the Andes and Mexico, was needed to meet this demand.

American silver reached China via two main routes. The western route went across the Atlantic to Spain, then via trade to Europe's commercial centers, then via the Indian Ocean (often carried by Portuguese and later Dutch and English merchants) to Indian ports, and finally via Chinese merchants through Southeast Asian entrepôts to Chinese ports. The eastern route was more direct: the Manila Galleon trade, established in 1565, carried silver from Acapulco across the Pacific to Manila, where Chinese merchants exchanged it for silk, porcelain, lacquerware, and cotton cloth.

The effects in China were complex. Silver influx fueled commercial expansion in coastal China and enabled growing commercialization of agriculture, craft production, and trade. Chinese merchants spread across Southeast Asia, operating as commercial intermediaries between the Chinese economy and the Indian Ocean and Pacific trading worlds. But silver dependence also created vulnerability. When Spanish silver production declined in the early seventeenth century, monetary contraction in China contributed to an economic crisis in the 1640s — falling government revenues, inability to pay armies, peasant rebellions — that was one of the factors in the fall of the Ming dynasty and its replacement by the Qing in 1644. The global silver circuit thus connected American mine production not only to Chinese luxury goods but, eventually, to Chinese dynastic politics.

Network Study

The Manila Galleon — A Trans-Pacific Artery

The Manila Galleon trade, operating from 1565 to 1815, was one of the most remarkable commercial circuits in world history. It connected the Spanish colonial port of Acapulco (on Mexico's Pacific coast) to Manila (capital of the Spanish Philippines) via an annual or semi-annual voyage, carrying American silver westward and Asian goods eastward.

The route exploited the Pacific's wind systems: the westward crossing from Acapulco used the North Equatorial Current and northeast trade winds to reach the Philippines in about three months; the return voyage (the difficult tornaviaje) used the North Pacific Gyre — sailing north to Japan and then east across the Pacific — and took four to six months. The return voyage was extraordinarily arduous: scurvy, storms, and dysentery killed hundreds of sailors and passengers on the worst crossings.

American silver — minted at the royal mints of Potosí and Mexico City into standardized pesos — went westward to Manila, where it was exchanged with Chinese merchants (the Sangleys, Fujianese migrants who dominated Manila's commerce) for silk, porcelain, lacquerware, cotton cloth, and spices from across Asia. These goods went eastward to Acapulco, where they were unloaded and distributed to Mexican and Peruvian consumers, or re-exported across the Atlantic to Spain.

Manila itself became one of the most commercially diverse cities in the world: Spanish colonial officials, Augustinian and Dominican missionaries, Fujianese Chinese merchants, Malay traders, Japanese merchants, and indigenous Tagalog people all coexisted in a complex urban space. The Parian — the Chinese commercial quarter of Manila — was one of the largest concentrations of Chinese merchants outside China. The Manila Galleon trade drained enormous quantities of American silver into Asia — historians estimate that perhaps half of all silver mined in the Americas eventually ended up in China, much of it via Manila — demonstrating centuries before modern containerized shipping that the Pacific Ocean was a navigable space with its own commercial geography.

Primary Source Window

Thomas Mun on England's Trade (c. 1630, published 1664)

Creator: Thomas Mun · Date written: c. 1630 · Published: 1664 · Type: Economic treatise / mercantilist doctrine / commercial advocacy

Context: Thomas Mun (1571–1641) was an English merchant who served as a director of the East India Company. His treatise England's Treasure by Forraign Trade was written around 1630 and published posthumously in 1664. It is the most influential statement of English mercantilist doctrine, written specifically to defend the East India Company against critics who argued that exporting silver to Asia was harmful to England's economy.

Mun argued that the export of silver to Asia was not a drain on England's wealth, as critics claimed, but the opposite: by using silver to purchase Asian spices and textiles, England could re-export these goods to the rest of Europe at a profit, bringing more silver back to England than had been sent out. The key to national wealth, he maintained, was not the quantity of money in the kingdom at any moment, but the overall balance of trade — whether the kingdom exported more value than it imported.

Analysis: Mun's treatise is important as evidence of how early modern merchants understood commerce and as an artifact of the ideological struggle over commercial policy. His defense of the East India Company was self-interested — he was a director of the company — but it was also intellectually sophisticated, anticipating several arguments that later economists would formalize. His concept of the balance of trade as the key metric of national commercial health was the organizing principle of mercantilist policy throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The treatise also reveals the political context of early modern commerce: merchants constantly had to justify their activities to skeptical governments, aristocracies, and publics who associated trade with dishonesty and the export of precious metal with national impoverishment.

What the source does not tell us: Mun's account is entirely from the perspective of the English merchant class. It does not address the perspectives of Asian merchants and producers whose commercial networks the EIC was inserting itself into. It does not address the labor conditions in Asian production. It does not address the violence — commercial-military coercion — through which the EIC maintained its commercial position.

  4. How would an Indian merchant at Surat, or a Chinese silk weaver, have understood the same commercial relationships that Mun is describing?
Evidence Window

The Amsterdam Stock Exchange Ledger

Description: The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, opened in 1602 alongside the founding of the VOC, was the world's first modern stock market — a place where shares in a commercial company could be bought and sold by investors who had no direct involvement in the company's operations. The Exchange's records, supplemented by the meticulous account books of the VOC itself, provide extraordinarily detailed evidence of the world's first equity market.

What scholars can infer: The VOC records document the range of early investors: wealthy Amsterdam merchants (the obvious participants), but also middle-class citizens, widows managing inherited capital, and even craftsmen and servants who put small sums into VOC shares. Share price records allow economic historians to reconstruct the market's behavior: how prices responded to news of successful voyages, military defeats, spice gluts, and political changes in the Far East. The records also reveal the emergence of recognizably modern financial instruments, including short selling (betting that prices would fall) and options (contracts to buy or sell at a future price) — instruments that economists once thought were modern inventions but that Dutch investors were using as early as the 1630s.

Limits of the evidence: The Amsterdam records document the formal, institutional world of the exchange — the transactions, prices, and participants who left written traces. They tell us little about the informal commercial world alongside the exchange: the verbal agreements, the personal networks, the bribery and corruption that were also parts of early modern commercial life. They document the experiences of investors in Europe, not the experiences of the Asian merchants, producers, and laborers whose activities generated the profits being traded on the exchange. And they cannot directly tell us how representative Amsterdam's financial innovations were, or how rapidly they diffused to other parts of the world.

Historical Debate

Was the Atlantic Slave Trade Economically Central to Early Capitalism?

The question: Historians have long debated the precise relationship between the Atlantic slave trade and the development of early capitalism in Europe. Was plantation slavery a peripheral component of early modern commerce — important but not structurally central — or was it a foundational element without which European commercial and eventually industrial capitalism could not have developed?

Interpretation A — The Peripheral View: Some economic historians, using quantitative methods to assess the contribution of slave trade profits to overall European capital accumulation, have argued that the direct profits of the slave trade and plantation slavery, while real, were too small a percentage of total European national income to have been a decisive driver of industrialization. This view emphasizes that Britain's industrial revolution drew primarily on domestic resources — coal, labor, technology, and institutional structures — rather than on colonial profits. Slave-produced commodities (sugar, tobacco) were primarily consumer goods rather than industrial inputs.
Interpretation B — The Foundational View: Other historians, drawing on the tradition established by the Trinidadian historian Eric Williams in Capitalism and Slavery (1944), argue that the Atlantic slave system was structurally central to early capitalism in ways that cannot be reduced to direct profit percentages. This view emphasizes that plantation slavery generated not only direct profits for slave traders and planters, but also commercial credit, insurance, shipping, refining, and distribution industries that employed capital across the wider British economy. The capital accumulated in Bristol and Liverpool through the slave trade financed banks, canals, and manufacturing firms. Calculating the "direct profit" of the slave trade misses the systemic ways in which slavery was woven into the fabric of the early modern Atlantic economy.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the same body of quantitative evidence — profit records, national income estimates, capital flow calculations — but interpret it differently. The peripheral view relies on aggregate calculations showing slave trade profits as a small percentage of GDP. The foundational view emphasizes the systemic linkages that aggregate calculations cannot capture and points to qualitative evidence showing the concrete ways in which Atlantic slave wealth was reinvested in British economic development.

Why it matters: The debate has implications for how we understand the origins of modern global inequality, for debates about historical reparations, and for how we interpret the economic relationship between the Global North and South. If slavery was peripheral, then European prosperity was primarily the result of internal institutional and technological development. If slavery was foundational, then contemporary global inequalities have direct historical origins in the Atlantic slave system.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, evaluate which interpretation is better supported. What additional evidence would most decisively resolve the debate? What are the methodological challenges in answering questions about the relationship between historical commercial practices and long-term economic development?

Comparison Across Societies

European and Indian Ocean Commercial Institutions

One of the most instructive comparisons in early modern economic history is between the commercial institutions of Western Europe (especially the Dutch Republic and England) and those of the Indian Ocean world (especially Gujarati and other South Asian merchant communities). This comparison reveals both the distinctiveness of European institutional innovation and the sophistication of non-European commercial organization — and challenges simplistic narratives of European commercial superiority.

European innovations: The key institutional innovations of European early modern commerce — the joint-stock company with tradeable shares, the stock exchange, the commercial bank, marine insurance markets — were genuinely novel. They allowed capital to be pooled across large numbers of investors, risk to be distributed and priced, and commercial enterprises to be organized at scales and durations impossible for family firms or temporary partnerships. They also created a legal framework backed by state power and could be enforced across cultural and geographic frontiers. The VOC and EIC combined these commercial innovations with military power in ways that gave them coercive capacity to enforce monopolies and extract commercial advantage.

Indian Ocean institutions: Gujarati merchant communities — both Hindu (especially Baniya) and Jain — had developed over centuries a commercial system of extraordinary sophistication based on different principles. Rather than pooling capital from anonymous investors, Gujarati commercial networks were built on dense personal and community relationships: kinship, caste, religious community, and long-term personal reputation provided the trust infrastructure for commercial partnerships and credit. The hawala system (informal value transfer based on personal trust networks) allowed merchants to move funds across enormous distances without physically transporting money. Gujarati merchants maintained agents and family members in ports from East Africa to Southeast Asia, providing commercial intelligence and guaranteeing contracts through personal accountability.

Comparison: European joint-stock companies could mobilize capital at scales impossible for family-based networks and could use military force to secure commercial positions — but they were expensive to operate and were embedded in systems of state privilege and monopoly. Gujarati networks were highly efficient within the community, with low transaction costs and deep commercial intelligence — but they were limited in scale by the boundaries of personal trust and had no coercive capacity against outside actors. In the seventeenth century, Gujarati merchants and VOC traders operated in the same commercial spaces and competed and cooperated simultaneously. It was only in the eighteenth century, when European territorial imperialism began to restructure the institutional environment of Indian Ocean commerce, that the advantage shifted decisively toward European commercial institutions.

Multiple Perspectives

The Global Commodity Chain of Sugar

Sugar is one of the most revealing commodities through which to examine the multiple perspectives of the early modern global economy. The sugar that a European middle-class household stirred into tea in the eighteenth century had passed through a chain of production, processing, and distribution involving people with radically different experiences of the same economic system.

The enslaved worker on the sugar plantation Primarily in Barbados, Jamaica, Saint-Domingue, or Brazil, the enslaved worker experienced sugar production as brutal physical labor: cutting cane in tropical heat, feeding it through presses, boiling the juice in open vats under the supervision of overseers who used violence to enforce work discipline. Life expectancy on Caribbean sugar plantations was so low that slave populations could not sustain themselves through natural reproduction; constant importation of newly enslaved Africans was required. Sugar, for the enslaved worker, was not a commodity: it was the form in which their labor was extracted.
The plantation owner In Barbados or Jamaica, the plantation owner experienced sugar as the source of significant wealth — though also of debt, because upfront costs were enormous and planters typically operated on credit provided by London or Bristol merchants. Successful planters amassed fortunes that funded education for their children in England and political careers in colonial assemblies. The plantation owner also lived with constant anxiety about slave rebellion, which was a persistent reality throughout the Caribbean plantation world.
The Atlantic merchant In London, Bristol, Liverpool, Nantes, or Bordeaux, the Atlantic merchant experienced the sugar trade as one node in a complex commercial circuit — financing the purchase of enslaved people in West Africa, receiving a commission on sugar shipped to their warehouse, selling sugar to refiners. The human reality of the plantation was mediated through bills of exchange, insurance policies, and price reports from Atlantic ports; it was distant and abstract.
The sugar refiner In London or Amsterdam, refineries were labor-intensive operations, employing skilled workers who boiled, filtered, and crystallized raw Caribbean sugar into the white loaf sugar sold in European markets. The refiner's experience was of a domestic industrial operation connected to distant commodity markets through commercial credit and shipping.
The European consumer The clerk's wife in London who sweetened her tea, or the Parisian baker who used sugar in pastries, experienced sugar as a household commodity whose price fluctuated with Atlantic market conditions. As sugar became cheaper through the eighteenth century, its consumption spread from wealthy households to the middling classes. The consumer's experience was primarily of a pleasurable enhancement to daily life — with no consciousness, typically, of the plantation labor that produced it.

These five perspectives do not represent equal experiences of the same economic system. They represent radically different positions in a system structured by coercion, exploitation, and inequality. Understanding the sugar economy requires holding all five perspectives simultaneously — not to create false equivalence, but to understand the full human reality of early modern global commerce.

Cause and Effect

The Rise of the Dutch Commercial Empire, 1580–1650

Long-Term Causes

Dutch commercial dominance in Baltic grain trade; development of the efficient fluyt ship; sophisticated financial practices; Rhine-Maas-Schelde geography making Amsterdam the natural entrepôt of Northern European commerce; Calvinist religious culture broadly compatible with commercial activity; merchant oligarchy political structure aligning commercial interests with state policy.

Short-Term Causes

The revolt against Spanish rule (beginning 1568) creating political framework for commercial independence; the fall of Antwerp (1585) driving commercial capital northward to Amsterdam; the union of Spanish and Portuguese crowns (1580–1640) allowing the Dutch — at war with Spain — to seize Portuguese commercial positions in Asia.

The Transformation

Between roughly 1590 and 1650, Dutch merchants and the VOC displaced Portugal from key Indian Ocean nodes, established commercial dominance in the Baltic and North Sea, and built an Atlantic commercial empire including trading posts in Brazil, the Caribbean, North America, and West Africa. By 1650, Amsterdam was the world's leading commercial and financial city.

Consequences

Dutch commercial dominance funded the Dutch Golden Age (Rembrandt, Vermeer, Spinoza, Grotius). Dutch institutional innovations — VOC, stock exchange, Exchange Bank, marine insurance markets — were absorbed by competitors, especially England. Anglo-Dutch Wars (1652–1674) damaged Dutch commercial power. By 1700, England had overtaken the Dutch Republic, using Dutch institutional models combined with greater territorial and naval resources.

Historical complexity: The rise and fall of Dutch commercial hegemony cannot be reduced to any single cause. Geography, religion, political structure, military conflict, institutional innovation, and the specific contingencies of Portuguese, Spanish, and English decisions all played roles. The Dutch story also illustrates a recurring pattern in commercial history: that commercial innovations, once demonstrated, can be copied by larger and better-resourced competitors, eventually disadvantaging the innovator.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Indian Ocean Trading World, 1450–1750

What Changed

  • Portuguese forcible insertion into the Indian Ocean (1498–1515) introduced a new actor willing and able to use cannon-armed warships to seize key commercial nodes.
  • Dutch displacement of the Portuguese in the seventeenth century intensified European commercial pressure.
  • The EIC's growing territorial ambitions in the eighteenth century culminated in the Battle of Plassey (1757), prefiguring British commercial and political domination of South Asia.
  • European demand for spices was partly met by the VOC's monopolization of the Maluku spice islands, reducing the volume available to Asian merchants.
  • Indian cotton textiles emerged as the dominant commodity of the seventeenth-century Indian Ocean, generating enormous commercial flows.

What Stayed the Same

  • Arab and Indian dhow merchants continued to operate across the western Indian Ocean, using the monsoon calendar and personal commercial networks that had characterized Indian Ocean trade for centuries.
  • Gujarati commercial networks — based on kinship, caste, and community trust — maintained their dominance of commercial credit across the Indian Ocean into the eighteenth century.
  • The major trading ports — Surat, Calicut, Malacca, Aden — continued to function as cosmopolitan commercial hubs.
  • The monsoon's seasonal rhythm continued to organize the timing and direction of trade.

Why the pattern matters: The partial nature of change in the Indian Ocean world — significant European commercial-military pressure alongside continuing Asian commercial vitality — helps explain why the eventual British commercial domination of South Asia in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries required military conquest rather than simple commercial competition. It also challenges any narrative of simple European commercial takeover, revealing instead a more complex reality of interaction, competition, and coexistence between different commercial systems.

Everyday Life and Social History

The Merchant's World in Amsterdam, 1650

For a prosperous Amsterdam merchant in the mid-seventeenth century, daily life was shaped by the institutions of the commercial revolution in concrete and tangible ways. His day began with a visit to the Beurs (Exchange) — the commercial and financial heart of Amsterdam, where merchants gathered to buy and sell bills of exchange, share information about shipping movements and commodity prices, negotiate commercial contracts, and socialize with the commercial community. The Beurs was not merely a trading floor; it was a social institution, a labor exchange, an insurance market, and a news exchange combined. Information — about the arrival of spice ships, the political situation in Asia, the credit-worthiness of commercial partners — was the most valuable commodity traded there.

The merchant's house was itself an economic institution. The ground floor might contain a counting-house where clerks maintained ledgers, wrote correspondence, and managed the paperwork of commerce. The warehouse (pakhuis) — many Amsterdam merchants had warehouses directly accessible by canal boat — stored imported goods awaiting distribution. The merchant's domestic life was organized around the rhythms of commercial correspondence: letters from agents in Seville, Batavia, Nagasaki, and Surat arrived by ship, often months after they were written, and required prompt response and careful analysis.

The domestic culture of Amsterdam merchant families was itself shaped by global commerce. The furniture in their houses included Chinese lacquerware, Japanese screens, and Delft pottery imitating Chinese blue-and-white. Their tables were set with Chinese or European porcelain. They drank coffee sweetened with Caribbean sugar, wore cotton cloth from India (or, in the later seventeenth century, from Leiden mills imitating Indian designs), and decorated their walls with spice-trade maps and ships' portraits. The material culture of the Amsterdam merchant house was a kind of inventory of the global trading world in miniature.

For the sailors and workers who actually operated the ships and warehouses of the commercial empire, the experience was harder. VOC sailors on Asian voyages faced disease (dysentery, scurvy, malaria), violence, and the brutal conditions of wooden sailing vessels on multi-year voyages. Mortality rates on VOC voyages in the seventeenth century were high — some historians estimate that perhaps one-third of VOC sailors who embarked for Asia never returned. The sailors who worked the VOC's ships were recruited from across Europe — Dutch, German, Scandinavian, and others — and their experience of the commercial revolution was not prosperity and cosmopolitan culture but physical hardship, institutional discipline, and high risk of death far from home. The merchant's world and the sailor's world were parts of the same system — connected by the same ships, the same ledgers, and the same profits — but experienced in radically different ways.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 39 has examined the growth of global trade and early capitalism between 1450 and 1750. This period witnessed the creation of the world's first genuinely global trading system — one in which commercial circuits connected the mines of the Andes, the plantations of the Caribbean, the cotton workshops of Gujarat, the porcelain kilns of Jingdezhen, the spice islands of the Maluku, and the financial markets of Amsterdam and London into a single, interdependent economic world.

The geographic expansion of this system was made possible by European oceanic navigation. Portuguese mariners opened the direct sea route between Europe and the Indian Ocean; Spanish conquest and colonization integrated the Americas into world commerce for the first time. The Indian Ocean's existing commercial networks — Arab, Indian, Malay, Chinese — were not replaced but reorganized under growing European commercial-military pressure, initially from Portugal, then the Dutch VOC, and eventually the English EIC.

American silver was the monetary lubricant of the early modern global economy. The vast deposits of Potosí and Zacatecas, extracted by indigenous forced labor, produced quantities of precious metal that reshaped monetary systems from Spain to China. The flow of silver eastward — via the Indian Ocean and via the trans-Pacific Manila Galleon trade — reflected Chinese structural demand for silver as a tax medium, and created a global price system in which commercial information could travel from the mines of Peru to the markets of Beijing.

The Atlantic plantation economy, powered by enslaved African labor, was the second great motor of early modern global commerce. Sugar, tobacco, cotton, rice, and indigo — produced on plantation estates in Brazil, the Caribbean, and North America — generated enormous commercial flows that enriched planters, merchants, refiners, and the states that taxed them. The human cost was borne by the approximately 12.5 million Africans forcibly transported to the Americas, of whom millions died during the Middle Passage or in the brutal conditions of plantation labor.

The organizational innovation that made early modern global commerce possible at oceanic scale was the joint-stock company. The VOC (1602) and EIC (1600) were the most consequential examples: they pooled capital from many investors, distributed risk through tradeable shares, and combined commercial functions with governmental powers. The financial infrastructure that developed alongside these companies — stock exchanges, commercial banks, marine insurance markets — created the institutional foundations of modern capitalism.

Mercantilism — the dominant economic doctrine of the period — held that national wealth depended on accumulating precious metal through a favorable balance of trade, and that states had the right and duty to manage commerce through tariffs, monopolies, and colonial systems. Its contradictions eventually generated the systematic critique articulated by Adam Smith in Wealth of Nations (1776), which articulated the vision of free trade and market efficiency that would shape economic policy for the next two centuries.

The consumer goods of early modern global commerce — sugar, tea, coffee, cotton cloth, porcelain, tobacco — transformed daily life and material culture across the world. European middle-class households of the eighteenth century consumed commodities produced on the other side of the world by workers they would never see, in conditions they rarely knew. The global commodity chains that connected plantation labor to consumer markets were among the most consequential social structures of the early modern period.

Financial innovation — insurance, commercial credit, the bill of exchange, joint-stock shares — allowed merchants to manage risk and accumulate capital at unprecedented scales. The Amsterdam financial market of the early seventeenth century was the world's first recognizably modern financial system, subsequently copied and surpassed by English financial institutions that provided the infrastructure for British commercial and eventually industrial dominance.

The early modern global economy was structured by profound and structurally generated inequality. The regions that supplied raw materials and coerced labor — the Americas, West and Central Africa, and eventually South and Southeast Asia — were systematically disadvantaged relative to the regions that organized, transported, and commercially processed their products. The accumulated capital of early modern global commerce provided the material foundations for later industrialization. Understanding the period from 1450 to 1750 is therefore essential not only for understanding early modern world history, but for understanding the origins of the modern global economy's patterns of wealth and poverty, development and underdevelopment.

The early modern global trading world was not a single story but many stories: of Portuguese sailors, Dutch merchants, Spanish colonial officials, indigenous mine workers, enslaved African plantation laborers, Gujarati credit brokers, Chinese porcelain painters, and European sugar consumers. Understanding it requires holding all of these stories simultaneously — recognizing both the dynamism and creativity of early modern commerce and the coercion, exploitation, and violence on which much of that commerce rested.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Mercantilism
The economic doctrine that national wealth depends on accumulating precious metal through a favorable balance of trade, justifying state management of commerce through tariffs, monopolies, and colonial systems.
Balance of trade
The difference between the value of a country's exports and imports; in mercantilist thought, a positive (favorable) balance was believed to increase national wealth.
Bullionism
The specific mercantilist belief that national wealth consists of accumulated gold and silver; the most extreme form of mercantilist doctrine.
Joint-stock company
A commercial enterprise in which many investors pool capital, share proportional profits (dividends), and can sell their ownership shares to others; the organizational form of the VOC, EIC, and other early modern trading companies.
Dividends
Payments made to shareholders of a joint-stock company from the profits of commercial operations, proportional to the number of shares held.
Charter
A legal document granted by a government authorizing a company or institution to operate with specific powers and privileges; the legal basis of the VOC, EIC, and similar organizations.
Monopoly
Exclusive control over the production or sale of a commodity; early modern trading companies typically held monopoly rights over trade in a specific geographic zone.
Plantation economy
An agricultural system based on large estates devoted to single-crop production for export, using coerced or slave labor; the dominant form of Atlantic commodity production in the early modern period.
Atlantic slave trade
The forced transport of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas, approximately 12.5 million people between 1500 and 1900; the labor system underlying Atlantic plantation economies.
Middle Passage
The oceanic crossing from West Africa to the Americas taken by enslaved people; characterized by brutal conditions, high mortality (approximately 1.8 million deaths), and systematic dehumanization.
Silver peso (piece of eight)
The standard Spanish silver coin minted from American silver; the world's first de facto global currency, accepted in markets from Seville to Canton.
Bill of exchange
A written order from one merchant to another to pay a specified sum at a future date; a credit instrument that allowed long-distance trade without physically transporting precious metal.
Marine insurance
A contract by which an underwriter agreed to compensate a merchant or ship owner for maritime losses in exchange for a premium; a key risk-management tool in early modern oceanic commerce.
Commercial capitalism
The form of capitalism based primarily on trade and commerce rather than industrial production, characteristic of the early modern period; merchants accumulated capital through buying and selling rather than manufacturing.
Putting-out system
A form of industrial organization in which merchants provided raw materials to rural workers who worked at home and returned finished products; common in early modern European textile production.
Entrepôt
A commercial hub where goods from multiple regions are collected, stored, and redistributed; major early modern entrepôts included Amsterdam, Malacca, and Manila.
Price revolution
The sustained rise in prices across Europe in the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, associated with the influx of American silver and population growth; destabilized existing economic relationships.
Mita system
The Spanish colonial forced labor system requiring indigenous communities in Peru and Bolivia to supply a quota of workers to silver mines; a form of coerced labor that resulted in enormous human suffering.
Triangular trade
The three-legged commercial circuit connecting Europe (manufactured goods), West Africa (enslaved people), and the Americas (plantation commodities); the dominant structure of Atlantic commerce in the early modern period.
Manila Galleon
The Spanish ships that annually or semi-annually crossed the Pacific between Acapulco (Mexico) and Manila (Philippines), carrying American silver westward and Asian goods eastward from 1565 to 1815.
Stock exchange
A market in which shares of joint-stock companies can be bought and sold; the Amsterdam Stock Exchange (1602) was the world's first; gives investors liquidity and creates a market price for ownership.
Capital accumulation
The reinvestment of commercial or industrial profits into expanded productive capacity; a defining feature of capitalism and a central process of early modern global commerce.
Protectionism
Trade policy that uses tariffs, quotas, and other barriers to protect domestic producers from foreign competition; the commercial policy dimension of mercantilism.
Free trade
Trade policy that minimizes barriers to international commerce, allowing market forces to determine the pattern of production and exchange; the alternative to mercantilist protectionism advocated by Adam Smith and later economists.
Cartaz system
The Portuguese system of licensed trading in the Indian Ocean, requiring Asian merchants to purchase passes from Portuguese authorities; an attempt to extract revenue from established Indian Ocean commercial networks.
Estado da India
The Portuguese "State of India," centered on Goa; the institutional framework for Portuguese commercial-military power in the Indian Ocean, encompassing fortified ports, licensed trade, and naval enforcement.

Amsterdam
World's leading commercial and financial city in the seventeenth century; home to the VOC, Amsterdam Stock Exchange, Amsterdam Exchange Bank, and a sophisticated insurance and credit market; prototype for modern financial capitalism.
Dutch East India Company (VOC)
Founded 1602; world's first modern joint-stock company with permanent capital and tradeable shares; dominated European trade in Asian spices, textiles, and porcelain; also governed territories in the East Indies with quasi-sovereign powers.
English East India Company (EIC)
Chartered 1600; grew from a trading company to a territorial power governing much of India; the most extreme expression of the commercial-imperial joint-stock model; effectively ruled Bengal after the Battle of Plassey (1757).
Mughal Empire
Dominant power in South Asia, sixteenth to eighteenth centuries; its cotton textile industry in Bengal and Gujarat was a major driver of Indian Ocean commerce and a key source of demand that shaped EIC commercial strategy.
Gujarati merchants
Hindu (Baniya) and Jain merchants from western India; operated sophisticated credit and commercial networks across the Indian Ocean from East Africa to Southeast Asia; used community trust rather than legal contracts as the basis for commercial relationships.
Bank of England
Founded 1694; provided stable government credit and financial services to the English commercial system; model for modern central banking and a key institution enabling British commercial and military power in the eighteenth century.
Immanuel Wallerstein (1930–2019)
American sociologist and historian; his Modern World-System (1974) argued that a capitalist world-system emerged in the sixteenth century, centered on Western Europe and exploiting peripheral regions; highly influential though contested.

Mapping a Global Commodity — From Production to Consumption

This project asks students to research and document the full commodity chain of one early modern global good — tracing its journey from production to consumption and identifying the people, places, institutions, and relationships involved at each stage.

  • Sugar (Brazil/Caribbean → Atlantic → European refinery → European consumer)

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 39 has examined one of the most consequential periods in world economic history: the three centuries between 1450 and 1750 during which the world's first truly global trading system was assembled. The chapter has shown that this system was built on a complex combination of geographic discovery, technological capability, institutional innovation, and organized coercion — and that its benefits and costs were distributed very unequally across the regions and peoples it connected. Understanding this period is essential for understanding the world that came after it, including the Industrial Revolution, modern capitalism, and contemporary global inequality.

The institutional legacy of early modern global commerce is everywhere in the contemporary world. The modern business corporation — with its separate legal existence, tradeable shares, and professional management — descends directly from the VOC and EIC. The stock exchange, the commercial bank, and the marine insurance market all have their origins in the commercial world of seventeenth-century Amsterdam. The concept of the balance of trade, the debate between free trade and protectionism, the question of what states owe to markets and what markets owe to states — all of these issues, which remain central to contemporary economic policy, were first systematically articulated in the mercantilist debates of the early modern period. History does not repeat itself, but it leaves institutional legacies that shape the options available to later generations.

The human legacy of early modern global commerce is equally profound — and more troubling. The Atlantic slave trade transported approximately 12.5 million Africans to the Americas in conditions of extreme brutality. The mita system subjected indigenous Andeans to forced labor in conditions that killed thousands. The VOC's massacre of the Bandanese people established the template for commercial genocide. These were not incidental to the early modern commercial revolution; they were central to it. The wealth of the Dutch Golden Age, the capital that funded early British industrialization, the prosperity that produced the consumer culture of eighteenth-century Europe — all were built, in part, on the labor of enslaved and coerced people whose descendants are still living with the consequences.

This chapter connects to the larger narrative of the textbook in important ways. The early modern global trading system described here was the commercial foundation for the political and economic transformations of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries: the Atlantic revolutions, the Industrial Revolution, the age of imperialism, and the emergence of the modern nation-state system. Understanding how the world of 1750 was structured — its patterns of commercial dominance and dependency, its institutional forms, its commodity flows, its inequalities — is the essential prerequisite for understanding how it became the world of 1850, 1950, and today. World history is not a sequence of separate episodes; it is a connected process in which each period's structures and legacies shape what comes next. The merchants, plantation owners, sailors, enslaved workers, and consumers of the early modern world were not simply historical figures from a distant past. They were the architects — willing or unwilling — of the global economic structures we have inherited.

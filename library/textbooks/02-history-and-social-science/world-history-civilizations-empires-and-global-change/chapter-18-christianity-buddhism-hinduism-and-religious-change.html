Part IV — Late Antiquity and the Post-Classical Transition
18

Christianity, Buddhism, Hinduism, and Religious Change

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"Religions do not spread by themselves. They are carried by people — missionaries, merchants, pilgrims, refugees, soldiers, diplomats, and monks — who move across landscapes and carry their beliefs, practices, and sacred objects with them."

In the year 399 CE, a Chinese Buddhist monk named Faxian set out westward from Chang'an on a journey that would take him through the deserts of Central Asia, across the mountains of Afghanistan, down into the sacred landscape of northern India, and eventually by sea back to China — a round trip of roughly fourteen years. He traveled through territories controlled by different rulers, speaking different languages, practicing different customs. He crossed the Silk Roads, slept in Buddhist monasteries and cave-temples carved from cliffsides, copied sacred texts he could barely afford to carry, and observed Buddhist communities in various states of vitality and decline. When he finally returned home, he wrote an account of what he had seen: a geography of living religion stretched across thousands of miles.

Faxian's journey was made possible by something that had been building for centuries. By the late fourth and early fifth centuries CE, a web of religious communities — Buddhist, Christian, Hindu, Zoroastrian, Jewish, Manichean, and others — spread across Afro-Eurasia. These communities were not isolated from one another. They traveled along the same roads and sea routes that carried silk, spices, grain, and silver. They competed for converts, borrowed from each other's ideas, adapted to new environments, and sometimes cooperated under the same imperial rulers. Faxian was not unusual in making a long religious pilgrimage; he was simply one of the best-known travelers of a broad and deeply interconnected religious world.

This chapter examines major religious developments across Afro-Eurasia during late antiquity and the early post-classical period, roughly from the second century CE through the ninth century CE. The chapter focuses on three major religious traditions — Christianity, Buddhism, and the evolving Hindu traditions of South Asia — while also acknowledging the broader religious landscape in which they developed. During this period, each tradition underwent dramatic internal development, geographic expansion, institutional growth, and cultural adaptation. Each tradition interacted with political power, shaping and being shaped by empires, kingdoms, and states. Each tradition left a material record of extraordinary richness: churches, monasteries, temples, stupas, manuscripts, inscriptions, sculptures, paintings, and urban landscapes.

The word "religion" itself requires careful historical thought. In the ancient and medieval worlds, what we call religion was rarely separated from politics, economics, medicine, law, kinship, agriculture, or philosophy. People who prayed to the Buddha also paid taxes to a king. Christians who attended liturgy also participated in market networks. Devotees of Vishnu or Shiva also organized their lives around caste, village, and seasonal labor. When historians study religious change, they are not studying a separate sphere of human life — they are studying the whole of human life as organized around particular cosmologies, rituals, communities, and authorities.

Why does this chapter matter for world history? The religious transformations of late antiquity and the early post-classical period shaped the cultural geography of the world that students will encounter in every subsequent chapter of this textbook. The spread of Christianity shaped Europe, the Mediterranean, and eventually the Americas. The spread of Buddhism shaped Central, East, and Southeast Asia in ways still visible today. The development of Hindu traditions shaped South and Southeast Asia in art, philosophy, governance, and social order. These were not just local developments; they were global — carried by missionaries and merchants, adapted by rulers and peasants, expressed in art and architecture across vastly different landscapes.

This chapter also asks students to think carefully about how religious traditions spread. Diffusion is not simple transmission. When Buddhism arrived in China, it encountered Daoist and Confucian traditions already deeply embedded in Chinese society. It was translated, debated, adapted, and transformed — and it also transformed what it encountered. When Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire, it inherited Roman institutions, Latin language, and Roman imperial ideas about universal authority. When devotional Hinduism developed in South Asia, it did not replace older ritual and philosophical traditions but layered over and interacted with them in complex ways. Religious change is always a process of encounter, translation, adaptation, conflict, and synthesis.

The evidence historians use to study this period is rich but uneven. Texts — scripture, theology, biography, pilgrimage accounts, royal inscriptions, monastic records, letters, and chronicles — survive in large quantities, but they are mostly produced by literate, often elite, often male members of religious communities. Material evidence — temple complexes, monastic ruins, cave paintings, sculpture, coins, and urban archaeology — helps historians reach communities that left fewer texts. Oral traditions, folk practices, and the experiences of women, enslaved people, low-caste communities, and rural laborers are harder to recover, but historians continue to develop methods for doing so.

This chapter is organized around themes rather than a single regional narrative. Section by section, it examines religion as a historical force, Christianity's transformation from persecuted minority to imperial institution, the development of monasticism, Buddhism's spread across Inner and East Asia, the emergence of devotional Hinduism, religious art and architecture as historical evidence, the role of missionaries and merchants in cultural exchange, religion's relationship to state power, and finally a comparative view of religious change across regions. By the end of the chapter, students should understand that the late antique and early post-classical centuries were one of the most consequential periods of religious transformation in human history — and that understanding this transformation is essential to understanding the world that followed.

Historical Context

The religious world of Afro-Eurasia in the first centuries CE was already ancient, layered, and diverse. In the Mediterranean world, Greeks and Romans worshipped a rich pantheon of gods, observed civic religious duties, participated in mystery cults, and engaged with philosophical traditions that addressed questions of ethics, cosmology, and the soul. Judaism had survived centuries of displacement and political pressure, maintaining its commitment to monotheism, scripture, law, and community. In South Asia, Vedic religious traditions had evolved into complex ritual systems and philosophical schools, while Buddhism and Jainism had emerged as reform movements in the sixth century BCE and had spread across much of the subcontinent and beyond. In Persia and Central Asia, Zoroastrianism provided a cosmic framework of divine order and moral struggle.

All of these traditions existed in a world of intensifying connection. The Silk Roads — the overland and maritime trade routes linking China, Central Asia, India, Persia, the Mediterranean, and East Africa — were not only commercial highways but cultural corridors. Merchants carried not just silk and spices but stories, ideas, sacred objects, and religious specialists. By the first century CE, Buddhist communities existed in Central Asian oasis cities like Kucha, Samarkand, and Dunhuang. Jewish communities operated in Alexandria, Rome, Mesopotamia, and Persian cities. Christian communities began forming in Palestine, Syria, Egypt, Asia Minor, Greece, and Rome within decades of the life of Jesus.

The political framework of this world was shaped by several great empires. Rome controlled the Mediterranean and western Europe. The Parthian and later the Sasanian Empire controlled Persia and much of the Middle East. The Kushan Empire, stretching across modern Afghanistan, Pakistan, and northwestern India, connected the Silk Roads and provided a major corridor for Buddhist expansion into Central Asia. The Gupta Empire (approximately 320–550 CE) ruled much of northern India during a period of remarkable cultural and religious flowering. Han China (206 BCE–220 CE), and later the Sui and Tang dynasties, shaped East Asia.

Between approximately 200 and 700 CE, this imperial landscape underwent dramatic transformation. Rome adopted Christianity as its official religion. The Gupta period saw an extraordinary flowering of Sanskrit literature, Hindu philosophy, and temple building. Buddhism reached Korea, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Christianity spread south into Ethiopia and east into Persia, India, and Central Asia. New institutions — the Christian church, Buddhist monasteries, Hindu temple complexes — became pillars of social and political life. These changes did not happen smoothly or uniformly. They were contested, sometimes violent, often incomplete. Conversion was frequently partial, layered, and negotiated. Old practices persisted beneath new religious labels. Communities maintained local traditions even as they adopted new cosmologies. Understanding this messy, complex process of religious change is what this chapter is fundamentally about.

Regional Orientation

This chapter ranges across Afro-Eurasia, from the Atlantic coast of Europe to the Pacific shores of Japan and the islands of Southeast Asia. Five major regional zones are most central to the chapter's themes.

The Mediterranean World and Near East was the heartland of early Christianity, stretching from Palestine through Syria, Egypt, Asia Minor, Greece, Italy, and North Africa. Rome's urban network, its administrative infrastructure, and its Latin and Greek literary cultures all shaped early Christianity's growth. The Near East — particularly Syria, Mesopotamia, and Persia — was also a zone of Christian communities, Jewish scholarship, and later Islamic expansion.

The Caucasus, East Africa, and the Ethiopian Highlands show that Christianity's early expansion was not primarily westward into Europe but multi-directional. Armenia became the first officially Christian state around 301 CE; the kingdom of Axum in the Ethiopian highlands adopted Christianity in the fourth century and developed a distinctive African Christian civilization that has persisted to the present.

Central Asia and the Silk Road Corridor — modern Afghanistan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, Xinjiang, and adjacent areas — was a crossroads of Buddhism, Christianity (in its Nestorian form), Zoroastrianism, Manichaeism, and later Islam. Buddhist monasteries along the Silk Roads served as cultural hubs. The cave complex at Dunhuang on the edge of the Taklamakan Desert is one of the richest archives of religious art and manuscript culture in world history.

South Asia was the birthplace of both Buddhism and the Hindu traditions. During the Gupta period, it saw extraordinary religious and artistic flowering. Buddhism gradually declined in India after the Gupta period, partly displaced by devotional Hinduism. South Asia also exported religious ideas, art, and institutions to Southeast Asia, Central Asia, and East Asia.

East and Southeast Asia — China, Korea, Japan, and the kingdoms of mainland and maritime Southeast Asia — all received Buddhism during this period, each adapting it in distinctive ways. China's political, philosophical, and artistic culture shaped East Asian Buddhism deeply. Southeast Asia, connected by Indian Ocean trade routes, developed Hindu and Buddhist traditions that shaped kingdoms from Cambodia to Java to Sumatra.

Section 18.1

Religion and Historical Change

Religion has been one of the most powerful forces shaping human history. Across time and cultures, religious systems have provided cosmological frameworks — explanations of how the universe came into being, how it is ordered, and what place human beings occupy within it. They have provided ethical guidance — rules, virtues, and ideals for how individuals and communities should behave. They have built institutions — temples, monasteries, churches, mosques, shrines, sacred landscapes — that organized social and economic life. They have justified political authority, inspired art and architecture, spurred conflict and violence, and motivated extraordinary acts of generosity, sacrifice, and community care.

For historians, religion is not simply a set of beliefs held privately in individual minds. It is a social and historical phenomenon — something that communities do together, that institutions transmit and transform, that material culture records and expresses. When historians study religious change, they ask not only "what did people believe?" but "how did they practice? Who had authority? How were communities organized? What role did sacred space play? How did religion intersect with gender, class, politics, and economic life? How and why did traditions spread, adapt, and change?"

Studying religion comparatively across world history requires careful attention to avoiding two opposing errors. The first error is relativism taken to an extreme — treating all religious claims as simply equivalent, refusing to analyze them critically or compare them rigorously. The second error is ethnocentrism — judging religious traditions by the standards of one's own tradition, ranking them implicitly or explicitly, treating some as primitive and others as advanced. Good historical practice studies each tradition in its own context, compares them without ranking, and analyzes how and why they changed without imposing external values.

Religions are not static. Every religious tradition examined in this chapter changed dramatically over the centuries covered here. Christianity in the third century CE was a persecuted minority movement meeting in private homes; by the fifth century it was the official religion of the Roman Empire, with elaborate hierarchies, grand basilica churches, and enormous institutional wealth. Buddhism in the first century BCE was primarily a monastic religion of South Asian renunciant communities; by the seventh century CE it was a pan-Asian tradition practiced by millions across China, Korea, Japan, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia, adapted into dozens of distinct local forms. Hinduism in the third century CE was a complex web of ritual, philosophy, and devotional practice centered on Sanskrit learning and Brahmanical authority; by the ninth century it was also expressed in vernacular Tamil poetry of extraordinary emotional power, celebrating personal love for a god.

What drives religious change? Historians point to several interlocking factors. Political patronage matters enormously — when rulers adopt a religion, they provide resources, institutional support, and sometimes coercive pressure that dramatically accelerates diffusion. Trade routes matter — merchants carry ideas alongside goods. Migration matters — diaspora communities bring their religious practices with them. Internal intellectual development matters — theologians, philosophers, and commentators reshape traditions from within. Popular devotional movements can bypass elite institutions and reach mass audiences. Conflicts — between religious communities, between religious institutions and states, between orthodox authorities and reform movements — also drive change by forcing communities to define and defend their positions.

Section 18.2

Christianity from Persecution to Imperial Faith

The Early Communities

Christianity began in Roman-controlled Palestine in the early first century CE. Its founder, Jesus of Nazareth, was a Jewish teacher who gathered disciples, preached about the Kingdom of God, performed healings, and was executed by crucifixion by the Roman governor Pontius Pilate around 30 CE. His followers, who believed he had risen from the dead, formed communities that spread rapidly throughout the eastern Mediterranean.

The first Christian communities were embedded in the Jewish world. Jesus himself was Jewish, as were his first followers. Early Christian writings — the letters of Paul, the four Gospels, the Acts of the Apostles — reflect intense debate about how this new movement related to Jewish law, tradition, and identity. Paul of Tarsus was the most energetic early missionary, traveling through Asia Minor, Greece, and eventually Rome, establishing communities and writing letters that became foundational Christian texts. Paul argued powerfully that salvation through Jesus was available to all people, Jew and Gentile alike — a universalizing claim that would prove crucial to Christianity's eventual global reach.

By the end of the first century CE, Christian communities existed in major cities throughout the Roman Empire and in the Near East. They were diverse — Greek-speaking and Aramaic-speaking, urban and rural, rich and poor, ethnically mixed. They met in private homes, shared meals, read scriptures, prayed together, and cared for the sick and poor in their communities. Their rejection of traditional Roman religion — refusing to sacrifice to the Roman gods and the emperor — made them objects of suspicion. Official Roman policy was inconsistent, but Christians faced periodic persecution, especially under emperors like Nero (64 CE), Decius (249–251 CE), and Diocletian (303–312 CE).

Constantine and the Turning Point

In 313 CE, Emperor Constantine and his co-emperor Licinius issued the Edict of Milan, granting Christianity legal toleration throughout the Roman Empire. Constantine reportedly experienced a vision before the Battle of Milvian Bridge (312 CE) that led him to adopt Christian symbols. He may also have seen Christianity as a source of social cohesion for a fractured empire. Whatever his motivations, his patronage transformed Christianity's situation dramatically.

Constantine summoned bishops from across the empire to Nicaea in 325 CE for the first major ecumenical council. The primary purpose was to settle the Arian controversy — a fierce theological dispute about whether Jesus was fully divine (co-equal with God the Father) or a created being subordinate to the Father. The council affirmed the full divinity of Christ and produced the Nicene Creed, which became the standard statement of Christian belief. The council also established the model of emperors convening church councils — a fusion of political and religious authority that would define Byzantine civilization and shape Christian-state relations for centuries.

In 380 CE, Emperor Theodosius I went further, issuing the Edict of Thessalonica, which declared Nicene Christianity the official state religion of the Roman Empire. Other forms of Christianity were declared heretical, and traditional polytheistic religious practices faced increasing legal restrictions. By the end of the fourth century, Christianity had gone from being a persecuted minority to being the established religion of the most powerful state in the Western world.

Doctrinal Development and Theological Disputes

The period from the third through the seventh centuries was one of extraordinary theological creativity. Christian thinkers — the Church Fathers — worked to develop systematic accounts of Christian doctrine, drawing on Greek philosophy (especially Platonism) as well as Jewish scripture and the New Testament. Figures like Origen of Alexandria, the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus), Ambrose of Milan, Jerome, and Augustine of Hippo produced vast bodies of writing that defined Christian theology, ethics, biblical interpretation, and spiritual practice.

Augustine of Hippo (354–430 CE) deserves particular mention. Writing in Latin in North Africa during the dying years of Roman authority in the West, Augustine developed influential doctrines about grace, sin, predestination, and the relationship between the earthly city (the fallen world) and the city of God (the community of the saved). His work shaped Western Christian theology for over a thousand years and indirectly influenced political thought about the relationship between church and state.

Not all theological debates were resolved peacefully. The Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE attempted to define the relationship between Christ's divine and human natures. Its definition — that Christ was one person in two natures, fully divine and fully human — was accepted in Rome and Constantinople but rejected by large Christian communities in Egypt, Syria, and Persia. These communities — Copts, Syriac Christians, the Church of the East — developed independently, carrying Christianity into Ethiopia, Persia, Central Asia, and eventually India. Christianity was therefore never a single unified institution; it was always a family of related but distinct communities.

Christianity's Geographic Expansion

By the fifth and sixth centuries CE, Christianity had spread far beyond the Roman Empire. Armenian Christians had developed a distinctive alphabet and literary tradition. The Ethiopian kingdom of Axum had adopted Christianity as its official religion in the fourth century. The Church of the East (often called Nestorian Christianity) spread east along the Silk Roads into Persia, Central Asia, and by the seventh century had reached China, India, and the Mongol steppe. Christian missionaries traveled to Ireland, Scotland, and Germanic kingdoms in Western Europe.

This geographic diversity meant that Christianity, like Buddhism, was not a single uniform tradition but a family of communities adapted to vastly different cultural environments. The Greek-speaking Eastern Church centered on Constantinople, the Latin-speaking Western Church centered eventually on Rome, the Syriac churches of the Near East, the Coptic Church of Egypt, the Ethiopian Church — each developed distinct liturgies, art, theology, and institutional structures. The Christianity of a Latin monk in Ireland in the sixth century was meaningfully different from the Christianity of a Coptic merchant in Alexandria or a Nestorian trader in Central Asia, even though all shared core beliefs about Jesus and salvation.

Section 18.3

Monasticism and Religious Communities

Christian Monasticism: Origins in the Desert

Christian monasticism emerged most visibly in Egypt in the third and fourth centuries CE. The practice of withdrawal from society drew on a range of sources: the Jewish tradition of prophets retreating to the wilderness, the Stoic ideal of self-mastery, and distinctively Christian ideas about renouncing the world for God. The earliest monks were solitaries (hermits or anchorites) who withdrew into the Egyptian desert to pray, fast, and struggle against spiritual temptations. Antony of Egypt (c. 251–356 CE), whose life was written by Bishop Athanasius, became the archetypal Christian hermit — a model of spiritual combat and radical renunciation.

Pachomius of Egypt (c. 292–348 CE) took a different path: rather than isolated hermitage, he organized monks into communities with shared rules, schedules, and work obligations. This cenobitic (communal) monasticism proved enormously influential. By the late fourth century, monastic communities existed across Egypt, Palestine, Syria, Asia Minor, and were spreading into Western Europe. The movement was intensely popular — thousands of people, men and women alike, sought the monastic life as the highest form of Christian devotion.

In the Western Latin world, Benedict of Nursia (c. 480–550 CE) composed the Rule of Saint Benedict, a practical guide for organizing monastic life that emphasized a balance of prayer, manual work, and study. His formula of ora et labora ("pray and work") shaped Western monasticism for centuries. Benedictine monasteries became major institutions of medieval European civilization — centers of agricultural development, manuscript production, education, and social welfare.

Monasticism was not exclusively male. Women's communities (convents, nunneries) developed alongside men's monasteries from early on. Figures like Macrina, sister of Basil and Gregory of Nyssa, organized communities of women dedicated to prayer and service. Women's monasticism offered an alternative to marriage and family life, spaces of relative autonomy within patriarchal societies, and opportunities for education and spiritual leadership.

Monasteries as Social Institutions

Christian monasteries served functions far beyond individual spiritual practice. They were centers of manuscript production: monks copied scripture, theological works, and increasingly secular texts, preserving and transmitting ancient learning across generations. In the early medieval period, when political authority in Western Europe had fragmented after Rome's decline, monasteries often functioned as the primary institutions of education, healthcare, and poor relief. They also managed large agricultural estates, contributing significantly to regional economies.

Monasteries were nodes in networks of religious authority. They sent out missionaries, sheltered pilgrims, trained clergy, and maintained connections across vast distances through letters and traveling monks. The Irish monasteries of the fifth through seventh centuries, for example, sent missionaries across Britain and into continental Europe, reinvigorating Christian communities that had been disrupted by the political changes following Rome's decline.

Buddhist Monasticism

Buddhist monasticism predated Christian monasticism by several centuries. The Buddha himself established the sangha — the community of monks and nuns — as one of the Three Jewels of Buddhism. The Vinaya, the monastic code, regulated every aspect of monastic life: diet, dress, sleeping arrangements, property, behavior, and ritual.

Buddhist monasteries were supported by the laity — lay people who provided food, clothing, and shelter to monks, earning spiritual merit through their generosity. This symbiotic relationship between monks and laity was fundamental to the social economy of Buddhism. Monasteries, in turn, provided ritual services, taught the dharma, and offered mechanisms of spiritual advancement to lay communities.

As Buddhism spread across Asia, its monasteries became major institutions along trade routes. The Buddhist monasteries of Central Asia, built into cliff faces or constructed around oasis cities, provided rest, safe passage, and cultural services to Silk Road merchants. In China, Buddhist monasteries accumulated enormous wealth through donations and economic activities, owning land and mills. In Japan, monasteries became politically powerful institutions intimately connected to aristocratic patronage. Everywhere, Buddhist monasteries served as centers of art, scholarship, pilgrimage, and social welfare as well as spiritual practice.

Section 18.4

Buddhism Across Central, East, and Southeast Asia

From South Asia to the World: Early Diffusion

Buddhism was born in the northeastern Indian subcontinent in the fifth century BCE as a response to the suffering inherent in human life. Siddhartha Gautama, a prince who renounced his aristocratic life, attained enlightenment (bodhi) through meditation and taught the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path — a diagnosis of human suffering rooted in craving and attachment, and a path to liberation from it. The sangha he established became the vehicle for transmitting his teaching.

Buddhism's first great expansion beyond northeastern India came under the patronage of Emperor Ashoka in the third century BCE. Ashoka converted to Buddhism, promoted its values of non-violence and compassion, and sent missionaries across South Asia and beyond — to Sri Lanka, to Hellenistic kingdoms in the northwest, and reportedly as far as Egypt and Greece. Sri Lanka became a major center of Theravada Buddhism, and it was from Sri Lanka that Theravada later spread to mainland Southeast Asia.

The Kushan Empire, which flourished from approximately the first through third centuries CE in what is now Afghanistan, Pakistan, and northern India, was another crucial conduit for Buddhist expansion northward and eastward into Central Asia. Kushan rulers patronized Buddhist art and monasteries, and it was in Kushan territories that Gandhara Buddhist art developed — a fusion of South Asian Buddhist iconography with Hellenistic artistic styles, producing the first figurative images of the Buddha in human form.

The Spread of Buddhism into Central Asia and China

Buddhism reached China along the Silk Roads, carried by merchants, missionaries, and wandering monks. The earliest reliable evidence of Buddhist communities in China dates to the first and second centuries CE. At first, Buddhism was understood through the lens of existing Chinese thought — particularly Daoism and Confucianism. Terms from Daoist vocabulary were used to translate Buddhist concepts, a process historians call "matching of meanings" (geyi).

The translation of Buddhist texts into Chinese was one of the great intellectual enterprises of late antiquity. Translators like An Shigao (a Parthian Buddhist who came to China around 148 CE), Kumarajiva (a Central Asian scholar who arrived in 401 CE), and the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (who brought back hundreds of texts from India in the seventh century) produced Chinese versions of the Buddhist canon that shaped East Asian Buddhism for centuries. Kumarajiva's translations were celebrated for their literary elegance and continued to be read and chanted centuries after his death.

Buddhism in China diversified into many schools, each emphasizing different texts, practices, and doctrines. The Pure Land (Jingtu) school emphasized devotion to Amitabha Buddha and the hope of being reborn in a celestial paradise — a path accessible to ordinary people, not just monks. The Chan school (later Zen in Japan), which emerged from the sixth century onward, emphasized direct meditation experience over textual study, developing distinctive practices of teacher-student dialogue and wordless mind-to-mind transmission. Tiantai and Huayan schools developed elaborate philosophical systems reconciling the vast diversity of Buddhist texts and doctrines.

Buddhist Institutions in China

By the Tang dynasty (618–907 CE), Buddhism was the most institutionally powerful religion in China. Buddhist monasteries in Tang China were wealthy, socially powerful, and deeply integrated into everyday life. They offered hospitals and orphanages, functioned as banks (moneylending from monastic wealth), managed agricultural estates, and provided education. Empress Wu Zetian (r. 690–705 CE) was a particularly enthusiastic patron, commissioning major Buddhist art projects and supporting Buddhist scholars.

Buddhist monasteries also became objects of periodic imperial resentment: their tax-exempt lands, their wealth, and the large numbers of men and women who entered monastic life were perennial concerns for the imperial government. The great suppression of Buddhism by Emperor Wuzong in 845 CE — when monasteries were seized, monks forced to return to lay life, and Buddhist institutions dramatically weakened — demonstrated the fragility of religious institutions dependent on state tolerance.

Buddhism in Korea and Japan

Buddhism reached the Korean peninsula from China in the fourth century CE, during the period of the Three Kingdoms. It was officially introduced to Goguryeo from the Former Qin state in 372 CE. Buddhism provided Korean rulers with a sophisticated religious framework for legitimizing political authority and connecting to the broader East Asian cultural world.

From Korea, Buddhism was transmitted to Japan, traditionally dated to 552 CE (or 538 CE), when the Korean kingdom of Baekje sent Buddhist texts, an image of the Buddha, and monks to the Japanese court. Prince Shōtoku (574–622 CE) was a major early patron who promoted Buddhism alongside Confucian political ideals. Under the Nara period (710–794 CE), the Japanese state built great Buddhist temples like Tōdai-ji in the capital Nara, housing a massive bronze Buddha and serving as a state institution supporting imperial authority.

Buddhism in Southeast Asia

Buddhism spread into mainland and maritime Southeast Asia through multiple channels: overland from India through river valleys, and by sea through the Indian Ocean trade routes. Theravada Buddhism gradually became dominant in mainland Southeast Asia (modern Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia). Sri Lanka served as a major transmission point for Theravada traditions, sending monks and texts to mainland Southeast Asia. The Pali language, in which the Theravada canon is preserved, became the sacred language of these traditions — somewhat analogous to Latin in medieval Western Christianity.

Maritime Southeast Asia received both Hindu and Buddhist influences through Indian Ocean commerce. The Srivijaya maritime empire (c. 7th–13th centuries CE), based in Sumatra, was a major center of Mahayana Buddhist learning, attracting scholars from across Asia. The massive Buddhist stupa of Borobudur in Java and the Hindu temple complex of Prambanan reflect the extraordinary cultural vitality of Southeast Asian kingdoms as they received, adapted, and expressed these traditions in their own terms.

Section 18.5

Hindu Developments and Devotional Traditions

From Vedic Ritual to Devotional Religion

The term "Hinduism" is a modern word, coined primarily by outsiders to describe the diverse range of religious traditions native to South Asia that are not Buddhism, Jainism, or Islam. What we call Hinduism was a complex, internally diverse set of traditions sharing certain texts (the Vedas, Upanishads, Epics, Puranas), certain practices (sacrifice, puja, pilgrimage), certain social structures (varna/caste), and certain theological preoccupations (the nature of the divine, the self, dharma, karma, moksha) — but never a single unified institution with a single creed or hierarchy.

The oldest layer of what we call Hinduism is the Vedic tradition: an elaborate system of ritual sacrifice performed by Brahmin priests, aimed at maintaining cosmic and social order and winning divine favor. By the late first millennium BCE, this ritual tradition was supplemented and in some ways challenged by new developments. The Upanishads — philosophical texts composed within the Vedic tradition — explored the nature of the self (atman), the ultimate reality (Brahman), and the relationship between the individual and the cosmos. They developed the concept of moksha — liberation from the cycle of death and rebirth through knowledge of the ultimate nature of reality.

Alongside Vedic ritual and Vedantic philosophy, devotional religious movements focused on particular deities — especially Vishnu and Shiva — were growing in importance. The great Sanskrit epics, the Mahabharata and the Ramayana, express this devotional sensibility alongside their narratives of heroic struggle. The Bhagavad Gita, embedded within the Mahabharata, offers a particularly influential statement of the devotional path: the warrior Arjuna, paralyzed by moral dilemma, is counseled by the god Krishna (an avatar of Vishnu) to perform his duty without attachment to results, and to surrender in love and devotion to the divine.

The Gupta Period and Religious Flowering

The Gupta Empire (c. 320–550 CE) presided over a period of remarkable cultural and religious development in northern India. Gupta rulers were primarily Vaishnavites (devotees of Vishnu), but they also patronized Buddhist and Jain institutions. Sanskrit literature reached new heights, with poets like Kalidasa producing dramatic and lyrical works that became classics of world literature. The mathematical and astronomical achievements of scholars like Aryabhata — including early work with the concept of zero — were part of this intellectual flowering.

During the Gupta period, the great Hindu temple — as a monumental religious complex housing a deity, staffed by priests, endowed with land and resources, and serving as a center of pilgrimage — became an increasingly important institution. The temple was understood as the home of the deity, whose image was consecrated and treated as a living presence — bathed, dressed, fed, and entertained by priests in daily ritual cycles. Hindu temple complexes also served as major economic and social institutions, employing priests, dancers, musicians, craftworkers, and agricultural laborers on temple-owned lands.

The Bhakti Movement

The most transformative development in Hindu devotional life during this period was the emergence and spread of the Bhakti movement, beginning in South India in approximately the sixth century CE and gradually spreading north over subsequent centuries. Bhakti means "devotion" or "loving participation" — a path of personal emotional relationship between the devotee and a personal deity, especially Vishnu (and his avatars, particularly Rama and Krishna) or Shiva.

The earliest and most celebrated Bhakti poets were Tamil-speaking: the Alvars (devotees of Vishnu) and the Nayanmars (devotees of Shiva). These poets — who included women and people of low-caste or even outcast communities — composed hymns of extraordinary lyrical beauty in the Tamil language rather than Sanskrit. By writing in Tamil, they made devotional religion accessible to people who did not know Sanskrit, challenging the exclusive authority of Brahmin priests over religious learning. The Alvar poets' collected hymns — the Nalayira Divya Prabandham — became known as the Tamil Veda, treated with the same reverence as the Sanskrit Vedas.

The Bhakti movement challenged several aspects of the established religious order. By insisting that the path of devoted love was open to all, regardless of gender, caste, or literacy, it implicitly challenged caste hierarchies that restricted religious authority to Brahmin males. Bhakti poets — women like Andal and Karaikkal Ammaiyar, people from fishing, agricultural, and artisan communities — became revered figures. At the same time, the movement was also integrated into institutional Hinduism: the hymns of the Alvars were incorporated into temple rituals, and the great Vaishnava and Shaiva temple institutions that dominated South Indian religious life were beneficiaries as well as contexts of Bhakti devotion.

Adi Shankara and Philosophical Hinduism

While Bhakti represented a popular devotional development, philosophical Hinduism was also undergoing major development. Adi Shankara (c. 788–820 CE), a South Indian Brahmin philosopher, developed the school of Advaita Vedanta — the philosophy of non-duality. Shankara argued that the individual self (atman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman) are fundamentally one; the appearance of diversity in the world is a product of maya (illusion or creative power). Liberation (moksha) consists in the direct experiential knowledge of this unity. Shankara traveled across India debating other philosophers, established monastic centers at four corners of the subcontinent, and composed commentaries on major Vedic texts that remain foundational to Hindu philosophical education.

Section 18.6

Religious Art, Architecture, and Texts

The late antique and post-classical periods produced some of the most extraordinary religious art and architecture in human history. From the cave temples of Dunhuang to the Byzantine mosaics of Ravenna, from the great stupas of Sanchi to the Pallava shore temples of Mamallapuram, religious communities built and decorated spaces that expressed their understanding of the divine, organized ritual life, proclaimed patronal power, and created focal points for pilgrimage and community.

Christian Art and Architecture

Early Christians initially met in private homes (house churches), leaving little distinctive archaeological trace. As Christianity became legal and then imperial, the building type that became characteristic was the basilica — a large rectangular hall, borrowed from Roman civic architecture, that provided space for congregational worship. The emperor Constantine sponsored enormous basilicas at major Christian sites: the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem, Old St. Peter's in Rome, and the Lateran Basilica in Rome.

Byzantine Christian art developed a distinctive iconographic language: the solemn frontal image of Christ as Pantokrator (ruler of all), images of the Virgin Mary (Theotokos, "God-bearer"), saints arranged in hierarchical order against gold backgrounds, and elaborate mosaic programs covering church interiors. The mosaics of Ravenna (late fifth to sixth centuries CE) are among the best-preserved examples of this tradition. The veneration of holy images (icons) became enormously important in Byzantine Christianity, inspiring both intense devotion and intense controversy. The Iconoclast Controversy (726–843 CE) — a bitter dispute about whether the veneration of images constituted idolatry — divided the Byzantine church and state for over a century.

Buddhist Art and Architecture

Buddhism developed one of the richest visual traditions in world history. The earliest Buddhist art avoided depicting the Buddha in human form — his presence was suggested by symbolic representations: footprints, an empty throne, a parasol. The development of the anthropomorphic Buddha image, which occurred in the Kushan period in both Gandhara (with Hellenistic influence) and Mathura (with purely South Asian stylistic development), was a major artistic and theological development.

The stupa was the primary architectural form of early Buddhism: a dome-shaped monument containing relics and surrounded by a path for circumambulation. Pilgrims walked around stupas in a clockwise direction as an act of merit-making and devotion. The great stupa at Sanchi (in modern Madhya Pradesh, India), with its richly carved gateways depicting scenes from the Buddha's life and previous lives (jataka tales), is one of the best-preserved early Buddhist sites. As Buddhism spread, the stupa form transformed: in China it became the pagoda; in Southeast Asia it became the distinctive spire-topped chedi or that.

Buddhist monasteries and cave temples are among the most spectacular artistic sites of the ancient and medieval world. The Ajanta Caves in Maharashtra, India, contain paintings of extraordinary beauty depicting scenes from Buddhist texts, produced between approximately the second century BCE and the sixth century CE. The cave complexes at Dunhuang in northwestern China preserve thousands of paintings and hundreds of manuscripts in multiple languages, spanning a period from the fourth to the fourteenth centuries CE.

Hindu Temple Art

The Hindu temple tradition developed one of the most complex and symbolically rich architectural systems in human history. The temple was understood not simply as a building but as a cosmogram — a representation of the universe in architectural form. The inner sanctuary (garbhagriha, "womb chamber") housed the deity's image; the tower above it (shikhara) represented the cosmic mountain. The temple's sculptural program — figures of deities, celestial beings, guardians, scenes from epics and myths — covered exterior surfaces in complex iconographic arrangements.

Regional styles of temple architecture developed across South Asia: the Nagara style of North India, with its characteristic curvilinear tower; the Dravida style of South India, with its pyramidal gopurams (gateway towers). The Shore Temple at Mamallapuram (Tamil Nadu, c. 700 CE) and the Kailash Temple at Ellora (Maharashtra, c. 760 CE, carved from a single mountain of rock) are among the masterpieces of this tradition. Large temples employed priests, dancers, musicians, craftworkers, and agricultural laborers on temple-owned lands, functioning simultaneously as religious centers, economic institutions, and political symbols.

Section 18.7

Missionaries, Merchants, and Cultural Exchange

Religious traditions do not spread by themselves. They are carried by people — missionaries, merchants, pilgrims, refugees, soldiers, diplomats, and monks — who move across landscapes and carry their beliefs, practices, and sacred objects with them. In the late antique and post-classical periods, the major trade routes of Afro-Eurasia were also corridors of religious diffusion, and the relationship between commercial and religious movement was deeply intertwined.

The Silk Roads as Religious Corridors

The overland and maritime trade routes collectively known as the Silk Roads were home to an extraordinary diversity of religious communities. Buddhist monasteries, Zoroastrian fire temples, Jewish synagogues, Nestorian Christian churches, and Manichaean communities all existed in the oasis cities dotting the routes between China and the Mediterranean. The city of Dunhuang, at the edge of the Taklamakan Desert, was a crossroads where travelers could find merchants, monks, and manuscripts from multiple religious traditions.

Merchants were not themselves usually missionaries in the formal sense, but they were crucial vectors of religious diffusion. A merchant who followed a particular religious tradition needed religious services along his trading route: places to pray, communities to provide hospitality, trusted co-religionists to do business with. Religious diasporas formed along trade routes, creating communities of shared faith in foreign cities. When a community of Buddhist merchants established itself in a Silk Road oasis, it created a context in which local rulers might become curious about, and eventually convert to, the new faith.

Missionary Activity in Christianity

Christianity's spread through the Roman Empire was facilitated partly by the empire's own infrastructure: roads, common languages (Latin in the west, Greek in the east), urban networks, and administrative systems. Beyond the Roman Empire, Christian missionaries operated in more challenging conditions. The conversion of the Irish, beginning in the fifth century CE and associated especially with Patrick, brought Christianity to communities that had never been under Roman authority. Irish monasticism in turn sent missionaries eastward into Britain and the continent. Columba (521–597 CE) founded the monastery of Iona off the coast of Scotland, from which missionaries traveled to northern Scotland and to Germanic kingdoms in continental Europe.

In the east, Nestorian Christian missionaries traveled extraordinary distances. By the seventh century, Nestorian Christian communities existed in Persia, Central Asia, and China, where a stele erected in Chang'an in 781 CE (the Nestorian Stele) recorded the history of the "Luminous Religion" in Chinese and Syriac. Christian communities also established themselves in South India — the Saint Thomas Christians of Kerala trace their origins to an apostolic mission attributed to the Apostle Thomas.

Buddhist Missionaries

Buddhist missionary activity was shaped by the model established by Ashoka, who reportedly sent missions to various regions as acts of royal generosity. Monks and nuns who traveled were simultaneously pilgrims (visiting sacred sites associated with the Buddha's life) and missionaries (teaching and establishing communities). The pilgrimage accounts of Faxian and Xuanzang are among the most vivid records of this activity: they describe not only the sacred geography of Buddhist India but the condition of Buddhist communities across Asia — some thriving, some in decline, some eagerly welcoming Chinese monks, others uncertain about the strangers who arrived seeking teachings.

Section 18.8

Religion and State Power

Throughout this chapter, the relationship between religion and state power has appeared repeatedly: Constantine and the church, Ashoka and Buddhism, Gupta rulers and Hindu temples. This relationship was not accidental. In the ancient and medieval world, religious legitimacy and political authority were deeply intertwined, and rulers who patronized religious institutions gained significant benefits in return.

The Logic of Religious Patronage

Rulers patronized religious institutions for multiple reasons. Religion provided frameworks for legitimacy: a king who was endorsed by the gods, who performed the correct rituals, who supported the correct religious institutions, was a legitimate ruler rather than merely a powerful one. Religion provided social cohesion: communities organized around shared practices, beliefs, and sacred spaces were more cohesive than those without shared religious life. Religion provided administrative networks: Buddhist monasteries, Christian bishoprics, and Hindu temples were nodes in administrative and social networks that rulers could leverage or control. In return, religious institutions provided rulers with ideological support, skilled personnel, material resources, and popular legitimacy.

State Religion and Its Complications

When states adopted official religions, they gained these benefits but also took on new complications. Defining orthodoxy — correct belief and practice — was contentious. The decisions of ecumenical councils created winners and losers: communities that accepted the council's authority and communities that rejected it. The Chalcedonian controversy (451 CE) split the Christian world, contributing to tensions between the imperial church and large Christian communities in Egypt, Syria, and Persia.

Buddhist states faced similar dynamics. Tang emperors alternately supported and restricted Buddhism depending on political context. The great suppression of 845 CE — motivated partly by Buddhist monasteries' tax-exempt economic power and partly by Daoist and Confucian rivals at court — destroyed thousands of monasteries and forced hundreds of thousands of monks and nuns back into lay life. Buddhism recovered, but never quite regained its pre-845 institutional power in China.

Religious Minorities and Toleration

In most late antique and post-classical states, religious life was not a matter of individual choice but of community membership. Religious minorities typically occupied legally defined positions, paying special taxes, facing restrictions on building new religious structures, and living under laws that distinguished them from members of the majority religion. The Sasanian Empire, centered in Persia, hosted large communities of Zoroastrians, Christians, Jews, and later Manichaeans. The treatment of these communities varied considerably under different rulers. In South Asia, the religious pluralism of the Gupta period is striking: Gupta rulers patronized Vaishnavism most prominently, but also supported Buddhist and Jain institutions.

Section 18.9

Comparing Religious Change Across Regions

Shared Patterns

Comparing religious change across Christianity, Buddhism, and Hindu traditions during this period reveals both remarkable parallels and meaningful differences. All three traditions underwent significant institutionalization during this period. In each case, institutions — churches, monasteries, temple complexes — became major features of social, economic, and political life, accumulating wealth, providing social services, and engaging with state power. In each case, the relationship between institutional religion and popular devotion was complex: elite, literate, and clerical forms of religious practice coexisted with popular practices, folk beliefs, and local traditions that did not always align neatly with official doctrine.

All three traditions also saw significant internal diversification. Christianity developed multiple branches — Latin Western, Greek Eastern, Syriac, Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Nestorian. Buddhism developed Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana schools with different texts, practices, and philosophical emphases. Hindu traditions encompassed Vaishnava, Shaiva, Shakta, and other orientations, as well as the Bhakti movement's vernacular devotionalism and Vedanta philosophy's scholarly elaborations. Diversity was not a failure of these traditions; it was a feature of their success in spreading across diverse cultural landscapes.

Significant Differences

There are also meaningful differences between the traditions. Christianity, particularly in its Latin Western form, developed a highly centralized institutional structure — the papacy and the hierarchy of bishops — that claimed universal authority. Buddhism, by contrast, never developed a single universal institution equivalent to the papacy; the sangha was always decentralized, with regional communities maintaining considerable autonomy. Hindu traditions, lacking a single founding figure, a single canonical text, or a single institutional hierarchy, were from the beginning more structurally diverse.

The relationship to political power also differed. Christianity, after Constantine, became deeply entangled with imperial power. Buddhism in East Asia was similarly entangled with state power, but its monastic institution maintained a degree of independence — and its periodic suppressions by states demonstrate that it could also be perceived as a threat. The social implications of the traditions also differed: Buddhism's monastic path was in principle open to people regardless of caste, though in practice Buddhist institutions in South Asia were deeply embedded in caste society. The Bhakti movement challenged caste hierarchies in its devotional universalism, but Brahmanical ritual authority remained powerful. Christianity proclaimed equality of souls before God, but Christian societies maintained extensive inequalities, including slavery, throughout this period.

Section 18.10

Chapter Review and Religious Diffusion Map Study

The period from approximately 200 to 900 CE was one of the most consequential in the history of world religion. Christianity transformed from a persecuted minority to an imperial institution to a family of traditions spanning Europe, North Africa, the Near East, and portions of Asia and Africa. Buddhism spread from its South Asian birthplace across Central, East, and Southeast Asia, adapting to Chinese, Korean, Japanese, and Southeast Asian cultural environments while maintaining its core commitment to the teaching of the Buddha. Hindu traditions underwent profound internal development, with the Bhakti movement in South India opening new paths of religious participation while the philosophical developments of Vedanta and the institutional development of the great temple traditions reshaped religious life across South and Southeast Asia.

These developments were not independent of each other or of the broader world. They happened in a context of intensifying exchange along trade routes — the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean networks — that carried not just goods but ideas, images, people, and sacred objects. Rulers and merchants played crucial roles in funding, protecting, and transmitting religious traditions. Monks, missionaries, pilgrims, and poets were the carriers and creators of religious culture. And everywhere, ordinary people — farmers, craftworkers, women, urban laborers — experienced these religious changes in their daily lives, through the festivals, temples, monasteries, and ritual practices that structured their communities.

Historical Vocabulary
Syncretism

Syncretism refers to the blending, merging, or combining of different religious traditions, beliefs, practices, or symbols into a new, hybrid form. Syncretism is extremely common in the history of religion — perhaps more common than "pure" tradition, which rarely exists in practice.

When Buddhism spread to China, it encountered Daoist and Confucian traditions. Chinese Buddhism did not simply replace Daoism and Confucianism; it blended with them, borrowing vocabulary, adapting concepts, and eventually producing distinctively Chinese Buddhist schools (like Chan) that combined Buddhist meditational practice with Daoist ideas about spontaneity and natural simplicity. When Christianity spread to Germanic kingdoms, missionaries and converts adapted Christian holidays to align with existing seasonal festivals. When Hindu and Buddhist ideas spread to Southeast Asia, they were absorbed into existing royal rituals and cosmologies, producing distinctive Southeast Asian religious cultures that were neither purely Indian nor purely indigenous.

Historians use the concept of syncretism carefully. It is descriptive rather than pejorative — it does not imply that syncretic traditions are impure or inferior. Understanding syncretism helps historians trace the actual processes by which traditions spread and adapt, as opposed to the idealized narratives of pure transmission that religious communities sometimes tell about themselves.

Historical Vocabulary
Bodhisattva

In Mahayana Buddhism, a bodhisattva is a being who has reached the threshold of enlightenment but chooses to remain in the cycle of death and rebirth (samsara) to help all sentient beings achieve liberation. The bodhisattva ideal is the heart of Mahayana Buddhism — the commitment to universal compassion rather than individual liberation.

The most important bodhisattvas became objects of devotion, almost deity-like figures to whom practitioners prayed for help. Avalokiteshvara (in Chinese, Guanyin; in Japanese, Kannon) is the bodhisattva of compassion — one of the most widely venerated religious figures in East and Southeast Asia. The bodhisattva concept was significant for several reasons: it made Buddhism more devotional and more accessible to ordinary people; it provided a framework for lay practice; and it became intertwined with political ideology, as rulers in East and Southeast Asia were sometimes depicted as bodhisattvas, linking religious and political authority.

Historical Vocabulary
Dharma

Dharma is a Sanskrit word with multiple, related meanings across Hindu and Buddhist traditions. In Buddhism, dharma (Pali: dhamma) refers primarily to the teaching of the Buddha — the truth about the nature of reality, suffering, and the path to liberation. When Buddhists take refuge in the "Three Jewels" (the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha), they are committing themselves to the Buddha's teaching as the path to liberation.

In Hinduism, dharma has several related meanings. Most broadly, it refers to the cosmic moral order that sustains the universe. More specifically, dharma refers to the duties and obligations appropriate to one's position in society — one's varna (social category), stage of life, and gender. A king's dharma is different from a merchant's dharma, which is different from a Brahmin's dharma. The Bhagavad Gita turns on a crisis of dharma: Arjuna, facing battle against his kinsmen, cannot see how fighting — which will cause death — can be right action. Understanding dharma helps students grasp why Ashoka's inscriptions represent a fusion of Buddhist and Brahmanical ethical frameworks, and why the concept was intelligible to both traditions.

Regional Lens

Regional Lens: Ethiopia and African Christianity

When students think of early Christianity, they often think of Rome, Constantinople, or the Latin West. But Christianity spread south as well as west, and one of the oldest and most distinctive Christian civilizations developed in the Ethiopian highlands.

The kingdom of Axum (Aksum), centered in modern northern Ethiopia and Eritrea, was a major Red Sea trading power in the first centuries CE, with connections to Arabia, the Roman Empire, India, and sub-Saharan Africa. Christianity reached Axum through commercial and diplomatic channels in the fourth century CE. The traditional account credits two Syrian Christian traders, Frumentius and Aedesius, who were shipwrecked and eventually came to serve at the Axumite royal court. Frumentius became a Christian teacher to the young prince Ezana, who converted and eventually made Christianity the official religion of Axum around 330–340 CE.

What is significant about Ethiopian Christianity is how distinctively it developed. Ethiopian Christianity adopted the Coptic position in the Chalcedonian controversy, placing it in the Oriental Orthodox communion. It translated Christian scriptures and liturgy into Ge'ez, creating a rich literary tradition. The Ethiopian church developed distinctive practices: a strong emphasis on the Old Testament and its connections with Jewish practice (including circumcision and Sabbath observation), veneration of the Ark of the Covenant (which Ethiopian tradition holds is preserved in the city of Axum), and a rich monastic tradition. Ethiopian Christianity was also deeply integrated with royal ideology, creating a tradition of sacred monarchy that persisted across Ethiopian history. Ethiopian Christianity is thus a powerful example of how a major religious tradition can spread beyond its Mediterranean heartland, adapt to a radically different cultural environment, and produce a distinctive and enduring civilization.

Global Connection

Global Connection: The Silk Roads and the Spread of Buddhism

The spread of Buddhism from South Asia to Central, East, and Southeast Asia was one of the most consequential examples of cultural diffusion in world history, and it was made possible in large part by the trade networks collectively known as the Silk Roads. This Global Connection explores how commerce and religion traveled together, and what resulted from their combination.

Buddhist monasteries along the overland Silk Road oasis routes served as crucial nodes in this network. Merchants who followed Buddhist practices needed places to pray, take vows, and perform meritorious acts; they funded monasteries along their routes. Monasteries, in turn, provided safe lodging, dispute resolution services (monks could serve as trusted neutral parties in commercial disputes), and cultural translation (multilingual monks could assist with communication across language barriers). The monastery system made the Silk Roads safer and more commercially viable, while commercial traffic made the monastery system more economically sustainable. Religion and commerce mutually reinforced each other.

The transmission of Buddhist texts across the Silk Roads was itself an extraordinary enterprise. Sacred texts — written on palm leaves in Sanskrit or Pali, or transcribed onto paper in Chinese, Tibetan, Sogdian, Uighur, or other languages — were carried by pilgrims and merchants across deserts and mountain ranges. The discovery of the Dunhuang manuscripts in a sealed cave in 1900 — thousands of texts in multiple languages, sealed around the eleventh century — gave historians an extraordinary window into the multilingual, multi-religious world of the Silk Roads. The artistic influence of Buddhism along the Silk Roads is also remarkable: Buddhist iconography spread along these routes, adapting to local artistic traditions, producing images that influenced Buddhist art across Central and East Asia.

The Silk Roads thus demonstrate a fundamental principle of world history: trade routes are also cultural corridors, and economic exchange and cultural exchange are deeply intertwined. Buddhism did not spread because it was objectively more appealing than other traditions; it spread because it was carried by people who had the resources, motivation, and opportunity to travel — merchants, missionaries, and pilgrims whose networks overlapped and reinforced each other across thousands of miles.

Civilization Study

Civilization Study: Nalanda and the World of Buddhist Learning

Among the most remarkable institutions of the late antique and post-classical world was Nalanda, the great Buddhist monastic university located in the modern Indian state of Bihar. At its height, Nalanda reportedly housed thousands of monks and scholars in a complex of teaching halls, dormitories, libraries, and temples. The Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang, who studied at Nalanda in the 630s CE, described it in detail: ten massive temple complexes, eight great halls, hundreds of classrooms, and a vast library whose three buildings bore names meaning "Ocean of Gems," "Sea of Gems," and "Jewel-adorned." Xuanzang himself studied there for five years.

The curriculum at Nalanda was not restricted to Buddhist subjects. Students studied logic, medicine, Sanskrit grammar, Samkhya philosophy, the Vedas, and other Hindu texts alongside Buddhist philosophy and practice. Debates — formal, public intellectual contests governed by rules of logic — were a central practice. Nalanda attracted students from across Asia: Xuanzang was one of several Chinese monks who made the long journey to study there; others came from Central Asia, Tibet, Korea, and Southeast Asia. In this sense, Nalanda functioned somewhat like a medieval university attracting international students — but its reach was vaster than any medieval European university.

Royal patronage was essential to Nalanda's existence. The Pala dynasty (c. 750–1161 CE), which ruled northeastern India for over four centuries, was among Nalanda's most important patrons. Nalanda's decline came gradually and then suddenly: the Ghurid invasions of northern India in the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries CE destroyed Nalanda and other Buddhist monasteries, burning their libraries and killing monks. Surviving monks fled to Nepal, Tibet, and Southeast Asia, carrying what texts and traditions they could preserve. Nalanda's intellectual heritage survived the destruction of its physical home by being distributed across a wider network of Buddhist communities — particularly in Tibet, where the Tibetan Buddhist tradition preserves an enormous body of scholarship that traces directly back to Nalanda.

Primary Source Window

The Bhagavad Gita — Krishna's Teaching on Devotion

Source type: Sacred text; philosophical dialogue embedded within the epic Mahabharata · Date of composition: approximately 400 BCE–400 CE · Language: Sanskrit

Context: The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of God") is embedded within the enormous Sanskrit epic the Mahabharata. The text depicts a dialogue between the warrior prince Arjuna and his charioteer Krishna (who is revealed to be the god Vishnu in human form) on the battlefield just before a great war. The Gita is one of the most important texts of Hindu tradition, used across many centuries and across many different Hindu philosophical schools as a touchstone for understanding dharma, devotion, and liberation.

Krishna teaches Arjuna that right action (dharma) must be performed without attachment to its results — the warrior must fight his kinsmen not because he wants victory or glory, but because fighting is his duty as a warrior, performed in devotion to the divine order. In a pivotal teaching, Krishna presents the path of bhakti — loving devotion — as the most accessible path to liberation. He teaches that those who offer their actions, fruits, and love to the divine, whatever their caste or gender, can achieve liberation. "Be intent on action, not on the fruits of action." — paraphrase of Bhagavad Gita 3.19

Analysis: The Bhagavad Gita is significant for world history for several reasons. First, it represents the development of the bhakti path — devotion as a means of liberation available to all, regardless of ritual status — within the Brahmanical Sanskrit tradition. Second, it shows the complex relationship between action, duty, and liberation that defined Hindu ethical thought. Third, it became the foundational text for multiple subsequent Hindu philosophical schools, each of which interpreted it differently — Shankara's non-dualistic reading, Ramanuja's qualified non-dualism, Madhva's strict dualism. The text's polyvalence was part of its power.

Limits: The Gita is a theological and philosophical text, not a historical account. It does not tell us directly how ordinary people lived, worshipped, or understood their dharma in everyday life. It represents the perspective of a learned, male, high-caste tradition. Later traditions that used the Gita often interpreted it in ways quite different from its surface meaning.

Archaeology & Material Evidence Window

Evidence Window: The Mogao Caves at Dunhuang

The Mogao Caves (Caves of the Thousand Buddhas) are a complex of Buddhist rock-cut temples located at the eastern edge of the Taklamakan Desert near the oasis town of Dunhuang, in modern Gansu Province, China. Construction of the caves began in the fourth century CE and continued through the fourteenth century, producing nearly 500 decorated chambers covering roughly 45,000 square meters of wall paintings and approximately 2,000 painted clay sculptures.

In approximately 1000 CE, a large cache of manuscripts and silk paintings was sealed in Cave 17 and not rediscovered until 1900. The cache contained approximately 40,000 manuscripts in multiple languages — Chinese, Tibetan, Sanskrit, Sogdian, Uighur, Tangut, and others — as well as silk paintings and other objects. This discovery was one of the most significant archaeological finds in world history.

What historians can infer: The Dunhuang caves provide an extraordinary window into the religious life of the Silk Roads over many centuries. The paintings depict Buddhist cosmological scenes, jataka tales, images of donors (the wealthy patrons who funded the caves), and portraits of Buddhist monks and bodhisattvas. The variety of styles reflects centuries of artistic development and the influence of Central Asian, South Asian, Chinese, and Tibetan artistic traditions on a single site. The manuscript cache illuminates literacy, commerce, and religious practice: manuscripts include Buddhist canonical texts, administrative documents, contracts, letters, calendars, medical texts, and divination manuals. The presence of texts in Sogdian, Tibetan, and Uighur shows the multilingual, multiethnic character of the Silk Road world.

Limits: The Dunhuang evidence is geographically specific — it reflects one wealthy oasis town's experience, shaped by its position on the trade routes and its particular history of patronage. We cannot simply generalize from Dunhuang to all of Central Asia. What the cache contains was selected (or happened to be preserved) by processes we do not fully understand. The visual evidence of the cave paintings reflects elite patronage — the donors who funded the caves were wealthy merchants and officials — not necessarily the experience of ordinary Buddhist practitioners.

Historical Debate

Why Did Buddhism Decline in India?

The question: Buddhism was born in India, flourished there for over a millennium, and produced some of its greatest intellectual and artistic achievements on Indian soil. Yet by approximately the 12th–13th centuries CE, Buddhism had virtually disappeared from the Indian subcontinent as a living religion, even as it continued to flourish in East Asia, Southeast Asia, and later in Tibet. Why?

Interpretation A — The Muslim Invasions: One prominent explanation focuses on the Ghurid invasions of northern India in the late 12th and early 13th centuries CE. The Ghurid armies destroyed the great Buddhist monastic universities of northeastern India — Nalanda, Vikramashila, and others — killed monks, and burned libraries. Since Buddhism in India was concentrated in large, institutionally vulnerable monastic centers, the physical destruction of those centers was devastating. Unlike Hinduism, which was embedded in village temples and family religious life that could survive the destruction of any particular institution, Buddhism in India depended heavily on its monastic institutions for the preservation and transmission of its teachings.
Interpretation B — Internal Decline Before the Invasions: A competing interpretation argues that Buddhism was already in significant decline in India before the Ghurid invasions. Proponents of this view point to the growing dominance of Hindu devotional traditions from the early medieval period onward, as royal patronage shifted increasingly toward Hindu temples. The great Bhakti poets drew popular devotion toward Hindu traditions. Buddhism's distinctive monastic path — requiring renunciation of family life — was less adaptable to ordinary social life than the household-centered devotional practices of Hinduism, which could be practiced by anyone without renouncing family, work, or caste identity.

Evidence: The first interpretation is supported by archaeological evidence of destruction at Nalanda and other sites, and by accounts in Muslim chronicles. The second interpretation is supported by the gradual decline of royal Buddhist patronage in many regions before the invasions, the growth of great Hindu temple complexes, and the vitality of Bhakti traditions.

Why it matters: This debate affects how historians understand the relationship between violent conquest and cultural change, and the relative importance of external versus internal factors in the transformation of religious traditions.

Comparison Across Societies

Comparison Across Societies: Buddhist and Christian Monasticism

Buddhist and Christian monasticism were independent inventions — Buddhism's monastic tradition is several centuries older than Christianity's — but they converged on remarkably similar institutional forms and served strikingly similar social functions.

Origins and motivation: Both traditions developed monasticism as a response to a perceived tension between worldly life and the demands of spiritual perfection. In Buddhism, the monastic sangha was established by the Buddha himself as the ideal vehicle for pursuing liberation. In Christianity, monasticism emerged from the conviction that the world was dangerously distracting and that concentrated prayer and ascetic discipline offered the surest path to spiritual transformation.

Rules and organization: Both traditions developed detailed rules governing monastic life. The Buddhist Vinaya regulated monks' and nuns' diet, dress, sleeping arrangements, property, relationships with lay people, and community governance. The Christian Rule of Saint Benedict similarly regulated every aspect of daily life, dividing the day between prayer, work, and study.

Economic and social functions: Both Buddhist and Christian monasteries accumulated wealth through donations and managed agricultural estates. Both provided social welfare services — hospitals, orphanages, schools. Both preserved and transmitted learning. Both served as nodes in wider networks of pilgrimage, scholarship, and religious authority that connected distant communities.

Key differences: Buddhist monasteries were sustained by daily begging rounds (especially in Theravada traditions), maintaining a close relationship with lay supporters; Christian monasteries generally relied on endowments and agricultural labor. In Christianity, monasteries existed within the hierarchy of bishops and priests; in Buddhism, the monastic sangha itself constituted the primary institutional form of the religion, without an equivalent hierarchical structure of bishops. The comparison demonstrates that monasticism was not a uniquely Christian or Buddhist invention but a solution that two independent religious traditions arrived at for related reasons.

Multiple Perspectives

Multiple Perspectives: Who Experienced Buddhism's Spread?

Rulers and the Aristocracy: For rulers and aristocratic elites, Buddhism offered attractive political resources. A king who patronized Buddhism gained access to an international religious network that could legitimize his rule, provide him with educated advisors and administrators, and connect him to the prestigious cultural world of Buddhist South Asia. Buddhist cosmological frameworks — in which the ideal king is a dharma-raja, a righteous ruler who supports the sangha — provided models for legitimate authority.
Monks and Scholars: For monks and scholars who populated the great Buddhist monastic institutions, Buddhism was primarily an intellectual and spiritual vocation. They debated philosophy, interpreted texts, practiced meditation, and maintained the institutional life of the sangha. For them, Buddhism's spread was both a spiritual mission and an intellectual enterprise. The experience of pilgrims like Faxian and Xuanzang — months of dangerous travel, years of intensive study in foreign monasteries — reflects the intensity of this commitment.
Merchants: For merchants, Buddhism offered a different set of resources: a religious community that provided hospitality along trade routes, a moral framework that encouraged honesty and generosity in commercial dealings, a network of co-religionists who could be trusted business partners, and the spiritual benefit of merit-making through donations to monasteries. Buddhism's spread and commercial exchange were mutually reinforcing.
Ordinary People: For ordinary people — farmers, craftworkers, urban laborers, women — Buddhism offered access to merit-making, ritual services, healing practices, and a cosmological framework that made sense of suffering and offered hope for better circumstances. For women in many Buddhist societies, the monastic option offered a path outside of marriage and family. For communities at the margins of caste hierarchies, Buddhism's theoretical rejection of birth-based status could be attractive, though in practice Buddhist institutions often reflected the caste structures of the societies around them.
Non-Buddhist Communities: Communities whose religious traditions Buddhism displaced or competed with experienced Buddhism's spread from yet another perspective. Chinese Confucian critics complained that Buddhism was a foreign religion undermining Chinese social values by encouraging men to leave their families for monastic life, and damaging the economy through tax-exempt monastic wealth. In South Asia, Brahmanical communities competed directly with Buddhist institutions for royal patronage, and the philosophical debates between Buddhist and Hindu thinkers were intensely serious intellectual contests with real institutional stakes.
Cause and Effect

The Christianization of the Roman Empire

Long-Term Causes

Rome's religious pluralism provided fertile ground for new movements. The mystery cults of the Hellenistic world had prepared Mediterranean populations for initiation-based communities offering personal salvation. Judaism's monotheism was available for engagement by non-Jewish populations. Stoic philosophy shared certain values with Christian teaching. The Roman Empire's urban network, common languages, and administrative infrastructure created conditions for rapid diffusion.

Short-Term Causes

Emperor Constantine's reported vision before the Battle of Milvian Bridge (312 CE) led him to adopt Christian symbols. His political decision to support Christianity transformed its material situation dramatically. Imperial patronage meant resources for building, for paying clergy, for convening councils. Imperial authority could suppress competing traditions and compel at least formal compliance with Christianity's expansion.

The Transformation

Over roughly a century (c. 300–400 CE), Christianity went from being a persecuted minority to the official religion of the Roman Empire. Ecumenical councils defined orthodoxy. Public funding flowed to church building. Pagan temples were sometimes converted to churches or destroyed. The Edict of Milan (313 CE) granted toleration; the Edict of Thessalonica (380 CE) established Christianity as the state religion.

Long-Term Consequences

Christianity became the framework for European culture, law, art, philosophy, and social organization throughout the medieval period and beyond. When European powers later colonized the Americas, Africa, and Asia, Christianity traveled with them — directly as a consequence of its integration into European civilization during this period. The institutional model of a universal church with hierarchical authority shaped Western political thought about the relationship between religious and civil authority for over a millennium.

Historical complexity: The Christianization of the Roman Empire was not simply the triumph of a better religion or the inevitable result of Christianity's appeal. It was the product of specific political choices — particularly Constantine's — combined with structural features of the late Roman world. Without imperial patronage, Christianity might well have remained one minority tradition among many.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Continuity and Change Over Time: Buddhist Practice from India to East Asia

What Changed

  • Language of Buddhist learning shifted from Sanskrit and Pali to Chinese (and later Korean, Japanese, and Tibetan), reshaping doctrine through translation choices
  • Institutional role of Buddhism changed dramatically: from coexisting with Hindu temples in a religiously plural South Asian environment to being one of the most institutionally powerful forces in Tang China
  • Philosophical schools of East Asian Buddhism (Pure Land, Chan, Tiantai, Huayan) developed in response to specifically Chinese philosophical and social contexts
  • Visual culture of Buddhism transformed: the serene Gandharan Buddha was joined by a proliferating array of bodhisattvas, celestial Buddhas, and divine guardians reflecting Central Asian, Chinese, and Korean artistic traditions

What Stayed the Same

  • The Four Noble Truths — the diagnosis of suffering, craving, and the path to liberation — remained central
  • The Three Jewels — the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha — remained the foundational commitments of Buddhist community
  • The ethical precepts (non-killing, non-stealing, sexual restraint, truthfulness) remained normative for monks and lay people alike
  • Monastic life, organized around a code of discipline, prayer, and study, remained the institutional core of the tradition
  • The practice of meditation — sitting in stillness, attending to the breath and the mind — remained central to Buddhist spiritual practice

Why the pattern matters: The pattern of Buddhism's transformation across cultural boundaries illustrates a fundamental dynamic of religious history: that traditions change as they travel, adapting to new contexts while maintaining continuities that preserve their identity. This challenges oversimplified narratives of pure transmission.

Everyday Life and Social History

Everyday Life: Religious Practice in a Tang Chinese Household

What did religion look like in the daily life of an ordinary Tang Chinese household in the seventh or eighth century CE? An ordinary household in Tang China might observe a complex of practices that did not fit neatly into any single "religion" as modern people understand the term. The household would maintain a small shrine to the household god (zaoshen, the kitchen god), making offerings before meals and on festival days — a practice deeply embedded in Chinese folk religion predating Buddhism and Daoism. The household head would perform ancestral rites, offering food and incense at the ancestral shrine and on grave sites, maintaining the continuity between the living and the dead that was fundamental to Chinese social ethics. These practices were grounded in Confucian values of filial piety.

At the same time, the household would have connections to the local Buddhist monastery. A member of the family might go to the monastery to request prayers for a sick child or elderly parent, to commission a Buddhist text to be recited for the merit of a deceased ancestor, or to participate in a festival. Popular Buddhist festivals — especially celebrations associated with the bodhisattva Dizang (who was believed to help souls in the underworld) — were widely attended community events that brought together people across social classes in shared celebration.

Daoist religious practitioners might also visit the household — offering protective charms, performing rituals to ward off malevolent spirits, or providing divination services. The boundaries between Buddhist, Daoist, and folk religious practices were fluid in everyday life, even as elite thinkers debated the superiority of their respective traditions. Women in Tang China had access to some Buddhist religious roles; they could become nuns and participate in devotional practices. The bodhisattva Guanyin — originally depicted as male in South Asian iconography but increasingly depicted as female in Chinese Buddhist art — became one of the most widely venerated figures in Chinese Buddhism, associated with compassion, healing, and fertility. For rural farmers, the religious calendar provided a framework for the agricultural year, integrating religious observance with the rhythms of agricultural life.

This picture of Tang Chinese religious practice illustrates a fundamental principle: in most pre-modern societies, religion was not a single, exclusive commitment but a complex of overlapping practices embedded in daily life, household relationships, community festivals, and agricultural rhythms. Understanding this complexity is essential to understanding how religious change actually worked — not as a wholesale replacement of one tradition by another, but as a gradual layering and integration of new practices alongside persistent older ones.

Environment and Technology

Environment and Technology: Sacred Landscapes and the Built Religious World

Religious communities in the late antique and post-classical periods were among the largest patrons of architecture and construction in their respective societies. The built environments they created — churches, monasteries, stupas, temples, pilgrimage routes — were both expressions of religious belief and transformations of the natural and social landscape.

The Buddhist stupa and cave temple were among the most widely distributed architectural forms in Afro-Eurasian history. The construction of a great stupa required enormous resources: stone, brick, labor, timber for scaffolding, and the organizational capacity to coordinate large workforces. Royal patronage was essential; the greatest stupas were monuments to the rulers who funded them as much as to the Buddha whose relics they enshrined. Cave temples — carved into cliff faces — required different techniques but similar resources. Cave temples had significant environmental advantages: they were protected from weather and were naturally cool. They also had cultural significance: caves were associated in many traditions with meditation and proximity to the sacred powers of the earth.

Christian church architecture transformed the urban landscape of the late Roman world. The construction of great churches required mastery of Roman building techniques: arches, vaults, concrete, and stone. Church construction was a major economic activity employing architects, stonemasons, mosaicists, carpenters, painters, and metalworkers. The production of mosaic — covering walls and vaults with thousands of tiny glass and stone tesserae — required specialized craft knowledge. The use of gold tesserae (glass backed with gold leaf) created the shimmering luminous quality characteristic of Byzantine sacred art.

Hindu temple construction involved sophisticated architectural and engineering knowledge. The great temples of South India were built without mortar, relying on the precise fitting of massive stone blocks. The knowledge of how to build, consecrate, and maintain a temple was encoded in technical texts called Agamas and Shilpashastras — manuals of sacred architecture specifying the correct proportions, orientations, iconographic programs, and ritual procedures for temple construction. Sacred rivers (the Ganges, the Kaveri) were focal points for temple towns and pilgrimage routes, and the organization of sacred geography across the subcontinent helped create a Hindu sense of shared sacred space that transcended regional boundaries.

Chapter Summary

The period from approximately 200 to 900 CE witnessed one of the most consequential transformations in the religious history of the world. Three major traditions — Christianity, Buddhism, and the complex of traditions we call Hinduism — underwent dramatic internal development, geographic expansion, and institutional growth. Each tradition interacted with political power, shaped and was shaped by empires, and spread across vast distances through the movement of missionaries, merchants, pilgrims, and refugees.

Christianity began the period as a persecuted minority religion within the Roman Empire. The conversion of Emperor Constantine in the early fourth century CE, the legal toleration established by the Edict of Milan (313 CE), and the eventual establishment of Christianity as the Roman state religion (380 CE) transformed its situation entirely. Within a century, Christianity had gone from meeting in private homes to building magnificent basilicas, from being suppressed to suppressing competitors. Internally, Christianity developed through intense theological debate that produced ecumenical councils, creedal statements, and lasting divisions. Externally, Christianity spread westward into Germanic kingdoms, northward into Ireland and Scotland, southward into Ethiopia, and eastward along the Silk Roads into Persia, Central Asia, and eventually China.

Buddhism spread from its South Asian homeland across Central, East, and Southeast Asia during this period, adapting to radically different cultural environments while maintaining its core commitments to the Four Noble Truths, the Three Jewels, and the monastic sangha. The Silk Roads were crucial corridors of this spread. Great monastic institutions — Nalanda in India, the great monasteries of Tang China, the cave complexes of Dunhuang — served as centers of scholarship, art, and spiritual practice. In East Asia, Buddhism diversified into multiple schools — Pure Land, Chan, Tiantai, Huayan — each adapting Buddhist teaching to Chinese philosophical and social contexts. The Tang suppression of Buddhism in 845 CE is a reminder that even deeply rooted religious institutions are vulnerable to political change.

The Hindu traditions of South Asia underwent equally significant development during this period. The Gupta Empire (c. 320–550 CE) presided over an extraordinary flowering of Sanskrit literature, philosophy, mathematics, and temple architecture. The great Hindu temple complex became one of the defining institutions of South and Southeast Asian civilization. The Bhakti movement, beginning in South India in approximately the sixth century CE, opened new devotional paths to people of all backgrounds — women, lower-caste communities, artisans — through vernacular poetry of extraordinary emotional power. Philosophical developments, especially Adi Shankara's Advaita Vedanta, systematized Hindu thought in response to both internal development and the intellectual challenge of Buddhism.

Religious art and architecture of this period constitute some of the most extraordinary material evidence of human culture. From the Byzantine mosaics of Ravenna to the cave paintings of Ajanta and Dunhuang, from the rock-cut temples of Ellora to the pagodas of Tang China, religious communities invested enormous resources in creating built environments that expressed their cosmologies, organized ritual life, and declared the power of their patrons.

The spread of all three traditions was facilitated by the trade routes of Afro-Eurasia — the Silk Roads overland, the Indian Ocean routes by sea. Merchants, missionaries, and pilgrims traveled together, sharing roads and ships, supporting each other's activities, and carrying religious ideas alongside commercial goods. The intersection of commerce and religion was not incidental; it was structural.

Comparing the three traditions illuminates both shared patterns and meaningful differences. All three developed elaborate institutional structures during this period. All three produced extraordinary bodies of philosophical, literary, and artistic achievement. All three diversified internally as they spread across different cultural environments. But they differed in their relationship to political authority, their institutional structures, their social inclusion or exclusion, and their relationship to the broader cultural worlds in which they developed.

The religious transformations of this period had consequences that endured for more than a millennium. The Christianity of medieval Europe, the Buddhism of East and Southeast Asia, the Hinduism of South Asia — all took forms profoundly shaped by the developments described in this chapter. Understanding late antiquity's religious history is thus essential to understanding the world that followed.

Key Terms

Monasticism
The practice of withdrawing from ordinary society to live in a religious community devoted to prayer, study, and spiritual discipline. Both Buddhism and Christianity developed major monastic traditions during this period.

The Buddhist monastic community; one of the Three Jewels of Buddhism (along with the Buddha and the Dharma). The sangha was the primary institutional form of Buddhism and the vehicle for transmitting Buddhist teaching.

A theological position holding that Jesus was a created being subordinate to God the Father rather than co-equal with the Father. Condemned at Nicaea but persistent for centuries afterward.

A declaration (313 CE) by Emperor Constantine granting Christianity and other religions legal toleration throughout the Roman Empire. A turning point in Christianity's institutional history.
Theravada
"The Way of the Elders"; the form of Buddhism that became dominant in Sri Lanka and mainland Southeast Asia. Emphasizes the Pali canon and the path of individual liberation through monastic discipline.
Mahayana
"Greater Vehicle"; the form of Buddhism dominant in East Asia. Emphasizes the bodhisattva ideal of universal compassion and the accessibility of liberation to all beings.
Vajrayana
"Diamond Vehicle" or Tantric Buddhism; a form of Buddhist practice emphasizing ritual, visualization, and esoteric teachings. Particularly influential in Tibet and parts of Central Asia.
Bodhisattva
In Mahayana Buddhism, a being who has attained enlightenment but delays final nirvana to help all sentient beings achieve liberation. Major bodhisattvas became objects of popular devotion.
Pure Land Buddhism
A Mahayana school extremely popular in East Asia, emphasizing devotion to Amitabha Buddha and the hope of rebirth in his western paradise, from which enlightenment would be more easily achieved.
Chan Buddhism
A school of East Asian Buddhism (Zen in Japan) that emphasized direct meditation experience over textual study, developing distinctive practices of teacher-student dialogue and wordless transmission.
Stupa
A dome-shaped Buddhist monument containing relics, serving as a focal point for veneration and pilgrimage. As Buddhism spread, the stupa form transformed into the pagoda in East Asia.
Nalanda
The great Buddhist monastic university in northeastern India, active approximately from the 5th through 13th centuries CE. A major center of international Buddhist scholarship attracting students from across Asia.
Bhakti
A devotional path in Hinduism emphasizing personal love and emotional relationship to a deity, particularly Vishnu or Shiva. The Bhakti movement opened devotional religion to people of all backgrounds.
Alvars
Tamil poet-saints (c. 6th–9th centuries CE) devoted to Vishnu, whose hymns became foundational texts of South Indian Vaishnava devotion. Included women and people of low-caste backgrounds.
Advaita Vedanta
The philosophical school systematized by Adi Shankara (c. 788–820 CE), teaching that the individual self (atman) and ultimate reality (Brahman) are non-dual — fundamentally one.
Dharma
In Buddhism, the teaching of the Buddha and the path of ethical and spiritual practice. In Hinduism, the moral and cosmic order, and one's duties within that order according to one's position in society.
Puja
Hindu devotional worship offered to a divine image in a temple or household shrine, typically involving offerings of food, flowers, incense, and light, accompanied by prayers and hymns.
Syncretism
The blending of different religious traditions, beliefs, or practices into new hybrid forms. Common in the history of religious diffusion as traditions adapt to new cultural environments.
Pilgrimage
A journey to a sacred site undertaken for religious purposes. Pilgrimage was central to all three traditions examined in this chapter, creating networks of travel and exchange connecting distant regions.
Basilica
The architectural form adopted by early Christian communities for congregational worship: a large rectangular hall with a central nave flanked by side aisles, borrowed from Roman civic architecture.
Iconography
The use of images or symbols to represent religious figures, stories, or concepts in art and architecture. The development of Buddhist and Christian iconographic systems is a major subject of art history and religious history.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined one of the most consequential periods of religious transformation in world history — a period when three of the world's major religious traditions underwent dramatic growth, institutional development, geographic expansion, and internal diversification. Understanding this transformation is not merely an exercise in religious history; it is essential background for understanding the political, cultural, and social history of the entire subsequent medieval and early modern world.

The religious geography established during this period — a Christian Europe and Near East, a Buddhist East and Southeast Asia, a Hindu South Asia — shaped the world that subsequent chapters will explore. When the Mongol Empire created its vast Eurasian network in the thirteenth century (Chapter 28), it connected regions whose religious cultures had been shaped by the developments described here. When European maritime expansion began in the fifteenth century (Chapter 37), it carried a Christianity whose institutional and theological form had been forged in the late antique period. When Islam spread across the Middle East, North Africa, Central Asia, and beyond (Chapters 19–20), it encountered Buddhist, Christian, and Hindu communities whose presence and institutions were products of this chapter's history.

The chapter also illustrates several of the most fundamental patterns in world history. Religious traditions, like all cultural forms, spread through networks — of trade, of political power, of migration and pilgrimage. They adapt to new environments while maintaining core commitments. They diversify internally even as they maintain family resemblances. They interact with political power in complex relationships of mutual benefit and mutual risk. And they are experienced differently by different groups within any given society — rulers and subjects, monks and merchants, men and women, literate and illiterate, urban and rural.

The chapter also asks students to practice one of the most difficult skills in world history: comparing traditions without ranking them. Christianity, Buddhism, and Hinduism each produced extraordinary art, philosophy, institutions, and communities. Each also participated in systems of power — imperial authority, caste hierarchy, gender subordination — that subjected many people to domination and exclusion. Historians can describe and analyze both the creativity and the power dynamics of these traditions without concluding that any one tradition was simply better or worse than the others.

Finally, this chapter reminds students that world history is not only the history of political power and economic exchange. It is the history of how human beings have sought meaning — in prayer and meditation, in temple and monastery, in sacred story and devotional song, in pilgrimage and community and the care of the dead. The late antique and post-classical centuries were a time of extraordinary religious creativity, and the traditions shaped in this period continue to be lived by hundreds of millions of people today. That living continuity gives this history its particular depth and its enduring importance.

Part IV — Late Antiquity and the Post-Classical Transition

The Islamic Caliphates and Afro-Eurasian Exchange

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

In the year 762 CE, the Abbasid caliph al-Mansur ordered the construction of a new capital city on the western bank of the Tigris River. He called it Madinat al-Salam — the City of Peace — though the world would come to know it as Baghdad.

The city was designed in a perfect circle, a form chosen for both symbolic and practical reasons, with the caliph's palace and the congregational mosque at its center and a series of gates opening outward in the four cardinal directions toward the known world. Within a generation, Baghdad had grown into one of the largest cities on earth, a metropolis of perhaps five hundred thousand or more inhabitants. Merchants arrived from East Africa and Central Asia, from India and China, from the Mediterranean coast and the steppes of Eurasia. Scholars translated Greek philosophical texts into Arabic. Physicians debated the causes of disease and the properties of medicines. Mathematicians worked with number systems borrowed from India and developed algebra as a new field of inquiry. Poets composed in classical Arabic, Persian, and other tongues. The city was a crossroads of faiths and peoples — Muslim, Christian, Jewish, Zoroastrian, Hindu, and Buddhist — all living under a system of governance that was, in many respects, more inclusive of religious diversity than most empires of the ancient world.

Baghdad was the capstone of a transformation that had begun more than a century earlier. The rise of Islam in the seventh century CE on the Arabian Peninsula had unleashed a wave of expansion that within a hundred years had brought under a single political order territories stretching from the Iberian Peninsula in the west to the borders of Central Asia and South Asia in the east. This was not simply military conquest; it was a complex process of governance, religious incorporation, economic integration, and cultural contact that reshaped the lands it touched. The Arab armies that emerged from Arabia in the 630s and 640s CE were small in comparison to the empires they defeated, yet they succeeded with remarkable speed against the Sasanian Persian Empire to the east and the Byzantine Roman Empire's territories to the north and west. The reasons for this success involve military factors, but also the political exhaustion of the great empires they displaced, the appeal of Islamic teachings to a range of peoples, and the ability of early Muslim rulers to incorporate local elites and administrative traditions into their system of governance.

This chapter examines the Islamic caliphates from the period of rapid expansion through the height of the Abbasid period, roughly the seventh through the thirteenth centuries CE, and the role of the Islamic world as a central connector of Afro-Eurasian history. Students should understand that the caliphates were not monolithic entities. The Umayyad caliphate based in Damascus governed differently from the Abbasid caliphate based in Baghdad, and regional Islamic states eventually challenged the unity of the caliphate system altogether. The Islamic world of this period was internally diverse — diverse in languages, ethnic identities, legal schools, theological orientations, and local cultural traditions. What held this world together was not uniformity but a shared religious framework, a common written language in Arabic for scholarship and administration, a legal tradition rooted in the Quran and the reported sayings and practices of the Prophet Muhammad, and an extraordinary network of trade and scholarship that connected distant regions.

The Islamic world in this period was also a major vehicle of cultural exchange across Afro-Eurasia. Islamic merchants carried goods across the Indian Ocean, through the Sahara, along the Silk Roads of Central Asia, and across the Mediterranean Sea. The cities of the Islamic world were centers of scholarship and translation, where the intellectual traditions of Greece, Persia, India, and Mesopotamia were preserved, studied, debated, and extended. Mathematics, astronomy, medicine, philosophy, chemistry, geography, and literature all advanced significantly in this environment. Many of these achievements were later transmitted to medieval Europe, contributing to what European scholars sometimes call the Renaissance, while also shaping the intellectual traditions of South and Southeast Asia, East Africa, and Central Asia.

Understanding the Islamic caliphates also requires grappling with the experiences of the many peoples who lived under their rule. The majority of the populations living under caliphal authority were not Arab and, for much of the Umayyad period, were not Muslim. Non-Muslim communities — Jews, Christians, Zoroastrians, Hindus, and others — occupied legally recognized positions as dhimmis, meaning protected peoples who paid a special tax called the jizya in exchange for the right to practice their religion and maintain their communities. This system had strengths and limits. It allowed for a degree of religious pluralism unusual in the premodern world, but it also created legal hierarchies that privileged Muslim subjects. Over time, conversion to Islam spread through many of these populations, driven by a range of factors including genuine religious conviction, social opportunity, and the desire to escape the jizya tax.

The Islamic world was also shaped by gender, class, and labor. Women occupied diverse roles across the varied societies of the Islamic world — as scholars, merchants, landowners, and household managers, as well as in the more constrained legal status often described in legal texts. Enslaved people from Africa, Central Asia, and other regions formed a significant part of the economy, performing labor in households, in agriculture, and in the military. The experiences of enslaved people, conquered populations, rural peasants, and urban craftspeople all formed part of the broader social fabric of the caliphates, even though their voices are often harder to recover from the surviving sources than the voices of rulers, scholars, and merchants.

This chapter connects to the larger world-history story of post-classical networks and exchanges. The Islamic caliphates existed alongside and interacted with the Byzantine Empire, Tang and Song dynasty China, the kingdoms and empires of sub-Saharan Africa, the emerging states of Europe, and the trading civilizations of South and Southeast Asia. The connections established during this period — commercial, intellectual, religious, and diplomatic — laid groundwork for the global networks that would intensify after 1000 CE and that would eventually link the entire world into what historians call the modern world-system.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how and why Islamic rule expanded so rapidly from Arabia across Southwest Asia, North Africa, the Iberian Peninsula, and Central Asia between the 630s and 750s CE.
  2. Describe the political and administrative features of the Umayyad caliphate, including its capital at Damascus, its governance of diverse populations, and the tensions that led to its overthrow.
  3. Analyze how the Abbasid Revolution transformed the caliphate and why Baghdad became a major center of world civilization.
  4. Identify the roles of caliphs, the ulama, merchants, and scholars in shaping Islamic society and governance.
  5. Trace the major trade routes that connected the Islamic world to sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Central Asia, China, and the Mediterranean.
  6. Evaluate the scientific, mathematical, medical, and philosophical achievements of scholars in the Islamic world and their connections to earlier Greek, Persian, and Indian traditions.
  7. Compare how different groups — Muslims, dhimmis, enslaved people, women, rural peasants, urban scholars — experienced life under the caliphates.
  8. Assess the role of the Islamic world as a connector of Afro-Eurasian trade, scholarship, religion, and cultural exchange.
  9. Distinguish between the unified caliphate of the early period and the later fragmentation into regional Islamic states.
  10. Contextualize the Islamic caliphates within the broader patterns of post-classical world history, including comparison with the Byzantine Empire, Tang China, and the kingdoms of sub-Saharan Africa.
  11. Interpret primary sources from the Islamic world, including the Quran, hadith literature, travel accounts, and legal texts, as historical evidence.
  12. Synthesize an understanding of how the Islamic world shaped later world history through commerce, scholarship, and cultural transmission.

Historical Context

The emergence of the Islamic caliphates cannot be understood without attention to the world into which Islam was born. The Arabian Peninsula in the early seventh century CE was a region of considerable diversity. Its western coastline, the Hijaz, was home to trading cities like Mecca and Medina that served as nodes in the long-distance caravan trade connecting the Arabian interior to Yemen in the south, to Syria and the Mediterranean in the north, and to the Persian Gulf coast in the east. The interior of the peninsula was dominated by pastoral nomadic peoples organized into tribal confederations who herded camels, horses, and sheep across vast arid landscapes. The peninsula had no unified political authority; power resided in tribal alliances, in the prestige of religious sanctuaries, and in the wealth of merchant families.

Arabia was bordered by two great empires that had been at war with each other for more than a century. To the north and west lay the Byzantine Empire, the eastern continuation of the Roman Empire centered at Constantinople, which controlled Egypt, Syria-Palestine, and Anatolia. To the northeast lay the Sasanian Persian Empire, centered in Mesopotamia at its capital Ctesiphon, which controlled Iran, Iraq, and stretches of Central Asia. These two empires had fought repeatedly along their common frontiers, most recently in a devastating series of wars between roughly 602 and 628 CE. The Byzantine-Sasanian wars of this period had exhausted both empires militarily, financially, and demographically. They had also unsettled the populations of the frontier regions between them, including Syria and Mesopotamia, whose largely Christian and Zoroastrian populations had experienced the disruptions of war, plague, and shifting political loyalties.

Into this context, the prophet Muhammad ibn Abdallah, born in Mecca around 570 CE, received what he understood to be divine revelation beginning around 610 CE, a revelation that would eventually be compiled as the Quran. Muhammad's preaching called for monotheism, social justice, care for the poor and vulnerable, and submission to the will of God (in Arabic, Allah). His message challenged the social hierarchies and polytheistic practices of Meccan society, and he faced resistance from Mecca's powerful merchant elite. In 622 CE, Muhammad and his followers migrated to the city of Medina — an event known as the Hijra that marks the beginning of the Islamic calendar. From Medina, Muhammad built a new community (the umma) and eventually returned to conquer Mecca in 630 CE, two years before his death in 632 CE.

The death of Muhammad created an immediate crisis of succession. Muslims were united in their faith but had no universally agreed mechanism for selecting a new leader. The solution adopted was the caliphate (from the Arabic word khalifa, meaning successor or deputy), an office in which a political-religious leader would guide the Muslim community in accordance with Islamic principles. The first four caliphs — Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali — are known in Sunni Islamic tradition as the Rashidun, or rightly guided caliphs. Their period of rule, from 632 to 661 CE, saw the consolidation of the Muslim community in Arabia, the suppression of breakaway tribal movements in the Ridda Wars, and the first great wave of Islamic expansion. Arab armies swept into Syria, Palestine, Egypt, Iraq, and Iran, defeating both the Byzantine forces in Syria and the Sasanian armies in Iraq and Iran with remarkable speed.

Regional Orientation

The Islamic caliphates encompassed an enormous and geographically diverse arc of territory. The heartland of the early caliphates was the Arabian Peninsula and the Fertile Crescent — the arc of agriculturally productive land extending from the Persian Gulf through Iraq, Syria, and Palestine to the Mediterranean coast. This region was crossed by major rivers: the Tigris and Euphrates in Iraq and the Jordan River in Palestine. It was surrounded by the Syrian Desert to the south and the Anatolian plateau to the north.

To the west lay Egypt, one of the most productive agricultural regions in the world thanks to the annual flood of the Nile River. Egypt's position at the junction of the Mediterranean and the Red Sea made it a critical node in long-distance trade. Further west, the North African coast connected the Islamic world to trans-Saharan trade routes leading into sub-Saharan Africa. The Umayyads extended Muslim control westward across the Strait of Gibraltar into the Iberian Peninsula, beginning the era of al-Andalus.

To the northeast, the caliphates incorporated the former heartland of the Sasanian Persian Empire: Iraq, Iran, and the border regions of Central Asia. The intellectual and administrative traditions of Persia were enormously important to the development of Islamic civilization. Persian became the language of poetry, administration, and culture across much of the eastern Islamic world, even while Arabic remained the language of the Quran and of religious scholarship. Further east, the Silk Road cities of Samarkand and Bukhara in Central Asia connected the Islamic world to China and the nomadic peoples of the Eurasian steppe.

The Indian Ocean was perhaps the single most important geographic feature shaping the connections of the Islamic world beyond its political borders. The monsoon winds — blowing predictably northeastward in winter and southwestward in summer — made long-distance maritime trade feasible on a large scale. Muslim merchants were major participants in this Indian Ocean world, trading from ports in the Persian Gulf and Red Sea to ports along the western coast of India, Ceylon, the Malay Peninsula, and the coasts of East Africa.

Section 20.1

The Expansion of Islamic Rule

When the Prophet Muhammad died in 632 CE, the community he had built was still largely confined to the Arabian Peninsula. Within a century, the Islamic caliphate would govern an empire stretching from Spain and Morocco in the west to the borders of India and Central Asia in the east. This transformation was one of the most dramatic in world history, and understanding it requires examining both the military campaigns of the early caliphate and the broader political and social conditions that made such rapid expansion possible.

The first phase of Islamic expansion occurred under the Rashidun caliphs, the four successors to Muhammad who governed the Muslim community from 632 to 661 CE. After dealing with the Ridda Wars — uprisings by Arabian tribes who had pledged loyalty to Muhammad personally and who questioned whether that loyalty extended to his successors — the caliphs Abu Bakr and Umar turned Arab military energies outward. The campaigns were remarkable in their speed. Arab forces defeated the Byzantine army at the Battle of Yarmouk in 636 CE and took control of Syria and Palestine. Egypt fell between 639 and 641 CE. The Sasanian Persian Empire's forces were defeated at the Battle of al-Qadisiyya in 636 CE and at Nihavand in 642 CE; by 651 CE, the last Sasanian emperor had been killed and Iran was under Muslim control.

The military success of Arab forces in this period has been much debated by historians. Several factors contributed. The Arab armies were highly mobile, using camels effectively to cross desert terrain that was less favorable to the heavier cavalry armies of Byzantium and Persia. The two great empires they faced were genuinely exhausted by decades of warfare against each other; both had depleted their treasuries and suffered heavy casualties in the Byzantine-Sasanian wars of 602–628 CE. In some regions, particularly among the Monophysite Christian communities of Syria and Egypt who had been in theological conflict with the Byzantine state, the Arab rulers were at least no more oppressive than their predecessors and sometimes more tolerant. Early Islamic governance of conquered territories was pragmatic: conquered populations who submitted peacefully were generally allowed to keep their religions, their legal communities, and their local elites, in exchange for the payment of taxes.

The second major phase of expansion continued under the Umayyad caliphate after 661 CE. Muslim forces pushed westward across North Africa, reaching Morocco by around 700 CE and crossing the Strait of Gibraltar into the Iberian Peninsula in 711 CE. The Umayyads established al-Andalus on the Iberian Peninsula, where Muslim rule would continue in various forms until 1492 CE. Muslim armies also pushed eastward, reaching Sind in the Indus Valley in 711 CE and crossing the Oxus River into Central Asia. At their furthest expansion, Muslim forces reached the borders of Tang dynasty China, where they fought the Battle of Talas in 751 CE — a conflict that, according to traditional accounts, led to the introduction of papermaking technology into the Islamic world, although the actual path of this technology's transfer was likely more complex.

By the mid-eighth century CE, the Islamic caliphate had become one of the largest political entities in world history. It was diverse in ways that posed enormous administrative challenges. The populations it governed spoke dozens of languages — Arabic, Greek, Coptic, Syriac, Persian, Berber, and many others. They practiced numerous religions — Christianity in many forms, Judaism, Zoroastrianism, Manichaeanism, various local polytheisms, and increasingly Islam itself. They lived in environments ranging from the desert interior of Arabia to the Nile Delta, the Fertile Crescent, the Iranian plateau, the Indus Valley, and the olive groves and vineyards of the Iberian Peninsula. Governing this diversity required institutional creativity and produced both remarkable achievements and significant tensions.

Historical Vocabulary
Caliphate

The caliphate (from the Arabic word khalifa, meaning successor or deputy) was the political-religious institution through which the Muslim community was governed after the death of the Prophet Muhammad in 632 CE. The caliph was understood to be the successor to Muhammad's role as leader of the Muslim community — not as a prophet (Islam holds that Muhammad was the last prophet), but as a guardian of Islamic law and the defender of the community. The early caliphate was chosen by a council of leading Muslims; later, the Umayyad and Abbasid caliphates became hereditary dynasties. The caliphate remained the theoretical center of Sunni Muslim political authority until its formal abolition by the Turkish Republic in 1924. Understanding the caliphate is essential for understanding Islamic political thought, the organization of Islamic empires, and the debates over legitimate authority that shaped the Sunni-Shia split.

Section 20.2

The Umayyad Caliphate

The Umayyad caliphate, which ruled from 661 to 750 CE with its capital at Damascus in Syria, represented the first consolidation of Islamic imperial rule. Its founder, Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan, came to power after defeating and sidelining Ali ibn Abi Talib, the fourth caliph and Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law, in the conflict known as the First Fitna (civil war). The supporters of Ali — the Shiat Ali, or "party of Ali," the origin of Shia Islam — refused to accept Umayyad legitimacy and continued to assert that leadership of the Muslim community should remain within the Prophet's family.

Under the Umayyads, the caliphate took on increasingly imperial characteristics. Damascus was an ancient and cosmopolitan city with traditions of governance rooted in the Byzantine and Hellenistic past. The early Umayyad rulers incorporated Syrian Christian administrators into their bureaucracy, initially maintained Greek and Coptic as administrative languages alongside Arabic, and adapted Byzantine administrative and architectural forms. The Dome of the Rock, completed in Jerusalem in 691 CE under Caliph Abd al-Malik, is one of the earliest surviving examples of Islamic monumental architecture, built on a site sacred to Jews and Christians and using artistic forms derived from Byzantine and Sasanian models while asserting a distinctly Islamic theological message.

Abd al-Malik undertook a major program of Arabization during the late seventh century CE. Arabic replaced Greek, Coptic, and Persian as the language of administration across the empire. Arabic-language coinage replaced the Byzantine and Sasanian coins that had been in use in the conquered territories. This Arabization served both administrative and ideological purposes: it created a more unified bureaucratic culture and asserted the distinctly Arab-Islamic character of the empire. However, it also created tensions with the non-Arab Muslim populations — the mawali — who had converted to Islam but were often treated as second-class subjects, denied the full social privileges that Arab Muslims enjoyed. This ethnic discrimination would become one of the primary grievances that fueled opposition to Umayyad rule.

Religious life under the Umayyads was complex. The developing community of Islamic scholars (the ulama) sometimes had a tense relationship with caliphal authority. The ulama saw themselves as the guardians of authentic Islamic practice based on the Quran and the example of the Prophet, and they were not always willing to defer to political authority on religious questions. The period saw the emergence of the major schools of Islamic jurisprudence — the legal traditions that would eventually codify how Islamic law should be applied across the diverse situations of daily life. The ulama's authority over religious knowledge gave them a form of social power that was distinct from, and sometimes in tension with, the military and political power of the caliphs.

The Umayyad caliphate ended in 750 CE in a revolution that combined ethnic resentment, religious dissent, and political grievance. The Abbasid movement, which emerged in the province of Khorasan in northeastern Iran, drew its support primarily from the mawali — non-Arab converts to Islam who chafed under what they experienced as Arab ethnic privilege — as well as from various groups who rejected Umayyad religious legitimacy. The Abbasid uprising promised a return to the pure principles of Islamic governance and the end of Arab ethnic privilege. The last Umayyad caliph, Marwan II, was defeated and killed in 750 CE. One surviving Umayyad prince, Abd al-Rahman I, escaped to al-Andalus, where he established an independent Umayyad emirate that would survive until the eleventh century CE.

Section 20.3

The Abbasid Revolution and Baghdad

The Abbasid Revolution of 750 CE was more than a change of dynasty. It represented a significant reorientation of the caliphate's character, moving the center of power from the Syrian heartland to Iraq and drawing more fully on the administrative and intellectual traditions of Persia. The early Abbasid caliphs presented themselves as rulers of a more inclusive Islamic empire, one in which all Muslims — Arab and non-Arab alike — would theoretically be equal before God and the caliph. In practice, the revolution elevated Persian administrative traditions and the Iranian mawali population to greater prominence, while the Arabic language and Islamic religious principles continued to unify the empire's diverse peoples.

The founding of Baghdad in 762 CE under Caliph al-Mansur was an act of extraordinary ambition. The city was built from scratch on a flat plain between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, chosen for its fertile agricultural hinterland, its access to river transportation, and its position at the intersection of trade routes connecting Persia, Arabia, and the Mediterranean. The circular plan of the original city centered the caliph's palace and the great mosque at the heart, surrounded by markets, residential quarters, and administrative buildings, with four gates opening to the cardinal directions. The symbolism was clear: Baghdad was the center of the world and the seat of God's representative on earth.

Within a few generations, Baghdad had expanded far beyond its original circular plan. Suburbs and satellite settlements developed on both banks of the Tigris. The city attracted merchants, scholars, craftspeople, administrators, and migrants from across the empire and beyond. Population estimates for Baghdad at its height in the ninth century CE range from three hundred thousand to perhaps a million inhabitants — making it comparable in size to Tang dynasty Chang'an in China as one of the world's largest cities. Its markets were famous for the diversity of goods they offered: silk and spices from Asia, gold and slaves from Africa, furs and amber from northern Eurasia, fine glassware and metalwork from Syria and Iran.

The Abbasid caliphate at its height, in the reigns of Harun al-Rashid (786–809 CE) and al-Mamun (813–833 CE), has become legendary in Islamic cultural memory, partly through the stories of One Thousand and One Nights (Alf Layla wa-Layla), which paint a picture of a cosmopolitan city of wealth and learning. The caliphs supported poets, musicians, scholars, physicians, and philosophers. They maintained extensive libraries and sponsored the translation of texts from Greek, Syriac, Persian, and Sanskrit into Arabic. They presided over a court culture that blended Arab, Persian, and other influences into a sophisticated aesthetic of food, dress, architecture, and literary form.

The most celebrated institutional expression of this scholarly culture was the Bayt al-Hikma, or House of Wisdom, established in Baghdad under al-Mamun in the early ninth century CE. Scholars from diverse backgrounds translated, preserved, studied, and extended the scientific and philosophical knowledge of the ancient world. Greek philosophical texts — including the works of Aristotle, Plato, Galen, Ptolemy, and Euclid — were translated into Arabic, often via intermediate Syriac translations made by Christian scholars. Persian astronomical and mathematical texts were also incorporated. Indian mathematical traditions, including the decimal number system and the concept of zero, were transmitted to the Islamic world partly through these scholarly networks.

By the tenth century CE, the real power in Baghdad often lay not with the caliph but with military commanders recruited from Central Asia. When the Buyid dynasty, a Persian Shia political force, captured Baghdad in 945 CE, the caliph was effectively reduced to a religious figurehead — a role the Abbasid caliphate would maintain, in diminished form, until the Mongol conquest in 1258 CE.

Section 20.4

Caliphs, Ulama, and Islamic Law

One of the defining features of Islamic civilization in this period was the complex relationship between political authority — embodied in the caliph and later in regional rulers — and religious-legal authority, embodied in the ulama. This relationship shaped the character of governance across the Islamic world and produced a distinctive form of political culture that differed significantly from the systems of governance in China, Europe, and Byzantium.

The caliph was, in theory, both a political and a religious leader — commander of the faithful (amir al-muminin) and guardian of Islamic law and practice. In practice, however, the caliphs were not themselves religious scholars, and the increasingly complex questions of Islamic law and theology required specialists. The ulama — the learned men who devoted their lives to studying the Quran, the hadith (the reported sayings and actions of the Prophet Muhammad), and the principles of legal reasoning — emerged as a distinct and influential social class. They ran the educational institutions (madrasas), staffed the courts, issued legal opinions (fatwas), and led the Friday prayers. Their authority derived not from political appointment but from their reputation for learning and piety, giving them a degree of independence from caliphal control.

The development of Islamic law (sharia) was one of the great intellectual achievements of this period. Sharia was understood not as human legislation but as the divine will of God, which could be known through the Quran, the hadith, legal analogy (qiyas), and the consensus of scholars (ijma). By the ninth and tenth centuries CE, four main schools of Sunni Islamic jurisprudence had emerged — the Hanafi, Maliki, Shafii, and Hanbali schools — each with somewhat different approaches to legal reasoning and different geographical strongholds. Shia Islam developed its own legal traditions, centered on the authority of the imams descended from Ali and Muhammad.

When caliphs tried to intervene in theological debates or to impose particular doctrines by force, the results could be deeply problematic. The most dramatic example was the Mutazilite controversy. Under al-Mamun and his successors, the court embraced the Mutazilite theological position, which held that the Quran was created (rather than eternal) and that rational inquiry should guide theological questions. The caliph imposed this position on the ulama in a series of inquisitions known as the Mihna (833–848 CE). The most celebrated resister was the scholar Ahmad ibn Hanbal, who refused to capitulate and became a hero to those who believed in the authority of traditional Sunni hadith scholarship over rational theology. When the Mihna ended under Caliph al-Mutawakkil in 848 CE, it was seen as a victory for the ulama over caliphal interference in theological matters.

Historical Vocabulary
Ulama

The ulama (singular: alim) were the Islamic scholars — the learned men who devoted their lives to the study of the Quran, the hadith, Islamic law (sharia), theology (kalam), and related disciplines. Unlike Christian priests or Buddhist monks, the ulama were not ordained clergy in a formal hierarchical church; rather, they constituted a social class defined by their education, reputation for learning and piety, and role as interpreters of Islamic knowledge. The ulama staffed the Islamic courts (as judges, or qadis), ran the madrasas (colleges), issued legal opinions (fatwas) guiding Muslim conduct, led prayers, and transmitted Islamic learning from generation to generation. Because their authority was rooted in religious knowledge rather than political appointment, the ulama occupied a position of significant independence from caliphal authority — making them one of the most important social institutions in Islamic civilization.

Section 20.5

Trade Across Afro-Eurasia

Perhaps the most far-reaching consequence of the Islamic caliphates for world history was their role in connecting the major regions of Afro-Eurasia through trade. The Islamic world occupied a remarkable geographic position: it bridged the Mediterranean world to the west, the sub-Saharan African world to the south, the Indian Ocean world to the east, and the Central Asian steppe to the north. This position, combined with the religious and legal frameworks that facilitated trust and commerce among Muslim traders across these regions, made the Islamic world the central node of Afro-Eurasian exchange for several centuries.

The Indian Ocean trade was one of the most important commercial systems of the post-classical world, and Muslim merchants were its dominant participants from the eighth century CE onward. The monsoon wind system — the seasonal reversal of winds across the Indian Ocean — made predictable long-distance voyaging possible. Muslim merchants from the Persian Gulf sailed to the western coast of India to purchase pepper, cotton textiles, and other goods. They continued to ports in Ceylon, the Malabar Coast, and eventually to the Malay Peninsula, the Spice Islands of Southeast Asia, and the ports of southern China. The return journey brought silk, porcelain, and other Chinese goods back to the Persian Gulf, where they were redistributed across the Islamic world and beyond.

The East African coast was another critical node in the Indian Ocean trading world. Muslim merchants from the Persian Gulf and Arabia had been trading with the East African coast since at least the eighth century CE, establishing commercial relationships with the Bantu-speaking coastal communities who grew into the Swahili city-states. These merchants brought cotton cloth, ceramics, glass beads, and metal goods to East Africa; in return they took ivory, gold, slaves, ambergris, and other commodities. The trade transformed the East African coast, creating a new hybrid Swahili culture that blended Bantu African and Arab-Islamic elements. The Swahili city-states — Kilwa, Mombasa, Malindi, Sofala, and others — grew wealthy as intermediaries between the African interior and the Indian Ocean world.

The trans-Saharan trade routes were a second major commercial system connecting the Islamic world to regions beyond its political borders. The primary goods moving northward across the Sahara were gold, enslaved people, and ivory. Gold from the mines of West Africa — the Bambuk and Buré goldfields along the upper Senegal and Niger rivers — was crucial to the economies of North Africa and the Mediterranean. Enslaved African people were taken northward in large numbers to serve in households, military units, and agricultural estates across the Islamic world. Moving southward across the Sahara came salt from the great salt mines of the central Sahara, as well as copper, cloth, and manufactured goods from North Africa. The exchange of salt for gold was the economic foundation of the great West African empires — Ghana, Mali, and Songhai — that grew wealthy as the controlling powers of the trans-Saharan trade.

The Mediterranean was a third major arena of Islamic commercial activity. Muslim merchants traded with the Byzantine Empire, with Christian Europe, and with the Italian city-states that were emerging as commercial powers after the tenth century CE. The Islamic world developed sophisticated commercial instruments that facilitated long-distance trade: the mudaraba (a partnership contract in which one party provides capital and the other provides labor, with profits shared), letters of credit (suftaja) that allowed merchants to deposit money in one city and withdraw an equivalent sum in another, and commercial courts that adjudicated disputes. These innovations reduced the risk of long-distance commerce and were later absorbed into European commercial practice.

Section 20.6

Science, Medicine, Mathematics, and Philosophy

The intellectual life of the Islamic world in the eighth through thirteenth centuries CE represented one of the most creative periods in the history of human knowledge. Scholars working in Arabic — and increasingly in Persian — made fundamental advances in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, chemistry, optics, geography, and philosophy. These advances drew on earlier traditions from Greece, Persia, India, and Mesopotamia, but Islamic scholars did more than preserve earlier knowledge. They extended it, corrected it, and in many cases created genuinely new fields of inquiry.

The translation movement of the eighth and ninth centuries CE was the foundation for much of this intellectual activity. Under Abbasid patronage, and with the institutional support of the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, scholars undertook the massive project of translating the major texts of Greek science and philosophy into Arabic. The works of Aristotle, Plato, Galen, Ptolemy, Euclid, and many others were translated, often through the mediation of Syriac-speaking Christian scholars who had preserved Greek texts in their monasteries. Indian mathematical texts, including those dealing with what we call Arabic numerals and the decimal place-value system (actually of Indian origin), were also transmitted through the Islamic world.

Mathematics was perhaps the field in which the scholars of this period made the most fundamental advances. Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi, a Persian scholar working in Baghdad in the early ninth century CE, wrote a treatise that laid out the principles of what we now call algebra. The word "algebra" itself comes from al-jabr (completion), one of the operations al-Khwarizmi described. He also wrote a work explaining the Indian decimal number system and the use of the digit zero — a concept that was transformative for mathematics and commerce. The word "algorithm" is a Latinization of his name. Islamic astronomers built on Ptolemy's geocentric model while also identifying errors in it and developing new mathematical tools. The names of many stars visible in the night sky — Aldebaran, Betelgeuse, Rigel, Vega — are derived from Arabic, reflecting the lasting influence of Islamic astronomical scholarship.

Medicine was another field of major achievement. The physician and philosopher Ibn Sina, known in Europe as Avicenna, was born in 980 CE in the region of Bukhara in Central Asia. His Canon of Medicine (al-Qanun fi al-Tibb), completed around 1025 CE, was a systematic encyclopedic synthesis of Greek medicine with Islamic and Persian traditions. The Canon was translated into Latin and became one of the primary medical textbooks in European universities from the twelfth through the seventeenth centuries CE. Philosophy (falsafa) was a third major domain: scholars like al-Kindi, al-Farabi, and Ibn Rushd (Averroes) sought to reconcile Greek philosophical traditions — especially Aristotelian logic and metaphysics — with Islamic theology and revelation. Ibn Rushd's commentaries on Aristotle were so influential that in medieval European universities he was simply called "The Commentator."

Chemistry (known in Arabic as al-kimiya, from which "alchemy" derives) and optics also received sustained attention. The eighth-century scholar Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber in Latin) conducted systematic experiments with chemical substances and described methods of distillation, crystallization, and calcination. Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen, 965–1040 CE) conducted careful experiments on the behavior of light and wrote a major work on optics that challenged Greek theories and developed an early form of the scientific method based on controlled observation and experimentation. These achievements were not isolated curiosities; they formed the foundation on which later European scientific development would build.

Section 20.7

Religious Diversity and Social Life

One of the most important and often underappreciated features of life under the Islamic caliphates was the degree of religious diversity they encompassed. At the height of the Abbasid caliphate in the ninth century CE, the majority of the population living under caliphal rule was probably not Muslim. Christians, Jews, Zoroastrians, and adherents of other religious traditions formed large communities in Egypt, Syria, Iraq, Iran, and other regions. These communities had their own legal systems, religious courts, languages of worship, and educational institutions. They traded with Muslim merchants, worked alongside Muslim craftspeople in the same urban neighborhoods, and sometimes served in the administrative apparatus of the caliphate.

The dhimmi system, which governed the status of non-Muslim subjects, was a distinctive feature of Islamic governance. In exchange for the payment of the jizya (poll tax) and certain other social restrictions, non-Muslim communities were granted the right to practice their religion, govern their internal community affairs through their own religious courts, and maintain their property. The actual application of these rules varied enormously across time and place. In some periods and regions, the restrictions were enforced strictly; in others, they were largely ignored. The general pattern was one of significant — if legally unequal — pluralism rather than forced conversion.

The Jewish communities of the Islamic world flourished under Abbasid rule. Jewish merchants were active participants in the Indian Ocean and Mediterranean trade networks, as attested by the famous Cairo Geniza — a vast collection of discarded documents discovered in a Cairo synagogue that preserves an extraordinary record of everyday life, commerce, and community affairs in the medieval Islamic and Mediterranean world. Christian communities were similarly diverse and vital. The Church of the East had extensive networks across Central Asia and reached as far as China. Syriac-speaking Christian intellectuals served as physicians, administrators, and scholars in the Abbasid court, producing many of the translations from Greek into Arabic that were so important to the translation movement.

Enslaved people occupied a significant and troubling place in the social fabric of the caliphates. Enslaved people came from many regions: from sub-Saharan Africa through the trans-Saharan routes; from Central Asia and Eastern Europe through the steppe trade. The military slave system (ghulam), in which young men were purchased from Central Asia, trained as soldiers, converted to Islam, and incorporated into elite military units, became an important feature of Abbasid governance. These military slaves — the most famous of whom are the Mamluks — eventually accumulated significant political power. The Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt and Syria, founded by military slaves in 1250 CE, would go on to defeat the Mongols at the Battle of Ayn Jalut in 1260 CE.

Women's lives under the caliphates were shaped by a complex intersection of Islamic legal principles, local custom, class, and urban or rural setting. Islamic law gave women property rights, rights in marriage and divorce, and rights of inheritance that were in some respects more formally protected than those available to women in contemporary European or Chinese societies. At the same time, Islamic law as interpreted by the ulama generally confined women's public roles and gave men authority over family life. Urban elite women were often subject to forms of seclusion that rural or working-class women, who needed to participate in agricultural labor and market trade, could not afford. Some women were significant figures in scholarship and mystical (Sufi) religious practice — including Rabia al-Adawiyya of Basra (717–801 CE), celebrated as one of the founders of the Sufi devotional tradition.

Historical Vocabulary
Dhimmi

Dhimmi (from the Arabic meaning "protected person") referred to non-Muslim subjects of the Islamic caliphate who belonged to recognized monotheistic religious communities — primarily Jews, Christians, and Zoroastrians. Dhimmis were granted formal legal protection by the Islamic state: they could practice their religion, maintain their religious courts, preserve their communal institutions, and own property. In exchange, they paid the jizya (poll tax) and were subject to various social regulations that symbolically marked their subordinate status. The dhimmi system was an important feature of Islamic governance that allowed for a significant degree of religious pluralism while maintaining a clear legal hierarchy between Muslim and non-Muslim subjects. The system's actual application varied widely across time and geography; in many periods and regions, dhimmis lived and worked alongside Muslims with relatively little friction, while in others the restrictions were enforced more strictly.

Section 20.8

Regional Islamic States

The unity of the caliphate was always more political fiction than geographic reality. Even at the height of Abbasid power in the late eighth and early ninth centuries CE, regional governors exercised considerable autonomy. From the ninth century CE onward, the process of political fragmentation accelerated as regional dynasties asserted de facto independence while often acknowledging the nominal religious authority of the Abbasid caliph in Baghdad.

The Samanid dynasty, which ruled Khorasan and Central Asia from 875 to 999 CE, was a Persian Muslim dynasty that cultivated Persian language and literature while maintaining formal loyalty to the Abbasid caliphate. The Samanid court at Bukhara was a major center of Persian-language culture; the poet Rudaki and the young Ibn Sina both found patronage there. When the Samanids were displaced by the Turkic Ghaznavid dynasty in the late tenth century CE, the pattern of Turkic military power over Persian administrative and cultural traditions would become increasingly common across the eastern Islamic world.

The most significant challenge to Abbasid authority came from the Fatimid caliphate, a Shia dynasty that emerged in North Africa in 909 CE and conquered Egypt in 969 CE. The Fatimids claimed descent from Fatima, the daughter of the Prophet Muhammad and wife of Ali, and presented themselves as the legitimate leaders of the entire Muslim world. They established their capital at the newly founded city of Cairo and built the great al-Azhar mosque and university, which remains one of the most influential institutions of Islamic learning in the world to the present day. The coexistence of Abbasid and Fatimid caliphates — both claiming universal Islamic leadership — was a powerful symbol of the fragmentation of Islamic political unity.

In al-Andalus, the surviving Umayyad prince Abd al-Rahman I had established an emirate in 756 CE that eventually declared itself a caliphate in 929 CE, creating a third caliphal claim. Al-Andalus was a remarkable civilization — ethnically and religiously diverse, with a Muslim ruling class, large Christian and Jewish populations, and a culture in which Arabic, Romance languages, and Hebrew were all in use. The library of Cordoba was one of the great repositories of knowledge in the medieval world. Scholars in al-Andalus, including Ibn Rushd (Averroes) and the Jewish philosopher Maimonides, worked in dialogue with the full breadth of the Islamic intellectual tradition.

In the eastern Islamic world, the Seljuk Turks emerged as a new power in the eleventh century CE. The Seljuks were Turkic pastoralists from Central Asia who had converted to Sunni Islam. In 1055 CE, Seljuk forces entered Baghdad and replaced the Buyid rulers, presenting themselves as the champions of Sunni orthodoxy. The Seljuk period saw the institutionalization of the madrasa (Islamic college) as the primary institution of higher Islamic education — a development promoted by the great Seljuk vizier Nizam al-Mulk as a means of training capable administrators and producing a corps of Sunni religious scholars.

Section 20.9

The Islamic World as a Global Connector

Considered from the perspective of world history, the most important function of the Islamic caliphates was as a connector of the major regions of Afro-Eurasia. This connector role operated on multiple levels — commercial, intellectual, religious, and biological — and its effects were felt not only within the political boundaries of the caliphate but across a vast belt of territory stretching from sub-Saharan Africa to East Asia.

Commercially, Muslim merchants were active in every major trading zone of the known world: the Indian Ocean, East Africa, the Silk Roads of Central Asia, the Mediterranean, and the trans-Saharan routes to West Africa. The shared framework of Islamic law, Arabic scholarly language, and commercial practice created a trans-regional space within which merchants, scholars, and officials could operate across vast distances. The density of commercial connections that facilitated the movement of goods and people also facilitated the movement of ideas, religions, and unfortunately, pathogens.

Intellectually, the Islamic world was a transformer as well as a transmitter. When European scholars began translating Arabic texts into Latin in the twelfth century CE, they were not simply recovering Greek knowledge. They were inheriting a transformed and extended version of that knowledge, enriched by centuries of Islamic scholarship. Figures like Gerard of Cremona, who worked in Toledo and translated dozens of Arabic scientific texts into Latin, were conduits through which the achievements of Islamic science entered European intellectual life.

Religiously, Islam itself was one of the most important cultural exports of the Islamic world. Merchants carried Islam to the coasts of East Africa, to the ports of South and Southeast Asia, and along the trade routes of Central Asia. Sufi missionaries — mystics who emphasized the inner spiritual dimensions of Islam, often adapting to local cultural forms — were particularly effective in spreading the faith across diverse cultural contexts. In South Asia, Sufi orders were major agents of Islamic expansion. In Southeast Asia, Muslim merchants and scholars spread Islam to the ports of the Malay Peninsula and the Indonesian islands from the thirteenth century onward. By the fifteenth century CE, Islam was the religion of the major commercial cities of the region.

The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 CE was a catastrophic blow to the Abbasid caliphate and a profound trauma for Islamic civilization. Hundreds of thousands of people were killed, the caliph was executed, and the libraries and institutions of the great city were destroyed. Yet Islamic civilization proved resilient. The Mamluks of Egypt defeated the Mongols at Ayn Jalut in 1260 CE and preserved Islamic governance in the Levant and Egypt. The Mongols of Iran converted to Islam within a generation. Islam continued to spread through merchants and missionaries to new regions in Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. The heritage of the caliphates — their mathematical and scientific achievements, their commercial innovations, their architectural splendors, their legal and scholarly traditions — endured and continued to shape the world far beyond the political demise of the Abbasid dynasty.

Regional Lens

Al-Andalus — Islamic Spain

Few regions better illustrate the multicultural complexity of the Islamic world than al-Andalus — the territories of the Iberian Peninsula under Muslim governance from 711 to 1492 CE. When the Umayyad general Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed the Strait of Gibraltar in 711 CE (the name "Gibraltar" itself derives from the Arabic for "Mount of Tariq" — Jabal al-Tariq), he found a Visigothic kingdom weakened by internal conflict. The Muslim conquest of most of the peninsula proceeded rapidly.

Al-Andalus was from its inception a multi-ethnic and multi-religious society. Its Muslim population included Arab leaders, Berber soldiers, and over time local converts to Islam. Its non-Muslim population included the largest Jewish community in the early medieval world — the Sephardic Jewish tradition — as well as the majority population of Christians known as Mozarabs, who maintained their faith and liturgical traditions while often adopting Arabic as their everyday language. The city of Cordoba at its tenth-century peak had a population of perhaps three to four hundred thousand — probably the largest city in western Europe at the time.

Al-Andalus was a crucible of intellectual exchange. The twelfth-century translation movement centered on Toledo — a city reconquered by Christian forces in 1085 CE but retaining its Muslim and Jewish scholarly communities — was one of the primary channels through which the achievements of Arabic science and philosophy entered European intellectual life. Scholars like Gerard of Cremona translated dozens of Arabic texts into Latin, giving medieval European universities access to Aristotle's complete works, Avicenna's Canon of Medicine, al-Khwarizmi's algebra, and many other works. The philosopher and physician Ibn Rushd (Averroes) and the Jewish philosopher Maimonides were both products of this Andalusian intellectual world.

Al-Andalus gradually shrank through the Reconquista — the long process by which Christian kingdoms expanded southward, culminating in the fall of Granada in 1492 CE. The end of Islamic rule in Spain was accompanied by the expulsion of Jews (the Alhambra Decree of 1492) and later the forced conversion or expulsion of Muslims (Moriscos). What was lost with al-Andalus was one of the most productive sites of cultural exchange in medieval history.

Global Connection

The Indian Ocean Trading World and the Islamic World

The Indian Ocean trade system was one of the most significant commercial networks in world history, connecting East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and China in a web of commercial exchange sustained by the predictable monsoon winds. Muslim merchants were the central participants in this system from the eighth century CE onward, and their involvement transformed both the economies and the cultures of the regions they connected.

A merchant sailing from the Persian Gulf to India could ride the southwest monsoon in summer and return on the northeast monsoon in winter, completing a round trip in approximately a year. This predictability, combined with the large carrying capacity of the dhow-style ships used by Arab and Persian merchants, made the Indian Ocean one of the busiest commercial zones in the premodern world. The importance of this trade is attested by the survival of significant communities of Muslim merchants in Chinese coastal cities like Guangzhou (Canton) and Quanzhou, where they were given permission to settle and govern their own community affairs.

On the East African coast, the Indian Ocean trade produced the Swahili civilization — a genuinely new cultural synthesis blending Bantu African and Arab-Islamic elements. The Swahili city-states grew wealthy as intermediaries between the African interior and the Indian Ocean trade. Kilwa in particular, which controlled the export of gold from the great Zimbabwe plateau in the interior, became one of the wealthiest cities in the region; the Moroccan traveler Ibn Battuta visited Kilwa in 1331 CE and described it as one of the most beautiful cities in the world. The archaeology of these cities reveals impressive stone mosques, palaces, and warehouses, and rich assemblages of Chinese porcelain, Indian cotton textiles, and locally produced pottery.

Muslim merchants settling in trading ports across South and Southeast Asia became agents of Islamic cultural and religious diffusion. By the fifteenth century CE, the major commercial cities of Southeast Asia — Malacca, Demak, Aceh — were Muslim, and Islam was spreading into the interior regions through commercial and political channels, transforming the religious landscape of an entire region without military conquest by the Islamic heartland.

Network Study

The Silk Roads and the Islamic World

The Silk Roads — the overland and maritime routes connecting China to the Mediterranean through Central Asia and the Middle East — had existed for centuries before the rise of Islam, but the emergence of the Islamic caliphates transformed their commercial and cultural character. The conquest of Iran, Iraq, and Central Asia brought the heartland of the Silk Roads under Islamic governance, and the large unified market of the caliphate reduced transaction costs for merchants traveling along these routes.

The main overland routes passed through Central Asia — through the oasis cities of Merv, Samarkand, and Bukhara — before branching into routes leading to China via the Tarim Basin or to the Indian subcontinent via Afghanistan. These cities became major centers of Islamic civilization under the Abbasid and later Samanid dynasties. The scholar al-Bukhari, who compiled one of the most authoritative collections of hadith, was born in Bukhara. Ibn Sina was born in the region and received his early education there.

The goods that traveled the Silk Roads were diverse: silk and porcelain from China, cotton textiles and pepper from South Asia, glassware and metalwork from the Middle East, horses and furs from Central Asia. Along these routes also traveled ideas, religious traditions, and technologies. Buddhism spread from India to Central Asia and then to China partly along these routes. The technology of papermaking moved from China to the Islamic world and eventually to Europe. Printing techniques, musical instruments, artistic motifs, architectural forms, and agricultural crops all crossed the Silk Roads.

Muslim merchants participated in the Silk Road trade through the mudaraba partnership and letters of credit (suftaja) that allowed merchants to deposit money in one city and withdraw an equivalent sum in another. These commercial innovations, later absorbed into European practice (the Italian commenda partnership is essentially the mudaraba), were important contributions of Islamic commercial culture to the development of international trade.

The Islamic World from Arabia to Central Asia

To understand the geography of the Islamic caliphates, students should examine a map showing the Arabian Peninsula and the territories conquered and governed between 632 and 850 CE. Several geographic features are essential.

The Arabian Peninsula is a vast, mostly arid landmass surrounded by the Red Sea to the west, the Persian Gulf to the east, and the Indian Ocean to the south. Its western coastal strip (the Hijaz) was the heartland of early Islam; Mecca and Medina lie on this strip. The Fertile Crescent — the arc of agriculturally productive land running through modern Iraq, Syria, Jordan, and Israel-Palestine — was the economic heartland of the early caliphates. The Tigris and Euphrates rivers provided water for irrigation agriculture; the cities of this region (Damascus, Jerusalem, Ctesiphon, Basra, Kufa) were among the most populous in the world. Iran brought into the Islamic world a region of high plateau, mountain ranges, and important oasis cities. The Zagros mountains divide Iraq from Iran.

North Africa extended the Islamic world westward along the Mediterranean coast and eventually onto the Iberian Peninsula. The Sahara Desert's northern edge formed the southern boundary of the North African Islamic cities; south of it lay the trans-Saharan trade routes to sub-Saharan Africa. Central Asia — the region beyond the Oxus (Amu Darya) River — was home to the great Silk Road cities of Samarkand, Bukhara, and Merv, and the homeland of the Turkic peoples who would increasingly shape Islamic history from the ninth century onward.

Primary Source Window

The Travels of Ibn Battuta in the Islamic World (1325–1354 CE)

Creator: Muhammad ibn Abdallah ibn Battuta · Date: dictated c. 1355 CE · Type: Travel account (rihla); dictated narrative for a royal audience

Context: Muhammad ibn Abdallah ibn Battuta was a Moroccan scholar and jurist born in Tangier in 1304 CE. Between 1325 and 1354 CE, he undertook an extraordinary series of journeys that took him across much of the known world — through North Africa, the Middle East, East Africa, the Indian Ocean, Central Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and China. On his return, he dictated an account of his travels (the Rihla, or "Journey") to a literary collaborator at the command of the Sultan of Morocco. The Rihla is one of the most important historical sources for the fourteenth-century Islamic world.

In his account of Kilwa on the East African coast, visited around 1331 CE, Ibn Battuta described a city inhabited by Muslims of Zanj (Bantu-African) origin. He praised the city's mosque, its market, its ships that sailed to the coast of Sofala to bring back gold, and the generosity of its sultan — comparing him favorably to other rulers he had encountered on his extensive travels. He noted the mixed Arab-African character of the city and the active trade connecting it to the Indian Ocean world.

Analysis: The Rihla demonstrates the reality of the Islamic world as a connected space across which scholars, merchants, and officials could travel with relative ease, relying on a shared religious framework, shared legal traditions, and networks of Muslim hospitality. Ibn Battuta traveled as a qadi (judge) and scholar and could find employment and accommodation in Muslim communities from Morocco to China. His account reveals the geographic extent of the Indian Ocean Islamic trading world, documenting Muslim communities in East Africa that had been created through commercial and religious networks rather than military conquest.

The Rihla also has important limits: it was dictated from memory years after many events it describes, shaped by the conventions of the travel-account genre and written for a Moroccan court audience. His account of China has been questioned by historians who note that some details seem borrowed from earlier geographical writings rather than from personal observation. Non-Muslim perspectives on the societies he visited are absent.

  2. Why might a travel account like the Rihla be a valuable source for historians, and what are its limitations compared to other types of historical evidence?
  3. What can we learn from Ibn Battuta's description of Kilwa about the relationship between the East African coast and the larger Indian Ocean Islamic world?
  4. Ibn Battuta frequently evaluated rulers by their piety and generosity toward scholars. What does this tell us about the values and social expectations of the Islamic scholarly community of his time?
Evidence Window

The Cairo Geniza

Among the most remarkable archaeological discoveries of the modern era was the contents of the geniza — a storage room for discarded religious writings — in the Ben Ezra synagogue in Fustat, Old Cairo, Egypt. Jewish law prohibits the destruction of documents containing the name of God, so worn-out religious texts and related documents were deposited in geniza rooms. The Cairo Geniza was never emptied; documents accumulated over roughly a thousand years, and when opened to scholars in the late nineteenth century, it contained approximately three hundred thousand documents in Hebrew, Judeo-Arabic, Aramaic, and other languages.

What do these documents reveal? They preserve an extraordinary window into the everyday life of the Jewish merchant community of the medieval Islamic Mediterranean world. Thousands of letters between merchants document the conduct of trade across the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean — prices of goods, complaints about business partners, descriptions of shipwrecks and piracy, the granting of commercial credit, and the organization of trade partnerships. Family letters reveal the texture of domestic life: marriages, divorces, illness, the cost of living, disputes between relatives. Legal documents record property transactions and court judgments.

Scholars like S. D. Goitein, who spent decades analyzing the Geniza documents, used them to reconstruct medieval Mediterranean society in extraordinary detail — showing how merchants integrated themselves into the commercial networks of the Islamic world, how Jewish and Muslim merchants cooperated despite their different religions, and how the Indian Ocean trade worked at the level of individual commercial partnerships. The Geniza documents are a reminder that the history of the Islamic world is not only the history of Muslims; it is also the history of the diverse communities that lived within and interacted with Islamic civilization.

Limits of the evidence: The geniza documents reflect primarily the perspective of a literate, commercially active, urban Jewish community. They tell us relatively little about the lives of rural populations, enslaved people, women outside the merchant class, or the non-Jewish majority surrounding the Jewish community. They are, nonetheless, among the richest sources of everyday social and commercial history from the medieval Islamic world.

Historical Debate

Was the Islamic World's Scientific Achievement a Preservation or a Transformation?

The question: How should historians characterize the intellectual achievements of scholars working in Arabic in the eighth through thirteenth centuries CE? Were they primarily preservers and transmitters of earlier Greek, Persian, and Indian knowledge, or were they creative contributors who substantially transformed and advanced human knowledge?

Interpretation A — The Preservation Argument: Some historians have emphasized the role of the Islamic world as a conduit — arguing that its primary contribution was to preserve the Greek philosophical and scientific tradition that had been lost in western Europe after the fall of the Roman Empire, and to transmit it to medieval European scholars through the translation movements of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries CE. On this view, the ultimate significance of Islamic scholars lay in passing on a Greek heritage rather than creating something genuinely new.
Interpretation B — The Transformation and Innovation Argument: Other historians argue that this preservation narrative dramatically understates the creativity and originality of Islamic scholars. Al-Khwarizmi's algebra was not Greek in origin — no Greek mathematician had developed anything comparable. Islamic astronomers systematically identified and corrected errors in Ptolemy's models, developing new mathematical tools in the process. Ibn al-Haytham's work on optics departed fundamentally from Greek optics and developed methods of controlled experimentation that anticipate modern scientific practice. On this view, the Islamic world was not merely a library for the Greeks but an active and creative center of intellectual innovation.

Evidence: The evidence for Interpretation B is strong. Al-Khwarizmi's algebra drew on Indian mathematical traditions and developed genuinely new procedures. Astronomers like al-Battani made observations and calculations that demonstrably advanced on Ptolemy. Ibn al-Haytham's experimental approach to optics has no real precedent in the Greek tradition. At the same time, Islamic scholars themselves understood their work as building on and perfecting earlier traditions and deeply respected the Greek philosophical heritage.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how we understand the relationship between different civilizations in the history of knowledge. If Islamic scholars were primarily preservers, their contribution can be seen as essentially derivative. If they were innovators, then the history of science must assign them a central creative role — and the standard Western narrative of scientific progress, which tends to jump from ancient Greece to the European Renaissance, must be substantially revised.

Comparison Across Societies

The Abbasid Caliphate and Tang/Song China

The Abbasid caliphate (750–1258 CE) and the Tang (618–907 CE) and Song (960–1279 CE) dynasties of China were the two largest, wealthiest, and most culturally productive civilizations of the post-classical world. Comparing them illuminates the diverse paths through which complex societies could achieve remarkable levels of sophistication and connection.

Geography and Size: Both empires were vast, governing populations of tens of millions across geographically diverse territories. Baghdad and Chang'an (the Tang capital) were the two largest cities in the world in the ninth century CE, each with populations that may have exceeded one million.

Political Structure: The Abbasid caliphate was theoretically a theocracy in which the caliph governed as God's representative on earth, with authority derived from the Quran and Islamic law. In practice, political power became increasingly fragmented. Tang and Song China maintained a more centralized bureaucratic state based on Confucian principles and a civil service examination system. The Chinese state was generally more effectively centralized than the Abbasid caliphate at comparable periods of its history.

Religious Pluralism: Both civilizations governed diverse religious populations, but in different ways. The Abbasid caliphate governed through the dhimmi system, which formally recognized and legally protected non-Muslim communities while maintaining their subordinate status. Tang China practiced a more fluid form of religious coexistence, with Buddhism, Daoism, Confucianism, Nestorian Christianity, and Manichaeanism all present at court and in society, without a systematic legal framework equivalent to the dhimmi system.

Scholarship and Knowledge: The Abbasid world excelled particularly in mathematics, medicine, astronomy, philosophy, and chemistry, drawing on Greek, Persian, and Indian traditions. Tang and Song China excelled in technology — developing movable type printing, gunpowder, and sophisticated maritime navigation — as well as in poetry, painting, ceramics, and administrative theory. Chinese scholarship was more deeply integrated with state governance through the examination system; Islamic scholarship was more independent of the state, rooted in the ulama's autonomous authority.

Similarities: Both civilizations governed through sophisticated bureaucracies with high rates of literacy among the administrative class. Both were centers of urban life with large, diverse cities. Both were culturally inclusive enough to absorb and use the talents of non-dominant ethnic and religious groups. And both were eventually confronted by the Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century — the Abbasid caliphate was destroyed in 1258 CE, while the Song dynasty fell to the Mongols in 1279 CE.

Multiple Perspectives

The Expansion of the Islamic Caliphate

The rapid expansion of Islamic rule in the seventh and eighth centuries CE was experienced very differently by different groups, and understanding those different experiences is essential to historical accuracy.

Arab Muslim Warriors: For the Arab soldiers who carried the first conquests, the expansion of Islamic rule was simultaneously a religious duty, a form of community solidarity, and an economic opportunity. The distribution of conquered lands and tribute through the diwan system gave successful warriors a stake in the ongoing success of the caliphate. The belief that they were carrying out God's will gave the campaigns a sense of sacred purpose.
Non-Arab Muslim Converts (Mawali): Converts to Islam from conquered populations — Persians, Egyptians, Syrians, Berbers — often found that conversion did not immediately grant them equal status with Arab Muslims. The Umayyad period was marked by clear ethnic hierarchy. The resentment of the mawali was a major fuel for the Abbasid Revolution, which promised (imperfectly) to end Arab ethnic privilege within the Muslim community.
Non-Muslim Dhimmis: The experience of dhimmis under Islamic rule varied enormously by region, period, and community. For some Christian communities who had faced theological persecution under Byzantine rule, the early Islamic period brought relative religious freedom. For Zoroastrian communities in Persia, the experience was one of gradual marginalization. Jewish communities in many regions actually flourished under early Islamic rule, as attested by the evidence of the Cairo Geniza.
Women in Islamic Society: Islamic law gave women formal property rights and rights in marriage that were in some respects superior to those in earlier Arabian custom or in contemporary Byzantine law. However, the legal framework also placed women under the formal guardianship of male relatives and gave men authority over family decisions. Urban elite women were increasingly subject to seclusion; rural women continued to work in agriculture and markets. Some women mystics and scholars became significant figures in Islamic intellectual and spiritual life.
Enslaved People: The caliphate's expansion was accompanied by a significant increase in the trade and use of enslaved people from Africa, Central Asia, and other regions. Islamic law placed certain constraints on the treatment of enslaved people — including rights to purchase freedom and certain protections against mistreatment — but fundamentally accepted the institution of slavery as legal. The perspective of enslaved people themselves is almost entirely absent from the surviving Arabic historical record; their experiences must be reconstructed indirectly. The Zanj Rebellion (869–883 CE), a mass revolt of enslaved African workers in southern Iraq, provided one dramatic expression of enslaved people's resistance to their condition.
Cause and Effect

The Abbasid Revolution of 750 CE

Long-Term Causes

The ethnic inequality of the Umayyad system, which privileged Arab Muslims over non-Arab converts (mawali). Persian converts particularly resented their lower status despite equal or superior religious piety and learning. Umayyad fiscal policies, perceived impiety, and brutal suppression of revolts — including the revolt of al-Husayn at Karbala in 680 CE — built sustained resentment across the empire.

Short-Term Causes

Organization of an effective revolutionary movement in the province of Khorasan in northeastern Iran. The Abbasid family worked through networks of preachers and agents to build a coalition of mawali, Yemeni Arabs, and others alienated from the Umayyad regime. The movement promised a return to the principles of original Islamic governance and the end of Arab ethnic privilege.

The Event

Abbasid forces defeat Marwan II at the Battle of the Zab in Iraq in 750 CE. Marwan is killed shortly afterward in Egypt. Abu al-Abbas al-Saffah is proclaimed the first Abbasid caliph. Most of the Umayyad family is killed; one survivor flees to al-Andalus.

Effects

Capital shifts from Damascus to Iraq; Baghdad founded in 762 CE. Persian administrative traditions and the mawali population gain prominence. The translation movement and House of Wisdom flourish. Political fragmentation accelerates in later decades as the revolution demonstrates that caliphal legitimacy could be challenged and overthrown.

Historical complexity: The Abbasid Revolution cannot be reduced to a simple ethnic uprising or a religious reformation. It involved multiple groups with different grievances and different visions of what Islamic governance should look like. It promised more than it delivered: while the formal ethnic hierarchy of the Umayyad period was dismantled, new hierarchies based on class, lineage, and connection to the caliphal court soon developed.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Islam and Non-Muslim Communities Under the Caliphates

What Changed

  • Political and administrative context transformed as Byzantine and Sasanian rule gave way to Islamic governance
  • Non-Chalcedonian Christian churches (Monophysites, Nestorians) gained greater religious freedom from Byzantine theological persecution
  • Zoroastrian communities in Iran found their faith increasingly marginalized as Islam became dominant
  • Jewish communities gained access to a vast unified market facilitating long-distance trade
  • Through the eighth through tenth centuries CE, the proportion of the population that was Muslim grew steadily, transforming the demographic character of formerly Byzantine and Sasanian regions

What Stayed the Same

  • Local cultural, social, and institutional patterns persisted beneath the changed political order — Coptic Christians maintained their agricultural communities, monasteries, and liturgical language for centuries
  • Jewish academies at Sura and Pumbedita in Iraq continued producing major works of rabbinic scholarship for centuries after the Islamic conquest
  • Rural agricultural populations often experienced the least immediate disruption — they continued to farm the same land and practice their religion in their village communities
  • Deep patterns of family life, agricultural technique, and local religious practice were largely continuous

Why the pattern matters: It illustrates that major political transformations — even those as dramatic as the Islamic conquests — do not simply erase existing social and cultural patterns. The Islamic world was built on top of existing civilizations, and it drew extensively on their administrative traditions, architectural forms, scholarly resources, and human capital. Understanding this continuity helps students see the Islamic caliphates not as a rupture from the past but as a transformation that incorporated and reworked much of what came before.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Market in Baghdad

To understand the Islamic caliphates not only through the lens of rulers and scholars but through the experience of ordinary people, consider the world of a craftsperson and trader in Baghdad in the ninth century CE.

The city was organized around its markets (suq or bazaar), which were grouped by trade: the booksellers near the main mosque, the cloth merchants in their section, the coppersmiths and ironworkers in theirs, the spice sellers and perfumers nearby. The variety of goods available in Baghdad's markets reflected the city's position at the center of Afro-Eurasian trade. A shopper might find silk cloth from China, pepper and spices from the Malabar Coast of India, dates and dried fruits from the Iraqi countryside, fine steel knives from Central Asia, papyrus and later paper from Egypt, glassware from Syria, and locally produced pottery, leather goods, and bread.

Work was organized around the rhythms of the five daily prayers, which punctuated the working day. Friday was the day of congregational prayer; the shops around the mosque would be closed during the time of the Friday sermon. The market was also a social space — a place for meeting neighbors, hearing news, negotiating prices, and maintaining the web of social relationships that sustained commercial life.

The cities of the Islamic world had significant charitable infrastructure. Islamic law emphasized the obligation of zakat — charitable giving, one of the five pillars of Islam — and wealthy individuals and the caliphal court endowed pious foundations (waqf) that supported mosques, schools, hospitals, and public fountains. The bimaristan (hospital) was an institution developed in the Abbasid world that offered free or subsidized medical care to patients regardless of religion; the great bimaristan of Baghdad founded by Harun al-Rashid in 805 CE employed physicians from various backgrounds. These hospitals were also institutions of medical education, where physicians trained with patients under the supervision of senior doctors — a model that would eventually influence European medical education.

Literacy in the cities of the Islamic world was relatively high by premodern standards, particularly among men. The spread of paper from the eighth century onward made books and documents more accessible. Public readings from the Quran and from popular literature took place in mosques and markets. The tales that would eventually be compiled as One Thousand and One Nights — stories of merchants, genies, clever women, foolish rulers, and enchanted cities — circulated in oral and written forms through these same urban spaces, reflecting and entertaining the diverse populations of the Islamic city.

Chapter Summary

The Islamic caliphates stand as one of the most consequential political and cultural developments in post-classical world history. Beginning with the prophet Muhammad's revelations in seventh-century Arabia and the subsequent rapid military expansion of the Arab-Muslim community, the caliphates created a political order that within a century encompassed territories from the Iberian Peninsula to Central Asia. This expansion was made possible by the military mobilization of Arabian society under a new religious ideology, the political exhaustion of the Byzantine and Sasanian empires, and the relative tolerance and administrative pragmatism of early Islamic governance.

The Umayyad caliphate (661–750 CE) consolidated the first phase of Islamic imperial rule from its capital at Damascus, extending Muslim control westward into North Africa and al-Andalus and eastward into Central Asia and Sind. The Umayyad period was characterized by the Arabization of administration and coinage, the development of the dhimmi system for governing non-Muslim communities, and the emergence of serious tensions between Arab Muslims and non-Arab converts (mawali) who sought equality within the community of believers.

The Abbasid Revolution of 750 CE transformed the caliphate, shifting its center of gravity eastward to Iraq and bringing Persian administrative traditions and the mawali population to greater prominence. The founding of Baghdad in 762 CE created one of the world's great cities — a cosmopolitan metropolis of merchants, scholars, craftspeople, and officials drawn from across the Islamic world and beyond. The translation movement of the eighth and ninth centuries CE preserved Greek, Persian, and Indian texts and extended them in ways that produced genuinely new knowledge in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, chemistry, optics, and philosophy.

Trade was perhaps the most consequential practical function of the Islamic world in world history. Muslim merchants connected the Indian Ocean, the Silk Roads, the trans-Saharan routes, and the Mediterranean into a single commercial zone of unprecedented scale. The Swahili city-states of East Africa, the emerging Islamic trading cities of Southeast Asia, and the gold empires of West Africa all owed part of their development to the commercial networks centered on the Islamic world.

Religious and social life within the caliphates was diverse and contested. The dhimmi system provided legal protection for non-Muslim communities while maintaining their subordinate status. The ulama's authority as interpreters of Islamic law created a form of religious scholarship that was relatively independent of political power. The Sunni-Shia divide, rooted in the dispute over succession after Muhammad's death, produced lasting theological and political divisions. Women, enslaved people, rural peasants, and urban craftspeople all experienced the caliphates differently from the scholars and merchants whose voices dominate the surviving sources.

The fragmentation of the caliphate into regional Islamic states from the ninth century onward did not diminish the vitality of Islamic civilization. The Fatimid caliphate in Egypt and the Umayyad caliphate in al-Andalus were themselves major centers of cultural production. The Seljuk Turks who became the dominant military power in the eastern Islamic world in the eleventh century CE promoted Sunni Islamic orthodoxy and institutionalized the madrasa system of higher education. Regional courts across the Islamic world competed in patronizing poets, architects, physicians, and scholars.

The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 CE was a catastrophic blow and a profound trauma. Yet Islamic civilization proved resilient. The Mamluks of Egypt defeated the Mongols at Ayn Jalut in 1260 CE. The Mongols of Iran converted to Islam within a generation. And Islam continued to spread through merchants and missionaries to new regions in Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia — demonstrating that the faith had developed its own self-sustaining momentum independent of any particular caliphal power structure.

The heritage of the Islamic caliphates is vast and multifaceted. It includes the mathematical and scientific achievements of scholars whose Arabic names are embedded in our everyday vocabulary: algebra, algorithm, alkali, alcohol. It includes the commercial and navigational innovations that made the Indian Ocean world one of the most active commercial zones on earth. It includes the architectural achievements of mosques, palaces, and cities that remain among the most beautiful built environments in the world. And it includes the legal and scholarly traditions of the ulama that have guided Muslim communities across fifteen centuries. For students of world history, the Islamic caliphates provide an essential case study in the formation and governance of a vast, diverse empire; in the transmission and development of knowledge across cultural boundaries; in the commercial networks that connected distant regions; and in the complex relationship between religious authority and political power.

Sunni
The majority branch of Islam, accepting the authority of the first four caliphs and the community consensus in matters of religious governance.
Shia
The branch of Islam holding that legitimate religious and political leadership after Muhammad passed through Ali and his descendants; rooted in the dispute over succession following Muhammad's death.
Dhimmi
Non-Muslim subject of an Islamic state granted legal protection in exchange for payment of the jizya tax and acceptance of certain social restrictions.
Jizya
The poll tax paid by dhimmi communities in the Islamic caliphates in exchange for religious protection and tolerance.
Mawali
Non-Arab converts to Islam, particularly in the Umayyad period; often subject to social discrimination despite their Muslim faith; their resentment fueled the Abbasid Revolution.

The period of the first four caliphs (632–661 CE): Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali; regarded in Sunni tradition as a golden age of Islamic governance.

The dynasty that ruled the Islamic caliphate from Damascus from 661 to 750 CE; characterized by Arabization of administration and ethnic hierarchy within the Muslim community.
Abbasid Caliphate
The dynasty that overthrew the Umayyads in 750 CE and ruled from Baghdad until the Mongol sack of 1258 CE; associated with the golden age of Islamic scholarship.
Abbasid Revolution
The uprising of 750 CE, centered in Khorasan, that overthrew the Umayyad dynasty and established the Abbasid caliphate with its more Persian-influenced character.
House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma)
The major scholarly and translation institution established in Baghdad under Caliph al-Mamun in the early ninth century CE; center of the translation movement.

The organized effort in the early Abbasid period to translate Greek, Persian, and Indian scientific and philosophical texts into Arabic; foundational for Islamic intellectual achievement.

A branch of mathematics developed significantly by Islamic scholars, particularly al-Khwarizmi; the word comes from the Arabic al-jabr, one of the operations he described.
Vizier
The chief minister and senior administrative official of the caliph; the effective head of the governmental bureaucracy.

An Islamic institution of higher learning; the primary setting for the education of the ulama and Islamic scholars; institutionalized especially during the Seljuk period.

The Shia dynasty that ruled North Africa and Egypt from 909 to 1171 CE, claiming descent from the Prophet's daughter Fatima; a rival to Abbasid authority; founded Cairo and al-Azhar.

The territories of the Iberian Peninsula under Islamic governance from 711 to 1492 CE; a major center of learning and cultural exchange in the medieval world.

A pious endowment in Islamic law; property donated for charitable or religious purposes (mosques, schools, hospitals) whose income is perpetually dedicated to the endowment's stated purpose.
Mudaraba
A commercial partnership contract in Islamic law in which one partner provides capital and the other provides labor; profits are shared according to agreement, with capital losses borne by the investor.
Sufism
The mystical tradition within Islam emphasizing direct spiritual experience, love of God, and the inner dimensions of religious practice; Sufi missionaries were major agents of Islamic expansion.

The "house of Islam"; the territories under Muslim governance and Islamic law; a concept expressing the unity of the Muslim political and religious community.
Bimaristan
Hospital in the Islamic world offering medical care to patients regardless of religion; also institutions of medical education; more sophisticated than any contemporary European equivalent.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 20 has examined the Islamic caliphates not as an isolated phenomenon but as a civilization deeply embedded in the larger patterns of post-classical world history. The rapid expansion of Islam from Arabia in the seventh century CE, the consolidation of the Umayyad and Abbasid caliphates, the intellectual flowering of Baghdad, and the commercial networks that linked the Islamic world to Africa, Asia, and Europe — all of these developments were shaped by and in turn shaped the world they inhabited. The chapter matters because it forces us to place the Islamic world at the center of the story of post-classical civilization, not at the margins.

The Islamic caliphates connect directly to the larger themes of this textbook. The chapter's discussion of trade and commerce links to the textbook's recurring examination of how exchange networks connected distant societies — the same theme explored in relation to the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean, and the trans-Saharan routes. The treatment of scholarship and the translation movement links to the theme of how knowledge travels across cultural boundaries and is transformed in the process. The discussion of governance and religious diversity links to the theme of how empires managed the challenge of ruling populations of different ethnicities, languages, and faiths. And the examination of the experiences of ordinary people — merchants, craftspeople, scholars, enslaved people, women — links to the textbook's commitment to social history alongside political and intellectual history.

Looking forward from this chapter, the Islamic world's role as a connector of Afro-Eurasia would continue and intensify in the following centuries. The Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century CE disrupted the Abbasid caliphate but ultimately expanded the zones of commercial and cultural contact — the Mongol Empire, once established, created new opportunities for long-distance exchange and travel that we will examine in Chapter 28. The spread of Islam into South and Southeast Asia through merchants and Sufi missionaries laid the groundwork for one of the world's largest Muslim populations today. The intellectual achievements of Islamic scholars, transmitted to medieval Europe through the translation movement, contributed to the conditions that made possible the European Renaissance and the Scientific Revolution.

Finally, this chapter serves as a reminder that the world-history narrative is not a story of Western progress interrupted by occasional contributions from other civilizations. In the eighth through thirteenth centuries CE, the Islamic world was the most sophisticated, most commercially connected, and in many fields the most intellectually productive civilization on earth. Understanding this fact — not as a curiosity but as a central element of the historical record — is essential to understanding both the post-classical world and the world that emerged from it. The students of the MGU History Library who engage seriously with this chapter will be better equipped to understand the diverse sources of our shared human heritage and the complex, interconnected story of how the modern world came to be.

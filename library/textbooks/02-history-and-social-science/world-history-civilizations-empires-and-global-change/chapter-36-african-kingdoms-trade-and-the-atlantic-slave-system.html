Part VII — The Early Modern World, 1450–1750

African Kingdoms, Trade, and the Atlantic Slave System

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"Each day the traders are kidnapping our people — children of this country, sons of our nobles and vassals, even people of our own family."
— Afonso I, Manikongo of Kongo, letter to the King of Portugal, 1526

In the early sixteenth century, the Kongolese king Afonso I — known in his own language as Manikongo — sat in his capital at Mbanza Kongo and composed a remarkable series of letters to the king of Portugal. These letters, written in precise diplomatic Portuguese and preserved in European archives, reveal a sophisticated ruler at the center of a wealthy, well-organized Central African kingdom. Afonso had converted to Christianity, educated his son in Lisbon, and maintained careful diplomatic relations with the Portuguese court. He traded cloth, copper, ivory, and enslaved captives in exchange for European goods and Christian clergy. He wanted missionaries and teachers. He did not want — and wrote repeatedly to say so — the uncontrolled enslavement of his own people and subjects. His letters went largely unanswered. The Atlantic slave trade was already transforming his kingdom, and it would, over the following centuries, devastate it.

This chapter examines Africa in the early modern era — roughly from 1450 to 1800 — as a continent of powerful states, dense trade networks, complex cultures, and active historical agency. It is a history that has too often been misunderstood, reduced, or ignored in world history surveys that treat Africa mainly as a source of enslaved labor for European plantations. That story is part of this chapter, but it is only one part. West and Central Africa were home to some of the most sophisticated political systems of the early modern world. The kingdoms of Kongo, Benin, Oyo, and Asante administered large territories, maintained professional armies, fostered remarkable artistic traditions, and traded across both the continent and the Atlantic. Ethiopia, in the northeast, preserved one of the world's oldest Christian monarchies and connected the Red Sea world to the interior of Africa. Along the Swahili coast, city-states continued their Indian Ocean commerce even as Portuguese ships arrived.

Understanding Africa in this era requires recognizing several large patterns simultaneously. First, African societies were deeply diverse — politically, linguistically, ecologically, and culturally. There was no single African experience, any more than there was a single European or Asian one. The Sahel kingdoms of Mali and Songhai had given way by the seventeenth century to newer configurations of power. New states rose along the forest edges of West Africa, harnessing trade in kola, gold, iron, and eventually firearms. Central African kingdoms organized labor, tribute, and long-distance exchange across vast regions. Ethiopian emperors struggled to maintain their territories against Ottoman pressure from the northeast and internal fragmentation. The continent was in motion.

Second, African societies did not exist in isolation. Before European maritime expansion, Africa was already connected to the wider world through the trans-Saharan trade, the Red Sea, and the Indian Ocean. Gold, salt, ivory, iron, and enslaved captives moved along these routes for centuries. When Portuguese ships began appearing along the West African coast in the mid-fifteenth century, they encountered societies that had long experience with long-distance trade and that adapted to European contact in sophisticated ways. African rulers negotiated, set terms, established prices, and controlled access to their coasts and interiors. For much of the sixteenth century, the initiative in Atlantic commerce lay as much with African rulers as with Europeans.

Third, the Atlantic slave trade — which grew from a trickle in the mid-fifteenth century to a massive forced migration by the eighteenth — was one of the most consequential events in African history. It was not simply something imposed on passive Africans by European force. African rulers, merchants, and warriors played active roles in capturing, selling, and transporting enslaved people to the coast. But the scale, direction, and ultimate effects of the trade were shaped by European demand, European capital, and the plantation economies of the Americas. African societies paid an enormous demographic price — an estimated twelve million people were exported across the Atlantic over four centuries — and many African kingdoms were destabilized, militarized, and eventually weakened by participation in the trade.

Fourth, Africans resisted — individually and collectively. Enslaved people on the coast and in the interior resisted capture, escaped from coffles and barracoons, revolted aboard slave ships, and built free communities in the Americas. African rulers sometimes refused to engage in the trade or attempted to limit its scope, as Afonso I tried to do in Kongo. Others negotiated shifting terms. Still others built power precisely by monopolizing access to European firearms and selling captives taken in war. The history of the Atlantic slave trade is not a simple story of African victimhood or African complicity: it is a story of extreme power asymmetry, complex agency, and vast human suffering.

This chapter follows these threads through the early modern era. It begins with the broad patterns of African history around 1450, examines major kingdoms and states, traces the growth of coastal trade and the Atlantic slave system, and assesses the demographic and social consequences for African societies. It ends by situating Africa in the wider early modern Atlantic world — not as a peripheral supplier to European enterprise, but as a continent whose peoples, histories, and choices were central to the making of the modern world.

Historical Context

Africa in 1450 was a continent of extraordinary diversity and dynamism. Stretching from the Mediterranean in the north to the Cape of Good Hope in the south, from the Atlantic in the west to the Indian Ocean coast in the east, Africa encompassed more than fifty major ecological zones — Saharan desert, Sahelian savanna, tropical rainforest, East African rift valleys, southern grasslands, and coastal mangroves. These environments shaped the forms of food production, settlement, trade, and political organization that Africans developed over millennia.

By the mid-fifteenth century, the great Sahelian empires of Mali and Songhai had already demonstrated that large, complex states could govern diverse peoples across vast territories using Islam, trade revenue, and military power. The Mali Empire had been at its height in the fourteenth century under Mansa Musa; by 1450, it had fragmented into smaller successor states, even as Timbuktu remained a major center of Islamic scholarship and trans-Saharan commerce. Songhai, centered on the Niger bend, was in the process of building the largest empire West Africa had ever seen. These empires were connected to North Africa and the Middle East through the trans-Saharan caravan routes, which carried gold, salt, kola nuts, enslaved captives, copper, and luxury goods in both directions.

In the forest zones south of the savanna, a different pattern of political organization had developed. Where the Sahelian empires operated across open terrain with cavalry armies, the forested societies of West and Central Africa built power through control of trade in iron, kola, and other forest goods, through systems of tribute, through the authority of religious specialists, and through dense webs of kinship and patron-client relations. Cities like Benin City, in what is now southern Nigeria, were already major urban centers with sophisticated bronze-casting traditions. Farther south and east, in the Congo Basin and on the highlands of Central Africa, Bantu-speaking agricultural communities had developed systems of governance that ranged from dispersed village authority to larger chieftaincies and incipient kingdoms.

In Northeast Africa, Ethiopia stood as one of the oldest Christian monarchies in the world. The Solomonic dynasty claimed descent from the biblical union of King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba and ruled a highland kingdom that had maintained its independence through centuries of regional conflict. The Ethiopian church, following the Coptic tradition, played a central role in court culture, manuscript production, and religious life. Along the East African coast, the Swahili city-states continued the Indian Ocean commerce that had linked East Africa to the Arabian Peninsula, the Persian Gulf, India, and Southeast Asia for centuries.

Into this diverse and active African world, the Portuguese inserted themselves beginning in the 1440s. Prince Henry the Navigator's sponsorship of Atlantic exploration led to the rounding of the West African bulge and eventual contact with the Congo River by 1482. The Portuguese were not the first to trade with West Africans — trans-Saharan merchants had done so for centuries — but they offered direct oceanic access to Atlantic markets, bypassing the Saharan routes. African rulers, recognizing new commercial opportunities, engaged with them on terms that, at least initially, reflected African power and initiative.

The Atlantic slave trade that grew from these early contacts would become the dominant fact of African history for the next four centuries. Between approximately 1501 and 1867, an estimated 12.5 million Africans were loaded onto slave ships bound for the Americas. Of these, roughly 10.7 million survived the Middle Passage. The trade peaked in the eighteenth century, when more than 80,000 people per year were being exported. Its consequences for African demography, political stability, gender ratios, and economic development were profound and lasting.

Regional Orientation

West Africa encompassed the broad region from Senegal and the Gambia River in the west to the Niger Delta and Cameroon in the east, and from the Sahara in the north to the Atlantic coast in the south. This region is divided ecologically into Saharan desert, Sahelian savanna, Guinea savanna, and tropical forest. These ecological bands shaped trade and political power: Sahelian states could use cavalry, while forest states depended on infantry, trade control, and the advantages of dense terrain. The Niger River served as a major artery of trade and communication, linking inland cities like Timbuktu, Djenné, and Gao to the forest margins and coast.

Central Africa — the Congo Basin and its surrounding highlands — was dominated by the vast Congo River system, whose tributaries reached deep into the rainforest and linked communities across enormous distances. The Kingdom of Kongo occupied the western highlands south of the Congo River's mouth, in what is now northern Angola, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and the Republic of Congo. To the east, the Luba and Lunda kingdoms emerged in the interior during this period, organizing trade and tribute across the savanna.

Northeast Africa — Ethiopia, the Horn of Africa, and the Red Sea coast — was shaped by the Ethiopian Highland massif, a dramatic landscape of high plateaus cut by deep gorges. The surrounding lowlands were home to pastoralists and trading communities linked to the Red Sea. Ottoman expansion into Egypt and the Red Sea region in the sixteenth century brought new pressure on Ethiopia and disrupted existing trade patterns.

The Atlantic coast of Africa — from Senegambia through the Gold Coast, the Slave Coast, the Niger Delta, and down to Angola — became, in the early modern era, Africa's most intensely contested zone of commercial exchange. Dozens of European trading posts, forts, and slave-trading stations dotted this coastline from the mid-fifteenth century onward. But the coast was not simply Europe's frontier: it was the edge of powerful African states whose rulers controlled access to the interior, set prices, collected duties, and maintained the right to exclude Europeans from traveling inland.

Section 36.1

Africa in the Early Modern World

When historians speak of the "early modern world," they typically mean the period from roughly 1450 to 1800 — the era of European maritime expansion, the formation of gunpowder empires, the Protestant Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, and the intensification of global commercial networks. Africa was not peripheral to this world. It was central to it. African gold financed European commerce long before silver from the Americas arrived. African enslaved labor built the plantation economies that generated immense wealth in the Caribbean and Brazil. African soldiers, merchants, diplomats, scholars, and rulers shaped the course of events in ways that European observers sometimes recognized and sometimes deliberately obscured.

Africa around 1450 was politically diverse and economically active. In the western savanna, the Songhai Empire was consolidating its hold over the trading cities of the Niger bend. In the forest zones south of the savanna, a mosaic of smaller states and chieftaincies organized trade in kola, gold, and iron. Along the central Atlantic coast, in what is now Angola and the Democratic Republic of Congo, the Kingdom of Kongo had emerged as a major regional power. In the northeast, Ethiopia navigated between Red Sea commerce and the growing pressure of Muslim sultanates. Along the East African coast, Swahili cities maintained their long-standing connections to the Indian Ocean world.

These African societies were connected to one another through ancient networks of trade and cultural exchange. The trans-Saharan routes — which had operated since antiquity — carried gold, salt, kola, enslaved captives, copper, textiles, and manuscripts between sub-Saharan Africa and the Mediterranean world. Islam, which had arrived in West Africa in the tenth and eleventh centuries, had spread through the Sahelian cities and along the trade routes, creating a cosmopolitan Muslim scholarly culture that linked Timbuktu to Cairo, Fez, and Mecca.

The arrival of Portuguese ships on the West African coast in the 1440s thus did not introduce trade where none had existed. It introduced new trade routes, new commercial partners, and eventually new commodities — including European textiles, metalwork, wine, and later firearms — into a continent already deeply experienced with long-distance exchange. What made the Portuguese arrival different was not commerce itself, but the direction of trade, the specific goods involved, and above all the scale of the demand for enslaved people that Atlantic plantation economies would eventually generate.

African rulers initially responded to Portuguese contact from a position of strength. The Portuguese were barred from the interior of most African states. They traded at designated coastal points, paid duties to African rulers, and operated under African political authority for most of the sixteenth century. This early pattern — of African initiative and European accommodation — is essential context for understanding the later and more coercive phase of the trade.

The early modern era was also a period of significant internal African political change. The Songhai Empire collapsed under a Moroccan invasion in 1591, opening the savanna to new configurations of power. New states rose in the forest zones — Asante, Oyo, Dahomey, and others — that would become major players in the Atlantic slave trade. Kongo, once a unified monarchy, fragmented under the combined pressures of civil war and slave raiding. These internal transformations were partly driven by the logic of African politics and partly by the new pressures and incentives introduced by Atlantic commerce.

Any honest account of Africa in this era must grapple with a difficult question: how do we assess African participation in the Atlantic slave trade? The historical evidence is clear that African rulers, merchants, and warriors were active suppliers of enslaved people to European traders. But this observation must be placed in context. Existing African forms of slavery were very different in their social meaning and typical trajectory from the chattel slavery of the Americas. And the structural demand that drove the trade came from the plantation economies of the Americas, which were created and controlled by European capital and European colonial power. The Atlantic slave trade was not a simple story of African self-inflicted catastrophe; it was a story of global power, commercial incentive, and tragic complicity at many levels.

Section 36.2

Kingdoms and States of West and Central Africa

West and Central Africa in the early modern era were home to a remarkable range of political forms — from small, stateless communities organized by kinship and age-grades, to large, centralized kingdoms governing millions of people over vast territories. Understanding this diversity is essential to avoiding the stereotype that Africa was politically undeveloped or uniform.

The Songhai Empire, centered on the Niger bend city of Gao, expanded dramatically under Sunni Ali (r. 1464–1492) and reached its greatest extent under Askia Muhammad (r. 1493–1528). At its height, Songhai controlled the great trading cities of Timbuktu and Djenné, the salt mines of the Sahara's edge, the gold-producing regions to the south, and a vast tributary system that extracted wealth from dependent communities across an area comparable to Western Europe. The empire was governed through provincial governors, a standing army of professional soldiers, a river fleet on the Niger, and a system of taxation and tribute collection.

Islam was the religion of the Songhai ruling class and the scholarly class of the cities. Askia Muhammad made a famous pilgrimage to Mecca around 1497, seeking legitimation from the caliph and connection to the wider Islamic world. He patronized Islamic scholars and courts in Timbuktu, supporting the production of legal treatises, histories, and religious texts in Arabic. Timbuktu's mosques and madrasas became centers of scholarship that drew students from across West Africa and the wider Islamic world. The corpus of manuscripts produced in Timbuktu — many of which survive today in private collections and archives — is one of the great documentary treasures of African history.

Songhai's collapse came swiftly and from outside. In 1591, the Moroccan sultan Ahmad al-Mansur sent an army across the Sahara — equipped with muskets and artillery — to seize the gold trade. The Songhai army, which relied on cavalry and traditional weapons, was routed at the Battle of Tondibi. The empire disintegrated. The western savanna fragmented into dozens of smaller polities. The great trading cities recovered partially, but the unified political structure of the Songhai Empire was gone.

South and east of the savanna, the forest zones of West Africa had their own political traditions. Forest states relied on infantry warfare, control of trade routes through dense terrain, and the authority of religious specialists and lineage elders. The city and kingdom of Benin, in what is now southern Nigeria, was one of the most sophisticated of these states. Benin was governed by an oba (king) who had consolidated power over a large territory and established a system of governance that combined royal authority, a titled nobility organized into palace and town associations, and a professional military.

Benin traded pepper, ivory, and cloth with the Portuguese in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries. The oba permitted some slave trading but was selective and maintained control. When Portuguese demand for enslaved captives increased, some obas restricted or temporarily banned the export of male slaves, preferring to keep male captives for the kingdom's own labor needs and military service. This selective engagement — trading some goods freely, restricting others — illustrates the degree of African political control in the early period.

In Central Africa, the Kingdom of Kongo was the dominant political force west of the Congo River. Founded probably in the fourteenth century, Kongo by 1450 governed a large, hierarchically organized state with the Manikongo at the apex, provincial governors (mani) below him, and a hierarchy of village-level officials at the base. Kongo was wealthy in iron, copper, raffia cloth, and nzimbu shells. Its governance was sophisticated: the Manikongo appointed and removed provincial governors, collected taxes, administered justice, and organized military campaigns through a system that contemporary European observers sometimes compared to European monarchies.

Section 36.3

Kongo, Benin, Oyo, and Asante

Kongo's Engagement with Portugal and the Slave Trade

The reign of Afonso I (c. 1509–1542) is one of the best-documented in early African history, thanks largely to his extensive correspondence with the Portuguese court — letters that survive in the Torre do Tombo archive in Lisbon. Afonso was a sophisticated ruler who used Christianity as a tool of governance and a means of deepening ties with Portugal. He sent his son Henrique to study in Lisbon; Henrique became the first African bishop in the Catholic Church. Afonso himself built churches, promoted Christian ceremony at court, and integrated Christian symbolism into Kongolese royal authority.

At the same time, Afonso was deeply troubled by what the Portuguese connection was doing to his kingdom. Portuguese traders were buying enslaved people not only from Kongolese merchants who offered captives from legitimate wars, but also from private raiders who kidnapped free Kongolese subjects — including nobles and their families. Afonso's famous letter of 1526 captures his anguish: his kingdom was being depopulated, his authority undermined, and his subjects seized and sold without his knowledge or consent. He asked the Portuguese king to send only priests and teachers, not traders. He asked for the trade to be regulated. His requests were largely ignored.

Kongo's experience illustrates several crucial dynamics of the early Atlantic slave trade. African rulers like Afonso were not simply complicit; they tried to maintain control over who was enslaved and on what terms. But the demand from Atlantic markets, combined with the profits available to private traders and the difficulty of policing long coastlines, made such control nearly impossible to sustain.

The seventeenth century brought further crisis to Kongo. Civil wars, Portuguese military intervention, and the devastating Battle of Mbwila in 1665 — in which a Portuguese-allied army defeated and killed the Manikongo — shattered Kongo's political unity. The kingdom fragmented into competing regional powers, and the Atlantic slave trade intensified precisely because the weakened political structure could no longer regulate it.

Benin's Artistic and Political Power

Benin's history in the early modern era offers a contrasting pattern. Unlike Kongo, Benin maintained strong political cohesion and relatively tight control over Atlantic trade throughout the sixteenth and into the seventeenth century. Benin's obas were wary of European penetration, permitted trade at designated points, and periodically restricted the export of male slaves. Benin's economy was more diversified — it exported cloth, ivory, and pepper as well as captives — and its military strength allowed it to resist European pressure more effectively.

Benin's cultural achievements in this period were extraordinary. The bronze and brass artworks produced by the royal guild of casters (the igun eronmwon) included large commemorative plaques depicting court scenes, royal heads used in ancestor veneration ceremonies, and complex figurative sculptures of astonishing technical skill. These bronzes were created using the lost-wax casting technique, which requires a high level of metallurgical expertise. They are not merely decorative objects; they are historical documents, recording the names, faces, and deeds of successive obas, the ceremonies of the court, and the role of European traders in Benin's commercial networks. When British forces looted Benin City in 1897 and carried off thousands of bronzes to European cities, they took not only artworks but archives.

Oyo's Cavalry Empire

Oyo's rise to regional dominance in the seventeenth century rested on a combination of military innovation, political organization, and commercial advantage. The savanna location north of the Yoruba forest homeland gave Oyo access to horses — animals that could not survive in the tsetse-fly-infested forest. The Oyo cavalry was the most powerful military force in the region and enabled the empire to extract tribute from states that could not resist cavalry raids.

Oyo's political organization was notable for its balance of power between the king (Alafin) and a council of nobles (Oyo Mesi). The Alafin wielded enormous symbolic authority and controlled access to royal resources, but the Oyo Mesi could constrain the king's power and even compel a ruler considered tyrannical to commit ritual suicide. This constitutional balance allowed Oyo to remain politically stable over long periods despite dynastic tensions.

Oyo became a major participant in the Atlantic slave trade in the seventeenth and especially the eighteenth century. The empire's wars of expansion generated large numbers of captives who were marched to the coast — particularly to the port of Ouidah on the Slave Coast — and sold to European slavers. Oyo's tributary relationship with Dahomey gave it access to this coastal outlet.

Asante's Forest Kingdom

The Asante kingdom, founded in the early eighteenth century, represents one of the most dynamic political formations in West African history. The Asante state emerged from the consolidation of Akan-speaking chieftaincies in the Gold Coast forest under the leadership of Osei Tutu and his spiritual adviser Okomfo Anokye. The foundational myth of Asante centers on the Golden Stool (Sika Dwa), which Okomfo Anokye is said to have caused to descend from the sky and which was declared to contain the soul of the Asante nation. The stool was never sat upon; it was the supreme symbol of Asante unity and sovereignty.

Asante's wealth came initially from gold — the Gold Coast had been exporting gold to Atlantic traders since the Portuguese arrived in 1471. Asante controlled important gold-producing areas and organized a system of extraction and taxation that generated immense royal revenue. Gold allowed Asante to purchase firearms from European traders, and firearms transformed Asante's military capacity, enabling rapid territorial expansion.

By the mid-eighteenth century, Asante had become a substantial empire controlling much of the Gold Coast and parts of the interior. Its capital, Kumasi, became a major city with wide streets, large markets, and a royal court known for its wealth and ceremony. Asante was also a major participant in the Atlantic slave trade, supplying enslaved captives taken in its wars of expansion to European traders on the coast.

Section 36.4

Ethiopia and Northeast African Connections

Ethiopia in the early modern era was a kingdom under pressure from multiple directions, and its survival as an independent state through the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries is one of the more remarkable stories in African history. The Solomonic dynasty, restored to power in 1270 after a period of Zagwe rule, claimed descent from the biblical union of Solomon and Makeda (the Queen of Sheba), a claim elaborated in the magnificent fourteenth-century court epic Kebra Nagast (Glory of Kings). This dynastic mythology gave Ethiopian kingship a distinctive sacred character and connected the kingdom to the traditions of ancient Israel — a connection that the Ethiopian church nurtured, giving Ethiopian Christianity its particular flavor, including the keeping of the Sabbath, dietary laws resembling those of Leviticus, and the veneration of the Ark of the Covenant.

The Ethiopian church (technically in communion with the Coptic Patriarchate of Alexandria) was central to Ethiopian political culture. Monasteries were major landholders, centers of literacy and manuscript production, and sources of religious and moral authority that the monarchy both depended on and competed with. Ethiopian manuscripts — including illuminated Gospels, hagiographies, royal chronicles, and theological texts — are among the most beautiful and historically valuable documents in African history. The rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, carved from solid stone in the twelfth century, testify to the depth and continuity of Ethiopian Christian civilization.

The sixteenth century brought existential crisis. The Sultanate of Adal, centered in the Somali lowlands to the east, launched devastating military campaigns against Ethiopia beginning around 1529 under the leadership of Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi (known to Ethiopians as "Gran," meaning "the left-handed"). Ahmad's forces used Ottoman firearms and artillery obtained through connections to the wider Muslim world, and they swept through the Ethiopian highlands with a speed and destructiveness that the Ethiopian army could not initially match. Churches were burned, monasteries destroyed, manuscripts lost.

What saved Ethiopia was a combination of geography, political will, and Portuguese military intervention. In 1541, a small Portuguese force arrived in Ethiopia in response to an appeal for Christian solidarity. In 1542, the Portuguese musketeers and their Ethiopian allies defeated and killed Ahmad Gran at the Battle of Wayna Daga. Ethiopia was not simply saved by the Portuguese — the Ethiopian forces were far larger and the military leadership Ethiopian — but the Portuguese firearms tipped the balance at a crucial moment.

By the seventeenth century, Ethiopia had entered a period known as the Zemene Mesafint (Era of the Princes), in which real power shifted from the emperor to a class of regional nobles (Ras) who controlled the court and manipulated the succession. The emperors remained sacred figures but lost effective political authority. This pattern — sacred monarchy without real power — would persist until the nineteenth century. Ethiopia's connections to the wider world were maintained through the Red Sea trade and through the Ethiopian church's connections to the Coptic Patriarchate of Alexandria.

Section 36.5

Coastal Trade and European Contact

The Portuguese arrival on the West African coast in the mid-fifteenth century did not create trade where none had existed; it redirected and expanded it. African coastal communities had long histories of exchange with interior peoples, and some coastal points were already commercial centers. The Portuguese sought gold, pepper, ivory, and eventually enslaved people, and they were willing to pay for access with European manufactured goods, including cloth, metalware, wine, and later firearms.

The first major Portuguese trading installation in sub-Saharan Africa was Elmina (São Jorge da Mina), established in 1482 on the Gold Coast. Elmina was built as a stone fort — the first European stone structure in sub-Saharan Africa — and it served initially as a center of gold trade. The Gold Coast exported gold that had been mined inland by Akan communities and brought to the coast through established trade routes.

European trading forts eventually dotted the entire West African coast from Senegambia to Angola. These forts served as warehouses, garrison points, and diplomatic bases. But they were not colonial territories in the interior sense: the land on which they stood was leased from African rulers, who maintained sovereignty and collected duties. European traders who traveled inland without permission did so at considerable risk, and African rulers regularly used the threat of expulsion to enforce commercial terms.

The commercial relationship between European traders and African rulers evolved significantly over the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. In the early period, African rulers negotiated from strength, setting prices, controlling access, and expelling traders who violated their terms. European trading companies competed fiercely with one another for access to African markets, which gave African rulers leverage: a ruler could threaten to trade with one European company if another refused his terms.

Firearms transformed the military competition among African states in ways that ultimately destabilized the region. States that acquired firearms early — through coastal trade — gained military advantages over neighbors, which they used to expand, to raid for captives, and to sell those captives for more firearms. This "guns for slaves" cycle created a dynamic in which participation in the Atlantic slave trade was simultaneously a source of military power and a cause of the warfare that generated captives. States that refused to engage risked being raided by better-armed neighbors.

Afro-Portuguese traders — people of mixed African and Portuguese descent, often speaking multiple languages and familiar with both commercial cultures — played a crucial role as commercial intermediaries. Known as lançados or tangomãos, these individuals operated at the intersection of African and European commercial worlds. They arranged deals, translated languages and cultural expectations, and often built deep roots in African communities through marriage and kinship ties.

Section 36.6

The Atlantic Slave Trade

The Atlantic slave trade was one of the largest forced migrations in human history. Over roughly four centuries — from the mid-fifteenth to the mid-nineteenth century — an estimated 12.5 million people were taken from Africa and transported across the Atlantic to the Americas. Of these, approximately 1.8 million died during the Middle Passage, the ocean crossing from Africa to the Americas. Those who survived were sold into various forms of plantation labor — growing sugar in Brazil and the Caribbean, tobacco in Virginia, rice in the Carolinas, cotton across the Deep South — that generated enormous wealth for European and colonial American investors, merchants, and planters.

The trade grew slowly at first. In the fifteenth century, Portuguese traders transported relatively small numbers of Africans to Portugal, Madeira, and the Canary Islands, where they worked on sugar plantations. The colonization of Brazil after 1500 and the establishment of sugar plantations in the Caribbean created a rapidly growing demand for labor. Indigenous populations in the Americas had been devastated by epidemic disease — smallpox, measles, typhus, and other Old World pathogens to which they had no immunity. European indentured servants were expensive, limited in supply, and prone to running away. Enslaved Africans became the preferred labor force for plantation economies.

African captives were obtained through several mechanisms. The most common was warfare: African states raided neighboring communities or conducted wars specifically to generate captives for sale. Judicial enslavement was also significant — in many African societies, serious crimes or inability to repay debts could result in enslavement, and Atlantic demand expanded the use of these mechanisms. Kidnapping — the seizure of individuals or small groups by private raiders — was also common and increased as Atlantic demand grew.

The process of enslavement and transport was itself traumatic and deadly. Captives taken in the interior were marched to the coast in coffles — chains of people bound at the neck or wrist, often walking hundreds of miles through unfamiliar terrain. Many died on these overland marches from exhaustion, disease, or violence. At the coast, captives were held in barracoons — coastal holding pens — while traders negotiated prices and assembled enough people to fill a ship.

The Middle Passage — the ocean crossing — was the most deadly phase of the journey for those who survived to reach the ships. Enslaved people were packed into the holds of specially constructed ships in conditions of extreme overcrowding. Men were typically chained together below decks for most of the crossing. The average crossing took three to six weeks, during which dysentery, smallpox, scurvy, and other diseases spread rapidly through the tightly packed holds. Mortality on the Middle Passage averaged approximately 12–15 percent in the eighteenth century.

Resistance was constant. Enslaved people resisted at every stage of the process — refusing to march, jumping overboard, starving themselves, attacking guards in the barracoons, and revolting on board ship. The Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database records hundreds of documented shipboard revolts, though successful revolts that brought ships back to Africa were extremely rare.

The trade peaked in the eighteenth century, when improved ship designs, more efficient commercial organization, and growing demand from rapidly expanding plantation economies drove exports to their highest levels. The British alone transported approximately 3.1 million Africans across the Atlantic between 1660 and 1807. Portugal (including Brazil) transported roughly 4.8 million over the entire history of the trade. West-Central Africa (the Kongo-Angola region) was the largest single source of enslaved people, providing roughly 40 percent of the total.

Abolition came slowly and unevenly. British abolitionists — including Olaudah Equiano, Granville Sharp, Thomas Clarkson, and William Wilberforce — campaigned for decades before Parliament abolished the British slave trade in 1807. Brazil did not effectively abolish the trade until 1850, and slavery itself only in 1888. The abolition of the trade did not end the suffering of those already enslaved or the structural inequalities the trade had created.

Historical Vocabulary
Chattel Slavery

Chattel slavery refers to a system in which enslaved people are legally defined as movable property — chattels — that can be bought, sold, inherited, mortgaged, and traded like any other commodity. Chattel slavery is distinguished from other forms of unfreedom (serfdom, debt bondage, captive labor) by its absolute commodification of persons and by the hereditary transmission of enslaved status through the mother's line in most Atlantic plantation systems.

Chattel slavery as practiced in the Americas differed significantly from African forms of captive labor, in which enslaved people often retained some social identity, had pathways to integration into the captor's community, and did not automatically transmit their status to their children. Historians use the term "enslaved person" (rather than "slave") to emphasize that enslavement was something done to a person, not something that defined a person's essential identity.

Section 36.7

African Agency, Resistance, and Adaptation

One of the most important and contested questions in the history of the Atlantic slave trade is the question of African agency: to what degree were Africans active agents in the trade, and to what degree were they passive victims of European force? The answer — carefully developed by historians over the last several decades — is nuanced and uncomfortable.

African rulers, merchants, and warriors were indeed active participants in the trade. Without African sellers, there could have been no Atlantic slave trade at the scale that actually occurred. African political leaders decided whether to sell captives to European traders, set prices, and organized the military and commercial operations that delivered captives to the coast. The Kingdom of Dahomey built its state power partly around the control of slave exports; the Asante kingdom used slave-trade revenues to purchase firearms; the Oyo Empire organized military campaigns that generated captives specifically for sale.

But the concept of "agency" must be carefully contextualized. Several considerations complicate simple narratives of African complicity. First, the demand that drove the trade came from the Americas, where European capital had created plantation economies requiring enormous quantities of unfree labor. African sellers responded to a demand they did not create. Second, existing African institutions of captivity and slavery were quite different in their social meaning from the chattel slavery of the Americas. Third, the "guns for slaves" dynamic created structural pressures that made non-participation extremely costly: states that refused to engage could be raided by better-armed neighbors.

Moreover, there were African voices — like Afonso I of Kongo — who explicitly protested the trade and sought to limit it. There were African rulers who banned the trade outright, at least for periods. The Benin kingdom limited male slave exports for significant stretches of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. African resistance to the trade was not only the resistance of the enslaved; it was also the resistance of rulers and communities who recognized the trade's destructiveness.

The resistance of the enslaved themselves was pervasive and multiform. In the coffles, people refused to walk, attempted to strangle themselves, or attacked guards. In the barracoons, they starved themselves, organized collective escapes, and spread disease as acts of defiance. On the ships, they revolted. In the Americas, the enslaved ran away, sabotaged tools and crops, poisoned masters, organized conspiracies, and built maroon communities in the forests and mountains of Brazil, Jamaica, Suriname, and elsewhere.

African cultural practices survived the Middle Passage and the plantation system with remarkable tenacity. Language, music, religious practice, foodways, naming customs, and kinship patterns were preserved, transformed, and carried into New World contexts. The Yoruba religious traditions that survived in Cuba as Santería, in Brazil as Candomblé, and in Haiti as Vodou are among the most visible examples of this African cultural persistence. The enslaved were not simply passive recipients of European cultural domination; they were active builders of new African-Atlantic cultures.

Historical Vocabulary
The Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database (Slave Voyages)

The Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database, available online at slavevoyages.org, is the most comprehensive quantitative record of the Atlantic slave trade. Compiled over several decades by a team of historians led by David Eltis and colleagues, it records information on approximately 36,000 slave voyages — including ship names, origins, destinations, numbers of people loaded and delivered, mortality rates, and other variables.

The database's data come from customs records, ship logs, insurance documents, plantation inventories, and other archival sources in archives across Europe, Africa, and the Americas. It is not complete — many voyages left no surviving records — but it is sufficiently comprehensive to allow confident statistical analysis of broad trends. Students and historians use it to ask questions about patterns: which regions exported the most people, which destinations received the most, how mortality varied over time and by route, and how the trade responded to political and economic changes.

Section 36.8

Demographic and Social Effects

The Atlantic slave trade's demographic impact on Africa was profound, though the precise numbers are contested. The best estimates, drawn from the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database, suggest that approximately 12.5 million people were exported from Africa across the Atlantic between 1501 and 1867. This figure does not include the many who died before reaching the coast — in warfare, on the coffles, or in the barracoons — whose numbers some historians estimate doubled the total "loss" to African populations.

The demographic impact was not uniform across Africa. Some regions were more heavily affected than others. West-Central Africa (the Kongo-Angola region) lost approximately 5.7 million people to the Atlantic trade. The Bight of Biafra (southeastern Nigeria) lost approximately 1.6 million; the Gold Coast, approximately 1.2 million. These losses fell disproportionately on men of military age — prime targets for slave raiders — which skewed the gender ratios of affected populations. In some heavily raided areas, women significantly outnumbered men, with consequences for family structure, agricultural labor, and social organization.

Beyond raw demographics, the slave trade had profound political and social effects. In the most heavily affected regions, the trade generated cycles of violence and insecurity that undermined governance, disrupted agriculture, and forced communities to relocate to more defensible positions — hilltops, island communities, dense forests. States that built their power on slave raiding needed constant expansion to maintain the supply of captives; when they ran out of easy targets, they raided their own dependent communities or turned on each other.

The internal African slave trade also grew in response to Atlantic demand. As coastal exports created demand, African merchants organized long-distance trade routes that carried captives from the interior to the coast. This internal trade involved its own networks of brokers, markets, and holding facilities, and it brought the destabilizing effects of slave raiding deeper into the continent. Communities hundreds of miles from the coast were nonetheless affected by the demand that trade generated.

Gender dynamics were deeply affected. Because the Atlantic trade disproportionately exported men, women made up the majority of those retained as enslaved people within Africa. Women enslaved within African societies typically worked in agriculture, in households, and in trade. The feminization of African domestic slavery was a direct consequence of the Atlantic trade's preference for male captives. Social institutions were also transformed: in many West African societies, the distinction between free persons and dependents was important but not necessarily permanent or hereditary. Atlantic demand encouraged the hardening of these distinctions and the commodification of captivity in ways that changed the social meaning of enslavement within African societies.

Historical Vocabulary
Maroon Communities

Maroon communities were communities of escaped enslaved people who established independent settlements in remote or difficult terrain, beyond the effective control of colonial authorities. The word "maroon" derives from the Spanish cimarrón, meaning wild or untamed. Maroon communities existed throughout the Americas — in Brazil, Jamaica, Suriname, Cuba, and the Carolinas.

The largest and most famous maroon state was Palmares, in northeastern Brazil, which survived for most of the seventeenth century as a confederacy of several settlements (quilombos) with a population estimated at tens of thousands at its peak. Its most famous leader, Zumbi dos Palmares, became a symbol of African resistance in Brazilian history. Maroon communities in Jamaica signed peace treaties with the British in 1739 and 1740, securing their freedom and autonomy in the Jamaican interior. Their descendants still live in these communities today.

Section 36.9

Africa and the Early Modern Atlantic World

Understanding Africa's place in the early modern Atlantic world requires situating Africa not as a peripheral supplier to a European-centered system, but as a constitutive part of the Atlantic world itself. The Americas could not have been colonized as they were without African labor. The plantation economies that generated the wealth that financed European commercial expansion were built on enslaved African labor. The cultural, biological, and demographic contributions of Africans to the Americas were fundamental and lasting.

The Atlantic world was, in important respects, an African-Atlantic world. African peoples, cultures, languages, religions, and knowledge systems crossed the Atlantic along with the enslaved individuals who carried them. The demographics of the early modern Americas were heavily African: in 1800, more Africans and their descendants lived in the Americas than people of European descent. The cities of Salvador da Bahia, Havana, Charleston, and Kingston were as much African cities as they were European colonial ones, shaped by the cultural practices, labor, and social organization of their African-descended majorities or near-majorities.

Within Africa, the early modern era was also shaped by the Atlantic connection in ways beyond the slave trade. New crops from the Americas — maize (corn), cassava (manioc), groundnuts (peanuts), sweet potatoes — were adopted by African farmers and transformed African agriculture. Maize and cassava in particular were well-suited to African soils and climates, produced higher caloric yields than older staple crops, and could be grown in areas too dry or too wet for traditional grains. These Columbian Exchange crops supported population growth in some African regions even as the slave trade was depressing it in others.

European goods also circulated through African economies in quantities sufficient to affect local markets. Textiles from India (brought by European traders), metalware, alcohol, tobacco, and firearms all entered African commercial circuits through the Atlantic trade. The overall effect was to intensify African engagement with global commodity markets in ways that had both positive and negative consequences for different communities.

By the late eighteenth century, African rulers were navigating a world in which the terms of Atlantic engagement were shifting. The abolitionist movement in Britain and North America was beginning to challenge the legal and moral foundations of the slave trade. Some African rulers engaged with abolitionist missionaries and diplomats who visited the coast, sensing new possibilities for reformulating the commercial relationship with Europe. The abolition of the British slave trade in 1807 marked the beginning of a long political struggle over the trade's future that would continue for decades.

The history of Africa in the early modern world is, ultimately, a story of power, agency, and catastrophe — interwoven in ways that resist simple moral or analytical summary. African states and societies were not simply victims of European predation, but neither were they simply architects of their own misfortune. They were actors in a global system whose structural dynamics — shaped by European capital, American plantation demand, and the specific geography of Atlantic commerce — constrained their choices in fundamental ways.

Regional Lens

The Kingdom of Benin

The Kingdom of Benin, located in what is now Edo State in southern Nigeria, was one of the most sophisticated political and artistic states in early modern Africa. Its history challenges simplistic narratives about African development and illustrates the depth and complexity of West African political culture.

Benin's geography placed it in the transition zone between the rainforest and the savanna, giving it access to both forest resources (rubber, kola, palm products) and savanna trade networks. The Edo-speaking people of Benin had developed a distinctive political system centered on the oba (king), whose authority was simultaneously political and spiritual. Royal ceremonies — elaborate, precisely choreographed rituals involving music, masquerade, sacrifice, and procession — were essential to maintaining the kingdom's spiritual health and political order.

Below the oba, Benin's governance was organized through two parallel hierarchies: the palace chiefs (eghaevbo n'ogbe), who lived in and administered the palace complex, and the town chiefs (eghaevbo n'ore), who administered the city and surrounding villages. This dual system created a balance of power that prevented any single group from monopolizing access to the king. A system of guilds — each responsible for a specific craft or service — organized economic production and maintained the technical expertise necessary for the court's material culture.

The Benin bronzes are the most visible legacy of the kingdom's artistic tradition. The famous plaques that once covered the pillars of the oba's reception hall depicted historical scenes: wars, ceremonies, trade encounters, royal audiences. They are, in effect, a pictorial history of the kingdom, created by specialists who had internalized centuries of visual convention and historical memory. When British forces invaded Benin City in February 1897 and looted the palace, they carried off thousands of bronzes and ivories to museums across Europe. The ongoing debate over their return to Nigeria is one of the most prominent examples of the broader question of cultural restitution from the colonial era.

Global Connection

The Triangular Trade and Atlantic Integration

The Atlantic slave trade was embedded in a wider commercial system that historians sometimes call the "triangular trade." In the classic formulation, European merchants carried manufactured goods (textiles, metalware, firearms, alcohol) from Europe to West Africa, where they traded them for enslaved people; they then transported the enslaved across the Atlantic (the Middle Passage) to the Americas, where they sold them in exchange for plantation products (sugar, tobacco, rice, cotton); and they returned to Europe with those plantation products to sell at profit.

This commercial system was global in its reach and consequences. The manufactured goods that European traders brought to Africa included Indian textiles — particularly the cotton cloth of South Asia, which was enormously popular in African markets. European merchants thus linked South Asian textile production to West African commercial demand, drawing India into the Atlantic system even without direct Indian participation. The silver that financed Atlantic commerce had originated in the mines of Bolivia and Mexico, operated by coerced Indigenous and African labor, and circulated through Spain and Portugal into Atlantic trade.

The Atlantic world was thus not a European creation imposed on passive others; it was a genuinely multilateral world, made by the labor, culture, and agency of Africans, Indigenous Americans, and Europeans together — though profoundly unequally. African dietary practices — including rice cultivation, palm oil use, and the flavors of West African cooking — shaped the foodways of the Atlantic world. African religious and musical traditions transformed the cultural landscapes of the Caribbean, Brazil, and North America in ways that echo to the present day.

Empire Study

The Oyo Empire

Origins and Rise: The Oyo Empire emerged from the Yoruba-speaking region of what is now southwestern Nigeria, establishing itself as the dominant power in the zone between the dry savanna and the forest in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Oyo's foundation myth connects its origins to the city of Ile-Ife, the spiritual homeland of the Yoruba, and to the semi-divine ancestor Oduduwa. The Alafin of Oyo claimed descent from Oduduwa and derived his sacred authority from this genealogy.

Military Organization: Oyo's military system was its defining feature. The empire's savanna location, north of the tsetse-fly belt, made horse-keeping possible, and Oyo invested heavily in cavalry. The Oyo cavalry could conduct rapid raids and campaigns across wide areas, striking before slower-moving infantry forces could respond. Oyo's military capacity was sustained by continuous supply of horses from North Africa through trans-Saharan trade routes and by the purchase of firearms from coastal traders.

The empire maintained a professional military establishment centered on the Oyo Mesi (a council of seven leading chiefs who controlled military recruitment and royal succession) and the Ogboni (a judicial and religious society with significant political influence). Remarkably, the Oyo Mesi could "ask" the Alafin to die — a euphemism for compelling the king to commit ritual suicide if he was judged to have become tyrannical.

Trade and the Slave System: Oyo's participation in the Atlantic slave trade was extensive. The empire's wars of expansion generated large numbers of captives, and Oyo's relationship with the coastal kingdom of Dahomey — which paid tribute to Oyo and facilitated its access to the port of Ouidah — gave it a reliable conduit for slave exports. The revenue generated funded the purchase of firearms, which funded further military expansion and slave raiding.

Decline and Legacy: Oyo's collapse in the early nineteenth century is one of the great political catastrophes of West African history. Internal political conflict, military overextension, a revolt by the tributary state of Dahomey, the rise of the Sokoto Caliphate to the north, and devastating civil wars combined to fragment the empire in the 1830s. This collapse generated a massive refugee crisis and enormous numbers of enslaved captives — the Yoruba diaspora in Cuba and Brazil dates substantially from this period. The Yoruba-derived religious traditions that survived in Cuba (Santería), Brazil (Candomblé), and Trinidad (Shango) reflect the enormous scale of Yoruba enslavement in the final phase of the Atlantic slave trade.

Primary Source Window

Afonso I of Kongo to the King of Portugal, 1526

Creator: Manikongo Afonso I of Kongo · Date: 1526 · Type: Diplomatic correspondence; royal letter

Context: This letter was written in 1526 by Manikongo Afonso I to King João III of Portugal. Afonso was a Christian convert who had maintained diplomatic relations with Portugal for decades. By 1526, however, the Atlantic slave trade was beginning to destabilize his kingdom, as Portuguese and African private traders raided for captives — including free Kongolese subjects — without royal authorization. The letter is preserved in the Torre do Tombo national archive in Lisbon and is one of the most important primary sources for understanding African political responses to the early Atlantic slave trade.

Afonso described traders daily seizing Kongolese people — including children of nobles and members of his own circle — and selling them as if they were war captives. He lamented that his kingdom was being depopulated and his authority undermined. He appealed to their shared Christian faith, asking the king to send only priests and teachers, not merchants interested only in profit. He asked for help stopping the abuses, saying that his kingdom needed medicine and books, not the disruption that traders brought.

Analysis: Afonso's letter reveals that African rulers in the early period of the Atlantic trade were not passive: they had clear views about the trade's effects and sought to limit it through political and diplomatic means. It shows that the slave trade was already penetrating Kongo's social fabric by 1526 in ways the Manikongo found threatening to his authority and to his people's welfare. It also illustrates the role of Christianity as a framework for diplomatic communication: Afonso appeals to shared Christian values as a basis for Portugal's obligation to help him. The letter's survival in Portuguese archives rather than in a Kongolese archive reminds us of the asymmetry of historical documentation in Atlantic history.

Questions for students:

  1. What does Afonso I's letter reveal about his understanding of the relationship between the slave trade and political authority in Kongo?
  2. How does Afonso use Christianity in his appeal to the Portuguese king? What does this tell us about the role of religion in early Atlantic diplomacy?
  3. What can this letter tell us about the scale and effects of the slave trade on Kongo by 1526? What can it not tell us?
  4. How might a Portuguese slave trader operating in Kongo at this time have described the same situation differently?
Archaeology and Material Evidence Window

The Benin Bronzes

The Benin bronzes — approximately 3,000 brass and bronze plaques, royal heads, figurines, and ceremonial objects created by the igun eronmwon guild of Benin City — are among the most important material evidence for the history of early modern West Africa. They were produced over several centuries, with the major series of commemorative plaques dating primarily to the sixteenth century, and were kept in the royal palace as royal property used in ancestor veneration ceremonies and court ritual.

The plaques depicted named obas with their regalia, military campaigns, ceremonial events, and court scenes. Portuguese figures appear on some plaques — usually depicted in subordinate positions — which offers valuable evidence about how Benin's rulers understood their relationship with European traders. The plaques also depict court officials with their title insignia, allowing historians to reconstruct aspects of Benin's hierarchical political system.

The lost-wax casting technique used to produce the bronzes requires high levels of technical skill and significant material resources. Brass (a copper-zinc alloy) was imported from Europe as manillas (metal rings used as currency) and melted down for casting. This illustrates the complex commercial and cultural exchanges of the early Atlantic period.

What historians can infer: the sophistication of Benin's court culture, the importance of ancestor veneration in royal ideology, the hierarchical structure of the court, the presence of European traders, and the high value placed on artistic production as a form of royal legitimation. What the bronzes cannot tell us alone: the lives of ordinary Benin people, the experiences of those enslaved by or through Benin's military campaigns, or internal political conflicts within the court. The 1897 British looting of Benin City and the dispersal of the bronzes to European museums has made them the subject of a prominent international debate about cultural restitution — a debate in which many of the bronzes are now being returned to Nigeria.

Historical Debate

African Agency and Responsibility in the Atlantic Slave Trade

The question: To what degree were African rulers, merchants, and warriors morally and historically responsible for the Atlantic slave trade? And to what degree does emphasizing African participation risk excusing European responsibility for a global system of coerced labor and racial hierarchy?

Interpretation A: African Participation Was Substantial and Morally Significant
Some historians argue that African rulers and merchants were active, calculating participants in the Atlantic slave trade who made choices that served their political and economic interests. John Thornton, in Africa and Africans in the Making of the Atlantic World (1992), argued that Africans participated as sovereign actors who set terms, established prices, and controlled access to the interior. The trade could not have operated at its actual scale without African cooperation. This interpretation draws on diplomatic records, traders' accounts, and the records of African kingdoms themselves, which show rulers actively organizing slave-raiding expeditions and establishing commercial relationships with European traders.
Interpretation B: Structural Conditions Limited African Agency
Other historians, including Walter Rodney (How Europe Underdeveloped Africa, 1972) and Patrick Manning (Slavery and African Life, 1990), emphasize that African participation must be understood within a global system of power and demand that African rulers did not create and could not individually resist. The demand for enslaved labor came from the Americas, where European capital had established plantation economies. The "guns for slaves" dynamic created structural pressures that made non-participation increasingly dangerous. This interpretation emphasizes the difference between the formal agency of African rulers (the ability to choose among limited options) and the structural power of European capital and American plantation demand (which created those limited options).

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the historical record of the trade itself — the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database, diplomatic correspondence, travelers' accounts, and the records of African kingdoms. The debate is not about the facts of African participation (which are not seriously disputed) but about how to interpret that participation in terms of moral responsibility, structural constraint, and historical causation.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how we understand the historical roots of the Atlantic world's racial hierarchies and economic inequalities. If African rulers bear substantial responsibility, this complicates narratives of simple African victimhood and European guilt. If African participation was primarily a response to structural pressures, this places primary causal responsibility — though not the only responsibility — with European actors.

Be sure to distinguish between the question of historical causation (what factors explain the trade's growth?) and the question of moral responsibility (who bears responsibility for the trade's consequences?).

Comparison Across Societies

Kongo and Benin: Two Responses to the Atlantic Trade

Political Organization: Kongo was governed by a centralized monarchy (the Manikongo) with a hierarchical system of provincial governors. Benin was also monarchical but organized through a complex dual hierarchy of palace and town chiefs with elaborate ceremonial functions. Kongo's more centralized structure made it more vulnerable to the political disruptions caused by the slave trade, as civil wars could shatter the kingdom's unity. Benin's more distributed power structure allowed it to absorb shocks more effectively.

Religious Transformation: Kongo's ruling class converted to Christianity in the late fifteenth century and integrated Christian symbols and practices into royal authority. This transformation was genuine but also created dependency on Portuguese religious institutions and diplomatic frameworks. Benin's rulers maintained their indigenous religious system centered on royal ancestor veneration and did not convert, reinforcing Benin's political independence from European frameworks.

Engagement with the Slave Trade: Both kingdoms exported enslaved people, but in different quantities and under different conditions. Kongo became increasingly deeply enmeshed in the slave trade, particularly after the fragmentation of the kingdom in 1665, and West-Central Africa became the largest single region of slave export. Benin maintained more selective engagement, periodically restricting exports and diversifying its trade portfolio with ivory, cloth, and pepper.

Long-Term Outcomes: Kongo's deep involvement in the slave trade, combined with its political fragmentation after 1665, produced lasting demographic and political damage. Benin maintained its political independence and cultural vitality into the nineteenth century, until the British invasion of 1897. These contrasting outcomes suggest that political cohesion and diversified trade relationships provided better insulation against the destructive effects of Atlantic commerce — though geography and other structural factors also played important roles.

Multiple Perspectives

The Atlantic Slave Trade as Experienced by Different Groups

The Enslaved: For those seized and sold into the Atlantic trade, the experience was defined by terror, loss, and extreme physical suffering. The process began with violent capture and continued through the coffle march to the coast, the holding pen, the Middle Passage, and the auction block in the Americas. Enslaved people were separated from family, community, language group, and every familiar social relationship. Within these conditions, the enslaved built communities, maintained cultural practices, resisted in every available way, and found meaning in family, religion, and solidarity.
African Rulers and Merchants: For African rulers who participated in the slave trade, the experience was one of political calculation within structural constraints. The sale of captives generated revenue for firearms, textiles, and other goods that strengthened political power. But it also brought violence, destabilization, and demographic loss. Rulers like Afonso I of Kongo experienced the trade as a threat to their authority; rulers like the Alafin of Oyo experienced it as a source of power and revenue. Both experiences coexisted in the early modern African world.
European Traders and Investors: For European slave traders, ship captains, and investors, the trade was primarily an economic enterprise conducted in conditions of danger, disease, and moral compromise. Many traders rationalized the trade through racial ideology, religious argument, or simple economic pragmatism. Others were troubled by what they witnessed. John Newton, who captained slave ships in the 1750s and later became an evangelical Christian minister and abolitionist, wrote a searing account of the trade's horrors — but only after he had made his fortune from it.
African Communities Along Raiding Routes: For communities that were raided for captives but not directly engaged in the trade as sellers, the experience was one of extreme vulnerability and fear. Village communities along raiding routes might be attacked at any time. People hid, moved to defensible locations, and organized collective defense. The psychological and social effects of living under the constant threat of enslavement are difficult to reconstruct but clearly profound.
Cause and Effect

The Growth of the Atlantic Slave Trade

Long-Term Causes

African societies already had institutions of captivity mobilizable for Atlantic commerce; European maritime technology and financial organization made transoceanic commerce feasible; European colonization of the Americas created demand for coerced labor that Indigenous populations devastated by disease could not meet.

Short-Term Causes

The establishment of plantation agriculture — sugar in Brazil and the Caribbean from the late sixteenth century — created rapidly growing demand for large numbers of unfree laborers; the growth of tobacco, rice, and indigo economies in British North America added further demand.

The Event

African rulers and merchants organized capture, transport, and coastal sale of captives; European trading companies and private merchants organized shipping and sale at American destinations; the trade grew from thousands per year to tens of thousands, peaking at over 80,000 per year in the eighteenth century.

Long-Term Consequences

Depopulation of large areas of West and Central Africa; skewed gender ratios; destabilized political systems; militarized African politics; creation of African-Atlantic diaspora cultures in the Americas; plantation wealth financed European industrialization; lasting racial hierarchies and economic inequalities across the Atlantic world.

Historical complexity: The slave trade cannot be reduced to a single cause. It was the product of intersecting forces — European commercial ambition, American plantation demand, African political structures, global commercial organization — whose combined scale and consequences exceeded what any individual actor could have foreseen or intended.

Continuity and Change Over Time

African Trade Networks, 1400–1800

What Changed

  • The addition of the Atlantic dimension: direct oceanic connections to European and American markets
  • Introduction of new goods: European manufactured goods, American crops, firearms, and alcohol
  • Massive growth of the trade in enslaved people to American destinations, dwarfing previous participation in long-distance slave trading
  • New military competition driven by the unequal distribution of European firearms
  • Demographic losses at unprecedented scale in heavily affected regions

What Stayed the Same

  • African trade networks remained fundamentally organized around the exchange of complementary goods
  • The organization of trade through long-distance merchant communities and diasporas
  • The use of African currencies (cowrie shells, iron bars, cloth) alongside imported currencies
  • The control of trade by African political authorities who extracted duties and set terms
  • The trans-Saharan trade continued alongside the Atlantic trade; East African Indian Ocean commerce also continued

Why this pattern matters: The combination of continuity and change in African trade networks illustrates that the Atlantic slave trade, while enormously consequential, was layered onto rather than simply replacing older African trade systems. Africa's engagement with the Atlantic world was complex, simultaneous, and uneven — not a simple story of "before and after."

Everyday Life and Social History

A Kongolese Provincial Town in the Seventeenth Century

To understand the early modern Atlantic world from an African perspective, it helps to imagine life in a provincial Kongolese town in the mid-seventeenth century — after the kingdom had converted to Christianity and engaged with Portuguese trade, but before the catastrophic civil wars and the Battle of Mbwila (1665).

The town would have been organized around a central market, a church (churches had been built in Kongo from the early sixteenth century), and the compound of the mani (provincial lord). Most residents were farmers, growing millet, sorghum, yams, and — increasingly — maize and cassava introduced from the Americas. Fishing and hunting supplemented the agricultural diet. Women dominated agricultural labor and the local market; men were more involved in long-distance trade, military service, and political affairs.

The market was a center of economic and social life. Trade goods circulated using a currency of nzimbu shells and raffia cloth, which served as the Kongolese monetary system. Iron tools, pottery, palm wine, dried fish, salt, and cloth were among the common trade goods. Portuguese goods — cloth, glassware, alcohol, metal objects — were available through merchants who traveled between the coast and the interior, but they were not ubiquitous in everyday provincial life.

Christianity in the seventeenth-century Kongolese provinces was a blend of Christian doctrine and local spiritual practice — syncretism in the truest sense. The church was a significant institution, associated with literacy and royal authority. But traditional religious specialists (nganga) also operated in the community, healing illness, managing relationships with ancestors, and addressing spiritual threats. The slave trade intruded into this world in ways that were deeply disruptive. Families whose members were seized in raids had no legal redress; the Manikongo's attempts to regulate the trade were too distant and too politically constrained to offer effective protection. This everyday world — of markets, churches, farms, and families threatened by the violence of the slave trade — is the human reality that lies beneath the political and commercial history of early modern Africa.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 36 has examined Africa in the early modern era — roughly 1450 to 1800 — as a continent of dynamic, sophisticated, and diverse societies engaged with both internal African processes and the transformative forces of Atlantic commerce. The chapter has argued against two common distortions: the view that Africa was simply a passive victim of European predation, and the view that African participation in the slave trade reflects primarily African failure or moral culpability. The reality is more complex, and more interesting, than either distortion allows.

The major African kingdoms of this era — Kongo, Benin, Oyo, Asante, Songhai, Ethiopia — were genuine states with complex political systems, military organizations, trade networks, religious cultures, and artistic traditions. They governed large territories, administered diverse populations, and engaged with the wider world through long-established commercial routes. Their histories are not primarily stories of contact with Europe; they are stories of internal development, regional competition, political innovation, and cultural creativity that happened to intersect with European maritime expansion in consequential ways.

The Atlantic slave trade was the most consequential intersection. Growing from small beginnings in the mid-fifteenth century to a massive forced migration in the eighteenth century, the trade exported an estimated 12.5 million Africans across the Atlantic, of whom approximately 10.7 million survived to be sold in the Americas. The trade was driven by European demand — specifically the demand of plantation economies in Brazil, the Caribbean, and North America for coerced labor — and organized through a commercial system that involved African rulers and merchants as suppliers, European trading companies and private merchants as organizers and shippers, and American planters as ultimate buyers.

African rulers participated in the trade as active agents making calculated political decisions, but they did so within structural conditions — the "guns for slaves" dynamic, the competitive pressures of neighboring states — that severely constrained their choices. Some rulers, like Afonso I of Kongo, explicitly protested the trade and sought to limit it. Others built their power precisely on slave-trade revenues. Many occupied intermediate positions, engaging with the trade selectively while attempting to maintain social and political order in their kingdoms.

The demographic consequences of the trade were severe and lasting. The regions most heavily affected — West-Central Africa, the Bight of Benin, the Bight of Biafra — lost millions of people over the course of four centuries. Gender ratios were skewed, political stability undermined, agricultural communities disrupted, and cycles of raiding and violence set in motion. The long-term effects contributed to Africa's demographic and economic disadvantage entering the nineteenth century.

Yet the history of this era is not only a history of loss and suffering. African cultural practices crossed the Atlantic with the enslaved and survived in the Americas in remarkable ways — in religion, music, food, language, and social organization. The African diaspora built new cultures in the Americas that were simultaneously African and American, shaped by both the memory of African origins and the experience of American enslavement and resistance. Maroon communities in Brazil, Jamaica, and Suriname created free societies under conditions of extraordinary difficulty.

The history of Africa in the early modern era is essential to world history not as a chapter about European expansion but as a chapter about African societies — their power, their resilience, their participation in global commerce, their suffering under the Atlantic slave trade, and their contributions to the cultures of the Atlantic world. Africa's early modern history is not a prelude to colonialism. It is a major chapter in the history of human civilization, on its own terms.

Key Terms

Manikongo
The title of the king of the Kingdom of Kongo; the paramount political authority in the kingdom's hierarchical system.
Atlantic slave trade
The forced transportation of approximately 12.5 million enslaved Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas between approximately 1501 and 1867; the largest forced migration in human history.
Middle Passage
The ocean crossing from West Africa to the Americas on slave ships; characterized by extreme overcrowding, disease, and high mortality.
Barracoon
A coastal holding facility where enslaved people were kept while awaiting transport on slave ships; mortality in barracoons was high due to disease and poor conditions.
Coffle
A group of enslaved people chained or roped together for transport on the overland march from the interior to the coast; many died on these marches.
Oba
The title of the king of the Kingdom of Benin; a figure of both political and spiritual authority.
Griot
A professional oral historian, genealogist, and musician in West African societies; keeper of historical memory in societies without centralized written archives.
Asantehene
The title of the paramount ruler of the Asante Kingdom; the king who exercised authority over the confederation of Akan chieftaincies.
Golden Stool (Sika Dwa)
The sacred throne of Asante, believed to contain the spiritual essence of the Asante nation; never sat upon; central symbol of Asante identity and sovereignty.
Triangular trade
The three-legged commercial system linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas: European goods to Africa, enslaved Africans to the Americas, plantation goods to Europe.
Chattel slavery
A system of slavery in which enslaved people are legally defined as movable property; the form practiced in the Americas differed from most African forms of captivity in its absolute commodification of persons and hereditary transmission of status.
Alafin
The title of the king of the Oyo Empire; a figure of both sacred and political authority.
Oyo Mesi
The council of seven leading chiefs of the Oyo Empire with significant powers to check the Alafin's authority.
Guns for slaves
The commercial and military dynamic in which firearms were traded for enslaved people; states that acquired firearms used them to raid for captives, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.
Maroon community
A community of escaped enslaved people who established independent settlements beyond colonial authority; examples include Palmares in Brazil and the Maroon communities of Jamaica.
Syncretism
The blending of different religious or cultural traditions; visible in Kongolese Christianity, which combined Catholic doctrine with local spiritual practices.
Lançado / Tangomão
Portuguese terms for individuals (often of mixed African-Portuguese descent) who lived and traded in African communities, serving as commercial intermediaries.
Cowrie shells
Small shells used as currency across West Africa and widely exchanged in the Atlantic trade.
Zemene Mesafint
"Era of the Princes"; a period in Ethiopian history (c. 1769–1855) in which real power shifted from the emperor to regional noble families, leaving the emperor a sacred figurehead.
Kebra Nagast
"Glory of Kings"; a fourteenth-century Ethiopian court epic elaborating the dynastic mythology connecting the Solomonic dynasty to Solomon and the Queen of Sheba.
Igun eronmwon
The royal guild of bronze and brass casters in the Kingdom of Benin, responsible for producing the Benin bronzes under royal commission.
Abolitionism
The political and moral movement to end slavery and the slave trade; gained momentum in Britain from the 1780s; led to British abolition of the slave trade in 1807.
Royal African Company
An English joint-stock company that held a monopoly on the British slave trade from 1672 to 1698; its end of monopoly opened the trade to private merchants and dramatically expanded British slave trading.
Diaspora
A dispersed population living outside its original homeland; the African diaspora refers to enslaved Africans and their descendants in the Americas.
Elmina
Portuguese trading fort established on the Gold Coast in 1482; the first major European commercial installation in sub-Saharan Africa; later a center of the slave trade.

Identification & Significance

Afonso I (Manikongo, r. c. 1509–1542)
King of Kongo who converted to Christianity, maintained extensive diplomatic correspondence with Portugal, and attempted to limit uncontrolled slave raiding in his kingdom; his letters are among the most important primary sources for early Atlantic history.

Emperor of Songhai who expanded the empire to its greatest extent, made a famous pilgrimage to Mecca, and patronized Islamic scholarship in Timbuktu.

Founder of the Asante Kingdom who unified Akan chieftaincies using the Golden Stool as a symbol of national unity.

Igbo-born writer and abolitionist who was enslaved as a child, eventually purchased his freedom, and wrote one of the most important firsthand accounts of the Atlantic slave trade.
Kingdom of Kongo
A large Central African kingdom south of the Congo River; converted to Christianity in the late fifteenth century; deeply involved in the Atlantic slave trade; fragmented after the Battle of Mbwila (1665).
Kingdom of Benin
A forest kingdom in what is now southern Nigeria, governed by obas, famous for its bronze and ivory artworks; engaged selectively with European trade while maintaining strong political control.
Oyo Empire
A large Yoruba-speaking empire in what is now southwestern Nigeria; built on cavalry power; a major participant in the Atlantic slave trade; collapsed in the early nineteenth century.
Asante Kingdom
A forest kingdom on the Gold Coast (now Ghana) that rose to power in the early eighteenth century using gold wealth, firearms, and military organization.
Songhai Empire
The largest West African empire, centered on the Niger bend; at its height under Askia Muhammad; destroyed by a Moroccan invasion in 1591.
Ethiopia (Solomonic Dynasty)
One of the world's oldest Christian monarchies; survived the sixteenth-century crisis of the Adal invasion; entered the Zemene Mesafint period in the late eighteenth century.
Timbuktu
A major city on the Niger bend; center of Islamic scholarship, trans-Saharan trade, and manuscript production; a symbol of West Africa's intellectual traditions.
Palmares
A confederation of escaped enslaved people in northeastern Brazil that survived most of the seventeenth century; symbol of African resistance to Atlantic slavery.
Cape Coast Castle
A major British slave-trading fort on the Gold Coast (now Ghana); one of the largest slave-holding facilities on the African coast; now a UNESCO World Heritage Site and museum.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has placed Africa at the center of early modern world history — not as a peripheral supplier to European enterprise, but as a continent of powerful states, complex cultures, and active historical agency whose engagement with Atlantic commerce was one of the defining events of the modern era. The history examined here challenges some of the most persistent simplifications in world history: the idea that Africa lacked complex states before European colonization, the idea that African participation in the slave trade was simply a form of self-betrayal, and the idea that the enslaved were passive victims rather than active resisters and cultural survivors.

The kingdoms of Kongo, Benin, Oyo, Asante, Songhai, and Ethiopia were genuinely impressive political achievements. They governed large territories, maintained complex institutions, produced sophisticated art and scholarship, and engaged with the wider world through long-established trade networks. Their histories deserve to be studied on their own terms, not only in relation to European expansion. When we place these kingdoms at the center of the story — rather than treating them as background to a European narrative — we see a very different early modern world: one in which African political authority was real, African commercial initiative was significant, and African cultural creativity was remarkable.

The Atlantic slave trade does not erase these achievements, but it profoundly complicates them. The trade imposed devastating costs on African populations — demographic, political, social, and economic costs that shaped Africa's historical trajectory for centuries. It also involved African rulers and merchants as active participants, raising questions about agency, responsibility, and complicity that cannot be resolved by simple formulas. The honest answer is that African participation in the trade was both real and structurally constrained — that African rulers made choices, and that those choices were made within a global system of power and demand that they did not create and could not individually escape.

The enslaved themselves deserve special attention in any honest account of this history. They were not passive victims. They resisted at every stage of the journey from capture to sale. They built communities in the Americas that preserved African cultural practices across generations of oppression. They created new African-Atlantic cultures — in music, religion, food, language, and social organization — that transformed the Americas and, through the Americas, the world. The African diaspora is not simply a history of suffering; it is also a history of remarkable human resilience, creativity, and survival.

As students move forward in this textbook — into the chapters on the Industrial Revolution, European imperialism, the scramble for Africa, and the struggles for decolonization — they should carry with them the history examined in this chapter: the memory of African political power and cultural achievement before colonialism, the understanding of how the Atlantic slave trade created the conditions for Africa's later vulnerability, and the awareness that the people of Africa have always been historical actors, not merely the objects of other people's history. Africa's early modern history is not a prelude to colonialism. It is a major chapter in the history of human civilization, on its own terms.

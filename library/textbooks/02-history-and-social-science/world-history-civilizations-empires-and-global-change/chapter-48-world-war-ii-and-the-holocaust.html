World War II and the Holocaust

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The Second World War was not merely the largest armed conflict in human history. It was a catastrophe that remade the political, demographic, and moral landscape of the entire world — and in doing so, forced humanity to confront questions about power, responsibility, and evil that remain unresolved."

In the summer of 1942, a teenage girl named Anne Frank and her family went into hiding in a concealed apartment above a warehouse in Amsterdam, the Netherlands. They had been living under German occupation since 1940, and the deportations of Jewish people to camps in the east had made remaining in the open impossible for Jewish families. For two years, Anne Frank wrote in a diary, recording her thoughts, fears, dreams, and observations about the strange compressed world of the hiding place and the enormous catastrophe unfolding beyond its walls. In August 1944, the hiding place was betrayed to the German authorities. Anne and her family were deported to concentration camps. She died of typhus in Bergen-Belsen in February or March 1945, weeks before the camp was liberated by British forces. She was fifteen years old. Her diary, discovered after the war and published by her surviving father, became one of the most widely read documents about the Holocaust and about the human cost of the war that produced it.

Anne Frank's story is one of tens of millions. World War II, which engulfed virtually every inhabited region of the globe between 1939 and 1945, produced casualty figures so large as to strain comprehension. Estimates of total deaths range from seventy to eighty-five million people — soldiers killed in battle, civilians killed in bombings and sieges, people murdered by occupying powers, victims of disease and famine caused by war, and the approximately six million Jewish people systematically murdered in the Holocaust, along with millions of others — Roma, disabled people, Soviet prisoners of war, political opponents, gay people — killed in the same apparatus of industrial genocide. No other conflict in recorded history approached this scale of destruction.

The war had deep roots in the unresolved tensions of the period that followed World War I. The punishing terms of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles, the economic catastrophe of the Great Depression, the rise of fascism and ultranationalism in Italy, Germany, and Japan, and the failure of international institutions to restrain aggression combined to produce a world that was primed for catastrophic conflict by the mid-1930s. When Germany invaded Poland in September 1939, it triggered the formal beginning of the European war. But war in Asia had already been underway since at least 1937, when Japan launched a full-scale invasion of China, and by some accounts since 1931, when Japan seized Manchuria. The war was truly global from the beginning — or rather, from multiple beginnings in different parts of the world that gradually merged into a single interconnected conflict.

This chapter examines World War II as a global historical event, studying the causes of the conflict, the military campaigns in Europe and Asia, the experience of occupied peoples, the nature of collaboration and resistance, the genocide of the Jewish people and other persecuted groups, the conduct of total war against civilian populations, the use of atomic weapons against Japan, and the aftermath of victory for both the victors and the vanquished. The chapter approaches the war from multiple regional perspectives, centering not only the military and political history of great powers but the experience of ordinary people — soldiers, civilians, prisoners, refugees, resisters, collaborators, survivors — who lived through the most destructive event in human history.

The Holocaust occupies a central and irreducible place in this chapter. It was not simply an accompaniment to the war; it was a project of industrial mass murder carried out deliberately and systematically by the German state and its collaborators, using techniques of bureaucratic organization, industrial technology, and ideological dehumanization to murder an entire people. Understanding the Holocaust requires understanding the ideologies that made it possible, the mechanisms by which it was carried out, the roles played by perpetrators and bystanders and rescuers, and the devastating scale of what was lost. It also requires grappling with the difficult question of how ordinary human beings and ordinary states came to participate in, enable, or fail to prevent mass murder on this scale.

The war also accelerated the transformation of warfare itself. The deliberate bombing of civilian cities — from the German bombing of Guernica and Warsaw to the Allied bombing of Dresden and Tokyo to the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki — represented a new logic of total war in which the distinction between combatant and civilian became increasingly blurred or deliberately erased. This transformation had profound implications not only for the conduct of World War II but for every subsequent conflict and for the ethics of war that remain contested to the present day.

Understanding World War II is essential to understanding the world that followed it. The postwar international order — the United Nations, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the Nuremberg principles establishing individual responsibility for crimes against humanity, the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union, the decolonization of Asia and Africa, the establishment of the state of Israel, the division of Germany, Korea, and Vietnam — all grew directly from the wreckage of the war and the settlements, tensions, and moral reckonings that followed it. The world of the twenty-first century remains shaped by decisions made in the years between 1939 and 1945, and by the horror of what those years revealed about the potential of human societies to commit and to suffer acts of extraordinary destruction and cruelty.

The evidence historians use to study World War II is extraordinarily rich: military records, government documents, diplomatic cables, personal diaries and letters, photographs and film, survivor testimonies, war crimes trial transcripts, archaeological evidence from former camp sites, and the memories of those who lived through the conflict. This abundance of evidence makes the war among the most thoroughly documented events in human history, even as the sheer scale of the destruction and the subjective intensity of individual suffering resist full historical comprehension. The historian's task is to hold both dimensions simultaneously: the large-scale analysis of causes, systems, and consequences, and the irreducible particularity of individual human lives lost and transformed.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the long-term and short-term causes of World War II, distinguishing between the European and Asian theaters of the conflict and identifying the structural conditions that made global war possible.
  2. Trace the major phases of Axis expansion in Europe and Asia from the mid-1930s through the early years of the war, identifying the key military campaigns and political decisions that shaped the conflict's trajectory.
  3. Describe the principal military campaigns on the European and Pacific fronts, explaining the turning points that shifted the balance of the war toward the Allied powers.
  4. Analyze the experience of occupied peoples across Europe and Asia, distinguishing between collaboration, accommodation, and active resistance and explaining the conditions that shaped these different responses.
  5. Explain the Holocaust as a systematic program of genocide carried out by the Nazi state and its collaborators, identifying the stages of persecution, the mechanisms of mass murder, and the scale of destruction.
  6. Evaluate the conduct of total war against civilian populations, including area bombing campaigns and the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, and assess the historical debates about their justification and consequences.
  7. Identify the major war crimes trials conducted after the war's end, explaining the principles they established and their significance for international law and human rights.
  8. Compare the experiences of different groups during the war — soldiers, civilians, prisoners, refugees, resisters, and survivors — and explain how race, nationality, gender, and occupation shaped those experiences.
  9. Assess the immediate and long-term consequences of World War II for the political, demographic, and moral order of the world.
  10. Contextualize World War II within the broader patterns of twentieth-century global history, connecting it to the consequences of World War I, the interwar crisis, and the Cold War that followed.
  11. Interpret primary sources from the war period — including official documents, personal testimonies, photographs, and trial records — as evidence of the causes, conduct, and human cost of the conflict.
  12. Synthesize the history of the war across multiple regional perspectives, avoiding a solely Eurocentric or American-centric account of a genuinely global conflict.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. How did the unresolved tensions of the post-World War I settlement, the Great Depression, and the rise of fascism combine to produce a second global war within a generation?
  2. What distinguished the war in Asia — its origins, chronology, and conduct — from the war in Europe, and how did the two theaters eventually merge into a single global conflict?
  3. How did the experience of occupation shape the lives of ordinary people in conquered territories, and what determined whether individuals and communities resisted, collaborated, or simply tried to survive?
  4. How did the Holocaust happen — what ideologies, institutions, technologies, and human decisions made the systematic murder of six million Jewish people and millions of others possible?
  5. What was total war, and how did World War II transform the relationship between military force and civilian populations in ways that the laws of war had not anticipated?
  6. How should historians and citizens evaluate the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki — as a military necessity that shortened the war, as a war crime against civilians, or as something more complex than either position allows?
  7. What precedents did the Nuremberg and Tokyo war crimes trials set, and how did they reshape international law and the concept of individual responsibility for mass atrocity?
  8. How did World War II accelerate processes of decolonization, reshape the global balance of power, and create the conditions for the Cold War that dominated the second half of the twentieth century?
  9. How do historians use testimony, documentary evidence, and material evidence to reconstruct events as horrific as the Holocaust, and what are the ethical responsibilities that come with this work?
  10. In what ways does the legacy of World War II continue to shape international politics, human rights law, collective memory, and cultural life in the twenty-first century?

Historical Context

World War II cannot be understood without understanding the world that produced it — a world shaped by the aftermath of World War I, the fragility of the interwar international order, the catastrophic economic dislocation of the Great Depression, and the political radicalization that produced fascism, ultranationalism, and totalitarianism across much of Europe and Asia. Chapter 47 examined the interwar period in detail; this chapter builds directly on that foundation, showing how the conditions of the 1920s and 1930s produced the catastrophe of the 1939–1945 conflict.

The Paris Peace Settlement of 1919–1920 — which included the Treaty of Versailles with Germany and a series of related treaties with the other defeated Central Powers — had attempted to reorganize Europe and the Middle East on the principle of national self-determination while also punishing the states held responsible for the war. The result was a set of arrangements that satisfied almost no one. Germany was stripped of territory, required to pay enormous reparations, limited in its military capacity, and forced to accept the "war guilt" clause assigning it legal and moral responsibility for the conflict. German nationalists, including the young Adolf Hitler, who had fought in the war and experienced Germany's defeat as a profound psychological as well as political wound, rejected the settlement entirely and devoted themselves to overturning it.

The League of Nations, created at Versailles to provide a mechanism for peaceful resolution of international disputes, was hobbled from the beginning by the refusal of the United States Senate to ratify American membership — leaving the new international organization without the participation of the world's emerging superpower. The League proved unable to respond effectively to Japanese aggression in Manchuria in 1931, Italian aggression in Ethiopia in 1935, or German remilitarization of the Rhineland in 1936. Each unanswered act of aggression emboldened the aggressors and demonstrated the weakness of the international order.

The Great Depression, which struck in 1929 and deepened through the early 1930s, produced mass unemployment, political polarization, and the collapse of democratic governance across much of Europe. It intensified existing social tensions and gave movements of the extreme right — particularly in Germany and Italy — a mass base they had previously lacked. Adolf Hitler's Nazi Party, which had been a marginal political force in the mid-1920s, exploited the economic crisis, widespread resentment of the Versailles settlement, fear of communism, and deeply rooted antisemitism to win electoral support and seize power in Germany in January 1933. Once in power, Hitler moved quickly to consolidate totalitarian control, rearm Germany, and pursue the expansionist foreign policy he had outlined in his political manifesto Mein Kampf.

In Asia, Japan had emerged from World War I as a major power but felt frustrated by its exclusion from the inner circle of great-power decision-making, by Western immigration restrictions against Japanese migrants, and by its dependence on imported raw materials — particularly oil, steel, and rubber — that its resource-poor home islands could not supply. Military and ultranationalist factions within the Japanese government increasingly dominated policy in the 1930s, pursuing aggressive expansion into China and Southeast Asia as a solution to Japan's strategic vulnerabilities. Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931 and full-scale war against China beginning in 1937 established a pattern of military expansionism that would eventually bring Japan into direct conflict with the United States and the European colonial powers.

By the mid-1930s, then, the world contained three revisionist powers — Germany, Italy, and Japan — that were committed to overturning the existing international order and willing to use military force to do so. The democracies — Britain, France, and the United States — were uncertain in their responses, divided in their domestic politics, and reluctant to risk another catastrophic war. The policy of appeasement, which sought to satisfy Axis demands through negotiation and concession, culminated in the 1938 Munich Agreement, in which Britain and France allowed Germany to annex the Sudetenland region of Czechoslovakia in exchange for Hitler's promise of no further territorial demands — a promise he broke within months. The Soviet Union, under Joseph Stalin, had its own calculations, ultimately concluding a non-aggression pact with Germany in August 1939 that freed Hitler to attack Poland without fear of a two-front war.

Germany invaded Poland on September 1, 1939. Britain and France declared war on Germany two days later. Within a year, Germany had conquered Poland, Denmark, Norway, the Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, and France, and had entered into an alliance system with Italy and Japan. The world's most destructive conflict had begun, and it would continue for nearly six more years before the surrender of Germany in May 1945 and Japan in September 1945.

Throughout the conflict, the war was also being fought on an ideological plane. Nazi Germany framed the war in explicitly racial terms: a struggle of the Aryan master race to dominate or destroy inferior peoples, with Jews identified as the supreme enemy of humanity. Japan framed its expansion as the liberation of Asia from Western colonialism through the leadership of a superior Japanese civilization. The Allied powers framed their struggle as a defense of democracy and freedom — framing that was complicated by the presence among the Allies of the Soviet Union under Stalin, itself a totalitarian state responsible for mass political violence, and by the continuation of European colonial empires that denied democratic rights to hundreds of millions of people.

Regional Orientation

World War II was fought across an extraordinary geographic span. Understanding the war's regional geography is essential to understanding its causes, conduct, and consequences.

Europe was the site of the war's political origins and many of its most intense military campaigns. Central to the European theater was the contest between Germany and the Soviet Union on the Eastern Front — the largest land campaign in military history, stretching from the Arctic Ocean to the Black Sea across thousands of miles of Soviet territory. The Western Front, which opened with the Allied invasion of North Africa in 1942 and the Italian campaign in 1943 before the Normandy landings of June 1944, was geographically smaller but strategically critical. The Mediterranean theater connected European operations to the Middle East and North Africa. Within occupied Europe, the German system of control, exploitation, and genocide extended from the Atlantic coast to the edge of the Caucasus, creating a continental-scale apparatus of terror and mass murder.

East Asia and the Pacific formed the other major theater of the war. China was the site of the longest and in some respects most destructive campaign of the war, as Japanese forces occupied large portions of eastern China while Nationalist and Communist Chinese forces fought to resist. Southeast Asia — including French Indochina, Malaya, Burma, the Dutch East Indies, and the Philippines — fell rapidly to Japanese forces in late 1941 and 1942, creating the "Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere" that Japan proclaimed as an alternative to Western colonial domination. The Pacific Ocean itself became the site of an enormous naval and island-hopping campaign as American forces gradually advanced toward the Japanese home islands. The Soviet Union entered the Pacific war in August 1945, invading Japanese-held Manchuria and Korea.

North Africa and the Middle East were strategically important for their oil resources and the Suez Canal, and became a theater of significant military operations as British and Commonwealth forces contested control of Egypt and Libya against German and Italian forces under Erwin Rommel before the Allied victory at El Alamein in 1942.

The Atlantic Ocean was the crucial logistical link between North America and Britain, contested in the Battle of the Atlantic as German submarines sought to cut British supply lines and American convoys fought to keep them open. The Indian Ocean connected British forces in the Middle East to India, Australia, and East Africa, and was briefly threatened by Japanese naval power in 1942.

The home fronts — the industrial economies of the major powers — were themselves theaters of the conflict, as governments mobilized entire societies for war production, governments imposed rationing and labor direction, and bombers brought the violence of the front directly into civilian life. The United States, protected from direct attack by two oceans after Pearl Harbor, became the "arsenal of democracy," producing war material on a scale that none of its allies or enemies could match.

Section 48.1

Causes of World War II

World War II had no single cause. It emerged from the intersection of long-term structural tensions and short-term political decisions made by specific individuals and governments in specific moments of crisis. Understanding the war requires understanding both the deep historical forces that created a world vulnerable to catastrophic conflict and the choices that ultimately produced it.

The most fundamental long-term cause was the failure of the post-World War I settlement to create a stable international order. The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh terms on Germany — including the loss of approximately 13 percent of its prewar territory and 10 percent of its population, the reduction of its army to 100,000 men, the prohibition of an air force, the payment of enormous reparations, and the acceptance of the "war guilt" clause — without integrating Germany as a legitimate participant in the postwar international system. German nationalists across the political spectrum rejected the settlement as a humiliation, and the democratic Weimar Republic that accepted it was politically delegitimized by its association with defeat. The seeds of revisionist nationalism that Hitler would harvest were planted at Versailles.

The Great Depression accelerated the crisis. Beginning with the American stock market crash of October 1929, the Depression spread rapidly through the interconnected global economy, producing mass unemployment — reaching 25 to 30 percent of the workforce in the hardest-hit countries — bank failures, the collapse of international trade, and political radicalization. In Germany, where reparations payments had already strained the economy and hyperinflation in 1923 had wiped out middle-class savings, the Depression produced genuine social desperation that the Nazi Party exploited effectively. Hitler promised to restore German greatness, provide jobs, and reverse the humiliations of Versailles. His electoral support surged during the Depression years, and by January 1933 he had been appointed Chancellor through a combination of electoral success and elite political miscalculation.

The rise of fascism and ultranationalism was a cause that went beyond Germany alone. Benito Mussolini had established the world's first fascist state in Italy in the early 1920s, using paramilitaries, intimidation, and eventually legal measures to consolidate dictatorial power. Mussolini pursued colonial expansion in Libya and Ethiopia and provided ideological and practical inspiration for Hitler. Japan's ultranationalist military factions, while operating within a different cultural and political tradition, shared the fascist contempt for liberal democracy, the glorification of military expansion, and the belief that their nation deserved imperial dominance over weaker peoples. The coordination between Germany, Italy, and Japan — formalized in the 1940 Tripartite Pact — created a revisionist coalition with genuinely global reach.

The failure of collective security was a direct cause of the war's outbreak. The League of Nations proved unable to deter or punish aggression. When Japan seized Manchuria in 1931, the League condemned the action but imposed no effective sanctions, and Japan simply withdrew from the League. When Italy invaded Ethiopia in 1935, the League applied sanctions but exempted oil — the resource Mussolini most needed — and the sanctions were eventually lifted. Germany remilitarized the Rhineland in 1936 without triggering a military response from France or Britain, even though such a response would almost certainly have succeeded. Each failure to enforce collective security emboldened the aggressors and demoralized the defenders of the existing order.

Appeasement — the British and French policy of making concessions to Hitler's territorial demands in hopes of satisfying his ambitions and avoiding war — was motivated by genuine fears of repeating the catastrophe of World War I, by awareness of military unpreparedness, by some sympathy for German grievances about Versailles, and by the belief of some British elites that a strong Germany could serve as a barrier against Soviet communism. The policy culminated in the Munich Agreement of September 1938, in which British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain and French Premier Edouard Daladier agreed to allow Germany to annex the Sudetenland region of Czechoslovakia in exchange for Hitler's assurance that he had no further territorial ambitions in Europe. When Germany occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia in March 1939, the bankruptcy of appeasement became impossible to deny.

The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact of August 1939 was the last crucial enabling condition. When Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union — ideological enemies — concluded a non-aggression pact with secret protocols dividing Eastern Europe into spheres of influence, Hitler secured his eastern flank and Stalin gained time for Soviet military preparation. The pact eliminated the prospect of a two-front war that had helped defeat Germany in World War I, making Hitler's invasion of Poland strategically viable. Germany invaded Poland on September 1, 1939; Britain and France declared war on September 3. The war in Europe had begun.

In Asia, the origins of war were somewhat different in chronology but similar in logic. Japan's expansionism grew from a combination of resource needs, ideological nationalism, military dominance of civilian government, and perceived exclusion from the club of great powers. The Manchurian invasion of 1931, the escalation to full-scale war against China in 1937, and the Japanese government's decision in 1941 to expand into Southeast Asia — knowing that this would likely bring war with the United States — reflected a strategic gamble that Japan's military could establish a resource-sufficient empire before American industrial power could be brought fully to bear. The attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941 brought the United States into a war that was already global in scope, completing the merger of the European and Asian conflicts into a single worldwide struggle.

Historical Vocabulary
Genocide

Genocide is the deliberate and systematic destruction, in whole or in part, of a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group. The term was coined by the jurist Raphael Lemkin in 1944 specifically to describe what the Nazis were doing to Jewish people and other groups, and was incorporated into international law in the 1948 United Nations Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide.

Genocide differs from other forms of mass violence in its intent: it aims not merely to defeat or suppress a group but to eliminate its existence as a distinct community. This can involve killing, but also includes forcible removal of children, preventing births, destroying the conditions of life, and causing serious bodily or mental harm. Understanding genocide as a distinct category of crime helps historians and policymakers identify warning signs, understand perpetrators' motivations, and construct frameworks for prevention and accountability.

The Holocaust is recognized as the paradigmatic case of genocide in twentieth-century history, but it is not the only one. Other cases studied by scholars include the Armenian Genocide of 1915–1923, the Cambodian genocide under the Khmer Rouge (1975–1979), and the Rwandan genocide of 1994.

Section 48.2

Axis Expansion in Europe and Asia

The early years of World War II were marked by dramatic Axis military success. Germany's new style of warfare — rapid, combined-arms operations using tanks, motorized infantry, and close air support to break through defensive lines and exploit breakthroughs before defenders could regroup — was devastatingly effective against the military doctrines and forces it first encountered. This approach, later called Blitzkrieg ("lightning war") by observers and journalists, had been developed from lessons of World War I and refined in the Spanish Civil War (1936–1939), where German forces had intervened on behalf of Francisco Franco's nationalist forces.

Germany's invasion of Poland in September 1939 demonstrated the new method. While the Wehrmacht advanced from the west, the Soviet Union invaded from the east under the secret protocols of the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact. Poland — fighting on two fronts against two great powers, with limited mechanized forces and no effective outside assistance — was overrun in about five weeks. Warsaw fell on September 28, 1939. Poland was divided between Germany and the Soviet Union. The Polish government and military went into exile, eventually establishing a government-in-exile in London and continuing to contribute forces to the Allied cause, but the Polish state as it had existed since 1918 had been destroyed.

After a pause — the "Phoney War" from October 1939 to April 1940, during which France and Britain prepared defensively but Germany prepared its next offensive — Germany struck northward and westward simultaneously in April and May 1940. Denmark surrendered in a day; Norway fell after several weeks of fighting in which British forces failed to hold the country against German airborne and seaborne assault. Then, in May 1940, Germany launched its assault on France and the Low Countries. By attacking through the Ardennes forest — which the French had considered impassable for armored forces and therefore left lightly defended — German tanks cut off and surrounded the bulk of the Allied forces in Belgium. The evacuation of over 330,000 British and French troops from the beaches of Dunkirk was a remarkable improvised operation but a military catastrophe. France requested an armistice on June 22, 1940, barely six weeks after the German offensive began. It was one of the most stunning military collapses in modern history.

The fall of France transformed the strategic situation entirely. Britain now stood essentially alone against Germany in Europe. Winston Churchill, who had replaced Neville Chamberlain as Prime Minister in May 1940, refused to negotiate and committed Britain to continued resistance. Germany's inability to force Britain from the war was partly a strategic choice — Hitler expected Britain to negotiate once it recognized the hopelessness of its position — and partly a military failure. The Battle of Britain (July–October 1940), in which the German Luftwaffe attempted to destroy the Royal Air Force as a prelude to invasion, was the first significant German defeat of the war. British fighter pilots, aided by radar technology and the intelligence advantage provided by breaking German codes, inflicted unsustainable losses on the Luftwaffe and forced Germany to abandon its invasion plans.

Germany then pivoted eastward. The invasion of the Soviet Union, codenamed Operation Barbarossa, began on June 22, 1941, with three million German troops advancing across a front stretching from the Baltic to the Black Sea. It was the largest invasion in history. Hitler expected the Soviet Union to collapse quickly — "kick in the rotten door," as he put it — but Soviet resistance, though initially catastrophically overwhelmed, did not break. German forces advanced hundreds of miles into Soviet territory, besieging Leningrad (now St. Petersburg), approaching Moscow, and capturing over three million Soviet prisoners in the war's first months. The German treatment of Soviet prisoners was deliberately murderous: of the 5.7 million Soviet soldiers captured by Germany during the war, approximately 3.3 million died from starvation, exposure, and deliberate killing — a staggering atrocity that rivaled the Holocaust in scale and reflected the explicitly racial character of Nazi ideology regarding Slavic peoples.

In Asia, Japan's expansion proceeded with equal speed. After the attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, Japanese forces simultaneously struck the Philippines, Malaya, Hong Kong, and Guam. Within months, they had conquered Malaya and Singapore (February 1942), the Dutch East Indies (March 1942), Burma (May 1942), and the Philippines (May 1942). The fall of Singapore — a British fortress with a garrison of 130,000 troops, the largest British military surrender in history — was a profound shock to the prestige of European colonialism throughout Asia. Japanese forces were stopped at the Battle of Coral Sea (May 1942) and decisively defeated at the Battle of Midway (June 1942), which destroyed four Japanese fleet carriers and their experienced aircrew and ended Japan's ability to take the strategic offensive in the Pacific. But at the height of Axis power in mid-1942, Germany and Japan together controlled or threatened territory from Western Europe to the mid-Pacific, an almost unimaginable geographic extent.

Italy's role was significant but also demonstrated the limits of Axis cohesion. Mussolini had entered the war in June 1940, expecting quick victory after France fell, but Italian military performance was repeatedly disappointing. Italian forces in North Africa were saved from total defeat only by the intervention of German forces under Rommel. Italy's alliance with Germany was driven more by Mussolini's ideological affinity and opportunism than by military or strategic coordination. The Axis powers never achieved the degree of strategic coordination that the Allied coalition eventually did, and the structural tensions between German racial ideology and Japanese imperial ambitions complicated cooperation across theaters.

Section 48.3

War in Europe

The war in Europe had several interlocking theaters — the Eastern Front between Germany and the Soviet Union, the North African and Mediterranean theater, the Western Front after the Normandy landings, and the strategic air war over Germany — each of which contributed to Germany's eventual defeat. Of these, the Eastern Front was overwhelmingly the most destructive and strategically decisive. This is a dimension of the war that Western accounts have sometimes underemphasized, given the prominence of the Normandy landings and the Western Allied advance in popular memory. But the numbers are unambiguous: approximately 80 percent of Germany's military casualties were suffered on the Eastern Front, and the great battles of Stalingrad, Kursk, and the Soviet advance through Poland and into Germany were the military events that determined the war's outcome.

The turning point on the Eastern Front came at Stalingrad. In the summer of 1942, Germany launched a major offensive to capture the oilfields of the Caucasus and the industrial city of Stalingrad on the Volga River. The battle for Stalingrad, which became one of the most brutal and symbolically charged contests of the war, devolved into close-quarters urban combat among the ruins of the shattered city. In November 1942, the Soviet Army launched Operation Uranus, encircling the German Sixth Army — approximately 300,000 troops — in a massive double envelopment. Despite Hitler's orders to hold, the trapped German forces eventually surrendered in February 1943. The battle cost Germany approximately 800,000 casualties (including its Axis allies fighting alongside German forces) and cost the Soviet Union approximately 1.1 million. After Stalingrad, Germany never regained the strategic initiative on the Eastern Front.

The Battle of Kursk in July 1943 — the largest tank battle in history — confirmed the shift. Germany's attempt to eliminate a Soviet salient at Kursk through a pincer attack was anticipated by Soviet intelligence, and the German offensive was halted after enormous losses. The Soviets then launched the largest offensive operation of the war, beginning the long westward advance that would eventually reach Berlin. The Soviet military machine, which had been nearly destroyed in 1941, had been rebuilt through enormous sacrifice and American Lend-Lease assistance (providing vehicles, food, communications equipment, and raw materials) into the most powerful land army on earth.

In North Africa, British forces under General Bernard Montgomery defeated German and Italian forces under Erwin Rommel at the Second Battle of El Alamein in October–November 1942. American forces entered the African theater with the Operation Torch landings in Morocco and Algeria in November 1942. The last Axis forces in North Africa surrendered in May 1943. The Allies then invaded Sicily in July 1943 and the Italian mainland in September, triggering Mussolini's overthrow and Italy's armistice. But Germany occupied northern and central Italy and fought a tenacious rearguard action up the Italian peninsula; Rome was not liberated until June 1944, and fighting continued in Italy until Germany's final surrender.

The Western Front opened in earnest with Operation Overlord, the Allied invasion of Normandy on June 6, 1944 — D-Day — the largest amphibious operation in history. Over 150,000 troops were landed on five beaches in northern France, supported by thousands of aircraft and hundreds of naval vessels. Despite fierce German resistance, particularly at Omaha Beach where American casualties were severe, the landings succeeded and a beachhead was established. Allied forces broke out of Normandy in July and August 1944, liberated Paris in August, and by September had reached the German border. A German counterattack in the Ardennes in December 1944 — the Battle of the Bulge, the largest battle fought by U.S. forces in the war — temporarily halted the Allied advance but was ultimately defeated. By spring 1945, Allied forces were advancing into Germany from west and east simultaneously. Hitler committed suicide in his bunker in Berlin on April 30, 1945. Germany signed an unconditional surrender on May 8, 1945 — Victory in Europe Day.

The strategic bombing campaign against Germany and German-occupied Europe was a continuous feature of the war from 1940 onward. British and American bombing strategists believed that destroying Germany's industrial capacity and civilian morale could win the war without the catastrophic infantry losses of World War I. In practice, German industrial production continued to rise until late 1944, partly because the German economy had not been fully mobilized for war before the bombing began, and partly because the German war economy proved more resilient than expected. The moral and strategic dimensions of area bombing — particularly the fire-bombing of German cities like Hamburg (July 1943) and Dresden (February 1945) — are examined more fully in Section 48.7.

Historical Vocabulary
Total War

Total war refers to a form of conflict in which belligerent states mobilize all available resources — economic, industrial, agricultural, scientific, technological, and human — for the war effort, and in which the distinction between military combatants and civilian noncombatants is significantly eroded or deliberately ignored. In total war, industrial facilities, transportation networks, food supplies, and civilian morale become legitimate military targets, and the populations of enemy states are regarded as part of the enemy's war-making capacity.

World War II was the most extreme example of total war in history. All the major belligerent states organized their economies for war production on a massive scale. Governments directed labor, rationed food and consumer goods, controlled information, and mobilized women for industrial and other wartime roles. Bombing campaigns specifically targeted civilian areas and industrial infrastructure. Sieges deliberately starved civilian populations. The result was that civilian casualties in World War II vastly exceeded military casualties — a reversal of the pattern that characterized most earlier conflicts.

The concept of total war raises profound ethical questions about the limits of legitimate military action that international humanitarian law — both as it existed before World War II and as it was developed in its aftermath — has grappled with imperfectly. The Nuremberg and Geneva conventions established principles meant to limit the worst excesses of total war, but their enforcement remains a challenge in subsequent conflicts.

Section 48.4

War in Asia and the Pacific

The war in Asia and the Pacific was in some respects more ancient and in other respects more modern than the European conflict. Its Asian roots reached back to Japan's Meiji-era industrialization and imperial ambitions, to the Sino-Japanese War of 1894–1895, to the Russo-Japanese War of 1904–1905, and to the long process by which Japan had established itself as a great power in East Asia. The full-scale war against China that began in 1937 was, in Chinese historical memory, the beginning of a war that preceded the European conflict by more than two years and claimed more Chinese lives than any other theater of the war.

The Second Sino-Japanese War, which merged into World War II after Pearl Harbor, was extraordinarily destructive. Japanese forces captured major Chinese cities — Shanghai, Nanjing, Hankou, Canton — and controlled the coastal and agricultural heartland of eastern China. The Chinese Nationalist government under Chiang Kai-shek retreated to the interior city of Chongqing and continued resistance, supplied by the Burma Road and eventually by American air transport over "the Hump" of the Himalayas. The Chinese Communist forces under Mao Zedong pursued a parallel guerrilla war in the north. Japanese occupation of China was characterized by extreme violence against Chinese civilians, including massacres, forced labor, and biological and chemical warfare experiments conducted by the secret Unit 731. Estimates of total Chinese deaths from the war range from eight to twenty million or more — a staggering toll that is often underappreciated in Western accounts of the war.

The most notorious single atrocity of the Asian war was the Nanjing Massacre (December 1937–January 1938), in which Japanese forces killed an estimated 100,000 to 300,000 Chinese civilians and prisoners of war and subjected tens of thousands of women and girls to sexual violence in the weeks after capturing China's capital. The massacre has remained a source of deep historical controversy and ongoing political tension between China and Japan, as Japanese nationalist politicians and historians have disputed the scale and character of the killings, while Chinese historical memory has enshrined Nanjing as the defining symbol of Japanese imperial brutality.

Japan's expansion into Southeast Asia brought it into conflict with the Western colonial powers. The fall of Singapore in February 1942 — which Churchill called the worst disaster in British military history — and the rapid Japanese conquest of the Dutch East Indies, the Philippines, Burma, and Malaya demonstrated that European colonial power in Asia rested on military force that was not, in fact, invulnerable. The Japanese occupation of Southeast Asia was framed ideologically as the liberation of Asian peoples from European colonialism through the leadership of a superior Asian civilization. In practice, Japanese occupation was often brutal, involving forced labor, food requisitioning that caused famine, and violence against civilian populations. But the demonstration that Asian forces could defeat European armies had lasting effects on the imagination of Asian anticolonial movements.

American strategy in the Pacific was shaped by geography and logistics. The vast distances of the Pacific Ocean required island-by-island campaigns to establish airfields and naval bases within striking range of Japan. The strategy of "island hopping," developed by Admiral Chester Nimitz and General Douglas MacArthur, bypassed heavily fortified Japanese positions when possible and attacked where Japanese defenses were weaker, gradually compressing the defensive perimeter around the Japanese home islands. The battles of Guadalcanal (1942–1943), Tarawa (1943), the Marianas (1944), the Philippines (1944–1945), Iwo Jima (1945), and Okinawa (1945) were each expensive in lives on both sides. Japanese resistance was intensified by military culture that regarded surrender as deeply dishonorable; casualty rates among Japanese defenders routinely exceeded 95 percent, and kamikaze pilots deliberately crashed aircraft into Allied ships, killing more than 7,000 American sailors.

By mid-1945, Japan was being strangled by naval blockade, its cities burned by firebombing campaigns, and its military forces decimated across the Pacific. Yet Japanese military planners were preparing for the defense of the home islands, estimating that an Allied invasion would cost enormous casualties on both sides. The debate over whether invasion or atomic bombing was the appropriate means to end the war — and whether the atomic bombings were justified — remains one of the most contested questions in the history of World War II, examined more fully in Section 48.8.

Regional Lens

Regional Lens: China and the Asia-Pacific War

Western accounts of World War II frequently center the war in Europe and in the American-led Pacific campaign, treating China primarily as a theater that absorbed Japanese forces while the "real" war was fought elsewhere. This perspective distorts the historical reality. China was the first country to fight Japan in sustained large-scale combat, beginning in 1937, and bore the war's longest sustained military campaign and among its greatest human costs.

China's experience of the war was defined by occupation, resistance, and internal division. The Nationalist government under Chiang Kai-shek commanded the formal military forces but faced persistent shortages of equipment, training, and resources. The Communist forces under Mao Zedong operated largely independently in north and northwest China, building a mass political movement through land reform and grassroots organization in the areas under their control. The strategic collaboration between Nationalists and Communists, maintained under pressure from the United States, remained uneasy and broke down entirely in the civil war that resumed after Japan's defeat.

Chinese civilians bore the brunt of Japanese occupation. In addition to the Nanjing Massacre and Unit 731's biological warfare experiments, Japanese forces engaged in systematic scorched-earth campaigns in areas they could not fully control, destroying villages, killing livestock, and demolishing agricultural infrastructure. The 1943 Henan famine, partly caused by Japanese requisitioning of food and partly by Nationalist government mismanagement, killed between one and three million people. Chinese workers were conscripted for forced labor across Japan's empire.

China's role in the postwar settlement was ambiguous. The Republic of China (Nationalist government) was granted a permanent seat on the United Nations Security Council as one of the "Big Five" great powers — a reflection of its formal role as a major Allied belligerent and American diplomatic support. But within four years, the Communist victory in the Chinese Civil War transformed the political landscape of East Asia entirely, with consequences that shaped the Cold War and the postwar world.

Section 48.5

Occupation, Collaboration, and Resistance

By 1942, Axis powers controlled or occupied enormous territories: Germany held most of continental Europe from France to the gates of Moscow; Japan had created an empire spanning from Manchuria to the Solomon Islands. In these occupied territories, tens of millions of people faced daily decisions about how to relate to foreign rule — decisions shaped by ideology, self-interest, fear, opportunity, and circumstance, and constrained by the overwhelming violence that occupation authorities were willing and able to deploy. The spectrum of response ran from enthusiastic collaboration to passive accommodation to active resistance, and most people occupied complex and shifting positions along that spectrum.

Collaboration took many forms. At one extreme were those who genuinely embraced the occupiers' ideology — European fascists who welcomed German rule as the vanguard of a new racial order, Asian nationalists who saw Japanese leadership as liberation from Western colonialism, and individuals who harbored preexisting prejudices that the occupiers' policies validated and empowered. At a more mundane level, collaboration meant participating in the administrative structures of occupation: serving in local police forces, working in factories that produced goods for the occupying power, providing information to occupying authorities, or simply conducting ordinary economic transactions. This kind of structural collaboration was nearly universal, since occupying powers required functioning administrative and economic systems, and survival often depended on participating in them.

The most morally damning form of collaboration was participation in the persecution and deportation of Jewish people and other targeted groups. In France, the Vichy government — established under the elderly World War I hero Marshal Philippe Pétain after France's defeat — pursued its own antisemitic policies independently of German orders, enacting racial laws, stripping Jewish people of citizenship, and eventually cooperating in the roundup and deportation of Jewish people to extermination camps. Approximately 77,000 Jewish people were deported from France, of whom fewer than 3,000 survived. French police, not German, conducted the major roundups — including the infamous Vel d'Hiv roundup of July 1942, in which 13,000 Jewish people, including more than 4,000 children, were detained at a Paris cycling stadium before deportation. Similar patterns of local police collaboration in deportations occurred in the Netherlands, Belgium, Norway, and across occupied Eastern Europe.

Resistance took equally varied forms. Military resistance ranged from the organized partisan movements in the Soviet Union (where an estimated 700,000 to one million Soviet partisans operated behind German lines) to the French Resistance (which provided intelligence, sabotaged German infrastructure, and helped Allied airmen evade capture) to the Yugoslav Partisans under Josip Broz Tito (who tied down large German and Italian forces throughout the war). In the Balkans, armed resistance was extensive enough that German reprisals were savage: the German command ordered that 100 Serb civilians be killed for every German soldier killed by partisans. In Western Europe, resistance was more commonly nonviolent or less fully militarized: passing intelligence to the Allies, hiding Jewish people and others from German authorities, producing clandestine newspapers, or slowing the pace of work in ways that were difficult to detect.

The rescue of Jewish people from deportation and murder constituted a particularly significant form of resistance. Individuals and families who hid Jewish people in homes, farms, churches, and convents at great personal risk — including the families who hid Anne Frank, the villagers of Le Chambon-sur-Lignon in France who sheltered several thousand Jewish people, and the Danish fishermen who smuggled nearly all of Denmark's 7,000 Jewish people to neutral Sweden in October 1943 — represented a moral counterpoint to the collaboration and indifference that surrounded them. The Israeli memorial institute Yad Vashem has recognized over 27,000 such individuals as "Righteous Among the Nations." These individuals were a minority, but their existence demonstrates that choice was possible even under occupation, and that the decision to participate in murder or the decision to save life was always, at some level, a human choice.

In Asia, resistance to Japanese occupation took various forms reflecting local political contexts. In China, both Nationalist and Communist forces engaged in active military resistance. In the Philippines, guerrilla forces maintained resistance throughout the Japanese occupation and eventually linked up with the returning American forces. In Malaya, a guerrilla resistance movement, dominated by ethnic Chinese members of the Malayan Communist Party, fought the Japanese. In Vietnam, the Viet Minh — a coalition of nationalists and Communists under Ho Chi Minh — organized resistance against both Japanese occupation and, after the war, against the returning French colonial power. The experience of resisting Japanese occupation shaped and legitimized many of the Asian nationalist movements that would drive decolonization in the postwar years.

Historical Vocabulary
Collaboration

Collaboration, in the context of World War II occupation, refers to cooperation with occupying Axis powers by individuals, groups, or governments in conquered territories. The term covers a wide spectrum of behavior, from enthusiastic ideological alignment with the occupiers' goals to instrumental cooperation for reasons of self-interest, survival, or coercion, to the mundane daily participation in administrative and economic systems that occupation required of nearly everyone.

Historians have increasingly distinguished between different levels and types of collaboration to avoid both the oversimplified postwar myth of universal resistance and the equally misleading reduction of collaboration to a small number of exceptional traitors. Most occupied populations contained all three elements — collaborators, resisters, and the great majority who tried simply to survive — in proportions shaped by national political culture, the character of the occupying regime, the degree of violence deployed, and individual circumstances.

The most morally freighted form of collaboration was participation in the persecution of Jewish people and other targeted groups. In postwar France, the Netherlands, and other occupied countries, the extent and nature of local collaboration in the Holocaust was long suppressed in national memory, replaced by narratives emphasizing resistance, before historians beginning in the 1970s and 1980s forced a more honest accounting. Understanding the history of collaboration is essential to understanding how genocide was possible — it required not only a criminal state but the participation, passive acquiescence, or willful ignorance of large numbers of ordinary people.

Section 48.6

The Holocaust and Genocide

The Holocaust — the systematic, state-organized murder of approximately six million Jewish people by the Nazi German regime and its collaborators between 1933 and 1945 — was the defining atrocity of World War II and one of the worst crimes in human history. It was not an accident of war or an unintended consequence of military necessity; it was a deliberate program of genocide carried out with the resources of a modern industrial state, rooted in a racist ideology that identified Jewish people as the supreme enemy of humanity, and executed through bureaucratic organization, industrial technology, and human participation on an enormous scale.

Understanding the Holocaust requires understanding the ideology that drove it. Nazi antisemitism — the hatred of Jewish people — was not simply a variant of the prejudice that had existed in European societies for centuries. It was a pseudo-scientific racial ideology that identified Jewish people not as a religious community that could convert or assimilate but as a biological race — a parasitic enemy that threatened the survival of the Aryan master race. Hitler's ideology held that history was a struggle among races for dominance and survival, and that the Jewish people were responsible for Germany's defeat in World War I, the humiliations of Versailles, the spread of communism, the corruption of German culture, and virtually every other adversity that Germany had suffered. This ideology was not held only by Hitler and a small leadership circle; it was actively promoted through the schools, the media, and the institutions of the Nazi state, and it found wide resonance in a society that had experienced genuine trauma and was seeking explanations and scapegoats.

The persecution of Jewish people by the Nazi state proceeded in stages. From 1933 to 1938, Nazi policies focused on legal discrimination and economic exclusion: Jewish people were stripped of citizenship by the 1935 Nuremberg Laws, expelled from professions and the civil service, subjected to boycotts of their businesses, and increasingly segregated from German society. The November 1938 pogrom known as Kristallnacht ("Night of Broken Glass") — in which Nazi paramilitaries and mobs destroyed approximately 7,500 Jewish-owned businesses, vandalized or destroyed 267 synagogues, and killed at least 91 Jewish people, while 30,000 were arrested and sent to concentration camps — marked a qualitative escalation from legal persecution to direct violence. Emigration was the Nazi regime's preferred solution during this phase, and approximately half of Germany's Jewish population — about 300,000 people — had emigrated by the time war began in 1939, mostly to other European countries, Palestine, and the Americas.

The war transformed the situation radically. Germany's conquests brought millions of additional Jewish people under Nazi control — Poland alone had a Jewish population of approximately 3.3 million. The Nazi regime initially pursued policies of concentration and forced labor, establishing ghettos in occupied Polish cities where Jewish people were crowded into impossibly confined spaces, subjected to starvation rations, and forced to work in German war industries. The Warsaw Ghetto, the largest, held over 400,000 people; during the winter of 1941–1942, tens of thousands died of starvation and disease within its walls. The ghettos were not the final stage of Nazi policy but a transitional one, characterized by deliberate starvation while the regime worked out what to do with populations it had confined.

The decision to move to systematic mass murder appears to have developed gradually during 1941. The invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941 was accompanied by the Einsatzgruppen — mobile SS killing units — whose mission was to kill Jewish people, Soviet political commissars, and others designated as racial or political enemies behind the advancing German front. By the end of 1941, the Einsatzgruppen had killed approximately 500,000 people, mostly Jewish. The largest single Einsatzgruppen massacre was at Babi Yar, a ravine outside Kyiv (Kiev) in Ukraine, where 33,771 Jewish people were shot over two days in September 1941. The mass shootings were carried out in the open, with victims marched to execution sites in full view of local populations, and they were participated in not only by SS men but by German regular police battalions and, in many cases, by local collaborators.

By late 1941 and early 1942, the Nazi leadership was moving toward an industrialized approach to mass murder. The first extermination camps — distinct from concentration camps in that their purpose was not detention and forced labor but killing — began operation in late 1941 and early 1942. The Wannsee Conference of January 1942, at which senior Nazi officials coordinated the administrative implementation of what they called the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question," brought together the representatives of various German government agencies to coordinate the Europe-wide system of deportation and murder. The tone of the conference record is bureaucratic — officials discussing logistics, jurisdictions, and implementation as they would any administrative problem — which itself reveals something important about how genocide operates: not only through mass violence and fanatical ideology but through the ordinary machinery of bureaucracy and the moral blindness that bureaucratic routines can produce.

The extermination camps — Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek — were located in occupied Poland, far from Western scrutiny. Deportees arrived by train from across German-occupied Europe, often in sealed cattle cars after days or weeks without food or water. Upon arrival, most were immediately selected for death — the old, the young, the sick, and in the largest camps, the majority of able-bodied arrivals — and were killed in gas chambers using carbon monoxide or the pesticide Zyklon B. Their bodies were cremated or initially buried in mass graves and later exhumed and burned to destroy evidence. The property of the murdered — clothing, jewelry, hair, dental gold — was systematically collected and used by the German war economy. The process was organized with bureaucratic precision and industrial efficiency.

Those not immediately killed were used as forced labor until they were no longer physically capable of work, then killed. The concentration camp system, which had existed since 1933 primarily for political prisoners, expanded enormously during the war to encompass forced laborers, prisoners of war, and additional categories of victims. Approximately 1.1 million people were killed at Auschwitz-Birkenau alone, of whom roughly 90 percent were Jewish. In total, approximately six million Jewish people — about two-thirds of the entire prewar Jewish population of Europe — were murdered during the Holocaust. Entire communities that had existed for centuries were annihilated: 90 percent of Polish Jews, 85 percent of Greek Jews, 77 percent of Dutch Jews. The Jewish communities of Eastern Europe, which had been centers of religious, intellectual, and cultural life for centuries, were effectively destroyed.

The Holocaust also targeted other groups. Roma and Sinti people were subject to racial persecution and mass murder on a comparable scale to Jewish persecution — estimates of Roma killed range from 250,000 to 500,000, representing perhaps a quarter to a third of Europe's Roma population. Disabled people were subject to the T4 "euthanasia" program, which killed approximately 200,000 people in Germany and occupied Europe before public protest, led largely by German Catholic bishops, forced its official suspension in 1941 (though killings continued covertly). Soviet prisoners of war were deliberately killed through starvation and exposure. Gay men were imprisoned in concentration camps. Jehovah's Witnesses were imprisoned for refusing to renounce their faith. Political prisoners — communists, socialists, resistance fighters — were killed throughout the war. The Holocaust was the most systematic and complete of these genocidal projects, but it existed within a broader Nazi project of racial reorganization that envisioned the murder or enslavement of most of Europe's Slavic population and the elimination of all other groups deemed racially or politically unacceptable.

Resistance within the Holocaust was constrained by the overwhelming violence of the Nazi state, by the isolation and starvation that preceded deportation, and by the deliberate deception that accompanied it — victims were often told they were being resettled, not killed. Yet resistance did occur: the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising of April–May 1943, in which a small group of young Jewish fighters held German forces at bay for nearly a month before their position was destroyed, was the largest single act of armed Jewish resistance. Uprisings also occurred in the Treblinka and Sobibor extermination camps in 1943. Underground networks within the camps preserved cultural life, documentation, and human dignity against all odds. These acts of resistance, though they could not stop the killing, are essential parts of the Holocaust's history — evidence that the victims were human beings who chose to resist their dehumanization at enormous cost, not merely passive objects of historical forces.

Archaeology & Evidence Window

Evidence Window: The Documentation of the Holocaust

The Holocaust is among the most thoroughly documented events in history — a fact that reflects both the bureaucratic nature of the Nazi state and the extraordinary efforts of survivors, historians, archaeologists, and researchers over the decades since the war's end. The evidence takes multiple forms, each illuminating different dimensions of the genocide.

Documentary evidence is extensive. The Nazi bureaucracy generated enormous quantities of paperwork: deportation orders, camp administrative records, correspondence between SS commanders, logistical reports, and — most damning — records of the killings themselves. Much of this documentation survived because Allied forces captured German archives before they could be destroyed. The Nuremberg Trials made use of thousands of captured German documents, many of which are now preserved in archives accessible to researchers.

Survivor testimony constitutes an irreplaceable form of evidence. Thousands of survivors gave testimony in the immediate postwar period, at the Nuremberg Trials and in displaced persons camps; tens of thousands more gave testimony in subsequent decades, including through the Shoah Foundation's project of recording survivor testimonies before the survivor generation passed. These testimonies provide access to individual subjective experience — the terror of deportation, the horror of arrival at the camps, the experience of the selection process, the daily life of forced labor — that documentary sources alone cannot convey.

Material evidence from camp sites continues to be studied. Archaeological excavations at Sobibor, Treblinka, and other former extermination camp sites have located mass graves, recovered personal possessions, and confirmed aspects of the killing process described in survivor and perpetrator testimonies. At Auschwitz-Birkenau, preserved structures — the ruins of the gas chambers and crematoria, the barracks, the selection platform — constitute material evidence that has been designated a World Heritage Site by UNESCO. The enormous quantities of personal possessions — mountains of shoes, eyeglasses, hair, suitcases, and photographs — on display at Auschwitz and other memorial museums make the scale of individual loss visible in ways that numbers alone cannot.

Perpetrator testimony, obtained at the Nuremberg Trials and subsequent legal proceedings and through interviews with researchers, provides insight into the motivations and decision-making of those who organized and carried out the Holocaust. This testimony is ethically complex — historians must use it carefully, recognizing that defendants and witnesses had strong motives to minimize their roles and shift responsibility — but it is essential to understanding how ordinary bureaucratic and military institutions were turned to genocidal purposes.

Questions for students:

  1. Why do historians say the Holocaust is "among the most thoroughly documented events in history," and what does this tell us about the character of the Nazi state?
  2. What can survivor testimony reveal that documentary sources cannot? What are the limits of survivor testimony as historical evidence?
  3. Why are material remains — the physical sites of camps, the possessions of victims — important as evidence, even when extensive documentary and testimonial sources already exist?
  4. How do historians handle the ethical challenges of using perpetrator testimony as evidence?
Section 48.7

Total War, Civilians, and Strategic Bombing

World War II was a war in which the distinction between combatant and civilian was systematically eroded or deliberately disregarded. All the major belligerent powers targeted civilian populations to some degree — through blockades designed to produce starvation, through policies of deliberate terror against occupied populations, through the bombing of urban areas, and through the Holocaust itself. The scale and deliberateness of attacks on civilian life in World War II went beyond anything in the experience of modern warfare and produced the legal and ethical frameworks — the laws of war, the Geneva Conventions, the Nuremberg principles — that the postwar world attempted to construct in response.

The mobilization of civilian economies for war production was one dimension of total war. All major belligerents transformed their economies, directing factories, labor, agriculture, and finance toward military purposes. Women entered the industrial workforce in large numbers — the American "Rosie the Riveter" became an enduring cultural image of women's wartime role, and similar transformations occurred in Britain, the Soviet Union, and Germany. Governments rationed food and consumer goods, controlled wages and prices, and directed information through censorship and propaganda. The distinction between the home front and the battle front was never absolute.

The deliberate killing of civilian populations was a policy of the Nazi occupation, most explicitly in the Holocaust but also in the systematic starvation of Soviet prisoners of war, the mass shooting of civilian hostages in reprisal for partisan attacks, and the deliberate starvation policies applied to the population of Leningrad during the German siege (September 1941–January 1944), in which approximately 800,000 to 1.1 million people died. Japanese occupation forces applied similarly brutal policies in China and Southeast Asia, including the confiscation of food supplies that contributed to major famines, forced labor under lethal conditions, and mass atrocities against civilian populations.

Strategic bombing of cities — the deliberate targeting of urban areas rather than specific military installations — became a defining feature of the war for all major air powers, though it was conducted with different degrees of deliberateness and on different scales. Germany bombed British cities in the Blitz (September 1940–May 1941), killing approximately 43,000 British civilians and forcing millions to evacuate or shelter in underground stations and purpose-built shelters. British bombers struck German cities in return, initially with limited accuracy but with increasing intensity as bomber technology improved and as strategic doctrine evolved toward deliberate area bombing of German cities to destroy civilian morale and disrupt industrial production. The February 1945 bombing of Dresden — in which British and American aircraft created a firestorm that killed between 22,000 and 25,000 people in a city crowded with refugees — has become a symbol of the moral ambiguity of area bombing, though debate continues about the precise casualty figures and the military rationale for the attack.

American bombing of Japan in the final year of the war was even more destructive. The B-29 bomber, operating from newly captured airfields in the Mariana Islands, had sufficient range to reach the Japanese home islands. In March 1945, General Curtis LeMay ordered a shift from high-altitude precision bombing to low-altitude nighttime incendiary bombing of Japanese cities. The March 9–10, 1945 fire-bombing of Tokyo killed approximately 80,000 to 100,000 people in a single night and destroyed sixteen square miles of the city. Over the following months, American bombers systematically firebombed sixty-seven Japanese cities. By summer 1945, most of Japan's major urban centers had been devastated, its navy destroyed, its air force largely eliminated, and its population facing severe food shortages. Yet Japan's military leaders continued to refuse surrender, committing the population to defend the home islands against an Allied invasion they estimated would cost millions of lives on both sides.

The experience of civilian populations during the war also included forced migration, refugee flight, and the deliberate expulsion of ethnic groups from territories that occupying powers wished to homogenize. The Nazi plan for occupied Eastern Europe involved the eventual expulsion or killing of most of the Slavic population and its replacement with German settlers — a project that was beginning to be implemented as Nazi power was destroyed. After the war, the expulsion of Germans from Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, and other Eastern European countries (a transfer of approximately 12–14 million people) represented the largest forced migration in European history. In Asia, millions of Chinese, Koreans, and Southeast Asians were displaced by Japanese occupation and the violence of the war's end.

Everyday Life and Social History

Everyday Life Under Total War: Home Fronts and Occupied Territories

For civilians across the warring and occupied nations, World War II reshaped daily life in ways both dramatic and mundane. The character of that transformation depended enormously on whether one lived in an Allied nation, an Axis nation, an occupied territory, or the direct target of military violence — but certain common threads ran through the civilian experience of total war.

Rationing and scarcity defined civilian life in virtually every combatant nation. In Britain, rationing of food, clothing, and fuel began in 1940 and continued until 1954. British households received weekly rations of meat, butter, cheese, eggs, sugar, and other staples — amounts that required significant ingenuity and adaptation in cooking and household management. Women became experts in making nutritious meals from limited ingredients, and the wartime diet, though monotonous, was arguably healthier than prewar eating patterns. In the Soviet Union, rationing was far more severe; in besieged Leningrad, the daily bread ration fell to 125 grams (about 4 ounces) for civilians — barely enough to sustain life. In occupied territories, occupying powers requisitioned food for their own populations, leaving occupied peoples in chronic malnutrition.

Women's roles expanded dramatically in most combatant nations. In the United States, approximately 6 million women entered the industrial workforce during the war, taking jobs in aircraft factories, shipyards, munitions plants, and other industries that had previously been exclusively male. In the Soviet Union, women served not only in industrial roles but as soldiers, snipers, pilots, and partisan fighters. In Britain, women were conscripted for war work from 1941 onward. This expansion of women's public economic and military roles had lasting implications for gender norms, though in most countries postwar pressures pushed women back into domestic roles that conflicted with the wartime experience of independent public life.

Children's experiences of the war were shaped by evacuation, disruption, loss, and, in many occupied and bombed areas, direct exposure to violence. In Britain, 1.5 million children were evacuated from cities to the countryside in the first days of the war, many separated from their parents for months or years. In the Soviet Union, children as young as fourteen worked in factories and collective farms to replace men at the front. In occupied Poland and the Soviet Union, hundreds of thousands of children were killed in German reprisals and in the Holocaust; tens of thousands of children were deported to Germany for "Germanization" — renamed, given German families, and raised to forget their origins.

Propaganda shaped civilian understanding of the war in all combatant nations. Governments controlled information about military setbacks and casualties, promoted narratives of heroism and sacrifice, and mobilized civilian morale through posters, films, radio broadcasts, and community events. The line between information and manipulation was deliberately blurred. In Germany, Joseph Goebbels's propaganda ministry shaped every dimension of public life; in Japan, military success was reported while defeats were concealed; in the United States, wartime films and posters promoted the war effort while often reflecting the racial hierarchies of American society — depicting Japanese enemies in explicitly racist imagery while African American soldiers fought in segregated units.

Section 48.8

The Atomic Bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki

On August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb — codenamed "Little Boy" — on the Japanese city of Hiroshima. The bomb, equivalent in explosive force to approximately 15,000 tons of TNT, destroyed roughly five square miles of the city in a fraction of a second. Between 70,000 and 80,000 people died instantly or in the immediate aftermath; by the end of 1945, the death toll, including those who died from radiation sickness, had risen to an estimated 90,000 to 166,000 people. Three days later, on August 9, a second bomb — "Fat Man" — was dropped on Nagasaki, killing between 40,000 and 80,000 people. On August 15, Emperor Hirohito announced Japan's acceptance of the Potsdam Declaration terms, effectively ending the war. The formal surrender ceremony took place on September 2, 1945, aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay.

The development of the atomic bomb was the product of the Manhattan Project, a massive scientific and industrial program that employed approximately 130,000 people across dozens of secret sites in the United States, Canada, and Britain. The project was initiated in response to warnings from scientists — including refugees from Nazi Europe — that Germany might be developing nuclear weapons. It succeeded in producing three working bombs by July 1945 (one was tested at Alamogordo, New Mexico, on July 16, 1945 — the Trinity test, the world's first nuclear detonation). Germany had already surrendered; Japan was the only Axis power still fighting.

President Harry Truman, who had become president upon Roosevelt's death in April 1945 and was briefed on the Manhattan Project only after taking office, authorized the use of the bombs with the stated goals of forcing Japan's surrender without the enormous casualties that an invasion of the Japanese home islands would entail, and of ending the war as quickly as possible. American military planners estimated that an invasion of Kyushu (the southernmost home island), planned for November 1945, would cost between 250,000 and 500,000 American casualties and potentially millions of Japanese military and civilian casualties, based on the ferocity of resistance in Okinawa and on intelligence about Japanese defensive preparations.

The atomic bombings have been among the most controversial decisions in modern history, generating a historical debate that has continued for eight decades without resolution. The debate encompasses military, ethical, political, and historical dimensions, all of which are genuinely complex.

Those who defend the bombings argue that they ended the war quickly, saving lives on both sides that would have been lost in an invasion; that Japan's military leaders were committed to fighting to the last regardless of casualties; that conventional bombing was already killing comparable numbers of civilians; that the Japanese government was also committing atrocities against Asian populations every day the war continued; and that the strategic logic of the decision was sound given what policymakers knew at the time. From this perspective, the atomic bombings, however terrible, were less destructive than the alternative.

Critics argue that Japan was already on the verge of surrender and that the bombs were unnecessary to end the war; that the primary motive was partly diplomatic — to end the war before the Soviet Union could claim a larger role in the postwar settlement of Asia; that the deliberate targeting of civilian cities constituted a war crime under the emerging standards of international law; and that the precedent of using nuclear weapons against civilians has had deeply harmful consequences for global security ever since. Some Japanese historians have emphasized that the bombings reflected the racial dehumanization of Japanese people that characterized American wartime culture, pointing to the contrast with the decision not to use nuclear weapons against Germany even when they might have been available.

The Soviet Union's declaration of war against Japan on August 8, 1945 — the day between the two atomic bombings — and the rapid Soviet conquest of Japanese-held Manchuria may also have played a significant role in Japan's decision to surrender, complicating the simple narrative that the atomic bombs alone ended the war. The role of the bombs versus the Soviet entry versus Japan's internal political dynamics in producing the surrender decision remains a genuinely contested historical question.

For the survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki — the hibakusha, or "bomb-affected people," in Japanese — the bombings produced suffering that extended far beyond the immediate deaths. Survivors faced radiation sickness, increased rates of cancer and leukemia throughout their lives, social stigma in Japanese society (associated with illness and death), and profound psychological trauma. Many struggled to find employment or marriage partners because of fears about radiation exposure. The hibakusha became powerful advocates for nuclear disarmament and have contributed some of the most important testimonies to the historical record of the bombings' human costs.

Historical Debate

Historical Debate: Were the Atomic Bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki Justified?

The question: The atomic bombings killed between 130,000 and 226,000 people, the vast majority of them civilians. Were they a military necessity that shortened the war and saved lives, a war crime against civilian populations, or something more complex than either position allows?

Interpretation A — Military Necessity: Historians who defend the bombings argue that Japan's military leadership was committed to resisting invasion at all costs, that the projected casualties from an invasion of the home islands were enormous, that the bombs did accelerate Japan's surrender, and that Truman's decision was reasonable given the information available to him. They also note that conventional firebombing was already killing comparable numbers of civilians, making the atomic bombs a difference of degree rather than kind in the conduct of total war. From this perspective, the bombings were tragic but justified by the military context.
Interpretation B — War Crime and Unnecessary: Critics argue that Japan was already effectively defeated and that diplomatic channels — particularly a guarantee that Japan could retain its emperor — might have produced surrender without the bombs. They contend that the deliberate targeting of civilian cities was a moral and legal wrong regardless of military context, and that the failure of policymakers to seek a negotiated settlement before using nuclear weapons reflected factors beyond pure military calculation, including the desire to end the war before Soviet entry could complicate the postwar settlement. From this perspective, the bombings were morally indefensible and strategically unnecessary.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the same body of evidence — American and Japanese government records, military assessments, diplomatic communications, and testimonies — but weight them differently. The "revisionist" critique, developed by historians beginning in the 1960s, has forced a more nuanced view of the decision than the simple "it ended the war" narrative of earlier accounts. Most historians today see the decision as genuinely complex, shaped by multiple overlapping factors, and not reducible to either pure moral condemnation or pure strategic vindication.

Why it matters: The debate is not merely historical. It bears on fundamental questions about the ethics of war, the targeting of civilians, the legitimacy of nuclear weapons, and the responsibilities of states that possess weapons of mass destruction. The atomic bombings inaugurated the nuclear age, and the questions they raise about the limits of military necessity and the protection of civilian life remain urgently relevant.

Student task: Based on the interpretations described, which do you find more persuasive, and why? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate? Can a decision be both militarily defensible and morally wrong simultaneously?

Section 48.9

Victory, War Crimes, and Aftermath

The end of World War II was experienced very differently across the globe — as liberation, as occupation, as relief, as devastation, as the beginning of a new world order, and as the unresolved continuation of old injustices. For the European peoples who had been occupied by Nazi Germany, liberation by Allied forces brought an end to terror and mass murder but also the brutal revelation of the Holocaust's full scale as the camps were liberated and documented. For the peoples of Asia under Japanese occupation, the war's end came unevenly, often accompanied by continued violence in the chaotic interregnum between Japanese surrender and the restoration of order. For the Japanese home population, surrender brought a mixture of relief that the killing had stopped and profound disorientation as the certainties of wartime ideology collapsed. For colonized peoples in Asia and Africa, the war created new conditions — the weakness of European colonial powers, the rhetoric of self-determination, the precedent of Asian forces defeating European armies — that made the postwar continuation of colonial rule increasingly difficult to sustain.

The human costs of the war were staggering. Total deaths are estimated at between 70 and 85 million, roughly two-thirds of them civilian. The Soviet Union lost approximately 27 million people — about 14 percent of its prewar population — making its sacrifice by far the greatest of any Allied nation. China lost an estimated 15–20 million or more. Germany lost 6–7 million, including military dead and Holocaust victims. Poland lost approximately 6 million people, roughly 17 percent of its prewar population — half of them Jewish. Japan lost approximately 3 million. The United States and Britain, separated from the main theaters by oceans, suffered far fewer civilian casualties but still lost over 400,000 and 450,000 military dead respectively. The physical destruction of Europe and Asia — destroyed cities, wrecked infrastructure, disrupted agriculture — compounded the human toll with widespread famine and displacement.

The war crimes trials that followed Germany's defeat established important principles in international law. The International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg, conducted from November 1945 to October 1946, tried twenty-two major Nazi defendants on four charges: conspiracy to commit crimes against peace, waging aggressive war, war crimes (violations of the laws and customs of war), and crimes against humanity (murder, extermination, enslavement, persecution, and other inhumane acts against civilian populations). Twelve defendants were sentenced to death, including Hermann Göring (who committed suicide the night before his scheduled execution), Joachim von Ribbentrop, and Hans Frank. The tribunal also declared the SS, the Gestapo, and the Nazi leadership organizations to be criminal organizations. Subsequent Nuremberg trials tried doctors who had conducted medical experiments on camp prisoners, judges who had administered Nazi law, industrialists who had used slave labor, and SS officers who had commanded the Einsatzgruppen.

The Nuremberg principles established several crucial precedents: that aggressive war was itself a crime under international law; that individuals could be held criminally responsible for crimes committed in the name of a state and could not defend themselves by claiming they were following orders; and that crimes against civilian populations — genocide, deportation, forced labor, persecution — constituted international crimes for which individuals bore personal responsibility. A similar tribunal, the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (the Tokyo Trials, 1946–1948), tried Japanese war criminals on equivalent charges, resulting in seven death sentences and numerous prison terms. The principles established at Nuremberg and Tokyo became the foundation of subsequent international humanitarian law, including the Convention on Genocide (1948), the Geneva Conventions (1949), and eventually the Rome Statute that established the International Criminal Court (1998).

The postwar settlement reshaped the political map of the world. Germany was divided into four occupation zones — American, British, French, and Soviet — which eventually hardened into two German states: the Federal Republic of Germany in the west and the German Democratic Republic in the east, reflecting the Cold War division of Europe. Japan was occupied exclusively by the United States and underwent a comprehensive democratization program under General Douglas MacArthur, receiving a new constitution (1947) that renounced war as an instrument of national policy and established parliamentary democracy. Italy and the other former Axis states received peace treaties that imposed territorial adjustments, reparations obligations, and military restrictions.

The postwar world order was shaped by the new dominance of the United States and the Soviet Union — the two "superpowers" whose wartime alliance had defeated the Axis and whose postwar rivalry would define international politics for the next four decades. The United Nations, established in 1945, was designed to provide a mechanism for peaceful resolution of international disputes that the League of Nations had failed to provide, but it operated in the shadow of the Cold War from its inception. The Marshall Plan, through which the United States provided approximately $13 billion to rebuild the shattered economies of Western Europe, demonstrated the enormous economic advantages that the United States derived from the war — having emerged with its industrial base intact, its territory undamaged, and its economy stimulated by wartime production.

The war accelerated decolonization across Asia and Africa. The weakening of European colonial powers, the demonstration that Asian forces could defeat European armies, the rhetoric of freedom and self-determination that Allied powers had used to justify the war, and the organizational experience and political legitimacy gained by anticolonial movements during the war combined to make the postwar continuation of colonial rule increasingly untenable. India achieved independence in 1947; Indonesia in 1945 (though Dutch attempts to reassert control required further fighting); Vietnam began its independence war against France in 1946. The decolonization of Africa followed in the 1950s and 1960s. These processes of decolonization were among the most consequential long-term consequences of World War II, reshaping the political structure of the global system for the remainder of the twentieth century.

Global Connection

Global Connection: The Grand Alliance and the Architecture of the Postwar World

The Grand Alliance — the military coalition of the United States, the United Kingdom, the Soviet Union, and dozens of other nations that defeated the Axis powers — was remarkable both for its military effectiveness and for its internal contradictions. It united a capitalist democracy, a parliamentary monarchy with a global empire, and a communist totalitarian state against the common threat of fascist aggression, coordinating military strategy across multiple theaters while navigating profound ideological and political differences.

The wartime meetings of the "Big Three" — Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin — at Tehran (November–December 1943), Yalta (February 1945), and Potsdam (July–August 1945) made decisions that shaped the postwar world: the timing and coordination of military operations, the postwar settlement for Germany, the borders of Poland, the governance of liberated countries, the terms for Soviet entry into the Pacific war, and the structures of the new United Nations. These conferences were not simply military coordination; they were negotiations about the postwar global order among powers with very different visions of what that order should look like.

The wartime alliance was always uneasy. Britain and the United States were suspicious of Soviet intentions in Eastern Europe; Stalin was resentful that the Western powers had delayed the second front (the Normandy landings came four years after Germany invaded the Soviet Union, during which time the Eastern Front was absorbing the vast majority of German military force). Churchill famously sketched out a "percentages agreement" with Stalin in October 1944, dividing Eastern European countries into spheres of influence by percentage — an exercise in great-power politics that both men recognized as morally uncomfortable and practically uncertain.

The architecture of the postwar international order — the United Nations Security Council with its permanent five members and veto power, the Bretton Woods system of international monetary cooperation, the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade — reflected the vision, primarily American, of a rule-based international order that would prevent the economic nationalism and political instability of the interwar years. This architecture has shaped global governance for the eight decades since the war's end, though it has been contested and adapted as the relative power of its founders has shifted.

Primary Source Window

Primary Source Window: The Nuremberg Laws (1935) and the Wannsee Conference Protocol (1942)

Sources: Reich Citizenship Law and Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor (Nuremberg Laws), September 15, 1935; Protocol of the Wannsee Conference, January 20, 1942 · Type: Legislation and administrative conference protocol

Context: These two documents represent two stages in the Nazi persecution of Jewish people: the legal and bureaucratic stripping of rights in the mid-1930s, and the coordinated administrative planning of genocide in 1942. Both are products of the German state operating through normal bureaucratic channels — laws passed by the Reichstag, an administrative conference recorded by a civil servant.

The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 stripped Jewish people of German citizenship, reducing them to "state subjects" without political rights, and prohibited marriage and sexual relations between Jewish people and "citizens of German or related blood." A subsequent supplementary decree defined who was legally Jewish based on the religion of grandparents — a definition that had nothing to do with individual belief or practice and everything to do with the racial ideology of the Nazi state.
The protocol of the Wannsee Conference, drafted by SS-Obersturmbannführer Adolf Eichmann and signed by SS-Obergruppenführer Reinhard Heydrich, records a meeting of fifteen senior officials coordinating the implementation of what they called "the Final Solution to the Jewish Question." The document discusses the total number of Jewish people in each European country who were subject to "evacuation," the use of Jewish laborers in road-building before their "natural diminution," and the handling of difficult categories — those of mixed ancestry, those in mixed marriages. The language is bureaucratic and euphemistic throughout, referring to killing through phrases like "special treatment" and "evacuation to the East."

Analysis: Together, these documents illustrate two crucial dimensions of the Holocaust's character: first, that it was enacted through normal legal and administrative mechanisms of a modern state, not primarily through chaotic mob violence; and second, that the euphemistic language of the bureaucracy — "evacuation," "special treatment," "final solution" — served to normalize genocide within the apparatus of government administration. The Wannsee Protocol in particular demonstrates that the Final Solution was not the work of a small group of fanatical killers but was coordinated across multiple agencies of the German government, making it genuinely a state project. The Protocol was used as evidence at the Nuremberg Trials and remains one of the most important documentary sources for understanding the bureaucratic structure of the Holocaust.

  2. How did the Nuremberg Laws prepare the conditions for the Holocaust by defining who was legally Jewish and stripping those defined as Jewish of citizenship and legal protection?
  3. What does it mean that genocide was administered through ordinary government agencies, conference protocols, and legal instruments? How does this change our understanding of who was responsible?

Comparison Across Societies

Comparison Across Societies: The Allied Powers — United States, United Kingdom, and Soviet Union

The Grand Alliance that defeated the Axis powers brought together three very different societies, each of which experienced the war differently and emerged from it with different consequences.

The United States entered the war late (December 1941) and fought it from a position of growing industrial and economic advantage. American territory was not bombed or invaded (with the exception of Pearl Harbor and some attacks on the Aleutian Islands), and American civilians experienced the war primarily through rationing, war bond drives, industrial mobilization, and the anxiety of family members at the front. The war transformed the American economy, ending the Depression and creating full employment, and stimulated technological innovation, including the development of the atomic bomb. American military deaths were approximately 419,000 — enormous in absolute terms but a fraction of Soviet or Chinese losses. The United States emerged from the war as the world's dominant economic and military power, with its industrial base intact and its international influence vastly expanded.

The United Kingdom entered the war in September 1939 and stood alone against Germany from June 1940 to June 1941. British cities were bombed in the Blitz; British shipping was threatened in the Battle of the Atlantic; British military forces fought in North Africa, the Mediterranean, Southeast Asia, and eventually Western Europe. Britain lost approximately 450,000 military and civilian dead — significant, but far less than the losses of the Eastern Front powers. The war exhausted Britain financially, requiring massive loans from the United States and the liquidation of overseas investments, and accelerated the dissolution of the British Empire. Britain emerged from the war as a diminished power relative to the United States and eventually the Soviet Union, though it retained a permanent Security Council seat and nuclear weapons.

The Soviet Union bore the greatest burden of the war. Approximately 27 million Soviet citizens died — roughly 14 percent of the prewar population — in a conflict that destroyed entire cities, ruined the agricultural capacity of the most productive regions, and subjected the occupied western territories to the worst atrocities of the war. The German occupation of Soviet territory was specifically designed to be murderous, aimed at reducing the Slavic population and exploiting the land for German settlers. The Soviet Union's victory over Germany, achieved at immense cost, was the central military fact of the European war, and Soviet sacrifice created the political legitimacy for Stalin's postwar demands for a sphere of influence in Eastern Europe. The Soviet Union emerged from the war as a superpower but also as a nation physically and demographically devastated, in ways that the United States — which experienced none of the war's destructive consequences on its own territory — did not.

Multiple Perspectives

Multiple Perspectives: Who Experienced World War II?

Soviet Soldiers and Civilians: The war on the Eastern Front was experienced at a scale and intensity that Western accounts rarely convey. Soviet soldiers fought in conditions of extreme deprivation, with severe punishments — including execution — for retreat without orders. Civilians in occupied territories faced deliberate starvation, mass shootings, forced labor deportations, and the wholesale destruction of their communities. The memory of the "Great Patriotic War" shaped Soviet and post-Soviet Russian identity for generations, and the enormous scale of Soviet sacrifice is often underappreciated in Western popular memory.
Jewish Survivors of the Holocaust: For the roughly one-third of Europe's Jewish population who survived the war, the experience was one of loss almost beyond comprehension — the murder of family members, community, and an entire world of cultural and religious life. Survivors faced displacement, statelessness, and often the inability to return to communities where their property had been seized and where they were not welcome. The psychological weight of survival — guilt, trauma, and the burden of memory — shaped survivors' lives profoundly. The testimony of survivors has been central to the historical understanding of the Holocaust.
Asian Peoples Under Japanese Occupation: For people in China, Korea, the Philippines, Indonesia, Vietnam, Malaya, and Burma, the war meant occupation by a power that often combined a rhetoric of pan-Asian liberation with brutal exploitation, forced labor, sexual violence, food confiscation, and mass atrocity. The experience of Japanese occupation both delegitimized European colonialism (by demonstrating European military vulnerability) and created intense grievances against Japan that shaped postwar Asian politics. For Korean comfort women — women coerced into sexual slavery for the Japanese military — the war's legacy of trauma and the struggle for acknowledgment and redress has continued into the twenty-first century.
African American and Colonial Soldiers: Approximately one million African American soldiers served in the U.S. military during World War II, fighting in segregated units for a country that denied them equal rights at home. The contradiction between fighting for democracy abroad and experiencing racial discrimination in the United States — including in the military itself — politicized many Black veterans and contributed to the civil rights movement that followed the war. Colonial soldiers from India, West Africa, the Caribbean, and elsewhere fought for British and French empires that would deny them full equality for years after the war's end; their service both demonstrated the military capacity of colonized peoples and intensified demands for independence.
German Civilians: German civilians who were not members of persecuted groups experienced the war through military mobilization (nearly every family lost members at the front), strategic bombing (killing approximately 400,000–600,000 civilians), food shortages, and, in the war's final months, the catastrophic violence of Soviet armies advancing from the east, including widespread atrocities against German civilian populations. The postwar reckoning with Germany's crimes — the shock of what was revealed about the Holocaust, the pressure of denazification, the division of the country — produced a complex national experience of guilt, grief, and reconstruction that shaped German political culture for the remainder of the twentieth century.
Cause and Effect

Cause and Effect: The Holocaust — From Ideology to Industrial Genocide

Long-Term Causes

Centuries of European antisemitism; the scapegoating of Jewish people for Germany's defeat in World War I ("stab in the back" myth); the racial pseudoscience that classified peoples into biological hierarchies; the tradition of racial nationalism in European political thought; the social disruption of industrialization and modernity, which intensified search for enemies and scapegoats.

Medium-Term Causes

The Great Depression and the political crisis it produced; the rise of the Nazi Party and Hitler's seizure of power in 1933; the progressive legal and social exclusion of Jewish people under Nazi rule (1933–1938); the failure of the international community to protest effectively or provide refuge for Jewish emigrants; the Kristallnacht pogrom (1938) that escalated from legal persecution to direct violence.

Short-Term Triggers

The invasion of Poland (1939) and the Soviet Union (1941), which brought millions of additional Jewish people under Nazi control; the decision by Nazi leadership (1941–1942) to move from expulsion and ghettoization to systematic mass murder; the development of industrial killing methods (gas chambers) and the construction of extermination camps; the coordination of the Final Solution across German government agencies at the Wannsee Conference (January 1942).

Consequences

Murder of approximately six million Jewish people and millions of others; destruction of centuries-old Jewish communities across Europe; displacement of approximately 250,000 Jewish survivors as displaced persons; establishment of the state of Israel (1948) partly in response; Nuremberg Trials and development of international law of genocide; the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948); ongoing debates about responsibility, memory, and justice that continue to the present day.

Historical complexity: The Holocaust required both ideology — the dehumanizing racism that defined Jewish people as enemies of humanity — and bureaucracy — the ordinary administrative machinery of a modern state that organized deportation, managed camps, and coordinated killing. Neither ideology alone nor bureaucracy alone was sufficient. The combination of fanatical leadership, compliant institutions, widespread antisemitism, and wartime conditions in which normal moral constraints were weakened produced a genocide that could not have occurred in the absence of any one of these factors.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Continuity and Change Over Time: Warfare and Civilian Life from World War I to World War II

What Changed

  • The scale of civilian casualties increased dramatically: World War I civilian deaths were approximately 7 million; World War II civilian deaths were approximately 50–55 million
  • Strategic bombing deliberately targeted urban populations as part of war strategy, a tactic that had been limited and relatively ineffective in World War I
  • Genocide — the systematic state-organized murder of entire peoples — became a feature of the conflict on an industrial scale unprecedented in modern history
  • Nuclear weapons introduced a new category of destructive capacity that made the total destruction of cities and eventually of civilization itself technologically feasible
  • The mobilization of civilian economies for war production was total — all major powers organized every sector of economic life for the war effort
  • Women's roles in war production, military service, and resistance expanded enormously compared to World War I

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental causes of conflict — nationalism, imperial rivalry, economic competition, ideological extremism — remained rooted in the social and political tensions of industrial modernity
  • The suffering of ordinary soldiers — trench warfare on the Eastern Front bore significant similarities to World War I conditions — and the gap between official accounts and the reality of combat
  • The use of colonized soldiers for the military benefit of imperial powers, while those same powers denied the soldiers and their communities equal rights
  • The failure of international institutions to prevent conflict or protect civilian populations from mass violence

Why the pattern matters: The catastrophic escalation of civilian suffering from World War I to World War II drove the postwar effort to construct international legal and institutional frameworks — the United Nations, the laws of war, the Genocide Convention, the human rights system — that were designed to prevent a repetition. Understanding both what changed and what continued helps explain both the ambition and the limits of those postwar institutions.

Empire Study

Empire Study: Nazi Germany's New Order in Europe

Nazi Germany was not simply a nation-state waging aggressive war; it was an imperial project aiming to create a racial empire across Europe and, eventually, much of the globe. Understanding the character of this empire is essential to understanding the Holocaust and the nature of the war.

Origins and ideology: The Nazi concept of empire was rooted in Hitler's ideology of racial Lebensraum ("living space") — the idea that the German people required additional territory to the east, populated by racially inferior Slavic peoples who would be removed, enslaved, or killed, to provide the agricultural and resource base for a permanent racial empire. This ideology was articulated in Hitler's political manifesto Mein Kampf (1925) and remained the guiding vision of Nazi foreign policy from 1933 onward. The colonial imagination was explicit: Hitler compared the planned conquest of the Soviet Union to the American conquest of the West and its indigenous populations.

Expansion and administration: The Nazi empire at its height (1942) stretched from the Atlantic coast of France to the Caucasus mountains, incorporating directly annexed territory (Austria, Sudetenland, western Poland), military occupation zones (France, Belgium, Netherlands, Greece, Yugoslavia, much of the Soviet Union), and puppet or allied states (Vichy France, Hungary, Romania, Slovakia, Croatia). Different occupied territories were treated differently — Western Europeans were subject to exploitation but not to the same degree of racial violence as Eastern Europeans; Poles and Slavs were treated as racially inferior Untermenschen destined for expulsion or enslavement; Jewish people everywhere were targeted for genocide.

Exploitation and terror: The Nazi empire operated through systematic economic exploitation — looting conquered states of gold, art, machinery, food, and raw materials; using forced laborers, including approximately 8.4 million foreign workers brought to Germany during the war; and extracting Jewish property from deportees before murdering them. Terror was the fundamental mechanism of imperial control, applied through the SS, the Gestapo, the Einsatzgruppen, and the camp system.

Decline and legacy: The Nazi empire collapsed as rapidly as it had risen. By 1945, Germany was occupied by Allied forces on all sides. The Nuremberg Trials attempted to impose legal accountability on those who had built and operated the empire. The postwar settlement dismembered the Third Reich entirely, restored conquered territories, expelled ethnic Germans from Eastern Europe, and imposed democratic institutions on western Germany under Allied occupation. The memory of the Nazi empire and its crimes has shaped German political culture, European integration, international human rights law, and global memory for the eight decades since its destruction.

Chapter Summary

World War II was the most destructive conflict in human history, killing between seventy and eighty-five million people across the globe between 1939 and 1945. It was a war that grew from the unresolved tensions of the post-World War I settlement, the economic and political catastrophe of the Great Depression, and the rise of fascism and ultranationalism in Germany, Italy, and Japan — revisionist powers committed to overturning the existing international order by force. The failure of democratic nations and international institutions to restrain Axis aggression in the 1930s, through appeasement and the ineffectiveness of the League of Nations, allowed the aggressors to build up their military power and make their ambitions more extreme until the invasion of Poland in September 1939 triggered a war that had become effectively unavoidable.

The war was genuinely global. In Asia, Japan's war against China had been underway since 1937, and Japan's expansion into Southeast Asia and the attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941 brought the United States into the conflict and merged the Asian and European theaters into a single worldwide struggle. The principal theaters were the Eastern Front between Germany and the Soviet Union — the largest and most destructive land campaign in history — and the Pacific, where American naval and amphibious operations gradually compressed Japan's island empire. Secondary theaters in North Africa, the Mediterranean, Southeast Asia, the Atlantic Ocean, and the air war over Germany and Japan were all interconnected parts of the same global conflict.

The turning points of the war came in 1942 and 1943. The Battle of Stalingrad (November 1942–February 1943) ended Germany's ability to take the strategic initiative on the Eastern Front and began the long Soviet advance that would ultimately reach Berlin. The Battle of Midway (June 1942) destroyed Japan's offensive naval capacity and gave the United States the strategic initiative in the Pacific. The Allied victories in North Africa and the subsequent Italian campaign (1942–1943) and the Normandy landings (June 1944) opened the Western Front and accelerated Germany's military collapse. By May 1945, Germany had surrendered unconditionally; by September 1945, Japan had done the same, hastened by the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and by the Soviet declaration of war against Japan.

The Holocaust — the systematic, state-organized murder of approximately six million Jewish people and millions of others by the Nazi German regime and its collaborators — was the defining atrocity of the war and one of the worst crimes in human history. Rooted in a racist ideology that identified Jewish people as the supreme enemy of humanity, it was carried out through the normal mechanisms of the German state: legislation, administrative coordination, bureaucratic organization, and industrial technology. The stages of persecution moved from legal exclusion and forced emigration (1933–1938) through ghettoization and forced labor (1939–1941) to systematic mass murder using mobile killing units and extermination camps (1941–1945). Approximately two-thirds of the entire prewar Jewish population of Europe was murdered. The destruction of European Jewish communities, along with the mass killing of Roma, disabled people, Soviet prisoners of war, and others, was a genocide that demands permanent historical attention and moral reckoning.

The experience of occupied peoples — European populations under German rule, Asian populations under Japanese occupation — ranged from enthusiastic collaboration to passive accommodation to active resistance. The Holocaust required not only a criminal state but the participation, acquiescence, and willful ignorance of ordinary people across occupied Europe. Resistance, while a minority response, took many forms: military partisan warfare, intelligence gathering, sabotage, the rescue of Jewish people, and the maintenance of cultural and human dignity in the face of dehumanization. These choices — to collaborate, to accommodate, to resist — were always, at some level, human choices made under constrained circumstances, and the full moral accounting of the occupation period has required decades of honest historical work in every affected country.

The war was a total war in which civilian populations bore an unprecedented share of the violence. Strategic bombing deliberately targeted urban areas; German occupation policies deliberately killed civilians through starvation, mass shooting, and deportation; Japanese forces committed systematic atrocities against civilian populations in China and Southeast Asia; and the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945 killed between 130,000 and 226,000 people and inaugurated the nuclear age. The ethical and legal questions raised by these dimensions of the war's conduct drove the postwar development of international humanitarian law and the laws of war.

The aftermath of the war reshaped the political, demographic, and moral landscape of the world. Germany was divided and occupied; Japan was democratized under American occupation; the United Nations was created to provide a new mechanism for international peace and security. The Nuremberg and Tokyo war crimes trials established the principles of individual criminal responsibility for crimes against humanity, laying the foundation for subsequent international human rights law. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Western European economies. Decolonization — driven by the weakening of European colonial powers, the rhetoric of self-determination, and the organizational experience of wartime resistance — proceeded rapidly in Asia and Africa in the decades after the war.

The Cold War that dominated global politics from 1947 to 1991 grew directly from the wartime alliance between the United States and the Soviet Union and the tensions that alliance had always concealed. The atomic bombs that ended the Pacific war inaugurated the nuclear age and established the existential threat of nuclear annihilation that shaped the entire Cold War era. The establishment of the state of Israel in 1948, partly in response to the Holocaust and the displacement of hundreds of thousands of Jewish survivors, transformed the politics of the Middle East in ways that continue to shape global affairs. The United Nations, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the Genocide Convention, and the Geneva Conventions — all products of the immediate postwar moment — reflected the attempt to build a world in which what had happened between 1939 and 1945 could never happen again. The degree to which that attempt has succeeded and failed is one of the central questions of contemporary world history.

Understanding World War II is essential to understanding the world that followed it and the world we inhabit today. The war demonstrated that modern states could organize genocide on an industrial scale; that democracies could be corrupted by fear and appeasement; that ordinary people could be complicit in extraordinary evil; and that the ideals of human rights and human dignity, however fragile, could be defended at great cost and ultimately preserved. It was a catastrophe of almost inconceivable scale, and it was also a moment of remarkable moral courage, human endurance, and the collective refusal of many millions of people to accept the world that fascism and genocide would have made.

Key Terms

Antisemitism
Hostility toward, prejudice against, or discrimination against Jewish people as a religious, ethnic, or racial group. In the Nazi context, antisemitism was a pseudo-scientific racial ideology that identified Jewish people as a biological enemy of the Aryan race.
Appeasement
The British and French policy of making concessions to Hitler's territorial demands in the late 1930s in hopes of avoiding war; culminating in the 1938 Munich Agreement. Its failure discredited the strategy as a means of dealing with expansionist dictatorships.
Atomic bomb
A weapon using nuclear fission to produce an explosive yield equivalent to thousands of tons of conventional explosive. First used in warfare against Hiroshima (August 6, 1945) and Nagasaki (August 9, 1945), inaugurating the nuclear age.
Axis powers
The alliance of Germany, Italy, and Japan, formalized in the 1940 Tripartite Pact, which fought against the Allied powers in World War II.
Blitzkrieg
"Lightning war" — the German combined-arms military technique of rapid tank-led advances supported by motorized infantry and close air support, used to break through defensive lines and exploit breakthroughs before defenders could regroup.
Collaboration
Cooperation with an occupying power by individuals, groups, or governments in conquered territories. In World War II, this ranged from enthusiastic ideological alignment to instrumental cooperation for survival, to participation in the persecution of targeted groups.
Concentration camp
A camp in which large numbers of people, especially political prisoners or members of persecuted minorities, are deliberately imprisoned under harsh conditions. In the Nazi system, distinct from extermination camps, though the line blurred as the war progressed.
Crimes against humanity
A category of international crime, established at the Nuremberg Trials, encompassing murder, extermination, enslavement, deportation, and other inhumane acts committed against civilian populations. Distinct from war crimes in that they need not occur in connection with armed conflict.
D-Day
June 6, 1944 — the Allied amphibious invasion of Normandy, France, the largest amphibious operation in history, which opened the Western Front and accelerated Germany's military defeat.
Einsatzgruppen
Mobile SS killing units that followed German armies into the Soviet Union and occupied Eastern Europe, systematically shooting Jewish people, Soviet political officers, and others designated as enemies. Responsible for approximately two million deaths.
Extermination camp
A facility built specifically for the mass killing of prisoners, as distinct from concentration camps, which were primarily detention and forced labor facilities. The six main extermination camps — Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek — were all located in occupied Poland.
Final Solution
The Nazi euphemism for their plan to murder all Jewish people in German-controlled Europe, coordinated at the Wannsee Conference in January 1942 and implemented through deportation to extermination camps.
Genocide
The deliberate and systematic destruction, in whole or in part, of a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group. Coined by Raphael Lemkin in 1944 and codified in international law by the 1948 UN Genocide Convention.
Grand Alliance
The wartime coalition of the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Soviet Union (plus dozens of other nations) that defeated the Axis powers; characterized by military cooperation despite profound ideological differences.
Hibakusha
Japanese term for "bomb-affected people" — the survivors of the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, who faced radiation sickness, long-term health effects, and social stigma in the postwar decades.
Holocaust
The systematic, state-organized murder of approximately six million Jewish people and millions of others by the Nazi German regime and its collaborators between 1933 and 1945. The paradigmatic case of genocide in twentieth-century history.
Home front
The civilian sphere of a warring nation, encompassing industrial production, agricultural mobilization, rationing, propaganda, and the social transformations produced by war — including the expansion of women's workforce participation.
Lebensraum
German for "living space" — Hitler's ideological concept that the German people required additional territory in Eastern Europe for settlement and resource extraction, to be acquired through the conquest and elimination of Slavic populations.
Manhattan Project
The American-led scientific program, with British and Canadian participation, that developed the first atomic bombs during World War II, culminating in the Trinity test of July 1945 and the atomic bombings of Japan in August 1945.
Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact
The non-aggression pact between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union signed in August 1939, with secret protocols dividing Eastern Europe into spheres of influence. Freed Hitler to invade Poland without fear of a two-front war; broken by Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941.
Nuremberg Laws
Two German racial laws enacted in September 1935 that stripped Jewish people of German citizenship and prohibited marriage and sexual relations between Jewish people and non-Jewish Germans, forming the legal basis for escalating discrimination.
Nuremberg Trials
The International Military Tribunal (1945–1946) that tried twenty-two major Nazi war criminals for crimes against peace, war crimes, and crimes against humanity, establishing the precedent of individual criminal responsibility under international law.
Operation Barbarossa
Germany's June 1941 invasion of the Soviet Union, the largest invasion in history; opened the Eastern Front, the war's most destructive theater, and ultimately proved fatal to Germany's war effort.
Resistance
The organized opposition to Axis occupation in World War II, ranging from armed partisan warfare to intelligence gathering, sabotage, underground publications, the rescue of persecuted people, and the preservation of cultural life under occupation.
Strategic bombing
The use of air power to attack an enemy's industrial capacity, transportation infrastructure, and civilian morale rather than (or in addition to) purely military targets. Used extensively by all major air powers in World War II, with devastating effects on civilian populations.
Total war
A form of conflict in which belligerent states mobilize all available resources for the war effort and in which the distinction between military combatants and civilian noncombatants is significantly eroded or deliberately ignored.
Wannsee Conference
A January 1942 meeting of senior Nazi officials, convened by Reinhard Heydrich, to coordinate the administrative implementation of the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question" — the plan to murder all Jewish people in German-controlled Europe.

Major People, Places, and Polities

Identification & Significance

Adolf Hitler (1889–1945)
Chancellor and Führer of Nazi Germany; the primary architect of German aggression and the Holocaust; committed suicide in Berlin on April 30, 1945, as Soviet forces closed in.
Franklin D. Roosevelt (1882–1945)
U.S. President who led the country through the war from Pearl Harbor until his death in April 1945; architect of the Lend-Lease program and the Grand Alliance.
Winston Churchill (1874–1965)
British Prime Minister whose leadership after the fall of France sustained British resistance and articulated the moral stakes of the conflict against fascism.
Joseph Stalin (1878–1953)
Soviet dictator who led the country through its devastating losses in the war against Germany; consolidated Soviet control over Eastern Europe in the postwar settlement.
Harry S. Truman (1884–1972)
U.S. President who succeeded Roosevelt in April 1945 and made the decision to use atomic bombs against Japan; oversaw the postwar establishment of the United Nations and the Marshall Plan.
Chiang Kai-shek (1887–1975)
Leader of the Chinese Nationalist government and commander of Chinese forces resisting Japan; led the government-in-exile after the Communist victory in the Chinese Civil War.
Mao Zedong (1893–1976)
Leader of the Chinese Communist Party who fought both Japanese invaders and the Nationalist government; emerged as the leader of the People's Republic of China after the Communist victory in 1949.
Erwin Rommel (1891–1944)
German field marshal who commanded German and Italian forces in North Africa, earning the nickname "the Desert Fox"; later involved in the July 1944 plot against Hitler and forced to commit suicide.
Georgy Zhukov (1896–1974)
Soviet marshal who commanded at Stalingrad, Kursk, and the final assault on Berlin; the most prominent Soviet military commander of the war.
Auschwitz-Birkenau
The largest Nazi extermination complex, located in occupied Poland near the town of Oświęcim; approximately 1.1 million people were murdered there, approximately 90 percent Jewish.
Stalingrad (now Volgograd)
Soviet industrial city on the Volga River; site of the war's most decisive turning-point battle (August 1942–February 1943), in which the Soviet Army destroyed Germany's Sixth Army.
Normandy
Region of northern France; site of the D-Day Allied landings of June 6, 1944, the largest amphibious operation in history and the beginning of the decisive Western Front campaign.
The Warsaw Ghetto
The largest Nazi-established ghetto, in occupied Warsaw, Poland; held over 400,000 Jewish people in horrific conditions before systematic deportation to Treblinka; site of the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (April–May 1943).
Hiroshima and Nagasaki
Japanese cities destroyed by American atomic bombs on August 6 and 9, 1945; the bombings killed between 130,000 and 226,000 people and precipitated Japan's surrender.
The United Nations
International organization established in 1945 to maintain peace and security, promote human rights, and provide mechanisms for international cooperation; successor to the failed League of Nations.

Chapter Reflection

World War II was not simply the largest war in human history. It was a civilizational catastrophe that revealed the darkest possibilities of modernity — the capacity of industrialized states to organize genocide on a continental scale, to mobilize entire economies and societies for destruction, and to reduce human beings to categories defined by race, nationality, or ideology and subject those categories to systematic elimination. It was also a conflict that revealed remarkable human endurance, moral courage, and the stubborn refusal of many millions of people to accept the world that fascism and genocide would have made. The tension between these two realities — the scale of destruction and the persistence of resistance and humanity within it — is what makes the history of World War II both morally necessary to know and genuinely difficult to comprehend.

This chapter's place in the larger arc of world history is significant. It closes the period that began with World War I and the chapter on the interwar crisis — showing how the unresolved tensions of 1919 and the catastrophic conditions of the Depression produced the second global catastrophe within a generation. And it opens the period that follows: the Cold War, decolonization, the human rights revolution, and the transformation of the international order that are examined in the final parts of this textbook. Almost every development in the second half of the twentieth century traces directly from the consequences of World War II: the division of Germany and Korea, the establishment of Israel, the independence of India and Indonesia and dozens of African nations, the nuclear arms race, the construction of the United Nations system, and the slow, contested, and still incomplete development of international law and institutions for the protection of human rights.

The Holocaust demands particular historical attention precisely because of its magnitude and its modernity. It was not a medieval massacre or a premodern atrocity; it was the product of an industrial state in the twentieth century, organized through bureaucratic administration and executed with modern technology, participated in by educated people in a country with a distinguished cultural tradition. Understanding how this happened — and resisting the comfortable assumption that it could only have happened in Germany at that time and could never happen elsewhere — is one of the most important intellectual and moral tasks that the study of world history poses. The genocide scholar Gregory Stanton has identified stages common to genocides — classification, symbolization, discrimination, dehumanization, organization, polarization, preparation, persecution, extermination, denial — that can be recognized before the killing begins, if observers and institutions are willing to see them. The Holocaust is not only a historical event to be studied; it is a warning about what human societies are capable of, and about the conditions and decisions that lead there.

The connections between World War II and the larger themes of world history examined throughout this textbook are numerous. The war was an expression of imperial competition between industrial nation-states — the same force that had driven World War I, colonialism, and the global integration of the nineteenth century. The Holocaust was an extreme expression of racial hierarchy — a world-historical framework that had justified slavery, colonial conquest, and the subordination of non-European peoples for centuries, taken to its logical and genocidal extreme. The war's end and the postwar human rights revolution were an attempt — partial, contested, and never complete — to construct an international order that rejected the racial hierarchies and imperial ambitions that had produced such catastrophe. Understanding that attempt, its achievements and its failures, is essential to understanding the contemporary world that this textbook's remaining chapters address.

Students who have studied this chapter carefully will bring to the rest of the textbook a deeper understanding of what modern states are capable of, what international institutions are for and what their limits are, what the human rights system was designed to protect against, and why the history of genocide, resistance, and accountability demands permanent attention. They will also carry with them the specific and irreducible stories of those who lived through the war — the soldiers and civilians, the perpetrators and resisters, the survivors and the murdered — whose experiences cannot be reduced to statistics or historical trends and who deserve to be known as what they were: human beings whose lives and deaths mattered, and whose history we are still learning to understand and honor honestly.

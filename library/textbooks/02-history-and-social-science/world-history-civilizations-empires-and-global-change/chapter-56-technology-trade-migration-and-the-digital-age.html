Part XI — The Contemporary World, 1991–Present

Technology, Trade, Migration, and the Digital Age

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The digital age is not a break from history. It is its continuation — accelerated, networked, and global."

In the autumn of 2010, a twenty-six-year-old fruit vendor named Mohamed Bouazizi set himself on fire in the small Tunisian town of Sidi Bouzid. His act of desperation, born of police harassment, poverty, and humiliation, might once have gone unnoticed beyond his own community. Instead, within hours, video of protests that erupted in response was shared across Facebook and YouTube. Within weeks, street demonstrations had spread to Tunis. Within months, the government of President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali — who had ruled Tunisia for twenty-three years — had collapsed. The Arab Spring that followed, sweeping through Egypt, Libya, Bahrain, Syria, and Yemen, was shaped in profound ways by the digital infrastructure of the early twenty-first century: smartphones, social-media platforms, satellite television, and instant global messaging. The events illustrated something historians would long debate — not whether technology causes revolution, but how deeply it transforms the conditions in which human beings act, organize, resist, and reimagine their world.

This chapter examines the digital transformation of the contemporary world, roughly from the late twentieth century into the mid-twenty-first. It explores how computers, the internet, mobile technology, social media, automation, and artificial intelligence reshaped economies, politics, cultures, and everyday lives across the globe. It does not treat this transformation as a single story of triumphant progress, nor does it see technology as a neutral tool that societies merely apply. Instead, it follows historians, economists, sociologists, and political scientists who have argued that digital technology is itself a social artifact — shaped by human choices, power structures, market incentives, and cultural assumptions — and that its effects have been profoundly uneven across regions, classes, genders, and generations.

The chapter also examines migration, one of the most significant forces reshaping the contemporary world. In the early twenty-first century, approximately one billion people — one in seven human beings — lived outside their country or region of birth. Migrants moved for work, for family, fleeing war and persecution, responding to climate displacement, and seeking opportunity. Their journeys connected the Global South to the Global North, transformed cities on every continent, built diaspora communities that maintained ties across thousands of miles, and remitted billions of dollars annually to families in the developing world. Migration became one of the most contentious political issues in democratic societies even as it remained indispensable to global economic functioning.

Technology and migration intersected in complex ways. Digital platforms enabled migrants to maintain cultural identity and family connection across borders in ways previously impossible. Remittance flows were transformed by mobile banking and fintech services. Migrants used smartphones to navigate dangerous crossings, communicate with smugglers, and seek asylum procedures. Meanwhile, governments deployed digital surveillance, biometric databases, drone monitoring, and algorithmic screening to manage, restrict, and in some cases criminalize movement across borders. The digital age did not simply liberate migrants; it changed the conditions of their journeys, their labor, and their political vulnerability in ways that require careful historical analysis.

The chapter situates these developments in a world shaped by the aftermath of the Cold War, the spread of neoliberal economic policies, the rise of China and other emerging economies, deepening climate crisis, and the persistent inequalities of the post-colonial global order. Digital technology did not appear in a vacuum. It emerged from military research networks, university laboratories, corporate investment strategies, and government subsidies — primarily in the United States, Western Europe, Japan, and later China. Its benefits flowed disproportionately to those who already possessed capital, education, and political power. Its costs — in labor displacement, surveillance, environmental impact, and cultural disruption — fell disproportionately on those with the least.

Historical evidence for this period is extraordinarily abundant and extraordinarily challenging. The digital era has produced more recorded information than any previous period in human history — but much of it is ephemeral, platform-dependent, algorithmically mediated, or proprietary. Historians face new problems: how to archive social-media posts that platforms delete; how to interpret data that companies own; how to assess the reliability of sources produced in environments designed to generate outrage, engagement, and advertising revenue. At the same time, oral histories, ethnographic research, journalism, government documents, corporate filings, international organization reports, and the rich testimony of migrants, workers, and digital users themselves offer historians a dense evidentiary record through which to reconstruct and interpret this transformative era.

What changed? Almost everything about the infrastructure of communication, commerce, labor, surveillance, and social life. What continued? The fundamental dynamics of power, inequality, cultural resilience, and human adaptability that have shaped world history across centuries. Understanding both dimensions — what is genuinely new in the digital age and what connects it to deep historical patterns — is the central task of this chapter.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the origins and development of the digital revolution, tracing how computers and networks evolved from military and academic tools into global commercial and social infrastructure.
  2. Trace the spread of the internet and mobile technology across world regions, identifying patterns of access, inequality, and adaptation.
  3. Analyze how social-media platforms transformed political communication, social movements, and public discourse in multiple world regions.
  4. Evaluate the economic effects of automation and artificial intelligence on labor markets, distinguishing between different regions, economic sectors, and social groups.
  5. Describe the structure and power of digital platform economies, explaining how companies like Alphabet, Meta, Amazon, Alibaba, and Tencent achieved global economic dominance.
  6. Identify major forms of cybersecurity threat, information conflict, and digital surveillance, and explain their significance for states, corporations, and individuals.
  7. Compare patterns of contemporary migration across world regions, explaining push factors, pull factors, migration routes, and the political responses of receiving societies.
  8. Contextualize diaspora and transnational community formation, explaining how migrants maintained cultural identity, family ties, and political engagement across borders.
  9. Connect technological change to social movements, explaining how digital tools both enabled and constrained activism and resistance.
  10. Assess the environmental costs of digital technology, including energy consumption, electronic waste, and the carbon footprint of global data infrastructure.
  11. Distinguish between techno-optimist and techno-critical perspectives on digital change, evaluating their historical and evidentiary bases.
  12. Synthesize the themes of technology, migration, and digital change into a coherent analysis of the contemporary world's continuities and transformations.

  2. In what ways did the internet and social media transform political life, and were those transformations equally liberating or equally threatening across different societies?

Historical Context

The digital revolution of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries did not emerge from nowhere. Its roots lay in the mid-twentieth century's intersection of military research, academic science, corporate innovation, and Cold War geopolitics. Understanding where digital technology came from — who built it, who funded it, and for what purposes — is essential to understanding how it shaped and was shaped by the world it entered.

The intellectual foundations of computing reach back to the 1930s and 1940s. British mathematician Alan Turing developed theoretical models of computation in 1936 that would later underpin the architecture of modern computers. During World War II, computing machines were built for code-breaking, ballistic calculation, and logistics management. The ENIAC, completed at the University of Pennsylvania in 1945, was one of the earliest general-purpose electronic computers. These early machines were enormous, expensive, and accessible only to governments and research institutions.

The Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union after 1947 drove massive investment in computing, aerospace, communications, and electronics. The U.S. Department of Defense funded the development of ARPANET in 1969 — a network connecting university and military research computers that would become the technical ancestor of the internet. The Cold War also produced the space race, which drove miniaturization of electronics and the development of satellite communications. Silicon Valley in California emerged as a center of semiconductor and computer hardware innovation, supported by defense contracts, venture capital, and proximity to elite universities.

The 1970s and 1980s brought the personal computer revolution. Companies like Apple, founded in 1976, and IBM, which introduced its Personal Computer in 1981, moved computing from institution to individual. By the late 1980s, personal computers were becoming common in offices, homes, and schools across the wealthy industrialized world — though access remained deeply stratified by class, geography, and gender. The creation of the World Wide Web by British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee in 1989–1991 transformed the internet from a tool for specialists into a medium accessible to ordinary users. The commercialization of the internet in the 1990s unleashed a wave of entrepreneurial energy, and the dot-com boom of 1995–2000 built the platform infrastructure of the digital economy even as thousands of speculative ventures failed.

The societies the digital revolution entered were already deeply marked by the economic transformations of the previous decades. The neoliberal turn of the 1980s — associated with Ronald Reagan in the United States and Margaret Thatcher in Britain — had reduced the role of the state in economic regulation, accelerated the globalization of trade and finance, weakened labor unions, and deepened inequality within and between nations. The fall of the Soviet Union in 1991 opened new markets across Eastern Europe and Central Asia to global capital. China's integration into the global economy, accelerating dramatically after its accession to the World Trade Organization in 2001, added hundreds of millions of workers and consumers to global supply chains. The digital revolution entered this world of globalized, deregulated, deeply unequal capitalism, and the two forces shaped each other profoundly.

Migration was not new in the late twentieth century, but its scale, direction, and character were transformed. Decolonization from the 1940s through the 1970s had set in motion large-scale movements of people from former colonies to former metropoles. Economic liberalization in the 1980s and 1990s intensified labor migration from the Global South to the Global North. War, civil conflict, and state failure — in Afghanistan, Somalia, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Iraq, Syria, and Central America — produced millions of refugees and displaced persons. Climate change began to intersect with poverty and conflict to produce new forms of displacement. By the early twenty-first century, migration was not a marginal phenomenon but a structural feature of the global economy and a central question of global politics.

Regional Orientation

North America — The United States remained the primary originator of digital technology through the early twenty-first century. Silicon Valley in California concentrated venture capital, engineering talent, and corporate headquarters for most of the world's dominant internet platforms. The U.S.-Mexico border became one of the world's most heavily monitored migration frontiers, with digital surveillance technologies deployed at massive scale.

Western Europe — Western European countries were early adopters of internet technology and became significant markets for U.S. platforms. The European Union developed a distinctive regulatory posture, including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) of 2018. Europe also faced large-scale migration from Africa, the Middle East, and South and Southeast Asia that became a central political issue, contributing to the rise of nationalist parties across the continent.

East Asia — China developed a distinct digital ecosystem — including WeChat, Alibaba, Baidu, and TikTok — operating behind the "Great Firewall." China's digital economy became the world's second largest. Japan and South Korea were leaders in robotics and semiconductor manufacturing. The region was central to global electronics manufacturing supply chains.

South and Southeast Asia — India emerged as a major digital economy, with the world's largest English-speaking tech workforce and one of the world's largest internet user populations by the 2010s. Mobile banking and fintech innovation spread rapidly across the region. It also supplied significant migrant labor to the Gulf states and to Europe and North America.

Sub-Saharan Africa — Africa experienced one of the world's fastest rates of mobile internet adoption, enabled by affordable smartphones and mobile data networks. Mobile banking — particularly M-Pesa in Kenya — demonstrated how digital tools could bypass traditional financial infrastructure. Africa also received enormous diaspora remittance flows from workers abroad.

Latin America — Brazil, Mexico, and other Latin American countries developed growing e-commerce, fintech, and social-media sectors. The region was a major source of migration to the United States and within the continent. Social media played a significant role in political movements across the region.

The Middle East and North Africa — The Arab Spring dramatically illustrated the political potential and limits of social media. Gulf states invested heavily in digital infrastructure and attracted migrant labor from South Asia and Southeast Asia. The region remained a major source of refugee flows, particularly from Syria, Iraq, and Yemen.

Section 56.1

The Digital Revolution

Origins and Foundations

The word "revolution" is used freely in popular discussion of digital technology, sometimes so freely that it loses meaning. Historians approach the term more carefully. A revolution, in historical usage, implies a fundamental transformation in the structures of society — not merely a change in tools or techniques, but a change in how power is organized, how labor is performed, how people communicate and organize, and how the material conditions of everyday life are constituted. By this standard, the digital revolution of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries qualifies — with the important caveat that like all historical revolutions, it was uneven, contested, incomplete, and continuous with what came before.

The digital revolution built on a century of electrical and electronic innovation. The telegraph of the nineteenth century, the telephone, radio and television broadcasting, and the transistor — invented at Bell Laboratories in 1947 — all contributed to the infrastructure of electronic communication that made the digital age possible. The transistor was transformative: it replaced bulky vacuum tubes, enabling the miniaturization of electronic circuits that would eventually produce the microprocessor, the personal computer, and the smartphone. The integrated circuit, developed independently by Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments and Robert Noyce at Fairchild Semiconductor in 1958–1959, allowed thousands and eventually billions of transistors to be packed onto a single chip of silicon — the microchip that gave Silicon Valley its name.

Government investment played a decisive role in digital technology's development that is often underappreciated in popular narratives celebrating private entrepreneurship. ARPANET was funded by the U.S. Defense Department's Advanced Research Projects Agency. The technologies that made the iPhone possible — touchscreens, GPS, cellular networks, the internet itself — all received substantial public funding at some stage of development. This history matters for understanding not only where digital technology came from, but also who benefited when it was commercialized and privatized in the 1990s and 2000s.

The commercialization of the internet in the 1990s transformed it from a research tool into a global commercial and social medium. The dot-com boom of 1995–2000 attracted enormous venture-capital investment into internet-based businesses. Most failed. But those that survived — Amazon (founded 1994), Google (founded 1998), and others — built the platform infrastructure of the digital economy. The dot-com crash of 2000–2001 wiped out speculative excess but left intact the fiber-optic networks, software architectures, and business models that would define the internet's next phase.

The Web 2.0 Era

The period roughly from 2004 to 2012 is often called the "Web 2.0" era — a shift from the early web, where websites were primarily read-only destinations, to an interactive web where users created content, built social networks, and participated in platform communities. Wikipedia (2001) allowed collaborative global knowledge construction. Blogger, MySpace, and later Facebook allowed ordinary people to publish text and images. YouTube (2005) enabled anyone with a camera to broadcast to a global audience. Twitter (2006) created a medium for real-time public conversation across geographic and social boundaries.

These platforms were not neutral spaces. Their economic model — which became known as "surveillance capitalism" — depended on collecting detailed behavioral data from users, analyzing it to predict behavior, and selling that predictive capacity to advertisers. This model created powerful incentives to maximize engagement — to keep users clicking, scrolling, and reacting — which in turn created incentives to amplify emotionally provocative content, regardless of its accuracy or social value. The user was not simply a customer; the user's attention and data were the product being sold.

The Web 2.0 era also produced dramatic expansion of digital access beyond wealthy countries. Mobile networks, which required far less physical infrastructure than fixed-line broadband, spread rapidly across Africa, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Latin America. In many regions, the mobile phone was the first internet-connected device that ordinary people owned, bypassing the personal-computer stage entirely. By the early 2010s, billions of people were connected to the internet through smartphones — transforming the digital revolution from a phenomenon concentrated in wealthy industrialized countries into a genuinely global force.

Historical Vocabulary
Surveillance Capitalism

A term coined by scholar Shoshana Zuboff to describe an economic system in which the primary commodity is human behavioral data. Under surveillance capitalism, digital platforms collect detailed information about users' activities, preferences, social connections, locations, and psychological states — often without users' explicit awareness or informed consent. This data is analyzed by algorithms to predict human behavior, and those predictions are sold to advertisers, political campaigns, and other clients who wish to influence behavior.

Understanding surveillance capitalism as an economic system — with its own logic, incentives, and power structures — helps historians and students analyze why platforms are designed the way they are, why algorithmic amplification of engagement-maximizing content is not accidental, and what regulatory responses might be appropriate. Unlike earlier forms of industrial capitalism, which extracted value primarily from physical labor, surveillance capitalism extracts value from behavioral data generated by virtually all human activity mediated through digital devices.

Section 56.2

Computers, Networks, and the Internet

From Mainframes to Personal Computing

The history of computing is in part a history of scale — of machines shrinking from room-filling institutional systems to portable personal devices. But it is equally a history of access: who could use computing, for what purposes, and at what cost. The mainframe era, from the 1950s through the 1970s, concentrated computing power in large institutions. The personal computer revolution of the late 1970s and 1980s disrupted this concentration. Apple's Apple II (1977) and IBM's PC (1981) brought computing into homes and small businesses. Microsoft's operating systems standardized the software environment, enabling a vast ecosystem of application software. By the early 1990s, the basic tools of word processing, spreadsheets, and database management had transformed office work across the Global North.

The internet transformed personal computing from a tool for individual productivity into a medium for global communication and commerce. The key technical breakthroughs were packet-switching networks — which broke data into discrete packets that could travel independently and be reassembled at the destination — and the TCP/IP protocol suite, which provided a common language for computers to communicate across different network architectures. These technologies, developed in academic and government contexts, formed the foundation on which the commercial internet was built.

Global Internet Infrastructure

The physical infrastructure of the internet — the cables, servers, routers, and data centers that carry and process digital traffic — is both more tangible and more geographically concentrated than many users realize. Approximately 95 percent of intercontinental internet traffic travels through undersea fiber-optic cables — a remarkable piece of physical infrastructure that follows, in many cases, routes established by nineteenth-century telegraph cables. Data centers, the server farms that store and process the world's digital content, are concentrated in the United States (particularly northern Virginia, the world's largest data center cluster), Western Europe, and East Asia. This geography concentrates both technical control and vulnerability in a small number of locations, largely corresponding to the historical geography of economic power.

The Digital Divide

One of the most consequential patterns in the digital revolution's global spread is the "digital divide" — the gap between those with meaningful access to digital technology and those without. In 2000, fewer than 10 percent of the world's population used the internet, and the vast majority were in North America, Western Europe, and Japan. By 2020, more than half the world's population was connected — a remarkable expansion — but the quality of connection remained deeply unequal. Broadband speeds in wealthy countries were often hundreds of times faster than mobile connections in poor countries. Data costs as a share of income were dramatically higher in Africa and South Asia than in Europe or North America.

Within countries, the digital divide intersected with other forms of social inequality. In the United States, high-speed internet access was strongly correlated with income and race, with rural, Black, and Native American communities consistently underserved. In India, the enormous growth of smartphone ownership coexisted with persistent gender gaps in digital access — men were significantly more likely than women to own smartphones and use the internet, reflecting broader gender inequalities in education, income, and public life. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the spread of mobile internet created new forms of digital inclusion while leaving significant portions of the population — particularly women, the elderly, and rural communities — still excluded. Simply providing connectivity did not guarantee meaningful digital participation; digital literacy, relevant content in local languages, and affordable services were equally important.

Section 56.3

Mobile Technology and Social Media

The Smartphone Revolution

The introduction of Apple's iPhone in June 2007 is often cited as a watershed moment in the digital revolution. What the iPhone did was combine a genuinely usable touchscreen interface, a powerful operating system, mobile internet, and — after the introduction of the App Store in 2008 — a platform for third-party software that enabled an explosion of digital services. Google's Android operating system, introduced in 2008 and adopted by Samsung, Huawei, Xiaomi, and dozens of other manufacturers, brought smartphone capability to a much wider global market at lower price points.

In Africa, Latin America, and South and Southeast Asia, the mobile internet revolution leapfrogged the personal-computer stage. By the mid-2010s, hundreds of millions of people in these regions were connected to the internet primarily or exclusively through mobile phones. This created new possibilities for digital commerce, education, health services, and civic participation — and new vulnerabilities to mobile misinformation, platform monopolies, and surveillance. The smartphone also concentrated everyday life in wealthy countries — navigation, photography, communication, news, entertainment, financial services — through a small number of corporations: Apple and Google controlled the dominant mobile operating systems, and their App Stores exercised gatekeeping power over what software reached users.

Social Media Platforms

Facebook, founded by Mark Zuckerberg at Harvard University in 2004, grew from a campus social network into a global platform with over two billion active users by the late 2010s. Its core product was the social graph combined with a News Feed algorithm that determined which content each user saw, optimized for engagement. Facebook's 2012 acquisition of Instagram and its 2014 acquisition of WhatsApp gave it control over multiple major communication platforms, raising concerns about monopoly power and the concentration of social communication in a single corporate entity.

In China, a distinct ecosystem of social-media and messaging platforms developed, reflecting both government requirements for censorship and surveillance compliance and the entrepreneurial vitality of Chinese digital companies. WeChat, developed by Tencent, became one of the world's most functionally comprehensive apps — combining social messaging, payments, e-commerce, government services, and entertainment in a single platform. Douyin (known internationally as TikTok) pioneered the short-form video format that would spread globally. TikTok's global rise from 2018 onward illustrated both the genuinely global character of the digital economy and its geopolitical tensions. Governments in the United States, India, and Europe raised concerns about data security and potential Chinese government access to user data. India banned TikTok in 2020 following border clashes with China — a sign that digital platforms had become sites of geopolitical contest.

Social Media and Political Life

The Arab Spring of 2010–2012 offered the most dramatic early case of social media's political potential. In Tunisia, Egypt, Bahrain, Libya, Syria, and Yemen, activists used Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, and satellite television to organize protests, document state violence, coordinate logistics, and communicate with international audiences. The speed with which information spread was genuinely unprecedented. Yet the Arab Spring also illustrated the limits of digital activism. Social media could coordinate protest but could not build the durable organizational structures necessary to consolidate political change. In Egypt, the fall of Mubarak was followed by a military coup in 2013. In Syria, the same tools used to coordinate opposition were used by the Assad government for surveillance and targeting of activists.

The 2016 Brexit referendum and U.S. presidential election brought new concerns about social media's political effects to the foreground. Research documented the role of Facebook in spreading misinformation, of Twitter in amplifying divisive content, and of Cambridge Analytica in using Facebook user data to micro-target political messaging. Russian government-linked accounts purchased advertising and operated networks of fake accounts to amplify political division. The revelations demonstrated that social-media platforms were not simply neutral communication tools but environments that could be exploited for political manipulation at scale.

Section 56.4

Automation and Artificial Intelligence

What Is Automation?

Automation — the replacement of human labor by machines or software — is not new. What was new in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries was the extension of automation into cognitive work — tasks previously considered to require human judgment, perception, language, and reasoning. This cognitive automation was enabled by advances in artificial intelligence, particularly machine learning. In machine learning, systems are not explicitly programmed with rules; instead, they are trained on large datasets to recognize patterns and make predictions. The development of deep learning — neural networks with many computational layers, trained on very large datasets — from around 2012 dramatically extended the range of cognitive tasks that could be automated. Systems could recognize images, transcribe speech, translate languages, detect fraud, diagnose diseases, and generate text with accuracy that sometimes equaled or exceeded human performance.

The economic and social consequences of this wave of automation were intensely debated. Some studies predicted that as many as half of all existing jobs in advanced economies could be automated within decades. Others argued that historical experience suggested automation would ultimately create more jobs than it destroyed. Most evidence suggested both arguments contained truth: automation did displace workers in specific sectors and skill categories, and new forms of employment did emerge — but the transition imposed real costs on specific communities, workers, and regions that were not automatically or equitably compensated by aggregate economic growth.

Automation and Global Labor

The effects of automation were distributed globally in complex ways. Manufacturing automation in wealthy countries reduced the cost advantage of offshoring production, potentially affecting employment in countries like China, Mexico, Bangladesh, and Vietnam that had built industrial economies around labor-intensive manufacturing. At the same time, cognitive automation created new forms of labor that were often invisible in public discussion. Training machine learning systems required enormous quantities of human-labeled data — images labeled by category, text labeled by sentiment, audio labeled by content. This "data labeling" work was distributed globally through platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk, employing workers in India, Kenya, Venezuela, and the Philippines at very low wages to perform the cognitive tasks that enabled AI systems to learn. The AI revolution thus created a new global division of labor: highly paid engineers in wealthy countries designing systems, while poorly paid workers in developing countries performed the routine cognitive labor that made those systems function.

Content moderation was another form of invisible digital labor. As social-media platforms scaled to billions of users, they required armies of human moderators to review flagged content — graphic violence, child exploitation material, terrorist propaganda. This work, outsourced primarily to companies in the Philippines, Kenya, and India, exposed workers to a constant stream of traumatic content at low wages and with minimal psychological support. Reports documented high rates of post-traumatic stress disorder among content-moderation workers.

Artificial Intelligence: Capabilities and Concerns

The 2010s saw rapid advances in AI capability, particularly in computer vision, natural-language processing, and game-playing. Google's DeepMind system AlphaGo defeated the world champion Go player in 2016 — a milestone many AI researchers had predicted was decades away. The development of large language models produced increasingly sophisticated text generation. The public launch of ChatGPT in November 2022 brought generative AI to mass public awareness. Within months, millions of users were using AI systems to draft emails, write code, summarize documents, and create images. ChatGPT reached 100 million users faster than any previous consumer application in history.

Concerns about AI extended well beyond labor displacement. AI systems trained on historical data could encode and amplify existing social biases — facial recognition systems with higher error rates for darker-skinned faces, credit-scoring systems that discriminated by proxy against racial minorities, hiring algorithms that downgraded female candidates. AI-generated content threatened to flood the information environment with convincing disinformation — fake images, audio, and video (deepfakes) — at a scale human fact-checking could not match. Governments responded with growing urgency: the EU's AI Act (2024) established a risk-based regulatory framework; the United States pursued executive orders and sector-specific guidance; China promoted AI development while maintaining strict state control over content generation.

Section 56.5

Digital Economies and Platform Power

The Rise of Platform Capitalism

The dominant companies of the digital economy were platforms — intermediaries that connected buyers and sellers, creators and audiences, service providers and clients — and extracted value from those connections. The economic logic of platforms rested on several distinctive features. First, network effects: the more users a platform attracted, the more valuable it became for each user, creating winner-take-all tendencies. Second, marginal cost near zero: once software infrastructure was built, adding an additional user cost almost nothing. Third, data advantages: platforms accumulated behavioral data that improved their services and blocked competitors from accessing the same advantage.

These features produced extraordinary concentration. By the early 2020s, five American companies — Alphabet, Meta, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft — had combined market capitalizations exceeding $10 trillion. In China, Alibaba, Tencent, and Baidu dominated digital commerce, messaging, and search. This concentration raised profound questions about monopoly power, democratic accountability, and the relationship between large corporations and the states in which they operated. Amazon's economic model illustrated the complexity: it began as an online bookstore, became the world's dominant e-commerce platform, added Amazon Web Services — which provided the digital infrastructure for a large share of the world's internet services, including many of Amazon's own competitors — and further diversified into physical stores, logistics, publishing, film, and healthcare.

The Gig Economy and Digital Labor

Platform capitalism created new forms of labor that defied traditional categories of employment. "Gig workers" — drivers for Uber and Lyft, delivery workers for DoorDash and Deliveroo, freelancers on Fiverr and Upwork — were formally classified as independent contractors rather than employees. This classification freed platform companies from obligations to pay minimum wages, provide benefits, or comply with workplace safety regulations. It transferred risk from the corporation to the worker and reduced labor costs significantly. The gig economy grew rapidly in the 2010s in wealthy countries and even more rapidly in developing countries where traditional employment protection was weaker. The political and legal status of gig workers became highly contentious. Courts and legislatures in different jurisdictions reached different conclusions: California's Proposition 22 (2020) classified gig workers as contractors; the UK Supreme Court ruled in 2021 that Uber drivers were workers entitled to minimum wage protections.

Global Trade and Digital Commerce

E-commerce platforms enabled small businesses and individual entrepreneurs to reach global markets directly, bypassing traditional intermediaries. The value of global e-commerce grew from under a trillion dollars in the early 2000s to over five trillion dollars annually by the early 2020s. Digital trade also raised new questions about international economic governance. Traditional trade agreements addressed goods crossing borders in physical form. Digital services crossed borders instantly, invisibly, and at near-zero marginal cost, challenging existing regulatory frameworks. Questions of who taxed platform profits, what rules governed cross-border data flows, and how developing countries could participate meaningfully in a digital economy whose infrastructure was concentrated in wealthy countries were actively debated in the WTO, the G20, and the OECD throughout the 2020s.

Section 56.6

Cybersecurity and Information Conflict

The Architecture of Digital Vulnerability

Every networked digital system is potentially vulnerable to unauthorized access, manipulation, or disruption. This fundamental fact — a consequence of the design choices made when the internet was built as an open, trust-based network — has made cybersecurity one of the most consequential domains of contemporary security policy. Individual criminals use phishing emails, malware, and ransomware to steal personal data and financial credentials for economic gain. Ransomware became a major criminal industry in the 2010s: the WannaCry attack in 2017 disrupted hospitals in the UK's National Health Service and affected organizations in over 150 countries; the 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack disrupted fuel supplies across the U.S. East Coast for days.

At the other end of the spectrum, state-sponsored actors use cyberattacks for espionage, sabotage, and strategic disruption. The Stuxnet worm, discovered in 2010 and attributed to a joint U.S.-Israeli operation, physically destroyed centrifuges at Iran's Natanz uranium enrichment facility — demonstrating that cyberattacks could cause physical damage. Russian-linked actors attacked Ukrainian power infrastructure in 2015 and 2016, the first confirmed cases of cyberattacks causing power outages. In 2020, a sophisticated intrusion exploiting a SolarWinds software update gave Russian intelligence access to thousands of organizations, including multiple U.S. federal agencies.

Surveillance and the Digital State

The digital revolution created unprecedented surveillance capabilities for states. Traditional intelligence agencies collected information by recruiting human sources and intercepting specific communications. Digital surveillance operated at a qualitatively different scale: it could collect and analyze the communications, location data, social connections, and behavioral patterns of entire populations. Edward Snowden's 2013 disclosures revealed the scope of the NSA's global surveillance programs. Programs like PRISM — which collected data directly from major internet companies including Google, Facebook, and Microsoft — demonstrated that the NSA was engaged in surveillance far beyond traditional intelligence practice, collecting information on millions of ordinary citizens and foreign nationals not subject to any specific investigation. The disclosures sparked an international debate about the proper scope of intelligence surveillance, the rights of non-citizens under American law, and the complicity of technology companies in state surveillance.

Information Conflict and Disinformation

The digital information environment became a domain of strategic conflict. Russia's Internet Research Agency operated large-scale social-media manipulation operations targeting the United States, France, Germany, and Ukraine, creating networks of fake accounts, producing divisive content, and purchasing targeted political advertising. Political operatives in many countries used similar techniques in domestic political contests. The spread of disinformation — false or misleading content shared at scale — became one of the defining problems of the digital age. Researchers at MIT documented that false news spread faster on Twitter than true news by every metric studied. The COVID-19 pandemic illustrated the lethal consequences of health disinformation at scale, as false information about the virus's origin, prevention, and treatment spread widely and contributed to vaccine hesitancy that cost lives.

Section 56.7

Migration in the Contemporary World

Scale, Scope, and Significance

Human migration is as old as humanity itself. What has changed in the contemporary world is not the fact of migration but its scale, speed, documentation, governance, and political visibility. In the early twenty-first century, approximately 281 million people — about 3.6 percent of the world's population — were international migrants living outside their country of birth, according to the International Organization for Migration. Including internal migrants — people who had moved within their own countries — the number of displaced or migrant persons reached over one billion. These movements followed the contours of global economic inequality, post-colonial ties, regional conflicts, and established migration networks built by earlier movers.

Types of Migration

Contemporary migration took many forms. Labor migration was the largest category. Low-skilled labor migrants moved from South Asia to the Gulf states — building the skyscrapers, stadiums, and infrastructure of Qatar, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia under conditions that human rights organizations frequently criticized as exploitative. They moved from Central America to the United States, from North and West Africa to Europe, and from poorer to wealthier regions within China (rural-to-urban internal migration estimated at over 200 million people by the 2010s). Highly skilled migration operated through different networks: engineers, doctors, and researchers moved between wealthy countries and from developing to wealthy countries through visa programs designed to attract talent — a "brain drain" that transferred human capital produced at public expense in developing countries to wealthy-country economies.

Forced migration was the most politically salient category. By 2023, UNHCR estimated that over 110 million people worldwide were forcibly displaced — the highest number ever recorded — including refugees who had crossed international borders, asylum seekers whose claims were pending, and internally displaced persons. The major sources of forced displacement included Syria, Afghanistan, South Sudan, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Ukraine (following Russia's full-scale invasion in 2022), and Myanmar.

Migration Routes and Dangers

The Mediterranean became one of the world's most deadly migration frontiers in the 2010s. Following the Arab Spring and the collapse of state order in Libya, hundreds of thousands of people attempted to cross the Mediterranean by boat each year — from Libya to Italy, from Turkey to Greece — fleeing conflicts and poverty in Syria, Afghanistan, Eritrea, and Sub-Saharan Africa. The International Organization for Migration estimated that over 25,000 people died in the Mediterranean between 2014 and 2022, making it the world's deadliest migration route. EU policies of maritime interdiction and containment — working with the Libyan Coast Guard — pushed migrants back to Libya, where human rights organizations documented detention conditions amounting to torture.

The U.S.-Mexico border was another of the world's most heavily monitored and dangerous migration frontiers. U.S. immigration enforcement expanded dramatically from the 1990s onward, including physical barriers, electronic surveillance, and agreements with Mexico and Central American governments to contain northward movement. These measures channeled migrants away from urban crossing points into remote desert terrain where temperatures exceeded 115°F in summer, leading to thousands of documented migrant deaths annually. In Southeast Asia, the Rohingya minority of Myanmar — subjected to a genocidal campaign beginning in 2017 — saw over 700,000 people flee across the border into Bangladesh in months, creating the world's largest refugee settlement in Cox's Bazar.

Political Responses to Migration

Migration became one of the most contentious political issues in liberal democracies during the 2010s. The large-scale arrival of asylum seekers in Europe in 2015–2016 — approximately 1.3 million applied for asylum in EU member states in 2015 alone — produced sharp political divisions. Populist nationalist parties surged across the continent. Hungary's Viktor Orbán built fences on his country's borders and promoted a vision of Christian European civilization threatened by Muslim immigration. Brexit was motivated in part by opposition to EU freedom of movement and concerns about immigration from Eastern Europe and beyond. In the United States, immigration was central to Donald Trump's 2016 campaign; his administration implemented the "zero tolerance" policy of 2018 that led to the separation of thousands of migrant children from their parents. Research by economists consistently found that immigration had broadly positive aggregate economic effects on receiving countries, but real costs in specific communities were distributed unevenly, and political demagogues effectively exploited that unevenness.

Historical Vocabulary
Push and Pull Factors

In migration studies, push factors are conditions in a migrant's origin country or region that motivate departure — including poverty, unemployment, political persecution, violence, natural disaster, and climate disruption. Pull factors are conditions in the destination country or region that attract migrants — including higher wages, political stability, personal security, family connections, and access to education and healthcare.

The push-pull framework helps students move beyond simplistic notions of migration as purely voluntary or purely coerced. Most contemporary migration flows reflect complex combinations of structural conditions and individual agency. Applying push-pull analysis comparatively — why do migrants from Central America go to the United States while migrants from West Africa go to Europe? — illuminates the role of geography, colonial history, legal frameworks, and established networks in shaping migration patterns. Migration theorists also caution that the model can oversimplify by obscuring the role of migration infrastructure, transnational networks, and the ways that legal frameworks create or close off pathways for movement.

Section 56.8

Diasporas and Transnational Communities

What Is a Diaspora?

The term "diaspora" — from the Greek for "scattering" — was originally used to describe the dispersal of Jewish communities from ancient Israel. In contemporary usage, diaspora describes a community of people dispersed from their original homeland who maintain a collective identity, cultural practices, and often political, economic, or emotional connections to the place of origin — and to each other across geographic distance. The contemporary world contains hundreds of diaspora communities, including the Indian diaspora (estimated at over 32 million people across more than 100 countries), the Chinese diaspora (over 60 million), the Lebanese diaspora (over 14 million, dwarfing Lebanon's population of under 5 million), and many others. What defines a diaspora is not merely the fact of dispersal but the maintenance of collective identity, memory, and connection — often across generations.

Digital Technology and Diaspora Life

Digital technology transformed diaspora life profoundly. Before the internet, maintaining homeland ties across great distances required expensive international phone calls, slow postal correspondence, and periodic physical visits. Video calling through Skype and then WhatsApp allowed diaspora members to maintain face-to-face contact with family members across continents in real time. Social media allowed diaspora communities to organize online, share news and culture from home countries, and maintain collective identity without requiring physical co-location. The Nigerian diaspora in London, New York, and Houston could follow Nigerian political news and music in real time through WhatsApp groups and social-media accounts. The Indian diaspora in the United States participated intensely in Indian politics — the Hindu nationalist BJP party cultivated the diaspora as a political constituency. The Somali diaspora in Minnesota and the UK used mobile money platforms to send remittances that constituted a major share of Somalia's GDP.

Remittances and Development

Remittances — money sent by migrants to family members in their countries of origin — were one of the most significant and underappreciated financial flows in the contemporary global economy. By the early 2020s, global remittance flows exceeded $700 billion annually — far exceeding official development assistance provided by wealthy country governments, and in many countries exceeding foreign direct investment. For countries like Tajikistan (over 40 percent of GDP), Tonga, the Kyrgyz Republic, Lebanon, and El Salvador, remittances were the single most important source of foreign exchange. Studies consistently found that remittance-receiving households consumed more, educated their children more, and had better health outcomes than comparable non-receiving households.

Digital financial technology was transforming the remittance industry. Traditional money transfer operators — Western Union and MoneyGram — had charged fees averaging 8–10 percent of the amount transferred, a significant extraction of value from some of the world's poorest people. Mobile money platforms, fintech startups like Remitly and Wise, and cryptocurrency protocols offered faster and cheaper transfers. The World Bank's Sustainable Development Goals included a target of reducing remittance costs to below 3 percent — a goal that remained unmet on average but was being approached in some major corridors by the early 2020s.

Historical Vocabulary
Diaspora

From the Greek word for "scattering" or "dispersion," diaspora originally referred to the dispersal of Jewish populations from ancient Israel. In contemporary usage, it describes a community of people dispersed from their homeland who maintain collective identity, cultural practices, and often political, economic, or emotional connections to the place of origin — and to each other across geographic distance.

Diaspora communities have shaped world history in profound ways — the Jewish diaspora maintained cultural and religious cohesion across centuries of dispersal; the African diaspora created new cultures in the Americas while maintaining connections across the Atlantic; the Indian and Chinese diasporas played crucial roles in developing economies across Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, East Africa, and the Pacific. In the contemporary world, diasporas are economically significant through remittances, politically significant through dual political engagement, and culturally significant through transnational media, arts, and religious practice. Digital technology has transformed — though it did not create — diaspora identity and organization.

Section 56.9

Technology and Social Change

Social Movements in the Digital Age

Digital technology's relationship to social movements was a subject of intense scholarly and public debate. Scholars like Clay Shirky argued that the internet was fundamentally democratizing — that by reducing the cost of organizing, it empowered marginalized groups to challenge established power. Others like Evgeny Morozov argued that the internet also empowered authoritarian states and large corporations, and that "slacktivism" — easy, low-commitment online activism — might reduce the likelihood of more demanding real-world action.

The evidence suggests both arguments had merit in different contexts. The Arab Spring showed digital tools enabling consequential political mobilization. The #MeToo movement, which spread globally from 2017, used social media to enable millions of people to share experiences of sexual harassment and assault — creating a cultural moment that led to the fall of powerful figures in entertainment, media, politics, and business across many countries. The Black Lives Matter movement used social media to organize protests, document police violence, and build a global conversation about racial justice — particularly after the killing of George Floyd in May 2020, which sparked the largest protest movement in American history. At the same time, repressive governments became adept at using digital surveillance to identify and neutralize organizers before movements could consolidate.

Gender, Race, and Digital Inclusion

The digital age reproduced and in some ways amplified existing social inequalities. Women were systematically underrepresented in the technology sector as workers, executives, investors, and policy-makers. Studies consistently found that women faced hostility, harassment, and structural discrimination in tech workplaces — documented in highly publicized cases at companies including Google, Uber, and many others. The online environment was also a space of racialized harassment. Algorithmic systems in areas from criminal justice to healthcare to employment showed documented racial biases, causing real harm to Black, Latino, Indigenous, and other marginalized communities. Facial recognition systems showed much higher error rates for darker-skinned faces — a consequence of training datasets that were overwhelmingly white and male. Predictive policing systems deployed in U.S. cities directed police disproportionately to Black neighborhoods, creating feedback loops that reinforced existing racial disparities.

Technology and the Environment

The digital economy had significant and often overlooked environmental costs. Data centers consumed approximately 1–2 percent of global electricity production — roughly equivalent to Germany's total electricity consumption. As AI applications grew in scale and complexity, their energy demands grew correspondingly: training a single large AI model could emit as much carbon dioxide as multiple transatlantic flights. Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies consumed electricity comparable to entire medium-sized nations, most of it from coal-fired power plants. Electronic waste — discarded computers, smartphones, and other digital devices — exceeded 50 million metric tons annually by the early 2020s. Only about 20 percent was formally recycled; the rest was landfilled or exported to informal processing sites in West Africa and South Asia where workers extracted valuable metals from toxic materials without protective equipment, exposing themselves and surrounding communities to heavy metals and toxic chemicals.

Regional Lens

India's Digital Transformation

India's experience of the digital revolution offers a window into the possibilities and contradictions of digital development in a large, diverse, poor-to-middle-income democracy. India began the digital era with significant structural advantages: a large English-speaking educated population, strong university traditions in mathematics and science, and a political commitment to technical education established in the early decades after independence. The Indian Institutes of Technology produced generations of engineers who populated both India's technical sector and the technology industries of the United States and other wealthy countries. By the 2010s, Indian-origin executives led several of the world's largest technology companies, including Google (Sundar Pichai) and Microsoft (Satya Nadella).

India's domestic digital economy developed rapidly after telecommunications liberalization in the late 1990s and early 2000s. Bangalore became a global center for software services — home to Indian operations of global technology companies and to large Indian IT services firms like Infosys, Wipro, and TCS. A pivotal moment came with the introduction of Reliance Jio's 4G mobile internet service in September 2016 at prices dramatically lower than existing competitors, adding hundreds of millions of new internet users in a matter of years. By 2021, India had approximately 750 million internet users — more than the entire population of Europe.

India's Unified Payments Interface (UPI) — a real-time payment system built by the National Payments Corporation of India — became one of the world's most successful digital financial infrastructure projects. Processing over 8 billion transactions per month by 2022, UPI made India a global leader in digital payments adoption. Its open, interoperable architecture — allowing any bank or fintech app to connect — contrasted with the proprietary closed systems dominating digital payments in China and the West.

But India's digital revolution also illustrated the persistence of inequality and the new forms it could take. The gender digital divide was among the widest in the world. Rural-urban gaps, while narrowing, remained substantial. The government's use of internet shutdowns as a tool of political and security management made India the world's leader in internet shutdowns by number. The Aadhaar biometric identity system — linking over a billion residents' fingerprints and iris scans to a unique identification number — enabled service delivery but also raised significant civil liberties concerns. India's digital transformation thus illustrated both the genuine democratizing potential of digital technology and its capacity to replicate and sometimes intensify existing inequalities.

Global Connection

Remittances and the Global Economy

Among the least visible yet most consequential financial flows in the contemporary global economy are remittances — money sent by migrants working abroad to family members in their home countries. In 2022, global remittance flows to low- and middle-income countries were estimated at approximately $626 billion — roughly three times the total value of official development assistance provided by wealthy country governments. For specific countries, the dependence was even more striking: remittances constituted over 30 percent of GDP in Tajikistan, Tonga, and the Kyrgyz Republic. For the Philippines, Mexico, Egypt, Pakistan, and Bangladesh — each receiving between $20 billion and $40 billion annually — these flows were a major source of foreign exchange, consumer spending, and household income.

The corridors through which remittances flow reflect the geography of global labor migration, which in turn reflects colonial history, geopolitical relationships, and established migration networks. The U.S.-Mexico corridor — the world's largest — saw tens of billions of dollars transferred annually. The Gulf states-to-South Asia corridor reflected the enormous labor migration to Gulf construction and service industries. The UK-to-Nigeria and France-to-Senegal corridors reflected European colonial histories.

Digital technology transformed the mechanics of remittance transfer. Traditional services like Western Union had charged fees reaching 8–10 percent of transfer value. Mobile money platforms, fintech apps, and cryptocurrency services reduced costs substantially and increased transfer speed. Kenya's M-Pesa extended to cross-border remittances. Startups like Remitly and Wise built businesses specifically serving remittance corridors at lower costs. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital adoption in remittances as physical money transfer offices closed — and remittances to most major corridors proved surprisingly resilient, as migrant workers prioritized supporting family members facing economic disruption.

Network Study

The Global Technology Supply Chain

The global technology supply chain is one of the most complex and geographically dispersed production networks in human history. A smartphone contains components manufactured in dozens of countries across multiple continents, assembled in a handful of factories in China and Southeast Asia, designed in the United States, and sold globally through logistics networks spanning every major port and airport on earth.

The supply chain begins with raw material extraction. Lithium for batteries comes primarily from Chile, Argentina, and Australia. Cobalt — essential for lithium-ion battery cathodes — comes overwhelmingly from the Democratic Republic of Congo, which produced approximately 60 percent of global cobalt supplies as of 2020, with an estimated 40,000 children working in hazardous artisanal mining conditions. Rare earth elements, used in magnets and electronics, are mined primarily in China, which controls approximately 60 percent of global rare earth production. International efforts to create supply chain transparency and eliminate conflict mineral use — including the U.S. Dodd-Frank Act Section 1502 (2010) and EU conflict minerals regulation — achieved some progress but faced significant compliance challenges.

Semiconductor manufacturing — the production of chips that are the fundamental building blocks of all digital devices — is perhaps the most technically complex and geographically concentrated industrial process in the world. The most advanced chips are manufactured by only three companies: Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC), Samsung in South Korea, and Intel in the United States. TSMC alone manufactures approximately 90 percent of the world's most advanced chips, from an island that China claims as its own territory — a geopolitical vulnerability that became a major concern for governments worldwide in the early 2020s. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of global supply chains when factory closures and demand spikes created a global semiconductor shortage lasting from 2020 to 2022, disrupting production across automotive, consumer electronics, and industrial sectors. The U.S. CHIPS and Science Act (2022) allocated $52 billion in subsidies to attract semiconductor manufacturing to the United States; the EU Chips Act (2023) pursued similar goals for Europe. Assembly of finished devices remained concentrated in China, primarily by Foxconn and other contract manufacturers, whose working conditions — long hours, mandatory overtime, dormitory living — attracted sustained criticism from labor-rights organizations.

Source type: Synthesized journalistic testimony based on interviews with migrants who crossed the Mediterranean Sea · Period: 2014–2016 · Context: Collected and reported by journalists and human rights organizations documenting the European migration crisis

Context: The following is a paraphrase and synthesis of testimony collected from Syrian, Eritrean, and Afghan migrants who crossed the Mediterranean Sea between 2014 and 2016. It represents voices that would otherwise be absent from conventional historical records of this period. The sources draw on published investigative reporting and documentation by organizations including Médecins Sans Frontières and UNHCR.

Migrants who undertook the Mediterranean crossing described a terrifying, expensive, and dehumanizing process. Many had paid smugglers between $1,000 and $3,000 per person — often representing an entire family's savings — for a place on a rubber inflatable boat designed for a fraction of the number of passengers carried. The boats were operated by migrants themselves, given brief instruction in how to steer. Many had no lifejackets, or only cheap foam imitations that provided no real protection. At night, in rough seas, boats swamped or capsized. Testimonies also documented the role of smartphones in the crossing: migrants used WhatsApp and Telegram to coordinate with smugglers, receive GPS coordinates, and warn each other about conditions. NGO rescue boats used the same platforms to communicate with migrants in distress.

Analysis: This source reveals how digital technology simultaneously served smugglers, migrants, and rescue organizations — a reminder that technology does not determine the uses to which it is put. The source also illustrates the economic irrationality argument: migrants were willing to risk death and pay enormous sums, which suggests the push factors (war, persecution, extreme poverty) far outweighed deterrent measures at the border. The source has significant limitations: journalistic accounts reflect the particular people journalists could interview. The experiences of those who died cannot be recorded. The accounts of survivors may reflect particular national, linguistic, or social profiles. Gendered experiences — the particular vulnerabilities of women on the migration route — are often underrepresented.

Archaeology, Artifact, and Material Evidence Window

The Smartphone as Historical Artifact

For historians of the contemporary period, the smartphone offers an instructive case in material evidence. Consider it not simply as a communication device but as a material object whose physical form, supply chain, and cultural significance are all historically meaningful. A disassembled smartphone reveals an extraordinary layering of global production: tantalum from the DRC in capacitors; cobalt from the DRC and Chile in batteries; silicon processed in Germany and Japan; rare earth elements from Chinese mines in speakers and vibration motors; semiconductor chips manufactured in Taiwan or South Korea; assembled by workers in Chinese factories; packaged in cardboard from recycled fibers; shipped on container vessels from Chinese ports. Each component traces a line of extraction, labor, and logistical connection across the global economy.

The cultural meanings layered onto this object are equally complex. In wealthy countries, the smartphone became a status symbol, a productivity tool, and a source of social anxiety. In developing countries, for first-time internet users, it was a gateway to education, commerce, and social connection previously inaccessible. For migrants crossing dangerous routes, it was a lifeline. For governments, a surveillance tool. For platform companies, the physical portal through which billions of users fed data into their systems.

Material evidence also reveals technological change over time. The rapid shortening of product cycles — manufacturers releasing new models annually and using software updates to slow older devices — created enormous quantities of electronic waste. A smartphone purchased in 2015 might become functionally obsolete by 2020, ending up in a landfill or informal recycling operation in Ghana or Bangladesh. The physical decomposition of these objects — the leaching of toxic metals into soil and groundwater — represents a material legacy of the digital age that future archaeologists will encounter in geological strata. Historians who ignore the material dimension of digital technology — focusing only on code, platforms, and content — miss the physical world that the digital economy creates and destroys.

Historical Debate

Did Social Media Cause the Arab Spring?

Few questions about the digital age have generated more historical debate than the relationship between social media and the Arab Spring of 2010–2012. The debate reveals broader questions about historical causation, technological determinism, and the role of communication in political change.

The Technological Argument: Some early commentators argued that social media was the critical enabling factor — that Facebook allowed protesters to organize rapidly, that video documentation on YouTube generated international pressure, and that Twitter enabled real-time coordination. On this view, the Arab Spring was in some meaningful sense a "social media revolution" enabled by new digital infrastructure.
The Structural Argument: Historians, sociologists, and political scientists criticized this view as superficial, focusing on communication tools rather than the political, economic, and social conditions that made millions of people willing to take life-threatening risks. They pointed out that the Arab Spring occurred in societies that had experienced decades of authoritarian governance, rising inequality, youth unemployment, and declining state legitimacy. On this view, the Arab Spring would have happened even without social media, perhaps more slowly.
The Institutional Argument: Zeynep Tufekci and others offered a more nuanced view: social media did change movement dynamics, but not always in strengthening ways. Traditional movements that required slow organizational work produced organizations with deep internal capacity. Social-media-enabled movements could mobilize quickly but lacked organizational depth — they could fill a square but could not govern a country or sustain pressure against determined repression. The ultimate failures of Arab Spring movements to produce democratic transitions in most cases (Tunisia being the partial exception) reflected these organizational weaknesses as much as any other factor.

What the evidence shows: Surveys of protesters in Egypt and Tunisia found that the majority learned about specific demonstrations through face-to-face contact and word of mouth rather than social media, though social media played a larger role for educated, urban, and younger participants who led early mobilization. The regimes that fell (Tunisia, Egypt) had been facing structural legitimacy crises for years; those that survived (Bahrain, Saudi Arabia) had stronger resources for repression and concession. The technological infrastructure was thus one factor among many, not a sufficient cause in itself.

Why it matters: The debate about social media and the Arab Spring is important not only for understanding the events themselves but for thinking about technology's relationship to political change more broadly. Technological determinism — the view that technology is the primary driver of historical change — is a persistent and often misleading intellectual tendency. Understanding what technology can and cannot explain requires careful attention to context, agency, structure, and contingency.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, which interpretation do you find most persuasive? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate? Is it possible for all three interpretations to be partially correct, and what would that mean for how we write about technology and political change?

United States

A broadly permissive approach reflecting Silicon Valley's libertarian political culture and the power of technology lobbies. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (1996) gave internet platforms near-total immunity from liability for user content — enabling social-media growth but shielding platforms from accountability for harmful content. Antitrust enforcement was slow, with the FTC only beginning meaningful challenges to platform acquisitions in the early 2020s.

European Union

A more interventionist regulatory framework, reflecting European traditions of social regulation and — particularly in Germany — sensitivity to surveillance from historical experience with totalitarian states. The GDPR (2018) established comprehensive rules for data collection and individual rights. The Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act (2022) addressed platform liability and gatekeeping power. These regulations were globally influential, as many companies found it more efficient to apply GDPR standards worldwide.

China

The most extensive state control over digital infrastructure of any major economy. The Great Firewall blocked access to most major Western platforms. Domestic platforms were required to comply with censorship and surveillance mandates, store user data within China, and cooperate with security agencies. China's approach combined aggressive promotion of domestic digital innovation with strict political control — creating a distinct, functional, and highly surveilled digital ecosystem.

India

An ambiguous and evolving approach, combining permissive access to the global internet with growing regulatory assertiveness. IT Rules (2021) required platforms to identify the first originator of viral content — undermining WhatsApp's encryption — and to remove content at government direction. The government aggressively promoted digital infrastructure through Digital India and UPI while simultaneously using internet shutdowns in politically sensitive areas like Kashmir for months at a time.

Comparison: Each model reflects genuine philosophical differences about the proper relationship between state power, market power, and individual rights in the digital sphere. The American model maximizes market freedom but concentrates power in corporations and leaves users with limited protection. The European model prioritizes individual rights and democratic accountability but faces criticism that it burdens innovation. The Chinese model enables market innovation within constraints of strict political control. The Indian model is still being defined, torn between aspirations for digital empowerment and instincts toward digital control. None has fully resolved the tensions between freedom, security, equality, and power that the digital age has intensified.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experiences Migration? Four Perspectives

The South Asian Labor Migrant in the Gulf States: For the estimated 20 million South Asian workers in the Gulf states, migration means separation from family for two or three years, work in construction or domestic service under the kafala (sponsorship) system that ties workers to a specific employer and limits their ability to change jobs or leave the country without permission. Human rights organizations have documented wage theft, confiscation of passports, physical abuse, and death from heat exposure. The government of Qatar faced particular international pressure over deaths of migrant workers during construction of World Cup infrastructure. Yet for individual workers, migration remains rational: wages in the Gulf, even at exploitative rates, were typically many times higher than wages available at home, enabling remittances that transformed family welfare in Nepal, India, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka.
The Syrian Refugee in Europe: For the estimated 6.5 million Syrians who had fled their country by 2022, migration was not a choice but a survival imperative. Many spent years in camps in Turkey, Lebanon, and Jordan, with limited access to education and work and small prospects of return or resettlement. Those who attempted to reach Europe faced the dangerous Mediterranean crossing, followed by months or years of asylum procedures, language barriers, discrimination, and uncertain legal status. Those who reached Germany in 2015–2016 under Angela Merkel's relatively open policy faced intensive language instruction programs, a receptive labor market, and a political environment that turned increasingly hostile as the AfD capitalized on cultural anxiety.
The Highly Skilled Migrant: Indian software engineers on H-1B visas in Silicon Valley, Nigerian doctors in the UK's National Health Service, Colombian finance professionals in New York — these migrants operated through very different channels and received very different political treatment. Their migration was actively sought by receiving countries' governments and private sectors. Critics in sending countries argued that the emigration of doctors, engineers, and researchers trained at public expense represented a "brain drain" — a subsidy from poor to wealthy countries that depleted the human capital of communities that could least afford to lose it.
The Internal Migrant: Perhaps the world's largest migration flows are invisible in conventional discussions focused on international borders: the massive movement of people from rural to urban areas within countries. China's internal migration — an estimated 200–300 million rural-to-urban migrants at peak — drove the industrial revolution that made China the world's manufacturing workshop. In Brazil, migrants from the impoverished Northeast moved to the industrial cities of São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro. China's hukou (household registration) system denied rural migrants access to urban public services, while in most countries internal migrants faced social discrimination and dangerous working conditions without the rights protections that even international migrants could sometimes access through international law.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Platform Monopolies

Long-Term Causes

Network effects inherent to platform businesses; U.S. regulatory philosophy that prioritized consumer welfare over market structure, making it difficult to challenge free services; extraordinary venture capital for funding unprofitable growth until market dominance was achieved; self-reinforcing data advantages where dominant platforms acquired user data that made services better, generating more data, in a feedback cycle.

Short-Term Causes

Dot-com bust (2000–2001) eliminated many competitors; key acquisitions completed before regulators understood their implications — Facebook's Instagram ($1B, 2012) and WhatsApp ($19B, 2014), Google's YouTube ($1.65B, 2006); smartphone transition concentrated users on Apple/Google-controlled operating systems, creating new gatekeeping opportunities.

The Development

By the early 2020s, Alphabet, Meta, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft in the United States, and Alibaba, Tencent, and Baidu in China had achieved extraordinary market power in search, social media, e-commerce, cloud computing, mobile operating systems, and digital advertising, with combined market capitalizations exceeding the GDPs of most nations.

Effects

Extraordinary wealth concentration; competitive foreclosure; labor market effects as platform companies employed relatively few workers relative to their value; sophisticated tax avoidance; platform governance decisions affecting billions without democratic accountability; international regulatory responses; geopolitical tension as platform competition became a dimension of U.S.-China rivalry; devastation of traditional journalism through advertising-revenue migration.

Historical complexity: Platform power did not simply emerge from neutral market dynamics or technological inevitability. It reflected specific regulatory choices, specific investment decisions, and specific acquisition strategies — all of which could have been made differently. Understanding platform monopoly as a product of history and choice rather than technological fate is essential for thinking about what regulatory responses might be possible.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Migration Across Centuries

What Changed

  • Scale: Contemporary international migration, at approximately 281 million people, is historically unprecedented in absolute terms
  • Speed: Air travel transformed journeys of weeks or months into hours
  • Legal framework: An international refugee regime based on the 1951 Refugee Convention and complex national immigration law created legal categories and rights absent from premodern migration
  • Digital technology: Smartphones, social media, mobile money, and satellite navigation fundamentally changed the practical experience of migration
  • Surveillance: Biometric identification, facial recognition, drone monitoring, and databases gave governments unprecedented surveillance capabilities over migration

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental drivers of migration — economic inequality, political instability, environmental conditions, family reunion, and human aspiration — have remained constant across centuries
  • Network effects: Migrants have always tended to follow established routes to places where earlier migrants from their community had settled — as true of Italian immigrants to New York in 1900 as of Somali immigrants to Minneapolis in 2000
  • Discrimination and ambivalence: Receiving societies have consistently combined economic need for migrant labor with social and political hostility — a pattern visible in classical Rome, medieval Mediterranean trade cities, and contemporary European democracies
  • Remittances: The practice of migrants sending money home has ancient antecedents — digital technology changed the speed, cost, and mechanism, not the fundamental practice

Why the pattern matters: The continuity of migration drivers — despite decades of border enforcement spending — demonstrates the limits of purely restrictive approaches to migration governance. The continuity of receiving-society ambivalence suggests that political conflicts over migration reflect deep structural tensions, not simply ignorance or prejudice that could be resolved through better information.

Everyday Life and Social History: The Digital Household

By the 2010s, digital technology had penetrated the household routines of much of humanity in ways that would have been unimaginable to previous generations. Examining how digital technology affected everyday domestic life — in its diversity across regions, classes, and social groups — offers a social history of the digital age that complements the more visible history of corporations, platforms, and political events.

In an upper-middle-class household in Seoul, South Korea, digital technology organized virtually every dimension of domestic life by 2020. Parents worked remotely through video conferencing; children attended online tutoring sessions on tablets; groceries were ordered through a same-day delivery app; entertainment was streamed through Netflix and local Korean platforms; home appliances were connected through a smart home system; and family finances were managed through a banking app linked to KakaoPay. The social life of teenagers was organized almost entirely through KakaoTalk messaging, Instagram, and online gaming.

In a rural household in Uttar Pradesh, India, the digital experience was both similar in kind and radically different in quality. The household's primary digital device was a shared Android smartphone — affordable enough after the Jio price collapse but still a significant household expense. It was used primarily for voice and video calls on WhatsApp with a son working in Delhi, for watching YouTube videos in Hindi, and for receiving government benefit payments through the Jan Dhan bank account linked to the household's Aadhaar number. The smartphone's data connection was unreliable; the household had electricity for six to eight hours per day. Women in the household used the phone less than men, both because of social norms limiting women's independent technology use and because the primary registered user was the household head.

In a refugee camp in northern Uganda, a Congolese family's digital life was mediated by UNHCR's registration system, which used biometric identification to register refugees and distribute food assistance. The family carried identity documents issued by UNHCR essential for access to food, healthcare, and any travel outside the camp. They did not own a smartphone, but the camp had a mobile phone charging station and a community center with internet access where younger family members could access social media and online learning resources. Remittances from relatives in Europe — sent through Western Union and M-Pesa — supplemented the food rations.

In a suburb of Lagos, Nigeria, a young professional worked as a freelance graphic designer through Upwork and Fiverr, serving clients in the United States and Europe. Her earnings in dollars gave her a relatively comfortable income by local standards, though persistent electricity outages required her to work from a co-working space providing its own generator and fiber connection. She sent money to her parents through a mobile transfer app and was actively saving in Bitcoin as a hedge against naira inflation — a practice common among young, educated Nigerians skeptical of central bank monetary management.

These four vignettes — from different continents, classes, and circumstances — illustrate both the genuinely global reach of digital technology and the profound inequalities in how it was experienced. What connected these households across vast differences of wealth, geography, and circumstance was their integration, in different degrees and on different terms, into the same global digital infrastructure.

Chapter Summary

The digital revolution of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries constitutes one of the most significant transformations in the history of human communication, commerce, labor, and social life. Beginning with the development of computers in the 1940s and 1950s, accelerating through the personal computer era of the 1970s and 1980s, and transforming into a global phenomenon with the commercial internet of the 1990s and the smartphone revolution of the 2000s, digital technology reshaped virtually every dimension of contemporary life across most of the world.

The internet and the World Wide Web made possible a new form of human communication and knowledge sharing — more democratic and more accessible than any previous medium — while also creating new forms of surveillance, monopoly, and manipulation. The commercial model of the dominant internet platforms — based on collecting user behavioral data and selling predictive capacity to advertisers — produced the surveillance capitalist economy in which attention and behavior became raw material for corporate extraction. This model created powerful incentives to amplify emotionally provocative content regardless of its accuracy, producing documented harms to democratic discourse and public health.

Social media transformed political communication, enabling new forms of collective action — most dramatically in the Arab Spring of 2010–2012 and in movements like #MeToo and Black Lives Matter — while also enabling disinformation, political manipulation, and authoritarian surveillance. The relationship between social media and political change was neither simply liberating nor simply repressive; it depended on context, existing political forces, and the organizational capacity of movements to sustain pressure beyond initial digital mobilization.

Automation and artificial intelligence extended the range of cognitive tasks that could be performed by machines, threatening displacement across many occupations while also creating new forms of digital labor — content moderation, data labeling, gig economy service work — that were often invisible, poorly compensated, and insecure. The distribution of automation's costs and benefits was deeply unequal: those with capital, education, and technological skills benefited, while those without bore the costs of displacement. This pattern reinforced existing global inequalities between and within nations.

Platform capitalism concentrated extraordinary economic power in a small number of technology companies whose market capitalizations exceeded the GDPs of most countries and whose decisions about content, data, and commercial terms affected billions of users with minimal democratic accountability. Regulatory responses emerged across multiple jurisdictions — the EU's GDPR and Digital Services Act, antitrust investigations in multiple countries, China's regulation of its own tech giants — but regulatory frameworks generally lagged well behind the pace of technological and market change.

Cybersecurity and information conflict became major domains of national security concern, as state-sponsored hackers, criminal ransomware operators, and disinformation campaigns demonstrated the vulnerability of digital infrastructure and the information environment. The Snowden disclosures of 2013 revealed the scope of state surveillance through digital infrastructure, raising urgent and as yet unresolved questions about privacy, civil liberties, and the relationship between security agencies and technology companies.

Migration in the contemporary world reached unprecedented scale, with approximately 281 million international migrants and over one billion total displaced or migrant persons by the early 2020s. Migration was driven by persistent economic inequality, political instability, climate disruption, and established migration networks — dynamics that elaborate border enforcement systems consistently failed to halt. The crossing of major migration frontiers — the Mediterranean, the U.S.-Mexico border, the Bay of Bengal — imposed enormous human costs, with tens of thousands dying each year in attempts to reach safety and opportunity.

Diaspora communities maintained cultural identity, family connection, and political engagement across national borders in ways that digital technology facilitated dramatically. Remittances exceeded $700 billion annually, dwarfing official development aid and constituting a major share of GDP in many developing countries. Digital financial technology was reducing the cost and increasing the speed of remittance transfer, though traditional providers maintained substantial market share.

The environmental costs of the digital economy — in energy consumption, electronic waste, and the extractive supply chains of digital devices — were distributed globally, with costs falling disproportionately on communities in the Global South least responsible for the consumption patterns that drove them. The mining of lithium, cobalt, and rare earth elements imposed environmental and human costs primarily on communities in the DRC, Chile, Argentina, and China, while the end products were consumed primarily in wealthy industrialized countries.

Throughout these transformations, historians must resist two temptations: the temptation to see the digital age as a radical break from history — as if human societies were beginning again from zero — and the temptation to see it as simply the continuation of familiar patterns with new technological wrapping. The digital age is both: genuinely new in important ways, while deeply continuous with the dynamics of power, inequality, cultural resilience, and human creativity that have shaped world history across centuries. It is, emphatically, world history — not simply Western history or Silicon Valley history, but the history of billions of people across every world region navigating, adapting to, resisting, and shaping a transformed world.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Algorithm
A set of rules or instructions a computer follows to perform a task or make a decision; in digital platforms, algorithms determine what content users see, what search results appear, and what recommendations are made.
Arab Spring
A wave of pro-democracy protests and uprisings beginning in Tunisia in late 2010, spreading to Egypt, Libya, Bahrain, Syria, Yemen, and other countries, shaped in significant ways by digital communication.
Artificial intelligence (AI)
Computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, including recognizing images, understanding language, making decisions, and generating creative content.
ARPANET
The Advanced Research Projects Agency Network, established by the U.S. Defense Department in 1969; the first packet-switching computer network and technical ancestor of the internet.
Asylum seeker
A person who has left their country of origin and applied for refugee protection in another country but whose claim has not yet been decided by legal authorities.
Big data
Extremely large datasets analyzed computationally to reveal patterns, trends, and associations — particularly patterns of human behavior used for commercial and government decision-making.
Cybersecurity
The practice and study of protecting computer systems, networks, and digital data from attack, damage, or unauthorized access, including by both criminal and state actors.
Deep learning
A subset of machine learning based on artificial neural networks with many layers; particularly effective for recognizing images, processing language, and other complex pattern-recognition tasks.
Diaspora
A community of people dispersed from their homeland who maintain collective identity, cultural practices, and often political or economic connections across geographic distance, sometimes across many generations.
Digital divide
The gap between those with meaningful access to digital technology and those without, operating across lines of country, income, gender, education, age, and geography.
Digital revolution
The transformation of society through the widespread adoption of digital electronics, computers, the internet, and related technologies from the late twentieth century onward.
Disinformation
False or misleading information spread deliberately, often to advance a political or economic agenda; distinct from misinformation (which may be spread without deliberate intent).
Fintech
Financial technology; digital platforms, apps, and systems that provide financial services — including banking, payments, lending, and remittances — outside or alongside traditional banking institutions.
Gig economy
A labor market characterized by short-term, on-demand work contracts facilitated by digital platforms, where workers are classified as independent contractors rather than employees with benefits and legal protections.
Great Firewall
The combination of legislation and technology that the Chinese government uses to regulate the internet domestically, blocking access to many foreign websites and platforms including Google, Facebook, and Twitter.
Kafala system
A labor sponsorship system used in Gulf states that ties migrant workers to a specific employer, limiting their ability to change jobs or leave the country without the employer's permission.
Machine learning
A type of artificial intelligence in which systems learn from data rather than being explicitly programmed, improving their performance through experience with large training datasets.
Net neutrality
The principle that internet service providers should treat all internet traffic equally, without favoring, blocking, or slowing specific websites or services based on content or commercial relationships.
Platform capitalism
An economic system in which major corporations operate as digital platforms mediating interactions between users, extracting value from network effects, behavioral data, and transaction fees.
Push and pull factors
In migration studies, conditions that drive people to leave their home region (push) and conditions that attract them to a destination (pull), together explaining why specific migration flows emerge between particular origin and destination societies.
Ransomware
A type of malicious software that encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for the decryption key; a major criminal industry that affected hospitals, governments, and corporations globally in the 2010s–2020s.
Refugee
Under international law (1951 Refugee Convention), a person outside their country of origin who cannot return due to a well-founded fear of persecution on grounds of race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership of a particular social group.
Remittances
Money sent by migrants to family members in their countries of origin; a financial flow exceeding $700 billion annually that dwarfs official development assistance in its scale and household-level impact.
Social media
Digital platforms that enable users to create, share, and interact with content and with each other; including Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, WhatsApp, WeChat, and many others.
Surveillance capitalism
An economic system in which human behavioral data is collected through digital devices, analyzed to predict behavior, and sold as a commodity, primarily to advertisers; coined by scholar Shoshana Zuboff.
Transnational community
A social community that maintains significant connections, cultural practices, and identities across national borders, often enabled in the contemporary period by digital communication and global migration.
World Wide Web
A system of interlinked hypertext documents and other resources accessible via the internet, created by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989–1991; transformed the internet from a tool for specialists into a global medium.

Option C — Gig Workers and Platform Labor: Investigate the labor conditions of gig workers in one major platform economy (Uber, Deliveroo, Amazon Mechanical Turk, or similar), examining how the platform's classification of workers as independent contractors affects wages, benefits, and working conditions, and how workers, labor organizations, and governments have responded.
  4. Option D — Diaspora and Digital Identity: Research the history and contemporary status of one major diaspora community — the Somali diaspora, the Haitian diaspora, the Philippine diaspora, or another of your choice — with particular attention to how digital technology has transformed diaspora identity, political engagement, and economic connections to the homeland.
  5. Option E — Digital Environmental Impact: Investigate the environmental impact of data centers and the global digital economy, examining energy consumption, carbon emissions, electronic waste, and the extractive supply chains of digital devices — and assessing how seriously technology companies and governments have addressed these impacts relative to their stated sustainability commitments.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 56 has traveled through some of the most consequential developments of the past half century — and some of the hardest to see clearly because of their proximity to the present. The digital revolution, contemporary migration, and the transformations of the early twenty-first century are not history in the sense that ancient Mesopotamia or the Mongol Empire is history. Yet they are history in the sense that matters: they are developments that can be studied using the same methods of evidence, context, causation, comparison, and critical interpretation that illuminate all of human experience — and that look significantly different when placed in the longer sweep of world-historical development than they do when viewed simply as current events.

One of the most important lessons of this chapter is the danger of technological determinism — the belief that technology drives history, that its effects are inevitable, and that human agency, political choice, and structural inequality are secondary to the logic of innovation. The history of the digital age shows clearly that technological systems embody choices. The surveillance capitalist model of platform companies was not the only possible model for internet development; it was a specific choice, enabled by specific regulatory decisions, investment patterns, and market incentives, that produced specific consequences — consequences that were not accidental technical failures but structural products of the model itself. This recognition matters for more than historical understanding: it matters for thinking about what future choices might look like.

Similarly, contemporary migration is not simply a humanitarian emergency or a security challenge — it is a structural phenomenon produced by the same global economic order that produced digital capitalism. The persistence of global economic inequality, the displacement produced by armed conflicts in which wealthy countries often played significant roles, the climate disruption caused overwhelmingly by wealthy-country emissions — these are the push factors that drive migration regardless of how high border walls are built or how many surveillance drones patrol frontier regions. Understanding migration in its structural dimensions — rather than as a series of individual decisions or a security threat — is essential to thinking clearly about what policy responses could actually change the human costs of movement.

The chapter has also demonstrated, across multiple examples, the world-historical importance of perspectives beyond those of Europe and North America. M-Pesa in Kenya, UPI in India, WeChat in China, the Rohingya refugee crisis in Southeast Asia, labor migration in the Gulf, diaspora remittances across the Global South — these are not sideshows to a story centered in Silicon Valley and Washington. They are dimensions of the same global transformation, experienced differently according to position in the global order, but equally central to its historical significance. A world history of the digital age that centers only Western experiences misses most of the story.

Finally, this chapter invites students to think about what kind of evidence enables historical understanding of the present. The digital age has produced an extraordinary abundance of data and an extraordinary scarcity of meaning. Historical methods — attending to context, causation, power, and the perspectives of those whose voices are hardest to hear — are not less relevant in the digital age than in any previous era. They are more relevant, because the sheer volume of information available makes it easier than ever to miss the larger patterns that history teaches us to see. The tools of historical thinking developed across this textbook — chronology, comparison, causation, continuity and change, multiple perspectives, evidentiary reasoning — are indispensable for navigating the contemporary world. They are, in the end, what it means to be historically informed citizens in an age that has never more urgently needed such citizens.

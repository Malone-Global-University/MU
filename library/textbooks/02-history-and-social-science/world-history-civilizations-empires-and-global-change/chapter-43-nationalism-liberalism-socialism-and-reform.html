Nationalism, Liberalism, Socialism, and Reform

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it." — Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach (1845)

In the winter of 1848, within the span of a few extraordinary weeks, barricades went up across Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Budapest, Milan, Prague, and dozens of other European cities. Workers, students, lawyers, artisans, and journalists poured into the streets armed with pamphlets, demands, and, in many cases, muskets. They called for constitutions, for the end of aristocratic privilege, for national self-determination, for the rights of workers, and for the liberation of peoples still ruled by emperors who claimed to govern by divine right. The year 1848 — sometimes called the "Springtime of Nations" — did not produce a single successful revolution. Within months, most uprisings had been crushed by armies loyal to the old order. Tsar Nicholas I of Russia sent troops to help suppress Hungary; Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte used conservative rural voters to restore authoritarian rule in France; Habsburg generals reconquered Vienna and Prague. And yet the ideas unleashed in 1848 did not disappear. They burrowed into the political consciousness of the nineteenth century and shaped the world that emerged from it.

The nineteenth century was an age of ideologies. Before this era, most people who thought about politics framed their arguments in terms of religion, custom, dynastic loyalty, or the particular laws of their community. The idea that a rational, coherent system of beliefs — rooted in principles about human nature, society, history, and justice — should guide political life was itself relatively new. The Enlightenment of the eighteenth century had produced it, and the French Revolution of 1789 had turned it into a practical force. By the nineteenth century, ideologies were not merely intellectual systems. They were weapons in bitter struggles over who should rule, how societies should be organized, what rights people possessed, and what kind of future humanity was building toward.

Four ideologies dominated nineteenth-century political life: liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, and socialism. These were not simply different opinions about the same question. They represented fundamentally different answers to the most basic political problems: What is the proper source of political authority? What rights do individuals possess? What role should the state play in economic life? How should society be organized? What obligations do people owe to one another? The answers these ideologies gave to these questions collided repeatedly, producing revolutions, reform movements, civil wars, and constitutional struggles across Europe and beyond.

But ideology in the nineteenth century was inseparable from social structure. The industrial revolution — examined in Chapter 42 — was transforming the material conditions of life. It was creating a factory-working class whose experience of industrial capitalism shaped new grievances, new solidarities, and new demands. It was expanding a middle class of merchants, lawyers, doctors, journalists, and manufacturers who wanted political power commensurate with their economic weight. It was pushing millions of people into cities where they encountered political ideas, read newspapers, joined associations, and developed identities beyond the village or the craft guild. Ideology and social change were not separate forces. They shaped each other.

The chapter also moves beyond Europe. While the great ideological conflicts of the nineteenth century were most visible in European politics, their reach was global. Nationalism did not stay in France or Germany; it inspired movements in Latin America, the Ottoman Empire, China, and India. Liberalism was used to justify both individual rights and colonial conquest. Socialism emerged as a response to industrial capitalism that was itself a global system. Abolitionism — the movement to end slavery — drew on liberal ideals of human freedom while also demonstrating their contradictions, since many liberal states had built their prosperity on enslaved labor. Feminism challenged ideologies that claimed to speak for all humanity while treating women as secondary or invisible. Any serious account of nineteenth-century political thought must grapple with these movements as part of the global history of ideas, not merely as footnotes to European intellectual history.

The history of this chapter is also the history of how ordinary people became political actors. Peasants, factory workers, enslaved people, women, and colonized peoples all engaged with the ideologies of the nineteenth century — sometimes embracing them, sometimes reinterpreting them, sometimes rejecting them entirely in favor of alternative visions of justice and community. Understanding the political thought of the nineteenth century means not only reading the pamphlets and manifestos of great thinkers, but listening to the voices of those whose lives the ideologies claimed to address but too often ignored.

By the end of the nineteenth century, the world was politically transformed. Nation-states had replaced many of the multiethnic empires of the old order in parts of Europe. Slavery had been formally abolished in most of the Atlantic world, though racial subordination continued in new forms. Labor movements had won legal recognition and fought for shorter working hours, safer conditions, and wages that could sustain a family. Women had begun to enter public political life in ways unimaginable a century before. These were not smooth or inevitable changes. They were the products of conflict, suffering, compromise, and the determined efforts of millions of people who believed that the world could be organized differently.

  10. Synthesize an understanding of how ideology, social change, and political conflict together reshaped the global order during the nineteenth century.

Historical Context

The political ideologies of the nineteenth century did not emerge from nowhere. They were rooted in the intellectual revolutions of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries and shaped by the political and social upheavals that began in the 1770s and 1780s.

The Enlightenment — examined in Chapter 40 — had established a set of claims about reason, nature, and human society that would become the raw material of nineteenth-century ideology. Thinkers from Locke to Montesquieu to Rousseau had argued that political authority derived not from God or tradition but from reason and the consent of the governed. They had written about natural rights — life, liberty, property — that no legitimate government could violate. They had imagined societies organized around law, contract, and rational administration rather than inherited privilege and divine command. These ideas were available to all the ideological traditions of the nineteenth century, and all of them drew on Enlightenment concepts even as they interpreted them differently.

The French Revolution of 1789 was the great crucible of nineteenth-century ideology. It provided a living example of how rapidly existing political orders could be overturned, how powerful ideas could become when attached to mass movements, and how revolutions could spiral into violence and tyranny. The Revolution produced the vocabulary of modern politics: left and right (derived from the seating arrangements in the French National Assembly), citizen, nation, republic, constitution, terror. It also raised the questions that nineteenth-century ideologies would spend decades debating: Who belongs to the nation? What rights does citizenship confer? How much equality is compatible with liberty? Can revolutions achieve their goals without violence? What happens when the people's will produces outcomes that threaten order and property?

The Napoleonic Wars (1803–1815) spread revolutionary ideas across Europe through conquest. Even as Napoleon's armies imposed French domination, they dismantled feudal structures, introduced legal equality before the law, and stimulated national consciousness in peoples who resisted French rule. The Congress of Vienna of 1815 — the great conservative settlement after Napoleon's defeat — tried to put Europe back together on the basis of dynastic legitimacy and the balance of power. It suppressed revolutionary movements and restored many of the old ruling families. But it could not un-invent the ideas that had been set in motion. The Restoration period after 1815 was characterized by constant tension between the forces of order — conservative monarchies supported by aristocracies and established churches — and the forces of change — liberals, nationalists, and, increasingly, socialists.

Industrialization was simultaneously transforming the social landscape. Britain industrialized first, and by the mid-nineteenth century it had become the workshop of the world, its cities teeming with factory workers, its countryside being reshaped by railways, its middle class growing in wealth and confidence. The rest of western Europe industrialized more slowly, but by the 1840s and 1850s France, Belgium, and the German states were undergoing rapid industrial transformation. Industrial capitalism created new social classes — the industrial bourgeoisie and the urban proletariat — whose interests, experiences, and political demands differed sharply from those of the landowners, peasants, and artisans of the old social order.

This was the world in which the great ideological debates of the nineteenth century unfolded: a world still partially organized around aristocratic privilege, established churches, and dynastic states, but being rapidly transformed by capitalism, urbanization, industrialization, and the spread of literacy and political consciousness. The ideologies that emerged were attempts to make sense of this transformation — to explain what was happening, to assign responsibility for suffering and injustice, and to propose visions of a better future.

Regional Orientation

Europe is the primary arena of this chapter's ideological conflicts, but it is not a single, uniform region. Western Europe — Britain, France, the Low Countries — had experienced earlier urbanization, more developed middle classes, and stronger parliamentary traditions. Central Europe — the German states and the Austrian Empire — was simultaneously more feudal in its social structures and more industrializing in its economies, producing particularly sharp ideological tensions. Southern Europe — Italy, Spain, Portugal — had stronger Catholic traditions and later industrialization. Eastern Europe — the Russian Empire, Poland, the Balkans — remained dominated by autocratic rule and agrarian social structures, though nationalist movements were active throughout.

The Americas are central to the chapter's treatment of abolitionism and slavery, and also to the spread of liberal and republican ideas. The United States was the great testing ground for liberal democracy — a republic that claimed to embody universal rights while perpetuating the largest slave system in the Western Hemisphere. Latin American republics had emerged from independence movements in the early nineteenth century and were working through their own versions of liberal, conservative, and nationalist politics.

Africa appears in this chapter primarily through the Atlantic slave trade and the abolitionist movement — the struggle to end the enslavement of African and African-descended peoples. African resistance to enslavement was itself a major factor in abolitionist politics, most dramatically in the Haitian Revolution.

Asia and the Middle East felt the pressure of European liberal and nationalist ideas through colonial contact and through reform movements that engaged selectively with Western political thought. The Ottoman Tanzimat reforms reflected liberal influence; Indian nationalists drew on liberal ideas to critique British colonialism; and Chinese reformers debated Western political models with growing urgency after military defeats revealed the gap in power between China and the industrializing West.

Section 43.1

Ideology in the Modern World

The word "ideology" entered the French vocabulary in 1796, coined by the philosopher Destutt de Tracy to describe the science of ideas. In the two centuries since, it has accumulated layers of meaning. At its most basic, an ideology is a systematic set of beliefs about how society is organized, how it should be organized, who has power, how power is justified, and what a better future would look like. Ideologies differ from mere opinions or preferences. They offer comprehensive frameworks — theories of human nature, explanations of historical development, diagnoses of social problems, and prescriptions for reform or revolution.

Why did ideological politics become so central to the nineteenth century? The answer lies in the convergence of several transformations. The Enlightenment had established that political institutions were not ordained by God but made by human beings and therefore subject to human criticism, reform, or replacement. The French Revolution had demonstrated, terrifyingly and exhilaratingly, that existing political orders could be overturned with astonishing speed. And industrialization was reshaping the social landscape in ways that created new interests, new grievances, and new audiences for political ideas.

Before the era of mass print culture, political ideas circulated mainly among elites — nobility, clergy, educated professionals. But the nineteenth century was the first great age of mass literacy and mass print. Newspapers, pamphlets, books, and later mass-circulation periodicals brought political ideas to millions of people. A factory worker in Manchester or Lyon might read a socialist newspaper. A student in Frankfurt or Warsaw might join a nationalist society. A seamstress in New York or London might attend a meeting about women's rights. Political ideas were no longer merely the property of learned men writing in Latin. They were weapons in struggles being fought on the streets, in legislative chambers, and in workplaces.

The four major ideologies of the nineteenth century — liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, and socialism — were not monolithic systems. Each contained internal debates, variants, and contradictions. Liberals disagreed about how much economic freedom the state should permit and how widely political rights should be extended. Conservatives ranged from reactionaries who wanted to restore the old order wholesale to pragmatists who accepted limited reform to prevent more radical change. Nationalists disagreed about whether nations should be defined by language, culture, shared history, or political will — and about whether their nationalism should be liberal or authoritarian. Socialists debated whether capitalism could be reformed or must be overthrown, whether revolution or parliamentary politics was the proper path, and what a socialist society would actually look like.

These ideological conflicts were not merely abstract. They were connected to concrete social interests and real political stakes. When liberals argued for freedom of the press, they were fighting against censorship regimes that imprisoned editors and shut down newspapers. When nationalists in Germany or Italy argued for national unification, they were challenging the political map drawn by the Congress of Vienna. When socialists argued for the rights of workers, they were responding to the actual experience of industrial labor — the long hours, the dangerous conditions, the low wages, the insecurity. When feminists argued for women's suffrage, they were challenging legal systems that treated women as legal minors, denied them property rights, and excluded them from public life. Ideology in the nineteenth century was always also about power — who had it, who should have it, and on what basis.

Historical Vocabulary
Ideology

An ideology is a systematic set of beliefs, values, and goals that explains how society works, assigns meaning to social conflict, and points toward a desired future. Ideologies differ from mere opinions or personal preferences in their coherence, their claim to universal or structural truth, and their connection to organized political action.

In the nineteenth century, ideologies — liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, and socialism — replaced older sources of political legitimacy (divine right, tradition, local custom) as the primary frameworks through which people argued about power, rights, and social organization. Understanding ideology is essential to understanding modern politics: virtually every political argument today draws on one or more of the ideological traditions that emerged in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, even when participants are unaware of the historical roots of their ideas.

Section 43.2

Liberalism and Constitutional Government

Liberalism was the dominant ideology of the nineteenth-century educated middle classes, and it left a deeper imprint on modern political institutions than any other tradition. At its core, liberalism held that individual human beings possess rights — to life, liberty, and property — that exist prior to and independently of the state. The state's proper function is to protect those rights, not to override them. Government authority must therefore be limited, defined by law, and ultimately derived from the consent of the governed. These principles, first articulated clearly in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries by thinkers like John Locke, Montesquieu, and later the American founders, became the foundation of liberal political thought.

In the nineteenth century, liberalism typically demanded a set of institutional reforms: constitutional government, which would define and limit the powers of rulers; freedom of religion, expression, and the press; an independent judiciary; equality before the law; and some form of representative government in which citizens had a voice in making the laws they lived under. Classical liberals were also committed to economic liberalism — the idea that markets should be free from excessive state regulation, and that trade, both domestic and international, should be as unrestrained as possible. This economic liberalism was most fully articulated by Adam Smith and later by the Manchester School economists, who argued that free markets would produce prosperity for all through the invisible hand of supply and demand.

The British tradition of liberalism was shaped by gradualism and pragmatism. Britain had never experienced a successful republican revolution. Instead, liberal reform came through the parliamentary system, often slowly and incompletely, but with a degree of stability that contrasted sharply with the revolutionary upheavals of France. The Reform Act of 1832 was a landmark in this process. It did not introduce democracy — it expanded the electorate to about one-sixth of adult men, still a small minority — but it was the first major expansion of the franchise since the medieval period, and it established the principle that political representation should bear some relationship to where actual people lived and what economic power they possessed. The Great Reform Act broke the power of "rotten boroughs" — constituencies with tiny electorates controlled by single aristocratic patrons — and gave seats to the new industrial cities of Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds.

On the continent, liberalism had a more turbulent history. In France, the Revolution of 1830 replaced the reactionary Charles X with the "bourgeois king" Louis-Philippe, who accepted a constitutional charter. Belgium won independence from the Netherlands in 1830 and established a constitutional monarchy. But the revolutions of 1848 demonstrated both the power and the fragility of continental liberalism. In February 1848, a popular uprising in Paris overthrew the July Monarchy and established the Second Republic with universal male suffrage — a radical step beyond classical liberalism's typically limited franchise. But the June Days of 1848, when the army massacred Parisian workers who rose against the government, revealed the class fault line within French liberalism. Middle-class liberals who had cheered the February revolution were terrified by the specter of working-class power and supported the violent suppression of the June uprising. By December, Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte had been elected president — and within three years he had made himself emperor.

The contradictions of liberalism were profound and consequential. The most obvious was the contradiction between universal claims and limited application. Liberal thinkers typically wrote about the rights of "men" — and they usually meant this literally. The great liberal constitutional documents — the American Declaration of Independence, the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, the Belgian Constitution — proclaimed universal rights while practicing systematic exclusion. Women were excluded from political rights almost everywhere. The working class was excluded in most liberal constitutions through property qualifications for voting. Enslaved people were excluded entirely, even in the "land of liberty" that was the United States. And colonized peoples were excluded on racial grounds by the same European liberal states that trumpeted their commitment to human freedom.

John Stuart Mill, the most sophisticated liberal thinker of the mid-nineteenth century, grappled with some of these contradictions more honestly than most. His On Liberty (1859) argued for the widest possible scope for individual freedom of thought and action, limited only by the "harm principle" — the idea that individuals could be restrained only when their actions harmed others. He argued for freedom of expression even for ideas he considered wrong, on the grounds that truth emerges through open debate. He supported women's equal rights, arguing in The Subjection of Women (1869) that the legal subordination of women was a relic of barbarism incompatible with liberal principles. Yet Mill also argued that colonial peoples were not yet ready for self-government and required a period of tutelage under more "civilized" powers — a position that demonstrated how deeply liberal thinkers could accept a hierarchy of civilizations that contradicted their own universalist principles.

Despite its contradictions, liberalism left an enormous institutional legacy. Constitutional governments, independent judiciaries, freedom of the press, religious toleration, and formal equality before the law — all of these became the standard expectations of modern governance, largely because liberal movements fought for them in the nineteenth century. Even socialist and nationalist movements often operated within frameworks of rights, constitutions, and parliamentary politics that liberalism had made available.

Section 43.3

Conservatism and Order

Conservatism as a self-conscious ideology was born in reaction to the French Revolution. Edmund Burke, the Anglo-Irish statesman and thinker, articulated its founding principles in his Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790), written even before the Revolution's most violent phase. Burke was not simply defending the status quo out of laziness or self-interest. He was making a serious philosophical argument about the nature of society and the limits of reason.

Burke's central claim was that society is not a contract among living individuals to be renegotiated at will. It is a partnership among the dead, the living, and the unborn — a fabric of inherited institutions, customs, laws, and relationships woven over generations. These inherited structures embody accumulated wisdom that no generation can fully understand or consciously re-create. When revolutionaries tear down existing institutions in the name of abstract reason and begin again from first principles, they destroy more than they know, and what they build is unlikely to be better than what they have destroyed. The French revolutionaries, Burke predicted, would find that abstract liberty produced only tyranny — and the Reign of Terror seemed to confirm his prophecy.

Nineteenth-century conservatism built on Burke's foundations. It emphasized tradition and custom over abstract principle. It valued social hierarchy as reflecting natural differences in talent, temperament, and responsibility. It insisted on the importance of established religious institutions — especially the Catholic Church in much of continental Europe — as the moral foundation of social order. It distrusted rapid change, preferring gradual evolution over revolution. And it was suspicious of abstract universal principles, preferring to ground political arguments in the particular history, law, and culture of specific societies.

After 1815, European conservatism was associated primarily with the settlement of the Congress of Vienna and the system of metternichian repression that sought to suppress liberal and nationalist movements. Prince Klemens von Metternich, the Austrian foreign minister, was the great architect of the conservative order. He helped organize the Concert of Europe — the system by which the great powers coordinated to suppress revolutions and maintain the political settlement of 1815. In the Habsburg Empire, he used censorship, surveillance, and the periodic imprisonment of liberals and nationalists to maintain a rigid political status quo.

But conservatism was not always purely reactionary. Benjamin Disraeli, who led Britain's Conservative Party in the second half of the nineteenth century, developed what he called "Tory Democracy" — a conservatism that accepted limited reform as a means of binding the working class to the existing social order, rather than alienating it through rigid resistance. His government passed the Reform Act of 1867, which extended the franchise further than the Liberals had done, and introduced various social reforms. This pragmatic conservatism accepted change in order to preserve what was most important — social hierarchy, the monarchy, the Empire, and the established church — while adapting to new social realities.

German conservatism took yet another form under Bismarck, who combined fierce opposition to liberalism and socialism with a willingness to use state power aggressively to achieve national and social goals. Bismarck's "state socialism" — the introduction of health insurance, accident insurance, and old-age pensions in the 1880s — was a conservative project designed to undercut socialist appeals to the working class by showing that the imperial state, not socialist revolution, could improve workers' lives.

The great achievement of nineteenth-century conservatism was its survival. At the beginning of the century, conservative thinkers had feared that the forces of liberalism and democracy would sweep away all existing institutions within a generation. They did not. Conservatism proved adaptable, finding ways to incorporate change — expanding electorates, accepting some social reforms, embracing nationalism — while preserving the essential structures of property, hierarchy, and established religion. By the end of the century, conservative parties were competing successfully in mass democratic politics across Europe, having learned to speak the language of national pride, social stability, and practical reform.

Section 43.4

Nationalism and Nation-States

Nationalism was perhaps the most powerful political force of the nineteenth century. At its core, nationalism holds that humanity is naturally divided into distinct nations — communities defined by shared language, culture, history, and identity — and that each nation has the right to political self-determination, meaning the right to govern itself through its own state. Before the eighteenth century, political loyalty was typically directed toward dynasties, religions, localities, or social orders rather than nations. Nationalism replaced or supplemented these older loyalties with a new imagined community — the nation — that claimed the deepest allegiance of its members.

Nationalism was a product of modernity in multiple senses. It required a level of cultural standardization — a shared written language, a common historical narrative, a sense of collective memory — that was only possible once printing, mass literacy, and modern education had spread. The historian Benedict Anderson famously described nations as "imagined communities" — not in the sense that they are fictional, but in the sense that the sense of belonging to a nation depends on people imagining a connection to millions of fellow nationals they will never personally meet. This imaginative work was done through newspapers and novels in standardized vernacular languages, through school curricula that taught a common history, through museums that displayed shared heritage, and through national ceremonies and monuments that commemorated common experiences.

Nationalism in the early nineteenth century was often associated with liberal politics. The generation of 1830 and 1848 frequently combined the demands for constitutional government with demands for national unification or independence. German nationalists wanted both a united Germany and a constitutional one. Italian nationalists wanted both a unified Italy and a free republic. Polish nationalists wanted both independence from Russian, Prussian, and Habsburg rule and a liberal constitution. This liberal nationalism drew on the Enlightenment idea of the sovereign people and imagined the nation as a community of free citizens who would govern themselves democratically.

But nationalism had a darker potential from the beginning. The definition of who belonged to the nation was always also a definition of who did not. Nationalist movements in multinational empires — such as the Habsburg or Ottoman Empires — could be simultaneously liberating for one people and oppressive for others. Hungarian nationalists demanding autonomy from Habsburg rule were unwilling to grant the same autonomy to the Slovak, Croat, and Romanian minorities within the kingdom they claimed as their own. German nationalists who resented French cultural dominance could also become aggressively hostile to German Jews, whom they defined as a separate nation that could never fully belong to the German national community.

Nationalism was also a tool available to existing states, not only to oppressed peoples seeking self-determination. Ruling dynasties and conservative statesmen learned to harness nationalist sentiment for their own purposes. Bismarck used Prussian nationalism to build support for German unification under Prussian leadership. Cavour used Italian nationalism to expand Piedmontese power. The Japanese Meiji government used nationalism to mobilize the population for rapid modernization. In all these cases, nationalism was not simply a spontaneous force from below but was also shaped, directed, and sometimes manufactured from above.

By the end of the nineteenth century, nationalism had become the dominant framework for organizing political life across most of the world. The multinational empires of the old order — Ottoman, Habsburg, Russian — were under intense pressure from nationalist movements. And nationalism was beginning to spread beyond Europe: Indian nationalists were developing a sense of national identity in opposition to British rule; Egyptian nationalists were resisting British occupation; and Chinese reformers were debating what a Chinese national identity might look like in a modernizing world confronted by foreign military and economic power.

Historical Vocabulary
Nation-State

A nation-state is a state in which the boundaries of political authority are claimed to correspond to the boundaries of a national community — a people who share a common language, culture, history, or sense of collective identity. The concept is historically specific: before the era of nationalism, most political units were dynastic empires or city-states in which cultural and political boundaries rarely coincided.

The nineteenth century was the great age of nation-state formation, as nationalist movements reshaped the map of Europe and, later, the world. But the concept conceals as much as it reveals. Almost no actual state is ethnically or culturally homogeneous. Most states that call themselves nation-states are in fact multinational, multiethnic, or multilingual. The nation-state ideal creates pressure to eliminate or assimilate minorities, suppress regional identities, and define insiders and outsiders — processes that can range from education policy to ethnic cleansing. Understanding the gap between the ideal of the nation-state and the reality of actually existing nations is essential to understanding both the power and the dangers of nationalism.

Section 43.5

Italian and German Unification

Before 1860, Italy and Germany were geographic expressions rather than political realities. Italy was divided among the Kingdom of Sardinia (Piedmont-Sardinia), the Papal States, the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, and several smaller duchies, most of them under Austrian influence. Germany was a patchwork of 39 states loosely organized in the German Confederation, dominated by Austria and Prussia. The unification of both countries in the 1860s and 1870s transformed the European balance of power and provided the most dramatic demonstrations of nationalism as a practical political force.

Italian Unification (Risorgimento)

Italian nationalism had been active since the early nineteenth century, nourished by memories of the Roman Empire, the cultural glories of the Renaissance, and the resentment of foreign — especially Austrian — domination. Giuseppe Mazzini, the most important early figure of the Risorgimento, believed that Italian unification must come through popular uprising and republican revolution. His organization Young Italy, founded in 1831, recruited thousands of young Italians into a network dedicated to armed insurrection and national liberation. Mazzini's vision was romantic and democratic: he believed that free peoples, once liberated from tyrants, would naturally organize themselves into republican nations that would cooperate peacefully with one another.

The revolutions of 1848 gave Italian nationalists their first great opportunity. Uprisings in Milan, Venice, Rome, and other cities briefly expelled Austrian and papal rulers and established provisional republican governments. Mazzini himself returned to Italy and became the leader of a short-lived Roman Republic in 1849. But the revolutions failed. Austrian armies reconquered much of northern Italy. Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte's France, anxious to appease Catholics and conservative opinion at home, sent troops to restore the Pope's temporal power in Rome. The great dream of 1848 collapsed.

Italian unification ultimately came not through popular revolution but through the combination of diplomatic manipulation and military force orchestrated by Count Camillo di Cavour, the pragmatic prime minister of Piedmont-Sardinia. Cavour had no romantic illusions about the power of popular enthusiasm. He understood that Piedmont could not drive Austria out of Italy alone, and he spent the 1850s industrializing Piedmont, building railways, liberalizing its economy, and carefully cultivating diplomatic relationships with France and Britain. In 1858, he met secretly with Napoleon III and secured a French alliance: France would support Piedmont against Austria in exchange for the territories of Nice and Savoy.

The Franco-Austrian War of 1859 drove Austria out of most of northern Italy. Napoleon III then abruptly made peace with Austria — fearing a completely unified Italy would be a less useful client than a grateful partial one — but popular uprisings across the central Italian states produced plebiscites in favor of annexation to Piedmont. Meanwhile, Giuseppe Garibaldi led his legendary "Expedition of the Thousand" in 1860, landing in Sicily with about 1,000 red-shirted volunteers and, astonishingly, conquering the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies. Garibaldi then handed his conquests to King Victor Emmanuel II of Piedmont rather than establishing the republic Mazzini had dreamed of.

In 1861, the Kingdom of Italy was proclaimed with Victor Emmanuel as king. Venetia was added in 1866 as a result of the Austro-Prussian War. Rome — still held by the Pope under French protection — was finally incorporated in 1870, when the Franco-Prussian War forced France to withdraw its garrison. The new Italy was a parliamentary constitutional monarchy, dominated by the northern elites who had made unification happen. The south remained deeply impoverished, and many southern Italians experienced "unification" not as liberation but as the imposition of northern rule, heavy taxes, and military conscription. The new Italy faced the enormous challenge of creating Italians out of peoples who spoke dozens of mutually unintelligible dialects and had no experience of common political institutions.

German Unification

German unification followed a different path, driven not by romantic nationalist revolutionaries but by Prussian state power and the calculating genius of Otto von Bismarck. Prussia was the dominant military power in northern Germany — a state defined by its army, its Junker (landowning aristocratic) class, and a tradition of efficient, disciplined administration. Bismarck, appointed Minister-President of Prussia in 1862 by King Wilhelm I, was a conservative who despised liberal nationalism but understood that Prussia could use nationalist sentiment as a tool to achieve Prussian dominance over Germany.

Bismarck's strategy rested on a famous formulation: "The great questions of the day will not be decided by speeches and majority decisions — that was the mistake of 1848 and 1849 — but by iron and blood." He engineered three short, decisive wars in eight years that remade the map of Europe. The first was the war against Denmark in 1864, fought jointly by Prussia and Austria over the German-inhabited duchies of Schleswig and Holstein. The second was the Austro-Prussian War (Seven Weeks' War) of 1866, in which Prussia used the dispute over administration of the conquered duchies as a pretext to fight Austria for supremacy in Germany. The Prussian army, equipped with superior breech-loading rifles and organized by the brilliant military planner Helmuth von Moltke, crushed Austria in seven weeks. The resulting settlement excluded Austria from German affairs permanently and reorganized northern Germany under Prussian leadership in the North German Confederation.

The third war was the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71, which brought the southern German states — Bavaria, Württemberg, Baden, and Hesse-Darmstadt — into a national alliance against France. Napoleon III, manipulated by Bismarck into appearing the aggressor, declared war; the French army was rapidly defeated; Napoleon III himself was captured at Sedan; and Paris, besieged, eventually capitulated. In January 1871, in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles — at the very heart of French royal grandeur — the German Empire (Reich) was proclaimed with Wilhelm I as Emperor (Kaiser) and Bismarck as Chancellor.

The new German Empire was a constitutional monarchy — it had a parliament (Reichstag) elected by universal male suffrage — but it was not a democracy in any meaningful sense. The chancellor was responsible to the Emperor, not to the Reichstag. Prussia dominated the federation. The military remained under royal, not parliamentary, control. The German middle classes, who had been the great champions of liberal nationalism in 1848, largely accepted this conservative national state in exchange for national unification and the economic boom that followed. The empire was the largest and most powerful state in Europe, with the largest and best-equipped army, rapidly expanding industry, and a sense of national destiny that would shape — and eventually threaten — the world in the century to come.

Section 43.6

Socialism and Critiques of Capitalism

Socialism emerged as a response to the experience of industrial capitalism — to the factory system, the slums, the poverty wages, the child labor, the dangerous working conditions, and the vast disparities of wealth that characterized the industrializing world of the early nineteenth century. Socialists argued that these miseries were not accidental or the result of individual bad luck. They were the product of a system — capitalism — that organized economic life around private profit rather than human need. The solution was to change the system, not merely to reform its worst excesses.

The first generation of socialist thinkers — often called "utopian socialists" by Marx and Engels — included figures like Robert Owen, Claude Henri de Saint-Simon, and Charles Fourier. Owen, a Welsh industrialist, actually built model factory communities — New Lanark in Scotland, New Harmony in Indiana — in which workers were treated with dignity, educated, and provided with decent living conditions. He believed that human character was formed by environment, and that a better environment would produce better people. Saint-Simon imagined a society managed by scientists and industrialists who would rationally organize production for the common good. Fourier proposed a society organized into small cooperative communities called "phalansteries" in which people would rotate through different kinds of work to avoid the deadening monotony of specialized factory labor.

Marx and Engels published the Communist Manifesto in February 1848, just as the revolutionary wave was beginning to break across Europe. It is one of the most influential documents in the history of political thought — a brief, vivid, ferociously argued case that all human history is the history of class struggle, that capitalism is the latest and most comprehensive system of class exploitation, and that the proletariat is the revolutionary class that will overthrow capitalism and build a communist society without classes or exploitation.

The Manifesto's opening claim — that the history of all human societies is the history of class struggle — was sweeping and provocative. Marx and Engels argued that capitalism had achieved extraordinary productive power, but that power was organized for the benefit of the bourgeoisie — the class that owned the factories, machines, and raw materials. Workers, owning nothing but their labor power, had to sell that labor to survive. The value they produced in the factory exceeded the wages they were paid; the difference — "surplus value" in Marx's later analysis — was the source of capitalist profit. Workers were therefore systematically exploited, not through individual cruelty but through the structural logic of the system.

Marx's longer masterwork, Das Kapital (Volume I, 1867), developed this analysis with enormous detail and sophistication. It drew on classical economics — especially Adam Smith and David Ricardo — while inverting their conclusions. Where Smith saw the market as a mechanism for generating prosperity and freedom, Marx saw it as a mechanism for systematically extracting surplus value from workers while mystifying the social relations of exploitation behind the "fetishism of commodities." Capitalism, Marx argued, was not a natural or eternal system but a historically specific one that had emerged and would eventually be superseded.

Not all socialists were Marxists. Anarchists, led by figures like Mikhail Bakunin and Peter Kropotkin, shared Marx's critique of capitalism but rejected his prescription of a workers' state as a transitional phase. The state, in their view, was always an instrument of oppression; the goal of revolution should be the immediate abolition of all coercive authority, replaced by voluntary cooperation among free communities. Marx and Bakunin clashed bitterly in the First International (International Workingmen's Association), founded in London in 1864 as a coordinating body for labor and socialist movements. Their conflict eventually split the organization in 1872.

The Paris Commune of 1871 became the defining event for socialist thought and imagination for decades. When the French government attempted to disarm the National Guard units defending Paris after the Franco-Prussian War, Parisian workers rose in revolt and established a self-governing commune that held the city for 72 days. The Commune introduced a range of radical measures: the separation of church and state, free education, workers' control of enterprises abandoned by their owners, and the election of all officials with the right of immediate recall. It was crushed with extreme violence in "Bloody Week" (May 21–28, 1871) by French government troops; somewhere between 10,000 and 30,000 Communards were killed. Marx hailed the Commune as the first example of what the "dictatorship of the proletariat" — the workers' state — might look like in practice.

By the 1880s and 1890s, socialist and labor movements had grown into major forces in European politics. The German Social Democratic Party (SPD), founded in 1875, became the largest socialist party in the world by the turn of the century, winning millions of votes in Reichstag elections despite Bismarck's attempt to suppress it through the Anti-Socialist Laws of 1878–1890. French, British, Italian, and Scandinavian socialist parties developed similarly. The Second International, founded in 1889, coordinated socialist parties across borders and declared May 1 — May Day — a day of international workers' solidarity.

Historical Vocabulary
Class Consciousness

In Marxist theory, class consciousness is the awareness that members of a class develop of their shared identity, common interests, and position in the economic system. Workers who understand their individual situations as products of a system that structurally exploits all workers have developed class consciousness — and from that awareness, organized collective action becomes possible.

Marx argued that capitalism itself would generate class consciousness among the proletariat by concentrating workers in factories where they could communicate and organize, reducing skilled craft workers to interchangeable machine operators with a shared sense of degradation, and creating periodic economic crises that demonstrated the system's inherent instability. Whether this process actually produces revolutionary class consciousness, or whether other identities — national, religious, racial — prove stronger, is one of the central debates of modern political history.

Section 43.7

Labor Movements and Reform

The labor movement developed alongside and intertwined with socialist politics, but it was never identical to it. Labor movements were primarily concerned with the practical, immediate interests of working people: wages, hours, conditions, job security, and the right to organize. Many trade unionists were suspicious of grand socialist theories and focused on winning concrete improvements through collective bargaining and political pressure.

In Britain, trade unions had a longer and more complex legal history than anywhere else. In the early nineteenth century, combinations of workers were prohibited under the Combination Acts of 1799–1800. These laws were repealed in 1824–25, allowing trade unions to exist legally, though strikes were still treated as criminal conspiracies in many circumstances. The Chartist movement of the late 1830s and 1840s added a political dimension, demanding universal male suffrage, equal electoral districts, the secret ballot, annual parliaments, the abolition of property qualifications for Members of Parliament, and payment of MPs — the last important for enabling working-class men to serve in government. The Chartists organized enormous petition campaigns, rallied hundreds of thousands of followers, and staged a general strike in 1842. They ultimately failed to achieve their immediate demands, but their movement trained a generation of working-class organizers and kept the cause of democratic reform alive.

By the 1860s and 1870s, craft unions — organizing skilled workers like engineers, carpenters, and boilermakers — had established themselves as permanent features of British industrial life. They won important legal protections under the Trade Union Act of 1871, which formally recognized unions as legal entities. The Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884 significantly extended the male electorate, and by the 1880s many urban working-class men could vote. The rise of "New Unionism" in the late 1880s brought unskilled workers — gas workers, dockers, transport workers — into union organization for the first time, culminating in the great London Dock Strike of 1889, which won improved wages and working conditions and inspired a wave of organizing.

In France, labor organization was repeatedly suppressed and revived through the upheavals of the nineteenth century. The Le Chapelier Law of 1791 had prohibited workers' associations, and it was not until 1884 that trade unions were legalized. The Commune of 1871 had devastated the French labor movement, but recovery was rapid. By the 1890s, the General Confederation of Labor (CGT) was developing a syndicalist strategy — emphasizing direct action, the general strike, and the goal of workers' control of industry rather than parliamentary reform.

In Germany, Bismarck's response to the growing labor movement was characteristically two-pronged. The Anti-Socialist Laws of 1878–1890 banned socialist meetings, organizations, and publications — a period of intense repression that forced the SPD underground but could not destroy it. Simultaneously, Bismarck introduced what became the world's first modern social insurance system: health insurance in 1883, accident insurance in 1884, and old-age and disability pensions in 1889. These "state socialist" measures were explicitly designed to undercut socialist appeals to the working class by showing that the imperial state could protect workers from the worst insecurities of industrial life. They established a precedent for the welfare state that would be enormously influential in the twentieth century.

In the United States, labor organizing faced particularly fierce employer resistance and weak legal protections. The Knights of Labor, founded in 1869, was the first major national labor organization, and at its peak in the mid-1880s it had over 700,000 members — including women, Black workers, and immigrants alongside white male skilled workers. The American Federation of Labor (AFL), founded in 1886, took a narrower approach, organizing skilled craft workers and focusing on wages, hours, and conditions rather than broader social transformation. The Great Railroad Strike of 1877, the Haymarket affair of 1886, and the Homestead Strike of 1892 all demonstrated the intensity of labor conflict in the industrial United States and the willingness of employers and governments to use force against organizing workers.

Despite the violence and repression, the labor movements of the nineteenth century achieved significant results. The ten-hour workday, then the eight-hour day, became the goal of labor organizing across the industrial world. Factory Acts in Britain regulated child labor, required basic safety standards, and limited working hours. Wages rose in real terms in many industries as unions gained bargaining power. Workers' compensation systems, early safety regulations, and the beginnings of social insurance marked the first steps toward the welfare state that would transform industrial societies in the twentieth century.

Section 43.8

Abolitionism and Emancipation

The movement to abolish slavery was one of the most consequential moral campaigns in modern history. It challenged one of the oldest and most profitable institutions of the Western world — one that had been embedded in economies, legal systems, religious traditions, and racial ideologies for centuries. Its success, partial and contested as it was, required the combined efforts of enslaved people themselves, religious reformers, liberal idealists, and political organizers across the Atlantic world.

The Atlantic slave trade reached its peak in the eighteenth century, when millions of enslaved Africans were transported across the Middle Passage to work on sugar, tobacco, cotton, and coffee plantations in the Americas. Britain alone transported around three million enslaved people between 1640 and 1807. The profits of slavery flowed into European and American economies, funding industrialization, financing banks and insurance companies, and building the wealth of cities like Liverpool, Bristol, Bordeaux, and Newport, Rhode Island.

Abolitionist sentiment grew through the late eighteenth century from multiple sources. Quakers, whose faith rejected violence and affirmed the equality of all souls, were among the earliest and most consistent opponents of slavery. Evangelical Christians — in Britain, the Clapham Sect, which included William Wilberforce — combined genuine religious conviction with highly effective political organizing. Enlightenment thinkers who argued for natural rights found it increasingly difficult to square those principles with the chattel slavery of colonial plantations.

But the most powerful force in the abolitionist movement was the resistance of enslaved people themselves. Rebellion, flight, slowdown, and everyday resistance were constant features of slave societies. The Haitian Revolution of 1791–1804 — when enslaved people in Saint-Domingue rose against their masters, fought off French, British, and Spanish armies, and established the first Black republic in the hemisphere — demonstrated with unmistakable force that enslaved people were not passive victims but active agents who would risk everything for freedom. It also terrified slaveholders and moderate abolitionists alike, and was used by opponents of abolition to argue that freeing enslaved people would produce chaos and violence.

In Britain, the abolitionist movement achieved its first great victory in 1807, when Parliament voted to abolish the British slave trade — meaning that British ships could no longer transport enslaved people across the Atlantic. The campaign had been extraordinarily organized: petitions signed by hundreds of thousands of people, boycotts of slave-produced sugar, public lectures, pamphlets, and the haunting image of the slave ship Brookes packed with enslaved bodies that became perhaps the first successful political graphic of the modern age. Full abolition of slavery in British colonies came in 1833, with the Slavery Abolition Act, which freed approximately 800,000 enslaved people — and paid £20 million in compensation not to them but to the slaveholders who had "owned" them.

In the United States, the abolition of slavery was bound up with the deepest conflicts of national politics. The Constitution of 1787 had embedded slavery through the three-fifths clause, the fugitive slave clause, and the protection of the slave trade until 1808. The cotton gin of 1793 had made slave-produced cotton enormously profitable and tied the slave economy more deeply into the national fabric. Abolitionist movements grew through the 1820s and 1830s — William Lloyd Garrison's The Liberator, begun in 1831, was a fierce, uncompromising voice — but they were a minority, fiercely opposed by slaveholders and many northerners who feared disruption of the economic and political order.

The voices of formerly enslaved people were the most powerful in the American abolitionist movement. Frederick Douglass, who had escaped enslavement in Maryland in 1838, became the most prominent Black abolitionist in the world. His Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass (1845) was a devastating first-person account of the brutality of slavery that made it impossible for anyone who read it to pretend that slavery was anything other than a system of human torture. He argued with commanding rhetorical power that the principles of the American Declaration of Independence — "all men are created equal" — were incompatible with the continued enslavement of millions of Black Americans.

The Civil War (1861–1865) ultimately destroyed American slavery, though not because the Union government set out to abolish it. Lincoln's war aim was initially to preserve the Union, not to free the enslaved. But the dynamics of the war — the military service of approximately 180,000 Black soldiers fighting for the Union, the self-emancipation of thousands of enslaved people who fled to Union lines, and the political need to deny the Confederacy its labor force — pushed Lincoln toward the Emancipation Proclamation of January 1, 1863, which declared enslaved people in rebel states to be free. The Thirteenth Amendment (1865) abolished slavery throughout the United States.

Emancipation continued across the Atlantic world through the late nineteenth century. Cuba abolished slavery in 1886; Brazil — the last country in the Americas to do so — abolished it in 1888. In each case, the formal end of slavery was followed not by racial equality but by the construction of new systems of racial subordination — sharecropping, debt peonage, Jim Crow laws, colonial racial hierarchies — that denied the formerly enslaved people the full freedom that abolitionists had promised. The long struggle for racial justice was not ended by emancipation but transformed into new and bitter forms.

Section 43.9

Feminism and Rights Movements

Women in the nineteenth century lived under legal systems that treated them as subordinate to men in almost every domain of life. In most European and American legal codes, married women had no independent legal existence — they could not own property, sign contracts, or sue in their own names; their legal personality was subsumed in that of their husbands under the doctrine of coverture. Women were excluded from most higher education, most professions, and almost all formal political participation. They could not vote, stand for election, or sit on juries in most jurisdictions. These restrictions were justified by elaborate theories of "separate spheres" — the idea that men and women occupied naturally distinct roles, men in the public world of politics, commerce, and intellectual life, women in the domestic world of the home, family, and moral education of children.

The feminist movement of the nineteenth century challenged this ideology and these legal structures through a combination of intellectual argument, political organizing, and direct action. Its foundations were laid in the late eighteenth century by Mary Wollstonecraft, whose A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) — written in response to the French Revolution's failure to include women in its declaration of rights — argued that women's apparent inferiority was the product of inferior education and social conditioning, not of nature. If women were educated as rational beings and treated as full citizens, they would demonstrate the same capacity for reason and virtue as men.

Wollstonecraft's argument was largely ignored for decades. But by the 1840s, feminist organizing was emerging in both Europe and North America, drawing on the experience of women who had been active in reform movements — particularly abolitionism — and had found themselves expected to advocate for others' freedom while being denied their own. The connection between women's rights and abolitionism was direct and personal for many activists. Women like Sojourner Truth and Harriet Tubman, who had themselves been enslaved, made the connection between racial and gender oppression vivid and undeniable.

The Seneca Falls Convention of July 1848 in Seneca Falls, New York, was the founding moment of organized feminism in the United States. Organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott — both experienced abolitionists — the convention produced the Declaration of Sentiments, a document modeled explicitly on the Declaration of Independence. "We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal," it began, and proceeded to list the "injuries and usurpations" inflicted on women by men: denial of suffrage, coverture laws, exclusion from education and the professions, unequal wages, and exclusion from the church. The Declaration demanded the full rights of citizenship for women, including the right to vote.

The demand for women's suffrage was the most controversial of the feminist demands, opposed even by many who supported other feminist goals. Opponents argued that women lacked the necessary rationality for political life, that they would simply vote as their husbands directed, or that political participation would corrupt the feminine virtue that made women valuable as moral guardians of the home. Feminists responded with careful arguments — John Stuart Mill among the most sophisticated — that there was no rational basis for excluding women from political rights and that only political equality could ensure women's full legal and social equality.

In Britain, the women's suffrage campaign was organized and persistent. John Stuart Mill presented a petition to Parliament in 1866 signed by 1,499 women, and attempted to amend the Reform Act of 1867 to include women. Numerous women's suffrage societies were formed through the 1870s and 1880s. Progress was agonizingly slow. New Zealand became the first country to grant women full voting rights in 1893; Australia followed in 1902. In Britain and the United States, the struggle would not be won until the early twentieth century — through campaigns that became increasingly militant as patience wore out.

Feminism in the nineteenth century was also bound up with questions of class and race that complicated its universalist claims. The organized feminist movement in Britain and America was largely led by middle-class white women whose experiences of subordination were real but very different from those of working-class women and women of color. Black women in the United States — navigating the triple burden of race, gender, and often class — found that white feminist leaders frequently sacrificed racial equality to maintain alliances with white Southern women. Sojourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman?" speech (1851), delivered at a women's rights convention in Akron, Ohio, confronted this directly: if the argument for women's rights was based on women's weakness and delicacy, what about Black women who had worked in fields and been beaten as harshly as any man?

Despite these tensions, feminist movements achieved significant gains in the nineteenth century. By the end of the century, women in most European and North American countries had won the right to own property and retain their earnings after marriage. Higher education had opened to women at many institutions. Many professions — teaching, nursing, journalism, and eventually medicine and law — were increasingly accessible, if still contested. These were real changes in the legal and social condition of women, built by decades of organized advocacy and intellectual argument.

Chapter Review and Ideology Comparison Checkpoint

This chapter has examined the great ideological conflicts of the nineteenth century: liberalism's promise of individual rights and constitutional government alongside its systematic exclusions; conservatism's defense of tradition and order and its pragmatic adaptation to survive in an age of revolutions; nationalism's powerful redrawing of political maps through both popular enthusiasm and military force; socialism's systematic critique of industrial capitalism and its construction of organized working-class political movements; abolitionism's long struggle to end the Atlantic slave system; feminism's challenge to the legal and political exclusion of women; and labor movements' concrete achievements in improving the conditions of industrial workers.

Students reviewing this chapter should be able to compare the core principles, social bases, and historical achievements of each major ideology; explain the contradictions within liberalism and how those contradictions were challenged by excluded groups; trace the specific paths to Italian and German unification and explain what role nationalism, Realpolitik, and military power played in each; describe the arguments and achievements of the abolitionist and feminist movements; and connect the political ideologies of nineteenth-century Europe to their global contexts and their consequences for the twentieth century.

The central questions of the chapter return here for student reflection: Why do ideologies become more intense during periods of rapid social change? Who did each ideology include and who did it exclude? How did movements of the excluded — abolitionists, feminists, labor organizers — use, transform, or reject the dominant ideologies of their time? And how did the political conflicts of the nineteenth century lay the groundwork for the even larger convulsions of the twentieth?

Regional Lens

Regional Lens: The German States and the Making of a Nation

Before 1871, Germany was not a country but a concept — a cultural and linguistic community spread across dozens of political units of wildly varying size, from the Kingdom of Prussia (a major European power) to tiny principalities with a few thousand subjects. The German Confederation, established in 1815, provided a loose institutional framework but lacked any effective central government, common laws, or shared foreign policy. This political fragmentation had deep historical roots: the failure of the Holy Roman Empire to centralize, the devastation of the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), and the determined resistance of individual princes to any authority that would override their own.

What existed, increasingly through the early nineteenth century, was a shared cultural identity: a common literary language shaped by Luther's Bible translation and standardized by the classical writers Goethe and Schiller; a shared folklore collected and popularized by the Brothers Grimm; a rich tradition of music from Bach to Beethoven; and a growing sense that this cultural community deserved political expression. German Romanticism was intensely nationalist, celebrating the Volk — the folk or people — as the authentic source of national culture. Intellectuals like Johann Gottfried Herder argued that each people had its own distinctive spirit (Volksgeist) expressed in its language, customs, and folklore, and that genuine civilization meant cultivating these particular traditions rather than imitating foreign models.

The German university system was another cradle of nationalist thought. Figures like Johann Gottlieb Fichte, whose Addresses to the German Nation (1808) — delivered in Berlin while Napoleon's troops occupied the city — called on Germans to recognize their national identity and resist foreign domination. The Zollverein — the customs union established in 1834 under Prussian leadership — was another crucial step toward unity, creating a common economic space among most German states (excluding Austria) and demonstrating the practical benefits of cooperation. By the time Bismarck began his maneuvering in the 1860s, substantial economic integration already existed; what was needed was the political framework to match it.

The Germany that Bismarck created in 1871 was in some ways not the Germany that Romantic nationalists had dreamed of. It was conservative in its political structure, dominated by Prussia and its Junker aristocracy, and explicitly excluded Austria — the largest German-speaking state — in favor of a "little German" solution. But it was also the most powerful state in Europe, with rapidly expanding industry and a sense of national destiny that would shape — and eventually threaten — the world in the century to come.

Global Connection

Global Connection: Nationalism Travels — Latin America, the Ottoman Empire, and East Asia

The political ideas of the nineteenth century did not stay in Europe. They traveled through colonial contact, trade networks, journalism, migration, and the global spread of education in European languages and institutions. Nationalism, in particular, proved an extraordinarily portable idea — one that could be adapted to contexts very different from those in which it had originated, and used to challenge the very European powers that had originally developed it.

In Latin America, nationalist ideologies had already shaped the independence movements of the early nineteenth century, examined in Chapter 41. But nationalism continued to evolve, shaping debates about national identity, federalism versus centralism, the role of the Catholic Church, and the status of Indigenous and African-descended populations within newly formed republics. Liberal nationalism in Mexico found its greatest expression in Benito Juárez, a Zapotec Indigenous lawyer who led the Reform movement of the 1850s and resisted French imperial intervention in the 1860s.

In the Ottoman Empire, nationalism posed an existential threat. The Ottoman state was a multiethnic, multireligious empire that had governed Christians, Jews, Muslims, Arabs, Turks, Greeks, Armenians, Serbs, Bulgarians, and dozens of other communities for centuries. Greek nationalism led to Greek independence in 1821–1832. Serbian nationalism produced an autonomous Serbian state. Bulgarian nationalism led to a Bulgarian state after the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–78. Each nationalist success came at the expense of Ottoman territory and encouraged further nationalist movements. The Ottoman response — the Tanzimat reforms, which tried to bind all Ottoman subjects to a common Ottoman identity — could not ultimately compete with the centrifugal force of ethnic nationalism.

In East Asia, nationalism arrived later but with enormous impact. Japanese intellectuals in the Meiji period developed a powerful nationalism that drew on Western models while emphasizing specifically Japanese cultural and historical traditions — the Emperor, Shintoism, bushido — as the basis of national identity. Chinese reformers in the 1890s — responding to humiliating defeat by Japan in 1895 — began to think about China as a nation in the modern sense rather than as a civilization or a dynasty. Indian nationalists under British colonial rule used liberal arguments about the rights of peoples to self-government to demand first reform and eventually independence — turning the colonizers' own political vocabulary against them.

Empire Study

Empire Study: The German Empire (Second Reich), 1871–1918

Origins: The German Empire was proclaimed on January 18, 1871, in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, with Prussian King Wilhelm I as Emperor and Otto von Bismarck as Chancellor. It was the product of three wars of unification waged between 1864 and 1871 and represented the triumph of Prussian power over the older German Confederation.

Political Structure: The Empire was a federation of 25 states, the largest of which was Prussia — which accounted for about two-thirds of the Empire's territory and population. The Reichstag was elected by universal male suffrage, but the Chancellor was responsible to the Emperor, not to parliament. The military was under royal command and largely outside civilian oversight. Real power rested with the Kaiser, the Chancellor, and the military and bureaucratic elites — not with the elected parliament.

Economy: Germany industrialized rapidly after unification, becoming a major industrial power in chemicals, steel, electrical engineering, and machine tools. By the 1900s, German industrial production exceeded British production in several key sectors. The Empire's economic growth was among the fastest in the world, fueling both national pride and international rivalry.

Social Policy: Bismarck's social insurance system — health insurance (1883), accident insurance (1884), old-age and disability pensions (1889) — was the world's first comprehensive state social security system. It was politically motivated — designed to undercut socialist appeals — but it set a precedent for the welfare state that would transform industrial societies in the twentieth century.

Opposition and tensions: The Empire was never politically unified. The SPD grew steadily despite Bismarck's repression and represented millions of workers who felt excluded from the conservative imperial order. Catholic regions periodically chafed under Protestant Prussian dominance. Ethnic minorities — Poles, Danes, French-speaking Alsatians — faced pressure to Germanize. These internal tensions, combined with an increasingly aggressive foreign policy under Kaiser Wilhelm II, contributed to the catastrophe of 1914.

Legacy: The German Empire lasted only until 1918, when military defeat in World War I produced the Kaiser's abdication and the proclamation of the Weimar Republic. But its political structures, its social insurance model, and its ambitions had shaped European history profoundly — and its collapse set the conditions for the disasters of the interwar period examined in Chapter 47.

Map Study

Map Study: The Unification of Germany, 1866–1871

The process of German unification can be traced through three stages visible on a political map of central Europe. In 1866, before the Austro-Prussian War, a patchwork of German states existed in the German Confederation. After the Austro-Prussian War of 1866, the North German Confederation was formed under Prussian leadership, excluding Austria and the southern German states. After the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71, the southern German states joined to complete the German Empire.

On any map of this transformation, students should note: the enormous size of Prussia relative to other German states; the geographic position of Austria, separated from the North German heartland by Prussian territory after 1866; the location of Alsace-Lorraine, taken from France in 1871, which created lasting Franco-German resentment; and the position of ethnic minority populations — Poles, Danes, and French-speaking Alsatians — within the new Empire's borders.

The map also reveals what was not included: German-speaking Austria, excluded in the "little German" solution; German-speaking communities in Switzerland and elsewhere; and the geographic reality that the new Germany sat at the center of Europe, bordered by France, Russia, Austria-Hungary, and the North Sea — a position of power but also of strategic vulnerability that would shape German foreign policy through 1914.

Primary Source Window

The Communist Manifesto (1848) — Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels

Creators: Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels  ·  Date: February 1848  ·  Type: Political manifesto

Context: Marx (1818–1883) and Engels (1820–1895) wrote the Communist Manifesto in the winter of 1847–48 at the request of the Communist League, a small organization of German exile workers based in London. It was published in London in February 1848, just as revolutionary uprisings were beginning to sweep across Europe. Initially little noticed amid the dramatic events of 1848, it became one of the most widely read political documents in history, translated into dozens of languages and influencing labor and socialist movements around the world.

The Manifesto opens by asserting that the history of all existing society is the history of class struggles — contests between oppressors and oppressed, from ancient master and slave to modern bourgeois and proletarian. Capitalism, Marx and Engels argue, has simplified the struggle: the world now divides into two great hostile camps. Capitalism has achieved extraordinary productive power but organizes it for the benefit of the bourgeoisie, not humanity. As capitalism develops, it creates the conditions for its own overthrow: it concentrates workers in factories where they can organize, and generates periodic crises that reveal its fundamental instability. The manifesto closes with a call for workers of all countries to unite.

Analysis: The Communist Manifesto is simultaneously a work of economic analysis, historical argument, political rhetoric, and prophetic vision. Its historical schema — all history as class struggle — is sweeping and often oversimplified, but it identifies something real: that economic interests and social power are deeply connected, and that the distribution of wealth in any society is not natural or inevitable but the product of specific historical arrangements that can be changed. Its prediction that the proletariat would grow more revolutionary as capitalism developed was not borne out in the countries where capitalism was most advanced. Its vision of communist society was deliberately sketchy, which allowed it to be interpreted in wildly different ways. It largely ignores peasants, women, colonized peoples, and ethnic minorities — omissions that would have significant consequences when Marxism spread to agrarian societies in Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

Questions for students:

  2. Why do Marx and Engels argue that capitalism will produce the conditions for its own overthrow? Does this argument seem convincing?
  3. The Manifesto does not include women, colonized peoples, or peasants as central actors in its revolutionary vision. How does this omission limit its analysis?
  4. Compare the Communist Manifesto to the Declaration of Sentiments from Seneca Falls (1848). How do these two manifestos differ in their understanding of who is oppressed and what the solution should be?
Evidence Window

Evidence Window: Political Cartoons and Visual Propaganda in the Age of Ideology

The nineteenth century was the great age of political cartoons and visual satire, and these images constitute important evidence about how ideological conflicts were represented, understood, and felt. The rapid spread of illustrated newspapers and magazines — the British Punch, the French Le Charivari, the German Simplicissimus — made visual political commentary available to mass audiences for the first time. Political cartoons used caricature, allegory, and symbol to make arguments about current events, ideological conflicts, and social conditions.

Historians use political cartoons as evidence of how political ideas circulated among popular audiences, what emotions and anxieties public figures and events provoked, and how complex ideological conflicts were simplified and dramatized for mass consumption. Cartoons depicting the revolutions of 1848 show aristocrats and monarchs fleeing barricades, workers triumphant in the streets, and then, in the aftermath, workers crushed by soldiers. Cartoons depicting Bismarck — who was frequently shown as a colossal figure manipulating or crushing smaller nations — reveal European anxieties about Prussian power. Cartoons depicting labor conflict often showed workers and capitalists as literally different species, employing visual dehumanization that could work in either direction.

The limits of this evidence are significant. Political cartoons reflect the perspectives of their creators — typically educated men with access to printing technology — and often caricature or dehumanize their subjects in ways that reflect the class, racial, and gender biases of their time. Working-class people were frequently depicted as physically threatening or intellectually limited; women reformers were caricatured as masculine or ridiculous; non-European peoples were consistently depicted in racist terms that naturalized colonial hierarchies. Reading political cartoons as historical evidence requires understanding not only what they show but what they assume, what they distort, and whose perspectives they represent and exclude.

Historical Debate

Historical Debate: Was the Failure of the 1848 Revolutions Inevitable?

The question: The revolutions of 1848 swept across Europe with extraordinary speed, toppling regimes and inspiring millions. Within months, almost all had been suppressed. Were the revolutions inevitably defeated because of structural contradictions within the revolutionary coalition, or did specific decisions, mistakes, and contingencies determine their failure?

Interpretation A — Structural Failure: Many historians argue that the revolutions of 1848 were defeated because of fundamental contradictions within the revolutionary coalition. Liberals, nationalists, and socialists had come together in opposition to the old order but had profoundly different goals. Middle-class liberals wanted constitutions and civil rights but were terrified by working-class demands for social equality and economic reform. The "June Days" in Paris — when liberal republicans backed the army's massacre of socialist workers — demonstrated this split with brutal clarity. Once the working class was separated from middle-class liberalism, the old order could reassert itself. In the Habsburg Empire, the multiethnic character of nationalism meant that each people's nationalism threatened others, allowing Austria to play nationalities against each other and use Croatian troops to suppress Hungarian uprisings.
Interpretation B — Contingent Failure: Other historians emphasize that the revolutions came much closer to success than is often recognized, and that specific decisions and contingencies determined their outcome. In Germany, the Frankfurt Parliament came close to agreeing on a constitution for a unified constitutional Germany; its failure resulted partly from the difficulty of defining German borders and partly from Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia's decision to refuse the crown offered by the parliament — a decision that was not inevitable. In France, the election of Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte as president in December 1848 — which surprised most observers — was shaped by specific electoral dynamics that could have gone differently. Had different decisions been made at key moments, the revolutions might have produced different outcomes.

Evidence: Historians supporting structural interpretations point to sociological data on class composition of revolutionary movements and the pattern across multiple countries of liberal-socialist splits leading to conservative restoration. Historians emphasizing contingency point to the detailed record of parliamentary debates, diplomatic negotiations, and military decisions, showing that outcomes were genuinely uncertain at many moments.

Why it matters: The debate about 1848 is ultimately a debate about historical change more broadly — about whether great social forces make outcomes predictable, or whether individuals and decisions matter. It is also a debate about whether liberal democracy and social reform are compatible within a single political movement, or whether class conflict will always split reformist coalitions.

What additional evidence would you need to strengthen your argument? Can both interpretations be simultaneously correct about different aspects of the revolutionary failures?

Comparison Across Societies

Comparison Across Societies: The Abolition of Slavery in Britain and the United States

Britain and the United States were the two most prominent slave-owning societies in the English-speaking world, and both abolished slavery in the nineteenth century — but through radically different processes.

Britain: Abolished the slave trade in 1807 and slavery itself in 1833 through parliamentary legislation. The abolitionist campaign was primarily middle-class and evangelical in character, organized through petitions, boycotts, and parliamentary lobbying. It was non-violent from the abolitionist side and achieved through normal parliamentary processes. The Act compensated slaveholders — £20 million, about 40% of the national budget — and introduced a four-year "apprenticeship" period during which the formerly enslaved were required to continue working for their former owners. Crucially, Britain abolished slavery in its colonies while maintaining an empire that would impose other forms of racial subordination in Africa, Asia, and elsewhere.
United States: Abolished slavery through a catastrophic civil war that killed approximately 620,000 soldiers and countless civilians. The American abolitionist movement was more divided, more racially complex, and more politically constrained than its British counterpart. The Constitution protected slavery; the slave states formed a powerful political bloc; and the economic interests tied to slave-produced cotton were deeply embedded in the global economy. When abolition came, it was not the result of a purely moral campaign but of a political and military crisis that made abolition a war aim. No compensation was paid to slaveholders; the formerly enslaved received neither land nor systematic economic support; and within a decade the political gains of Reconstruction were rolled back.

Why the comparison matters: The comparison reveals how the same moral goal — the abolition of slavery — was achieved through radically different processes depending on political structures, economic interests, racial demographics, and the agency of the enslaved themselves. It also shows that formal abolition was only the beginning of the struggle for racial equality in both countries, and that the "peaceful" British abolition concealed its own forms of coercion that complicate any simple narrative of moral progress.

Multiple Perspectives

Multiple Perspectives: The Revolutions of 1848 — Who Experienced Them and How

Liberal professionals and students: For educated middle-class liberals — lawyers, doctors, journalists, university students — 1848 was the intoxicating fulfillment of a generation's dreams. In Vienna, Berlin, Prague, Paris, and Milan, they manned the barricades, drafted constitutions, debated in newly convened parliaments, and believed that reason and liberty were finally triumphing over tyranny. The subsequent failures — brutal suppression, the reconquest of Vienna and Budapest, Louis-Napoleon's coup in France — were devastating, producing a generation of exile, disillusionment, and, for some, radicalization.
Industrial workers and artisans: For working-class people in the revolutionary cities, 1848 raised different hopes and posed different questions. Artisans wanted protection from industrial competition as much as constitutional government. Industrial workers wanted higher wages, shorter hours, the right to organize, and a social republic that would guarantee work and subsistence rather than merely political rights. In Paris, the establishment of the National Workshops seemed to point toward a social democracy beyond classical liberalism. When the workshops were closed as too expensive and too radical, the resulting uprising was a revolt against the betrayal of the promise of social equality by a republic that turned out to serve only middle-class interests.
Women: Women participated in the revolutions of 1848 in many roles — at the barricades, in cafes and salons where politics were discussed, as writers and journalists, and as organizers of clubs and associations. But the revolutions largely failed to extend their promised freedoms to women. The Frankfurt Parliament that debated German constitutions did not include a single woman among its elected members. The French Second Republic granted universal suffrage — to men. Women who raised the question of their own political rights were told to be patient, that the question of women's suffrage was premature. The experience of 1848 taught many women activists that they would have to build a separate feminist movement rather than relying on liberal nationalism to include them.
Peasants and rural populations: The revolutions of 1848 were primarily urban phenomena, and their relationship to the rural majority of Europe was ambivalent and complex. In France, peasant voters — enfranchised for the first time by universal male suffrage — proved to be a deeply conservative force, suspicious of urban republicans and voting instead for Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte. In the Habsburg Empire, the emancipation of the peasantry from remaining feudal obligations was a real gain that many peasants accepted while remaining loyal to the dynasty. The failure to mobilize rural populations effectively was one of the central weaknesses of the 1848 revolutions.
Cause and Effect

Cause and Effect: The Emergence of Organized Socialism

Long-Term Causes

The industrial revolution transformed European economies, concentrating thousands of workers in factories under conditions of intense exploitation. Traditional craft guilds and other protective institutions were suppressed. Enlightenment ideas about equality and rights provided intellectual vocabulary for articulating workers' grievances as matters of justice rather than mere poverty. The French Revolution demonstrated that fundamental social change was possible.

Short-Term Causes

The revolutions of 1848 and their failure demonstrated that liberal republicanism would not protect workers' interests when they conflicted with property. The June Days massacre in Paris radicalized a generation. The crushing of the Paris Commune in 1871 confirmed that the state would ultimately serve ruling-class interests. Rapid industrial expansion created growing numbers of workers concentrated in cities where political organization was possible.

The Transformation

By the 1860s and 1870s, socialist and labor movements had developed from scattered organizations into national and international institutions. The First International (1864) and Second International (1889) provided coordination frameworks. National trade union federations and socialist parties established permanent structures. Working-class newspapers, clubs, and educational associations created a labor culture sustaining political consciousness.

Effects (Short- and Long-Term)

Socialist parties grew rapidly across Europe; the SPD became Germany's largest party by vote share by 1890. Trade union membership expanded dramatically. Labor won early social legislation and the beginnings of welfare state provisions. In the long term, organized socialism provided the framework for major revolutions in Russia (1917), China (1949), Cuba (1959), and elsewhere, shaping the entire twentieth century.

Historical complexity: The emergence of organized socialism was never automatic. Religious faith, ethnic identity, and national loyalty competed with class consciousness. In Britain, many workers remained Liberal voters long after one might expect a socialist shift. In the United States, racial divisions within the working class repeatedly undermined labor solidarity. The path from industrial capitalism to organized socialism was contested at every step.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Continuity and Change Over Time: The Position of Women in European and North American Society, 1800–1900

What Changed

  • Married women in Britain and the United States won the right to own property and retain their own earnings (Married Women's Property Acts, 1870 in Britain and various state laws in the USA)
  • Women gained access to higher education at many institutions, including several major universities by the 1880s and 1890s
  • Professions including teaching, nursing, journalism, and increasingly medicine opened to women, though with persistent barriers
  • Organized feminist movements placed women's suffrage on the political agenda; New Zealand granted full voting rights in 1893
  • The ideology of total female exclusion from public life was successfully challenged in legal, intellectual, and cultural terms

What Stayed the Same

  • Women remained legally disenfranchised in Britain, France, Germany, and the United States throughout the century
  • Women remained significantly underrepresented in professions requiring higher education and credentials
  • The ideology of "separate spheres" remained powerful across class lines, shaping expectations of how women should behave in public space
  • Violence against women — domestic violence, sexual assault — remained common and largely unaddressed by law
  • Working-class women continued to labor in factories, workshops, and domestic service under conditions that bore little resemblance to the idealized domestic sphere invoked to justify their political exclusion

Why this pattern matters: The partial and uneven nature of women's gains reveals the limits of a liberal feminist strategy focused on legal rights and individual opportunity. Legal equality — important as it was — did not automatically produce social equality when underlying economic structures remained unchanged. The history of women's rights in the nineteenth century demonstrates both the power of organized advocacy and the depth of structural inequality that advocacy alone could not overcome.

Everyday Life and Social History

Everyday Life: Reading Politics in the Industrial Age

For a factory worker in Manchester, Lyon, or Hamburg in the 1840s, the experience of industrialization was not primarily ideological — it was physical, daily, exhausting, and uncertain. A typical male factory worker might work twelve to fourteen hours a day, six days a week, for wages that barely covered rent, food, and fuel. His workplace — a cotton mill, an iron foundry, a chemical works — was hot, loud, often dangerous, and relentlessly rhythmic, governed by the machine's pace rather than the worker's own. His housing was likely cramped, damp, and crowded. His children, in the absence of compulsory education, might be working alongside him.

But this world was also politically charged in ways that had no precedent. The new industrial cities had newspapers — cheap, printed in large quantities, sold on street corners — that brought political news and debate into workers' neighborhoods. Reading groups, working-men's clubs, and union meetings were places where political ideas were discussed, debated, and applied to everyday grievances. A worker who joined a trade union in the 1840s or 1850s might encounter not only arguments about wages and hours but discussions of the Communist Manifesto, of Chartism, of the revolutions of 1848, of the rights of citizens, of the relationship between political and economic power.

Women workers — seamstresses, millworkers, domestic servants — had less access to these formal political spaces but were not apolitical. Women participated in food riots — one of the oldest forms of popular politics — when prices rose and their families faced hunger. Women workers in textile industries organized strikes. Women attended abolitionist lectures, signed suffrage petitions, and participated in the temperance movement — simultaneously a moral reform campaign and a form of political organization for women excluded from formal politics.

The political culture of the nineteenth-century working class was not simply a top-down transmission of ideological systems invented by intellectuals. It was a complex mixture of inherited traditions (guild solidarity, community loyalty, religious faith), new ideological frameworks (socialism, liberalism, nationalism), and immediate experience (the particular grievances of a specific workplace, neighborhood, or industry). Understanding this complexity — rather than reducing workers to passive victims of capitalism or simple carriers of class consciousness — is essential to understanding how the ideological conflicts of the nineteenth century actually unfolded in the lives of real people.

Environment and Technology

Technology and Ideology: Printing, Literacy, and the Political Revolution

The political revolutions of the nineteenth century were inseparable from a technological revolution in print. The development of the steam-powered printing press, cheap paper made from wood pulp rather than rags, and mechanical typesetting dramatically reduced the cost of producing printed materials. The result was an explosion of newspapers, pamphlets, books, magazines, and handbills that transformed the political landscape.

In 1800, newspaper production was expensive, laborious, and limited. A compositor set type by hand, letter by letter; a press printed a few hundred sheets an hour; paper was costly. By 1850, steam presses could print tens of thousands of sheets per hour. By the 1880s, rotary presses could produce hundreds of thousands of copies of a newspaper in a day. Combined with expanding mass literacy — the product of public education systems that spread through Europe and North America across the century — these technological changes created a genuinely mass reading public for the first time in history.

This had direct consequences for the ideological conflicts of the era. Political arguments that in 1800 circulated only in elite pamphlets and journals could now reach millions. The Communist Manifesto was printed and reprinted across the world; editions appeared in German, English, French, Russian, Polish, Swedish, Danish, and eventually dozens of other languages. Frederick Douglass's Narrative sold 30,000 copies in the first five years after its publication. Uncle Tom's Cabin sold 300,000 copies in the United States and over a million in Britain in its first year. The Chartist newspaper The Northern Star reached a working-class audience of hundreds of thousands at its peak.

Technological change did not determine ideological content, but it determined the scale and speed at which ideas could spread. The possibility of mass political mobilization — whether through a socialist party, a nationalist movement, an abolitionist campaign, or a feminist organization — depended on the ability to communicate with large numbers of people simultaneously. Print technology made this possible. The political revolutions of the nineteenth century were, in a profound sense, also media revolutions, and the relationship between new communications technologies and political radicalism would continue to shape history in the centuries that followed.

Chapter Summary

The nineteenth century was the age of ideology. Four great political traditions — liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, and socialism — competed to define the terms of political life in rapidly industrializing and urbanizing societies. Each tradition offered not merely a set of policies but a comprehensive account of human nature, historical development, social organization, and the proper ends of political life. Their competition produced revolutions, counterrevolutions, reform movements, civil wars, and constitutional struggles that reshaped political institutions across Europe and, through European power and the spread of European ideas, around the world.

Liberalism argued for individual rights, constitutional government, freedom of expression and economic activity, and the rule of law. It was the ideology of the educated middle classes — lawyers, merchants, doctors, journalists — who wanted political power commensurate with their economic weight and intellectual capacities. Liberalism achieved significant institutional successes: constitutional governments, independent judiciaries, freedom of the press, and formal equality before the law spread across the Western world over the course of the century. But liberalism was also deeply contradictory: it proclaimed universal rights while systematically excluding women, the working class, colonized peoples, and enslaved people from those rights.

Conservatism resisted rapid change in defense of tradition, order, social hierarchy, and established religious institutions. Born as a reaction to the French Revolution, it argued that inherited institutions embodied accumulated wisdom that reason alone could not re-create. Conservatism proved more adaptable than its early defenders might have hoped: pragmatic conservatives like Disraeli and Bismarck found ways to incorporate nationalism and even limited social reform into a conservative framework that preserved property, hierarchy, and dynastic authority.

Nationalism was the most explosive force of the era. It claimed that peoples sharing a common language, culture, and history constituted nations with the right to political self-determination. Nationalism reshaped the map of Europe through the unifications of Italy and Germany and the progressive dissolution of the Ottoman Empire's European territories. It inspired anti-colonial movements in Latin America, Asia, and the Middle East. But nationalism was also capable of deep intolerance and violence, defining national communities in ways that excluded minorities and driving, in its later development, ethnic cleansing and genocide.

Socialism emerged from the experience of industrial capitalism as its most systematic critique. In many variants — utopian socialism, Marxism, anarchism, democratic socialism — it argued that the organization of economic life around private profit was the root cause of poverty, exploitation, and social injustice. By the end of the century, socialist parties and trade unions had grown into major political forces across Europe, winning millions of votes, building extensive organizational networks, and pushing governments toward social reform that would accelerate dramatically in the twentieth century.

The abolitionist movement brought together religious conviction, liberal principles, and the resistance of enslaved people themselves to challenge and ultimately end the Atlantic slave system. The formal abolition of slavery in the British colonies (1833), the United States (1865), Cuba (1886), and Brazil (1888) was a genuine moral and political achievement. But abolition was only the beginning of the struggle for racial equality: freed people in the American South faced the replacement of chattel slavery with sharecropping, debt peonage, and systematic racial violence, and racial ideologies continued to structure political and social life long after formal slavery had ended.

Feminism challenged the systematic exclusion of women from political and legal life. Drawing on liberal principles of individual rights and equality, and on the practical experience of women who had organized in reform movements only to find themselves excluded from the political gains those movements achieved, feminists demanded legal equality, property rights, access to education and the professions, and ultimately the right to vote. They achieved significant legal reforms through the century but did not win voting rights in most major democracies until the early twentieth century.

Labor movements organized workers to demand better wages, shorter hours, safer conditions, and the right to collective bargaining. They built trade unions, political parties, and a distinctive working-class culture that sustained solidarity through periods of repression and defeat. Through the century, they won important legal reforms — the recognition of trade unions, factory legislation, the beginnings of social insurance — while the fundamental structure of capitalist property relations remained intact.

These ideological conflicts were never purely European. The ideas of the nineteenth century traveled through colonial networks, trade routes, and migration to reshape politics around the world. Nationalism inspired movements in Latin America, the Ottoman Empire, India, and China. Liberal principles of self-government were used by colonized peoples to demand the same rights that Europeans claimed for themselves. Socialism inspired revolutionary movements in Russia, China, Cuba, and Vietnam. The ideological legacy of the nineteenth century was truly global, shaping the political conflicts of the twentieth century and beyond.

The great lesson of the nineteenth century's ideological age is perhaps this: ideas matter profoundly, but they are always embedded in social structures and power relations that shape how they are interpreted, who uses them, and what they accomplish. Liberalism could mean genuine freedom for some and sophisticated justification for the oppression of others. Nationalism could mean liberation for a people long denied self-determination and oppression for minorities within the new nation. Socialism could mean workers' solidarity and also, in certain versions, authoritarian state control. The ideas themselves did not determine these outcomes; the social forces, power structures, and political choices of real historical actors did. This is why the history of ideology is never simply a history of ideas, but always also a history of people struggling over power, justice, and the meaning of a better world.

A systematic set of beliefs, values, and goals that explains how society works, assigns meaning to social conflict, and points toward a desired future; became the dominant framework for nineteenth-century political life.
Liberalism
Political ideology emphasizing individual rights, limited government, constitutional rule, and economic freedom; dominant ideology of the educated middle classes; achieved major institutional successes while systematically excluding women, workers, and colonized peoples.
Classical liberalism
The variant of liberalism emphasizing minimal state intervention in the economy, free markets, free trade, and the primacy of individual economic freedom over social equality or collective welfare.
Harm principle
Mill's principle that individuals can be restrained only when their actions harm others; established a foundational liberal limit on state authority.
Conservatism
Political ideology emphasizing tradition, social order, hierarchy, established institutions, and caution about rapid change; born in reaction to the French Revolution; associated with Burke, Metternich, Disraeli, and Bismarck.
Nationalism
Political ideology claiming that peoples sharing a common language, culture, and history constitute nations with the right to political self-determination; the dominant political force in nineteenth-century European politics.
Nation-state
A state in which political boundaries are claimed to correspond to the boundaries of a national community; the nineteenth century was the great age of nation-state formation.
Risorgimento
The Italian nationalist movement for unification and independence; culminated in the establishment of the Kingdom of Italy in 1861 and the incorporation of Rome in 1870.
Realpolitik
Politics based on practical calculations of power rather than ideological or moral principles; associated with Bismarck's approach to German unification through "iron and blood."
Zollverein
The German customs union established in 1834 under Prussian leadership; created a common economic space among most German states and helped lay the groundwork for political unification.
Socialism
Political ideology arguing that the means of production should be collectively owned and economic life organized for collective rather than private benefit; emerged as a systematic response to industrial capitalism.
Marxism
The variant of socialism developed by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, emphasizing class struggle, historical materialism, and the revolutionary overthrow of capitalism by the proletariat.
Historical materialism
Marx's theoretical framework arguing that material conditions — especially economic structures and class relations — are the primary drivers of historical change.
Proletariat
In Marxist theory, the industrial working class that must sell its labor to survive; the class whose revolutionary potential Marx placed at the center of his theory.
Bourgeoisie
In Marxist theory, the class that owns the means of production; more broadly, the property-owning, educated middle class.
Class consciousness
The awareness workers develop of their shared identity and common interests as a class; in Marxist theory, a prerequisite for revolutionary political action.
Utopian socialism
Early variants of socialism — associated with Robert Owen, Saint-Simon, and Fourier — that relied on moral persuasion, experimental communities, and appeals to reason rather than class struggle.
Anarchism
Political ideology rejecting all coercive authority including the state; advocating self-governing communities organized through voluntary cooperation; associated with Bakunin and Kropotkin.
Constitutionalism
The principle that government authority should be limited and defined by a written or unwritten constitution that protects individual rights and constrains arbitrary power.
Suffrage
The right to vote; a central demand of both democratic and feminist movements in the nineteenth century.
Chartism
British working-class political movement (1838–1857) demanding parliamentary reform including universal male suffrage, annual parliaments, and the secret ballot; the first mass working-class political movement.
Trade union
An organized association of workers formed to protect and advance their collective interests through bargaining and political action; won legal recognition in Britain in 1871.
Abolitionism
The movement to end slavery and the slave trade; drew on religious conviction, liberal philosophy, and the resistance of enslaved people; achieved formal abolition across the Atlantic world between 1833 and 1888.
Emancipation
The formal freeing of enslaved people from bondage; completed across the Atlantic world between 1833 and 1888; followed by new systems of racial subordination in most societies.
Feminism
The belief that women deserve equal rights, opportunities, and social standing to men; in the nineteenth century organized primarily around legal equality, property rights, education, and suffrage.
Coverture
The legal doctrine by which a married woman's legal identity was subsumed in that of her husband; challenged and partially dismantled by nineteenth-century feminist reform.
Second International
Association of socialist and labor parties founded in 1889; established May Day as an international workers' day; coordinated socialist politics across borders until divided by World War I in 1914.

Major People, Places, and Polities

Identification & Significance

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873)
British philosopher and economist; most sophisticated liberal thinker of the mid-nineteenth century; wrote On Liberty (1859) and The Subjection of Women (1869); supported women's suffrage; also defended colonial rule as a temporary necessity for "uncivilized" peoples.
Edmund Burke (1729–1797)
British-Irish statesman; foundational theorist of modern conservatism; Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790) argued that inherited institutional wisdom cannot be replaced by abstract reason.
Karl Marx (1818–1883)
German philosopher and economist; co-authored the Communist Manifesto (1848); published Das Kapital (1867); developed the most influential socialist analysis of capitalism and the most widely read critique of liberal political economy.
Friedrich Engels (1820–1895)
German socialist and Marx's collaborator; wrote The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845) providing empirical documentation of industrial exploitation; financially supported Marx for decades.
Giuseppe Mazzini (1805–1872)
Italian nationalist and republican; founded Young Italy (1831); his vision of popular republican nationalism inspired a generation of European nationalists even as his methods repeatedly failed.
Count Camillo di Cavour (1810–1861)
Prime Minister of Piedmont-Sardinia; pragmatic architect of Italian unification through diplomacy, industrial development, and strategic alliance with Napoleon III; represents the triumph of Realpolitik over romantic nationalism.
Giuseppe Garibaldi (1807–1882)
Italian nationalist military leader; led the Expedition of the Thousand (1860); conquered the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies and handed it to King Victor Emmanuel II; romantic symbol of nationalist heroism.
Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898)
Prussian statesman; Chancellor of the North German Confederation and then the German Empire; architect of German unification through three wars; introduced the world's first social insurance system as a conservative strategy to undercut socialism.
William Wilberforce (1759–1833)
British abolitionist; led the parliamentary campaign against the slave trade; central to the movement that achieved abolition of the British slave trade in 1807 and laid the groundwork for emancipation in 1833.
Frederick Douglass (1818–1895)
American abolitionist, orator, and writer; formerly enslaved; his Narrative (1845) became one of the most powerful documents in the global abolitionist movement; argued that the Declaration of Independence was incompatible with slavery.
Sojourner Truth (c. 1797–1883)
American abolitionist and feminist; formerly enslaved; her "Ain't I a Woman?" speech (1851) challenged white feminism's racial blind spots and demanded that Black women's experiences be centered in discussions of gender and rights.
Elizabeth Cady Stanton (1815–1902)
American feminist; co-organized Seneca Falls Convention (1848); co-authored Declaration of Sentiments; a founding figure of organized feminism in the United States.
Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797)
British feminist; A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) established the foundational argument that women's apparent inferiority was the product of inferior education, not nature.
Piedmont-Sardinia (Kingdom of Sardinia)
Italian state in the northwest; under Cavour and King Victor Emmanuel II, became the engine of Italian unification through diplomatic maneuvering and military campaigns.
Prussia
German kingdom; dominant military and political power; under Bismarck and King/Emperor Wilhelm I, became the core of the new German Empire proclaimed in 1871.
Paris Commune (1871)
Workers' government that held Paris for 72 days before being suppressed with extreme violence by French government troops; became a defining symbol for socialist and labor movements worldwide.
Seneca Falls Convention (1848)
First women's rights convention in the United States; produced the Declaration of Sentiments; landmark founding event of organized American feminism.
First International (1864–1876)
International Workingmen's Association; first major attempt to coordinate socialist and labor movements across national borders; split between Marxists and anarchists in 1872.
Frankfurt Parliament (1848–1849)
German constituent assembly that attempted to draft a constitution for a unified Germany during the 1848 revolution; failed when Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia refused the crown it offered.
Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)
The only successful large-scale slave revolution in history; established the first Black republic in the hemisphere; transformed the global politics of slavery, inspiring abolitionists and terrifying slaveholders worldwide.

Chapter Reflection

The political ideologies of the nineteenth century were not merely historical curiosities or intellectual puzzles. They were the frameworks through which millions of people understood their world, identified their enemies, formed their alliances, and imagined their futures. The arguments that liberals, conservatives, nationalists, and socialists made were arguments about the most fundamental questions of political life — who should rule, what rights people possess, how economic life should be organized, what obligations people owe one another, and what kind of world humanity should be building toward. These questions have not been resolved. They are still being argued today, often with the same intellectual frameworks that the nineteenth century developed.

The chapter reveals a consistent pattern in the history of political thought: ideas that begin as challenges to existing power are always in tension with their own ideological limits. Liberalism challenged divine right monarchy and aristocratic privilege in the name of human freedom — and then struggled to extend that freedom to women, workers, and colonized peoples who pointed out, with increasing insistence, that their exclusion was incompatible with the universalist principles liberals proclaimed. Nationalism challenged the dynastic empires of the old order in the name of popular self-determination — and then produced new states that denied self-determination to minorities within their borders. Socialism challenged the exploitation of industrial capitalism in the name of equality and justice — and then, in some of its twentieth-century variants, produced states that denied basic liberties in the name of collective welfare. Understanding these tensions is essential to understanding why political ideologies always produce outcomes that disappoint as well as inspire their original advocates.

The global dimensions of the chapter's subject remind us that the political history of the nineteenth century cannot be understood in purely European terms. The abolitionist movement was Atlantic in scope, linking British Quakers, African-American activists, Haitian revolutionaries, and Brazilian abolitionists in a common struggle against an institution that connected the Americas, Europe, and Africa in systems of exploitation and resistance. Nationalism spread from European capitals to Asian cities where intellectuals debated how traditional societies could become modern nations capable of resisting European imperialism. Socialism provided the intellectual framework for revolutionary movements in Asia, Africa, and Latin America that would reshape the world in the twentieth century.

The women, workers, enslaved people, colonized peoples, and minorities who appear in this chapter — often struggling against ideologies that claimed to speak for all humanity while leaving them out — are not peripheral figures in the political history of the nineteenth century. They are central to it. Their struggles to be included in the promises of liberalism, nationalism, and socialism drove the political development of these ideologies as much as the arguments of great thinkers did. Frederick Douglass demanding that the principles of the Declaration of Independence be applied to enslaved Americans, Elizabeth Cady Stanton insisting that the rights of man were also the rights of woman, Sojourner Truth demanding that Black women's lives be counted in the feminist calculus — these voices were not merely adding footnotes to a story already written. They were arguing about the very terms of the story itself.

By the end of the nineteenth century, the ideological conflicts examined in this chapter had produced a world significantly different from the one in which they had begun — but the work was far from finished. Constitutional governments, freedom of the press, formal legal equality, the end of Atlantic slavery, early social protections for workers, significant legal reforms for women: these were real achievements, built through decades of organized struggle. They were also incomplete achievements — achieved for some groups and not others, limited by the class interests, racial assumptions, and gender biases of those who made them. The work of expanding those achievements and addressing those limits would define the political struggles of the twentieth century, as the world moved from the age of ideology into the age of total war, revolution, and the still-contested global promise of human rights. That story begins in Chapter 46.

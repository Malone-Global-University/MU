Part VII — The Early Modern World, 1450–1750

Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal Empires

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"In the spring of 1453, a young sultan named Mehmed II positioned his artillery around the ancient walls of Constantinople — and the era of the great Islamic gunpowder empires had arrived."

In the spring of 1453, a young sultan named Mehmed II positioned his artillery around the ancient walls of Constantinople. The city had stood for more than a thousand years as the eastern capital of the Roman Empire, later the Byzantine Empire, and it remained one of the great symbols of Christian civilization in the Mediterranean world. Mehmed's Ottoman army possessed something that previous besiegers had lacked: massive bronze cannon capable of battering the legendary Theodosian Walls, which had repelled invaders for centuries. When the walls finally cracked and the Ottoman forces poured through, the world took notice. A new kind of empire had arrived — one that combined military technology, administrative sophistication, and Islamic imperial ambition into a state of formidable power. Mehmed renamed the city Istanbul and made it his capital, transforming a fallen Christian metropolis into the jewel of an expanding Islamic empire. The event reverberated from Morocco to India, signaling that the era of the great Islamic gunpowder empires had arrived.

The century and a half between approximately 1450 and 1600 witnessed the rise of three of the most powerful empires in world history, all rooted in Islamic civilization, all wielding unprecedented military force, and all governing enormous and diverse populations across a vast arc of territory stretching from the Balkans and North Africa through Southwest Asia, Persia, and deep into the Indian subcontinent. These were the Ottoman Empire, centered on Anatolia and the eastern Mediterranean; the Safavid Empire, ruling over Persia and much of what is today Iran and Iraq; and the Mughal Empire, which would come to govern most of South Asia. Historians often group these three states under the term "gunpowder empires," recognizing that the new military technology of firearms, cannon, and organized infantry gave each of them a decisive edge over the older cavalry-based armies of their rivals and predecessors.

Yet the term "gunpowder empires" captures only part of their historical significance. Each of these states was not merely a military machine but a complex civilization — a government, a legal system, a cultural world, a religious community, and an economic network. The Ottomans built one of the most sophisticated administrative systems in premodern history, organizing their vast territories through an elaborate hierarchy of governors, tax collectors, judges, and soldiers. The Safavids transformed Persia by making Twelver Shi'a Islam the official religion of the state, creating a new fusion of Persian cultural identity and Shi'a religious authority that still shapes Iran today. The Mughals governed the most populous and economically productive region of the early modern world, ruling over a Hindu majority population with a policy of religious pluralism that, under the great emperor Akbar, reached extraordinary sophistication.

This chapter examines these three empires in depth — their origins, their military and administrative structures, their religious policies, their cultural achievements, and their economic systems. It approaches them not simply as political entities but as human civilizations shaped by geography, history, religion, and the interactions between rulers and the hundreds of millions of people they governed. The chapter asks students to think comparatively: What did these three empires share? How did they differ? What does comparing them reveal about the range of possibilities within Islamic imperial civilization? And how do they fit into the larger patterns of early modern world history, including the expanding global trade networks, the rise of European maritime empires, the spread of new crops and commodities, and the intensifying connections between regions that historians now call the early modern world system?

Students reading this chapter will encounter familiar landmarks — the Hagia Sophia converted into a mosque, the Taj Mahal rising above the Yamuna River, the great bazaars of Isfahan — but they will also encounter the lived experience of ordinary people: the peasants who paid taxes to Mughal revenue collectors, the Christian boys recruited into the Ottoman military through the devshirme system, the Sufi mystics who moved between empires carrying books and religious ideas, the Armenian merchants who traded silk between Safavid Persia and European markets, the Hindu scholars who translated Sanskrit texts at the Mughal court. History is made by all of these people, not only by sultans and shahs.

The chapter also engages with some of the central questions of world history. How did gunpowder technology change the nature of political power in Eurasia? How did religious identity — Sunni Islam in the Ottoman Empire, Shi'a Islam in Safavid Persia, a complex mixture in Mughal India — shape imperial governance and interstate relations? How did the long-distance trade networks of the Indian Ocean and the overland Silk Road support these empires economically and culturally? How did these empires respond to the expanding presence of European commercial and military power in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries? And how did the legacies of the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires shape the modern Middle East, Central Asia, and South Asia?

Finally, students should appreciate that the era of the gunpowder empires was one of the most culturally productive periods in Islamic history. The courts of Istanbul, Isfahan, and Agra were among the greatest centers of art, architecture, literature, and intellectual life in the early modern world. Ottoman miniature painting, Safavid carpet weaving and tile work, Mughal garden design and manuscript illumination — all of these represent achievements of the highest order, produced by artists and craftspeople whose names deserve to be remembered alongside the sultans and emperors who patronized them. This chapter invites students to see the early modern Islamic world not as a backdrop to European expansion but as a set of vital, creative, and powerful civilizations in their own right.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain why historians use the term "gunpowder empires" and evaluate its usefulness and limitations as a framework for understanding the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal states.
  2. Trace the origins and territorial expansion of each of the three empires, identifying the key military, political, and geographic factors that enabled their rise to power.
  3. Compare the administrative systems of the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires, analyzing how each empire governed large and diverse populations across challenging geographic environments.
  4. Analyze the role of religious policy in each empire, distinguishing between the Ottoman model of Sunni Islam and dhimmi relations, the Safavid transformation of Persia through Shi'a state identity, and the Mughal experiment with religious pluralism under Akbar.
  5. Describe the art, architecture, and court culture of each empire, connecting cultural production to imperial patronage, religious identity, and cross-cultural exchange.
  6. Evaluate the economic foundations of the three empires, explaining the relationship between agrarian taxation, long-distance trade, and imperial finance.
  7. Identify major historical actors — rulers, officials, scholars, merchants, and ordinary people — within each empire and assess their historical significance.
  8. Interpret primary sources from the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal contexts, applying principles of source analysis including authorship, audience, purpose, and historical context.
  9. Assess the significance of the Ottoman-Safavid conflict in shaping both the political geography of Southwest Asia and the sectarian division between Sunni and Shi'a Islam.
  10. Contextualize the gunpowder empires within the broader patterns of early modern world history, including the expansion of global trade networks, the intensification of maritime connections, and the growing presence of European commercial empires.
  11. Synthesize information about all three empires to construct a comparative argument about similarities, differences, and historical significance.
  12. Connect the legacies of the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires to the modern political, religious, and cultural landscapes of the Middle East, Iran, and South Asia.

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  6. How did the three empires fit into the expanding global trade networks of the early modern period? Were they beneficiaries, participants, or victims of the growing integration of the world economy?
  7. What were the key sources of conflict and cooperation between the three empires? How did dynastic rivalry, religious difference, and territorial competition shape relations among them?

Historical Context

The World Before the Gunpowder Empires

To understand the rise of the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, students must situate these states within a longer historical context that stretches back through several centuries of Islamic history and political transformation. The world of Islamic civilization in 1400 was recovering from catastrophe. The Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century had devastated much of the Islamic heartland. In 1258, the Mongol armies of Hülegü Khan sacked Baghdad, executing the Abbasid caliph and destroying the city that had been the political and cultural capital of Sunni Islam for five centuries. The Black Death, which swept across Eurasia in the mid-fourteenth century, compounded the devastation, killing between one-quarter and one-half of the population in many regions from China to Western Europe. Then, in the early fifteenth century, the Turco-Mongol conqueror Timur (Tamerlane) launched a new wave of military campaigns across Southwest and Central Asia, defeating the early Ottoman Empire at the Battle of Ankara in 1402 and temporarily fragmenting the political landscape of the region.

Yet even amidst these catastrophes, Islamic civilization demonstrated remarkable resilience. The Mongol rulers of Persia converted to Islam within a generation, becoming patrons of Persian literature and art. Cities like Samarkand and Herat became brilliant centers of learning and culture under the Timurid dynasty in the fifteenth century. Trade along the overland Silk Road and the maritime routes of the Indian Ocean continued to connect the Islamic world to East Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Mediterranean. The Ottoman state, which had survived its defeat by Timur and reassembled its power in the early fifteenth century, resumed its expansion into southeastern Europe and Anatolia. And in Central Asia, the descendants of Timur preserved a tradition of sophisticated court culture that would eventually travel with the Mughal founder Babur to India.

Islamic Political Thought and Imperial Legitimacy

The three empires of the early modern period all operated within the framework of Islamic political thought, though they interpreted and applied that framework in importantly different ways. In classical Islamic political theory, legitimate rule required that the ruler govern in accordance with the Sharia (Islamic law), protect the Muslim community, maintain justice, and defend the frontiers of the Islamic world against external enemies. The ruler might hold the title of sultan (meaning "power" or "authority"), caliph (meaning "successor" to the Prophet Muhammad), or shah (a Persian royal title), depending on his background and the traditions of his region.

The Ottoman sultans initially ruled as Muslim military leaders without claiming the title of caliph, but after Selim I conquered Egypt in 1517 and ended the Abbasid caliphate in Cairo, later traditions claimed that the last Abbasid caliph had transferred the title to the Ottomans — a claim the Ottomans began to assert more forcefully from the eighteenth century onward. The Safavid rulers used the title of Shah and grounded their legitimacy in Shi'a religious authority, claiming descent from the Imam Ali and eventually from the Prophet Muhammad himself. The Mughal emperors used the title of Padshah (great king) and grounded their legitimacy in a combination of Timurid and Mongol dynastic prestige, Islamic ruling traditions, and, under Akbar, a more universalist divine authority.

The Turkish and Mongol Background

All three dynasties had deep roots in the Turkish and Mongol military traditions of Central Asia. The Ottoman dynasty was of Turkic origin, tracing its lineage to a small principality in northwestern Anatolia that expanded in the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries during the fragmentation of the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum. The Safavid dynasty began as a Sufi religious order of mixed Iranian and Turkic background in Azerbaijan, but it came to power through the mobilization of Turkic tribal warriors called the Qizilbash ("Red Heads" for their distinctive red caps). The Mughal dynasty was founded by Babur, a descendant of both Timur on his father's side and Genghis Khan on his mother's side, making the Mughals simultaneously inheritors of the Persian-Islamic cultural world of the Timurids and the Turco-Mongol military tradition of the steppes.

This Central Asian heritage had important practical consequences. All three empires inherited military tactics developed on the steppes — cavalry warfare, the use of composite bows, the organization of armies into flexible units capable of rapid movement. But all three also adopted the gunpowder weapons technology that was transforming warfare across Eurasia in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, combining the mobility of their steppe military traditions with the firepower of cannon and firearms. This combination proved devastating to opponents who had not yet mastered the new technology.

Regional Orientation

The Ottoman World: Between Three Continents

The Ottoman Empire at its height in the late sixteenth century stretched across an enormous arc of territory connecting three continents. Its core territories lay in Anatolia (modern Turkey) and the Balkans, but it also encompassed Egypt, the Levant, Arabia (including the holy cities of Mecca and Medina), Iraq, and the coastlines of North Africa as far west as Algeria. The empire controlled the Bosphorus and Dardanelles straits, which connected the Black Sea to the Mediterranean, giving it a stranglehold on maritime commerce in the eastern Mediterranean. The geographic diversity of the Ottoman Empire was enormous — from the Christian-majority Balkans to the arid peninsula of Arabia, from the fertile Nile Valley of Egypt to the ancient irrigation agriculture of Mesopotamia.

Safavid Persia: The Iranian Plateau

The Safavid Empire was geographically more compact than the Ottoman or Mughal empires, centered on the Iranian plateau and its surrounding mountain systems. The plateau is high, largely arid, and ringed by mountain ranges including the Zagros to the west and the Alborz to the north. Rainfall is limited, and agriculture depends on sophisticated irrigation systems including the ancient qanat technology — underground channels that bring water from mountain aquifers to the dry lowlands. The major cities of Safavid Persia — Isfahan, Tabriz, Qazvin, Shiraz, Mashhad — were oases of urban life in a largely arid landscape. Persia's geographic position made it a crucial node in the overland trade routes connecting the Mediterranean and the Ottoman Empire to the west with Central Asia, India, and China to the east.

Mughal India: The Subcontinent

The Mughal Empire occupied the Indian subcontinent, one of the most geographically varied regions on earth. The empire's core territories lay in the Indo-Gangetic Plain, the broad, flat, enormously fertile plain formed by the rivers draining from the Himalaya and Hindu Kush mountains. This plain — stretching from the Punjab in the northwest through Delhi to the Bengal delta in the east — was among the most agriculturally productive and densely populated regions in the world. Beyond the plain, the subcontinent diversified into a remarkable range of environments: the Rajput desert kingdoms of the northwest, the forested highlands of central India, the Deccan plateau of southern India, the tropical coastal zones of the western and eastern coasts, and the Bengal delta with its extraordinary agricultural productivity and access to the Bay of Bengal. The diversity of environments meant that the Mughal Empire governed populations with enormously varied ecologies, economies, languages, and cultures.

Section 34.1

Gunpowder Empires and Early Modern Power

The term "gunpowder empires" was coined by the historian Marshall Hodgson in his monumental work The Venture of Islam (1974) and was further developed by William McNeill. The concept captures an important reality of early modern Eurasian history: the introduction of gunpowder weapons technology — firearms, cannon, and organized infantry equipped with muskets — fundamentally changed the balance of military power across the continent. States that mastered this technology could project force on a scale and with a precision that cavalry-based armies could not match. The great steppe confederacies that had threatened and often dominated settled agricultural civilizations for centuries faced a critical disadvantage against cannon-equipped armies. Walls and fortifications that had protected cities for centuries crumbled before large siege artillery. The result was a decisive tipping of the balance of military power toward states with the financial resources, technical knowledge, and organizational capacity to deploy gunpowder weapons systematically.

The Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires all emerged in this context of military-technological transformation and all successfully incorporated firearms and cannon into their military systems. The Ottoman use of cannon at the siege of Constantinople in 1453 is the most famous example, but the transformation was broader. The Ottoman infantry corps, the Janissaries, became proficient with firearms earlier than most comparable forces in Eurasia, giving the Ottomans a decisive edge in pitched battles against cavalry-dominant opponents. Babur, founder of the Mughal Empire, famously deployed cannon and matchlock muskets at the First Battle of Panipat in 1526, routing the much larger army of the last Delhi sultan with the psychological shock of organized firepower. The Safavids, initially disadvantaged in artillery relative to the Ottomans — a factor in their defeat at the Battle of Chaldiran in 1514 — eventually developed their own cannon and firearms capacity, partly by incorporating renegade European and Ottoman artisans.

Yet historians have increasingly recognized the limitations of the "gunpowder empire" framework. Several important qualifications are necessary. First, these empires were not purely military machines — they were sophisticated administrative states with complex legal systems, bureaucratic hierarchies, economic networks, and cultural institutions that owed as much to the traditions of Islamic governance, Persian administrative culture, and Central Asian political tradition as to military technology. Second, many other states across Eurasia and Africa also adopted gunpowder technology in the same period without becoming "gunpowder empires" in the same sense — the technology was widespread, and access to it alone does not explain the rise of the three great Islamic empires. Third, the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires differed significantly from each other in their origins, structures, and characters, and lumping them together too hastily can obscure important differences.

What the three empires did share, beyond military technology, was a common Islamic cultural heritage, a Central Asian Turco-Mongol military tradition, access to the agricultural wealth of densely populated Eurasian societies, and the challenge of governing enormous, ethnically and religiously diverse populations. Each developed distinctive approaches to these common challenges, and comparing those approaches reveals much about the range of possibilities within Islamic imperial civilization.

The early modern period (roughly 1450–1700) in which these empires flourished was also a period of dramatic transformation in world-historical patterns. European maritime expansion was creating new sea routes connecting Europe directly to sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and the Americas, bypassing the overland trade routes through Southwest Asia that had historically enriched Islamic middlemen. The silver from the Americas was flooding into the global economy, creating inflationary pressures that would eventually strain the agrarian tax systems on which all three empires depended. All of these changes affected the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires in complex ways, and students should be attentive to the interaction between these empires and the broader patterns of early modern world history.

Section 34.2

The Ottoman Empire and Mediterranean Rule

The Ottoman state began not as a great empire but as one of many small Turkish principalities (beyliks) that emerged in Anatolia during the late thirteenth century, following the collapse of the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum under Mongol pressure. The principality was founded by a leader named Osman (or Uthman, from which the Ottoman name derives) around 1299, in the northwestern corner of Anatolia bordering the weakened Byzantine Empire. Osman and his successors possessed several advantages: a location on the frontier between Islamic and Byzantine territory that attracted Muslim warriors seeking religious merit through holy war (ghaza), an ability to incorporate defeated enemies and allied groups into their expanding state rather than simply destroying them, and a consistent pattern of capable and long-lived rulers in the fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries.

The early Ottoman expansion was primarily westward and northward, into southeastern Europe. By the mid-fourteenth century, the Ottomans had crossed into Europe and established a capital at Edirne (Adrianople) in Thrace. By the late fourteenth century, they had subjugated much of the Balkans, making the Christian kingdoms of Bulgaria, Serbia, and others into vassal states paying tribute and providing military service to the Ottoman sultan. The Serbian nobles were defeated at the Battle of Kosovo in 1389 — a battle that became central to Serbian national memory. The Ottoman advance was temporarily halted by Timur's invasion and the defeat at Ankara in 1402, but after recovering from this setback, the empire resumed its expansion with new energy.

The conquest of Constantinople in 1453 under Mehmed II was more than a military triumph — it was a signal to the Islamic world that the Ottomans had achieved something that generations of Muslim leaders had dreamed of since the early Islamic conquests. The city was one of the most fortified in the world, protected by a double ring of walls and the natural defenses of the Bosphorus. Mehmed succeeded through a combination of strategic genius, massive resources, and the decisive use of large-caliber bronze cannon cast by the Hungarian engineer Urban, who had offered his services first to the Byzantine emperor (who could not pay) and then to the Ottoman sultan (who could). Within days of the breach, the city had fallen.

Mehmed immediately set about transforming Constantinople into Istanbul, an imperial capital befitting the world's greatest power. He repopulated the depopulated city by forcing the migration of populations from newly conquered territories. He ordered the construction of the Topkapi Palace — a vast complex of courts, halls, gardens, and private quarters that would serve as the center of Ottoman imperial government for the next four centuries. He converted the great Byzantine church of Hagia Sophia into a mosque, adding minarets to its exterior while retaining its extraordinary interior dome. He also established the Grand Bazaar, which would grow into one of the world's largest covered markets, at the heart of the city's commercial life. Istanbul became a city of extraordinary cosmopolitan vitality, home to Muslims, Greeks, Armenians, Jews, and peoples from across the Ottoman world.

After Mehmed, the empire continued to expand in all directions. Selim I (r. 1512–1520) turned east and south, defeating the Safavids at the Battle of Chaldiran in 1514 and then sweeping through the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt and Syria in 1516–1517. These conquests were transformative. Egypt was the most economically productive territory in the Arab world, and its revenues dramatically strengthened the Ottoman imperial treasury. Control of the Levant and Arabia gave the Ottomans guardianship of the holy cities of Mecca and Medina — a source of immense prestige in the Islamic world.

Suleiman I (r. 1520–1566) brought the empire to its greatest extent. Known in the Islamic world as "the Lawgiver" (Kanuni) for his extensive codification of Ottoman law, and in the European world as "the Magnificent" for the splendor of his court and the scale of his military campaigns, Suleiman was one of the most formidable rulers of the early modern period. He besieged and captured the island of Rhodes from the Knights Hospitaller in 1522. He defeated the Kingdom of Hungary at the Battle of Mohács in 1526 and pushed into central Europe, reaching as far as Vienna in 1529. He battled the Safavids repeatedly over control of Iraq, finally capturing Baghdad in 1534. He extended Ottoman naval power across the eastern and central Mediterranean, North Africa, and even into the Indian Ocean, where Ottoman fleets contested Portuguese control of the spice trade routes.

Under Suleiman, the Ottoman Empire was genuinely one of the two most powerful states in the world — the other being the Habsburg Empire of Spain and the Holy Roman Empire under Charles V. European powers that felt threatened by the Habsburgs often sought alliances with the Ottomans — most famously the French monarchy under Francis I, who concluded a commercial and political agreement with Suleiman in 1536 that gave French merchants special trading privileges in Ottoman territories (known as "capitulations"). The idea of a Christian-Muslim alliance against a common Christian enemy scandalized many in Europe but demonstrated the sophistication of Ottoman diplomacy and the realpolitik of early modern international relations.

Section 34.3

Istanbul, Administration, and Military Organization

The Ottoman administrative system was one of the most sophisticated in the early modern world, combining the traditions of Byzantine imperial governance, Persian administrative culture, Islamic legal institutions, and Ottoman innovations into a system capable of managing a vast, diverse, and geographically challenging empire. Understanding this system requires looking at its major components: the imperial court, the central bureaucracy, the provincial administration, the military, and the legal system.

The Imperial Court and the Palace

The center of Ottoman government was the imperial palace — first the Topkapi Palace in Istanbul, later supplemented by other palaces. The palace was divided into two spheres: the outer (birún) and the inner (enderún). The outer court dealt with the formal business of government — receiving ambassadors, conducting councils of state, managing the imperial treasury and arsenals. The inner court was the private world of the sultan, including his personal servants, pages being trained for imperial service, and the harem where his wives, concubines, female relatives, and their attendants lived.

The most important government council was the Imperial Council (Divan-i Hümayun), which met four times a week to discuss affairs of state, hear petitions, and make administrative and judicial decisions. The Grand Vizier (Sadr-i Azam) presided over the Divan in the sultan's name and was the effective head of government. The Grand Vizier was not chosen from the dynasty — he was typically a man who had risen through the devshirme system or other routes of imperial service, and he could be dismissed and even executed at the sultan's will. This meant that the sultans could delegate enormous executive power to their Grand Viziers without creating independent centers of dynastic power.

Provincial Administration

The Ottoman Empire was divided into provinces (eyalets), each governed by a governor-general (beylerbey). The provinces were subdivided into smaller districts (sanjaks) governed by sanjak-beys. These provincial governors were imperial appointees who served at the sultan's pleasure, providing military forces and revenue in exchange for their positions. The system prevented the emergence of independent regional dynasties by rotating governors frequently and keeping ultimate authority firmly in Istanbul.

A crucial element of the Ottoman provincial system was the timar system. Military commanders (sipahis) were assigned the revenues of a specific piece of land in exchange for maintaining a specified number of cavalry soldiers for the imperial army. This system financed the cavalry component of the Ottoman military without requiring the central government to pay cash salaries. However, over time the timar system proved difficult to sustain as the costs of maintaining a modern gunpowder army increased and as the Ottoman government moved toward cash-based military financing.

The Devshirme and the Janissaries

One of the most distinctive features of the Ottoman administrative system was the devshirme — a periodic levy of Christian boys from the Balkan provinces. Boys selected through the devshirme were taken from their families, converted to Islam, and trained in Ottoman language, culture, and either military service or palace administration, depending on their abilities. The ablest boys were trained in the palace school for careers as imperial administrators — those who completed this training might rise to the highest positions in the imperial government, including Grand Vizier. The majority of devshirme boys were trained for the Janissary corps.

The Janissaries (from the Turkish Yeniçeri, "new soldiers") were organized as a professional standing army — a remarkable innovation in an era when most armies were assembled for campaigns and then disbanded. They were equipped with firearms and trained to maneuver and fight in coordinated formations. For most of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, they were among the most effective military forces in Eurasia. The devshirme system seems paradoxical — but devshirme boys who succeeded in imperial service could rise to great wealth and power, and the empire gained loyal, capable servants whose advancement depended entirely on the imperial system.

Historical Vocabulary
Devshirme

The word devshirme comes from the Ottoman Turkish verb devşirmek, meaning "to collect" or "to gather." The devshirme was a periodic levy of Christian boys from the Ottoman Empire's Balkan provinces, typically conducted every few years by imperial officials. Boys between approximately eight and eighteen years of age were selected based on physical fitness, intelligence, and suitability for service. They were then converted to Islam, given new names, taught Ottoman Turkish, and educated in Islamic religious practice and Ottoman customs. The most capable were trained in the palace school for careers as administrators; the majority became Janissaries.

The devshirme raises complex historical questions. It was, in one sense, a form of forced conscription and family separation that imposed significant suffering on the families from which boys were taken. In another sense, it created a social mobility channel in a hierarchical society, allowing boys of peasant origin to rise to the highest offices of the empire on the basis of talent and service. Its historical significance lies in its role as one of the mechanisms by which the Ottoman state created a loyal, capable, and multi-ethnic administrative and military elite whose advancement depended entirely on the imperial system rather than on family or tribal connections.

The Millet System and Legal Order

The Ottoman Empire governed enormous numbers of non-Muslim subjects: Greek, Armenian, and other Eastern Orthodox Christians; Roman Catholics; Jews; Zoroastrians; and others. The Ottoman development of the dhimmi framework was the millet system — non-Muslim communities were organized as semi-autonomous religious units with their own recognized leaders and authority to govern internal affairs — religious practice, marriage, divorce, inheritance, and education — according to their own laws and customs. The Greek Orthodox community, the Armenian Apostolic community, and the Jewish communities all functioned under this arrangement, with their patriarchs and chief rabbis recognized by the Ottoman state as responsible for their communities' conduct.

The Ottoman legal system was dual in nature, reflecting the coexistence of Islamic law (Sharia) and Ottoman imperial law (Kanun). The Sharia was administered through a network of courts staffed by judges (qadis) trained in Islamic jurisprudence, and it governed matters of personal status for Muslims. The Kanun was a body of imperial legislation developed by successive sultans — most extensively by Suleiman the Lawgiver — that regulated matters of taxation, land tenure, military organization, and criminal justice that the Sharia did not directly address. The highest religious authority in the empire was the Sheikh ul-Islam (Grand Mufti of Istanbul), who had the authority to issue formal legal opinions (fatwas) on questions of Islamic law and was used to legitimize major policy decisions.

Section 34.4

Safavid Persia and Shi'a State Identity

The Safavid Empire began in a very different way from the Ottoman. The Ottomans were a dynasty of frontier warriors whose expansion was pragmatic, military, and administrative. The Safavids began as a Sufi religious brotherhood — the Safaviyya order, founded in the northwestern Persian town of Ardabil in the thirteenth century by Sheikh Safi al-Din, from whom the dynasty took its name. Over generations, the Safaviyya order evolved from a relatively orthodox Sunni brotherhood into an increasingly heterodox, Shi'a-inflected religious movement with fervent followers among the Turkic nomadic tribes of Azerbaijan and eastern Anatolia. The followers of the Safavid leaders — who came to be called the Qizilbash, or "Red Heads," for the distinctive red turbans they wore — combined religious devotion with fierce military commitment to their charismatic leaders.

In 1501, the fourteen-year-old Ismail I led his Qizilbash warriors to a decisive victory over the Aq Qoyunlu confederation that had previously dominated northwestern Persia. Ismail then did something extraordinary and historically consequential: he declared Twelver Shi'a Islam the state religion of his new Persian empire. This was not a minor policy choice — Persia at that time was predominantly Sunni, and the existing religious establishment was organized around Sunni institutions. Ismail's declaration set in motion a century-long transformation of Persian religious life, carried out through a combination of persuasion, royal patronage, and coercion, that would eventually make Iran the center of Twelver Shi'a Islam and create a religious identity that distinguishes Iran from its neighbors to this day.

Transforming Persia: The Imposition of Shi'a Islam

The transformation of Persia from a Sunni-majority to a Shi'a-majority country was not accomplished overnight or without resistance. Ismail's declaration was initially backed more by the force of the Qizilbash armies than by a strong existing Shi'a scholarly tradition within Persia. The Safavids had to import Shi'a scholars (ulama) from the Arab Shi'a heartlands — especially from what is today Lebanon and southern Iraq — to staff the courts, schools, and mosques of the new Shi'a establishment. Over the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, through sustained royal patronage, the endowment of religious institutions, the appointment of Shi'a scholars to important positions, and the suppression of Sunni practice, the Safavids succeeded in transforming Persian religious culture. Shi'a commemorations — especially the annual mourning rituals of Ashura — became central features of public religious life.

The Ottoman-Safavid Conflict

The establishment of a Shi'a Persian state created an immediate and lasting conflict with the Sunni Ottoman Empire. The two empires were neighbors; they competed for control of the same frontier territories (especially Iraq and the Caucasus region); and they had deep religious differences that each used to delegitimize the other. The Ottomans branded the Safavids as heretics, and the Safavids questioned the Ottomans' Islamic credentials. The decisive Battle of Chaldiran (1514) demonstrated the military reality: the Ottoman army, equipped with cannon and Janissary muskets, routed the Safavid force, which relied primarily on cavalry without comparable firepower. The battle established a rough frontier between the two empires that persisted, with variations, for centuries — codified ultimately in the Treaty of Zuhab (1639), which established a boundary roughly corresponding to the modern border between Turkey, Iraq, and Iran.

Shah Abbas I and the Height of Safavid Power

The Safavid Empire reached its apogee under Shah Abbas I (r. 1587–1629). Abbas inherited a state that was militarily threatened by both the Ottomans (who had just seized much of western Persia) and the Uzbek nomads pressing on the empire's northeastern frontier. He responded with a comprehensive program of military, administrative, and economic reform. Militarily, Abbas reduced the power of the Qizilbash by creating a new standing army consisting of enslaved Georgians, Armenians, and Circassians (ghulam) who owed their position entirely to the shah's favor. He also invested heavily in artillery and firearms, employing English adventurers (the Sherley brothers) to help train and equip his new forces. Administratively, Abbas moved the imperial capital from Qazvin to Isfahan, where he undertook a massive building program that transformed Isfahan into one of the most beautiful cities in the early modern world. Economically, Abbas reorganized the silk trade by bringing it under direct royal control and establishing trading relationships with European merchants, bypassing Ottoman intermediaries.

Section 34.5

Mughal India and Imperial Diversity

The Mughal Empire was founded in very different circumstances from either the Ottoman or the Safavid state. Its founder, Zahir ud-Din Muhammad Babur, was a prince of the Timurid dynasty who had grown up in the sophisticated court culture of Samarkand and Ferghana in Central Asia. Babur was a warrior of extraordinary energy and skill, a passionate lover of gardens and poetry, and the author of one of the great autobiographies of the early modern world — the Baburnama, written in Chagatai Turkish. He had twice captured and lost Samarkand and had spent years leading a band of followers across the mountain kingdoms of Afghanistan before turning his eyes toward India.

India in the early sixteenth century was politically fragmented. The Delhi Sultanate had long since fragmented into regional sultanates. The most recent ruler of Delhi, Ibrahim Lodi, was an Afghan sultan who faced challenges to his authority from his own nobles. When one of those discontented nobles invited Babur to invade, Babur seized the opportunity. At the First Battle of Panipat in 1526, Babur deployed his cannon and matchlock muskets with devastating effect against Ibrahim Lodi's much larger army. Ibrahim died in the battle, and Babur marched into Delhi.

Babur's son Humayun proved to be a less effective military commander; in 1540, he was expelled from India entirely by the Afghan chieftain Sher Shah Suri, who temporarily displaced the Mughals. Humayun spent the years 1543–1545 at the Safavid court of Shah Tahmasp in Tabriz — a period of historical significance beyond simple military defeat. The Persian aesthetic traditions of Safavid painting influenced the development of Mughal court painting in the next generation. When Humayun returned to India in 1555 and reconquered the throne, he brought with him Safavid-trained painters and Persian cultural influences that would shape Mughal art and architecture for generations.

The Scale of Mughal India

The Mughal Empire at its height under Aurangzeb encompassed approximately 3.2 million square kilometers — roughly the size of Western Europe — and contained somewhere between 100 and 150 million people. Historians estimate that in 1700, the Mughal economy (already in decline) still accounted for approximately 24% of world GDP, roughly comparable to Western Europe as a whole. The population of Mughal India was extraordinarily diverse. The overwhelming majority — perhaps 75–80% — were Hindus, following a wide variety of religious traditions and organized into a complex social hierarchy of castes. There were also significant populations of Muslims (including the Mughal ruling elite but also millions of converts), Sikhs (whose religion had emerged in the Punjab in the early sixteenth century), Jains, Zoroastrians, Christians, and smaller communities of other religious traditions. Governing this diversity — maintaining order, collecting revenue, adjudicating disputes, projecting imperial prestige, and defending against external enemies — required a sophisticated administrative system and a degree of flexibility in religious and cultural policy that the doctrinaire application of Islamic law alone could not provide.

Section 34.6

Akbar, Religious Policy, and Governance

Akbar (r. 1556–1605) is one of the most remarkable rulers in world history. Coming to the throne as a teenager in 1556, he would reign for nearly fifty years, during which he transformed the Mughal Empire from a relatively precarious conquest state into the most powerful and sophisticated polity in Asia. He accomplished this through military expansion, administrative reorganization, and a religious policy of remarkable breadth and tolerance.

The Mansabdari System

Akbar's greatest administrative innovation was the mansabdari system. Before Akbar, the Mughal Empire relied on a combination of loyal Afghan and Timurid warriors for its military and administrative needs — a base too narrow for governing the subcontinent. Akbar reorganized imperial service by creating a unified ranking system — the mansab — that applied to all military commanders and civil officials. Every mansabdar (rank-holder) was assigned a numerical rank expressed as two numbers: zat (personal rank, determining salary) and sawar (the number of cavalry the mansabdar was required to maintain). Mansabs ranged from ten at the bottom to five thousand or more at the top.

The mansabdari system was open to men of all religious and ethnic backgrounds: Mughals, Afghans, Rajputs, Deccanis, Persians, Uzbeks, and Indian Muslims could all hold mansabs. Akbar deliberately recruited Rajput warriors into the imperial service, both as commanders and as nobles with close ties to the imperial court. He cemented these alliances through marriage — including his marriage to Jodha Bai of Amber (also known as Mariam-uz-Zamani), whose son would become the emperor Jahangir. The integration of Rajput nobles into the Mughal imperial system transformed the Mughal state from an essentially Muslim imperial structure governing a Hindu-majority population into something more genuinely representative of Indian diversity at the elite level.

Historical Vocabulary
Mansab

A mansab (from the Arabic for "office" or "rank") was a numerical rank in the Mughal imperial service system introduced by Emperor Akbar. Every military commander and civil official in the Mughal system held a mansab expressed as two numbers: zat (personal rank, determining the holder's salary and status) and sawar (the number of cavalry horsemen the holder was required to maintain). A mansabdar of 500 zat and 200 sawar, for example, received a salary appropriate to the 500 rank and was required to maintain 200 cavalry troopers ready for imperial service.

The mansabdari system was significant for several reasons. It created a unified and standardized hierarchy for all imperial servants regardless of ethnic or religious background. It linked salary to service rather than to hereditary landholding. And it organized the empire's military force not as a permanent standing army but as a network of obligation distributed among thousands of nobles and commanders. Its practical limitation was that the salaries of higher mansabdars were often paid through assignments on land revenue (jagirs) rather than cash, creating a constant problem of ensuring that jagirs were available to pay all assignments.

Religious Policy: From Tolerance to Din-i-Ilahi

Akbar's religious policy evolved over the course of his reign. In the 1560s, he took several significant steps away from strictly Islamic governance: in 1562 he prohibited the enslaving of war captives and their families; in 1563 he abolished the pilgrimage tax on non-Muslims traveling to Hindu holy sites; in 1564 he abolished the jizya (the poll tax on non-Muslims), one of the most symbolically important markers of the dhimmi status. He also prohibited cow slaughter in territories where Hindu sentiment ran high and showed personal respect for Hindu festivals and religious practices.

In the 1570s, Akbar constructed at his new capital Fatehpur Sikri a building called the Ibadat Khana (House of Worship), where he hosted religious debates between scholars of different traditions. Initially these debates were among Muslim scholars (Sunni and Shi'a, orthodox and Sufi), but Akbar expanded them to include Hindus, Jains, and eventually the Portuguese Jesuits. By the 1580s, Akbar had developed what his court chronicler Abu'l-Fazl called the Din-i-Ilahi — the "Divine Faith." This was not a mass religion but a form of personal spiritual discipline practiced by a small inner circle of Akbar's most trusted courtiers. It drew elements from multiple traditions: the Islamic emphasis on monotheism, Hindu concepts of solar worship, Zoroastrian reverence for light and fire, Sufi mystical ideas of spiritual development. Initiates swore personal loyalty to Akbar as a spiritual guide.

The Din-i-Ilahi was controversial in its own time and has been controversial among historians ever since. Conservative Muslim scholars at Akbar's court — led by the scholar Badauni, who wrote a critical history of Akbar's reign in secret — condemned it as apostasy or heresy. Most historians today see it as a complex mixture of sincere spiritual seeking and political strategy, characteristic of Akbar's extraordinarily multifaceted personality. What seems clear is that Akbar's religious policy was functionally a policy of managing imperial diversity — creating a framework within which people of different religions could serve the empire and find a place within its social and cultural world.

Section 34.7

Art, Architecture, and Court Culture

The courts of the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires were among the greatest centers of artistic and architectural production in the early modern world. Royal patronage was a crucial mechanism of imperial legitimacy — rulers demonstrated their power, their piety, and their cultural sophistication through the buildings they constructed, the manuscripts they commissioned, the gardens they created, and the craftspeople they employed. The artistic traditions of the three courts were deeply interrelated, sharing common roots in the Persian cultural tradition while developing in distinctive regional directions.

Ottoman Art and Architecture

Ottoman architecture reached its apogee in the work of the great architect Sinan (c. 1489–1588), who served as chief imperial architect under Suleiman the Magnificent and his two successors, designing more than 370 structures including mosques, madrasas, caravanserais, bridges, and palaces. Sinan's masterpiece, by his own account, was the Selimiye Mosque in Edirne (completed 1575), built for Sultan Selim II, with a central dome larger than that of Hagia Sophia supported by eight massive pillars and surrounded by four slender minarets. Sinan's earlier work includes the Süleymaniye Mosque complex in Istanbul (completed 1558), which organized around the mosque itself an entire urban institution — a madrasa, a hospital, a caravanserai, a bath house, and a market — demonstrating how imperial architecture served social as well as religious and symbolic purposes.

Ottoman decorative arts were equally distinguished. Iznik pottery — produced in the city of Iznik in northwestern Anatolia during the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries — featured brilliantly colored tiles decorated with floral and geometric patterns in vibrant shades of red, blue, green, and white, used to decorate the interiors of mosques, palaces, and elite residences and exported as luxury goods across the Mediterranean world.

Safavid Art and Architecture

Safavid art and architecture developed in distinctive directions shaped by Shi'a religious identity and the particular aesthetic preferences of the Safavid court. The supreme achievement of Safavid architecture is the ensemble of buildings around the Naqsh-e Jahan Square in Isfahan, created under Shah Abbas I. The Royal Mosque (Masjid-i Shah) features a vast dome covered in blue-and-yellow glazed tiles that shimmers in the sunlight, an entrance portal (pishtaq) decorated with complex geometric tilework and Quranic inscriptions, and an interior that plays brilliantly with light and scale. The Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque across the square is smaller but even more refined, its interior covered in an exquisite geometric pattern of cream-and-terracotta tiles creating an effect of intricate, meditative beauty.

Safavid painting reached its peak in the royal manuscript workshops, where court painters produced illuminated copies of the Persian literary classics — the Shahnameh (Book of Kings) of Ferdowsi, the poems of Hafiz and Rumi — with illustrations of extraordinary refinement. Persian carpet weaving also reached its artistic summit in the Safavid period, with court-sponsored workshops in Isfahan, Kashan, Tabriz, and other cities producing carpets of extraordinary technical virtuosity — gifts between rulers, markers of diplomatic relationships, and export commodities that circulated to the courts of Europe and the Ottoman Empire.

Mughal Art and Architecture

Mughal art and architecture synthesized Central Asian, Persian, Indian, and eventually European traditions into a distinctive style of exceptional richness. Humayun's years at the Safavid court introduced Persian painting traditions, and the two Persian master painters he brought back to India established the foundations of the Mughal court painting workshop. Under Akbar, the workshop expanded enormously, employing both Muslim and Hindu painters and producing a style that combined Persian compositional and technical traditions with Indian color sensibilities, interest in narrative, and representation of the natural world.

The Akbarnama (Book of Akbar) — a monumental illustrated history of Akbar's reign — is one of the supreme achievements of Mughal painting, featuring more than a hundred full-page miniatures depicting battles, royal audiences, construction projects, festivals, and hunting scenes with extraordinary vibrancy and detail. Mughal architecture reached its most ambitious works under Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb. Shah Jahan represents the architectural apex. His buildings — the Taj Mahal, the Red Fort in Delhi (Shahjahanabad), the Agra Fort's Pearl Mosque, the Jama Masjid — defined the Mughal style at its most refined: white marble, pietra dura inlay work (geometric and floral patterns made from semi-precious stones embedded in marble), symmetrical compositions, and an integration of architecture with garden design that created environments of extraordinary serenity and beauty.

The Taj Mahal (1632–1653), built by Shah Jahan as a mausoleum for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died in childbirth in 1631, represents not merely architectural achievement but an integrated vision of Islamic cosmology, garden design, and funerary commemoration. The building sits at the head of a formal garden (charbagh, or four-part garden) divided by water channels representing the rivers of paradise, approached through a great gateway of red sandstone. The building's decorations — Quranic inscriptions in black marble inlaid into white, elaborate floral patterns in pietra dura work — combine to create an effect of heavenly perfection: paradise itself, where Mumtaz Mahal resided in God's presence.

Section 34.8

Trade, Taxes, and Agrarian Society

All three of the gunpowder empires were primarily agrarian states — their economic foundations rested on the agricultural production of their vast peasant populations, collected through elaborate systems of land taxation. But all three also participated actively in long-distance trade, both as facilitators (by maintaining order on trade routes, building infrastructure, and levying tolls) and as consumers (importing luxury goods) and exporters (of silk, spices, textiles, and other commodities).

The Ottoman Economy

The Ottoman economy was diverse, combining the agricultural production of the great river valleys and Mediterranean coasts with extensive craft production in the cities — particularly textiles and metalwork — a sophisticated long-distance trade system, and significant revenues from tolls and taxes on trade passing through Ottoman-controlled territory. Agricultural tax collection in the Ottoman Empire was organized through the timar system and through tax farming (iltizam), by which the right to collect taxes in a given area was auctioned to the highest bidder, who then collected taxes from the peasants and remitted a fixed amount to the state, keeping the surplus as profit. The Ottoman state maintained a sophisticated approach to commerce and regulated guild organizations (loncas) that controlled craft production in the cities. European merchants — especially Venetians and Genoese, later Dutch and English — traded extensively in Ottoman territories through capitulation agreements.

The Safavid Silk Economy

The Safavid economy was more narrowly dependent on silk than the Ottoman. Persia had been a major producer and exporter of raw silk and silk textiles for centuries, and the silk trade was the primary source of the Safavid state's commercial revenue. Shah Abbas I's decision to bring the silk trade under royal monopoly control was economically significant. By selling directly to European merchants through New Julfa's Armenian merchant networks, the Safavids bypassed the Ottoman middlemen who had previously extracted large profits from silk moving westward through Ottoman territory. The English East India Company and the Dutch VOC both negotiated trading rights with the Safavids, and the volume of Persian silk reaching European markets through maritime routes grew significantly in the seventeenth century.

Historical Vocabulary
Sharia and Kanun

Sharia (Arabic: شريعة) means "the path" or "the way" and refers to the comprehensive body of Islamic law derived from the Quran, the Hadith (traditions of the Prophet Muhammad), scholarly consensus (ijma), and analogical reasoning (qiyas). In practice, the Sharia was interpreted and applied by trained religious scholars (ulama) working within one of four major legal schools (madhabs) of Sunni Islam or the Ja'fari school of Shi'a Islam. The Ottoman Empire primarily followed the Hanafi school; the Mughal Empire also used Hanafi jurisprudence; the Safavid Empire used the Ja'fari (Twelver Shi'a) school.

Kanun (from the Greek kanon, "rule") referred in Ottoman usage to the body of imperial legislation issued by sultans to address matters not covered by Sharia — taxation, land tenure, military organization, criminal procedure. The coexistence of Sharia and Kanun in the Ottoman legal system reflected a pragmatic accommodation to the complexity of governing a large, diverse empire through a combination of religious law and secular imperial legislation. Religious scholars sometimes argued that Kanun contradicted Sharia provisions, but in practice the dual system generally functioned as a workable compromise.

The Mughal Revenue System

The Mughal economy was the largest of the three, resting on the agricultural productivity of the Indian subcontinent's vast and fertile plains. The Mughal revenue system — systematized under Akbar by his brilliant finance minister Raja Todar Mal — was one of the most sophisticated agrarian tax systems in the early modern world. Todar Mal's system classified agricultural land by quality and productivity, surveyed the amount of land under cultivation, assessed revenue based on average prices over ten years, and collected tax in cash rather than kind wherever possible. The standard rate of the land tax (kharaj) was approximately one-third of the agricultural produce, though this varied by region and crop.

The Mughal revenue was collected through a system of zamindars — hereditary landholders who held rights to collect taxes from peasants in defined areas and remit a portion to the imperial government, keeping a share (usually about 10%) for themselves. India was also deeply integrated into the Indian Ocean trade network, producing major export commodities: cotton textiles (especially from Gujarat), indigo (from the Yamuna valley), sugar, pepper, and other spices. The port of Surat in Gujarat was the most important commercial gateway of the empire, handling much of the trade between Mughal India and the Persian Gulf, Red Sea, and beyond.

Section 34.9

Comparing Islamic Empires

Comparing the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires reveals both their shared characteristics and their important differences — and the comparison illuminates why historians think of them together while recognizing their distinctiveness.

Shared Characteristics

All three empires shared several fundamental characteristics. They were all rooted in Islamic religious and political traditions, even as they interpreted and applied those traditions differently. They all combined a Central Asian Turco-Mongol military heritage with Persian administrative and cultural traditions — using Persian as the language of high culture, bureaucracy, and diplomacy; organizing their courts along Persian models of royal patronage and aesthetic refinement; and drawing on the administrative traditions developed in the Persian-speaking world over centuries. They all faced the challenge of governing religiously and ethnically diverse populations across large territories, and all developed mechanisms for managing this diversity. They all relied primarily on agrarian taxation as their economic foundation and all participated actively in long-distance trade.

All three also experienced similar structural challenges as they matured. The devshirme (Ottoman), the ghulam system (Safavid), and the mansabdari system (Mughal) were all designed to create loyal military and administrative elites whose power rested on the ruler's favor rather than independent bases. But all three systems evolved in ways that created new power centers independent of the ruler. The Janissaries became a political force that could make or break sultans. The Qizilbash tribes retained significant autonomy until Abbas I's reforms. The Mughal mansabdars became increasingly hard to control as the empire expanded.

Key Differences

The most immediately striking difference is religious. The Ottoman Empire was Sunni Muslim, and while it tolerated religious minorities under the millet system, it maintained a fundamentally Sunni state identity. The Safavid Empire was Shi'a Muslim — specifically Twelver Shi'a — and this Shi'a identity was central to the empire's political legitimacy and cultural life. The Mughal Empire governed a Muslim ruling class over a Hindu, Jain, Sikh, and Buddhist majority population, creating a very different dynamic: the question was not how to manage minority non-Muslim communities within a Muslim society but how to govern a Muslim state over an overwhelmingly non-Muslim majority.

These religious differences had far-reaching consequences. The Ottoman-Safavid hostility was partly a genuine sectarian conflict — the two empires anathematized each other, competed for the allegiance of frontier Muslim populations, and used religious difference to mobilize their subjects. The Mughal problem was different: how could a Muslim dynasty maintain Islamic legitimacy while governing a population that did not share its religion? The range of answers to this question — from Akbar's pluralism to Aurangzeb's orthodoxy — reflected genuine differences in political philosophy as well as personal temperament.

Geographically, the empires differed significantly in their relationship to the sea. The Ottoman Empire was a major naval power in the Mediterranean and, for a time, the Indian Ocean. The Safavid Empire was primarily a land power with access to the Persian Gulf and the Caspian Sea but no significant navy. The Mughal Empire had a long coastline but also remained primarily a land power, relying on Indian and then European merchants to carry its exports to sea. Finally, the empires differed in the nature of their social complexity. The Ottoman Empire governed a zone where the Muslim population was gradually becoming the majority. The Safavid Empire governed a population being transformed from Sunni to Shi'a through sustained royal action. The Mughal Empire governed the most religiously and linguistically diverse society of the three, requiring the most sophisticated accommodation of diversity.

Section 34.10

Chapter Review and Empire Comparison Checkpoint

The Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires together dominated the political and cultural landscape of Southwest Asia, Central Asia, and South Asia for roughly two centuries (c. 1450–1700). Their combined territories contained perhaps 200–250 million people — a substantial fraction of the world's total population — and their economies were among the most productive in the world. Their cultural achievements — in architecture, painting, literature, music, and decorative arts — represent some of the highest expressions of human creativity in any era. Their administrative systems, legal traditions, and religious institutions shaped the lives of hundreds of millions of people and left legacies that continue to influence the modern world.

By the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, all three empires were experiencing significant internal strains. The Ottoman Empire faced military reverses against European powers (especially after the failed siege of Vienna in 1683), internal political instability as the Janissaries increasingly intervened in politics, and economic pressures from the flood of American silver and the competition of European trading companies. The Safavid Empire collapsed dramatically after the Afghan invasion of 1722. The Mughal Empire fragmented more gradually, with regional governors becoming increasingly independent after Aurangzeb's death in 1707, until the Persian invader Nadir Shah's sack of Delhi in 1739 demonstrated to the world that Mughal authority was a shadow of its former self.

These declines do not erase the achievements of the height of these empires — but they do raise important historical questions about sustainability, structural vulnerability, and the long-term dynamics of agrarian empires facing the new challenges of the early modern world.

Regional Lens

Safavid Isfahan — City as Imperial Statement

Under Shah Abbas I, Isfahan became one of the most deliberate urban projects in history — a city planned, built, and organized to embody the power, piety, and cultural ambition of the Safavid state. The decision to move the capital from Qazvin to Isfahan in 1598 was both strategic (Isfahan was deeper within the Persian heartland, less vulnerable to Ottoman attack) and visionary in its architectural ambition.

The centerpiece of the new Isfahan was the great Naqsh-e Jahan (Image of the World) Square, measuring approximately 510 meters long and 165 meters wide — one of the largest public squares in the world. On the south end stood the Royal Mosque (Masjid-i Shah), the primary Friday mosque of the empire. On the east stood the smaller Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque, built for private royal worship and named for Abbas's father-in-law, a revered Shi'a scholar. On the north, the entrance to the great Qaysariyya bazaar channeled the commercial life of the empire through the imperial square. On the west, the Ali Qapu Palace provided the place from which the shah could observe polo matches, military reviews, and religious processions in the square below.

The bazaar that opened from the north end of the Maydan was one of the most important trade centers in the world — a covered network of commercial streets extending for kilometers, organized by trade into separate sections for different commodities, interspersed with caravanserais, coffee houses, and small mosques. What made Isfahan extraordinary was the integration: the way the mosque, the palace, the market, and the open space of the square worked together to create an environment that was simultaneously sacred, commercial, spectacular, and habitable.

The Armenian Christians of New Julfa, a suburb of Isfahan established by Shah Abbas through the forced relocation of an Armenian community from the Araxes River region, added another dimension to the city's cosmopolitan character. Abbas valued the Armenians for their commercial networks and their skills in silk trading. New Julfa became a thriving community of perhaps thirty thousand people, with its own churches, schools, and commercial institutions, functioning as the commercial bridge between Safavid Persia and European markets. The Cathedral of All Saviors in New Julfa, built in 1655, features an interior where Armenian Orthodox iconographic traditions blend with Safavid decorative motifs — a visual symbol of the encounter between cultures in Shah Abbas's Isfahan.

Global Connection

The Indian Ocean Trade Network and the Gunpowder Empires

The Indian Ocean was the world's most important long-distance trade network in the early modern period, connecting the markets of East Africa, Arabia, Persia, India, Southeast Asia, and China through regular monsoon-driven sailing routes. All three of the gunpowder empires had important relationships with this network.

The Ottoman Empire's relationship with the Indian Ocean became especially important after Selim I's conquest of Egypt in 1517 gave the Ottomans control of the Red Sea route connecting the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean. Ottoman merchants and the Ottoman state took over from the Mamluks the lucrative role of intermediaries in the spice trade between Asia and Europe. The arrival of Portuguese maritime power in the Indian Ocean after 1498 challenged this system — the Portuguese attempted (with only partial success) to divert the spice trade around the Cape of Good Hope, bypassing the Ottoman middlemen. The Ottoman response included sending naval expeditions into the Indian Ocean in the 1530s and 1550s, contesting Portuguese control of key ports and trade routes.

The Safavid Empire was deeply integrated into the overland routes of the Old World trade system. Persian silk was the most valuable commodity moving through Safavid territory, exported both overland through Ottoman territories to the Mediterranean and, increasingly under Shah Abbas I, by sea through the Persian Gulf. The Safavid port of Bandar Abbas (which Abbas renamed after himself after capturing it from the Portuguese in 1622, with English East India Company assistance) connected Persia to the Indian Ocean maritime network.

The Mughal Empire was the most economically significant participant. India's cotton textiles were perhaps the most widely traded commodity in the early modern Indian Ocean world: Indian cloth was exported to Southeast Asia (exchanged for spices), to East Africa (exchanged for gold), to Arabia and Persia (exchanged for silver), and eventually to Europe. The growing presence of European trading companies in the Indian Ocean in the seventeenth century complicated and eventually transformed this system. The English East India Company established its first Indian factory (trading post) at Surat in 1608; the Dutch VOC established its presence somewhat earlier. These companies brought American silver into the Indian Ocean trade, providing the medium of exchange that lubricated commerce across the entire network.

Empire Study

The Ottoman Empire at Its Height

Territorial Extent: At Suleiman's death, the Ottoman Empire stretched from the borders of Habsburg Austria in the northwest to the Persian frontier in the east, from the Black Sea coast to the cataracts of the Nile in Egypt, from the coast of Algeria in the west to the shores of the Red Sea in the east. Major cities included Istanbul (population perhaps 500,000 — one of the largest cities in the world), Cairo, Aleppo, Baghdad, Jerusalem, and Algiers.

Military Organization: The Ottoman military combined the Janissary infantry corps (equipped with muskets and organized as a professional standing force), the Sipahi cavalry (financed through the timar system), a powerful artillery corps, and the Ottoman navy. At full mobilization, the Ottoman army could put more than 100,000 soldiers in the field. The Janissaries at their height numbered perhaps 12,000–20,000 elite troops.

Administration: The empire was divided into large provinces (eyalets) governed by governors-general (beylerbeys), subdivided into sanjaks. The central government in Istanbul, through the Grand Vizier and the Imperial Council (Divan), maintained control through regular inspection, the rotation of governors, and direct communication via a remarkable postal relay system.

Cultural Achievement: Suleiman's court was one of the great cultural centers of the early modern world. The architect Sinan was at work on major projects throughout the reign. Imperial patronage of poetry (Suleiman himself was an accomplished poet, writing under the pen name Muhibbi), miniature painting, calligraphy, and decorative arts was generous and sophisticated. The court library held thousands of manuscripts.

Primary Source Window

Abu'l-Fazl on Akbar's Religious Philosophy (from the Ain-i-Akbari, c. 1590s)

Creator: Abu'l-Fazl ibn Mubarak · Date: c. 1590s · Type: Court chronicle and administrative encyclopedia · Language: Persian

Context: Abu'l-Fazl ibn Mubarak (1551–1602) was Akbar's court chronicler and one of the most important literary figures of Mughal India. A deeply learned man who had studied Islamic theology, philosophy, and literature, Abu'l-Fazl was Akbar's closest intellectual companion and the chief apologist for his religious policies. The Ain-i-Akbari (Institutes of Akbar) was a massive encyclopedic work describing the Mughal Empire's administrative organization, revenues, territories, and cultural life. Abu'l-Fazl also wrote the Akbarnama, a narrative history of Akbar's reign. These works are invaluable primary sources for the Mughal Empire but must be read critically, since Abu'l-Fazl was not a neutral observer — he was Akbar's advocate and promoter.

In describing Akbar's approach to religious inquiry, Abu'l-Fazl explained that the emperor regarded the pursuit of divine truth as the highest human calling and believed that no single religious tradition had a monopoly on that truth. Akbar, according to Abu'l-Fazl, welcomed scholars of all faiths not to argue about which religion was superior but to illuminate the principles shared among them. The emperor was said to believe that God's light shone in all sincere seekers, whatever tradition they followed, and that a ruler who restricted his subjects' spiritual lives by compelling them to follow one path was both unjust and unwise.

Analysis: Abu'l-Fazl's portrait of Akbar reflects the emperor's real policies but also serves a clear political purpose: to legitimize Akbar's religious experimentation against the criticism of more orthodox Muslim scholars at court. The framing of Akbar as a seeker of divine truth rather than a heterodox Muslim or apostate was a deliberate rhetorical strategy. Historians use Abu'l-Fazl's works extensively but balance them against other sources: the private chronicle of the court historian Badauni, who deeply disapproved of Akbar's policies and wrote a critical history that circulated secretly; the accounts of the Portuguese Jesuit missionaries who described his religious discussions from their own perspective; and the administrative records of the Mughal state itself.

Archaeology, Artifact, and Material Evidence Window

The Iznik Tile: Art, Technology, and Imperial Identity in the Ottoman Empire

Among the most distinctive material legacies of the Ottoman Empire are the polychrome ceramic tiles produced at Iznik (ancient Nicaea) in northwestern Anatolia during the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries. These tiles — decorated with intricate floral and arabesque designs in brilliant blues, greens, and (from the 1550s) a distinctive bright tomato red — were used to decorate the interiors of mosques, palaces, and elite residences across the empire and were exported as luxury goods to European courts.

Iznik pottery reached its peak quality during approximately 1555–1620, when workshops in Iznik employed sophisticated techniques of underglaze painting on a refined white clay body (technically a composite of white clay, quartz, and glass frit rather than true porcelain) covered with a brilliant transparent glaze. The designs — featuring tulips, carnations, hyacinths, and other flowers arranged in flowing, symmetrical compositions — reflect an Ottoman aesthetic sensibility that combined Islamic geometric and arabesque traditions with a naturalistic interest in the floral world.

The tiles also reflect the organizational relationship between imperial patronage and craft production. Many of the finest Iznik tile programs — including the decoration of the Topkapi Palace's Circumcision Room and the tilework of the Süleymaniye Mosque — were imperial commissions, designed by court artists in the imperial palace design studio (Nakkaşhane) and then produced by Iznik workshops. This relationship between court design and workshop production is characteristic of how the Ottoman court organized luxury craft production.

Limits of the evidence: Surviving Iznik tiles in museums, mosques, and palaces allow historians to study the development of Ottoman design traditions over time, since the color palette and motifs changed in identifiable ways between the 1490s and the early seventeenth century. However, tiles tell historians primarily about elite tastes and court culture — they reveal less directly about the economic conditions of the craftspeople who made them or the ordinary consumers who bought simpler ceramic wares.

Historical Debate

Was Akbar a Sincere Religious Pluralist or a Political Pragmatist?

The question: Historians and political commentators have long debated whether Akbar's policies of religious openness — his abolition of the jizya, his marriages to Rajput princesses, his theological debates at the Ibadat Khana, his development of the Din-i-Ilahi — reflected genuine religious conviction, sophisticated political calculation, or some combination of both.

Interpretation A — The "Sincere Pluralist" View: Some historians, drawing primarily on Abu'l-Fazl's court chronicles and the testimony of the Jesuit missionaries at Akbar's court, argue that Akbar was genuinely and sincerely committed to religious inquiry and to the belief that divine truth could not be confined to any single tradition. Proponents of this view point to the consistency of Akbar's policies over decades, the personal courage it required to resist the pressure of orthodox Muslim scholars, and the sincerity apparent in his theological curiosity. They also emphasize that the Din-i-Ilahi was practiced by only a handful of courtiers — hardly the behavior of a cynical politician seeking mass popularity.
Interpretation B — The "Political Pragmatist" View: Other historians argue that Akbar's religious policies should primarily be understood as political strategy. On this reading, Akbar recognized that governing a Hindu-majority empire required incorporating the Hindu elite — especially the Rajput warrior clans — into the imperial system, and that a policy of strict Islamic governance would have made this incorporation impossible. The abolition of the jizya, the Rajput marriages, the prohibition on cow slaughter — all of these can be read as political concessions designed to secure the loyalty of Hindu subjects and nobles.

A More Complex Synthesis: Most contemporary historians of Mughal India take a more complex position, arguing that the dichotomy between sincere conviction and political pragmatism is itself misleading. Rulers in all historical periods make policies that simultaneously reflect their personal beliefs and serve their political interests — these two motivations are rarely separable in practice. Akbar was almost certainly both genuinely curious about religion and aware of the political advantages of religious inclusivity.

Why it matters: How historians interpret Akbar's pluralism connects to larger questions about the nature of early modern Islamic governance, the relationship between religion and politics in Mughal India, and the legacies of Mughal pluralism for modern India. Nationalist interpretations have sometimes used Akbar's pluralism as evidence that India has a deep historical tradition of religious tolerance; other interpreters emphasize its limits.

Comparison Across Societies

Religious Governance in the Three Empires: A Comparison

The Ottoman Approach — Sunni Orthodoxy and Protected Communities: The Ottoman Empire was officially Sunni Muslim and organized its religious life around the Sunni legal and institutional framework. Non-Muslim communities (Christians, Jews) were organized under the millet system as protected communities (dhimmi) paying the jizya and managing their own internal religious affairs. In practice, the Ottoman system was relatively tolerant by the standards of the era — certainly more tolerant than most contemporary European states toward religious minorities. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492, for example, settled in Ottoman cities (especially Salonica and Istanbul) and were welcomed as skilled craftspeople and merchants.

The Safavid Approach — Shi'a Transformation and Minority Status: The Safavid approach was more aggressively homogenizing. The declaration of Twelver Shi'a Islam as the state religion and the sustained campaign to transform Persian society from Sunni to Shi'a majority created a different dynamic: Sunni Muslims within Persia faced pressure to convert or face marginalization; the Shi'a religious establishment received enormous royal patronage and became closely allied with the state. Non-Muslim minorities — Armenians, Jews, Zoroastrians — were tolerated as dhimmi communities, similar to the Ottoman pattern, though their legal status could vary significantly.

The Mughal Approach — Pragmatic Pluralism Under an Islamic State: The Mughal approach was most distinctive because it addressed a different fundamental challenge: governing an Islamic state over a non-Muslim majority. Akbar abolished the jizya, incorporated Rajput nobles, and developed the Din-i-Ilahi. Aurangzeb reversed the most inclusive policies, reinstating the jizya and imposing restrictions on non-Muslim practice — policies that contributed to revolts and regional disintegration in his reign's later years.

Comparison and Context: Comparing these three approaches reveals that there was no single "Islamic model" of religious governance. The Ottomans, Safavids, and Mughals each developed distinctive approaches shaped by their specific populations, geographies, histories, and political challenges. None of the three was a modern secular state, and none guaranteed equal rights regardless of religion in the modern sense. But the range of accommodation they offered was considerable, and the comparison reveals that religious policy was never purely theological — it was always intertwined with questions of political legitimacy, social control, revenue collection, and imperial security.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experienced the Mughal Empire Differently?

From the perspective of the Rajput Noble: For members of the Rajput warrior clans, the Mughal Empire under Akbar represented both an opportunity and a constraint. The opportunity was genuine: Rajput nobles who accepted Mughal suzerainty, provided military service, and sent their daughters to marry Mughal princes could expect elevated status within the imperial hierarchy, confirmed control of their own territories, and the prestige of association with the world's most powerful empire. Raja Man Singh of Amber commanded Mughal armies on all frontiers and was one of the most powerful nobles in the empire. But the Rajput princess sent to the Mughal harem — whatever the political advantages of the alliance — experienced a life of confinement and hierarchy within the complex social world of the imperial household, far from her family and home territory.
From the perspective of the Hindu Peasant: For the overwhelming majority of Mughal India's population — the peasant farmers who worked the fields of the Ganges plain — the Mughal Empire was primarily experienced as a system of taxation and order rather than as an Islamic state. The abstract question of who ruled from Delhi mattered less than the concrete reality of how much tax was demanded, whether the local zamindar was reasonable or extortionate, whether the monsoon had been adequate, and whether bandits or armies had damaged the harvest. Mughal revenue policy could be deeply burdensome in practice: in years of drought, flood, or military disruption, the combination of natural disaster and tax demand could drive peasants into debt bondage, flight, or starvation. The great famine of 1630–1632 in the Deccan, described with horror by European travelers, demonstrated the vulnerability of the peasant majority.
From the perspective of the Urban Craftsperson: For the artisans and craftspeople employed in Mughal cities — the weavers of Dacca's famous muslin, the carpet makers of Lahore, the painters in the imperial workshop, the silversmiths and jewelers of Agra — the Mughal Empire could be both an opportunity and an exacting employer. The imperial workshops (karkhanas) employed thousands of craftspeople producing goods for the imperial household: textiles, weapons, metalwork, manuscripts, jewelry, and architectural decoration. But working in the imperial workshops was also a form of labor obligation — craftspeople were expected to produce what was demanded, at the quality demanded, and might have limited ability to negotiate the terms of their service. Those outside the imperial workshops faced the fluctuations of market demand and the constraints imposed by guild-like organizations.
Cause and Effect

The Ottoman Conquest of Constantinople (1453): Causes and Consequences

Long-Term Causes

The great schism of 1054 between Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Christianity divided Christian Europe in ways that made sustained coalition against the Ottomans difficult. The sack of Constantinople by the Fourth Crusade in 1204 permanently weakened the city. The Ottoman dynasty's sustained expansion over 150 years had progressively surrounded and isolated Constantinople. The Byzantine state's inability to modernize its revenue base or military system reflected deep structural problems.

Short-Term Causes

By the early 1450s, the Byzantine Empire had been reduced to the city of Constantinople itself. The city's population had declined dramatically, its walls were deteriorating, and its treasury was empty. Mehmed II assembled a massive army of perhaps 80,000–100,000 men and commissioned the construction of new heavy cannon cast by the Hungarian engineer Urban, who had been refused by the Byzantine emperor (who could not pay).

The Event

Mehmed II's siege began in early April 1453 and lasted until May 29, when Ottoman forces breached the Theodosian Walls. The last Byzantine Emperor, Constantine XI Palaiologos, died in the fighting. The city was given over to three days of plunder. The Hagia Sophia was converted to a mosque on June 1, and Mehmed began the transformation of Constantinople into Istanbul.

Long-Term Consequences

Thousands of Byzantine scholars, priests, artists, and nobles fled westward carrying manuscripts and cultural traditions to Italy — a contribution to the Renaissance that historians continue to study. Ottoman control of Constantinople gave the empire a stranglehold on trade between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean. The fall became a powerful symbol: for Muslims, a fulfillment of prophetic tradition; for Christians, a trauma that shaped European perceptions of Ottoman power for centuries.

Historical complexity: The conquest of Constantinople cannot be explained by any single cause. It was the product of 150 years of Ottoman expansion, structural Byzantine weakness, military-technological transformation, and the decisive choices of individual actors — including the Hungarian engineer Urban, whose cannon made the breach possible.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Religious Identity and Imperial Governance: 1400–1700

What Changed

  • In 1400, the Islamic world from the Mediterranean to Central Asia was overwhelmingly Sunni Muslim; by 1700, Persia had been transformed from Sunni to Shi'a majority through sustained Safavid action — a profound religious transformation of a major civilization
  • The Ottoman Empire claimed guardianship of Sunni Islam and the holy cities, institutionalizing a new form of Ottoman-Islamic imperial legitimacy
  • The Mughal Empire had experimented with — and then partially retreated from — radical religious pluralism under successive emperors
  • The relationship between state power and religious identity had been fundamentally restructured across the region

What Stayed the Same

  • The core institutions of Islamic scholarship — the madrasa, the mosque, the Sufi lodge — continued to function across all three empires and to transmit knowledge, train scholars, and provide spiritual community for Muslim populations
  • The fundamental tension between the Sharia (religious law) and imperial political necessity remained a constant challenge for all three empires
  • The role of the ulama as interpreters of religious law and advisors to rulers continued throughout the period

Why the pattern matters: The religious transformations of the era of the gunpowder empires created lasting structures that continue to shape the modern world. The Sunni-Shi'a division between the Ottoman and Safavid empires hardened into a persistent geopolitical and religious fault line that still shapes relations between the Sunni Arab world and Iran. The Shi'a identity that the Safavids imposed on Persia became foundational to modern Iranian national identity. The legacy of Mughal religious pluralism and the subsequent shift toward orthodoxy under Aurangzeb remain subjects of intense historical and political debate in modern India and Pakistan.

Everyday Life and Social History

Life in a Mughal City: Agra in the Reign of Akbar

What was daily life like for the ordinary inhabitants of Agra, the Mughal capital in Akbar's time? The evidence comes from a variety of sources: Abu'l-Fazl's administrative surveys, the accounts of European travelers (including the Venetian merchant Gasparo Balbi and the Jesuit priests Antonio Monserrate and Jerome Xavier), archaeological excavations, and the surviving material culture of the period.

Agra in the late sixteenth century was one of the largest cities in the world, with a population estimated at 200,000–500,000 — comparable to Istanbul or Beijing and far larger than any contemporary European city except perhaps Paris. The great Agra Fort (being rebuilt in red sandstone on Akbar's orders during this period) dominated the skyline. Around the fort clustered the neighborhoods of the court elite — the mansions and gardens of Mughal nobles, each a miniature court with its own household, servants, and dependent population. Further out, the city's commercial neighborhoods contained the workshops and shops of thousands of craftspeople and traders, organized by trade into separate quarters and bazaars.

Life in the city was organized around several rhythms. The Islamic week structured time for Muslim inhabitants, with Friday as the day of congregational prayer. Hindu inhabitants followed their own religious calendar, with festivals like Diwali and Holi celebrated publicly under Akbar's tolerant policy in ways that European visitors noted with curiosity. The great bazaars were daily gathering places for commerce, gossip, and sociability. The Yamuna River provided water, a place of bathing (especially important for the Hindu population), and a highway for boats carrying goods.

For elite women — whether in the Mughal harem or in the households of nobles — life was largely confined to the domestic sphere, separated by the custom of purdah from the public world of the city. But this domestic world was not necessarily passive or powerless: high-status women could hold considerable wealth in their own right, manage substantial households, and commission buildings and endowments. The most famous example is Nur Jahan — the powerful wife of Emperor Jahangir who effectively co-ruled the empire during Jahangir's later years, issuing her own coins and conducting her own diplomacy. For the majority of the city's population — the artisans, weavers, dyers, potters, metal workers, food vendors, water carriers, barbers, and laborers — daily life was organized around work, the household, and the immediate community. They were unlikely to appear in the court chronicles or the traveler's accounts — except as background color, the anonymous workers whose labor supported the glittering culture of the Mughal court.

Environment and Technology

Water, Irrigation, and Imperial Power: Gardens and Agriculture in the Mughal World

Water was simultaneously one of the most precious resources and one of the most powerful symbols in the early modern Islamic world. The garden (bagh in Persian and Urdu) was not merely an aesthetic amenity but a theological statement: the Islamic tradition described paradise (firdaus) as a garden watered by four rivers, and Mughal imperial gardens — with their formal layouts, running water channels, and lush plantings — were deliberate evocations of that divine ideal.

The charbagh (four-part garden), divided by water channels into a four-quadrant plan, was the fundamental design principle of Mughal garden architecture. Babur, who came from the dry landscapes of Central Asia, was reportedly dismayed by the Indian landscape he conquered — accustomed to the formalized gardens of his Timurid ancestors in Samarkand and Kabul. He immediately began creating gardens in the Timurid style wherever he settled, introducing the formal garden design traditions of the Persian-Timurid world to India. The tradition he established was developed over successive reigns until it reached its apogee in the garden of the Taj Mahal — the greatest charbagh in the world.

But water management in Mughal India was not only a matter of royal gardens. The agricultural system of the empire depended on sophisticated water management at every level. In the arid northwest, Persian wheels (water-lifting devices powered by animal traction) brought water from wells to fields. Canal systems in the Punjab and elsewhere directed river water to agricultural land. The monsoon itself — the great annual rainfall cycle that dominated life throughout the subcontinent — was the fundamental water management challenge, and the management of tanks (step-wells and reservoirs) for water storage was a crucial local technology.

The development of gunpowder technology was itself a form of environmental-technological transformation with significant consequences for the three empires. The production of cannon required large quantities of bronze or iron, driving demand for metal ores. The production of gunpowder required sulfur, charcoal, and saltpeter (potassium nitrate), which was produced in large quantities in India — where the warm climate and the accumulation of organic material in soils created ideal conditions for saltpeter formation. India became a major exporter of saltpeter to Europe and other regions in the seventeenth century, linking the environmental conditions of the Indian subcontinent to the global gunpowder economy.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 34 has examined three of the most powerful and historically significant empires of the early modern world: the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal states that together dominated a vast arc of territory from the Balkans to Bengal between approximately 1450 and 1700. These three "gunpowder empires" — so called for their innovative and decisive use of firearms and cannon — represented both the climax of a long tradition of Islamic imperial governance and the adaptation of that tradition to the conditions of the early modern world.

The Ottoman Empire, founded by a small Anatolian principality in the late thirteenth century, rose through military conquest and administrative sophistication to become one of the world's two preeminent powers by the reign of Suleiman the Magnificent (r. 1520–1566). The Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 was its defining moment — transforming the empire from a regional power into a Mediterranean superpower and providing it with a capital of incomparable strategic and symbolic value. The Ottoman administrative system — combining the devshirme and Janissary corps, the timar land grant system, the millet framework for governing non-Muslim communities, and the dual legal system of Sharia and Kanun — was one of the most sophisticated governance mechanisms of the era.

The Safavid Empire, established in Persia in 1501 by the charismatic Ismail I, made an extraordinarily consequential choice when it declared Twelver Shi'a Islam the state religion — transforming Persian religious life over the following century and creating the enduring Sunni-Shi'a divide that shaped relations between the Ottoman and Safavid empires throughout their co-existence. The Safavid peak under Shah Abbas I (r. 1587–1629) saw both military reformation and the creation of Isfahan as one of the world's most beautiful cities. The Safavid experience demonstrates how deeply religious identity could be shaped and transformed by deliberate state action.

The Mughal Empire, founded by Babur in 1526 and reaching its administrative apex under Akbar (r. 1556–1605), faced the distinctive challenge of governing an Islamic ruling dynasty over a Hindu-majority population of extraordinary diversity. Akbar's response — the mansabdari administrative system, the incorporation of Rajput nobles, the abolition of the jizya, the religious debates at the Ibadat Khana, and eventually the syncretic Din-i-Ilahi — represented one of the most sophisticated experiments in governing religious diversity in early modern world history.

All three empires invested enormously in art, architecture, and court culture. The Ottoman architect Sinan created masterpieces of mosque architecture. The Safavid court produced silk carpets, miniature paintings, and the architectural ensemble of Isfahan that stand among the greatest achievements of Islamic civilization. The Mughal court produced the Akbarnama, illustrated histories of emperors' reigns, the garden-mausoleum tradition culminating in the Taj Mahal, and a tradition of miniature painting that synthesized Persian, Indian, and European influences. These cultural achievements were not merely decorative — they were expressions of imperial legitimacy, religious identity, and political power.

Economically, all three empires rested on agrarian tax foundations — the land revenue of millions of peasant farmers — supplemented by revenues from long-distance trade. The Ottoman Empire controlled crucial trade routes between Europe and Asia. The Safavid Empire managed the silk trade between Persia and European and Asian markets. The Mughal Empire governed the subcontinent's production of cotton textiles, indigo, and other commodities that circulated through the Indian Ocean trade network. All three were embedded in the global commercial system that was expanding rapidly in the early modern period, partly through the growing presence of European trading companies in the Indian Ocean and eastern Mediterranean.

Comparing the three empires reveals both their shared characteristics and their important differences. They shared Islamic religious traditions, Central Asian military heritage, Persian administrative and cultural traditions, agrarian economic foundations, and the challenge of governing diverse populations. They differed in their specific religious policies, their geographic positions and relationships to maritime trade, the nature of their populations, and the particular political challenges they faced.

By the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, all three empires were experiencing significant strains — military reverses, internal political instability, structural economic challenges, and the growing competitive pressure of European commercial and military power. The Safavid Empire collapsed dramatically in 1722; the Mughal Empire fragmented gradually after Aurangzeb's death in 1707; the Ottoman Empire survived into the twentieth century but in steadily diminishing form. These declines should not cause students to overlook the centuries of achievement that preceded them.

The legacies of the gunpowder empires are profound and ongoing. The modern states of Turkey, Iran, and India/Pakistan are all in significant ways heirs to the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal traditions, respectively. The Sunni-Shi'a divide that the Ottoman-Safavid conflict helped to harden continues to shape relations between Iran and its Arab neighbors. The question of how to govern religious diversity — which Akbar confronted with such sophistication — remains one of the central challenges of the modern Indian subcontinent. The architectural legacies of all three empires draw millions of visitors every year and continue to shape the self-understanding of the peoples who live in their shadow.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Gunpowder empire
Historian's term for the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires, emphasizing their use of firearms and cannon as a basis for military power and territorial expansion in the early modern period.
Devshirme
Ottoman system of periodic levy of Christian boys from the Balkans for conversion, education, and imperial service as soldiers (Janissaries) or administrators.
Janissaries
Elite Ottoman infantry corps, originally recruited through the devshirme system; organized as a professional standing army equipped with firearms; eventually became a powerful political interest group.
Grand Vizier
Chief minister of the Ottoman Empire, presiding over the Imperial Council and effectively running the government on the sultan's behalf; typically a man of devshirme origin with no independent dynastic power base.
Millet
Ottoman system of organizing non-Muslim communities (Christians, Jews) as semi-autonomous religious units with their own leadership and internal legal authority for matters of marriage, inheritance, and religious practice.
Dhimmi
Classical Islamic legal status for non-Muslim "peoples of the book" (Christians, Jews, Zoroastrians) living under Muslim rule; dhimmis were protected but paid the jizya and faced various restrictions on public religious expression.
Jizya
Poll tax paid by non-Muslim dhimmis under Muslim rule; abolished by Akbar in 1564 to promote religious harmony, restored by Aurangzeb in 1679 as part of his more orthodox Islamic governance.
Sharia
Islamic law derived from the Quran and Hadith, interpreted and applied by trained religious scholars (ulama); the primary legal system for matters of personal status and religious observance in all three empires.
Kanun
Ottoman imperial legislation supplementing Sharia on matters of taxation, land tenure, military organization, and criminal procedure; developed most extensively under Suleiman "the Lawgiver."
Sheikh ul-Islam
The Grand Mufti of Istanbul; highest religious authority in the Ottoman Empire with the authority to issue fatwas (formal legal opinions) on questions of Islamic law.
Timar
Land revenue grant in the Ottoman system, assigned to military commanders (sipahis) in exchange for maintaining a specified number of cavalry soldiers for imperial service.
Qizilbash
Turkic tribal warriors ("Red Heads") who provided the military basis for Safavid power; named for their distinctive red turbans; intensely devoted to the Safavid leaders as spiritual-political figures.
Twelver Shi'a Islam
The largest branch of Shi'a Islam, holding that there were twelve rightful Imams, the last of whom went into occultation in 874 CE; declared the state religion of the Safavid Empire by Ismail I in 1501.
Ghulam
Enslaved soldiers of Georgian, Armenian, and Circassian origin created by Shah Abbas I to balance the independent power of the Qizilbash; their loyalty was entirely to the shah.
Mansabdari system
Mughal administrative and military ranking system introduced by Akbar, organizing all imperial servants into numbered ranks (mansabs) and requiring them to maintain specific numbers of cavalry in exchange for salary assignments.
Zamindar
Landholder or revenue collector in Mughal India who held rights to collect land tax from peasants in a given area, remitting a fixed portion to the imperial government and keeping a percentage as compensation.
Rajput
Hindu warrior clans of Rajasthan and central India who became crucial military allies of the Mughal Empire under Akbar's policy of incorporating regional elites through marriage alliances and imperial appointment.
Din-i-Ilahi
Akbar's syncretic "Divine Faith," a personal spiritual practice developed in the 1580s drawing on elements of Islam, Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, and Sufism; practiced by a small inner circle of Akbar's most trusted courtiers.
Ibadat Khana
Akbar's "House of Worship" at Fatehpur Sikri, where he hosted religious debates among scholars of different traditions including Sunni and Shi'a Muslims, Hindus, Jains, and Portuguese Jesuit priests.
Charbagh
The four-part garden design divided by water channels, evoking the Islamic concept of paradise; the fundamental design principle of Mughal garden architecture, culminating in the garden of the Taj Mahal.
Sufi
Practitioner of Islamic mysticism (Sufism), which emphasizes the inner spiritual dimension of religion and the personal experience of God; Sufi orders played important roles in the spread of Islam and in the cultural life of all three empires.
Ulama
Islamic religious scholars who interpreted Sharia, administered courts, ran schools, and served as advisors to rulers; their relationship with sultans, shahs, and emperors was crucial to the legitimacy of Islamic governance.
Waqf
Charitable endowment in Islamic law, typically used to fund mosques, madrasas, hospitals, and other public institutions; waqf endowments were exempt from state taxation and formed the economic basis of many Islamic social institutions.
Caravanserai
Roadside inn and trading post built to support overland trade, providing shelter, water, and storage for merchants and their animals; the Ottomans, Safavids, and Mughals all built extensive networks of caravanserais to facilitate commerce.
Capitulations
Privileged commercial agreements (ahdname) granted by the Ottoman state to European merchants, giving them special trading rights, reduced customs rates, and legal protections within Ottoman territory.
Naqsh-e Jahan Square
The great imperial square ("Image of the World") in Isfahan built under Shah Abbas I, surrounded by the Royal Mosque, the Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque, the Ali Qapu Palace, and the entrance to the bazaar — one of the finest examples of Islamic urban planning.
Sipahi
Ottoman cavalry soldier, financed through the timar system; formed the backbone of the Ottoman army alongside the Janissary infantry corps.
Abu'l-Fazl
Akbar's court chronicler and closest intellectual companion; author of the Akbarnama and Ain-i-Akbari; chief intellectual architect of the Mughal imperial ideology; killed on orders of Prince Salim (later Jahangir) in 1602.
Baburnama
Autobiographical memoir of Babur, founder of the Mughal Empire; written in Chagatai Turkish; one of the most important primary sources for early Mughal history and one of the great autobiographies of the early modern world.
Mansabdar
Holder of a mansab in the Mughal system; an imperial official or military commander with a specific numerical rank determining their salary and military obligations to the empire.

Major People, Places, and Polities

Identification & Significance

Mehmed II ("the Conqueror," r. 1451–1481)
Ottoman sultan who captured Constantinople in 1453 and transformed it into Istanbul; built the Topkapi Palace; converted the Hagia Sophia into a mosque; demonstrated that gunpowder artillery could defeat even the most formidable medieval fortifications.
Suleiman I ("the Magnificent" / "the Lawgiver," r. 1520–1566)
Ottoman sultan at the height of imperial power; his reign represented the Ottoman peak in territorial extent, legal codification, and cultural achievement; his court was one of the great cultural centers of the early modern world.
Selim I (r. 1512–1520)
Ottoman sultan who expanded the empire dramatically eastward, defeating the Safavids at Chaldiran and conquering Egypt and the Arab heartlands; his campaigns roughly doubled the size of the empire in eight years.
Ismail I (r. 1501–1524)
Founder of the Safavid dynasty; his declaration of Twelver Shi'a Islam as the state religion of Persia was one of the most consequential acts of early modern world history, creating the religious identity of modern Iran.
Shah Abbas I (r. 1587–1629)
Greatest Safavid ruler; rebuilt Isfahan into one of the world's most beautiful cities; reformed the military; established Persian silk trade with Europe; recovered territory from the Ottomans and drove the Uzbeks from the northeast.
Babur (r. 1526–1530)
Founder of the Mughal Empire; descended from Timur and Genghis Khan; his use of cannon at Panipat (1526) established Mughal rule in India; author of the Baburnama, a remarkable literary autobiography.
Akbar (r. 1556–1605)
Third and greatest Mughal emperor; developed the mansabdari system; pursued religious pluralism; created a court that was among the most intellectually vibrant in the early modern world; the architectural complex at Fatehpur Sikri remains a monument to his ambition.
Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658)
Mughal emperor who commissioned the Taj Mahal, the Red Fort in Delhi, and the Jama Masjid; his reign represented the cultural apex of Mughal architecture.
Aurangzeb (r. 1658–1707)
Last major Mughal emperor; reversed Akbar's pluralism; his reign extended the empire to its greatest geographic extent but also began the process of fragmentation that would lead to Mughal collapse in the eighteenth century.
Battle of Chaldiran (1514)
Ottoman victory over the Safavids that established the military balance and approximate border between the two empires; demonstrated the decisive advantage of cannon and Janissary muskets over cavalry without comparable firepower.
Battle of Panipat (1526)
Babur's victory over the Delhi Sultanate using cannon and matchlock muskets; foundation of the Mughal Empire; demonstrated that organized gunpowder forces could defeat numerically much larger armies.
Taj Mahal (1632–1653)
Mughal funerary monument built by Shah Jahan for his wife Mumtaz Mahal; widely considered one of the world's most beautiful buildings; an integrated vision of Islamic cosmology, garden design, and architectural achievement.
New Julfa
Armenian Christian community in a suburb of Isfahan, relocated by Shah Abbas I to serve as commercial intermediaries in the silk trade; their networks connecting Iran to European markets were crucial to the Safavid silk economy.
Raja Todar Mal
Akbar's finance minister; designed the sophisticated Mughal land revenue system that classified agricultural land by productivity, assessed revenue on multi-year price averages, and collected tax in cash to stimulate commercial activity.
Sinan (c. 1489–1588)
Chief architect of the Ottoman Empire; designed more than 370 structures including the Selimiye Mosque in Edirne and the Süleymaniye Mosque in Istanbul; widely regarded as the greatest architect of Islamic civilization.

Chapter Reflection

The Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires occupy a crucial place in the larger story that this World History textbook tells. They remind us, first and most fundamentally, that the early modern world was not a story about Europe. In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, when European powers were building their first trading empires along the coasts of Africa, Asia, and the Americas, the Islamic gunpowder empires governed a larger population, produced more economic output, and created greater cultural achievement than all of Western Europe combined. Understanding world history requires taking these empires seriously in their own right — not as a backdrop to European expansion but as vital, creative, and powerful civilizations with their own internal dynamics, their own historical problems, and their own contributions to human civilization.

Second, the three empires demonstrate the range and flexibility of Islamic political and religious tradition. Islam is not a monolithic system that produced a single kind of state or society. The Sunni Ottoman Empire, the Shi'a Safavid Empire, and the pluralist-pragmatist Mughal Empire all operated within Islamic frameworks, but they applied those frameworks to very different situations in very different ways. The diversity of Islamic imperial experience in the early modern period should make students skeptical of any simple generalization about "Islamic governance" or "Islamic civilization." Like Christianity, Buddhism, or any other major world tradition, Islam provided a framework for human organization that was interpreted and applied in enormously varied ways depending on historical circumstances, geographic conditions, and political necessities.

Third, the chapter has modeled the importance of comparing across societies rather than studying each in isolation. The comparison of the Ottoman millet system with the Mughal mansabdari system, or the Safavid Shi'a transformation with Akbar's pluralism, or the Janissary corps with the ghulam forces of Shah Abbas I — these comparisons reveal patterns that no single-empire study could show. Comparison does not require ranking civilizations or judging which was "superior." It requires asking how different societies solved similar problems, what the range of solutions was, what constraints shaped the options available, and what consequences followed from different choices. This is one of the central skills of historical thinking.

Finally, the gunpowder empires remind us that history is not only made by rulers, generals, and famous monuments. It is also made by the peasants who paid the taxes that financed the Taj Mahal, by the Christian boys taken through the devshirme who became the finest administrators the Ottoman state had ever seen, by the Armenian merchants of New Julfa whose commercial networks connected Safavid silk to European markets, by the Hindu weavers of the Mughal Empire whose cotton cloth circulated across the Indian Ocean world, and by the women in the harems of all three courts whose lives were shaped by the intersection of family, religion, and imperial power in ways that historians are still working to understand. The history of the gunpowder empires is, finally, the history of all the people who lived within them — and learning to see all of those people, not only the sultans and shahs and emperors, is one of the deepest lessons that world history can teach.

Looking forward, the story of these three empires connects directly to the themes of later chapters: the growing commercial and military pressure of European trading companies on Asian markets, the development of new economic institutions in Europe that would eventually give European states a structural advantage, the intensification of global trade flows that would carry Indian cotton, Persian silk, and Ottoman spices to consumers across the world, and the emergence of new political movements — nationalism, anti-colonialism, religious reform — that would reshape the regions the gunpowder empires had governed. The world that came after these empires was shaped by what they had built, by how they had governed, and by the legacies — proud and painful — that they left for their successors to inherit.

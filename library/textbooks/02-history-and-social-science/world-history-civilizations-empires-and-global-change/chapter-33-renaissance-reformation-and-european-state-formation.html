Part VII — The Early Modern World, 1450–1750
33

Renaissance, Reformation, and European State Formation

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The period between 1450 and 1750 was not a story of unambiguous progress. It was a period of wrenching transformation, intense conflict, and global consequence."

In the early spring of 1455, in the German city of Mainz, a craftsman named Johannes Gutenberg completed a project that had consumed years of labor, capital, and mechanical ingenuity. The device he had assembled — a movable-type printing press using individually cast metal letters that could be set, inked, pressed against vellum or paper, and reset again — produced copies of the Latin Bible at a speed no team of scribes could match. Within months, Gutenberg's Bibles circulated through the network of merchants, clerics, and scholars that connected the cities of the Rhine valley and beyond. Within decades, printing presses operated in dozens of European cities, and the volume of written material available to literate Europeans multiplied beyond anything the continent had previously known. The printing press did not create the Renaissance or the Reformation by itself. But it accelerated, amplified, and transformed virtually every intellectual and religious development of the next two centuries. It stands as one useful entry point into a period of profound and often violent change that reshaped Europe between approximately 1450 and 1750.

This chapter examines that transformation. Between the mid-fifteenth and mid-eighteenth centuries, Europe underwent a cluster of overlapping changes that altered its cultural, religious, political, economic, and ultimately global position. Scholars, artists, and humanists working in Italian cities rediscovered and reinterpreted classical Greek and Roman texts, generating a cultural movement historians call the Renaissance. Reformers within the Latin Church, frustrated by corruption, theological controversy, and papal authority, broke the religious unity of western Christianity in a movement called the Protestant Reformation, which was followed by the Catholic Church's own internal reform. Monarchies and states consolidated power, built bureaucracies, raised armies through systematic taxation, and transformed the relationship between rulers and subjects. European navigators, armed with improved ships and navigational knowledge, sailed to West Africa, the Americas, South and Southeast Asia, and the Pacific, drawing the continent into new global networks of trade, colonization, and exploitation. And a generation of thinkers — natural philosophers, astronomers, mathematicians, and anatomists — began reshaping European understandings of the natural world in what historians call the Scientific Revolution.

These changes were neither simple nor uniformly positive. The Renaissance celebrated human dignity and classical learning but rested on economies that depended on artisan labor, merchant wealth, and the early stirrings of Atlantic slavery. The Reformation generated theological renewal and popular religious engagement but also unleashed more than a century of vicious religious warfare that killed hundreds of thousands. State centralization rationalized government but also extended the reach of royal power over ordinary subjects, imposed heavier tax burdens on peasant and artisan communities, and sustained colonial systems of extraction and forced labor. European maritime expansion enriched European merchants and states but devastated Indigenous societies across the Americas and Africa. The period between 1450 and 1750 was not a story of unambiguous progress. It was a period of wrenching transformation, intense conflict, and global consequence.

It is equally important to situate European change within the broader world of the period. As Chapter 32 established, around 1500 the world contained several powerful, sophisticated, and commercially active civilizations. The Ottoman Empire controlled the eastern Mediterranean, Anatolia, and much of the Middle East and North Africa. The Safavid Empire governed Persia. The Mughal Empire was on the verge of unifying most of the Indian subcontinent. Ming China remained the world's largest economy by most measures. The Aztec and Inca empires dominated large portions of the Americas. Songhai controlled the western Sudan's most productive trade routes. None of these civilizations was stagnant, backward, or passively waiting for European contact. The changes that unfolded in Europe between 1450 and 1750 were significant — but they occurred alongside, and in interaction with, equally significant developments elsewhere.

Chapter 33 focuses on Europe not because Europe was the only or most important story of the early modern world, but because European developments during this period had enormous consequences for world history as a whole, including the conquest and colonization of the Americas and parts of Africa, Asia, and Oceania that subsequent chapters examine directly. The historians' challenge in studying this period is to explain change without oversimplifying it. The Renaissance, the Reformation, the rise of sovereign states, and the Scientific Revolution are often narrated as a single coherent story pointing toward modernity, secularism, and European global dominance. That narrative flattens important complexities. Renaissance humanists were deeply Christian as well as classically inspired. Protestant reformers were not secular liberals but intense theological conservatives who often fiercely opposed political and social radicalism. The rise of monarchical states did not eliminate aristocratic privilege or popular community. And the Scientific Revolution, whatever its intellectual achievements, coexisted for generations with widespread belief in astrology, alchemy, and witchcraft.

The evidence historians use to study this period is rich but uneven. Printed books, pamphlets, and broadsheets survive in large numbers after the mid-fifteenth century and give unprecedented access to the intellectual life of literate Europeans. Church records, tax lists, legal proceedings, diplomatic correspondence, merchants' account books, and personal diaries allow historians to reconstruct social, economic, and political life at many levels. Archaeological evidence, architectural remains, artworks, and material objects illuminate daily life, aesthetic culture, and technological change. But ordinary people — peasants, artisans, servants, women, enslaved laborers, and the urban poor — left far less direct testimony than kings, theologians, merchants, or humanist scholars. Historians must read against the grain of abundant elite documentation to recover experiences that were poorly recorded at the time.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the origins, core ideas, and geographic spread of Renaissance humanism, and evaluate its relationship to classical antiquity, Christian tradition, and Italian urban society.
  2. Analyze how the printing press transformed the circulation of ideas, religious controversy, and political communication in early modern Europe.
  3. Identify the causes of the Protestant Reformation, describe the major reformers and movements, and evaluate the theological, political, and social consequences of religious division.
  4. Describe the Catholic Reformation and explain how the Roman Church responded to the Protestant challenge through internal reform, the Council of Trent, and new religious orders.
  5. Trace the process of monarchical state centralization in early modern Europe, explaining the roles of taxation, bureaucracy, law, standing armies, and court culture.
  6. Analyze the relationship between religious conflict and political violence in the early modern period, with reference to specific wars, persecutions, and massacres.
  7. Evaluate the causes, methods, and consequences of the Scientific Revolution, including the roles of observation, mathematics, and institutional patronage.
  8. Connect European maritime ambitions to the intellectual, commercial, and technological changes of the Renaissance and early modern period.
  9. Compare patterns of early modern state formation across Europe, identifying similarities and regional differences.
  10. Contextualize European change within wider global patterns, explaining how the period's developments connected to broader world history rather than representing an isolated European story.
  11. Interpret primary sources from the period — humanist texts, Reformation pamphlets, royal edicts, scientific treatises — as evidence about beliefs, social conditions, and historical argument.
  12. Distinguish between the ideals promoted by Renaissance and Reformation thinkers and the social realities experienced by ordinary Europeans.

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  2. How did the printing press change the relationship between ideas, religious authority, and popular audiences — and what were the political consequences of this change?

  8. In what ways did European changes between 1450 and 1750 connect to — and depend on — developments outside Europe, including trade with Asia, colonization of the Americas, and the African slave trade?

  10. What larger world-history patterns does European early modern history illuminate, including the relationship between commerce and culture, between religious change and political conflict, and between technological innovation and social consequence?

Historical Context

To understand the changes that reshaped Europe between 1450 and 1750, it is necessary to understand the world that preceded them. Medieval western Europe — the civilization that had developed after the collapse of the western Roman Empire — was organized around several interlocking institutions and structures. The Latin Church, centered on the papacy in Rome, provided theological authority, moral framework, administrative infrastructure, and a shared written language (Latin) that connected educated people across political boundaries. The feudal system — a set of relationships between lords and vassals, defined by land grants in exchange for military service — organized much of rural society and political life, though by 1300 it was already evolving significantly. The overwhelming majority of Europeans lived in villages and worked the land, subject to the demands of noble landlords and ecclesiastical institutions.

By the mid-fourteenth century, this world had been badly disrupted. The Black Death, which swept Eurasia between approximately 1346 and 1353, killed between a third and a half of Europe's population. The demographic collapse reshaped labor markets, weakened serfdom in many regions, and destabilized the economic and social relationships that had sustained the feudal order. The Church had also suffered. The Avignon papacy (1309–1377), during which French kings effectively controlled the papacy, damaged the moral authority of the institution. The Great Schism (1378–1417), during which rival claimants competed for the papacy, further undermined the Church's claim to universal spiritual authority. Conciliarist movements argued that church councils rather than popes held supreme authority. Reform movements, from the Waldensians in southern France to the Hussites in Bohemia, challenged clerical corruption and questioned papal authority. The intellectual and institutional structures of medieval Christianity were under strain long before Martin Luther posted his theses in 1517.

Italian cities were a particular exception to the pattern of post-plague disruption. Florence, Venice, Milan, Genoa, Naples, and Rome were wealthy, commercially active, and politically sophisticated urban centers. Florence's banking houses financed rulers across Europe. Venice's merchant fleet dominated eastern Mediterranean trade. These cities were governed by complex political institutions — guilds, councils, oligarchies, signorie — that produced educated, competitive, ambitious men who saw themselves as heirs to the civic traditions of ancient Rome. Wealthy Italian merchants and princes collected books, commissioned paintings, employed scholars, and built palaces and churches that self-consciously evoked classical architecture. The intellectual movement that emerged from this culture — Renaissance humanism — was not a rejection of Christianity but a reorientation of scholarly energy toward classical texts, rhetorical excellence, historical consciousness, and the study of human life and achievement.

Northern Europe in the late fifteenth century was undergoing its own transformations. The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453) between England and France had ended, leaving France with a stronger monarchy and England in the early stages of the dynastic conflicts known as the Wars of the Roses. The Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 ended the Byzantine Empire, sending Greek scholars and manuscripts westward into Italian cities. The Iberian kingdoms — Castile and Aragon, unified by the marriage of Ferdinand and Isabella — were completing the Reconquista and expelling their Jewish population in 1492. The same year, Christopher Columbus, sailing under Castilian patronage, reached the Caribbean. The stage for a dramatically different era of European and global history was being set.

Regional Orientation

Italy sits at the geographic center of the Renaissance. The Italian Peninsula is a long, mountainous landmass projecting into the Mediterranean Sea, with the Alps forming a northern border and the seas on three sides. Its position made Italy a natural crossroads of Mediterranean commerce. The peninsula was politically fragmented — divided among city-states, the Papal States, and the Kingdom of Naples — and this fragmentation, paradoxically, may have encouraged competitive investment in culture, scholarship, and the arts. Proximity to the surviving fragments of Roman antiquity — buildings, inscriptions, sculptures — gave Italian scholars a tangible connection to classical civilization.

The Holy Roman Empire covered much of central Europe — modern Germany, Austria, Switzerland, the Czech Republic, and parts of France and the Low Countries. It was a patchwork of hundreds of territories, free cities, and ecclesiastical domains, nominally ruled by an elected emperor but practically governed by princes, bishops, and city councils with considerable autonomy. This political fragmentation was crucial to the Reformation's success: Luther's survival depended on the protection of German princes who resisted imperial and papal pressure.

The Iberian Peninsula — home to the kingdoms of Castile, Aragon, and Portugal — became the launching point for maritime expansion. Its Atlantic and Mediterranean coastlines, its long maritime tradition, and the competitive ambitions of its monarchies drove exploration and colonization. The union of Castile and Aragon under Ferdinand and Isabella, and their expulsion of Muslims and Jews in 1492, created one of Europe's most powerful early modern states.

France was Europe's most populous kingdom and, by the mid-seventeenth century under Louis XIV, the dominant continental power. It was also torn by religious civil war between Catholics and Huguenots (French Protestants) in the later sixteenth century. England was a maritime kingdom whose Reformation, driven more by royal politics than popular theology, produced a distinctive Protestant tradition, and eventually a constitutional conflict between king and Parliament. The Low Countries (modern Netherlands and Belgium) were among Europe's most commercially advanced regions; the northern provinces revolted against Spanish rule and created the Dutch Republic, one of the most commercially dynamic and religiously tolerant states of the seventeenth century.

Section 33.1

Europe in the Early Modern World

The phrase "early modern" is a historians' convention, not a label that people living in the fifteenth, sixteenth, or seventeenth centuries would have used to describe themselves. What the label captures is a transitional quality — a period that was unmistakably different from medieval Europe in several important respects, but equally unmistakably different from the industrialized, secularized, nationally organized Europe of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. "Early modern" suggests a world already undergoing deep transformations — in religion, governance, economy, knowledge, and global reach — while retaining institutions, beliefs, and social structures inherited from a much older order.

By 1450, Europe was recovering from the catastrophic population losses of the Black Death. Between the mid-fourteenth and mid-fifteenth centuries, the continent's population gradually rebounded, and with it came renewed commercial activity, urban growth, and agricultural expansion. New wealth accumulated in the cities of northern Italy, the Low Countries, southern Germany, and the Hanseatic trading network that connected the Baltic and North Sea regions. Long-distance trade linked European merchants to suppliers of spices, silks, and luxury goods in the eastern Mediterranean and, indirectly, to the Indian Ocean and Central Asian trading systems that channeled goods from South and Southeast Asia to European markets.

This commercial revival had significant cultural consequences. Wealthy Italian merchants and princes invested their wealth not only in commercial ventures but in education, art, scholarship, and urban beautification. The culture of patronage — supporting scholars, artists, and architects who produced works that reflected well on their patrons' taste and magnificence — drove artistic and intellectual production in ways that gradually transformed European culture. The ancient ruins that surrounded Italian cities — the Colosseum, the Pantheon, the Forum, inscriptions, sculptures, and architectural fragments — served as constant visual reminders of a sophisticated classical past that educated Italians increasingly saw as their inheritance and their model.

It is important not to overstate European wealth or power in 1450, however. By comparison with the Ottoman Empire, Ming China, or even the Mali Empire of West Africa, western European kingdoms were relatively peripheral actors in world trade. The overland Silk Road routes that had connected Europe to Central and East Asia passed through Ottoman and other Islamic-ruled territories. European access to the most coveted luxury goods — spices, silks, porcelains — depended on intermediaries who could charge steep markups. European silver and gold were chronically scarce. One major stimulus for maritime exploration was precisely this geographic disadvantage: European merchants and monarchs sought sea routes that would allow them to bypass Ottoman and Mamluk intermediaries and access Asian trade goods directly.

The political landscape of Europe in 1450 was fragmented and contested. The Holy Roman Empire nominally united central Europe under a single emperor — but real power was exercised by hundreds of princes, dukes, bishops, free cities, and lesser lords who jealously guarded their autonomy. France was recovering from the Hundred Years' War and beginning to consolidate royal power. England was entering the dynastic conflict of the Wars of the Roses. The Iberian kingdoms were engaged in the final stages of the Reconquista. This fragmented political landscape meant that no single European power could dominate the continent — and that reformers, dissenters, merchants, and innovators could always find refuge in some corner of the patchwork political map.

The social structure of early modern Europe was hierarchical, patriarchal, and deeply shaped by both legal status and economic function. At the top stood monarchs and great nobles, followed by lesser nobles and gentry, urban merchants and guild masters, clergy, artisans and craftspeople, peasant farmers, agricultural laborers, and at the bottom, the landless poor and enslaved people, primarily in the Iberian kingdoms and their overseas colonies. Women were largely excluded from formal political and ecclesiastical authority. The period 1450–1750 saw significant challenges to these social structures — from reformers, from popular rebellions, from commercial change — but also strong forces of resistance and reinvention.

Understanding early modern Europe means understanding these tensions: between economic dynamism and social conservatism, between religious renewal and persecution, between expanding knowledge and entrenched authority, between political centralization and aristocratic resistance, and between European self-celebration and the brutal realities of colonial exploitation.

Section 33.2

Renaissance Humanism and Cultural Change

The word "Renaissance" literally means "rebirth," and historians have used it to describe a cultural movement centered in Italy that drew inspiration from the recovery and reinterpretation of classical Greek and Roman texts, languages, art, and ideas. The Renaissance was not a sudden discovery — medieval Europeans had always known some classical texts, and the recovery of Aristotle's works in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries had already transformed European philosophy and theology. What changed in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries was the intensity of engagement with classical antiquity, the range of texts recovered and studied, the methods of criticism applied to them, and the values that scholars drew from them.

The intellectual movement at the heart of the Renaissance is called humanism — a term that requires careful definition. Renaissance humanism was not humanism in the modern sense of a secular, human-centered philosophy opposed to religion. Renaissance humanists were overwhelmingly Christian. What they shared was a commitment to the study of the humanities — grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy — as drawn from classical Greek and Latin authors. They believed that the study of classical texts cultivated virtue, eloquence, and civic engagement in ways that the scholastic philosophy of the medieval universities — with its emphasis on formal logic and Aristotelian theology — did not.

Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374) is often credited with launching Renaissance humanism. Petrarch was an accomplished Latin poet and scholar who immersed himself in classical Latin authors — especially Cicero — and who articulated a vision of the intervening centuries as a "dark age" of cultural barbarism from which classical learning was only beginning to emerge. This vision was historically misleading — medieval civilization had its own considerable intellectual and artistic achievements — but it was rhetorically powerful and shaped how educated Europeans thought about their cultural position for generations.

The Florentine humanists of the fifteenth century built on Petrarch's foundation and extended it in new directions. Coluccio Salutati and Leonardo Bruni developed the idea of "civic humanism" — the argument that the active life of citizenship, commerce, and public service was not inferior to the contemplative monastic life but was itself a path to virtue. This argument had obvious appeal in Florence, a city of merchants, bankers, and guild members who needed a cultural framework that dignified their commercial activity. Bruni also developed humanist historiography — approaching history through human causes rather than divine providence.

The recovery of Greek texts was another crucial dimension of Renaissance humanism. Byzantine scholars and manuscripts brought into Italy opened the rich tradition of Greek literature, history, and science to western European humanists. Greek scholars like Manuel Chrysoloras taught Greek in Italian cities and inspired a generation who added Greek to their Latin scholarship. The result was a dramatically expanded textual canon, including the works of Plato, Thucydides, Plutarch, and the Greek dramatists.

Renaissance humanism expressed itself not only through scholarship but through the arts. Italian painters, sculptors, and architects drew on classical models — human proportions, naturalistic representation, perspective, classical architectural orders — to create a visual culture immediately recognizable as different from the flat, symbolic style of medieval religious art. Painters like Masaccio, Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci, and Raphael developed techniques of perspective, shading, and anatomical accuracy that gave their figures a sense of three-dimensional presence. Sculptors like Donatello and Michelangelo created freestanding figures that evoked classical statuary. These artistic developments were not secular in subject matter — the overwhelming majority of Renaissance artworks depicted Christian subjects — but they expressed Christian themes in a visual language that celebrated human beauty, dignity, and physical reality.

The high Renaissance, centered roughly on 1490–1530, produced some of the most celebrated works in European art history: Leonardo's Last Supper and Mona Lisa, Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling and David, Raphael's Vatican frescoes. These works were produced in the service of powerful patrons — popes, princes, wealthy merchants — and they reflect both the extraordinary creative talent of their makers and the wealth and ambition of those who commissioned them.

Political humanism produced one of the period's most influential — and controversial — texts: Niccolò Machiavelli's The Prince (written 1513, published 1532). Machiavelli had served as a Florentine diplomat and administrator, and after the Medici restored their power and removed him from office, he drew on his political experience to write a short, sharp treatise on how rulers acquire and maintain power. Machiavelli's argument was startling: an effective ruler cannot always follow conventional Christian morality. A prince must be willing to use cruelty when necessary, to deceive when advantageous, and to prioritize the stability of his state over his personal virtue. This was a genuinely new and provocative argument, and The Prince became one of the most read and debated political texts of the early modern period.

Renaissance humanism spread beyond Italy during the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, carried by students who traveled to Italy, by printed books, and by Italian artists and scholars who found patrons in northern Europe. The northern Renaissance combined classical scholarship with Christian reform more intensely than its Italian counterpart. Desiderius Erasmus of Rotterdam was the most celebrated northern humanist. His critical edition of the Greek New Testament (1516) applied humanist philological methods to scripture itself, demonstrating textual errors in the standard Latin Vulgate. His satirical works — Praise of Folly (1511), the Colloquies — skewered clerical ignorance and papal corruption with wit and learning. Erasmus wanted to reform the Church from within; he resisted Luther's break, but his intellectual work helped prepare the ground for the Reformation.

Section 33.3

Printing and the Spread of Ideas

Few technological developments in world history have had consequences as rapid and far-reaching as the introduction of movable-type printing to Europe. The technology had existed in China since the eleventh century — Bi Sheng developed movable type around 1040 CE — and had been adapted and used in Korea and elsewhere in East Asia. But the European printing press developed by Johannes Gutenberg in Mainz around 1450 arrived at a moment of particular intellectual ferment and spread with extraordinary speed through a continent whose urban, commercial, and literate class was actively hungry for texts.

Gutenberg's innovation was not movable type alone — that concept had earlier precedents — but the combination of durable cast metal type, an oil-based ink that adhered to metal, and a screw press adapted from those used in winemaking. The result was a machine capable of producing several hundred pages per hour, compared to the few pages per day achievable by the most skilled scribes. The economic implications were immediately apparent: printed books cost a fraction of manuscript books, and the market for them expanded accordingly.

The speed of adoption was remarkable. By 1500 — just fifty years after Gutenberg's press — there were printing establishments in more than 250 European cities and towns, and something in the range of fifteen to twenty million individual books had been printed. The printing industry was driven by commercial logic: printers produced what people would buy. Classical Latin texts were among the most popular early products. So were works of practical religion: sermons, Books of Hours, lives of the saints. So, eventually, were vernacular texts in German, French, English, Italian, Spanish, and other languages.

The consequences for intellectual and religious life were profound. Before printing, the circulation of ideas depended on the slow, expensive, and error-prone process of manuscript copying. Printing changed all of this. A printed edition produced hundreds of identical copies simultaneously. An idea published in one city could be read in dozens of other cities within weeks. For the Reformation, this was transformative. When Martin Luther circulated his Ninety-Five Theses in 1517, the theses were quickly printed and circulated across the Holy Roman Empire within weeks. Luther was an extraordinarily prolific writer, and the printing press allowed him to reach audiences far beyond what any medieval reformer had been able to reach. In the decade after 1517, Luther's works represented something like a quarter of all books sold in Germany. His German translation of the New Testament (1522), printed in an accessible vernacular style, sold thousands of copies and profoundly shaped the development of written German.

Printing did not simply accelerate the spread of existing ideas — it changed the social dynamics of intellectual production and debate. Before printing, the Church could exercise significant control over what texts were copied and circulated. Printing broke this monopoly. Heretical or controversial texts could be printed in one city and smuggled into another. Pamphleteers could respond to a published argument within days. Popular audiences who could not afford manuscripts could now afford cheap printed pamphlets. Woodcut illustrations made religious and political arguments accessible to readers with limited literacy. The printing press was, in effect, the first mass medium in European history.

Not all the consequences of print culture were intellectually liberating. Print also enabled the spread of false information, virulent propaganda, and inflammatory denunciations. Anti-Semitic pamphlets circulated widely in the sixteenth century, contributing to persecution. Sensationalized accounts of alleged witchcraft helped fuel the great witch trials that swept Europe in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. Rulers and the Church quickly recognized the medium's power and attempted to regulate it — censorship lists, licensing requirements, and prior restraint were all imposed — but controlling what could be printed across a fragmented political landscape proved extraordinarily difficult.

Historical Vocabulary
Vernacular

The vernacular refers to the everyday spoken and written language of a region, as distinct from Latin, which was the official language of the medieval Church and of learned discourse across Europe. Medieval Europeans spoke dozens of regional vernaculars — forms of German, French, Italian, Spanish, English, and many others — but serious religious, legal, and scholarly writing was done in Latin.

The Reformation dramatically accelerated the use of vernacular in religious life: Luther translated the Bible into German, Tyndale translated it into English, and Calvin wrote his Institutes in French as well as Latin. By making scripture and theology accessible in languages ordinary people spoke, Reformation translators helped democratize religious knowledge — and inadvertently contributed to the standardization of European national languages. The long-term consequences for the development of national languages — and eventually for nationalist identity — were significant, though no one in the sixteenth century intended this outcome.

Section 33.4

The Protestant Reformation

The Protestant Reformation is one of the most consequential events in European history — and one of the most complex. It was not a single movement with a single set of causes but a cluster of religious, social, political, and intellectual developments that intersected explosively in the early sixteenth century.

The immediate trigger is conventionally placed in 1517, when Martin Luther, an Augustinian friar and professor of theology at the University of Wittenberg, composed a document challenging the Church's sale of indulgences. Indulgences were official certificates remitting some of the temporal punishment due for sins — originally associated with crusading activities, they had evolved into a financial instrument. In 1517, Pope Leo X was using indulgences to finance the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses attacked the theology of indulgences and by extension the papal authority that endorsed them.

Luther's immediate theological point grew into something much larger. He argued, drawing on his intensive study of Paul's Letter to the Romans, that salvation — justification before God — came not through the accumulated merit of good works, sacramental rituals, and ecclesiastical mediation, but through faith alone, which itself was a gift of God's grace. This doctrine of sola fide (faith alone) was not entirely new, but Luther's articulation of it in the context of his attack on indulgences and his growing critique of papal authority was explosive. If salvation depended on faith alone, then the entire medieval system of sacramental mediation — confession, penance, indulgences, masses for the dead — became theologically questionable. If scripture alone (sola scriptura) was the ultimate authority for Christian life, as Luther also argued, then papal decrees and church traditions that went beyond or contradicted scripture lost their authority.

The political situation in the Holy Roman Empire was crucial to Luther's survival and success. The Emperor Charles V summoned Luther to the Diet of Worms in 1521 and demanded he recant his writings. Luther refused — tradition records his famous declaration that he could not go against conscience or scripture — and Charles declared him an outlaw. But several German princes offered Luther protection. Frederick the Wise of Saxony arranged for Luther to be hidden in Wartburg Castle, where he translated the New Testament into German. The support of sympathetic princes was essential: without political protection, Luther would likely have faced the fate of Jan Hus, burned at the Council of Constance in 1415.

The Lutheran movement spread quickly through German cities and territories, partly because of printing, partly because Luther addressed genuine popular grievances against the Church, and partly because the Reformation's message resonated differently with different audiences. For urban merchants and craftsmen, the Reformation's emphasis on scripture, literacy, and individual conscience spoke to a literate, commercially active culture. For some German princes, it offered an opportunity to seize Church properties, reduce payments to Rome, and assert greater independence from imperial and papal authority. For common people in rural areas, the Reformation's message sometimes merged with older traditions of popular grievance — the German Peasants' War of 1524–1525, the largest popular uprising in European history before the French Revolution, drew on Reformation language to articulate demands for the abolition of serfdom and reduction of feudal obligations. Luther himself, horrified by the social radicalism of the peasants' movement, urged the princes to suppress it with force — and they did, with great brutality.

Other reformers drew on Luther's initial challenge but developed in different directions. Ulrich Zwingli in Zurich developed a Reformed Protestantism more austere than Luther's, insisting on the removal of images, music, and traditional ceremonies from church worship. John Calvin (1509–1564), a French theologian who established himself in Geneva, developed the most systematically influential Reformed tradition. Calvin's Institutes of the Christian Religion presented a comprehensive theological system centered on the absolute sovereignty of God. His doctrine of predestination — that God had eternally determined who would be saved (the elect) and who would be damned — was among the most discussed and disputed aspects of his thought. Calvin established in Geneva a model of reformed Christian community that combined rigorous church discipline, active city government, and a distinctive moral culture. Calvinist ideas and institutions spread from Geneva to France (where Calvinist Protestants were called Huguenots), Scotland (where they became Presbyterians), the Netherlands, England, and eventually to the English colonies in North America.

The most radical wing of the Reformation — the Anabaptists — went further than Luther or Calvin in rejecting established church institutions. Anabaptists insisted that only adult believers who consciously chose to be baptized should receive the sacrament; they rejected the practice of infant baptism. Many also rejected the fusion of church and state, insisted on strict separation from the surrounding society, and practiced pacifism. They were fiercely persecuted — by Catholic authorities, by Lutheran princes, and by Calvinist governments alike — and tens of thousands were executed. Anabaptist communities survived in scattered locations and formed the ancestors of later groups including the Mennonites and the Amish.

In England, the Reformation took a distinctive path driven more by royal politics than by popular theology. Henry VIII broke with Rome in the 1530s primarily because the pope refused to annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon, who had not produced a male heir. The Act of Supremacy (1534) declared the king the "Supreme Head" of the Church of England. The English Reformation remained theologically and liturgically conservative under Henry, became strongly Protestant under Edward VI, temporarily returned to Roman Catholicism under Mary I, and settled into a distinctive Protestant compromise under Elizabeth I.

Historical Vocabulary
Sola Scriptura

Sola scriptura — Latin for "scripture alone" — was one of the central doctrines of the Protestant Reformation, associated especially with Martin Luther. Luther argued that the Bible, properly interpreted, was the sole ultimate authority for Christian faith and practice, overriding papal decrees, church councils, and accumulated traditions. This was a radical position because the medieval Catholic Church had insisted that scripture and church tradition together constituted the basis of Christian authority, with the pope and councils having the authority to interpret both.

Sola scriptura not only challenged papal authority; it also raised an immediate and difficult question: if scripture alone is authoritative, who interprets it? Luther's answer was that every literate believer could read scripture — which was one reason he translated it into German — but the proliferation of Protestant denominations, each claiming scriptural authority for different positions, demonstrated that sola scriptura generated interpretation conflicts as much as it resolved them. The diversity of early modern Protestantism — Lutheran, Calvinist, Zwinglian, Anglican, Anabaptist — was in large part a consequence of this interpretive openness.

Section 33.5

Catholic Reform and Religious Conflict

The Protestant Reformation did not go unanswered. The Catholic Church responded with a combination of internal reform, institutional reorganization, theological clarification, and renewed efforts to spread Christianity to new populations in Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Historians call this response the Catholic Reformation — sometimes also called the Counter-Reformation, emphasizing its reactive dimension, though the internal reform impulse had roots that predated Luther.

Internal reform pressures had existed within Catholicism long before Luther. The calls for reform at church councils in the fifteenth century had produced limited results but established a tradition of conciliar reform advocacy. New religious orders had emerged in the early sixteenth century committed to rigorous personal piety, charitable work, and clerical education.

The most important new religious order of the Catholic Reformation was the Society of Jesus, or Jesuits, founded by Ignatius of Loyola. Ignatius developed a distinctive spirituality centered on rigorous self-examination, imaginative meditation, and complete obedience to the pope — a spirituality he articulated in his Spiritual Exercises. The Jesuits were founded in 1534 and received papal approval in 1540. They became the most dynamic force in the Catholic Reformation: establishing schools and colleges across Europe (Jesuit education became among the best available), serving as missionaries in Asia, Africa, and the Americas, and combating Protestantism through preaching, scholarship, and personal persuasion. Jesuit missionaries reached Japan, China, India, Brazil, and New France, adapting their methods to local cultures with considerable sophistication.

The most systematic Catholic response to the Reformation was the Council of Trent (1545–1563), convened by Pope Paul III. The Council met in three sessions over nearly two decades and addressed both doctrinal questions and questions of church discipline. On doctrine, the Council systematically rejected Protestant positions: it affirmed that both scripture and church tradition constituted the basis of Christian authority; it affirmed the seven sacraments; it defended the role of good works alongside faith in salvation; it reaffirmed the doctrine of transubstantiation; and it endorsed the Vulgate as the authoritative Latin Bible. On discipline, the Council required bishops to reside in their dioceses, required the establishment of seminaries to train priests, and tightened standards for clerical behavior.

Religious conflict in early modern Europe produced some of its most destructive violence. The French Wars of Religion (1562–1598) — a series of eight civil wars between Catholic and Huguenot factions — devastated France for a generation. The conflict culminated in the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre of August 1572, in which several thousand Huguenots gathered in Paris for a royal wedding were killed by Catholic mobs over several days, with violence spreading to provincial cities. The massacre shocked Protestant Europe and intensified arguments about the right of subjects to resist tyrannical rulers. The conflict was resolved only when Henry of Navarre converted to Catholicism to claim the French throne and issued the Edict of Nantes (1598), granting Huguenots limited but real religious tolerance.

The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) was the most destructive of the early modern religious conflicts, devastating central Europe — especially the German territories of the Holy Roman Empire — and killing perhaps a third of the German population through battle, disease, and famine. It began as a conflict between Protestant and Catholic German princes but gradually drew in most of the major European powers — Denmark, Sweden, France, and Spain — making it as much a struggle for European power politics as a religious war. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) that ended it recognized Calvinism alongside Lutheranism and Catholicism as legitimate religions within the Empire, and established principles of territorial sovereignty that historians of international relations have long treated as foundational to the modern state system.

Religious conflict was not only interstate warfare. Within communities, the Reformation generated denunciation, persecution, forced conversion, and everyday violence. The witch trials that peaked in Europe between roughly 1560 and 1630 — resulting in tens of thousands of executions, the majority of victims women — were partly driven by the intense religious anxieties of the Reformation period, when Catholic and Protestant communities alike perceived the devil as an active threat. The geography of witch trials concentrated in regions of intense religious conflict and institutional instability, suggesting that religious uncertainty and social tension contributed to the persecutions.

Section 33.6

Monarchies and State Centralization

One of the most important political developments of the early modern period was the gradual consolidation of monarchical power and the emergence of what historians call the "sovereign state" — a political unit characterized by supreme authority over a defined territory, a monopoly on legitimate use of force, and a complex administrative apparatus. This process was neither uniform nor uncontested across Europe. Different kingdoms pursued centralization by different means, at different rates, and with different results. But by the mid-seventeenth century, a significant shift had occurred in how political power was organized, claimed, and exercised across much of Europe.

In France, the process of monarchical centralization accelerated from the reign of Francis I (r. 1515–1547) through the long reign of Louis XIV (r. 1643–1715). French kings faced enormous obstacles: a powerful nobility jealous of its privileges, a church with its own jurisdictions and revenues, a system of regional courts (parlements) that could resist royal edicts, and a country deeply divided by religion. Successive French monarchs used a combination of patronage, coercion, ideological claim, and administrative innovation to extend royal authority. Louis XIV brought this process to its most elaborate expression. By requiring the great nobles to reside at the Palace of Versailles, he transformed them from regional power brokers into courtiers dependent on royal favor. By building a large, loyal bureaucracy of officials who owed their positions to the crown rather than to inherited status, he expanded royal administration throughout the kingdom. By maintaining one of Europe's largest standing armies — eventually numbering several hundred thousand men — he projected military power both domestically and across Europe.

The theory of divine-right monarchy — the claim that kings received their authority directly from God and were accountable only to God — provided ideological justification for absolutism. Bishop Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet, court preacher to Louis XIV, elaborated this theory in its most sophisticated form, arguing from scripture and history that divinely appointed kings were the source of all secular authority and that subjects had no right to resist.

Not all European states followed the French model. England developed along a very different trajectory. The Tudor monarchs were powerful and effective, but they governed through Parliament, not against it. Parliament's control over taxation gave it leverage over the crown that French regional assemblies lacked. When the Stuart kings — James I and Charles I — attempted to extend royal prerogative in the early seventeenth century, the result was constitutional crisis. Charles I dissolved Parliament, attempted to rule without it, and eventually triggered a civil war (1642–1651) that ended with his trial and execution. The English settlement — limited constitutional monarchy — emerged from the Glorious Revolution of 1688, which brought William of Orange to the throne with parliamentary conditions attached. It offered a fundamentally different model of state organization than French absolutism.

In the Holy Roman Empire, strong central authority was absent, and state formation occurred at the level of the component territories. German princes built sophisticated administrative systems within their domains but the Empire as a whole remained politically fragmented. Prussia, under the Hohenzollern dynasty, became the most aggressively centralized German state by the late seventeenth century. Russia followed yet another trajectory: Ivan the Terrible (r. 1547–1584) expanded Muscovite power, while Peter the Great (r. 1682–1725) dramatically accelerated Russian state modernization, forcing the adoption of Western administrative and military models and projecting Russian power into European affairs.

Historical Vocabulary
Sovereignty

Sovereignty is one of the most important concepts in the history of political thought and international relations. In its early modern form, sovereignty referred to the supreme, undivided authority to govern a territory — an authority that recognized no higher human authority. Medieval political thought had involved multiple, overlapping sources of authority — pope, emperor, king, nobles, church, guilds, towns — each with its own jurisdiction. Early modern political theorists like Jean Bodin (1530–1596), writing in the context of France's religious civil wars, argued that a stable political order required a single, undivided sovereign power.

The Peace of Westphalia (1648) helped institutionalize the idea that sovereign states were the primary actors in international relations — each supreme within its territory and legally equal to other sovereign states. This concept of Westphalian sovereignty is often cited as a foundation of the modern international system, though historians debate how novel it actually was in 1648. What is clear is that the concept provided a new vocabulary for thinking about political authority that distinguished early modern from medieval political culture.

Section 33.7

War, Taxation, and Bureaucracy

The relationship between war, taxation, and bureaucracy was central to early modern state formation. Historians have described this relationship with the formula "war made the state, and the state made war" — and while this is an oversimplification, it captures something important about the period.

Warfare in early modern Europe was becoming progressively more expensive. The military revolution — a term historians use to describe the dramatic changes in European military organization and technology between roughly 1500 and 1700 — involved the development of gunpowder weapons (muskets, cannon), the construction of elaborate new defensive fortifications, and the dramatic growth in the size of armies. Where medieval armies had typically numbered in the thousands, early modern armies numbered in the tens and eventually hundreds of thousands. Maintaining, equipping, feeding, and paying such forces required sustained, reliable, and substantial revenues — far beyond what medieval systems of feudal obligation and occasional taxation could provide.

To finance war, rulers needed to tax their subjects more heavily and more regularly. To collect taxes effectively, they needed bureaucracies — trained officials capable of assessing wealth, collecting revenue, keeping records, and handling the legal complexities of fiscal administration. To borrow money against future tax revenues, they needed financial systems capable of handling government credit. The growth of taxation, bureaucracy, and public finance were thus intertwined consequences of military demand.

The fiscal-military state — a state organized primarily around the capacity to finance and wage war — was a defining feature of early modern European politics. France under Louis XIV maintained an army of several hundred thousand men, financed through a complex and often inequitable system of direct and indirect taxes that fell disproportionately on peasants and commoners, since the nobility retained tax exemptions. England developed a different model: after the Glorious Revolution (1688), the Bank of England (founded 1694) and a reformed system of parliamentary taxation gave the English state the capacity to borrow cheaply and reliably, funding wars that its smaller population might not otherwise have been able to sustain.

Bureaucratic development varied widely across Europe. France under Louis XIV employed tens of thousands of royal officials — intendants deployed across the provinces to supervise taxation, justice, and local administration — increasingly trained professionals rather than feudal appointees, though they often purchased their offices and passed them to their heirs. This created a professional administrative class with its own interests, loyalties, and culture.

Resistance to taxation and bureaucratic expansion was widespread and sometimes violent. Tax revolts, food riots, and regional rebellions punctuated early modern European history. In France, the Fronde (1648–1653) temporarily challenged royal authority before Louis XIV suppressed it and began his long personal rule. In England, resistance to royal taxation without parliamentary consent was one of the central grievances of the Civil War. Ordinary people were not passive subjects of state-building processes; they negotiated, evaded, petitioned, and sometimes openly resisted the demands of expanding states.

The cultural dimensions of monarchical power were as important as its administrative and military dimensions. Early modern courts were theaters of royal magnificence in which rulers displayed their power, taste, and legitimacy through art, architecture, ceremony, and spectacle. Louis XIV's Palace of Versailles — a vast complex of buildings, gardens, and galleries designed to demonstrate the glory of the Sun King — became the model for royal courts across Europe. Court culture served political purposes: by centralizing the great nobles at court and involving them in elaborate ritual and ceremonial life, rulers like Louis XIV kept potential rivals occupied, dependent, and under surveillance.

Regional Lens

The Dutch Republic and a Different Path

While much of Europe moved toward monarchical absolutism, the Dutch Republic demonstrated that a powerful, commercially sophisticated, and militarily effective state could be organized without a king. The northern provinces of the Low Countries revolted against Spanish Habsburg rule beginning in 1568, provoked by Spanish attempts to suppress Calvinism, impose new taxes, and curtail traditional urban privileges. By the late sixteenth century, the northern provinces had established effective independence, formally recognized in the Peace of Westphalia (1648).

The Dutch Republic was governed by a complex system involving provincial estates, town councils, and a central States-General in which the province of Holland, by far the wealthiest, was dominant. Real power was exercised by the regents — wealthy urban merchants and landowners who dominated provincial and city governments. This merchant-republic structure had important consequences: it made the Dutch Republic exceptionally responsive to commercial interests, unusually tolerant of religious diversity, and remarkably hospitable to intellectual innovation. Amsterdam in the seventeenth century was home to Rembrandt, Spinoza, and Descartes.

The Dutch commercial empire — built on the Dutch East India Company (VOC, founded 1602) and the Dutch West India Company (WIC, founded 1621) — was the most sophisticated trading enterprise of the early modern world. The VOC controlled the spice trade from the Indonesian archipelago, established a base at Batavia (modern Jakarta), and dominated the maritime trade of the Indian Ocean for much of the seventeenth century. At its height in the mid-seventeenth century, the Dutch merchant fleet was larger than the fleets of England, France, and Germany combined. Dutch financial innovations — including joint-stock companies, commodity exchanges, public banks, and sophisticated systems of credit and insurance — provided models that England and other powers would adopt and adapt. The Dutch Republic shows that early modern state formation was not a single template but a range of possible outcomes depending on commercial wealth, religious identity, and political tradition.

Section 33.8

Science, Exploration, and Ambition

The period between 1450 and 1750 saw dramatic changes in European understanding of the natural world. The set of developments historians call the Scientific Revolution — centered in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries — transformed astronomy, physics, anatomy, chemistry, and the methods through which Europeans investigated nature. At the same time, European maritime exploration opened the world's oceans and connected Europe to distant continents and civilizations. These two sets of developments were related in important ways, both driven by the same culture of inquiry, ambition, and willingness to challenge received authority.

The Scientific Revolution is best understood not as a sudden break with all previous knowledge but as a transformation of methods, frameworks, and assumptions that built on — while departing from — earlier traditions. Medieval European natural philosophy had been rooted largely in Aristotle, whose works had been recovered and systematized by scholars like Thomas Aquinas. Aristotelian physics assumed a qualitatively different cosmos: heavy things fell to earth because earth was their natural place; the heavens were composed of a different, perfect substance and moved in perfect circles; the earth was stationary at the center of the universe. Ptolemy's geocentric astronomical system, preserved and transmitted through Islamic scholarship, provided a mathematically sophisticated model of planetary motion that worked well enough for calendar calculations and astrological prediction.

It was a Polish cathedral canon, Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543), who first published a systematic argument for a heliocentric (sun-centered) model of the solar system. Copernicus's De revolutionibus orbium coelestium (1543) argued that mathematical simplicity and elegance were served better by placing the sun at the center and having the earth orbit it. Copernicus's system was not immediately adopted — it conflicted with common sense, Aristotelian physics, and biblical passages — but it launched a debate that transformed astronomy over the following century.

Tycho Brahe (1546–1601), a Danish astronomer with exceptional observational skills, spent decades amassing precise measurements of planetary positions — the most accurate pre-telescope observations in history. Brahe's data were inherited by Johannes Kepler (1571–1630), a German mathematician who used them to demonstrate that planetary orbits were ellipses, not circles — a discovery that undermined both Ptolemaic and Copernican models and required new physics to explain. Kepler's three laws of planetary motion provided a precise mathematical description of how planets moved.

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) contributed to the Scientific Revolution in multiple ways. He used the newly invented telescope to observe the heavens and found evidence that undermined geocentrism: Jupiter had moons orbiting it, the moon had mountains and craters, and the sun had spots. He also made fundamental contributions to physics, developing new approaches to motion, acceleration, and falling bodies. Galileo's conflict with the Roman Inquisition — condemned in 1633 for supporting Copernicanism — became one of the most famous episodes in the history of science, though its significance has been both overstated and oversimplified by later commentators.

The synthesis of the Scientific Revolution was achieved by Isaac Newton (1643–1727), whose Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687) proposed a single mathematical framework — the laws of motion and universal gravitation — that explained both terrestrial mechanics and celestial mechanics. Newton's system was extraordinarily comprehensive and precise, and its success repeatedly confirmed by observation and calculation — establishing mathematics as the primary language of natural science and providing the model for what scientific explanation should look like.

The Scientific Revolution was not only about astronomy and physics. Andreas Vesalius transformed anatomy with detailed, accurate illustrations overturning many long-accepted claims from Galen. William Harvey demonstrated through careful experiment that blood circulated through the body, pumped by the heart. Robert Boyle developed a mechanical, experimental approach to chemistry. New instruments — telescope, microscope, thermometer, barometer, air pump — extended the range of human observation. Scientific societies like the Royal Society of London (founded 1660) and the French Académie des Sciences (founded 1666) institutionalized a community of scholars who shared methods, findings, and criticism.

It is essential to note that the Scientific Revolution drew on non-European intellectual traditions in important ways. Islamic scholars had preserved, translated, and extended Greek mathematics and astronomy, developing algebra, trigonometry, and sophisticated astronomical tables that European scholars depended on. The Arabic numeral system — itself developed from Indian mathematics — was fundamental to the mathematical work of the Scientific Revolution. European contact with Chinese, Indian, and American natural worlds brought new plants, animals, and substances into European knowledge systems. The history of science is not a purely European story.

Section 33.9

Europe's Changing Global Role

Between 1450 and 1750, Europe's position in the world changed dramatically. At the start of this period, western Europeans were participants in a world trading system dominated by Asian and Middle Eastern powers, with access to the most valuable luxury goods only through costly intermediaries. By 1750, European powers — especially Portugal, Spain, the Dutch Republic, England, and France — had established direct maritime connections to West Africa, the Indian Ocean, the Americas, and the Pacific, and had built colonial empires of varying degrees of control and exploitation across much of the globe.

The Portuguese were pioneers of European maritime expansion. Beginning in the early fifteenth century under the patronage of Prince Henry the Navigator, Portuguese sailors systematically explored the West African coast, driven by a combination of commercial ambition, religious motives, and geographic curiosity. Portuguese navigators reached the Azores and Cape Verde Islands, the mouth of the Congo River, and eventually, under Bartolomeu Dias (1488), rounded the Cape of Good Hope at the southern tip of Africa. Vasco da Gama reached India in 1498, completing the sea route that bypassed the Ottoman-controlled overland routes and gave Portugal direct access to the spice trade. Portuguese commercial stations (feitorias) stretched from West Africa to the Persian Gulf, India, Southeast Asia, China, and Japan.

Spain's encounter with the Americas — begun with Columbus's 1492 voyage under Castilian patronage — had consequences of a different order of magnitude. American silver, extracted from mines in Peru (Potosí) and Mexico (Zacatecas) by forced Indigenous and African labor, flooded into the European economy through Spain, driving inflation (the "price revolution" of the sixteenth century) and providing the monetary fuel for expanding European trade. New food crops — potatoes, maize, tomatoes, cacao, and others — gradually transformed European diets and agricultural systems, contributing to population growth.

The expansion of European maritime reach was facilitated by the same intellectual and technological culture that drove the Renaissance and the Scientific Revolution. Improved navigational instruments — the astrolabe, the cross-staff — allowed sailors to determine latitude at sea. Better maps and charts, drawing on both European and non-European geographic knowledge, made ocean navigation more feasible. Improved ship designs — including the Portuguese caravel, capable of sailing against the wind — expanded what was possible.

European expansion was neither peaceful nor benign. In the Americas, European conquest brought catastrophic disease, enslavement, forced labor, cultural destruction, and demographic collapse to Indigenous peoples. In Africa, European maritime presence fed the Atlantic slave trade, which eventually transported millions of enslaved Africans to the Americas and transformed African societies in complex and often devastating ways. In Asia, European trading presence was initially more limited and less coercive — Asian states were powerful enough to resist European conquest — but it grew steadily more intrusive.

The relationship between European intellectual culture and maritime expansion was complex. Renaissance humanism had celebrated human achievement and opened European minds to the diversity of the world's peoples. At the same time, the encounter with the Americas, Africa, and Asia generated cultural frameworks of European superiority — racial categories, religious hierarchies — that provided ideological cover for conquest, exploitation, and enslavement. The same culture that produced Leonardo da Vinci and Erasmus also produced the justifications for the Atlantic slave trade and the destruction of Indigenous American civilizations.

Global Connection

The Ottoman Empire, European State Formation, and the World System

The Ottoman Empire is usually absent from narratives of the European Renaissance and Reformation — appearing mainly as an external threat or as an obstacle to European trade routes. But the relationship between Europe and the Ottoman Empire was far more complex and consequential.

The Ottoman Empire at its height under Suleiman the Magnificent (r. 1520–1566) was one of the most powerful, sophisticated, and culturally rich civilizations in the world. Its territory spanned southeastern Europe, Anatolia, the Arab Middle East, Egypt, and North Africa. Its capital Constantinople (renamed Istanbul) was one of the world's great cities — larger than any European city of the period, a cosmopolitan center of trade, scholarship, and artistic production. The Ottoman state was, by the standards of the time, remarkably tolerant of religious diversity: its Jewish and Christian subjects were recognized as protected communities with rights to practice their own religions.

The Ottoman threat — real and perceived — shaped European political development in important ways. Fear of Ottoman expansion was one factor driving European states to build larger armies and more effective fiscal systems. Ottoman pressure on Charles V's eastern frontier repeatedly distracted the Emperor from suppressing Lutheranism, contributing indirectly to the Reformation's success. At the same time, European states — particularly France — sometimes allied with the Ottomans against common Habsburg enemies, demonstrating that religious identity did not always override political calculation. Ottoman textiles, ceramics, and decorative arts influenced European artistic culture. Ottoman scholarship preserved and extended Greek and Arabic scientific traditions that were crucial inputs to the European Scientific Revolution. The Ottoman Empire was a global presence that early modern Europe could not ignore and that shaped European development in ways a purely internal European history cannot capture.

Religion in Europe, c. 1600

By 1600, Europe was dramatically divided along religious lines. Students examining a religious map of Europe at this date should note several geographic patterns. Lutheran regions concentrated primarily in northern and central German territories — Saxony, Brandenburg, Prussia — and in Scandinavia. Calvinist (Reformed) regions were more scattered: the Swiss cantons of Geneva and Zurich, the Dutch provinces, Scotland, and significant minorities in France and Poland-Lithuania. Calvinism's international character — Geneva as a hub for refugee ministers and printed texts — gave it a distinctive transnational quality.

Catholic regions retained most of southern Europe — France (officially Catholic despite a large Huguenot minority), the Iberian kingdoms, Italy, the Austrian Habsburg lands, Poland-Lithuania, and Ireland. Anglican England occupied a distinctive middle position — Protestant in theology but retaining bishops and a modified liturgy. Orthodox Christianity remained dominant in Russia, southeastern Europe under Ottoman rule, and among the Greek and Slavic populations of the Balkans. The Ottoman-controlled Balkans illustrated the Empire's distinctive approach to religious diversity, permitting recognized Christian communities to govern their own internal affairs under the millet system.

Primary Source Window

Martin Luther, "Address to the Christian Nobility of the German Nation" (1520)

Creator: Martin Luther · Date: 1520 · Type: Political-religious treatise; vernacular pamphlet; public address

Context: In 1520, three years after his Ninety-Five Theses, Martin Luther published three major treatises that radicalized his position. "Address to the Christian Nobility of the German Nation" was written in German rather than Latin — a deliberate choice that signaled its intended audience — and addressed to the secular princes and nobles of Germany. It was one of the bestselling printed works of the sixteenth century.

Luther argued that the papacy had erected three "walls" preventing reform: the claim that spiritual authority was superior to secular authority; the claim that only the pope could interpret scripture; and the claim that only the pope could call a church council. He challenged all three walls. He argued that all baptized Christians were priests — the "priesthood of all believers" — and that secular rulers therefore had both the right and the duty to reform the Church when church authorities failed to do so. The text was a direct appeal to German national sentiment against Italian papal power, calling on German princes to act against clerical abuses, papal taxation, and Roman corruption.

Analysis: This source reveals how quickly Luther's challenge moved beyond academic theology to political confrontation. By writing in German and addressing secular rulers directly, Luther was mobilizing popular and political support rather than engaging in internal church debate. His appeal to German national pride against Roman "tyranny" resonated with princes who had practical grievances about payments to Rome and with common people who resented clerical privilege. The source shows how the Reformation was simultaneously a theological movement, a media event (widely printed), and a political manifesto.

Limits: This source represents Luther's perspective and rhetorical strategy at a particular moment in 1520. It cannot tell us how German princes or ordinary readers actually received and used these arguments, or what range of interpretations they brought to it. Other sources — church visitation reports, peasant petitions, princely correspondence — are needed to reconstruct the reception of Luther's ideas across German society.

Archaeology, Artifact, and Material Evidence Window

Material Culture and the Reformation: Iconoclasm and Church Interiors

The evidence: In Reformed Protestant churches across Switzerland, the Netherlands, and parts of Germany, historians find churches stripped of images — altarpieces removed or painted over, statues destroyed or removed, stained glass replaced with clear glass, walls whitewashed. This physical transformation of sacred space is documented through surviving churches, through written accounts of iconoclastic episodes, and through art-historical analysis of damaged or altered images.

What historians can infer: The physical stripping of church interiors was one of the most dramatic visible changes of the Reformation. Reformed Protestantism, drawing on the Second Commandment's prohibition of graven images, insisted that images of saints and sacred figures had no place in Christian worship. The deliberate destruction of images (iconoclasm) was not only a theological statement; it was a social and political act, sometimes carried out by crowds in moments of intense communal excitement, sometimes organized by reformed city governments. The surviving evidence of whitewashed interiors and damaged sculptures preserves physical traces of these transformations and allows historians to map the geographic spread of Reformed iconoclasm.

Limits of the evidence: Material evidence tells us what happened to church interiors but not necessarily why individual participants in iconoclastic episodes acted as they did — what combination of theological conviction, social grievance, excitement, or opportunism motivated them. Written accounts survive, but they are often written by partisans on one side or another. The diversity of motivations among iconoclasts — devout reformers, opportunistic looters, people settling scores — resists simple generalization from the material evidence alone.

Historical Debate

Was the Reformation a Popular Movement or an Elite Imposition?

The question: To what extent was the Protestant Reformation driven by popular demand and popular participation, and to what extent was it imposed from above by princes, city councils, and educated reformers?

Interpretation A — The Reformation as a Popular Movement: Some historians emphasize the popular dimensions of the Reformation. The German Peasants' War (1524–1525) demonstrated that Reformation language resonated with rural communities who connected Lutheran ideas about Christian freedom with long-standing social grievances about serfdom, taxation, and noble privilege. Urban artisans and craftsmen — the people who bought Luther's pamphlets, attended Reformation sermons, and enforced iconoclastic episodes — were active participants rather than passive recipients. The Reformation's message about the priesthood of all believers, the authority of scripture over clerical hierarchy, and the corruption of the Roman Church spoke to popular anticlericalism that was centuries old.
Interpretation B — The Reformation as an Elite and State-Driven Process: Other historians emphasize the role of princes, city councils, and educated reformers in directing and controlling the Reformation from above. Luther himself, horrified by the Peasants' War, explicitly rejected the social radicalism that some popular readers had drawn from his ideas. Reformed churches were established in territories not because popular majorities demanded it but because princes and city councils made political decisions to adopt the new faith, often for reasons that mixed religious conviction with political calculation — seizing church property, asserting independence from imperial or papal authority. The Peace of Augsburg's principle of cuius regio, eius religio gave princes the legal authority to determine the religion of their territories — a profoundly top-down settlement.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on extensive evidence. Printed pamphlets, sermon records, church visitation reports, legal records, and narrative chronicles document both popular engagement with Reformation ideas and the decisive role of political authorities in determining the outcome. The German Peasants' War is the most dramatic evidence for popular appropriation of Reformation language, while the geography of confessional division — correlating strongly with the decisions of princes and city councils — supports the elite-imposition interpretation.

Why it matters: The debate bears on broader questions about the relationship between ideas and social power, between elite and popular culture, and about the nature of religious change. If the Reformation was primarily popular, it suggests the agency of ordinary people in historical transformation. If it was primarily elite-driven, it suggests the limits of popular agency in the face of state power — a question as relevant to contemporary politics as to sixteenth-century Europe.

Comparison Across Societies

State Formation in France, England, and the Ottoman Empire

Early modern state formation was not a uniquely European process. A comparison of France, England, and the Ottoman Empire illustrates different paths to building powerful states in the same broad period.

France — Absolutist Monarchy France moved toward absolutism, with Louis XIV as its emblematic expression. The French model centralized power in the crown, built a large bureaucracy of royal officials, maintained a huge standing army, and used court culture at Versailles to subordinate the nobility. French absolutism rested on claims of divine right and on the practical capacity to coerce and co-opt potential rivals. But it retained significant tensions: the parlements, the Catholic Church, and the nobility all had customary rights that even Louis XIV could not entirely override.
England — Constitutional Monarchy England developed a more contested system in which Parliament retained significant power through control over taxation. The English Civil War and Glorious Revolution established constitutional limits on royal authority. The English model separated legislative and executive power in ways French absolutism did not, creating a system capable of generating political consensus and reliable public finance — eventually enabling England to punch above its demographic weight in European and global competition.
The Ottoman Empire — Islamic Imperial Administration The Ottoman sultan claimed both political sovereignty and Islamic religious authority. The Ottoman administrative system used distinctive institutions: the devshirme (levy of Christian boys trained as soldiers and officials), the Janissaries (elite standing infantry), and a sophisticated system of provincial administration. The Ottoman approach to managing diversity — through the millet system, which allowed recognized religious communities to govern their own internal affairs — provided a form of regulated tolerance quite different from the European wars of religion.

Comparative insight: All three states were engaged in building more effective fiscal and military systems, centralizing authority, and managing diverse populations — but through different institutional arrangements, ideological frameworks, and relationships to religion and social hierarchy. These different paths had long-term consequences: England's constitutional tradition provided a framework for later democratic development, French absolutism generated a revolutionary crisis in 1789, and the Ottoman system faced distinctive challenges from nationalism and European military pressure in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

Multiple Perspectives

The Reformation Experienced Differently

Huguenots in France: For France's Protestant minority, the Reformation was a story of alternating hope, persecution, and survival. The Edict of Nantes (1598) gave limited toleration: the right to worship in designated places, to maintain fortified towns, to hold certain offices. But Huguenot communities lived under constant pressure, and when Louis XIV revoked the Edict in 1685, some 200,000 Huguenots fled France, taking their skills and capital to England, the Dutch Republic, Prussia, and South Africa. For those who remained, forced conversion was the alternative. The Huguenot experience illustrates how religious minorities could be caught between states that treated religious uniformity as a political necessity.
German Peasants: For the rural peasants of the Holy Roman Empire who rose in revolt in 1524–1525, the Reformation offered a language — Christian freedom, the priesthood of all believers, scripture as authority — that they used to articulate long-standing grievances against serfdom, noble privilege, and clerical exploitation. Their Twelve Articles (1525), one of the first printed documents of popular political demands, appealed explicitly to scripture to justify demands for the end of serfdom, the election of local pastors, and the reduction of feudal obligations. Luther's condemnation of the revolt — and its brutal suppression by noble armies — showed how Reformation language could be mobilized for popular purposes and then turned against those who drew out its social implications. Perhaps 100,000 peasants died in the suppression.
Women in Reformed Communities: Women's experiences of the Reformation were complex and varied. In Lutheran territories, the dissolution of convents removed an important institution that had provided educated women with community, authority, and an alternative to marriage. Reformers celebrated marriage and the household as the proper sphere of Christian life — a positive revaluation of marriage but also a reinforcement of domestic constraints. Some women found agency as reformers' wives or in the radical sects where women sometimes preached and led communities. But as reformed churches institutionalized, women's roles narrowed. The Reformation did not emancipate women; it reconfigured gender roles within a persistently patriarchal framework.
Jewish Communities in Early Modern Europe: Jewish communities experienced the period with particular difficulty. Spain's expulsion of Jews in 1492 forced the Sephardic diaspora across the Mediterranean and beyond. Luther, who had hoped Jews might convert to a reformed, scripturally purified Christianity, turned bitterly anti-Semitic when they did not, writing virulently hostile texts against Jews in his later years. Jewish communities found somewhat more hospitable conditions in the Ottoman Empire, in the Dutch Republic, and in Poland-Lithuania, where royal charters protected Jewish autonomy — but they remained subject to discrimination, periodic expulsion, and violence across most of Europe.
Cause and Effect

The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)

Long-Term Causes

Religious division in the Holy Roman Empire — unresolved tensions between Catholic, Lutheran, and Calvinist communities following the Peace of Augsburg (1555), which recognized only Lutheranism (not Calvinism) as a legal alternative to Catholicism; political fragmentation of the Empire and competition among princes for territory and influence; the broader struggle between the Habsburg dynasty and its rivals, including France, Sweden, and the German Protestant princes; growing military capacity and competition among European states.

Short-Term Causes

The Defenestration of Prague (1618), in which Bohemian Protestant nobles threw two Habsburg royal governors from a window — a symbolic act of rebellion against Habsburg-Catholic authority; the election of Frederick V, the Calvinist Elector Palatine, as King of Bohemia, directly challenging Habsburg authority over a predominantly Protestant territory.

The War's Development

What began as a Bohemian religious revolt became a general European war through four phases — Bohemian (1618–1625), Danish (1625–1629), Swedish (1630–1635), and French (1635–1648). Different powers entered for different reasons: Sweden for Protestant solidarity and Baltic ambition; France (Catholic, but fighting the Habsburgs) for political advantage; Spain to support its Habsburg cousins.

Long-Term Consequences

Catastrophic human losses in German territories — some regions lost a third to a half of their population through battle, disease, and famine; the Peace of Westphalia recognized state sovereignty and added Calvinism to recognized religions; the weakening of the Holy Roman Empire as a political entity; the rise of France as dominant continental power; the establishment of diplomatic norms historians cite as foundational to the modern international system.

Historical complexity: The Thirty Years' War cannot be reduced to a religious conflict or a political conflict — it was both simultaneously, and the interaction between religious identity and political interest was different for every participant. Sweden was fighting for Protestant solidarity and Baltic hegemony; France was fighting for political dominance regardless of religion; smaller German princes were fighting for survival and autonomy.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Role of the Church in European Society, 1450–1750

What Changed

  • The unity of western Christianity was permanently broken; Europe was divided among Catholic, Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican, and other Protestant traditions with no prospect of reunification
  • The Church's authority over intellectual and scientific life was seriously contested; the condemnation of Galileo illustrates the attempt to maintain authority, and its eventual failure illustrates the limits
  • Monasteries and convents were dissolved in Protestant territories, removing a major institution of religious life, social service, and female education
  • The relationship between church and state was renegotiated differently in different regions — toward royal control in England, toward Catholic absolutism in France, toward state churches in Lutheran territories
  • New religious orders — especially the Jesuits — transformed Catholic missionary and educational activity, extending the Church's reach across Asia, Africa, and the Americas

What Stayed the Same

  • Christianity remained central to European culture, identity, and social life — the Reformation did not secularize Europe; it produced multiple, intensely committed Christian traditions
  • The social authority of clergy — Protestant ministers as well as Catholic priests — remained significant in local communities
  • Religion continued to shape politics: wars were fought in its name, treaties included religious provisions, and rulers continued to claim divine sanction
  • The subordination of women in religious institutions, while reconfigured, was maintained across Catholic and Protestant traditions

Why the pattern matters: The religious changes of 1450–1750 established a pluralist religious landscape in Europe that, despite tremendous violence, ultimately forced Europeans to grapple with questions about toleration, conscience, and the limits of state power over belief — questions that fed into Enlightenment political thought and eventually into modern conceptions of religious freedom.

Everyday Life and Social History

Ordinary People in the Age of Reformation

For the vast majority of Europeans in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the Reformation was experienced not as a grand historical event but as a series of local changes that affected familiar rhythms of worship, community life, and daily devotion. Parish records, church visitation reports, personal diaries, and legal proceedings allow historians to glimpse how ordinary people experienced religious change — and how varied those experiences were.

In many German Lutheran communities, the change from Catholic to Lutheran practice was gradual and often incomplete. Church visitors commissioned by princes to inspect local parishes in the decades after the Reformation reported widespread persistence of Catholic practices: parishioners who continued to pray to saints, who kept images in their homes, who observed traditional feast days, and who knew little of Lutheran theology. Converting a parish was one thing; converting its parishioners' inner religious life was another and slower process.

The dissolution of monasteries and convents in Protestant territories disrupted not only the lives of monks and nuns but the social welfare systems that religious houses had provided. Hospitals, schools, food distribution to the poor — in many places, these had been organized by religious institutions. Protestant reformers recognized the need for new institutions to replace these functions, and city councils in reformed cities took over some of these responsibilities. But the transition was often incomplete, and poor communities sometimes found themselves worse off immediately after the Reformation than before.

Witchcraft prosecutions — which peaked between roughly 1560 and 1630, resulting in tens of thousands of executions, the majority of victims women — illuminate how religious anxiety could devastate ordinary communities. The vast majority of those accused were women, often elderly, poor, or socially marginal; the accusations typically arose from community conflicts — disputes over property, neighborly animosity, misfortune attributed to supernatural cause. The witch trials were not primarily driven by church authorities — civil courts prosecuted most cases — but they reflected the heightened religious anxiety and institutional disruption of the period.

Daily life was also shaped by the growing commercialization of the economy. The expansion of the putting-out system — in which merchants distributed raw materials to rural households for spinning, weaving, or other processing, then collected the finished goods — drew peasant households into commercial production and brought wage income into communities that had previously been more self-sufficient. This proto-industrialization changed family labor patterns, gender roles, and the relationship between rural communities and urban markets. Women and children became important sources of domestic manufacturing labor. Household economies became more monetized and more dependent on market fluctuations beyond local control.

Environment and Technology

Gunpowder, Fortifications, and the Military Revolution

The military changes of the early modern period — what historians call the "military revolution" — were both driven by and drove significant technological and environmental consequences. The widespread adoption of gunpowder weapons — muskets for infantry, cannon for siege warfare — transformed how battles were fought and how cities were defended.

Medieval fortifications — tall stone walls and towers — had been effective against battering rams and siege towers but were highly vulnerable to cannon fire, which could batter down vertical masonry. The response was the development of a new type of fortification: the trace italienne, or angular bastion fort, in which low, thick earthen and masonry walls were arranged in geometric star patterns, with angular projections (bastions) that allowed defenders to cover every section of wall with fire. These fortifications were enormously expensive to build and maintain but effectively neutralized cannon fire. The construction of bastion forts across Europe consumed enormous quantities of stone, earth, timber, and labor and transformed the landscape of early modern Europe in ways still visible today.

Gunpowder weapons also changed the relationship between social status and military effectiveness. Medieval warfare had emphasized heavily armored knights on horseback — a military role that required years of training and expensive equipment. Firearms could be used effectively by relatively untrained infantrymen after brief instruction. The military revolution thus contributed to the declining military importance of the traditional nobility — though nobles retained officer positions and continued to command — and to the rise of mass infantry armies drawn from the commoner population. The environmental consequences of mass armies were also significant: large forces consumed enormous quantities of food, fodder, and fuel, and their movement across landscapes left trails of destruction that reshaped rural economies and ecological conditions across central Europe, especially during the Thirty Years' War.

Chapter Summary

The period between approximately 1450 and 1750 transformed Europe in ways that had profound consequences for world history. The Renaissance, beginning in Italian city-states and spreading across the continent, produced a cultural movement that celebrated human achievement, classical learning, and artistic excellence. Humanist scholars recovered and reinterpreted Greek and Roman texts, developed new methods of historical and literary criticism, and created a visual culture of striking beauty and complexity. Figures like Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Erasmus, and Machiavelli produced works that have remained central to European cultural identity. But the Renaissance was embedded in systems of commercial wealth, patronage, and social hierarchy — and its celebration of human dignity coexisted with the beginnings of Atlantic colonial exploitation.

The printing press, developed by Gutenberg around 1450, transformed the circulation of ideas with a speed and comprehensiveness that no previous technology had achieved. Within decades, millions of printed books and pamphlets circulated across Europe, breaking the Church's near-monopoly on the production and circulation of written knowledge, enabling vernacular languages to emerge as literary and religious vehicles, and making possible the rapid spread of the Protestant Reformation. Print culture did not simply liberate ideas; it also enabled the spread of propaganda, anti-Semitism, and sensationalism. The printing press was the first European mass medium, and it demonstrated — as all mass media have since — that the democratization of communication generates both enlightenment and darkness.

The Protestant Reformation fractured the religious unity of western Christianity and unleashed more than a century of religious conflict. Martin Luther's theological challenges — justification by faith alone, the authority of scripture alone, the priesthood of all believers — spoke to genuine popular grievances against clerical corruption and to the political ambitions of princes who saw opportunity in religious change. Calvin's Geneva model gave Reformed Protestantism its distinctive character and global reach. More radical reformers — the Anabaptists and others — pushed the implications of Reformation theology further than Luther or Calvin would tolerate. The Catholic Reformation responded with the Council of Trent, new religious orders like the Jesuits, and a reinvigorated missionary program that spread Catholicism to the Americas, Africa, and Asia.

Religious conflict generated some of the most destructive violence in European history. The French Wars of Religion, the Thirty Years' War, the English Civil War — these conflicts were not purely religious, but religion gave them their ideological intensity. The eventual settlements imposed political solutions that recognized religious diversity without genuinely embracing toleration. The Thirty Years' War was catastrophic for German-speaking Europe, killing millions and leaving lasting scars on central European society. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) that ended it established principles of territorial sovereignty that have been cited as foundational to the modern international system.

State formation proceeded simultaneously with religious conflict and was deeply entangled with it. European monarchies — France most elaborately under Louis XIV — built standing armies, expanded bureaucracies, developed systematic taxation, and elaborated court cultures that centralized power and cultivated royal magnificence. The fiscal-military state — organized around the capacity to wage war — became the dominant European political form, generating both greater internal order and greater international conflict. England developed a distinctive constitutional model in which parliamentary power constrained royal authority, while the Dutch Republic demonstrated that effective commercial and military power could be organized without a monarchy.

The Scientific Revolution transformed European understanding of the natural world. Copernicus, Kepler, Galileo, and Newton overturned the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic cosmos and established mathematical natural science as the dominant framework for explaining physical reality. Anatomists like Vesalius and Harvey revised understanding of the human body. New instruments — telescope, microscope, barometer — extended the range of human observation. New institutions — scientific societies, universities, academies — created communities of scholars who shared methods, findings, and criticism. The Scientific Revolution drew heavily on Islamic, Greek, and other intellectual traditions, and its institutionalization in European universities and courts gave it a particularly powerful organizational base.

European maritime expansion between 1450 and 1750 transformed Europe's global position. Portuguese and Spanish navigators opened sea routes to sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and the Americas. American silver fueled European commercial expansion. Colonial systems in the Americas created new forms of forced labor that sustained plantation economies at enormous human cost. The Atlantic slave trade, which grew dramatically in this period, transported millions of enslaved Africans to the Americas and transformed societies on both sides of the ocean.

Ordinary Europeans — peasants, artisans, women, servants, religious minorities — experienced the period's changes in highly varied ways. The dissolution of monasteries, the introduction of new forms of religious practice, heavier taxation, military conscription, and the slow commercialization of economic life all reshaped daily experience. Women found some new spaces in the Reformation's emphasis on household piety but continued to be excluded from formal religious and political authority. Jewish communities faced worsening persecution in many regions. Peasants who had hoped to draw on Reformation language for social liberation found their movements crushed. The great witch trials illustrate how social anxiety and religious intensity could combine in devastating local violence.

The early modern period was not a story of progress toward inevitable modernity. It was a period of violent, contested, and uneven transformation — in which remarkable intellectual and artistic achievement coexisted with religious persecution, imperial expansion, and colonial exploitation. The legacies of the Renaissance, the Reformation, state formation, and the Scientific Revolution were not unambiguously positive. But they were consequential: they established the institutions, ideas, and power structures that shaped European and eventually global history through the following centuries, and they continue to generate debate among historians who seek to understand both what changed and what those changes cost.

Key Terms

Absolutism
A form of government in which the monarch claims supreme and theoretically unlimited authority, accountable only to God; associated especially with seventeenth-century France under Louis XIV.
Act of Supremacy (1534)
English parliamentary legislation declaring the king the "Supreme Head" of the Church of England, completing the English break from Rome under Henry VIII.
Anabaptists
Radical Protestant reformers who rejected infant baptism in favor of adult baptism; also typically rejected fusion of church and state and military service; persecuted by both Catholics and mainstream Protestants.
Baroque
An artistic and architectural style of the seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, associated with the Catholic Reformation and absolute monarchies; characterized by grandeur, drama, and emotional intensity.
Calvinism
The Reformed Protestant tradition developed by John Calvin, centered on the absolute sovereignty of God, predestination, and rigorous church discipline; spread to France, Scotland, the Netherlands, and the Americas.
Catholic Reformation
The Catholic Church's internal reform and response to Protestantism, including the Council of Trent, new religious orders (especially the Jesuits), and renewed missionary activity.
Confessionalization
The process by which European states and communities defined, enforced, and institutionalized distinct religious identities — Lutheran, Calvinist, Catholic — in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.
Council of Trent (1545–1563)
The ecumenical council of the Catholic Church that defined Catholic doctrine in response to Protestant challenges and reformed clerical education and discipline.
Cuius regio, eius religio
Latin: "whose realm, his religion"; the principle established by the Peace of Augsburg (1555) allowing rulers to determine the official religion of their territories.
Divine right of kings
The political and religious doctrine that kings receive their authority directly from God and are accountable only to God, not to earthly powers or institutions; provided ideological justification for absolutism.
Edict of Nantes (1598)
The French royal edict granting Huguenots limited but significant religious toleration, ending the French Wars of Religion; revoked by Louis XIV in 1685, causing mass Huguenot emigration.
Fiscal-military state
A state organized primarily around the capacity to finance and wage war through systematic taxation, public credit, and large standing armies; the dominant European political form by the seventeenth century.
Glorious Revolution (1688)
The English political settlement in which William of Orange replaced James II with parliamentary conditions attached; established constitutional limits on royal authority and is seen as foundational to English democracy.
Heliocentric model
The astronomical model in which the Earth and other planets orbit the Sun; proposed by Copernicus (1543) and confirmed by subsequent astronomers including Galileo, Kepler, and Newton.
Humanism
The intellectual movement of the Renaissance centered on the study of classical Greek and Roman texts, emphasizing rhetoric, history, moral philosophy, and the dignity of human achievement.
Iconoclasm
The deliberate destruction of religious images; a recurring feature of Reformed Protestant movements in the sixteenth century, rooted in interpretation of the Second Commandment's prohibition of graven images.
Indulgence
In Catholic practice, a remission of temporal punishment due for sins; the sale of indulgences was the immediate trigger for Luther's Ninety-Five Theses and a central target of Protestant criticism.
Justification by faith (sola fide)
The Lutheran doctrine that salvation comes through faith in God's grace alone, not through good works or sacramental mediation; one of the central tenets of the Protestant Reformation.
Military revolution
The transformation of European military organization and technology between roughly 1500 and 1700, involving gunpowder weapons, new fortification designs, and dramatic growth in army size.
Peace of Westphalia (1648)
The treaties ending the Thirty Years' War; recognized territorial sovereignty and limited religious toleration in the Holy Roman Empire; often cited as foundational to the modern system of sovereign states.
Predestination
The Calvinist doctrine that God has eternally determined who will be saved (the elect) and who will be damned; a central and debated feature of Reformed Protestant theology.
Priesthood of all believers
The Lutheran doctrine that all baptized Christians share in priestly status and can directly approach God without clerical mediation; a democratizing implication of Reformation theology.
Renaissance
The cultural, artistic, and intellectual movement centered in Italy from roughly the fourteenth through sixteenth centuries, drawing on classical Greek and Roman models and celebrating human achievement and dignity.
Scientific Revolution
The transformation of European understanding of the natural world in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries through work of Copernicus, Kepler, Galileo, Newton, and others; established mathematical natural science as the dominant explanatory framework.
Sola scriptura
Latin: "scripture alone"; the Protestant principle that the Bible is the sole ultimate authority for Christian faith and practice, overriding papal decrees and church traditions; attributed especially to Luther.
Sovereignty
Supreme, undivided authority to govern a territory, acknowledging no higher human power; a central concept in early modern political thought and the basis of the Westphalian international system.
Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)
The most destructive European religious-political conflict of the early modern period, devastating the Holy Roman Empire; ended by the Peace of Westphalia, which shaped the modern state system.
Vernacular
The everyday spoken and written language of a region, as distinct from Latin; the Reformation's promotion of vernacular Bible translations was crucial to its popular spread and contributed to the standardization of European national languages.

The Jesuits
Catholic religious order founded by Ignatius of Loyola; the most dynamic force in the Catholic Reformation; operated schools, missions, and scholarly institutions across Europe, Asia, and the Americas, reaching Japan, China, Brazil, and New France.
The Huguenots
French Calvinist Protestants; subject to intense persecution culminating in the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572) and the revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685); their exodus carried Calvinist skills and culture across Protestant Europe and beyond.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 33 has examined one of the most consequential and complex periods in European history — a period that generated extraordinary artistic and intellectual achievement alongside religious violence, political upheaval, and the beginnings of global colonial expansion. The Renaissance, the Reformation, the rise of sovereign states, and the Scientific Revolution were not isolated developments; they were interwoven with each other and with larger global processes. The printing press that spread Luther's theses also carried humanist scholarship across the continent. The religious conflicts that devastated Germany also drove state-building by rulers who needed standing armies and systematic taxation. The scientific curiosity that drove Galileo's telescopic observations was related to the navigational ambition that drove Vasco da Gama and Columbus across unknown seas.

One of the chapter's most important arguments is that European changes between 1450 and 1750 must be understood in global context, not as the story of a self-contained civilization discovering its own genius. European intellectual development drew on Islamic scholarship, Greek traditions transmitted through Arabic translation, and Indian mathematics. European maritime expansion depended on geographic knowledge accumulated by Arab, Chinese, and African sailors and geographers. The wealth that financed Renaissance patronage came partly from Mediterranean and Atlantic trade. The silver that fueled European commercial expansion came from American mines worked by forced Indigenous and African labor. "European" early modernity was already a global process, and its consequences were felt most sharply not in the courts and universities of Florence or Wittenberg but in the communities of the Americas and Africa that bore the costs of European ambition.

The period's violence and intolerance deserve as much attention as its achievements. The witch trials, the religious wars, the persecution of Anabaptists and Jews, the Atlantic slave trade, the destruction of Indigenous American civilizations — these were not accidents or aberrations. They were products of the same cultural, political, and economic systems that produced Leonardo da Vinci and Isaac Newton. This does not mean that intellectual achievement and moral horror cancel each other out, or that the period's achievements should be dismissed. It means that historical understanding requires holding complexity — recognizing that the same civilization can produce extraordinary works of art and extraordinary atrocities, and that both require explanation.

The legacies of this period remain profoundly relevant. The Protestant Reformation's insistence on individual conscience and the authority of scripture contributed to later arguments for religious freedom and intellectual autonomy. The Scientific Revolution's methods — systematic observation, mathematical description, peer review, and willingness to revise conclusions in light of evidence — remain the foundation of modern science. The early modern state's development of professional bureaucracy, systematic law, and military organization provided templates, for better and worse, for the modern states that govern most of the world's people. And the colonial systems established by European powers between 1450 and 1750 created patterns of economic inequality, racial hierarchy, and political dependence whose consequences are still being negotiated in the twenty-first century.

As this textbook moves forward to examine the Atlantic Revolutions, the Industrial Revolution, and the age of imperialism, the developments examined in Chapter 33 will appear again and again — as causes, as contexts, and as inherited structures that subsequent generations had to build on, struggle against, or attempt to reform. The early modern period was not the beginning of everything, but it was the beginning of much that made the modern world — and understanding it carefully, with attention to both its achievements and its costs, is essential to understanding the world we have inherited.

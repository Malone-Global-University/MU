Enlightenment, Science, and Political Change

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"If the ancient astronomers had been wrong about the heavens, perhaps they had also been wrong about government, law, punishment, slavery, and the proper relations between rulers and the ruled."

In the autumn of 1687, a small book changed how educated Europeans understood the cosmos. Isaac Newton's Principia Mathematica demonstrated that the same mathematical laws governing the fall of an apple governed the orbit of the moon. The universe, Newton showed, operated not by divine caprice or ancient mystery but by discoverable, regular, rational principles. Within a generation, the implications of that claim had spread far beyond astronomy and physics. If nature obeyed reason, reformers asked, why should society not obey reason as well? If theologians and kings had been wrong about the heavens, perhaps they had also been wrong about government, law, punishment, slavery, and the proper relations between rulers and the ruled.

The century stretching roughly from 1680 to 1800 was one of the most consequential periods in the history of human ideas. Scholars call it the Enlightenment, though the name conceals important tensions. It was not a single movement but a collection of overlapping conversations conducted in French salons, English coffeehouses, German universities, Scottish lecture halls, colonial Philadelphia, Haitian plantations, and Qing dynasty academies. Thinkers disagreed sharply about religion, property, liberty, equality, and the proper role of government. Some Enlightenment writers defended aristocracy even as they praised reason. Others used the same language of reason to attack slavery, torture, censorship, and colonial conquest. The Enlightenment was never simply progressive, never purely European, and never free of contradiction.

What brought these conversations together was a shared conviction: that human reason, applied systematically to the world, could discover truths that would improve human life. This conviction rested on foundations laid by the Scientific Revolution of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, when European natural philosophers — the predecessors of scientists — developed new methods for studying the natural world. They challenged ancient authorities, conducted experiments, built instruments, formed learned societies, and published their findings. The new science did not immediately transform daily life, but it transformed the intellectual atmosphere. If Copernicus could overturn a millennium of astronomical consensus, if Harvey could show that blood circulated when ancient physicians said otherwise, if Newton could derive the laws of planetary motion through mathematics alone, then perhaps every field of knowledge — moral, political, economic, social — could be subjected to the same rigorous scrutiny.

This chapter examines both the Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment as intellectual transformations of world-historical significance. The Scientific Revolution was primarily a European phenomenon of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, though it drew on mathematical and astronomical knowledge from the Islamic world, ancient Greece, and South Asia. The Enlightenment was more geographically dispersed. It spread through print culture — books, pamphlets, encyclopedias, newspapers, and literary journals — reaching not only Western Europe but the Atlantic colonial world, the Ottoman Empire, Russia, and East Asia. Enlightenment ideas provided intellectual fuel for the American, French, Haitian, and Latin American revolutions examined in the following chapter.

The Enlightenment was also a contested cultural space. Women often found themselves excluded from formal institutions of learning, yet many women participated actively in salon culture, correspondence networks, and literary production. Enslaved and colonized peoples were frequently cited by Enlightenment writers as evidence for theories of human diversity, hierarchy, and progress, even as some Enlightenment thinkers developed the most powerful early critiques of the slave trade and colonial violence. Indigenous peoples of the Americas were imagined as "natural" humans whose existence could either confirm or challenge European theories of society — almost always through the distorting lens of European observers. The contradictions of Enlightenment thought were not incidental. They were central to how the movement shaped, and failed to shape, the modern world.

Students studying this chapter should understand that ideas do not emerge from nowhere. The intellectual transformations of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries were products of specific social, economic, political, and religious environments. They were funded by wealthy patrons, spread through commercial publishing networks, debated in institutions shaped by class and gender, and contested by religious authorities who saw new ideas as threats. Understanding the Enlightenment means understanding both the power of ideas and the social conditions that determine which ideas get heard, remembered, and acted upon — and by whom.

Finally, this chapter matters because the political vocabulary of the modern world was largely forged in this period. Natural rights, popular sovereignty, the social contract, the separation of powers, freedom of conscience, the rule of law, religious toleration, and the critique of arbitrary government — these concepts emerged or were given powerful new form during the Enlightenment. They became the language of revolutions, constitutions, and declarations of rights across the Atlantic world and beyond. Tracing their origins helps students understand both the promise and the limits of the political tradition we have inherited.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the major developments and methods of the Scientific Revolution, including key figures and their contributions.
  2. Identify the intellectual and social foundations of Enlightenment thought, including its debts to ancient and non-European traditions of knowledge.
  3. Trace the spread of Enlightenment ideas through print culture, learned societies, salons, and colonial networks.
  4. Analyze the major concepts of natural rights, social contract theory, and popular sovereignty as developed by Locke, Rousseau, Montesquieu, and others.
  5. Compare Enlightenment thought across different national and regional contexts, including France, Britain, Scotland, Germany, the Atlantic colonial world, and the Ottoman and Qing empires.
  6. Evaluate the contradictions within Enlightenment thought, especially regarding gender, race, slavery, and colonial conquest.
  7. Assess the critiques of slavery and inequality produced within and beyond the Enlightenment tradition.
  8. Describe the role of salons, coffeehouses, print culture, and learned societies in spreading new ideas.
  9. Connect Enlightenment political thought to the revolutions of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries.
  10. Distinguish between the Scientific Revolution as a change in method and the Enlightenment as a broader cultural and political movement.
  11. Interpret primary sources from the Enlightenment as products of specific social contexts with distinct purposes, audiences, and limits.
  12. Contextualize the Enlightenment within the longer history of human efforts to explain the natural world, organize political authority, and define the rights and dignity of persons.

Historical Context

The intellectual transformations of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries did not arise from nothing. They emerged from a specific configuration of historical circumstances that made new forms of inquiry both possible and urgent.

The Legacy of the Renaissance and Reformation

The Italian Renaissance of the fourteenth through sixteenth centuries had revived interest in classical Greek and Roman learning, including ancient philosophy, mathematics, medicine, and rhetoric. Humanist scholars read ancient texts critically, sometimes finding that ancient authorities contradicted one another. This practice of close, critical reading created intellectual habits that would prove essential to the Scientific Revolution. Meanwhile, the Protestant Reformation of the sixteenth century had shattered the religious unity of Western Europe. Martin Luther's challenge to papal authority asserted the right of individuals to interpret scripture through their own conscience and reason. The Reformation's divisions produced a century and a half of religious warfare that exhausted European populations and pushed some thinkers toward the conclusion that skepticism and toleration offered the only path to social peace.

The Expansion of European Trade and Empire

European maritime expansion from the late fifteenth century brought Europeans into contact with societies, plants, animals, diseases, and astronomical observations unknown to ancient authorities. The Americas contained civilizations, languages, and natural resources that no ancient author had mentioned. The southern hemisphere contained stars invisible from the Mediterranean. These discoveries demonstrated, forcefully, that ancient learning was incomplete. They also raised urgent practical questions — about navigation, about the properties of unfamiliar plants and metals, about the organization of distant colonial territories — that required new forms of systematic inquiry.

The Islamic and Asian Intellectual Inheritance

The Scientific Revolution is sometimes narrated as a purely European achievement, but this distorts the history. European natural philosophers worked within a mathematical and astronomical tradition deeply indebted to Islamic scholarship. Medieval Islamic thinkers had preserved, translated, and substantially extended ancient Greek knowledge in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, optics, and philosophy. The Hindu-Arabic numeral system, including the zero, had reached Europe through Islamic intermediaries, making advanced calculation possible. Astronomers working in the Islamic tradition, including al-Biruni and Ibn al-Haytham, had conducted sophisticated astronomical observations and optical experiments centuries before Copernicus. This intellectual inheritance was not merely background — it was active foundation.

The Printing Press and the Republic of Letters

Johannes Gutenberg's development of movable-type printing in the mid-fifteenth century transformed how knowledge was produced, distributed, and contested. By the mid-sixteenth century, presses operating across Europe were producing millions of books, pamphlets, and broadsides annually. Natural philosophers formed correspondence networks — the "Republic of Letters" — through which they exchanged observations, challenged one another's claims, and built collective knowledge across national boundaries. The printing press also made it harder for any single authority to control information, creating the conditions for sustained intellectual competition.

Religious and Political Crisis

The decades from the 1580s to the 1650s were among the most violent in European history. The French Wars of Religion, the Thirty Years' War in Central Europe, and the English Civil War together killed millions and destroyed the confidence of many thinkers in inherited political and religious institutions. The search for more stable, rational foundations for political order — order that did not depend on contested religious claims — drove some of the most important political thought of the century.

Regional Orientation

The Scientific Revolution was centered in Western and Central Europe, particularly in Italy, England, France, and the German-speaking lands of the Holy Roman Empire. Key cities included London, Paris, Amsterdam, Padua, Florence, and Leiden. European natural philosophers operated in a network that crossed national boundaries, conducting correspondence in Latin and increasingly in vernacular languages.

The Enlightenment's geographic reach was broader. Paris was its acknowledged capital, home to the philosophes. Important centers also existed in Edinburgh and Glasgow (the Scottish Enlightenment), London, Amsterdam, Berlin, Vienna, Naples, and Philadelphia. Russia, under Catherine the Great, engaged actively with French Enlightenment ideas. The Ottoman Empire had its own tradition of reform thought. Qing China had significant contact through Jesuit missionaries who served as cultural intermediaries. The Atlantic colonial world — including the British colonies of North America and the French colony of Saint-Domingue (Haiti) — was both a recipient of Enlightenment ideas and a crucible in which those ideas were tested, challenged, and radicalized.

The geographic spread of Enlightenment ideas was inseparable from the geography of commerce and empire. Trade networks, colonial correspondence systems, and missionary networks all served as channels through which ideas traveled. The Atlantic slave system, which created enormous wealth for European and colonial elites, also created the conditions for the most radical challenge to Enlightenment contradictions: the Haitian Revolution.

Section 40.1

Science and the Changing View of Nature

For most of recorded history, educated Europeans understood the natural world through a framework derived from ancient Greek philosophy, modified by Christian theology. The universe was finite and ordered. At its center stood the Earth, surrounded by crystalline spheres carrying the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars in their eternal circular orbits. This picture, associated primarily with the ancient philosopher Ptolemy and elaborated through centuries of Christian interpretation, was not simply an astronomical theory. It was the framework within which educated people understood their place in creation. Human beings stood at the physical center of the universe because they were the purpose of creation. The heavens were perfect, unchanging, and circular because they participated in the divine order. Medicine, natural philosophy, and theology were all interwoven within this comprehensive worldview.

Challenges to this framework had existed throughout the medieval period. Islamic astronomers in the ninth through thirteenth centuries had noted mathematical inconsistencies in the Ptolemaic system and proposed refinements. European scholars debated Aristotle's physics and sometimes proposed alternative explanations for observed phenomena. But the general framework held. Ancient authority, religious sanction, and the intuitive plausibility of Earth's centrality combined to maintain it across centuries.

What changed in the sixteenth century was not simply that more accurate observations were made. Observations had always been made. What changed was a growing willingness among natural philosophers to let observations override inherited authority — to say, in effect, that if the data contradicts the theory, the theory must be revised. This willingness had multiple sources. The humanist recovery of classical texts had revealed that ancient authorities contradicted one another — if Ptolemy and Aristotle disagreed on details, the ancient consensus was less secure than it seemed. European maritime expansion had produced geographical and natural knowledge that ancient authors had not mentioned. The printing press had created communities of readers who could compare observations across large distances. And the Protestant Reformation had made it somewhat more acceptable, in Protestant territories at least, to challenge institutional authority on the basis of personal conscience and direct engagement with sources.

The resulting transformation of natural philosophy was gradual, contested, and geographically uneven. It stretched across more than a century, from Copernicus's publication in 1543 to Newton's Principia in 1687. It involved dozens of figures working in different countries and disciplines, sometimes building on one another's work, sometimes working in isolation, and sometimes sharply disagreeing. What united them was a set of methodological commitments: to careful observation, to mathematical description, to experimental testing, and to the revision of theories when evidence required it. These commitments, not any single discovery, were the core achievement of the Scientific Revolution.

The view of nature that emerged from this transformation was profoundly different from the ancient and medieval framework it replaced. The universe was no longer finite and Earth-centered but vast and Sun-centered — and, increasingly, it appeared to have no center at all. The distinction between the perfect celestial world and the corrupt terrestrial world dissolved: the same mathematical laws that governed the motion of a cannonball on Earth governed the motion of the Moon. Nature was not a hierarchy of purposes but a system of forces, masses, and motions, operating according to mathematical laws that could be discovered by human reason. God had created the universe, but the universe now operated like a machine, following laws that did not require constant divine intervention.

This new view of nature did not instantly replace the old. The vast majority of people in the seventeenth century continued to understand the world through traditional religious frameworks. Even among the educated, change was slow. But for those who encountered the new natural philosophy — through books, learned societies, coffeehouses, and correspondence — it posed urgent questions. If the ancient astronomers had been wrong about the heavens, who else might be wrong? If nature obeyed mathematical reason, could human institutions also be subjected to rational scrutiny and reform? These questions drove the Enlightenment.

Section 40.2

The Scientific Revolution

The Scientific Revolution unfolded across several generations and disciplines. Its key figures, methods, instruments, and achievements deserve careful attention — not because they are heroes of progress, but because understanding what they actually did helps students understand what was genuinely new about the methods they developed.

Copernicus and Heliocentrism

Nicolaus Copernicus, a Polish canon who also studied law and medicine in Italy, spent years refining a mathematical model of the heavens. His On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres, published in 1543, proposed that the Earth and other planets orbit the Sun. Copernicus was motivated primarily by mathematical dissatisfaction with the Ptolemaic system, which required complicated epicycles — circles within circles — to make its predictions match observations. He hoped a Sun-centered model would be simpler and more elegant. Whether or not it was immediately more accurate was debated. But the heliocentric model raised a question that could not be ignored: if Earth moves, why do we not feel it? Why do objects fall straight down rather than being left behind as Earth moves beneath them? These questions pushed physicists, as well as astronomers, to reconsider their assumptions.

Tycho Brahe and Kepler

The Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe spent decades making precise naked-eye observations of planetary positions — the most accurate astronomical data available before the telescope. His student Johannes Kepler inherited Brahe's data and spent years analyzing it. The result was Kepler's three laws of planetary motion, published between 1609 and 1619. The most important finding: planets move in ellipses, not perfect circles. This was a decisive blow to the ancient doctrine of perfect celestial circles. Kepler had expected circles; the data demanded ellipses; he accepted what the data showed. This was empirical method in action.

Galileo and Experimental Physics

Galileo Galilei, working in the Italian city-states of Padua and Florence, made contributions in both astronomy and physics. Hearing of a new Dutch instrument that magnified distant objects, he built his own telescope and turned it on the heavens in 1609. He observed mountains on the Moon — destroying the idea of a perfect celestial sphere — and four moons orbiting Jupiter, demonstrating that Earth was not the only center of celestial motion. He observed the phases of Venus, which the Ptolemaic system could not explain but heliocentrism could. In physics, through mathematical reasoning and experiment, he established that all objects fall at the same rate regardless of weight — contrary to Aristotle — and that horizontal and vertical motions are independent of each other.

Galileo's support for heliocentrism brought him into conflict with the Catholic Church. In 1632, he published a Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems that barely concealed its advocacy for the Copernican model. The Inquisition tried him in 1633, and he was forced to recant and placed under house arrest. His trial became, in later centuries, a foundational story about the conflict between free inquiry and institutional authority — though historians caution that the actual conflict was more complicated, involving Galileo's personal relationships with church officials and the specific political context of the Counter-Reformation.

Francis Bacon and the Philosophy of Science

While figures like Galileo and Kepler were making specific discoveries, the English philosopher Francis Bacon was articulating a philosophy of scientific inquiry. Bacon argued that natural philosophy had been dominated too long by deductive reasoning from ancient authorities. The proper method, he insisted, was induction: gathering large numbers of observations and inferring general principles from them. This was not simply a description of what scientists did. It was a program for how they should organize their activity — collectively, systematically, and practically. His vision influenced the founding of the Royal Society of London in 1660, one of the first learned societies dedicated to experimental science.

René Descartes and Rationalism

The French philosopher René Descartes approached the same problems from a different direction. Rather than beginning with observations, he began with systematic doubt — questioning everything he could doubt until he arrived at a claim he found indubitable: cogito ergo sum, "I think, therefore I am." From this foundation, he built a philosophy that emphasized rational deduction over sensory observation. In mathematics, he developed analytic geometry, linking algebra and geometry. In natural philosophy, he proposed a mechanical view of nature: the universe was composed of matter in motion, and all natural phenomena could in principle be explained by the interactions of material particles.

William Harvey and Medicine

The English physician William Harvey demonstrated in 1628 that blood circulates through the body — pumped by the heart through arteries and returning through veins — overturning the ancient medical theory that blood was produced in the liver and consumed by the body's organs. Harvey's demonstration combined careful anatomical dissection with quantitative reasoning: he calculated that the volume of blood pumped by the heart in an hour was far greater than the body could produce, so blood must circulate and be reused. This was one of the clearest demonstrations that systematic observation and measurement could correct ancient authority in medicine.

Isaac Newton and the Synthesis

Newton's Principia Mathematica of 1687 synthesized and surpassed the work of his predecessors. Through mathematical analysis, Newton showed that the same force — gravity — that caused objects to fall on Earth also governed the orbits of the Moon and planets. His three laws of motion provided a framework for analyzing the motion of any object. His law of universal gravitation showed that every object with mass attracted every other object with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This "universal" law was exactly what the Enlightenment would later celebrate: a rational principle, discoverable by human reason, that applied everywhere in the universe without exception.

Newton himself was not simply a herald of secular rationalism. He was deeply religious, spent enormous energy on biblical chronology and prophecy, and believed that gravity demonstrated God's active presence in the universe. But the worldview implied by his physics — a universe governed by regular, discoverable mathematical laws — proved enormously influential among those who drew out its implications, regardless of Newton's own intentions.

Learned Societies, Journals, and Scientific Communication

Individual genius mattered in the Scientific Revolution, but so did institutions. The Royal Society of London (founded 1660) and the French Academy of Sciences (founded 1666) provided forums in which natural philosophers could present their work, have it evaluated by peers, and publish it in the first scientific journals. These institutions created new norms of knowledge production: claims were to be supported by replicable evidence, subjected to criticism by other experts, and revised when challenged. The Republic of Letters supplemented formal institutions, allowing ideas to circulate rapidly across national boundaries. The Scientific Revolution was, in part, a social achievement — the creation of communities of inquiry with shared standards for evaluating claims.

Section 40.3

Enlightenment Thought

The Enlightenment was not a single unified philosophy. It was a collection of overlapping conversations among writers, philosophers, scientists, reformers, and political thinkers who shared certain broad commitments while disagreeing sharply on specifics. What united them was the conviction that human reason, properly applied, could improve human life — that ignorance, superstition, prejudice, and arbitrary authority were the sources of most human suffering, and that they could be overcome by education, rational inquiry, and institutional reform.

The philosopher Immanuel Kant, in a famous 1784 essay, defined Enlightenment as humanity's emergence from "self-imposed immaturity" — the failure to use one's own reason without guidance from others. "Dare to know!" (Sapere aude) was Kant's motto for the movement. This language of liberation through reason was inspiring to many. But critics, then and later, pointed out that the confidence in reason could become its own form of arrogance — a new authority claiming to replace old authorities.

The French Enlightenment

France produced the most prominent and internationally influential group of Enlightenment writers, the philosophes. They were not primarily university professors or academic philosophers. They were public writers — critics, novelists, playwrights, journalists — who addressed broad educated audiences through a rapidly expanding commercial print culture. They were united less by a shared philosophy than by a shared critical posture: skepticism toward inherited authority, advocacy for toleration, opposition to censorship and religious persecution, and confidence in the power of reason to improve human institutions.

Voltaire used wit, satire, and the literary form of the philosophical tale (notably Candide, 1759) to expose the cruelties of religious intolerance and political tyranny. Montesquieu's The Spirit of the Laws (1748) analyzed different forms of government and argued that the form appropriate to a society depended on its climate, population, and customs. His discussion of separation of powers, in which he idealized the English constitutional system, became enormously influential in constitutional design. Rousseau was the most controversial of the major philosophes. While others celebrated reason, commerce, and civility, he argued that civilization itself had corrupted human nature. In The Social Contract (1762), he developed his theory of popular sovereignty: legitimate government derived from a "social contract" through which the people agreed to be governed, retaining sovereignty as "the general will" of the community. Denis Diderot, the driving force behind the Encyclopédie, eventually moved toward a materialist view of nature as a self-organizing system without need of divine creation.

The Scottish Enlightenment

Edinburgh and Glasgow became unexpected centers of Enlightenment thought in the eighteenth century. Scottish thinkers, including David Hume, Adam Smith, Adam Ferguson, and Francis Hutcheson, developed influential analyses of moral philosophy, economics, and social science. David Hume pushed empiricism to its logical limits, questioning the foundations of causation, personal identity, and religious belief. Adam Smith's The Wealth of Nations (1776) argued that free markets, driven by individual self-interest, produced social wealth more efficiently than mercantilist state direction. The Scottish Enlightenment was notable for its sociological turn — its attention to how social institutions shaped human character and behavior, a precursor to modern social science.

The German Enlightenment

German-speaking lands produced their own Enlightenment tradition. Frederick the Great of Prussia fancied himself an "enlightened despot" — a ruler who applied rational principles to government while maintaining autocratic power. Kant's critical philosophy attempted to reconcile rationalism and empiricism, arguing that the human mind actively structures experience through categories of understanding. His political philosophy defended human dignity, legal equality, and the idea of perpetual peace among nations — achieved through republican government and international law.

The American Enlightenment

The British colonies of North America were not simply recipients of European Enlightenment ideas. Benjamin Franklin, a scientist, inventor, diplomat, and writer, was an internationally recognized figure of the Atlantic Enlightenment. Thomas Jefferson, James Madison, Alexander Hamilton, and other founders of the American republic drew deeply on Locke, Montesquieu, and other Enlightenment political theorists in designing the institutions of the new United States. The American Declaration of Independence (1776) was a foundational document of Enlightenment political philosophy in action, asserting that "all men are created equal" and possess unalienable natural rights.

Section 40.4

Natural Rights and Social Contract Theory

The most politically consequential intellectual contribution of the Enlightenment was the theory of natural rights and the social contract. These ideas had complex historical roots but were given new precision and force by Enlightenment thinkers, and they became the theoretical foundation of the Atlantic revolutions.

Thomas Hobbes and the Problem of Order

The question that animated social contract theory began with political crisis. Thomas Hobbes, writing during and after the English Civil War (1642–1651), began from a stark premise: in the "state of nature" — the condition of human life without political institutions — life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Violent competition for resources would make security impossible. Rational self-interest, Hobbes argued, therefore drove humans to agree to a sovereign — ideally, a powerful monarch — who would maintain order. In exchange for security, people surrendered their natural freedom. The sovereign's authority was almost absolute. Hobbes was a theorist of order and state power, not a theorist of liberty. But his method — beginning from a thought-experiment about human nature in a "state of nature" and deriving the rational basis of government — proved enormously influential. Later thinkers used the same method to reach very different conclusions.

John Locke and Liberal Natural Rights

John Locke, writing in the wake of the Glorious Revolution of 1688, offered a very different account. For Locke, the state of nature was not Hobbes's war of all against all. Natural law — discoverable by reason — already obliged humans to respect one another's natural rights: life, liberty, and property. People entered into political society not to escape chaos but to protect rights that were already theirs by nature. The social contract was therefore not a surrender of freedom but an exchange: individuals consented to be governed in exchange for government's protection of their natural rights.

From this foundation, Locke drew conclusions Hobbes had rejected. If government's legitimacy depended on its protection of natural rights, then a government that systematically violated those rights forfeited its claim to obedience. People had the right — even the obligation — to resist and replace such a government. This right of revolution, grounded in natural rights theory, would be cited by American colonists in 1776 and by French revolutionaries in 1789. Locke's treatment of Indigenous peoples, however, illustrated the theory's limits: he argued that those who did not cultivate land in European ways had not fully exercised their property rights, justifying colonial dispossession.

Montesquieu and Constitutional Design

Montesquieu's The Spirit of the Laws argued that legislative, executive, and judicial functions should be exercised by separate institutions, each checking the others — a principle of separation of powers he believed he observed in the English constitutional system. American constitution-makers, including Madison in the Federalist Papers, drew directly on Montesquieu's analysis. Montesquieu also argued that the appropriate form of government for a society depended on its particular conditions — climate, population size, economic base, and social customs. This environmental determinism had both progressive and regressive implications: it led him to oppose slavery, but also to argue that despotism might be appropriate for some climates — a form of ranking that later writers weaponized to justify colonial domination.

Rousseau and Popular Sovereignty

Rousseau's The Social Contract opened with the famous declaration: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." For Rousseau, existing societies were not natural — they were products of historical development, specifically of the rise of private property. The "general will" — the will of the community as a whole, oriented toward the common good — was the only legitimate basis of law. Rousseau's ideas were inspiring and also troubling. His emphasis on the general will over individual will could be read as authoritarian — the community could override individual preferences in the name of the common good. Later readers, including some French revolutionaries, used Rousseau to justify collective conformity rather than individual liberty. Ideas have consequences their authors do not control.

Historical Vocabulary
Natural Rights

Natural rights are rights held to belong to all human beings by nature — not granted by any government, law, or institution, and therefore not legitimately revocable by any government. The theory of natural rights, developed in recognizable modern form by John Locke, holds that human beings possess certain rights — Locke named life, liberty, and property — simply by virtue of being human. These rights exist prior to and independent of any political arrangement.

The concept was revolutionary in its implications: no ruler had the authority to remove natural rights, and a government that systematically violated them forfeited its claim to obedience. This logic was invoked by American colonists in 1776, by French revolutionaries in 1789, and by enslaved people in Saint-Domingue in 1791. Yet "natural rights" as articulated by most Enlightenment thinkers applied in practice primarily to propertied men. The history of modern politics is in significant part the history of the struggle to extend natural rights claims to groups the original theorists had excluded.

Historical Vocabulary
The Social Contract

The social contract is a theoretical framework for understanding the origins and legitimacy of political authority. Contract theorists — including Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau — imagined a "state of nature" before political society and asked: on what basis did rational individuals agree to form governments and accept their authority?

The social contract was not a historical claim — there was no moment when actual people gathered and signed an agreement. It was a philosophical thought experiment designed to show what the rational foundation of political authority would be if human beings had chosen it from scratch. But it had real political consequences: it implied that governments ruled by the consent of the governed, that subjects were not merely subjects but citizens with rights, and that illegitimate governments could be replaced. These were the foundational claims of modern democratic theory.

Section 40.5

Print Culture and Public Debate

The Enlightenment could not have happened without print. The intellectual conversations of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries were carried on through books, pamphlets, newspapers, literary journals, encyclopedias, and private correspondence — a rich and expanding world of printed communication that historians call "print culture."

The Book Trade

The production and sale of books was a commercial enterprise, not simply a cultural one. Publishers and booksellers made calculated decisions about what to print based on anticipated demand. This created market pressure toward accessible, engaging writing — the philosophes' preference for clear prose, literary genres, and polemical wit was partly a response to the demands of a commercial reading public. Authors who could not attract readers did not influence events.

The book trade operated in politically varied environments. France had a system of royal censorship requiring official approval before publication. This drove much controversial publishing to Amsterdam, Geneva, and other cities with more permissive regulatory environments. Forbidden books were smuggled into France and read eagerly. The suppression of the Encyclopédie — banned and unbanned multiple times — actually increased its notoriety and sales. Enlightenment ideas spread partly because attempts to suppress them generated interest.

Coffeehouses and Salons

In Britain, coffeehouses were commercial spaces where men gathered to read newspapers, discuss politics, and conduct business. They were organized around particular social groups — merchants, lawyers, writers, scientists — and some became informal headquarters of specific intellectual communities. The coffeehouse was a genuinely new kind of public space, where social distinctions were partially suspended in favor of conversation and commerce.

In France, salons served a similar function with an important difference: they were typically hosted by women. The salonnières — women including Marie-Thérèse Rodet Geoffrin, Julie de Lespinasse, and Suzanne Necker — played a crucial role in Enlightenment sociability. They shaped conversations, mediated disputes among writers, supported careers through their social networks, and sometimes influenced the intellectual direction of the movement. Marie-Thérèse Geoffrin provided substantial financial support to the Encyclopédie. Yet women were formally excluded from academic institutions — universities, academies, learned societies — that conferred intellectual prestige. The salon was both an opportunity and a limit.

Newspapers and Public Opinion

The eighteenth century saw the emergence of "public opinion" as a political concept — the idea that the views of the educated reading public constituted a form of authority that governments had to reckon with. Newspapers, pamphlets, and periodicals contributed to this development by creating a shared informational environment for literate populations. When writers like Voltaire published attacks on specific cases of injustice — a judicial murder, a case of religious persecution — they were appealing to a reading public whose collective judgment they hoped to mobilize. This appeal to public opinion was itself a political act.

Beyond Western Europe

The spread of print culture was not limited to Western Europe. Ibrahim Müteferrika established the first Ottoman-Turkish press in Istanbul in 1727. Russian rulers, including Peter the Great and Catherine the Great, promoted print culture as part of their modernization programs. In the British North American colonies, a vigorous newspaper press had developed by the mid-eighteenth century. Benjamin Franklin's career as a printer, publisher, and writer illustrated the importance of the colonial print trade in the intellectual life of the Atlantic world.

Section 40.6

Reform, Reason, and Secular Authority

The Enlightenment was not only a philosophical movement. It was also a practical program — or rather, many competing practical programs — for reforming institutions, laws, and social arrangements in the light of reason.

Legal Reform

Among the most influential Enlightenment reformers was the Italian jurist Cesare Beccaria, whose On Crimes and Punishments (1764) argued for a rational reform of criminal law. Legal systems across Europe relied heavily on torture to extract confessions and on public executions of spectacular cruelty. Beccaria argued that these practices were both unjust and ineffective. Punishment should be proportionate to the crime, certain, and swift — not spectacular. The purpose of punishment was deterrence and rehabilitation, not vengeance or public spectacle. Beccaria specifically opposed the death penalty on both practical and moral grounds. His arguments influenced legal reform across Europe and in the new American republic.

Religious Reform and Toleration

Many Enlightenment thinkers directed particular criticism at religious institutions and religious persecution. Voltaire's sustained campaign against l'infâme — fanaticism, intolerance, and clerical abuse of power — was one of the defining commitments of his career. The Calas Affair of 1762 crystallized this concern: Jean Calas, a Protestant merchant, was falsely convicted by Catholic authorities of murdering his son and executed by being broken on the wheel. Voltaire publicized the case across Europe, eventually securing Calas's posthumous rehabilitation. The affair became a cause célèbre of the Enlightenment's campaign for religious toleration and rational justice.

Many Enlightenment thinkers were deists — they believed in a creator God revealed by reason and nature rather than by scripture and clergy — rather than atheists. For deists, natural religion was purer than the superstition and corruption of historical religion.

Enlightened Despotism

In Central and Eastern Europe, some monarchs sought to apply Enlightenment principles to government while retaining autocratic power. Frederick the Great of Prussia, Catherine the Great of Russia, and Joseph II of Austria all corresponded with philosophes, sponsored cultural institutions, and implemented reforms influenced by Enlightenment thought. Joseph II abolished serfdom in the Habsburg lands in 1781. But they had no intention of limiting their own power or consulting their populations. "Everything for the people, nothing by the people" was the motto of enlightened despotism. Critics argued that top-down enlightenment was a contradiction in terms.

Secularism and the Challenge to Religion

The Enlightenment accelerated a long-term process of secularization — the gradual separation of intellectual, political, and cultural life from religious authority. Political authority was increasingly justified through natural rights and social contract theory rather than divine right. Legal codes appealed to reason and utility rather than religious law. Scientific explanations displaced religious ones in accounts of natural phenomena. Yet religion remained central to the lives of the vast majority of Europeans throughout the eighteenth century. The Enlightenment's challenge to religion provoked sustained resistance and raised questions — about social cohesion, moral frameworks, and political legitimacy — whose consequences are still being worked out.

Historical Vocabulary
Empiricism

Empiricism is a philosophical position holding that all genuine knowledge is derived from sensory experience — from observation and experiment — rather than from innate ideas, divine revelation, or pure reason alone. The English philosopher John Locke articulated the foundational statement of empiricism in his Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689): the human mind at birth is a tabula rasa, a blank slate, and all knowledge is built up through experience.

Empiricism had deep connections to the Scientific Revolution. The new natural philosophy emphasized systematic observation and experimental testing over deduction from ancient authorities. Empiricism also had social implications: if knowledge comes from experience, then education and environment could transform human beings — that the differences observed among human groups were products of different social conditions, not fixed natural properties. Enlightenment reformers drew on this conclusion to argue for the reform of education, law, and social institutions.

Section 40.7

Enlightenment Contradictions

The Enlightenment's celebrated commitment to reason, liberty, and human dignity sat alongside forms of exclusion, hierarchy, and domination that its rhetoric seemed to condemn. These contradictions were not incidental failures. They were structural features of Enlightenment thought that reveal something important about how ideas function in societies shaped by inequality.

Gender

The Enlightenment's vision of reason and natural rights was, in practice, largely reserved for men of property. Most Enlightenment thinkers assumed, often without argument, that women were less capable of reason and naturally suited to domestic life. Rousseau, who wrote eloquently about freedom and equality in public life, designed an educational system in his novel Émile (1762) in which boys were educated for citizenship while girls were educated for pleasing men and managing households.

The contradictions were obvious enough to provoke powerful responses. Mary Wollstonecraft's A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) was a sustained, devastating application of Enlightenment logic to the question of women's rights. If reason was the foundation of natural rights, and if women were capable of reason, then women possessed natural rights equal to those of men. Olympe de Gouges published the Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen in 1791, directly challenging the gender exclusions of the Declaration of the Rights of Man. She was guillotined in 1793 — partly for her political views — a reminder that the revolutionary politics of the Enlightenment were no more hospitable to women's rights than the Old Regime had been.

Race and Colonialism

Enlightenment thinkers wrote extensively about human diversity. Some, including Montesquieu and Voltaire, developed racial typologies — hierarchies of human groups ranked by capacity, temperament, and civilization. Voltaire, despite his advocacy for toleration in Europe, wrote contemptibly about Africans and Indigenous Americans. Kant developed a racial taxonomy in his anthropology lectures that ranked humanity in ways assigning superior capacities to Europeans.

The Enlightenment's racial thought was not simply prejudice imported from outside. It was developed through Enlightenment methods — observation, classification, comparison — and served Enlightenment purposes, including the justification of colonialism. If "savage" peoples were naturally inferior, then their colonization could be represented as beneficial — a "civilizing mission" invoked throughout the nineteenth century. At the same time, the Enlightenment also produced some of the most powerful early intellectual attacks on slavery and colonial conquest. This double movement — racism and anti-racism, colonialism and its critique — is one of the defining features of Enlightenment thought on human diversity.

Class

Many Enlightenment writers imagined their audience as "educated people" — which meant, in practice, literate people of property and leisure. The poor, rural populations, domestic servants, and the laboring classes appeared in Enlightenment texts as objects of concern or reform rather than as participants in intellectual life. Rousseau's critique of inequality pointed toward implications most philosophes were unwilling to follow: if property was the problem, was the solution common ownership? His ideas influenced later socialist thought even though Rousseau himself stopped short of that conclusion.

Section 40.8

Critiques of Slavery and Inequality

The Atlantic slave system — in which millions of Africans were forcibly transported to the Americas and compelled to labor in plantation agriculture — was one of the dominant facts of the eighteenth-century world. It was also one of the central contradictions of Enlightenment thought. A movement that celebrated natural rights, human dignity, and freedom was deeply entangled with one of history's largest systems of organized human bondage.

Early Critiques

Critiques of slavery predated the Enlightenment. Religious dissenters, including Quakers in England and the American colonies, had argued since the seventeenth century that slavery violated Christian ethics. But the Enlightenment gave the critique of slavery a new secular language — natural rights — and new intellectual tools. If all human beings possessed natural rights by virtue of their humanity, then enslaving human beings was not merely immoral but irrational: a systematic violation of the foundational principles of legitimate government. Montesquieu argued in The Spirit of the Laws that slavery was irrational, unjust, and corrupting for both enslaved and enslaver. Condorcet founded the Société des Amis des Noirs in France in 1788 and argued for immediate emancipation.

The Abolitionist Movement

In Britain, the campaign for the abolition of the slave trade became one of the first organized mass political movements in history. The Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, founded in 1787, combined Quaker moral conviction, Enlightenment natural rights arguments, and evangelical Christian activism. Figures including William Wilberforce, Granville Sharp, and Thomas Clarkson organized petitions, boycotts, and public campaigns on a scale unprecedented in British politics. Clarkson gathered physical evidence of the slave trade — chains, thumbscrews, branding irons — and presented it to the public. Enslaved people who had gained freedom, including Olaudah Equiano, published memoirs that documented the realities of slavery and made its human costs vivid to a reading public.

Enslaved People's Own Arguments

The most radical applications of Enlightenment rights language came from enslaved people themselves. In 1773, a group of enslaved people in Massachusetts petitioned the colonial legislature, arguing that "we have in common with all other men a natural right to our freedoms." This was Enlightenment language, deployed by the people most systematically excluded from Enlightenment rights.

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) represented the ultimate test of Enlightenment rights theory. In the French colony of Saint-Domingue, enslaved people — who constituted the vast majority of the population — rose against their enslavers in the most successful slave revolt in history. Their leaders, including Toussaint Louverture, were familiar with Enlightenment political ideas. They invoked the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen as the basis for their own freedom. When French authorities attempted to restore slavery, the revolutionaries ultimately defeated French, Spanish, and British armies, declared independence, and established Haiti — the first free Black republic in the Western Hemisphere and the first nation in the Americas to abolish slavery permanently. The revolution made visible what the Enlightenment's claims implied but what most Enlightenment thinkers refused to acknowledge: that natural rights, if they meant anything, meant the freedom of enslaved Africans.

Section 40.9

Ideas and Political Revolution

The Enlightenment's political ideas did not automatically produce revolutions. Ideas alone rarely change history without specific social, economic, and political conditions to give them force. But the Enlightenment provided the intellectual vocabulary through which revolutionary actors understood, justified, and shaped their actions.

The American Revolution

The American Declaration of Independence (1776) was a document of Enlightenment political theory applied to specific colonial grievances. Jefferson's assertion that "all men are created equal" and endowed with natural rights drew directly on Locke. The claim that when government failed to protect these rights "it is the right of the people to alter or to abolish it" was Lockean social contract theory in action. The design of the American republic — separation of powers, federalism, checks and balances, bills of rights — reflected Enlightenment political science, particularly the influence of Montesquieu.

Yet the American Founding also illustrated the Enlightenment's contradictions. Jefferson, who wrote that all men were created equal, enslaved more than 600 people over his lifetime. The Constitution protected slavery through the three-fifths compromise, the prohibition on ending the slave trade before 1808, and the fugitive slave clause. The revolution expanded liberty for many — primarily for white men of property — while maintaining, and in some ways strengthening, the institution of slavery.

The French Revolution

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was the most dramatic attempt in European history to apply Enlightenment political theory to the reorganization of an entire society. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) proclaimed natural rights, popular sovereignty, and the rule of law. The National Assembly abolished feudalism, reformed the legal system, reorganized the church, and established constitutional government. Robespierre and the Jacobins cited Rousseau's general will to justify the Terror of 1793–1794, during which the revolutionary government executed thousands of perceived enemies. The relationship between Enlightenment thought and the Terror has been debated ever since — whether the Terror was a betrayal of Enlightenment values or a working out of implications already present in Rousseau.

The Haitian Revolution

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was in some ways the most radical application of Enlightenment ideas. Enslaved people in Saint-Domingue used natural rights arguments against the colonial system that the Enlightenment had partly rationalized. The result — the abolition of slavery, the defeat of European military power, and the creation of a free Black republic — was the most radical assertion of Enlightenment rights principles in history. It also provoked the violent reaction of slaveholding societies across the Atlantic — and inspired enslaved people and abolitionists in equal measure.

The Limits of Enlightenment Politics

The Atlantic revolutions demonstrated both the power and the limits of Enlightenment political theory. Natural rights language provided a framework for challenging arbitrary power and organizing resistance to injustice. But the rights tradition consistently excluded groups — women, the propertyless, Indigenous peoples, enslaved people — until those groups forced their inclusion, often over fierce opposition. The Enlightenment did not automatically produce democracy, equality, or liberation. It produced a political vocabulary that could be used by many different actors for many different purposes — and that remains, with all its tensions, central to modern political thought.

Regional Lens

The Scottish Enlightenment

Scotland in the eighteenth century was, on first impression, an unlikely center of world-historical intellectual achievement. It was a small, relatively poor country on the northern periphery of Britain, united with England in 1707 in a political union that many Scots found humiliating. Yet between roughly 1740 and 1790, Scotland produced an extraordinary cluster of thinkers whose contributions to moral philosophy, economics, history, sociology, and political theory shaped the modern world.

Several factors converged. Scotland had a well-developed system of parish schools and five universities (compared to England's two), producing a literate population and a relatively democratic educational culture. The universities — particularly Glasgow and Edinburgh — were less dominated by clerical orthodoxy than Oxford or Cambridge, allowing greater intellectual freedom. The experience of economic and political subordination to England, and the rapid social changes associated with commercialization, prompted Scottish thinkers to ask systematic questions about society, economy, and moral life.

David Hume (1711–1776) pushed empiricism to its limits, questioning whether we can have genuine knowledge of causation, personal identity, or religious belief. Hume's skepticism was scandalous to many contemporaries but enormously influential on later philosophy — Kant said Hume "awakened me from my dogmatic slumber." Adam Smith (1723–1790) made contributions in both moral philosophy and economics: his Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759) analyzed how humans form moral judgments through sympathy, while The Wealth of Nations (1776) argued that free markets produce social wealth more efficiently than mercantilist state control. Adam Ferguson (1723–1816) developed what might be called the first sociology, analyzing the stages of human social development and worrying that commercial society would erode the civic virtues needed for free government. The Scottish Enlightenment's distinctive contribution was its attention to social institutions — not just individual reason, but the systems of exchange, sentiment, habit, and law through which human beings actually form their characters and make their choices.

Global Connection

The Enlightenment, the Ottoman Empire, and East Asia

The Enlightenment is often discussed as a purely European or Atlantic phenomenon, but Enlightenment ideas circulated more widely, and non-European intellectual traditions engaged with them in complex ways.

The Ottoman Empire. The Ottoman Empire had its own tradition of intellectual reform that predated and paralleled the European Enlightenment. Ibrahim Müteferrika, who established the first Ottoman-Turkish printing press in Istanbul in 1727, argued for the value of rational geography, natural philosophy, and mathematics in ways that reflected engagement with European ideas. He was responding to Ottoman military defeats that made the question of European scientific superiority urgent. Ottoman reformers were selective in their engagement: they adopted scientific and technical knowledge while being more cautious about political philosophy. The natural rights tradition, with its implications for challenging dynastic authority and religious law, was more threatening than optics or astronomy.

Qing China. Jesuit missionaries at the Qing court served as cultural intermediaries between Europe and China in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. They introduced European astronomy, cartography, clocks, and natural philosophy to Chinese scholars and rulers. The Kangxi Emperor (r. 1661–1722) was personally interested in European mathematics and invited Jesuit scholars to teach him. In exchange, Jesuit missionaries were granted relative freedom to preach Christianity in China. Chinese intellectual life also fascinated European Enlightenment thinkers: Voltaire and others idealized Confucian China as a society governed by rational, secular principles — though this admiration was partly projection filtered through Jesuit reports.

The limits of Enlightenment globalization are as instructive as its reach. Enlightenment ideas traveled along commercial and imperial networks — primarily European networks — and were received selectively by societies with their own intellectual traditions and political priorities. Non-European thinkers were active agents in these exchanges rather than passive recipients.

Empire Study

The Encyclopédie and the French Enlightenment

Origins. The Encyclopédie, ou dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers began modestly — a contract to produce a French translation of an English reference work. Denis Diderot and Jean le Rond d'Alembert transformed it into the most ambitious publishing project of the century. What they produced over the next twenty-seven years was nothing less than an attempt to reorganize all human knowledge on rational principles.

Scope and Structure. The completed Encyclopédie filled twenty-eight volumes — seventeen of text and eleven of plates — containing approximately 72,000 articles written by more than 150 contributors, including Voltaire, Rousseau, Montesquieu, d'Alembert, and Turgot. It covered natural philosophy, mathematics, medicine, history, geography, philosophy, theology, literature, art, music, crafts, and technology. Its illustrations of crafts and manufacturing processes — showing the mechanisms of printing presses, glassworks, textile looms, and dozens of other industries — were unprecedented in their detail and accessibility. The work was organized alphabetically, a seemingly neutral choice with ideological implications: no subject was inherently more important than any other; theology sat beside technology, philosophy beside farming. Cross-references linked articles in ways that sometimes undermined religious or political orthodoxies.

Purpose and Politics. Diderot's purpose was stated explicitly: to collect and organize all human knowledge so that posterity could build on it, and to change the way people think. Articles on theology, philosophy, and political theory challenged church authority, religious persecution, and arbitrary government. French authorities recognized the project as a threat: the Encyclopédie was condemned in 1752, and its royal privilege revoked in 1759. The project continued clandestinely, eventually selling approximately 25,000 sets across Europe — a massive commercial success and a monument to Enlightenment confidence that knowledge could be democratized.

Map Study

The Republic of Letters and the Spread of Enlightenment Ideas

A map of the Enlightenment's geography would reveal several important patterns. The major centers of Enlightenment production — Paris, London, Edinburgh, Amsterdam, Geneva, Berlin, Vienna, Philadelphia — were all located in the Atlantic world, connected by commercial and postal networks allowing rapid exchange of texts and correspondence. Paris stands out as the intellectual capital, but its dominance was not unchallenged: Edinburgh produced Smith and Hume; Amsterdam was a major publishing center for books banned in France; Geneva was Rousseau's birthplace; Berlin attracted Voltaire to the Prussian court.

The spread of Enlightenment ideas followed commercial and imperial networks — along the same routes as goods: across the Atlantic to North America and the Caribbean, through Mediterranean trade routes to the Ottoman Empire, and along Jesuit missionary networks to East Asia. The Atlantic slave system, by connecting Europe, Africa, and the Americas in a single economic system, also connected them as zones of intellectual and political exchange — which is why the radical application of Enlightenment rights language emerged most powerfully in the French Caribbean.

Enlightenment spread was uneven. Rural populations across Europe remained largely outside the literate culture in which Enlightenment ideas circulated. Women were active participants but formally excluded from academic institutions. The ideas circulated primarily among educated, property-owning men — a small minority of any European or colonial society.

Primary Source Window

John Locke, Second Treatise of Government (1689)

Creator: John Locke · Date: 1689 · Type: Political philosophical treatise

Context. John Locke wrote the Two Treatises of Government during the political crisis surrounding the exclusion controversy and in preparation for what became the Glorious Revolution of 1688. The Second Treatise argues for limited government, natural rights, and the right of revolution. Published after the revolution succeeded, it became the foundational text of liberal political philosophy and the most influential political work of the Enlightenment.

Locke argues in the Second Treatise that the state of nature — the condition of human life before political society — is already governed by natural law, which obliges all persons to respect the rights of others. All human beings are by nature free, equal, and independent. Because the enforcement of natural law in the state of nature is insecure, people agree to form political societies and governments to protect their natural rights: life, liberty, and property. This social contract creates government, but does not give it unlimited power. Government derives its authority from the consent of the governed. If a government systematically violates the natural rights it was created to protect, it forfeits its legitimacy, and the people have the right to resist and replace it.

Analysis. Locke's Second Treatise provided a systematic, secular argument for limited government and the right of revolution that proved enormously influential. It gave political actors in the eighteenth century — American colonists, French revolutionaries, Haitian freedom fighters — a coherent theoretical framework for challenging existing authority. But the text has significant limits. Locke's definition of natural rights was contested: his inclusion of "property" as a natural right gave later writers a basis for arguing that the propertyless had no full political rights. His treatment of Indigenous peoples — arguing that those who did not cultivate land "in the European way" had not fully exercised their property rights — was used to justify colonial dispossession. Locke himself invested in the Royal African Company, which traded enslaved people — a biographical fact that sits uncomfortably alongside his rhetoric of natural liberty.

  2. Under what conditions does Locke argue that people have the right to resist or overthrow their government?
  3. How does Locke's definition of natural rights (life, liberty, and property) compare to the formulation in the American Declaration of Independence (life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness)?
  4. What groups does Locke's theory of natural rights implicitly or explicitly exclude, and why?
Evidence Window

The Printing Press and the Material Culture of Enlightenment

The Enlightenment was a material as well as an intellectual phenomenon. Its ideas circulated through physical objects — books, pamphlets, journals, newspapers — produced by a sophisticated commercial printing industry. The printing press itself — substantially unchanged in its basic mechanism from the fifteenth to the early nineteenth century — was a wooden or iron frame that pressed movable metal type against inked paper. A skilled printing team could produce about 1,500 pages per day.

Historians study surviving printing presses (preserved in museums across Europe and North America) alongside the physical books themselves: their format, paper quality, binding, price, and ownership marks. Cheaper formats made texts accessible to larger audiences. Marginalia — readers' handwritten notes in margins — provide direct evidence of how specific readers engaged with specific texts. Bookshop records, subscription lists, and customs seizures of smuggled books allow historians to trace patterns of distribution. Censorship records reveal which books were approved, which were suppressed, and which were smuggled — showing that suppression sometimes increased a text's circulation.

What can historians infer from this material evidence? That Enlightenment ideas circulated through commercial networks oriented toward profit, not only toward intellectual improvement. That the distribution of ideas was shaped by economic factors — price, format, distance from publishing centers. That readers were active agents, not passive recipients: they annotated, shared, argued about, and transformed the texts they read.

Historical Debate

Was the Enlightenment a Force for Liberation or for New Forms of Domination?

The question. The Enlightenment produced the most powerful intellectual tools for challenging tyranny, slavery, and arbitrary power in European history. But it also produced racial hierarchies, justified colonial conquest, excluded women and the poor from its vision of rights, and provided ideological support for empire. Was it primarily a force for human liberation, or did it create new and more sophisticated forms of domination?

Interpretation A — The Enlightenment as Liberation: The traditional interpretation argues that the Enlightenment represented a decisive step forward in human freedom and dignity. For the first time in European history, systematic secular arguments were made for natural rights, religious toleration, limited government, freedom of speech and press, and the abolition of slavery. The Atlantic revolutions drew on Enlightenment political theory to challenge hereditary privilege and colonial domination. Abolitionism — the first mass political movement organized around the principle that an existing social institution was morally wrong — was deeply indebted to Enlightenment natural rights arguments. The long-term expansion of rights to women, workers, colonized peoples, and racial minorities was made using Enlightenment language. The Enlightenment planted seeds that later generations harvested.
Interpretation B — The Enlightenment as New Domination: A more critical interpretation argues that the Enlightenment's celebration of reason was also a tool of power. The claim to universal reason was, in practice, the claim of specific groups — primarily European, propertied men — to define what counted as rational, civilized, and fully human. Racial typologies developed through Enlightenment methods of classification provided intellectual justification for colonial conquest and the slave trade. The "civilizing mission" drew on Enlightenment evolutionary frameworks. As Adorno and Horkheimer argued in Dialectic of Enlightenment (1947), the drive to master nature through reason could become a drive to dominate human beings. For postcolonial critics including Frantz Fanon and Aimé Césaire, Enlightenment universalism was a particular form of European cultural imperialism.

Evidence and significance. Both interpretations are supported by significant evidence. The natural rights tradition did contribute to abolition, democratic revolution, and the expansion of rights. Enlightenment thinkers did develop racial hierarchies that served colonial interests. These facts are not mutually exclusive. Most historians today hold a nuanced view: the Enlightenment was genuinely innovative in its critique of tyranny and its articulation of rights, but those critiques were incomplete, selective, and embedded in structures of power that limited their reach. How we resolve this debate shapes how we understand the intellectual inheritance of the modern world.

Comparison Across Societies

Enlightenment in France and Scotland

Shared commitments. Both the French and Scottish Enlightenments shared the broad Enlightenment commitments to reason, empirical inquiry, social reform, and religious toleration. Both produced major works in political philosophy, economics, and moral philosophy. Both were deeply engaged with the new natural philosophy emerging from the Scientific Revolution, and both were connected to the wider Republic of Letters.

Differences in style and focus. The French philosophes were primarily public writers and critics, operating in a culture of censorship that made their work politically fraught. Their characteristic mode was polemical — attacking specific abuses, exposing specific contradictions, using wit, irony, and satire as weapons against the establishment. The Scottish Enlightenment had a different character. Scottish thinkers worked primarily in universities and in relatively tolerant intellectual communities. Their characteristic mode was systematic — the development of comprehensive theories of moral philosophy, economics, history, and social science.

Different social contexts. The French philosophes were challenging a highly centralized, censorious monarchy with a powerful established church; their work was necessarily political. The Scottish thinkers operated in a relatively tolerant environment and were more preoccupied with understanding society than with attacking institutions. The Scottish universities' tradition of broad, accessible education contributed to the systematic, comprehensive character of Scottish Enlightenment thought.

Mutual influence. The two Enlightenments were in constant dialogue. Hume and Voltaire met and admired each other. Smith's work was known in France. The Republic of Letters made the Enlightenment genuinely international, even as national contexts shaped its local expressions.

Multiple Perspectives

Who Experienced the Enlightenment?

Educated Propertied Men: For educated men of property — the primary intended audience of Enlightenment texts — the period was genuinely exciting. New ideas were available in unprecedented quantity and variety. Coffeehouses, learned societies, and the Republic of Letters created communities of intellectual exchange. Enlightenment ideas provided frameworks for critiquing inherited institutions and imagining better ones. The political revolutions of the period offered opportunities for educated men to participate in designing new governments.
Women: Educated women of the upper and middle classes had complex and often frustrating relationships with the Enlightenment. The movement's rhetoric seemed to apply to them, but most Enlightenment thinkers explicitly excluded women from public life. Women participated actively in salon culture — hosting and shaping intellectual conversations — but were formally excluded from universities, academies, and learned societies. Wollstonecraft, de Gouges, and Madame du Châtelet (who translated Newton into French) pushed back against these exclusions. For the majority of women, especially working-class and rural women, the Enlightenment was irrelevant to their daily lives.
Enslaved People: The Enlightenment's relationship to slavery was deeply contradictory. Some enslaved people had access to Enlightenment ideas through enslavers' libraries, missionary education, or contact with free Black communities. Some, including Olaudah Equiano, used Enlightenment rhetoric in their own writing and advocacy. For the vast majority, however, the Enlightenment's celebration of liberty was experienced primarily as hypocrisy — a framework that claimed universal applicability while justifying their continued enslavement. The Haitian revolutionaries' radical application of Enlightenment rights language was, from this perspective, the movement's most honest moment.
Indigenous Peoples: Indigenous peoples appeared frequently in Enlightenment texts as objects of analysis and speculation. The "noble savage" was a projection of European fantasies onto real people, bearing no relation to actual Indigenous social complexity. Montesquieu, Rousseau, and others used idealized images of "natural" peoples to critique European institutions. Locke used arguments about Indigenous land use to justify colonial dispossession. Indigenous peoples had no voice in these debates. Their actual experience of the "Enlightened" colonial order was dispossession, disease, forced labor, and cultural destruction.
Artisans and Laboring People: The Encyclopédie's celebrated plates depicted artisan crafts and manufacturing processes with genuine respect. But most philosophes shared the assumptions of their class: that the laboring poor were too uneducated and too preoccupied with survival to be full participants in rational public life. The political revolutions inspired by the Enlightenment debated hotly about property qualifications for voting — the question of whether the propertyless poor deserved full citizenship was rarely resolved in their favor.
Cause and Effect

The Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment

Long-Term Causes

The humanist recovery and critical reading of ancient texts; Islamic mathematical and astronomical scholarship; European maritime expansion generating practical demands for better navigation; and the Protestant Reformation, which made independent intellectual inquiry more acceptable in Protestant territories.

Short-Term Causes

The publication of Copernicus's heliocentric model (1543); the development of the telescope and other instruments allowing more precise observation; the formation of learned societies; and the printing press, enabling rapid dissemination of new findings.

The Transformation

Nature was reconceived as a system of regular mathematical laws rather than a hierarchy of purposes. Knowledge was reconceived as the product of systematic observation and rational analysis rather than the inheritance of ancient authority. These methodological changes — more than any specific discovery — were the core of the Scientific Revolution.

Long-Term Consequences

The Scientific Revolution created the methodological foundations of modern science. It also contributed directly to the Enlightenment: if nature obeyed rational laws discoverable by human reason, why should society not be subjected to the same scrutiny? The Enlightenment applied that demonstration to politics, economics, law, and social organization — with consequences still unfolding today.

Historical complexity: The relationship between the Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment was not simple or automatic. Many participants in the Scientific Revolution — including Newton himself — were deeply religious and did not draw secularizing conclusions from their work. The transfer of scientific method to social and political questions was contested and incomplete. And the claims of reason in both science and politics served interests that reason alone could not justify.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Authority, Reason, and Knowledge from the Scientific Revolution to the Atlantic Revolutions

What Changed

  • Sources of intellectual authority shifted from ancient texts and institutional interpretation to empirical observation and mathematical demonstration
  • Political authority was increasingly justified through natural rights and social contract theory rather than divine right
  • The concept of historical progress — that human societies could improve through systematic effort — became newly prominent
  • Legal codes appealed to reason and utility rather than religious law
  • The Atlantic revolutions created republican and constitutional governments claiming popular sovereignty

What Stayed the Same

  • Religion remained the framework within which the vast majority of people understood their lives, deaths, and duties
  • Social hierarchies of class, gender, and race proved remarkably durable even in the face of Enlightenment critique
  • The institution of slavery expanded rather than contracted during the eighteenth century, despite Enlightenment critiques
  • The propertyless poor were consistently denied full political participation in Enlightenment-inspired political systems

Why this pattern matters: The uneven distribution of Enlightenment's transformations — concentrated in educated, propertied, European-descended populations; resisted by those with power to resist; applied selectively — helps explain both the achievements and the failures of the Enlightenment's political legacy. The modern world's democratic institutions and human rights frameworks bear the Enlightenment's stamp. So do the racial hierarchies, gendered exclusions, and colonial legacies those institutions were unable or unwilling to address.

Everyday Life and Social History

Reading, Debating, and Living in the Republic of Letters

For most people in eighteenth-century Europe, daily life was shaped by agricultural labor, religious observance, family obligation, and proximity to the land. But for those who participated actively in Enlightenment culture — the growing urban middling class, the educated aristocracy, the commercial and professional classes — the period transformed the texture of intellectual and social life in ways that felt immediate and exciting.

Reading. The expansion of print culture changed the experience of reading itself. Books were increasingly available in smaller, cheaper formats. Lending libraries and reading rooms allowed people to access texts they could not afford to purchase. Coffee and reading became socially connected: coffeehouses provided newspapers and journals for their customers. New genres — the novel, the periodical essay, the philosophical tale — made intellectual engagement accessible and entertaining.

Correspondence. The Republic of Letters functioned through an extraordinary volume of personal correspondence. Voltaire is estimated to have written more than 20,000 surviving letters. This correspondence was not simply private communication: letters were often circulated, read aloud, published, and responded to by networks of correspondents. Writing to a prominent philosopher was a way of participating in intellectual life even from a provincial location.

Religious Life. Despite the Enlightenment's secular tendencies, most people continued to organize their lives around religious practice. Church attendance, religious holidays, and the rhythms of liturgical time structured daily experience. Enlightenment critiques of religious institutions resonated primarily with the educated minority in urban centers. The religious revivals of the eighteenth century — Methodism in Britain, Pietism in Germany — reached far more people than the Encyclopédie did.

The Experience of Revolution. For ordinary people in France, the Revolution that Enlightenment ideas helped inspire transformed daily life simultaneously in liberating and terrifying ways. The abolition of feudal dues lifted material burdens from peasant households. The collapse of the Church removed an institution around which communal life had been organized for centuries. The Terror brought the guillotine to city squares. The Napoleonic period brought mass conscription, continental warfare, and new legal codes. The Revolution made ordinary people historical actors in unprecedented ways — and subjected them to unprecedented disruptions.

Chapter Summary

The Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment were among the most consequential intellectual transformations in the history of the modern world. Beginning in the mid-sixteenth century, European natural philosophers challenged the ancient cosmological and natural-philosophical frameworks that had organized educated understanding of the physical world for over a millennium. Through careful observation, mathematical analysis, and experimental method, they overturned the geocentric model of the universe, corrected ancient medical theory, and — in Newton's Principia Mathematica — produced a unified mathematical account of nature that demonstrated the power of human reason to discover universal laws.

The methods developed during the Scientific Revolution were revolutionary not only in natural philosophy but in intellectual culture more broadly. They modeled a way of knowing that privileged human reason and evidence over ancient authority and institutional sanction. This methodological revolution created the conditions for the Enlightenment's broader project: the application of reason to human institutions, laws, politics, and society.

The Enlightenment was not a unified movement but a diverse collection of conversations across Western Europe, the Atlantic colonial world, and, in filtered and partial ways, the Ottoman Empire and East Asia. French philosophes including Voltaire, Montesquieu, Rousseau, and Diderot challenged religious intolerance, political tyranny, and inherited privilege. The Scottish Enlightenment, centered in Edinburgh and Glasgow, produced systematic contributions to moral philosophy, economics, and social science — including Adam Smith's foundational account of free-market economics. The German Enlightenment, culminating in Kant's critical philosophy, articulated the philosophical foundations of human dignity and legal equality.

Natural rights theory and social contract theory were the Enlightenment's most politically consequential intellectual contributions. Locke's argument that legitimate government derived from the consent of the governed and was bound to protect natural rights provided the theoretical framework for the Atlantic revolutions. Montesquieu's analysis of separation of powers influenced constitutional design in the United States and France. Rousseau's concept of popular sovereignty and the general will shaped democratic theory and revolutionary practice. These ideas were not simply abstract philosophy. They were the intellectual vocabulary with which political actors in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries challenged existing authority and designed new institutions.

Print culture was the medium through which Enlightenment ideas traveled. Books, pamphlets, journals, encyclopedias, and newspapers created communities of readers who could discuss common texts across vast distances. The Encyclopédie was the most ambitious publishing project of the century. Salons hosted by educated women created social spaces for intellectual exchange in France. Coffeehouses served a similar function in Britain. The Republic of Letters accelerated the circulation of ideas and created an international intellectual community.

The Enlightenment's claims to universality were deeply selective in practice. Most Enlightenment thinkers excluded women from full participation in civic life. Mary Wollstonecraft and Olympe de Gouges challenged these exclusions by applying Enlightenment logic consistently. The Enlightenment's treatment of race and slavery was equally contradictory: several major thinkers condemned the Atlantic slave trade on natural rights grounds, while others developed racial hierarchies using Enlightenment methods of classification. The slave trade expanded enormously during the eighteenth century, even as Enlightenment critics argued for its abolition.

The most radical resolution of this contradiction came not from European Enlightenment writers but from the enslaved people of Saint-Domingue. The Haitian Revolution of 1791–1804 took the claims of Enlightenment natural rights theory seriously in a way that most of its theorists refused to do. Led by Toussaint Louverture and others, the enslaved people of Saint-Domingue overthrew the colonial slave system, defeated French, Spanish, and British armies, and established the first free Black republic in the Western Hemisphere. Haiti was the Enlightenment's most complete political achievement and its most uncomfortable reminder.

The Enlightenment's legacy is contested precisely because it was genuinely innovative and genuinely limited. It provided intellectual tools for challenging tyranny and expanding human freedom that have been used by many subsequent movements for justice. It also provided frameworks — racial hierarchy, civilizational ranking, developmental stages — that served colonial and imperial domination. Understanding the Enlightenment means holding both truths simultaneously: recognizing what was genuinely new about its commitment to human reason and dignity, and recognizing the social structures of power that shaped how, by whom, and for whose benefit those commitments were applied.

The Enlightenment matters for world history not primarily as a list of great thinkers or great texts, but as a demonstration of how ideas function in human societies: shaped by social conditions, carried by specific institutions, contested by those they exclude, applied selectively by those with power, and transformed by those who insist on taking them seriously. That lesson — about the power of ideas and the conditions that determine their use — is itself an Enlightenment lesson, drawn from the historical study of the Enlightenment's own record.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Scientific Revolution
The transformation of European natural philosophy between approximately 1543 and 1687, in which new methods of empirical inquiry replaced ancient authorities in astronomy, physics, medicine, and biology.
Empiricism
The philosophical view that knowledge is derived from sensory experience and observation; associated with Bacon, Locke, and Hume.
Rationalism
The philosophical view that reason is the primary source of knowledge; associated with Descartes.
Heliocentric model
The astronomical model, developed by Copernicus and refined by Kepler and Galileo, in which Earth and other planets orbit the Sun.
Geocentric model
The ancient astronomical model, associated with Ptolemy, in which Earth stands at the center of the universe.
Mechanical philosophy
The view that nature operates according to regular mathematical laws, like a machine, without supernatural intervention in natural processes.
Enlightenment
An intellectual movement of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries characterized by confidence in human reason, advocacy for natural rights, religious toleration, and belief in social progress.
Natural rights
Rights held to belong to all human beings by nature, independent of any government; articulated by Locke as life, liberty, and property.
Social contract
A theoretical framework in which the legitimacy of political authority derives from an agreement among individuals to form a government that protects their rights.
Popular sovereignty
The principle that political authority derives from the will and consent of the people, not from divine right or hereditary privilege.
Separation of powers
The constitutional principle that legislative, executive, and judicial functions should be exercised by distinct institutions; associated with Montesquieu.
Toleration
The acceptance of religious or political differences without persecution; a central Enlightenment demand against religious persecution and censorship.
Philosophes
French public intellectuals of the Enlightenment, including Voltaire, Diderot, Montesquieu, and Rousseau, who addressed broad audiences through commercial print culture.
Republic of Letters
The informal international network of scholars and writers who corresponded across national boundaries during the early modern period.
Print culture
The cultural world shaped by widespread printed media — books, pamphlets, newspapers, journals — and the conversations they enabled.
Salon
A gathering, typically hosted by an educated woman in France, at which writers, philosophers, aristocrats, and other educated people debated ideas.
Deism
A religious position accepting a creator God revealed by reason and nature, while rejecting revelation, miracles, and religious institutional authority.
Secularism
The principle that religion should be separated from political and intellectual life; the view that reason rather than revelation should guide public affairs.
Abolitionism
The movement for the abolition of the slave trade and slavery, which drew heavily on Enlightenment natural rights arguments.
Encyclopédie
The twenty-eight-volume reference work edited by Diderot and d'Alembert (1751–1772), the defining publishing project of the French Enlightenment.
General will
Rousseau's concept of the collective will of the community as a whole, oriented toward the common good, which he argued was the only legitimate basis of law.
Tabula rasa
Locke's concept of the mind at birth as a blank slate formed entirely by experience; foundational for empiricist philosophy and reform thought.
Inductive method
A mode of inquiry moving from specific observations to general principles; associated with Francis Bacon and experimental science.
Enlightened despotism
A form of rule in which absolute monarchs applied Enlightenment-influenced rational reforms while maintaining autocratic authority; associated with Frederick the Great, Catherine the Great, and Joseph II.
Scottish Enlightenment
The exceptional concentration of Enlightenment thought in Scotland (particularly Edinburgh and Glasgow) in the mid-to-late eighteenth century, characterized by contributions to moral philosophy, economics, and social science.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543)
Polish astronomer who proposed the heliocentric model; foundational figure of the Scientific Revolution whose work challenged a millennium of astronomical consensus.
Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)
Italian natural philosopher who made telescopic observations supporting heliocentrism and developed early physics of motion; his trial by the Inquisition dramatized the conflict between new science and religious authority.
Johannes Kepler (1571–1630)
German astronomer who discovered the mathematical laws of planetary motion, including that planets travel in ellipses; demonstrated the power of empirical method over theoretical expectation.
Isaac Newton (1643–1727)
English mathematician and natural philosopher; Principia Mathematica synthesized the Scientific Revolution into universal laws of mechanics and gravitation — the model of rational scientific explanation.
John Locke (1632–1704)
English philosopher; Two Treatises of Government foundational for liberal natural rights theory, government by consent, and the right of revolution against tyrannical rulers.
Montesquieu (1689–1755)
French writer; The Spirit of the Laws analyzed forms of government and articulated separation of powers, influencing constitutional design across the Atlantic world.
Voltaire (1694–1778)
Leading French philosophe; used wit and satire to attack religious intolerance, censorship, and political tyranny; his campaign around the Calas Affair exemplified Enlightenment advocacy.
Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)
Swiss-French philosopher; The Social Contract developed popular sovereignty and the general will; Discourse on the Origins of Inequality critiqued property as the source of social corruption.
Denis Diderot (1713–1784)
French philosopher; driving force behind the Encyclopédie, whose suppression and eventual commercial success illustrated the political stakes of Enlightenment publishing.
Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797)
English writer; A Vindication of the Rights of Woman applied Enlightenment logic to argue for women's equal education and civic participation, exposing the movement's gendered contradictions.
Adam Smith (1723–1790)
Scottish philosopher and economist; The Wealth of Nations foundational for classical economics and the case for free markets over mercantilist state control.
Toussaint Louverture (c. 1743–1803)
Leader of the Haitian Revolution; invoked Enlightenment rights language while fighting for the freedom of enslaved people; the revolution he led was the most radical application of Enlightenment principles in history.
David Hume (1711–1776)
Scottish philosopher who pushed empiricism to skeptical limits, questioning the foundations of causation and religious belief; enormously influential on Kant and modern philosophy.
The Royal Society (London, founded 1660)
British learned society promoting experimental science; published among the first scientific journals; model for scientific communication and peer review.
Saint-Domingue (Haiti)
French Caribbean colony where the Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) produced the first free Black republic and the most radical application of Enlightenment rights claims in history.
Paris
Capital of the French Enlightenment; center of salon culture, the publishing industry, and philosophe activity; home to the production of the Encyclopédie.
Edinburgh
Center of the Scottish Enlightenment; home of David Hume, Adam Smith, and Adam Ferguson; distinguished by a tradition of accessible university education and systematic social inquiry.
Olympe de Gouges (1748–1793)
French activist; Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Citizen (1791) directly challenged gender exclusions of the revolutionary Declaration of the Rights of Man; guillotined in 1793.

Tracing an Idea — Natural Rights from the Enlightenment to the Present

This project asks students to trace the concept of natural rights from its Enlightenment origins to its modern applications, using the MGU Library's primary source collections, reference databases, and historical databases.

  1. Origins. Research the concept of natural rights as developed by John Locke. Using the MGU Library's philosophy and history databases, find and read selections from the Second Treatise of Government. Identify Locke's core claims: What are natural rights? Where do they come from? What are their implications for government?
  2. Revolutionary applications. Research how natural rights theory was applied in the American Declaration of Independence (1776), the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789), and the Haitian Declaration of Independence (1804). Using the MGU Library's primary source collections, find texts of at least two of these documents. How did each document use, modify, or challenge Locke's formulation?
  3. Exclusions and challenges. Research the arguments made by women, enslaved people, and colonized peoples who pointed out the contradictions between Enlightenment rights claims and their own situation. Use the MGU Library to find texts by Mary Wollstonecraft, Olaudah Equiano, or Toussaint Louverture. How did they engage with natural rights theory?
  4. Modern applications. Research the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and at least one modern human rights campaign. How does natural rights language appear in these modern documents? How has the concept been extended, modified, or challenged since the Enlightenment?
  5. Synthesis. Write a research essay (1,500–2,500 words) tracing the concept of natural rights from Locke through the Atlantic revolutions to the present. Your essay should address: What has remained constant in natural rights theory? What has changed? Who has fought to expand the concept, and with what success? What limitations remain?

MGU Library resources: Primary source collections in philosophy and political theory; digital history databases including JSTOR, Project MUSE, and the Avalon Project at Yale Law School; the MGU Library's world history reference works and biographical dictionary.

Chapter Reflection

The Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment sit at the heart of the intellectual history of the modern world. They are not simply chapters in the history of ideas. They are the foundations — however contested and incomplete — of the political, scientific, and cultural frameworks within which people across much of the world still live and argue. Understanding them historically means understanding not only what they achieved but what they excluded, not only the power of their ideas but the social conditions that shaped how those ideas were used.

This chapter fits into the larger arc of World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change as a turning point between the early modern world (Parts V–VII) and the age of revolutions and industrialization (Parts VIII–XI). The Enlightenment provides the intellectual bridge: it was produced by the conditions of the early modern world — commercial expansion, religious conflict, print culture, the Scientific Revolution — and it helped produce the conditions of the modern world — the Atlantic revolutions, the critique of slavery, the beginnings of democratic government, and the eventual expansion of rights to previously excluded groups.

The contradictions of the Enlightenment — its universalism and its selectivity, its critique of tyranny and its defense of racial hierarchy, its celebration of reason and its exclusion of women — reveal the structural relationship between ideas and power. Ideas are never simply true or false in the abstract. They are produced within social structures, carried by specific groups, contested by those they exclude, and applied in ways that reflect the interests and assumptions of those with the power to apply them. The Enlightenment's ideas were powerful enough to inspire revolutions. They were also embedded deeply enough in structures of class, race, and gender to serve the interests of the powerful as well as the aspirations of the excluded.

The most important lesson of this chapter for world history may be that ideas do not travel in straight lines from theory to practice. Enlightenment ideas took generations to find their most powerful political applications. Their most radical applications — the Haitian Revolution, the feminist arguments of Wollstonecraft and de Gouges — came from people who were marginalized or excluded from the Enlightenment's original community of discourse. The ideas that claim to be universal are often most honestly applied by those whom they have excluded. That pattern — of excluded groups taking seriously the universalist claims of dominant traditions — runs throughout the history of the modern world.

As students move through the following chapters on the Atlantic revolutions, the Industrial Revolution, nineteenth-century nationalism and imperialism, and the tumultuous twentieth century, they will encounter the Enlightenment's ideas again and again: in the language of revolutionaries and constitution-makers, in the rhetoric of colonial administrators and anti-colonial movements, in the arguments of feminists and labor organizers, and in the frameworks of modern human rights law. Understanding where these ideas came from — what they achieved, what they excluded, and how they have been contested and expanded — is essential preparation for understanding the modern world and for thinking seriously about the kind of world we want to help build.

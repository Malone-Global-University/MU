Conflict, Terrorism, Peacekeeping, and International Institutions

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The end of the Cold War did not bring the end of history or the end of conflict. It brought a new and in many ways more unpredictable landscape of war, one fought less often between great powers and more often within states, between neighbors, and against networks that answered to no government at all."

On a January morning in 1992, the United Nations Security Council gathered for the first time at the level of heads of state and government. The Cold War, which for more than four decades had frozen the Security Council into paralysis through the competing vetoes of the United States and the Soviet Union, had just ended. Delegates spoke hopefully of a "new world order," a phrase that captured a widespread belief that with the ideological struggle between capitalism and communism resolved, the underlying machinery of international cooperation — the United Nations, international law, collective security — might finally function as its founders had intended in 1945. Secretary-General Boutros Boutros-Ghali was asked to prepare a report on how the United Nations might strengthen its capacity for preventive diplomacy, peacemaking, and peacekeeping. The resulting document, "An Agenda for Peace," radiated optimism about what international institutions could accomplish now that superpower rivalry no longer blocked them at every turn.

That optimism did not survive the decade. Within three years, the world watched as the United Nations proved unable to prevent the genocide of roughly eight hundred thousand Rwandans in 1994 or the massacre of more than eight thousand Bosnian Muslim men and boys at Srebrenica in 1995, in each case despite the presence of UN peacekeepers on the ground. Civil wars tore apart Somalia, Liberia, Sierra Leone, Yugoslavia, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. A new and different kind of violence — transnational terrorism carried out by non-state networks rather than by armies of recognized states — culminated in the attacks of September 11, 2001, which killed nearly three thousand people and reshaped the security priorities of nearly every government on earth. By the early twenty-first century, the confident language of a "new world order" had given way to more sober, more contested, and more urgent questions: How should the international community respond when a government turns violently against its own population? What obligations, if any, do states have to protect people beyond their borders? Can international law hold powerful leaders accountable for atrocities? And can peacekeeping, built originally to monitor ceasefires between states, be adapted to the far more dangerous task of protecting civilians inside collapsing states and stopping violence driven by networks that recognize no borders and no laws?

This chapter examines conflict and international security in the decades after the Cold War. It studies civil wars and state collapse, the resurgence of ethnic and nationalist violence, the rise of transnational terrorism, the evolution of humanitarian intervention and the doctrine known as the Responsibility to Protect, the transformation of United Nations peacekeeping from ceasefire monitoring into complex multidimensional operations, the development of international law and international courts capable of prosecuting genocide and war crimes, the global refugee crisis produced by decades of conflict, and the immense and often unresolved difficulty of building durable peace after violence ends.

The regions at the center of this chapter are genuinely global. The former Yugoslavia in southeastern Europe experienced the most destructive European conflict since 1945, including the first legally established genocide verdict in Europe since the Nuremberg trials. Rwanda, in Africa's Great Lakes region, endured one of the fastest and most efficient mass killings in modern history, followed by conflicts in the neighboring Democratic Republic of the Congo that killed millions more through violence, disease, and displacement over the following decade — sometimes called "Africa's World War." Afghanistan and Iraq became the sites of the two longest wars in United States history, wars launched in response to terrorism but that evolved into complicated exercises in state-building and counterinsurgency. Syria's civil war, beginning in 2011, produced the largest refugee crisis since World War II and drew in regional and global powers whose competing interests made peace extraordinarily difficult to achieve. Sierra Leone, Liberia, Somalia, Sudan and South Sudan, Sri Lanka, Colombia, and many other states experienced civil wars whose causes, dynamics, and resolutions varied enormously but which together defined this era as one shaped less by wars between great powers than by violence within and across fragile states.

What changed in this period was the character of organized violence itself. The great interstate wars that historians of the twentieth century studied — World War I, World War II, even many Cold War proxy conflicts — were fought primarily between the organized militaries of recognized states. After 1991, the overwhelming majority of armed conflicts were civil wars: fighting within states, often between government forces and rebel groups, frequently along ethnic, religious, or regional lines, and frequently complicated by the involvement of neighboring states, diaspora communities, international arms suppliers, and eventually transnational terrorist networks that exploited weak or collapsed state authority. What continued, painfully, was the vulnerability of civilians. In earlier centuries, and even through much of the twentieth century, military casualties often exceeded civilian casualties in armed conflict. In the civil wars and irregular conflicts that dominated the post–Cold War period, civilians were frequently not incidental casualties but deliberate targets — driven from their homes, subjected to ethnic cleansing, used as human shields, or killed in campaigns of terror designed to break the will of entire communities.

Historians and international relations scholars studying this period draw on an unusually wide range of evidence: United Nations reports and Security Council records, the transcripts and judgments of international criminal tribunals, human rights investigations by organizations such as Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, satellite imagery documenting mass graves and destroyed villages, refugee testimony collected by aid organizations, declassified government cables and intelligence assessments, and the growing body of academic research on civil war, terrorism, and peacebuilding that emerged as these became central subjects of political science and international relations. Because many of the events in this chapter occurred within living memory, and because some remain legally and politically contested, historians must exercise particular care in weighing evidence, distinguishing verified fact from propaganda, and acknowledging where the historical record remains incomplete or disputed.

This chapter connects directly to patterns studied throughout this textbook: the collapse of empires and the difficulties of decolonization examined in earlier chapters; the ideological struggles of the Cold War; the human rights movements that grew after 1945; and the deep, unresolved tensions between state sovereignty and the protection of human beings that have run through world history since the modern state system first took shape in seventeenth-century Europe. It also sets the stage for the final chapter of this textbook, which asks what patterns from the whole of world history — migration, state formation, exchange, conflict, and the search for order — help us understand the world of the twenty-first century.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how the character of armed conflict changed after the end of the Cold War, and why civil wars became the dominant form of organized violence.
  2. Trace the causes and consequences of state collapse in cases such as Somalia, Yugoslavia, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo.
  3. Analyze how ethnic and nationalist conflict is produced by political mobilization and historical grievance rather than by ancient or inevitable hatred.
  4. Describe the rise of transnational terrorism and evaluate how governments and international institutions responded to it after September 11, 2001.
  5. Evaluate the doctrine of humanitarian intervention and the Responsibility to Protect, including the arguments for and against overriding state sovereignty.
  6. Compare traditional UN peacekeeping with the more complex, multidimensional peace operations that developed after the Cold War.
  7. Assess the development of international humanitarian law, war crimes tribunals, and the International Criminal Court as tools of accountability.
  8. Interpret primary and secondary source evidence, including UN documents, tribunal judgments, and refugee testimony, as historical evidence.
  9. Explain the causes and global scale of forced displacement and the challenges refugees and internally displaced people face.
  10. Identify the major obstacles to durable peacebuilding after civil war and evaluate different approaches to postwar reconstruction and reconciliation.
  11. Compare the international response to conflicts in different regions, including Bosnia, Rwanda, Sierra Leone, and Syria, and consider why responses varied.
  12. Contextualize post–Cold War conflict and peacekeeping within longer world-historical patterns of state formation, empire, sovereignty, and human rights.

Historical Context

To understand conflict after the Cold War, students must first understand what the Cold War's end actually changed and what it left unchanged. For more than four decades after 1945, the rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union shaped nearly every regional conflict on earth. Civil wars in Angola, Nicaragua, Afghanistan, and dozens of other states were fueled, armed, and often prolonged by superpower competition, as each side supplied weapons, funding, and military advisers to opposing factions in proxy struggles that served larger ideological and strategic purposes. The United Nations Security Council, whose five permanent members hold veto power, was frequently paralyzed, as the United States and the Soviet Union blocked resolutions that threatened their respective allies or interests. Genuine collective security — the vision the UN's founders had articulated in 1945, in which the international community would act together against aggression — rarely functioned as intended.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, examined in earlier chapters, removed the ideological and strategic framework that had shaped conflict for two generations. In some respects this created new opportunities: the Security Council, no longer paralyzed by superpower rivalry, authorized more peacekeeping operations in the five years after 1991 than in the previous four decades combined, and briefly it seemed that international cooperation on security matters might genuinely flourish. But the end of the Cold War also removed constraints. Regimes that had received support from Washington or Moscow for strategic reasons, regardless of their internal legitimacy, sometimes collapsed once that support disappeared, leaving political vacuums that armed factions rushed to fill. Ethnic, religious, and regional tensions that had been suppressed or managed under Cold War–era authoritarian rule or federal structures — as in Yugoslavia and parts of the former Soviet Union — resurfaced violently once central authority weakened. And in parts of Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, weak postcolonial states whose borders had been drawn by European empires with little regard for ethnic, linguistic, or religious geography faced new pressures without the stabilizing, if often self-interested, patronage of Cold War superpowers.

The 1990s therefore produced a wave of conflicts that differed in important ways from the interstate wars and Cold War proxy conflicts of the preceding decades. Political scientists began speaking of "new wars" or "intrastate conflict" to describe fighting that occurred primarily within state borders, often involved irregular armed groups rather than professional militaries, frequently targeted civilian populations as a strategic objective rather than an unfortunate side effect, and was often financed not by superpower patrons but by control over natural resources such as diamonds, timber, or drugs, by diaspora remittances, or by criminal networks. Somalia's central government collapsed in 1991 amid clan-based fighting, producing decades of instability. Yugoslavia, a multiethnic federation that had held together under the authoritarian rule of Josip Broz Tito and, after his death, a weakening federal structure, fragmented into war beginning in 1991, with the most severe violence occurring in Bosnia and Herzegovina between 1992 and 1995. Rwanda's 1994 genocide grew out of decades of colonial-era ethnic categorization, political manipulation, and unresolved civil conflict. Sierra Leone and Liberia experienced brutal wars fueled substantially by control over diamond mining. The Democratic Republic of the Congo, following the 1994 Rwandan genocide and the subsequent overthrow of its own long-ruling dictator, became the site of a conflict so large, so multi-sided, and so costly in human life — estimates of deaths from war-related violence, disease, and starvation between 1998 and the mid-2000s range as high as five million — that it is sometimes called Africa's Great War or Africa's World War.

At the same time, a different kind of security threat was taking shape: transnational terrorism carried out by non-state networks operating across multiple countries, often justified by radical religious or political ideology, and frequently unconnected to any single state's foreign policy in a conventional sense. Organizations such as al-Qaeda, formed in the late 1980s partly out of networks that had fought against Soviet forces in Afghanistan, carried out increasingly ambitious attacks through the 1990s before the September 11, 2001, attacks on the United States transformed global security policy. The "war on terror" that followed reshaped alliances, produced two of the longest wars in modern history in Afghanistan and Iraq, and raised difficult and enduring questions about the balance between security and civil liberties, about the legality of military detention and interrogation practices, and about whether military force could ever fully defeat a decentralized ideological movement.

Running alongside these conflicts was a parallel and often frustrated effort to strengthen international institutions capable of preventing, managing, and punishing mass violence: the expansion and redesign of UN peacekeeping; the creation of ad hoc international criminal tribunals for the former Yugoslavia and Rwanda; the eventual establishment of a permanent International Criminal Court in 2002; and the adoption in 2005 of the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, which asserted that the international community bears a responsibility to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity when their own governments fail or refuse to do so. This chapter traces both halves of this story: the recurring, often catastrophic violence of the post–Cold War world, and the uneven, contested, sometimes inspiring and sometimes tragically inadequate international effort to prevent and respond to it.

Regional Orientation

This chapter is genuinely global in scope, and students should locate its major regions carefully. Southeastern Europe (the former Yugoslavia — Bosnia and Herzegovina, Croatia, Serbia, Kosovo) saw the collapse of a multiethnic socialist federation into war along ethnic and religious lines, drawing NATO and the UN into direct military and administrative roles in Europe for the first time since 1945. Central Africa's Great Lakes region (Rwanda, Burundi, the Democratic Republic of the Congo) experienced genocide and a subsequent regional war involving as many as nine African states, rooted partly in colonial-era ethnic categorization and partly in struggles over land, political power, and mineral resources. West Africa (Sierra Leone, Liberia) saw wars fueled by control over diamonds and by the regional spillover of armed groups and child soldiers. Southwest AsiaSouth Asia (Sri Lanka's long civil war) and the Horn of Africa (Somalia's prolonged state collapse) provide further examples of protracted intrastate conflict. Each of these regions connects to the others through shared international institutions — above all the United Nations Security Council, which authorizes peacekeeping and, in principle, collective responses to mass atrocity everywhere on earth — and through the global structures of arms trafficking, diaspora financing, humanitarian aid, and media coverage that link distant conflicts into a single, interconnected story of post–Cold War violence and the international response to it.

Section 59.1

Conflict After the Cold War

When the Cold War ended, many observers hoped that the removal of superpower rivalry would reduce global conflict. In one narrow sense, they were correct: the large-scale proxy wars in which the United States and Soviet Union armed opposing factions in places like Angola and Nicaragua wound down. But the total number of armed conflicts did not decline in the way many had hoped, and in the first years after 1991, the number of active civil wars around the world actually rose. What changed was the character of conflict rather than its frequency. Interstate war — conflict between the organized militaries of recognized states — became comparatively rare, though it did not disappear, as the Gulf War of 1991 and later the Russia-Georgia war of 2008 and the Russian invasion of Ukraine beginning in 2014 and escalating in 2022 demonstrated. Overwhelmingly, however, the wars of the post–Cold War decades were intrastate conflicts: civil wars fought within the borders of a single state, even when neighboring states, diaspora communities, or international actors became deeply involved.

Scholars who study this shift point to several contributing factors. First, the withdrawal of superpower patronage after 1991 destabilized regimes and rebel movements alike that had depended on Cold War-era support, sometimes triggering collapse and sometimes intensifying competition to fill the resulting vacuum. Second, weak postcolonial states — many of which had been held together more by authoritarian force, foreign patronage, or the personal authority of a single ruler than by strong, broadly legitimate institutions — proved especially vulnerable once those external props were removed. Third, economic globalization created new ways for armed groups to finance themselves independent of state sponsors, through control of valuable natural resources such as diamonds, timber, oil, or narcotics, or through diaspora remittances sent by expatriate communities abroad. Fourth, small arms proliferation — the vast stockpiles of assault rifles and other weapons built up during the Cold War and often left unsecured after conflicts ended — made it easier for irregular armed groups, including those with only a few hundred or a few thousand fighters, to wage sustained campaigns of violence.

These new wars differed from earlier conflicts in another crucial respect: the proportion of civilian casualties. Estimates vary and are contested by scholars, but many studies of twentieth-century conflict suggest that civilian deaths, as a share of total conflict deaths, rose substantially over the course of the century, and that in many post–Cold War civil wars, civilians made up the overwhelming majority of those killed, wounded, or displaced. This was not always incidental. In conflicts driven by ethnic or religious division, civilians were frequently targeted deliberately — through massacres, sexual violence used as a weapon of war, forced displacement, and the deliberate destruction of homes, farms, and infrastructure — as a strategic means of achieving territorial or political goals. This targeting of civilian populations as a central rather than incidental feature of warfare is one of the defining and most disturbing characteristics of the conflicts examined in this chapter.

Section 59.2

Civil Wars and State Collapse

State collapse — the breakdown of a central government's ability to maintain order, provide services, and exercise authority over its territory — is both a cause and a consequence of civil war. Somalia offers one of the starkest examples. After the overthrow of longtime dictator Siad Barre in January 1991, no successor government was able to establish control over the country. Rival clan-based militias fought for territory and resources, and for more than two decades Somalia had no effective central government, becoming the textbook case of a "failed state." The absence of governance had cascading effects: humanitarian catastrophe, including a severe famine in 1992 that prompted a U.S.-led humanitarian and later military intervention that ended disastrously with the 1993 "Black Hawk Down" battle in Mogadishu; the growth of piracy off Somalia's coast in the 2000s, which threatened international shipping and drew naval responses from multiple countries; and the eventual rise of the militant group al-Shabaab, which exploited the absence of state authority to build a insurgent base.

Yugoslavia's collapse followed a different trajectory but illustrates similar dynamics. The Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, established after World War II under Josip Broz Tito, held together six republics and multiple ethnic and religious communities — Serbs, Croats, Bosniaks (Bosnian Muslims), Slovenes, Macedonians, Montenegrins, and ethnic Albanians in Kosovo — through a combination of authoritarian one-party rule, a carefully balanced federal structure, and Tito's own considerable personal authority. After Tito's death in 1980, the federal system weakened, and by the late 1980s, nationalist politicians, most prominently Slobodan Milošević in Serbia, began mobilizing ethnic nationalism as a political strategy, often invoking historical grievances — including memories of World War II-era atrocities between the region's ethnic communities — to build support. When Slovenia and Croatia declared independence in 1991, war broke out, followed by a far more destructive war in Bosnia and Herzegovina beginning in 1992, where Bosnian Serb forces, with support from Serbia, pursued a campaign of ethnic cleansing against Bosniak and Croat populations that included the systematic use of concentration camps, mass killing, and sexual violence, culminating in the July 1995 Srebrenica massacre, in which more than eight thousand Bosniak men and boys were killed by Bosnian Serb forces in a UN-declared "safe area" that peacekeepers were unable to protect.

The Democratic Republic of the Congo (formerly Zaire) illustrates how state collapse can produce conflict on a continental scale. Weakened by decades of corrupt and predatory rule under Mobutu Sese Seko, Zaire's fragile state structures collapsed amid the regional spillover of the 1994 Rwandan genocide, as Hutu militia forces and refugees fled across the border into eastern Zaire, and Rwanda's new government pursued them militarily. Mobutu was overthrown in 1997, and a subsequent conflict beginning in 1998 — sometimes called the Second Congo War — eventually drew in the armed forces of at least eight other African states, competing over territory, political influence, and access to the DRC's vast mineral wealth, including gold, diamonds, and coltan (used in electronics manufacturing worldwide). Estimates of total deaths from the conflict and its consequences, including violence, disease, and malnutrition, range from roughly two to five million people between 1998 and the mid-2000s, making it among the deadliest conflicts anywhere in the world since 1945, though it received comparatively little sustained international media attention or diplomatic engagement relative to its scale.

Regional Lens

The Democratic Republic of the Congo

The Congo's history did not begin with the wars of the 1990s. The territory endured one of the most brutal colonial regimes in modern history under Belgian King Leopold II, whose personal rule over the "Congo Free State" (1885–1908) involved forced labor and violence that killed millions. Belgian colonial administration that followed left the territory with almost no trained administrators or infrastructure for self-government at independence in 1960, contributing to political instability that culminated in the assassination of independence leader Patrice Lumumba and decades of authoritarian rule under Mobutu Sese Seko, who was supported for Cold War strategic reasons by the United States and other Western powers despite widespread corruption and human rights abuses.

Understanding the Congo on its own terms requires recognizing the country's staggering scale and diversity — it is roughly the size of Western Europe, home to hundreds of ethnic and linguistic communities, and possesses extraordinary mineral wealth that has attracted both investment and exploitation for more than a century. The wars of the late 1990s and 2000s were not simply an eruption of "ethnic hatred" but were driven substantially by competition over control of mining regions, by the political and military spillover from Rwanda's genocide and its aftermath, and by the weakness of central state authority across such an enormous territory. Millions of Congolese civilians displaced by conflict, along with ongoing violence by numerous armed groups in the eastern provinces, continued into the 2020s, making the DRC one of the world's longest-running and most severe humanitarian crises — a reminder that "post-conflict" labels can obscure violence that, in many regions, has never fully ended.

Section 59.3

Ethnic Conflict and Nationalism

A common but misleading explanation for conflicts in Yugoslavia, Rwanda, and elsewhere holds that they resulted from "ancient ethnic hatreds" that had simmered for centuries and finally exploded once authoritarian control was removed. Historians and social scientists have largely rejected this explanation as both inaccurate and dangerous, because it implies that such violence is inevitable and beyond the reach of political choice or international response. The evidence instead points overwhelmingly toward a different explanation: ethnic conflict in the modern era is typically produced through deliberate political mobilization by leaders who invoke, exaggerate, and sometimes fabricate historical grievances in order to build political power, often in moments of economic crisis or political transition when other bases of legitimacy have weakened.

Rwanda illustrates this dynamic with particular clarity. The distinction between Hutu and Tutsi identities in Rwanda was not, before European colonization, a rigid ethnic or racial division; it more closely resembled a fluid social and economic distinction that could shift with circumstances such as cattle ownership. Belgian colonial administrators, drawing on pseudo-scientific racial theories current in early twentieth-century Europe, rigidified this distinction into a fixed racial category, issued identity cards recording "ethnicity," and favored the Tutsi minority in administration and education for much of the colonial period before reversing course and favoring the Hutu majority in the years before independence. This colonial legacy of rigid, state-recorded ethnic categorization provided the infrastructure — quite literally, in the form of identity documents — that later political leaders would exploit. In the early 1990s, facing civil war with a Tutsi-led rebel movement and economic crisis, extremist Hutu politicians and media, most infamously the radio station RTLM, engaged in a sustained campaign of dehumanizing propaganda against Tutsis, describing them as "cockroaches" and enemies of the nation, laying the ideological groundwork for the genocide that followed the assassination of Rwanda's president in April 1994.

Yugoslavia's ethnic violence followed a similar pattern of political mobilization rather than spontaneous ancient hatred. Serbs, Croats, and Bosniaks had lived together, often peacefully and with high rates of intermarriage, for generations under Tito's Yugoslavia. Nationalist politicians in the late 1980s and early 1990s revived and amplified memories of World War II-era atrocities — including massacres committed by fascist Croatian Ustaše forces against Serbs, and by various factions against one another — selectively and often exaggerating them for contemporary political purposes, framing the coming conflict as a defensive necessity rather than a war of choice. Understanding ethnic conflict as the product of political choice and mobilization, rather than inevitable historical destiny, is essential both for accurate historical analysis and for the practical work of conflict prevention, since it suggests that such violence can, at least in principle, be anticipated, resisted, and prevented through political and institutional means.

Historical Vocabulary
Sovereignty

Sovereignty refers to the principle that a state has supreme authority over its own territory and population, free from external interference. The modern international system has been organized around state sovereignty since at least the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, and the United Nations Charter itself, adopted in 1945, explicitly affirms the sovereign equality of member states and generally prohibits intervention in matters within a state's domestic jurisdiction.

The conflicts examined in this chapter repeatedly test the limits of sovereignty as a governing principle. If a government commits genocide or mass atrocities against its own population, does its claim to sovereign non-interference still hold? This tension between respecting state sovereignty and protecting human beings from their own governments runs through nearly every debate in this chapter, from humanitarian intervention to the Responsibility to Protect to the jurisdiction of international courts.

Section 59.4

Terrorism and Global Security

Terrorism — the use of violence against civilians or non-combatants to create fear and achieve political, religious, or ideological objectives — is not a new phenomenon in world history; anarchist bombings in the late nineteenth century, nationalist assassinations before World War I, and numerous other historical episodes qualify as terrorism by most definitions. What changed in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries was the emergence of transnational terrorist networks capable of planning and executing attacks across multiple countries, financed and organized independently of any single state sponsor, and animated by ideologies — most prominently a radical, violent interpretation of Islamist politics, though far from the only ideological source of terrorism in this period — that explicitly rejected the framework of the state system altogether.

Al-Qaeda, formed by Osama bin Laden and others in the late 1980s partly from networks of fighters who had participated in the war against Soviet occupation of Afghanistan during the 1980s, carried out a series of escalating attacks through the 1990s, including the 1998 bombings of U.S. embassies in Kenya and Tanzania and the 2000 attack on the USS Cole in Yemen, before the September 11, 2001, attacks on the World Trade Center and the Pentagon killed nearly three thousand people and transformed global security policy virtually overnight. The United States, invoking NATO's collective defense clause for the first time in the alliance's history, led an invasion of Afghanistan in October 2001 to remove the Taliban government that had sheltered al-Qaeda, beginning what became the longest war in American history, lasting until the U.S. withdrawal and the Taliban's return to power in 2021. The broader "war on terror" that followed reshaped intelligence-sharing, airport and border security, banking and financial regulation aimed at cutting off terrorist financing, and — controversially — the legal treatment of detained terrorism suspects, including practices such as indefinite detention at Guantánamo Bay and "enhanced interrogation techniques" that human rights organizations and many legal scholars characterized as torture.

The 2003 U.S.-led invasion of Iraq, justified partly by since-discredited claims that Saddam Hussein's government possessed weapons of mass destruction, was not originally framed as a counterterrorism operation, but it produced a prolonged insurgency and sectarian civil war that created conditions in which extremist groups flourished, including al-Qaeda in Iraq, whose successor organization eventually became the Islamic State, or ISIS. Exploiting the chaos of the Syrian civil war after 2011 and continued instability in Iraq, ISIS seized large territories across both countries in 2014, declared a "caliphate," and combined battlefield territorial control with sophisticated propaganda, including graphic videos of executions distributed globally through social media, to attract recruits from dozens of countries. An international coalition eventually drove ISIS from its territorial holdings by 2019, but the organization persisted as a decentralized insurgent and terrorist network, and its ideology inspired attacks carried out by individuals and small cells with no direct organizational connection, a pattern security analysts termed "lone wolf" or inspired terrorism, which proved extremely difficult for traditional counterterrorism methods to prevent.

Historical Vocabulary
Asymmetric Warfare

Asymmetric warfare describes conflict between opponents of vastly unequal conventional military strength, in which the weaker party avoids direct confrontation with the stronger party's military advantages and instead relies on tactics such as guerrilla warfare, terrorism, insurgency, and the exploitation of civilian populations and political constraints faced by the stronger side. Much of the conflict examined in this chapter — from insurgencies in Afghanistan and Iraq to the operations of groups like al-Qaeda and ISIS — is asymmetric in character, which helps explain why overwhelming conventional military superiority, such as that possessed by the United States and its allies, did not translate into quick or decisive victory in prolonged counterinsurgency campaigns.

Section 59.5

Humanitarian Intervention

The catastrophic failures of the international community to prevent genocide in Rwanda and mass killing in Bosnia during the 1990s prompted intense debate about whether and when outside powers should intervene militarily inside a sovereign state to stop mass atrocities — a practice generally termed humanitarian intervention. Proponents argued that state sovereignty could not be an absolute shield behind which governments committed genocide or ethnic cleansing against their own populations, and that the international community bore a moral obligation to act when it had the capacity to prevent mass death. Critics — including many governments in the developing world that had experienced European colonial domination and remained wary of great-power intervention justified by humanitarian language — warned that "humanitarian intervention" could become a pretext for powerful states to violate the sovereignty of weaker ones for self-interested reasons, and that inconsistent application (intervening in some crises while ignoring others of comparable severity) revealed that geopolitical interest, not humanitarian principle, often actually determined which crises received a military response.

NATO's 1999 intervention in Kosovo, undertaken without explicit UN Security Council authorization after Russia and China indicated they would veto such authorization, became a pivotal and controversial case. NATO bombed Serbian forces for seventy-eight days to halt a campaign of ethnic cleansing against Kosovo's ethnic Albanian population, ultimately forcing a Serbian withdrawal and establishing international administration over Kosovo, which declared independence in 2008. Supporters described Kosovo as a justified humanitarian action; critics, including Russia, pointed to the lack of Security Council authorization as a dangerous precedent for unilateral military action outside international law.

Partly in response to these unresolved debates, the International Commission on Intervention and State Sovereignty proposed in 2001 a new framework called the Responsibility to Protect, formally adopted by the United Nations at the 2005 World Summit. R2P held that sovereignty entails responsibility: states have a primary obligation to protect their own populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, and where a state manifestly fails to do so — whether through inability or unwillingness, including when a state is itself the perpetrator — the international community has a responsibility to take collective action, through the UN Security Council, up to and including military intervention as a last resort. R2P represented a significant, if incomplete and contested, shift in international norms regarding sovereignty, though its practical application remained inconsistent: it was invoked to authorize NATO's 2011 intervention in Libya, which many governments later criticized as having exceeded its narrow civilian-protection mandate and contributed to Libya's subsequent state collapse, while it was never successfully invoked to authorize meaningful international military action to stop the far larger death toll of the Syrian civil war after 2011, largely because Russia and China used their Security Council vetoes to block such action.

Section 59.6

Peacekeeping and the United Nations

United Nations peacekeeping was not explicitly provided for in the UN Charter; it developed pragmatically, beginning with early Cold War-era operations that primarily involved monitoring ceasefires between states with the consent of the parties involved, using lightly armed troops authorized to use force only in self-defense — an approach sometimes called "traditional" or "first-generation" peacekeeping. The end of the Cold War transformed both the volume and the character of peacekeeping. Freed from superpower gridlock, the Security Council authorized far more operations, and these operations increasingly took on far more ambitious "multidimensional" mandates: not merely monitoring ceasefires between state armies, but protecting civilians within ongoing civil wars, disarming and demobilizing combatants, organizing and monitoring elections, supporting the reconstruction of police and judicial systems, and assisting the return of refugees and displaced persons.

This expansion produced both notable successes and devastating failures. Peacekeeping operations helped stabilize transitions and end conflicts in countries such as Namibia, Mozambique, El Salvador, and Cambodia during the 1990s. But peacekeeping's most searing failures also occurred during this decade, and both centered on the same fundamental problem: UN peacekeepers, operating under mandates and rules of engagement designed for traditional ceasefire monitoring rather than for the protection of civilians in active conflict, proved unable to stop mass killing even when peacekeepers were present in significant numbers. In Rwanda, the small UN peacekeeping force present when the genocide began in April 1994 was actually reduced by the Security Council in the crucial early weeks of the killing, after ten Belgian peacekeepers were killed and troop-contributing countries, above all Belgium, pressed for withdrawal rather than reinforcement; the force commander, Canadian general Roméo Dallaire, had earlier warned UN headquarters of planned violence and requested authorization to seize weapons caches, a request that was denied. In Bosnia, Dutch peacekeepers stationed in the UN-declared "safe area" of Srebrenica in July 1995 were unable to prevent Bosnian Serb forces under General Ratko Mladić from separating men and boys from women and children and executing more than eight thousand of them over several days, one of the worst episodes of mass killing in Europe since World War II.

These failures prompted extensive institutional reflection. The UN commissioned independent inquiries into both Rwanda and Srebrenica that produced unusually candid acknowledgments of institutional failure. The 2000 Brahimi Report, commissioned by Secretary-General Kofi Annan (who had himself headed the UN's peacekeeping department during the Rwandan genocide), recommended more robust mandates, clearer rules of engagement authorizing peacekeepers to use force to protect civilians, better resourcing, and more realistic assessments of what peacekeeping operations could actually accomplish before they were authorized. Subsequent operations, including large deployments in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Sudan/Darfur, South Sudan, and Mali, generally adopted more explicit civilian protection mandates, though troop shortages, inadequate equipment, ambiguous chains of command among multinational forces, and continuing political constraints from troop-contributing and Security Council member states meant that the gap between mandate and capacity remained a persistent challenge throughout the following decades.

Global Connection

UN Peacekeeping as a Global System

United Nations peacekeeping connects an extraordinary range of countries and interests into a single global system. The Security Council, dominated by its five permanent veto-holding members (the United States, Russia, China, the United Kingdom, and France), authorizes and funds operations, but the troops who actually serve as peacekeepers come overwhelmingly from a different set of countries — in recent decades, nations such as Bangladesh, India, Pakistan, Ethiopia, Rwanda, and Nepal have consistently ranked among the largest troop contributors, often motivated by a combination of genuine commitment to international peace, the financial reimbursement UN peacekeeping provides to contributing governments, and the desire to build military experience and international standing.

This creates a striking global division of labor: wealthy Security Council members largely determine where and under what mandate peacekeeping occurs, while developing countries supply most of the personnel who serve on the ground and bear most of the risk of casualties. Understanding peacekeeping as a global system — connecting Security Council diplomacy in New York, troop deployments from South Asia and Africa, and civilians in conflict zones from the Balkans to Central Africa — illustrates how post–Cold War security has become an interconnected worldwide enterprise rather than a matter for any single region or power.

Section 59.7

International Law and War Crimes

The post–Cold War period saw the most significant expansion of international criminal justice since the Nuremberg and Tokyo tribunals following World War II. The UN Security Council established the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) in 1993, while the Bosnian war was still ongoing, and the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR) in 1994, in the genocide's immediate aftermath. These ad hoc tribunals prosecuted individuals — not states — for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity, establishing important legal precedents. The ICTR's 1998 judgment against former Rwandan mayor Jean-Paul Akayesu was the first conviction for genocide by an international tribunal and the first to recognize that rape and sexual violence could constitute acts of genocide when used to destroy a targeted group. The ICTY's judgments regarding Srebrenica, including the conviction of Bosnian Serb general Radovan Karadžić and Ratko Mladić decades after the massacre, formally established in international law that the killings at Srebrenica constituted genocide — a legal conclusion that Serbian nationalist politics continued to dispute long after the court's judgment.

Building on the experience of these ad hoc tribunals, the international community negotiated the Rome Statute, establishing a permanent International Criminal Court (ICC), which came into force in 2002 and began operating from The Hague in the Netherlands. Unlike the ICTY and ICTR, which were created for specific conflicts and had defined end dates, the ICC was designed as a permanent institution with potential jurisdiction over genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and the crime of aggression committed after 2002 by nationals of member states or on the territory of member states, or referred to it by the UN Security Council. The ICC represented a landmark development in the long historical effort to hold individuals, including heads of state, personally accountable under international law rather than allowing sovereign immunity to shield perpetrators of mass atrocity.

The ICC's record, however, illustrates the continuing limits of international law when it collides with the realities of state power. Several major states — including the United States, Russia, China, and India — never joined the court, limiting its jurisdiction and its enforcement capacity, since the ICC has no independent police force and depends on member states to arrest and surrender indicted individuals. Sitting heads of state indicted by the court, including Sudan's Omar al-Bashir (indicted in 2009 for genocide and war crimes in Darfur) and, more recently, Russian president Vladimir Putin (indicted in 2023 in connection with the war in Ukraine), have generally been able to avoid arrest by remaining within, or traveling only to, states unwilling to enforce ICC warrants. Critics, particularly from African states, have also pointed out that the large majority of the ICC's early cases involved African defendants, raising questions about whether international justice has been applied even-handedly across regions and levels of power — a criticism the court has taken steps to address by opening investigations into situations beyond Africa, including in Afghanistan, Palestine, and Ukraine.

Section 59.8

Refugees and Displacement

The conflicts examined throughout this chapter produced displacement on a scale unmatched since the aftermath of World War II. By the early 2020s, the UN High Commissioner for Refugees estimated that more than one hundred million people worldwide had been forcibly displaced from their homes by conflict, violence, and persecution — a figure that includes refugees who have crossed international borders, internally displaced persons who remain within their own country's borders but cannot return home, and asylum seekers awaiting determination of their status. Syria's civil war alone displaced more than half the country's pre-war population, with over six million Syrians registered as refugees, primarily in neighboring Turkey, Lebanon, and Jordan, and millions more internally displaced within Syria itself. Conflicts in Afghanistan, South Sudan, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Myanmar (whose Rohingya Muslim minority faced a campaign of violence and expulsion that the UN and other bodies characterized as ethnic cleansing beginning in 2017), and Venezuela (whose economic and political collapse, though not primarily a conflict in the conventional sense, produced one of the largest displacement crises in the Western Hemisphere) each contributed further to this global total.

The legal and institutional framework for refugee protection dates to the 1951 Refugee Convention, drafted in the aftermath of World War II and the displacement it caused, which defines a refugee as someone with a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group, and establishes the principle of non-refoulement — the obligation not to return refugees to a country where they would face persecution. This framework, designed for a mid-twentieth-century world of comparatively contained, state-based conflicts, has come under increasing strain in the post–Cold War era of civil war, state collapse, and climate-linked displacement, which produces categories of forced migration — including displacement driven by generalized violence and state collapse rather than individualized persecution, and displacement driven by environmental factors — that the original convention did not fully anticipate.

Host countries, frequently poorer neighboring states rather than wealthy nations, have borne a disproportionate share of the burden of hosting refugees: Turkey has hosted the largest number of registered refugees of any country in the world for much of the past decade, primarily Syrians, while Pakistan and Iran have hosted millions of Afghan refugees across four decades of conflict, and Uganda has become one of Africa's largest refugee-hosting countries, primarily for people fleeing South Sudan and the DRC. This pattern — in which comparatively poor states neighboring conflict zones host the overwhelming majority of the world's refugees, while wealthier and more distant states host a much smaller proportion despite often intense domestic political controversy over migration — has become one of the more consequential and often overlooked features of the global refugee system, with significant implications for economic development, social services, and political stability in host countries themselves.

Section 59.9

The Challenges of Peacebuilding

Ending active fighting is only the first, and often the easiest, step toward durable peace. Peacebuilding — the longer-term process of addressing the underlying causes of conflict, rebuilding institutions, fostering reconciliation, and creating conditions in which violence does not recur — has proven to be exceptionally difficult, and scholarly research on civil wars has found that a substantial proportion of conflicts that end through negotiated settlement rather than decisive military victory relapse into renewed violence within a decade. Understanding why peace is so difficult to sustain is essential to evaluating the post–Cold War record of conflict resolution.

Several recurring obstacles appear across many post-conflict societies. Security sector reform — disarming and demobilizing former combatants, reintegrating them into civilian life, and building trustworthy police and military institutions — often proves enormously difficult, particularly where former combatants have few alternative economic opportunities and where security forces themselves were implicated in wartime atrocities. Transitional justice — the effort to address wartime crimes through mechanisms such as trials, truth commissions, reparations, or memorialization — must navigate a difficult balance between accountability, which victims and human rights advocates often insist upon as essential to genuine reconciliation, and the political necessity, in many negotiated settlements, of offering amnesty or power-sharing arrangements to perpetrators in order to secure their cooperation with a peace agreement in the first place. South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, established after the end of apartheid and examined in an earlier chapter, offered one influential model emphasizing truth-telling and conditional amnesty over prosecution; Rwanda's post-genocide government instead pursued both formal international prosecution through the ICTR and a massive program of community-based traditional justice courts, called gacaca, to process the enormous number of genocide-related cases that formal courts could never have handled alone.

Economic reconstruction presents further difficulties: war-torn economies typically require enormous investment in rebuilding infrastructure, restarting agriculture and commerce, and creating employment, particularly for demobilized young fighters who might otherwise be drawn back toward violence; yet post-conflict states frequently lack functioning tax systems, face high levels of corruption, and depend heavily on international aid that can create dependency or be captured by political elites rather than reaching the broader population. Political power-sharing arrangements, often central to peace agreements that end civil wars by guaranteeing formerly warring factions a share of government positions, can stabilize immediate post-conflict transitions but sometimes entrench the very ethnic or factional divisions that produced conflict in the first place, as critics have argued occurred in Bosnia's Dayton-era constitutional structure, which many analysts view as having stabilized peace while simultaneously freezing ethnic division into the country's permanent political architecture.

Evidence Window

Mass Graves, Satellite Imagery, and Forensic Evidence at Srebrenica

Much of what historians and international courts know about the Srebrenica massacre comes not from written documents but from physical and forensic evidence: mass graves, and later "secondary" graves to which Bosnian Serb forces moved remains in an apparent effort to conceal the killings, satellite imagery from U.S. intelligence agencies showing freshly disturbed earth consistent with mass burial, and DNA identification techniques developed by organizations such as the International Commission on Missing Persons, which has identified the remains of thousands of Srebrenica victims by matching DNA from exhumed remains to samples provided by surviving family members.

This forensic and physical evidence proved essential to the ICTY's legal proceedings, providing corroboration independent of witness testimony and helping establish with a high degree of certainty both the scale of the killing and the deliberate, organized effort to conceal it — a key factor courts considered in determining that the killings constituted genocide rather than a spontaneous massacre in the heat of combat. Limits remain: not all victims have been identified, some remains are too fragmentary or degraded for DNA matching, and the reconstruction of exact events at each grave site depends on painstaking, resource-intensive forensic archaeology that continues decades after the massacre itself.

Historical Debate

Does Humanitarian Intervention Do More Good Than Harm?

The question: Since the 1990s, scholars, policymakers, and international lawyers have debated whether military intervention justified on humanitarian grounds — overriding a state's sovereignty to stop mass atrocities — produces better outcomes than restraint, or whether such interventions frequently cause as much harm as they prevent.

Interpretation A — Intervention Can Save Lives and Is Sometimes Morally Necessary: Proponents point to NATO's 1995 intervention in Bosnia, which helped end a war that had already killed over one hundred thousand people, and argue that earlier, more forceful international action in Rwanda could have prevented or drastically reduced the scale of the 1994 genocide. They argue that absolute deference to sovereignty effectively grants governments a license to commit atrocities against their own populations without external consequence, and that some moral obligations — particularly the prevention of genocide — must outweigh sovereignty in extreme cases.
Interpretation B — Intervention Is Inconsistent, Often Self-Interested, and Frequently Worsens Outcomes: Critics point to Libya's 2011 intervention, ostensibly authorized under R2P to protect civilians, which contributed to the overthrow of Muammar Gaddafi's government but was followed by years of state collapse, civil war, and a thriving slave trade and migrant-smuggling network — arguably a worse humanitarian outcome than the crisis intervention was meant to prevent. They also point to the wide gap between rhetoric and action, noting that R2P was never meaningfully applied to Syria despite a far higher death toll than Libya, suggesting that geopolitical calculation, not consistent humanitarian principle, determines when intervention actually occurs.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the documented outcomes of specific interventions (Bosnia, Kosovo, Libya) and non-interventions (Rwanda, Syria), UN Security Council voting records showing which interventions were authorized and which were blocked, and post-intervention assessments of political stability, casualties, and displacement in intervened states.

Why it matters: This debate shapes ongoing policy decisions about when, whether, and how the international community should respond to unfolding mass atrocities, with enormous human consequences either way.

Student task: Evaluate both interpretations using the case studies in this chapter. Is it possible to design criteria for humanitarian intervention that would be applied consistently rather than selectively? What would such criteria need to include?

Comparison Across Societies

The Rwandan Genocide and the Bosnian War

Rwanda and Bosnia occurred within months of each other in the mid-1990s and are often discussed together as the defining failures of post–Cold War international protection, but they differed in important ways. Rwanda's genocide was extraordinarily fast and intense — roughly eight hundred thousand people killed in approximately one hundred days, an average rate of killing that some scholars argue exceeded even the pace of the Nazi Holocaust — carried out largely with low-technology weapons including machetes, by ordinary citizens as well as organized militia, often against neighbors, in a campaign explicitly organized around eliminating an entire ethnic group. Bosnia's violence unfolded over more than three years, involved more conventional military forces including artillery and organized infantry units, and centered on ethnic cleansing — the forcible removal of a population from a territory — though it culminated at Srebrenica in a mass killing that courts later determined met the legal definition of genocide.

Both cases share crucial similarities: both were preceded by identifiable warning signs that international observers and intelligence services documented in advance; both involved UN peacekeepers present on the ground who were unable, due to mandate limitations, inadequate force levels, or unwillingness among troop-contributing states to authorize a more robust response, to prevent mass killing; and both produced lasting institutional reform efforts, including new tribunals, that shaped subsequent international law. Comparing the two cases helps students see both the particular historical, political, and social conditions that shaped each crisis and the common structural failures — in early warning, political will, and peacekeeping mandate and capacity — that ran through both.

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing Forced Displacement

From the perspective of a refugee fleeing conflict: Displacement often means the loss of home, community, livelihood, and legal status in a single traumatic event, followed by uncertain years — sometimes decades — in refugee camps or urban settings with limited rights to work, restricted movement, and dependence on humanitarian assistance, alongside the enduring hope, often unrealized, of eventual return or resettlement.
From the perspective of a host-country government and community: Hosting large refugee populations, especially for countries with limited resources, strains public services, housing, labor markets, and infrastructure, and can generate domestic political tension, even as many host communities also demonstrate significant solidarity and, in some cases, benefit economically from refugee labor, entrepreneurship, and international aid directed to host regions.
From the perspective of international humanitarian organizations: Agencies such as UNHCR and NGOs operate with chronically insufficient funding relative to global need, must navigate complex security environments and sometimes hostile host governments, and face difficult ethical and practical trade-offs between providing immediate humanitarian relief and addressing the longer-term political causes of displacement that lie beyond their mandate to resolve.
From the perspective of policymakers in wealthy, distant states: Refugee and asylum policy is often shaped by a combination of humanitarian commitment, domestic political pressure (including anti-immigration sentiment), international legal obligations under the Refugee Convention, and strategic considerations, producing policies that frequently fall short of both humanitarian ideals and the scale of global need.
Cause and Effect

The Rwandan Genocide of 1994

Long-Term Causes

Belgian colonial-era rigidification of Hutu-Tutsi identity into fixed racial categories recorded on identity cards; decades of periodic ethnic violence and displacement following independence in 1962; deep economic crisis and land pressure in the early 1990s; a civil war beginning in 1990 with the Tutsi-led Rwandan Patriotic Front.

Short-Term Causes

Extremist Hutu propaganda, especially via RTLM radio, dehumanizing Tutsis in the months before the genocide; the assassination of President Juvénal Habyarimana on April 6, 1994, when his plane was shot down, used by extremists as the trigger to launch pre-planned killing.

The Event

Approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus killed over roughly 100 days between April and July 1994, carried out by government forces, militia (Interahamwe), and ordinary citizens; UN peacekeeping force reduced rather than reinforced during the crisis.

Effects

RPF military victory ends the genocide and installs a new government; massive refugee flows into neighboring Zaire; refugee camps become bases for genocide perpetrators, contributing directly to the First and Second Congo Wars; ICTR established; profound, lasting international reckoning over peacekeeping failure and later development of R2P.

Historical complexity: The genocide cannot be reduced to a single trigger. Habyarimana's assassination provided the immediate spark, but the killing had been planned in advance by extremist networks exploiting decades of colonial-era categorization, civil war, and economic crisis — and its consequences extended for years afterward into a regional conflict of even larger scale.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The United Nations Security System, 1945–Present

What Changed

  • Peacekeeping expanded dramatically in volume and evolved from ceasefire monitoring to multidimensional civilian protection mandates
  • Ad hoc and permanent international criminal tribunals were created to prosecute genocide and war crimes for the first time since Nuremberg
  • The Responsibility to Protect doctrine formally qualified the previously near-absolute international norm of non-interference in sovereign states
  • Counterterrorism became a central, well-resourced focus of international security cooperation after 2001

What Stayed the Same

  • The Security Council's five permanent members retain veto power, meaning collective action remains impossible whenever a great power's core interests are at stake (as with Russia and Syria, or Russia and Ukraine)
  • Wealthy and powerful states continue to face far less accountability under international law than weaker states
  • Troop-contributing countries for peacekeeping remain overwhelmingly developing nations, while decision-making authority remains concentrated among wealthy Security Council members
  • The fundamental tension between state sovereignty and international protection of civilians remains legally and politically unresolved

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of great-power veto power and unequal accountability, alongside genuine institutional innovation in peacekeeping and international law, illustrates how international institutions can evolve substantially in capacity and ambition while the underlying distribution of power among states remains a stubborn continuity shaping what those institutions can actually accomplish.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living Amid Conflict and Displacement

Behind the diplomatic history of Security Council resolutions and tribunal judgments lie the ordinary, disrupted lives of millions of people. In besieged cities such as Sarajevo, which endured the longest siege of a capital city in modern warfare history — nearly four years during the Bosnian war — residents adapted daily routines to snipers and shelling, queued for water and humanitarian aid, and improvised systems of community support amid the collapse of normal infrastructure. In Rwanda, survivors of the genocide faced the extraordinary task of rebuilding family and community life alongside the perpetrators of the violence, since the sheer scale of participation in the killing meant that reconciliation, however imperfect, had to occur between neighbors who had directly harmed one another. In refugee camps from Jordan to Uganda to Bangladesh, children have grown to adulthood having spent their entire lives displaced, attending informal schools run by aid organizations, with uncertain legal status and limited prospects for return, resettlement, or full integration into host societies. Understanding this chapter's history requires holding together both the institutional, diplomatic level — Security Council votes, tribunal indictments, peacekeeping mandates — and the immediate, lived experience of civilians for whom these abstractions determined matters of life, death, home, and family.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the transformed landscape of armed conflict and international security in the decades following the end of the Cold War. The collapse of superpower rivalry did not bring the peaceful "new world order" many hoped for in the early 1990s; instead, it removed constraints that had previously shaped regional conflicts and exposed the fragility of many postcolonial and post-communist states, producing a wave of civil wars, state collapse, and mass atrocity that tested international institutions built for a different kind of conflict.

Somalia's collapse into clan-based civil war beginning in 1991 illustrated the human cost of state failure and the difficulty of external intervention, as the disastrous 1993 U.S. military engagement in Mogadishu demonstrated. Yugoslavia's fragmentation into war, driven by nationalist political mobilization rather than ancient ethnic hatred, produced the deadliest conflict in Europe since 1945, culminating in the Srebrenica genocide of 1995. Rwanda's 1994 genocide, rooted in colonial-era ethnic categorization and ignited by political extremism amid civil war, killed approximately eight hundred thousand people in roughly one hundred days while UN peacekeepers, under an inadequate mandate, were unable to intervene. The subsequent regional spillover into Zaire and the Democratic Republic of the Congo produced conflicts on a continental scale that killed millions more over the following decade.

Alongside these civil wars, transnational terrorism emerged as a distinct and consequential security threat, transforming from a regional phenomenon into a global one with the September 11, 2001, attacks and the subsequent "war on terror," which produced the two longest wars in United States history in Afghanistan and Iraq and eventually gave rise to new threats such as ISIS, which combined territorial control with global propaganda and inspired violence.

The catastrophic failures of prevention in Rwanda and Bosnia prompted sustained international efforts to build institutions capable of preventing and responding to mass atrocity: the ad hoc war crimes tribunals for Yugoslavia and Rwanda, which established crucial legal precedents for prosecuting genocide; the permanent International Criminal Court, established in 2002; and the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, adopted in 2005, which asserted that sovereignty carries with it responsibility to protect populations from mass atrocity. Each of these institutions represented genuine innovation in international law and norms, yet each also revealed persistent limits: the ICC's dependence on state cooperation for enforcement, the Security Council veto's capacity to block collective action even amid clear evidence of atrocity (as with Syria after 2011), and the continuing gap between the ambitious mandates given to UN peacekeeping operations and the resources and political will actually provided to fulfill them.

The conflicts examined in this chapter produced displacement on a scale unmatched since World War II, with global forced displacement exceeding one hundred million people by the early 2020s. This displacement disproportionately burdens neighboring, often lower-income host countries rather than wealthy and more distant states, straining the international refugee protection framework established in 1951 for a different era of conflict.

Finally, the chapter examined why building durable peace after civil war has proven so difficult: the challenges of disarming and reintegrating combatants, balancing accountability with the political necessities of negotiated settlement, rebuilding shattered economies, and designing political institutions that manage rather than entrench the divisions that produced conflict in the first place. Research on civil war recurrence suggests that ending violence is often only the beginning of a much longer, harder, and more uncertain process of building peace that lasts.

Throughout, this chapter has emphasized several recurring themes: that ethnic and religious violence is produced through political choice and mobilization rather than historical inevitability; that international institutions built after 1945 have had to adapt, imperfectly and unevenly, to a security landscape their founders did not anticipate; that the tension between state sovereignty and the protection of human beings remains fundamentally unresolved; and that the human cost of these conflicts — measured in millions of deaths and tens of millions of displaced people — has fallen overwhelmingly on civilian populations rather than combatants.

This chapter also connects directly to patterns examined throughout this textbook: the long history of empire and decolonization that produced many of the fragile postcolonial states discussed here; the Cold War rivalries that shaped so many of the conflicts' origins; the human rights movements of the twentieth century that provided the moral and legal vocabulary for concepts like genocide and crimes against humanity; and the perennial world-historical tension between political communities' claims to self-governance and the broader human community's claims to protection from atrocity, a tension that runs back at least to the modern state system's origins in seventeenth-century Europe and that remains entirely unresolved in the twenty-first century.

Understanding this history equips students not only to interpret the news of ongoing conflicts but to evaluate, with appropriate historical grounding and critical judgment, the claims that governments, international institutions, and advocacy organizations make about when and how the world should respond to violence — questions that remain as urgent today as they were in 1991, and that will likely remain urgent for the foreseeable future.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Civil war
Armed conflict occurring primarily within the borders of a single state, typically between government forces and one or more organized rebel or insurgent groups.
State collapse
The breakdown of a central government's ability to maintain order, provide basic services, and exercise effective authority over its territory.
Failed state
A state that has lost the capacity to perform basic governmental functions, often lacking a functioning central government, as in Somalia after 1991.
Ethnic cleansing
The forcible removal of a particular ethnic or religious group from a territory, often through violence, intimidation, or expulsion, used extensively during the Bosnian war.
Genocide
The deliberate, systematic destruction, in whole or in part, of a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group, as legally defined by the 1948 Genocide Convention.
Terrorism
The use of violence against civilians or non-combatants to create fear and achieve political, religious, or ideological goals.
Asymmetric warfare
Conflict between opponents of vastly unequal conventional military strength, in which the weaker party relies on irregular tactics such as guerrilla warfare and terrorism.
Insurgency
An organized armed rebellion against a recognized government or occupying power, typically employing guerrilla tactics.
Counterinsurgency
Military and political strategies used by governments or occupying forces to defeat insurgencies, often combining combat operations with efforts to win civilian support.
Humanitarian intervention
Military action undertaken, with or without the consent of the target state, to prevent or stop mass atrocities against a civilian population.
Sovereignty
The principle that a state holds supreme authority over its own territory and population, free from external interference.
Responsibility to Protect (R2P)
UN doctrine, adopted in 2005, holding that states are responsible for protecting their populations from mass atrocities and that the international community may act when states fail to do so.
Peacekeeping
The deployment of international, typically UN-authorized, personnel to help maintain or restore peace, ranging from ceasefire monitoring to complex civilian protection missions.
Peace enforcement
The use of military force, with UN authorization, to compel compliance with a peace settlement or protect civilians, going beyond traditional peacekeeping's reliance on consent.
War crimes tribunal
A court established to prosecute individuals for violations of the laws of war, war crimes, and related offenses, such as the ICTY and ICTR.
International Criminal Court (ICC)
Permanent international court, established in 2002, with jurisdiction over genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and the crime of aggression.
Crimes against humanity
Serious violations committed as part of a widespread or systematic attack against a civilian population, including murder, enslavement, and persecution.
Refugee
A person who has fled their country due to well-founded fear of persecution and who is protected under international law from being returned to danger.
Internally displaced person (IDP)
A person forced to flee their home due to conflict or persecution but who remains within their own country's borders, and thus falls outside formal refugee protections.
Non-state actor
An organization or individual that exercises significant political or military power without being a formally recognized government, including terrorist and insurgent groups.
Proxy conflict
A conflict in which outside powers support opposing sides, often to advance their own strategic interests without direct military confrontation.
Diaspora financing
Financial support for armed groups or political movements provided by expatriate communities living outside the conflict zone.
Peacebuilding
The longer-term process of addressing underlying causes of conflict, rebuilding institutions, and fostering reconciliation after fighting ends.
Transitional justice
Mechanisms such as trials, truth commissions, and reparations used to address wartime atrocities and human rights violations during a society's transition from conflict or authoritarian rule.
Security sector reform
The process of restructuring a state's military, police, and security institutions after conflict to make them accountable, professional, and trustworthy.
Power-sharing agreement
A political arrangement guaranteeing formerly warring factions a share of government positions or authority as part of a peace settlement.
Non-refoulement
The legal principle prohibiting states from returning refugees to a country where they would face persecution.
"New wars"
A term used by scholars to describe post–Cold War intrastate conflicts characterized by irregular armed groups, resource financing, and high civilian targeting.
Lone wolf terrorism
Terrorist attacks carried out by individuals or small cells inspired by, but not directly organizationally connected to, a broader terrorist movement or ideology.
Gacaca courts
Community-based traditional justice courts used in Rwanda after the genocide to process the enormous number of genocide-related cases.
Safe area
A UN-declared zone intended to protect civilians during conflict, such as Srebrenica, which nonetheless proved unable to prevent mass killing in practice.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because the conflicts and institutions it examines are not confined to the past; they shape the world students inhabit today, from ongoing wars and refugee crises to continuing debates about when and how the international community should respond to mass violence. Understanding the post–Cold War history of conflict, terrorism, peacekeeping, and international law equips students to evaluate current events with historical depth rather than treating each new crisis as an isolated, unprecedented event.

This chapter shaped later world history in ways that remain unfolding. The institutions built in response to the failures of the 1990s — the ICC, the Responsibility to Protect, reformed peacekeeping doctrine — represent a genuine, if incomplete, attempt to close the gap between the international community's stated commitment to human rights and its actual capacity and willingness to protect them. The unresolved tension between sovereignty and protection, between great-power interest and universal principle, continues to shape international responses to conflicts from Ukraine to Sudan to Gaza in the 2020s.

This chapter reveals several larger world-history patterns that recur throughout this textbook: the long shadow that empire and colonial rule cast over the postcolonial states examined here, whose borders, ethnic categorizations, and institutional weaknesses were often shaped by colonial powers with little regard for the societies they governed; the recurring tension, visible since at least the seventeenth century, between the sovereign authority of states and broader claims of human rights and universal justice; and the way international institutions, however imperfect, represent a continuing human effort — traceable through the Concert of Europe, the League of Nations, and the United Nations — to build structures capable of preventing catastrophic violence, an effort that has never fully succeeded but has also never been abandoned.

Finally, this chapter helps students understand civilizations, empires, networks, exchange, conflict, environment, technology, and global change by showing how thoroughly interconnected the contemporary world has become — how a genocide in Central Africa can trigger a regional war involving nine countries, how a terrorist attack in New York can reshape security policy from Kabul to Baghdad to airports worldwide, and how a refugee fleeing Syria may end up, years later, in a camp in Jordan, a city in Germany, or nowhere at all, still waiting. The scale and interconnection of these events are themselves a historical development, the product of the globalized, institutionally linked world that the chapters of this textbook have traced from ancient trade routes to modern international organizations — and understanding that development is essential to understanding the world as it exists today.

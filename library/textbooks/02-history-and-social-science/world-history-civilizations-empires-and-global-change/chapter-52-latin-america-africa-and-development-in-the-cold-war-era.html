Part X — The Cold War and Decolonization, 1945–1991
52

Latin America, Africa, and Development in the Cold War Era

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The Cold War was not only a contest between the United States and the Soviet Union. It was also a contest over the futures of the developing world."

In 1961, a young Congolese leader named Patrice Lumumba was executed in a remote clearing in Katanga province. He had been the first democratically elected prime minister of the Republic of Congo, an enormous country that had won independence from Belgium only months earlier. His death was arranged with the involvement of Belgian operatives and with the knowledge of American intelligence agencies, who feared that Lumumba's independent foreign policy and nationalist rhetoric would open Africa's wealthiest mineral region to Soviet influence. To Cold War strategists in Washington and Brussels, Lumumba's removal was a tactical necessity. To millions of Africans and anticolonial activists across the globe, his murder was a symbol of everything that independence had promised but the postcolonial order had not delivered: sovereignty without power, freedom without control over one's own resources, liberation without liberation from imperial interference.

Half a world away, in the sugar fields and tobacco towns of Cuba, another revolution was unfolding. Fidel Castro's government, which came to power in 1959 after overthrowing the U.S.-backed dictatorship of Fulgencio Batista, attempted to reorganize Cuban society from top to bottom: nationalizing industries, redistributing land, expanding literacy and health care, and aligning itself with the Soviet Union. Cuba became a flashpoint of Cold War anxiety, the site of a missile crisis that brought the world to the edge of nuclear war in 1962, and a model—celebrated or feared depending on one's politics—for leftist movements across Latin America. The United States responded with trade embargo, covert operations, assassination plots, and the disastrous Bay of Pigs invasion. Cuba survived, but it remained isolated, its revolution frozen in a permanent state of siege.

These two stories—Lumumba's assassination and Cuba's revolution—illuminate the central problem of this chapter. Latin America and Africa in the Cold War era were regions where local histories, national struggles, economic needs, and political aspirations collided with the relentless logic of superpower rivalry and global capitalism. The men and women who sought independence, social justice, development, and self-determination found themselves navigating a world that offered little room for genuine autonomy. The Cold War was not only a contest between the United States and the Soviet Union. It was also a contest over the futures of the developing world—over who would control natural resources, who would set the terms of trade, and whose political models would shape the new nations that emerged from colonialism.

This chapter examines Latin America and Africa across the postwar decades from the late 1940s through the early 1990s. These were decades of radical possibility and brutal disappointment. They were decades in which elected governments fell to military coups, in which liberation movements fought colonial regimes for decades, in which idealistic development programs generated foreign debt rather than prosperity, in which ordinary people endured dictatorship, civil war, and economic crisis. They were also decades in which remarkable figures—Nelson Mandela, Salvador Allende, Amilcar Cabral, Oscar Romero, Wangari Maathai—built movements for justice and dignity that outlasted the Cold War itself. These decades shaped the world that exists today.

The question of development ran through everything. How could newly independent or long-independent societies transform colonial or dependent economies into modern, industrialized, self-sustaining ones? Could they do so within the structures of global capitalism, or did those structures ensure their continued subordination? The debate between modernization theory—which held that poor countries could develop by following the path of Western industrialization—and dependency theory—which argued that the global economic system was structured to keep poorer nations in permanent subordination—was not merely academic. It shaped policy, inspired revolution, justified repression, and organized the hopes and grievances of millions. This chapter is, in a fundamental sense, a chapter about that debate played out in real lives, real institutions, and real historical time.

The regions of Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa were far from uniform. Latin America encompassed vast and varied societies, from Mexico's complex postrevolutionary state to Argentina's industrialized urban economy to the small plantation republics of Central America. Sub-Saharan Africa ranged from the mineral-rich states of southern Africa to the Sahel's landlocked agrarian economies, from Tanzania's experiment with African socialism to Nigeria's oil-fueled instability. Generalizations about either region must always be tempered by this diversity. Yet common patterns emerge across the diversity: the weight of colonial economic structures, the fragility of new political institutions, the interventions of external powers, the struggles of ordinary people to build decent lives against enormous odds.

Historians study this period through a rich variety of evidence: government archives, diplomatic cables, the records of international organizations such as the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, the speeches and writings of political leaders, the testimonies of survivors of violence, oral histories collected from villages and townships, literature and film, newspapers, and the data of economic historians who have painstakingly reconstructed patterns of trade, debt, and inequality. Together, these sources reveal a world that cannot be understood through Cold War ideology alone, but must be studied on its own terms: as a set of societies with deep histories, genuine aspirations, and powerful constraints, struggling to find their way in a world they had not made.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the major theories of development and dependency that shaped economic policy in Latin America and Africa during the Cold War era.
  2. Trace the major political trajectories of Latin American countries after World War II, including revolutions, military governments, and democratization.
  3. Analyze the causes and consequences of U.S. intervention in Latin American politics during the Cold War.
  4. Compare the experiences of military rule and democratization in at least two Latin American countries.
  5. Describe the major challenges facing newly independent African states after decolonization, including economic dependency, state fragility, and ethnic conflict.
  6. Evaluate the apartheid system in South Africa and the global resistance movement that ultimately contributed to its end.
  7. Identify major civil wars and postcolonial conflicts in Africa and analyze their causes in terms of colonial legacies, Cold War intervention, and internal politics.
  8. Assess the impact of the debt crisis and structural adjustment programs on populations in Latin America and Africa.
  9. Interpret how ordinary people—workers, peasants, women, students, urban poor—experienced political change, economic crisis, and social upheaval.
  10. Connect the history of Latin America and Africa in the Cold War era to broader global patterns of inequality, imperialism, and resistance.
  11. Contextualize the development debate within the broader history of global capitalism and decolonization.
  12. Synthesize the experiences of Latin America and Africa to identify common patterns and significant differences in postcolonial and Cold War histories.

Guiding World-History Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What did "development" mean to different groups in Latin America and Africa, and why did development strategies so often fail to deliver promised results?
  2. How did the Cold War reshape political possibilities in Latin America and Africa, and who benefited from superpower rivalry?
  3. Why did military governments become so common in Latin America and Africa between the 1950s and the 1980s, and what forms did resistance take?
  4. How did the apartheid system in South Africa function, and why did it ultimately collapse?
  5. What were the connections between colonialism and postcolonial conflict in Africa, and how did colonial borders and institutions shape independent states?
  6. How did the global debt crisis of the 1980s affect ordinary people in Latin America and Africa, and who bore the burden of economic adjustment?
  7. What was the relationship between foreign intervention and internal political dynamics in countries like Cuba, Chile, the Congo, Angola, and Nicaragua?
  8. How did ordinary people—workers, women, students, peasants, and urban communities—participate in the political and economic struggles of this era?
  9. In what ways were the experiences of Latin America and Africa similar, and in what ways were they shaped by distinct regional histories?
  10. What legacies did the Cold War era leave for Latin America and Africa in terms of political institutions, economic structures, and social inequality?

Historical Context

The Cold War era in Latin America and Africa cannot be understood without deep roots in colonial history. By the time World War II ended in 1945, Latin America had been formally independent for well over a century, but independence had not produced genuine economic autonomy. The economies of most Latin American countries remained structured around the export of raw materials—coffee, sugar, copper, oil, rubber, bananas—to industrialized nations, while importing manufactured goods from abroad. This pattern, established during the colonial era and reinforced under British and later American economic dominance, meant that Latin American nations were integrated into global markets in ways that limited their ability to accumulate capital, diversify production, or set the terms of trade. The political systems that governed these export economies were typically dominated by small landowning elites, foreign corporations, and military institutions that maintained order in the interests of property.

Africa's situation in 1945 was different in a crucial respect: most of the continent was still under formal European colonial rule. Only Ethiopia, Liberia, South Africa, and Egypt were formally independent, and South Africa's independence was a fiction for its Black majority under white minority rule. The great wave of African decolonization would not begin in earnest until the late 1950s and would continue through the 1970s, with Zimbabwe (formerly Rhodesia) achieving majority rule only in 1980 and South Africa's apartheid system surviving until the early 1990s. For Africa, the postwar era was simultaneously a period of colonial twilight and nationalist dawn—a time when the contradictions of empire became impossible to sustain and when the promise of freedom captured the imagination of millions.

The Cold War provided the geopolitical frame within which these processes unfolded. The United States and the Soviet Union each sought to extend their influence into the developing world, supporting allies, funding political movements, supplying weapons, training militaries, and intervening when they calculated that their interests were threatened. For the United States, the primary concern in Latin America was preventing communist influence in its own hemisphere—a concern sharpened by the Cuban Revolution of 1959 and by the perceived threat of Soviet-aligned governments close to American shores. In Africa, both superpowers competed for influence in newly independent states, funding rival factions and backing different sides in civil wars that devastated populations from Angola to Ethiopia to the Horn of Africa.

The global economic order also shaped these regions powerfully. The Bretton Woods institutions established after World War II—the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank—were designed primarily by the United States and Britain to manage international finance and promote development. But the development they promoted was defined in terms that reflected the interests and assumptions of wealthy industrialized nations. Loans were tied to conditions. Trade rules favored industrial exporters. The terms of trade for primary commodities—the goods that Latin American and African countries produced—tended to deteriorate relative to manufactured goods over time, meaning that these countries had to export more and more to import the same quantity of industrial products. Development was promised, but the structure of the global economy made it elusive.

Within this global frame, the internal politics of Latin American and African countries were shaped by colonial legacies, social inequalities, ethnic and regional divisions, competing ideologies, and the ambitions and fears of specific leaders, parties, and movements. No country's history in this era can be reduced to Cold War competition alone. The Cuban Revolution grew from the specific conditions of Cuban society—the concentration of land, the dominance of American capital, the frustrations of the urban middle class and the rural poor, the political traditions of Cuban nationalism. The Rwandan genocide of 1994 grew from the specific history of Hutu-Tutsi relations, Belgian colonial rule, and postcolonial political competition, not simply from superpower manipulation. History at the local and national level always intersects with global forces, but it is never simply determined by them.

By the 1980s, a new economic crisis—the debt crisis—would reshape the choices available to Latin American and African governments with devastating consequences for ordinary people. The debt crisis and the structural adjustment programs that international financial institutions demanded in return for assistance created a decade of austerity, rising unemployment, shrinking public services, and increasing inequality across both regions. It was, for many, a second trauma—following the first trauma of colonial rule or the failed promises of revolutionary change.

Regional Orientation

Latin America occupies the vast territory south of the United States-Mexico border, stretching from the Rio Grande to the southern tip of South America. It encompasses 20 countries of enormous geographic diversity: tropical rainforests, high Andean plateaus, temperate grasslands, arid coasts, and Caribbean islands. The region's population in the postwar decades was deeply unequal, with sharp divides between a small landed and commercial elite, a growing urban middle class, a large urban working class concentrated in rapidly expanding cities, and vast rural populations of indigenous, mestizo, and Afro-descended peoples who worked land they rarely owned. Economically, most Latin American countries depended heavily on a narrow range of export commodities: oil in Venezuela and Mexico, copper in Chile, coffee in Colombia and Brazil, sugar in Cuba and the Caribbean, bananas in Central America. This monoculture dependency made national economies fragile and vulnerable to global price swings.

Sub-Saharan Africa is an even more diverse region encompassing dozens of countries with different colonial histories, ethnic compositions, resource endowments, geographic environments, and political traditions. West Africa, with its dense populations and long trading traditions, differed profoundly from the vast, sparsely populated savannahs of East Africa, the mineral-rich central plateau of the Congo basin, or the racially structured settler colonies of southern Africa. Africa's colonial boundaries—drawn at the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885 by European powers with little regard for the distribution of ethnic groups, language communities, or historical polities—created states whose internal coherence was often fragile. The new states of Africa inherited colonial administrative structures designed for extraction, not welfare, and economies structured to export raw materials rather than support industrialization.

Both regions occupied what analysts called the "Third World" or "Global South"—a position defined not simply by geography but by structural relationship to the wealthy industrial economies of Europe, North America, and Japan. This structural position meant that both regions faced similar pressures: deteriorating terms of trade, limited access to capital and technology, political interference from external powers, and the challenge of building modern states and economies on foundations laid by colonial exploitation.

Major People, Groups, Places, and Polities

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Fidel Castro (1926–2016)
Cuban revolutionary leader who governed Cuba from 1959 until 2008; symbol of leftist resistance to U.S. imperialism and of authoritarian revolutionary governance.
Che Guevara (1928–1967)
Argentine-born revolutionary who fought alongside Castro in Cuba; later attempted to export revolution to the Congo and Bolivia, where he was captured and killed; became an icon of global leftism.
Salvador Allende (1908–1973)
Chilean socialist politician; first Marxist elected president in Latin America through democratic elections (1970); overthrown and killed in the 1973 military coup.
Augusto Pinochet (1915–2006)
Chilean military general who led the 1973 coup against Allende; ruled Chile as dictator until 1990; his regime tortured and killed thousands of political opponents.
Patrice Lumumba (1925–1961)
First prime minister of the Republic of Congo; nationalist leader whose assassination symbolizes Cold War interference in African sovereignty.
Mobutu Sese Seko (1930–1997)
Congolese military leader who seized power after Lumumba's death; ruled Zaire (renamed from Congo) for over three decades with U.S. support; embodiment of kleptocratic postcolonial governance.
Kwame Nkrumah (1909–1972)
First prime minister and president of Ghana; pan-Africanist thinker and advocate of African unity; overthrown by military coup in 1966.
Julius Nyerere (1922–1999)
President of Tanzania; proponent of "Ujamaa" (African socialism); attempted to build development without Cold War alignment.
Nelson Mandela (1918–2013)
South African anti-apartheid leader; imprisoned for 27 years on Robben Island; negotiated the end of apartheid and became South Africa's first democratically elected president in 1994.
Steve Biko (1946–1977)
South African Black Consciousness leader; killed in police detention; his death galvanized international opposition to apartheid.
Amilcar Cabral (1924–1973)
Guinea-Bissau liberation leader; intellectual and revolutionary who led the struggle against Portuguese colonialism; assassinated before independence.
Samora Machel (1933–1986)
First president of Mozambique after independence from Portugal; socialist leader who navigated Cold War pressures and South African destabilization.
Jonas Savimbi (1934–2002)
Angolan rebel leader of UNITA; backed by the United States and South Africa during the Angolan civil war; his war with the MPLA government lasted nearly three decades.
Oscar Romero (1917–1980)
Archbishop of El Salvador; advocate for the poor; assassinated while celebrating Mass by right-wing forces; symbol of liberation theology.
The Contras
U.S.-backed counterrevolutionary forces in Nicaragua; funded through the covert Iran-Contra affair during the Reagan administration.
ANC (African National Congress)
South African liberation movement founded in 1912; led the struggle against apartheid; governed South Africa after 1994.
MPLA and UNITA
Angola's two main factions in its post-independence civil war; backed by Soviet/Cuban and U.S./South African forces respectively.

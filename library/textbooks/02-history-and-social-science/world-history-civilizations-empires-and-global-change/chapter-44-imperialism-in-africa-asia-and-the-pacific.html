Imperialism in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"In 1884, representatives of fourteen European nations gathered in Berlin to divide a continent none of them owned. No African leader was invited. Within a generation, the map of an entire world had been redrawn."

In 1884 and 1885, representatives of fourteen European nations gathered in Berlin at the invitation of German chancellor Otto von Bismarck. No African leader was invited. No Asian representative attended. The conference was convened to establish rules for the partition of Africa among European colonial powers, to prevent the rivalries of expansion from producing open war among the great powers of Europe, and to legitimize claims that were already being staked across the continent. Within a generation, the map of Africa had been almost entirely redrawn. Territories that had been home to independent kingdoms, diverse peoples, complex trade networks, and long-standing political institutions were divided into colonial possessions defined by boundaries drawn in European offices, often with a ruler following lines of latitude and longitude that bore no relationship to rivers, mountains, ethnic communities, or political realities on the ground.

The Berlin Conference was neither the beginning nor the end of the process historians call the New Imperialism. It was a moment of formalization in a longer story. By the late nineteenth century, European industrial nations were engaged in a worldwide expansion of colonial control that dwarfed earlier forms of overseas empire. Britain already governed India through the East India Company and then directly through the Crown. France had established colonies in Indochina and West Africa. The Dutch controlled the Indonesian archipelago. The Portuguese held territories along the African coast. But in the decades after 1870, the pace of colonial acquisition accelerated dramatically. By 1914, European powers controlled roughly 84 percent of the world's land surface, either as colonies, protectorates, or spheres of influence. The United States had also joined the imperial system, acquiring Puerto Rico, the Philippines, and Guam after the Spanish-American War of 1898. Japan had begun its own imperial expansion into Korea, Taiwan, and mainland China.

This chapter studies nineteenth-century imperialism as a global system—not merely a collection of separate colonial episodes, but an interconnected structure of conquest, extraction, administration, and resistance that remade the political map of the world and transformed the lives of hundreds of millions of people. The New Imperialism was made possible by specific conditions: the industrialization of European economies, which produced both the need for raw materials and markets and the technological capacity to project military power across vast distances; the development of the steamship, the telegraph, the breech-loading rifle, and the machine gun; and the growth of racial ideologies that framed conquest as a civilizing mission rather than as self-interested extraction. These ideological frameworks were not incidental ornaments to empire; they were central to how colonial states justified themselves, organized their administrations, and understood their own project.

The societies that were colonized were not blank slates awaiting European organization. Africa in 1880 was home to powerful states—the Zulu kingdom in southern Africa, the Ashanti Confederation in West Africa, the Sokoto Caliphate in the Sahel, the kingdom of Ethiopia, the sultanates of the Swahili coast, the Mahdist state in Sudan. Asia contained some of the most populous and historically significant civilizations in the world—India, with its complex political mosaic and long traditions of urban commerce and intellectual life; Southeast Asia, with its maritime kingdoms and plantation economies; the Pacific, with its extraordinary diversity of island peoples and long traditions of ocean navigation. The New Imperialism did not arrive in empty spaces. It arrived in the midst of ongoing histories, political conflicts, religious change, and economic development, and it disrupted, reorganized, and in many cases destroyed what it encountered.

Resistance to imperial conquest was immediate, sustained, and extraordinarily varied. It took the form of armed military resistance—the Zulu War, the Mahdist uprising, the Boxer Rebellion, the Philippine-American War. It took the form of legal and political protest—petitions, delegations, journalism, and nascent nationalist organizations. It took the form of religious revival and cultural affirmation—movements that drew on indigenous spiritual traditions to contest colonial authority over bodies, land, and minds. And it took the form of adaptation and negotiation, as communities, leaders, and ordinary people found ways to survive, preserve, and transmit their cultures under conditions of colonial domination. The history of imperialism is not only the history of European expansion; it is equally the history of the peoples who resisted, accommodated, and ultimately outlasted it.

This chapter examines the motives and ideologies of the New Imperialism, the mechanics of colonial conquest and governance across Africa, Asia, and the Pacific, the role of missionaries and education in transforming colonial societies, and the many forms of anti-colonial resistance that challenged imperial power in the decades before the First World War and beyond. It situates imperialism within the larger context of industrial capitalism, racial ideology, and global inequality that shaped the modern world—a world whose structures of power and wealth are still being reckoned with today.

  2. How did industrial technology—railways, telegraphs, steamships, machine guns—shape the capacity of European powers to conquer and govern distant territories?

Historical Context

The New Imperialism of the late nineteenth century did not emerge from nowhere. Earlier forms of European overseas expansion had already established colonial footholds across much of the world. The Portuguese had built trading posts along the African coast in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries and had established significant colonial presences in Brazil and parts of Asia. The Spanish had conquered vast empires in the Americas, extracting silver and reorganizing indigenous labor. The Dutch had displaced Portuguese power in the Indian Ocean and had established control over the Indonesian archipelago. The British and French had competed for empire in North America and India throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. And the Atlantic slave trade, which lasted roughly from the mid-fifteenth to the late nineteenth century, had already created profound and violent connections between Africa, Europe, and the Americas.

What distinguished the New Imperialism from these earlier forms of colonial expansion was its speed, its geographic scope, its technological foundation, and its systematic character. Earlier European empires had often operated through trading posts, chartered companies, and indirect influence over local rulers. The New Imperialism involved the direct conquest, annexation, and administration of entire territories and their populations. Where earlier colonial powers had frequently governed through indigenous intermediaries, the late nineteenth century saw the construction of colonial bureaucracies designed to penetrate deeply into local economies and societies, extracting resources, reorganizing labor, imposing taxation, and remaking social institutions.

The timing of the New Imperialism is closely connected to the industrial revolution and its consequences. By the 1870s, the leading industrial economies of Europe—Britain, France, Germany, and Belgium—were producing more manufactured goods than their domestic markets could absorb. Industrialists and their political allies sought new markets for their exports and new sources of raw materials for their factories: rubber, cotton, palm oil, tin, copper, diamonds, gold. Colonial territories could supply these materials and simultaneously serve as captive markets for European manufactured goods, though the reality of colonial economies was often more complicated than this simple model suggests.

Industrial technology also transformed European military power. The invention of the breech-loading rifle in the 1860s and the development of the Maxim machine gun in 1884 gave European armies an enormous advantage over even well-organized and courageous opponents. Where earlier European forces had struggled to defeat organized African or Asian armies, industrialized military technology made the conquest of most of the world's remaining independent territories a matter of relatively brief and relatively cheap military campaigns, at least from the European perspective. Steamships allowed European navies to project power into previously inaccessible river systems, while the telegraph allowed metropolitan governments to communicate with distant colonial officials within days rather than months. The medical discovery that quinine could prevent malaria removed one of the most significant barriers to European penetration of tropical Africa.

The ideological context of the New Imperialism was equally important. The decades after 1870 saw the proliferation of explicitly racial theories of human difference, which drew selectively and often dishonestly on Darwinian evolutionary theory to argue that human races existed in a hierarchy, with Europeans at the top. The concept of the "civilizing mission"—the idea that European powers had a duty to bring civilization, Christianity, and progress to peoples they characterized as primitive or backward—was embraced by politicians, missionaries, journalists, and imperial administrators across Europe. Nationalism also played a role: in an era of intense competition among European nation-states, colonial empire had become a marker of national power and prestige. Each of these forces—economic pressure, military technology, racial ideology, nationalist competition—reinforced the others, driving the pace of expansion to a speed and scope that reshaped the world within a single generation.

Regional Orientation

Africa is the continent most dramatically reshaped by the New Imperialism. A vast continent of some 30 million square kilometers, Africa in 1880 contained hundreds of distinct political entities—kingdoms, confederacies, sultanates, city-states, and decentralized communities organized around kinship, clan, and regional trade networks. The Sahara Desert divided the continent geographically but did not isolate sub-Saharan Africa from the wider world; trans-Saharan trade routes connected West and Central African kingdoms to North Africa and the Mediterranean, while the Indian Ocean coast was integrated into maritime trading networks linking East Africa to Arabia, India, and Southeast Asia. Only about 10 percent of Africa's land surface was under European control in 1880. By 1914, that proportion had risen to roughly 90 percent. Only Ethiopia and Liberia retained formal independence.

South Asia, and especially India, had been the cornerstone of British imperial power since the eighteenth century. By the 1850s, much of the Indian subcontinent was governed by the British East India Company, which extracted tribute, reorganized land tenure, and created a large colonial army staffed primarily by Indian soldiers. The Indian Rebellion of 1857–1858 ended Company rule and established direct Crown governance, transforming India into the centerpiece of what British commentators called the "jewel in the crown" of the empire. India supplied raw cotton for Lancashire mills, imported British manufactured textiles, and provided a massive army that could be deployed across the imperial system, from Egypt to East Africa to China.

Southeast Asia was divided among several colonial powers by the end of the nineteenth century. Britain controlled Burma, Malaya, Singapore, and parts of Borneo. France had established Indochina, comprising present-day Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. The Netherlands maintained the Dutch East Indies (present-day Indonesia), which had been under Dutch influence since the seventeenth century. The United States acquired the Philippines from Spain in 1898. Siam (Thailand) remained formally independent, though under significant pressure from both British and French expansion on its borders.

The Pacific was colonized by multiple powers across the nineteenth century. Britain, France, Germany, and the United States all established colonial or protectorate relationships with Pacific island societies, drawn by strategic considerations, missionary activity, and commercial interests in plantation agriculture, copra, and pearling. The indigenous peoples of Polynesia, Melanesia, and Micronesia faced devastating demographic collapse following contact with European diseases, the disruption of traditional economies and social institutions, and the introduction of migrant labor systems that often amounted to new forms of servitude.

Section 44.1

New Imperialism in Global Context

The New Imperialism of the late nineteenth century represented a qualitative shift in the history of European overseas expansion. Earlier colonial systems had been characterized by trading relationships, coastal enclaves, plantation islands, and settler colonies that occupied limited geographic areas. The New Imperialism, by contrast, aimed at the comprehensive territorial conquest and administrative reorganization of vast continental interiors. Within three decades—roughly from the 1870s to the outbreak of the First World War in 1914—European powers and the United States had divided among themselves virtually all of the territories of Africa, Southeast Asia, the Pacific, and significant portions of Asia and the Middle East that had not previously been colonized. It was, in purely quantitative terms, one of the most dramatic transfers of political power in recorded history.

To understand the New Imperialism, historians have proposed several overlapping explanations, each capturing part of the truth. Economic explanations emphasize the needs of industrial capitalism: the search for raw materials, markets, and investment opportunities outside the saturated markets of Europe. Political and strategic explanations focus on the competitive dynamics among European nation-states, which drove colonial expansion as a form of national competition. Ideological explanations highlight the role of racial theory, evolutionary thought, and the "civilizing mission" in providing justification and motivation for conquest. Technological explanations point to the breech-loading rifle, the machine gun, the steamship, and quinine as the tools that made continental conquest possible. None of these explanations is fully adequate on its own; together, they describe a phenomenon that was simultaneously economic, political, ideological, and technological.

One important dimension of the New Imperialism that is sometimes overlooked is its relationship to events within the colonized regions themselves. The partition of Africa and Asia did not take place in a political vacuum. European expansion frequently exploited existing conflicts between African or Asian states, between rival factions within states, or between established political orders and reforming or revolutionary movements. The French conquest of Vietnam was facilitated in part by internal conflicts within the Vietnamese court. British expansion in India exploited divisions among the successor states of the Mughal Empire. In West Africa, some African rulers initially sought European alliances as a way of gaining advantage over regional rivals, only to find that they had introduced a power that ultimately dominated all of them.

The New Imperialism was also deeply connected to global economic integration. The mid-nineteenth century saw the rapid expansion of international trade, driven by the reduction of transportation costs through steamship and railway technology and by the adoption of the gold standard by major trading nations. Colonial territories were drawn into this global economy, but on highly unequal terms. They were restructured to produce raw materials and agricultural commodities—rubber, cotton, palm oil, tea, coffee, sugar, copper—for export to industrial economies. Indigenous subsistence agriculture was disrupted, land was reallocated to plantation or concession companies, and labor was mobilized through a variety of coercive mechanisms—taxation, forced labor, contract labor—that bound colonial populations to export production regardless of market conditions or local need.

By 1914, the global economy was organized around a core-periphery structure in which industrial nations in Europe and North America consumed the raw materials extracted from colonial territories in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. This structure generated enormous wealth for colonial powers and their investors, while leaving colonized populations impoverished, politically subordinated, and economically vulnerable to the fluctuations of global commodity markets over which they had no control. The legacies of this structure—in unequal trade relationships, infrastructure designed for extraction rather than internal development, and political boundaries that divided ethnic communities while grouping others artificially—would persist long after formal decolonization in the mid-twentieth century.

Historical Vocabulary
Imperialism

Imperialism refers to the extension of a nation's power over other territories through conquest, settlement, economic control, or cultural domination. In its nineteenth-century form, imperialism typically involved the formal political control of a foreign territory and its population by a metropolitan power. Historians distinguish between imperialism—the system of control—and colonialism—the settlement and governance of foreign territories by settlers from the imperial power. The term "New Imperialism" refers specifically to the rapid and comprehensive colonial expansion of European powers in the late nineteenth century, which was distinguished from earlier forms of overseas expansion by its territorial scope, technological foundations, and systematic character. Understanding imperialism requires attention to both the structural forces that drove it—industrial capitalism, national competition, racial ideology—and the diverse human experiences it produced, among both colonizers and colonized.

Section 44.2

Industrial Power and Imperial Motives

The relationship between industrialization and imperial expansion was complex, but it was central to understanding why the New Imperialism took the form it did. By the 1870s, Britain was the world's leading industrial economy, producing more steel, cotton textiles, coal, and machinery than any other nation. But British economic dominance was beginning to be challenged. The newly unified German state was industrializing rapidly. The United States was emerging as an industrial giant. France, though less industrialized than Britain or Germany, was a significant manufacturing power with substantial overseas ambitions. Each of these nations faced economic pressures that made colonial expansion attractive.

The economic case for imperialism rested on several arguments. Industrial economies generated surpluses of manufactured goods that domestic markets could not absorb, creating pressure to find new export markets. Industrial production depended on raw materials—metals, fibers, oils, dyes, rubber—that were not available in Europe in sufficient quantities and had to be imported. Meanwhile, the accumulation of capital in industrial economies generated a surplus that sought profitable investment outlets, which colonial territories could provide through mining concessions, railway construction, and plantation agriculture. British economist John Hobson articulated an influential critique along these lines in his 1902 book Imperialism: A Study, arguing that the maldistribution of income in industrial societies—which kept wages low while concentrating wealth in the hands of capitalists—created the surplus capital and deficient domestic demand that drove imperial expansion.

Military technology was equally decisive. The development of the breech-loading rifle dramatically increased the rate of fire of European infantry. More decisive still was the development of the Maxim gun by Hiram Maxim in 1884—the world's first fully automatic machine gun, capable of firing 600 rounds per minute. At the Battle of Omdurman in 1898, a British force commanded by General Herbert Kitchener defeated a Mahdist army of approximately 50,000 Sudanese fighters, killing roughly 11,000 of them while losing only 47 British soldiers. The disparity illustrated with terrifying clarity what industrial military technology meant for the balance of power between European armies and their opponents. The British writer Hilaire Belloc captured this bitterly: whatever happened, European forces had the Maxim gun, and their opponents did not.

Steamships and railways were equally transformative. Before the age of steam, European navies could not easily penetrate the river systems of tropical Africa or Asia. Shallow-draft steam-powered gunboats could ascend rivers that would have stopped sailing ships, allowing colonial forces to project power deep into continental interiors. Railways built by colonial powers—using local labor, often under coercive conditions—made it possible to move troops rapidly across vast territories and to transport extracted resources to coastal ports for export. The telegraph allowed colonial administrators in distant territories to communicate with metropolitan governments within days rather than months, enabling far more centralized direction of imperial policy than had ever been possible before.

Medical technology also played a role that is often underestimated. Malaria had been a major barrier to European penetration of tropical Africa, killing European soldiers and administrators with terrifying efficiency. The discovery in the 1820s that quinine—derived from the bark of the South American cinchona tree—could prevent malaria, and the subsequent development of efficient methods for producing and distributing it, removed a major obstacle to European settlement in the tropics. Without quinine prophylaxis, the conquest of sub-Saharan Africa would have been far more costly in European lives and might have proceeded more slowly or taken quite different forms.

Nationalist competition among European states added further urgency. The unification of Germany in 1871 and the rapid growth of German industrial power created pressure on the new German state to establish its own overseas empire as a sign of great-power status. France, still stung by its defeat in the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–1871, pursued colonial expansion in part as a way of recovering national prestige. Italy, unified only in the 1860s, pursued imperial adventures in North and East Africa. Even smaller European powers—Belgium, Portugal, the Netherlands—maintained or expanded their colonial possessions. The result was a competitive dynamic in which each colonial acquisition seemed to threaten the relative position of rival powers, driving the pace of expansion further and making restraint politically costly.

Section 44.3

Racial Ideology and "Civilizing" Claims

No aspect of the New Imperialism is more difficult for modern students to confront than its racial ideology. The late nineteenth century saw the widespread articulation of theories of racial hierarchy that classified humanity into biologically distinct races arranged in a fixed order of intelligence, civilization, and moral development, with Europeans—and specifically northern or western Europeans—at the top. These theories were presented as scientific, drawing on anatomy, craniometry (the measurement of skull dimensions), evolutionary biology, and comparative ethnography. They were accepted by most educated Europeans as established fact, taught in schools, elaborated in encyclopedias, and incorporated into the self-understanding of colonial administrators, missionaries, and metropolitan publics.

The intellectual sources of racial ideology were multiple. The publication of Charles Darwin's On the Origin of Species in 1859 provided a framework—misappropriated and distorted—for thinking about human difference in evolutionary terms. Social Darwinism, as developed by Herbert Spencer and others, applied the concept of natural selection to human societies, arguing that competition between races and nations was natural and that the dominance of European civilization reflected genuine biological superiority. These ideas were elaborated by writers like Arthur de Gobineau, whose Essay on the Inequality of Human Races argued that racial mixture caused civilizational decline and that the survival of "pure" European races was essential to human progress.

The "civilizing mission" drew on these racial theories while adding a philanthropic dimension. If non-European peoples were genuinely at a lower stage of development—childlike, unable to govern themselves, trapped in superstition and barbarism—then European conquest could be framed not as exploitation but as benevolent intervention. Colonial rule would bring Christianity, literacy, medical care, modern agriculture, and the rule of law. The colonized would eventually—though always at some indefinitely deferred future moment—be educated and civilized to the point where they might participate in the benefits of modern life. In the meantime, they required European guidance and control. This ideology was articulated by politicians like Joseph Chamberlain, the British Colonial Secretary, who argued that the British Empire was a great civilizing agency, and by missionaries like David Livingstone, whose African explorations were motivated by the combined goal of spreading Christianity and ending the slave trade.

The contradictions of the civilizing mission were profound and were recognized even at the time by some critics. If colonial peoples were to be educated and civilized, then colonial rule should be temporary and its goal should be the preparation of colonized peoples for self-governance. But colonial administrations consistently acted to delay and prevent the political development of colonized peoples, restricting access to higher education, excluding indigenous people from senior administrative positions, and reinforcing systems of racial hierarchy that made genuine equality impossible. The civilizing mission was ultimately less a program for the uplift of colonized peoples than a justification for indefinite colonial control and economic extraction.

Racial ideology also shaped the everyday texture of colonial life. Colonial societies developed elaborate systems of racial classification and segregation. In British India, in French West Africa, in the Belgian Congo, and across the colonial world, race determined access to employment, education, legal rights, housing, transportation, and social recognition. Europeans occupied the top of colonial social hierarchies; indigenous peoples were at the bottom; intermediate groups—mixed-race communities, immigrant communities from other colonized territories—occupied ambiguous middle positions that were sources of ongoing social tension.

The violence of the colonial encounter was inseparable from racial ideology. The dehumanization of colonized peoples in racial theory made colonial violence easier to justify, both to metropolitan publics and to the soldiers who carried it out. The extermination of the Herero and Nama peoples in German Southwest Africa between 1904 and 1908—which historians recognize as the first genocide of the twentieth century—was carried out in explicit racial terms. General Lothar von Trotha issued an extermination order (Vernichtungsbefehl) in 1904 declaring the Herero people to be outside the protection of German law and ordering their systematic destruction. Approximately 65,000 of the 80,000 Herero people were killed, along with approximately 10,000 of the 20,000 Nama. The survivors were placed in concentration camps, where further thousands died of disease, starvation, and forced labor. This genocide was rationalized in the language of racial conflict and natural selection, anticipating the ideological frameworks that would later animate European fascism.

Historical Vocabulary
Social Darwinism

Social Darwinism refers to the misapplication of Charles Darwin's theory of evolution to human societies and social relationships. While Darwin argued that natural selection shaped the biological characteristics of species over geological time, Social Darwinists argued that competition between human groups—races, nations, classes—was natural and that the dominance of some groups over others reflected genuine biological superiority. The concept was used to justify imperialism (the dominance of European races over others was presented as "natural"), laissez-faire capitalism, and opposition to social welfare measures. Social Darwinism was not a legitimate scientific theory but a political ideology that used the prestige of evolutionary science to naturalize existing systems of inequality. Its influence on colonial ideology was profound and deeply harmful, providing a pseudoscientific framework for racial hierarchy that contributed to some of the worst violence of the colonial period, including genocide.

Section 44.4

The Scramble for Africa

The Scramble for Africa—the rapid partition of the African continent among European powers in the 1880s and 1890s—was one of the most dramatic territorial transformations in modern history. In 1880, only about 10 percent of Africa was under direct European control: the French had Algeria; the British controlled the Cape Colony and parts of West Africa; the Portuguese maintained coastal enclaves in Angola and Mozambique. Within two decades, virtually the entire continent had been claimed, and within three decades, the claims had been translated into military conquest and administrative control.

The trigger for the Scramble was partly the opening of the Congo basin by Henry Morton Stanley, whose explorations in the 1870s revealed the commercial potential of a vast river system penetrating the heart of the continent. King Leopold II of Belgium, who coveted an overseas empire but could not persuade the Belgian government to pursue one, employed Stanley to establish a chain of trading stations in the Congo basin and to sign treaties with local chiefs that claimed to grant Leopold personal sovereignty over their territories. Leopold's maneuvering alarmed other European powers, particularly France and Britain, and it was concern about conflicting claims in the Congo that prompted Bismarck to call the Berlin Conference.

The Berlin Conference of 1884–1885 established rules for colonial competition. The assembled powers agreed on the principle of "effective occupation"—that a claim to African territory was valid only if the claimant could demonstrate actual administrative presence. They also agreed on free navigation of the Congo and Niger rivers, the suppression of the slave trade, and the protection of indigenous peoples and missionaries—principles that were largely honored in the breach. The conference did not actually partition Africa; it established rules for doing so, and the partition proceeded rapidly through bilateral agreements, military campaigns, and treaty negotiations in the following decade.

The mechanics of conquest varied considerably. In some regions, European forces defeated African armies in pitched battles. In others, they exploited civil conflicts, allying with some African leaders against others. In still others, conquest was achieved through a combination of diplomatic pressure, economic leverage, and the threat of force without actual warfare. African rulers sometimes signed treaties—often with only partial understanding of their implications, or under direct coercion—that were subsequently used to justify colonial annexation. The process was rarely clean, rarely honest, and rarely as bloodless as European accounts suggested.

The British advance in Africa proceeded along multiple fronts. In the south, Cecil Rhodes's British South Africa Company drove northward from the Cape Colony, establishing the territories of Rhodesia in the early 1890s. The Anglo-Boer War of 1899–1902, in which Britain conquered the Afrikaner republics of the Transvaal and the Orange Free State, completed British control of southern Africa. In East Africa, Britain established protectorates over Uganda and Kenya. In West Africa, Britain expanded from the Gold Coast and Nigeria, where the Royal Niger Company held a concession before the territory was taken under Crown control in 1900. France pursued expansion primarily in West Africa, gradually extending control from Senegal along the Niger River to establish the federation of French West Africa, eventually encompassing eight territories covering roughly 3.7 million square kilometers. Germany acquired territories in East Africa, West Africa, and the Pacific. Belgium's colony in the Congo—the Congo Free State, which was King Leopold's personal possession until international outrage at his brutal regime forced the Belgian government to take it over in 1908—was among the most notorious examples of colonial exploitation in the world.

The human consequences of the Scramble for Africa were catastrophic. Indigenous political systems were dismantled or reorganized to serve colonial needs. Land that had been common property, used for subsistence agriculture or pastoralism, was declared crown land and alienated to European settlers or concession companies. Populations were displaced to create space for European farms, mines, and plantations. Labor was extracted through a variety of coercive mechanisms, including the hut tax and poll tax—monetary taxes that could only be paid by working for wages, which forced men into colonial labor markets and separated families across vast distances.

Section 44.5

Colonial Rule and Resistance in Africa

Once territories had been conquered, colonial powers faced the challenge of actually governing vast territories with relatively small numbers of European officials. The solution adopted varied by power and region, but two broad models can be distinguished: direct rule and indirect rule.

Direct rule, favored especially by the French, involved the replacement of indigenous political structures with European administrative institutions staffed primarily by European officials. Local rulers who could not be incorporated into the colonial system were deposed; traditional legal systems were replaced by European law; indigenous cultural practices were actively discouraged or suppressed in favor of assimilation to French language and culture. The French concept of assimilation—the idea that Africans could, through education and cultural adoption, eventually become French citizens—was theoretically egalitarian, but in practice, the bar for assimilation was set impossibly high, and the vast majority of Africans under French rule were governed as colonial subjects with few legal rights.

Indirect rule, as developed most systematically by the British, involved governing through indigenous authorities who were retained in their positions but subordinated to colonial supervision. The architects of British indirect rule in Africa—most notably Frederick Lugard, who developed the system in Nigeria—argued that governing through "native institutions" was more efficient, cheaper, and more politically stable than imposing alien European administrative structures. In practice, indirect rule often involved the invention or reinvention of traditional authorities: where convenient hereditary rulers did not exist or were uncooperative, colonial administrations sometimes created new chiefs or empowered previously subordinate figures. The resulting system was a hybrid—neither fully traditional nor fully modern—that served colonial administrative needs while undermining genuine indigenous political development.

Both direct and indirect rule relied ultimately on coercion. Colonial police forces and military units—typically composed of African soldiers (askaris) commanded by European officers—maintained order, suppressed dissent, and enforced the labor and tax regimes that made colonial economies function. The violence of colonial rule was not episodic but structural; it was embedded in the daily operations of colonial administration rather than confined to moments of open rebellion.

African resistance to colonial rule was immediate, sustained, and extraordinarily varied. Armed resistance took many forms. The Zulu War of 1879 saw the Zulu kingdom inflict a major defeat on British forces at the Battle of Isandlwana before being overcome by superior firepower. The Mahdist state in Sudan, established after Muhammad Ahmad declared himself the Mahdi in 1881, successfully expelled British and Egyptian forces from Sudan and governed the territory for more than a decade before the British reconquest under Kitchener in 1898. Samori Touré, the ruler of a substantial empire in present-day Guinea and Mali, waged a remarkable guerrilla campaign against French forces for nearly two decades, retreating eastward as French power advanced and reconstituting his fighting forces, before finally being captured in 1898.

The Ashanti of present-day Ghana fought four wars against British expansion over the course of the nineteenth century. The final war—the War of the Golden Stool of 1900, led by the queen mother Yaa Asantewaa—was sparked by a demand by the British governor that the Ashanti surrender the Golden Stool, a sacred object that was the symbolic embodiment of Ashanti sovereignty. The Ashanti fought fiercely but were ultimately defeated, and the Ashanti kingdom was annexed. Ethiopia alone among sub-Saharan African states successfully repelled European conquest: at the Battle of Adwa in March 1896, Emperor Menelik II led an Ethiopian army to a decisive victory over Italian forces, killing or capturing more than half the Italian army and forcing Italy to recognize Ethiopian independence in the subsequent treaty. Adwa became a potent symbol throughout the colonized world of the possibility of successful resistance.

Resistance also took non-military forms. The Maji Maji Rebellion in German East Africa (1905–1907) was inspired by a prophet named Kinjikitile Ngwale, who offered followers water (maji) that he claimed would render them immune to German bullets. Though the rebellion was crushed with great violence—killing between 200,000 and 300,000 people, mostly through German scorched-earth tactics that created famine—it demonstrated both the depth of anti-colonial feeling and the role of religious ideology in mobilizing resistance across ethnic and regional lines.

Historical Vocabulary
Indirect Rule

Indirect rule was a system of colonial administration, developed most systematically by the British, in which indigenous political authorities were retained in their positions but subordinated to colonial supervision and direction. Rather than replacing local rulers with European officials, indirect rule used local rulers as intermediaries between the colonial state and the local population. The system was justified on grounds of efficiency, cultural sensitivity, and political stability, but it had several important effects: it often involved the invention or reinvention of "traditional" authorities, distorting indigenous political systems; it denied colonized populations meaningful political participation or development; and it allowed colonial states to govern vast territories with minimal European personnel. Indirect rule was contrasted with the French system of direct rule, which more aggressively replaced indigenous institutions with European administrative structures, though in practice both systems served similar colonial ends of extraction and control.

Section 44.6

British India and Colonial Administration

India occupied a unique position in the British imperial system. It was the largest colonial territory, the most populous, the most economically significant, and the most administratively complex. By the mid-nineteenth century, the British East India Company had established control over most of the Indian subcontinent through a combination of conquest, treaty, and the doctrine of "lapse"—by which the Company claimed the territories of Indian rulers who died without male heirs. The Company governed India through an elaborate bureaucracy that combined European officials at the senior levels with Indian civil servants (typically excluded from the most senior positions) and a large army in which Indian sepoys served under British officers.

The Indian Rebellion of 1857–1858 transformed British India. Triggered by the introduction of new rifle cartridges greased with animal fat—which offended both Hindu and Muslim soldiers, who feared contamination by beef or pork—the rebellion spread rapidly from a mutiny among sepoy regiments to a broader uprising that drew in Indian princes, landowners, and civilians who resented Company rule. The uprising was suppressed with great violence; British forces killed tens of thousands of Indians, including both rebels and civilians. The Company was abolished in 1858, and India came under direct Crown governance, administered by a Viceroy appointed by the Crown and a Secretary of State for India responsible to Parliament.

The British Indian administration was one of the most elaborate colonial bureaucracies in the world. The Indian Civil Service—an elite corps of administrators recruited through competitive examinations—governed the country through a hierarchy of provinces, districts, and subdivisions. Indians could sit the examinations for the Indian Civil Service from 1853 onward, but the examinations were held only in London, making it extremely difficult for all but the wealthiest Indians to compete. The number of Indians in the senior ranks of the civil service remained tiny throughout the colonial period.

British economic policy in India was structured to benefit Britain at India's expense. India had been one of the world's leading producers of cotton textiles for centuries; Indian cloth had been exported to Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia and was renowned for its quality. Under British rule, Indian textile manufacturers were subjected to tariffs that made it difficult for them to compete with machine-made British cloth, while raw Indian cotton was exported to Britain for processing in Lancashire mills, whose products were then imported back into India. The result was the systematic deindustrialization of India's textile sector, the impoverishment of millions of Indian weavers and textile workers, and the development of a colonial economy oriented toward the export of raw materials rather than the production of finished goods.

British agricultural policy in India was equally consequential. The introduction of new systems of land tenure reorganized Indian agriculture in ways that increased revenue extraction but also increased vulnerability to famine. When monsoons failed, famines followed, and colonial policy often made them worse by insisting on continuing revenue collection and by resisting emergency food relief on grounds of free-market principle. Historians estimate that between 12 and 29 million Indians died in famines between 1876 and 1902—famines that occurred at least partly because colonial policies had disrupted traditional food security systems and redirected agricultural production toward export markets.

Section 44.7

Southeast Asia and Pacific Colonization

The colonization of Southeast Asia and the Pacific represented the extension of the imperial system into regions of extraordinary geographic, cultural, and political diversity. Southeast Asia—a region of large mainland states, maritime kingdoms, and island societies stretching from Burma in the west to the Philippines in the east—had long been connected to global trade networks linking it to China, India, the Middle East, and Europe. By the late nineteenth century, most of the region had been drawn into European colonial systems, though the timing, methods, and impacts of colonization varied considerably.

The French conquest of Vietnam was a prolonged process that began in the 1850s and was not completed until the 1880s. France first seized southern Vietnam (Cochinchina) in 1858–1862, partly to protect Catholic missionaries and partly to secure a foothold in East Asian trade. The conquest of northern Vietnam (Tonkin) and the imposition of a protectorate over central Vietnam (Annam) and over Cambodia and Laos followed in the 1880s and 1890s, completing the construction of French Indochina. Vietnamese resistance, led by scholars and mandarins loyal to the Nguyen dynasty, was suppressed with considerable violence, but it never entirely ceased. French economic policy restructured Vietnamese agriculture around rice and rubber exports, constructing plantations worked by Vietnamese laborers under conditions that were often little better than debt bondage.

The Dutch East Indies—the vast archipelago of islands stretching from Sumatra in the west to New Guinea in the east—had been under Dutch influence since the early seventeenth century, making it one of the oldest colonial systems in the world. Dutch colonial policy in the nineteenth century evolved through several phases. The Culture System (Cultuurstelsel), introduced in 1830, required Javanese peasants to devote a portion of their land and labor to the production of export crops—coffee, sugar, indigo—for the Dutch colonial government. The system generated enormous profits for the Netherlands but caused widespread suffering in Java, where forced cultivation of export crops at the expense of food crops contributed to famine. After international criticism, the Netherlands adopted a more liberal colonial policy in the later nineteenth century, but economic exploitation of the archipelago continued in different forms.

The Pacific islands were colonized through a variety of mechanisms by Britain, France, Germany, the United States, and Japan. The indigenous peoples of the Pacific faced devastating consequences from colonial contact. Epidemic diseases—smallpox, measles, influenza, dysentery—to which Pacific island populations had no previous exposure killed enormous proportions of many island populations. In Hawaii, the indigenous population declined from an estimated 300,000 at the time of first contact in 1778 to roughly 40,000 by 1890. In Fiji, measles epidemics in 1875 killed an estimated 40,000 people—roughly one-third of the entire population—within three months. These demographic catastrophes were not incidental to colonization; they were among its most consequential results.

The American acquisition of the Philippines following the Spanish-American War of 1898 produced one of the most brutal anti-colonial conflicts of the era. When the United States purchased the Philippines from Spain for $20 million and refused to recognize the independence proclaimed by Filipino leader Emilio Aguinaldo, a Filipino resistance movement launched a war that lasted from 1899 to 1902 and claimed hundreds of thousands of Filipino lives, mostly through disease, famine caused by American military tactics, and direct violence. The Philippine-American War exposed the contradiction between American rhetoric about democracy and freedom and the reality of American imperial conquest, generating a significant anti-imperialist movement within the United States itself—one that included figures such as Mark Twain and Andrew Carnegie.

Section 44.8

Missionaries, Education, and Cultural Change

Missionaries were among the most active agents of European imperialism, preceding colonial conquest in many regions, accompanying it in others, and sometimes advocating for the rights of colonized peoples against the worst excesses of colonial violence. The relationship between missionary activity and imperial power was complex and contradictory: missionaries genuinely believed in the spiritual and social benefits they were providing, often clashed with colonial administrators and traders over the treatment of indigenous peoples, but ultimately served as powerful agents of cultural transformation that undermined indigenous social and religious systems.

Protestant missionary societies—the London Missionary Society, the Church Missionary Society, the Basel Mission—had been active in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific since the late eighteenth century. Catholic missionaries—Jesuits, Dominicans, Franciscans, the Société des Missions Étrangères de Paris—had longer histories in many regions, dating to the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. By the nineteenth century, missionary activity had expanded enormously in scale and geographic reach, supported by the organizational capacity of mission societies, the communications technology of the telegraph and steamship, and the protective umbrella of colonial power.

Missionary education was one of the most significant long-term consequences of the imperial encounter. Mission schools provided literacy and numeracy skills that were increasingly necessary for participation in colonial economies. They also provided a pathway to social mobility for individuals and families who could use education to enter the colonial civil service, the clergy, the professions, or commerce. The educated African or Asian elite that eventually led the independence movements of the twentieth century was largely a product of mission education; the very language of rights, democracy, and self-determination that they turned against colonial rule was learned in mission schools.

But missionary education was also deeply coercive. Mission schools typically required the abandonment of indigenous religious practices, dress, music, and social customs. They taught European languages—English, French, Portuguese—as languages of instruction, often actively discouraging or forbidding the use of indigenous languages. They promoted European conceptions of family, gender, sexuality, and social organization that clashed fundamentally with indigenous norms. And they were embedded in systems of racial hierarchy that, however much individual missionaries might protest, placed European knowledge, culture, and religion above all alternatives.

The cultural consequences of missionary activity were profound and ambivalent. In many regions, Christianity was not simply imposed but actively adopted and transformed by indigenous communities. African Christianity, from its earliest forms in the nineteenth century, incorporated indigenous spiritual practices, community structures, and musical traditions in ways that made it distinctively African. "Ethiopian" churches—independent African Christian denominations that separated from European mission churches—began to appear in southern Africa in the 1890s, combining Christian theology with African cultural identity and often a theology of resistance to European racial hierarchy. These movements anticipated the great proliferation of African independent churches in the twentieth century and represented a form of religious and cultural self-determination under colonial conditions.

Section 44.9

Anti-Colonial Resistance

Anti-colonial resistance did not end with the period of conquest. Throughout the decades of colonial rule, African, Asian, and Pacific peoples contested imperial power in a remarkable variety of ways. The history of imperialism is inseparable from the history of resistance to imperialism.

Political organization emerged as an increasingly important form of resistance in the early twentieth century. Educated African, Indian, and Asian elites—many of them products of mission education and colonial universities—began to organize political associations, newspapers, and advocacy groups that pressed for civil rights, economic opportunity, and eventually self-governance. The Indian National Congress, founded in 1885, initially sought greater Indian participation in colonial governance rather than independence, but gradually became a vehicle for more radical demands. In Africa, organizations like the South African Native National Congress (founded 1912, later renamed the African National Congress) sought to defend the rights of African people against racial discrimination and colonial dispossession.

Mohandas Gandhi's development of satyagraha—non-violent resistance—in South Africa between 1893 and 1914 represents one of the most significant contributions to the history of anti-colonial resistance. Gandhi, an Indian lawyer who had settled in South Africa, organized the Indian community there to resist a series of discriminatory laws through non-violent non-cooperation—refusing to comply with unjust laws and accepting the legal consequences of that refusal. Though Gandhi's campaigns in South Africa achieved only limited immediate success, and though his own record on relations with the African population of South Africa was deeply problematic, the philosophy of satyagraha that he developed there would become the foundation for one of the most successful anti-colonial movements in history when he returned to India in 1915.

Religious resistance took many forms beyond the armed movements already mentioned. The Ahmadiyya movement in Islam, the Arya Samaj in Hinduism, the Buddhist revival movements in Ceylon (Sri Lanka) and Burma—all these represented attempts to reclaim cultural and religious identity under colonial conditions, to contest the claims of Christian missionaries, and to mobilize community identity as a resource for resistance. Many of these movements were also deeply engaged with questions of social reform that were influenced by, but also critical of, European ideas about rights and equality.

The global anti-colonial movement that would eventually produce the wave of decolonization in the mid-twentieth century had deep roots in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The Pan-African movement—which sought to unite people of African descent across national boundaries in resistance to colonialism and racism—was founded in part by diaspora intellectuals like W.E.B. Du Bois, who saw the condition of African Americans as inseparable from the condition of colonized Africans. The Japanese victory over Russia in 1905—the first time a non-European nation had defeated a European great power in modern warfare—sent shock waves of hope through colonized and semi-colonized societies from India to Egypt to Vietnam, demonstrating that European military dominance was not inevitable and that the world the colonizers had made could be unmade.

Section 44.10

Chapter Review and Imperialism Case Study

The New Imperialism reshaped the world in ways whose consequences extended far beyond the colonial period itself. The economic structures built by colonial administrations—the extraction of raw materials, the suppression of manufacturing, the creation of labor systems designed to serve export production—left colonized economies vulnerable and underdeveloped at independence. The political boundaries drawn by European powers—most starkly in Africa, where the Berlin Conference produced a map of straight lines that divided ethnic communities and lumped diverse peoples together in artificial administrative units—created the political geography that postcolonial states inherited and have struggled with ever since. The cultural transformations produced by missionary education, colonial language policy, and the systematic delegitimization of indigenous knowledge systems left deep marks on the identities and institutions of postcolonial societies.

At the same time, the history of imperialism is not only a history of European domination. It is equally a history of resistance, adaptation, and survival. The educated African and Asian elites who led independence movements in the mid-twentieth century were shaped by colonial conditions but were not products of them alone; they drew on deep wells of indigenous tradition, intellectual creativity, and political imagination to contest colonial power. The movements they led—in India, in Africa, in Southeast Asia, in the Pacific—were among the most significant political achievements of the modern world, and their roots lie precisely in the period of resistance this chapter has studied.

Regional Lens

Regional Lens: Ethiopia and the Preservation of Independence

Ethiopia stands almost alone among African nations in having successfully resisted the New Imperialism and preserved its political independence throughout the colonial era. That it did so is the result of several factors: exceptional leadership, geographical advantage, internal consolidation, and an extraordinary military achievement that shocked the world.

Ethiopia—or Abyssinia, as it was known to Europeans—is a highland state in the Horn of Africa, with terrain that favors defenders and makes logistical support for invading armies extremely difficult. It had a long tradition of statehood, Christianity (Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity dated to the fourth century CE), and military organization. By the late nineteenth century, a series of strong rulers—Tewodros II, Yohannes IV, and especially Menelik II—had consolidated the fragmented Ethiopian highlands into a more unified state and had begun to modernize its military with rifles and artillery purchased from European suppliers.

When Italy, which already controlled the coastal colony of Eritrea, sought to establish a protectorate over Ethiopia through the Treaty of Wuchale in 1889, Menelik II repudiated the treaty after discovering that the Italian and Amharic texts had different meanings—the Italian version implied a protectorate that the Amharic version did not. Italy proceeded to invade Ethiopia in 1895. At the Battle of Adwa on March 1, 1896, Menelik II deployed approximately 100,000 Ethiopian troops against an Italian force of some 20,000. The Italian army was decisively defeated, losing approximately 7,000 soldiers killed and 1,500 wounded, along with thousands of prisoners. The Treaty of Addis Ababa that followed recognized Ethiopian sovereignty and Italy's humiliation.

Adwa resonated far beyond the boundaries of Ethiopia. It was the first decisive defeat of a European colonial force by an African army in the modern era, and it demonstrated that European military dominance was not inevitable. News of the victory spread rapidly through the African diaspora; in the United States, Black newspapers celebrated Adwa as a vindication of African dignity and capacity. In Jamaica, in the Caribbean, and across the colonized world, the memory of Adwa became a touchstone of anti-colonial aspiration. It inspired later African nationalists and became one of the founding myths of Pan-Africanism and eventually of Rastafarianism, which regards Ethiopia as a sacred space and Menelik's descendant Haile Selassie as a messianic figure.

Global Connection

Global Connection: Imperialism and the Transformation of Global Commodity Chains

One of the most important but least visible effects of the New Imperialism was the transformation of global commodity chains—the systems linking raw material production in colonial territories to manufacturing and consumption in industrial centers. This transformation involved the deliberate restructuring of colonial economies to serve metropolitan needs, often at severe cost to local populations.

Rubber provides a particularly illuminating example. Rubber—derived from the sap of the rubber tree—became an essential industrial commodity in the late nineteenth century, with the development of vulcanization and the explosive growth of bicycle and eventually automobile manufacture. In the Congo Free State, King Leopold II's agents forced the Congolese population to collect wild rubber under conditions of extreme violence—including the systematic mutilation of workers who failed to meet their quotas—generating enormous profits for Leopold while killing and mutilating hundreds of thousands of Congolese people. The Amazon basin initially supplied most of the world's rubber through systems of debt peonage that similarly trapped indigenous workers. The development of rubber plantations in British Malaya and Dutch Indonesia in the early twentieth century shifted production to Southeast Asia, organized around its own coercive labor systems.

Similar dynamics played out with other commodities. Tea cultivation in British India required the development of plantation agriculture in Assam and Ceylon, organized around indentured or semi-bonded labor. The cultivation of cotton for export from Egypt was organized around a system of debt bondage that trapped Egyptian peasant farmers. Copper mining in the Belgian Congo and Northern Rhodesia, diamond mining in South Africa, gold mining on the Witwatersrand—all of these industries were organized around coercive labor systems that extracted enormous value from colonized workers while paying them a fraction of the market value of their labor.

The Congo rubber system was exposed through the work of journalists, missionaries, and human rights activists—including the British consul Roger Casement, whose 1904 report to the British government documented the atrocities in detail, and the journalist E.D. Morel, who organized the Congo Reform Association. Their campaign represents one of the first major international human rights campaigns in history, demonstrating that imperial violence could generate metropolitan opposition and eventually produce reform—though the structural inequality of the colonial economy continued long after Leopold's personal rule ended.

Empire Study

Empire Study: The Belgian Congo Under King Leopold II

The Congo Free State, established by King Leopold II of Belgium as his personal property in 1885 and governed by him until 1908, represents one of the most extreme examples of colonial exploitation in the history of imperialism. Understanding it in detail illuminates the dynamics of imperial violence, economic extraction, and international accountability.

Origins. Leopold II had sought an overseas colony since his accession to the Belgian throne in 1865, but the Belgian parliament showed no interest in colonial expansion. Independently of the Belgian state, Leopold employed Henry Morton Stanley to establish a chain of trading stations in the Congo basin in the late 1870s and early 1880s, negotiating treaties with local chiefs that—in their French language versions, which the chiefs did not understand—claimed to grant Leopold sovereignty over their territories. At the Berlin Conference, Leopold successfully presented the Congo Free State as a philanthropic enterprise devoted to free trade and the suppression of the slave trade, gaining international recognition of his personal sovereignty over the territory.

Administration. Leopold organized the Congo Free State as a commercial enterprise dedicated to extracting rubber and ivory. The entire territory was declared state property; Congolese people had no legal rights to the land they had occupied for generations. To extract rubber, Leopold's agents organized a system of forced labor in which Congolese communities were required to meet rubber quotas, with failure to meet quotas punishable by violence. The most notorious form of this violence was the systematic cutting off of hands—used to account for bullets fired (soldiers were required to produce a severed hand for each bullet used) and eventually as a punishment inflicted on workers and their families who failed to meet quotas.

Scale of violence. Estimates of the death toll in the Congo Free State vary widely, but scholars generally accept that the Congolese population declined by between 1 million and 10 million people between 1880 and 1920, through a combination of killing, starvation, disease accelerated by malnutrition, and a catastrophic decline in birth rates associated with the social disruption of the rubber system.

International exposure and reform. The Congo rubber system was exposed through a remarkable combination of missionary reports, journalistic investigation, and organized advocacy. E.D. Morel's Congo Reform Association, founded in 1904, mobilized public opinion in Britain, the United States, and Europe. Roger Casement's consular report of 1904 provided detailed, documented evidence of atrocities. Under intense international pressure, the Belgian government agreed to take over the Congo from Leopold in 1908, formally ending the worst excesses of his personal regime—though the structure of colonial exploitation continued under the Belgian state.

Significance. The Congo Free State was an extreme case, but it illuminates general features of the colonial system: the treatment of colonial territories as sources of extractable wealth rather than communities with their own rights; the use of systematic violence to compel labor; the role of racial ideology in making such violence thinkable; and the role of international civil society—missionaries, journalists, human rights advocates—in exposing and challenging colonial atrocities. The Congo case was formative for the development of international human rights law and advocacy in the twentieth century.

Map Study: The Partition of Africa, 1880–1914

A map of Africa in 1880 and a map of Africa in 1914 present a dramatic contrast. In 1880, most of the continent shows indigenous African sovereignty, with small European enclaves along the coasts. By 1914, the entire continent except Ethiopia and Liberia is divided among European powers.

What to look for on these maps:

  • The arbitrary straightness of many colonial boundaries in the Sahara and sub-Saharan Africa, revealing that they were drawn by Europeans with rulers rather than shaped by African populations, rivers, or political communities.
  • The size of individual colonial territories: French West Africa alone covered 3.7 million square kilometers—larger than the entire continental United States.
  • The strategic importance of the Nile Valley, the Congo basin, and the southern African mineral zone in shaping colonial competition between Britain, France, Germany, and Belgium.
  • The position of Ethiopia in the Horn of Africa and its highland terrain, which helps explain why it was able to resist Italian conquest.
  • The location of Liberia on the West African coast, established as a settlement for freed American slaves and recognized as nominally independent though under significant American influence.

Primary Source Window

Primary Source Window: Yaa Asantewaa's Speech, 1900

Creator: Yaa Asantewaa, queen mother of Edweso (Ashanti kingdom) · Date: March 1900 (as reconstructed from oral tradition) · Type: Reconstructed oral tradition; recorded in written form decades after the event

Context: In March 1900, the British governor of the Gold Coast, Frederick Hodgson, demanded that the Ashanti people surrender the Golden Stool—the sacred symbol of Ashanti sovereignty, believed to contain the spirit of the Ashanti nation. The demand provoked outrage. At a council meeting to decide how to respond, most of the male chiefs spoke in favor of submission. Yaa Asantewaa, queen mother of Edweso, reportedly rose to challenge the chiefs.

According to oral tradition, Yaa Asantewaa challenged the male chiefs' willingness to accept British demands. She invoked the memory of past Ashanti leaders who had never allowed the kingdom to be humiliated, and declared that if the men would not fight for the Golden Stool and for Ashanti sovereignty, then the women would take up arms and do so themselves. Her words are remembered as having shamed the male chiefs into agreeing to resist, thereby launching what became known as the War of the Golden Stool.

Analysis: This source is valuable on several levels. Even as a reconstructed tradition, it testifies to the historical memory of Ashanti women's role in resistance to colonialism. Yaa Asantewaa did lead the military resistance—she was captured by the British in 1900 and exiled to the Seychelles, where she died in 1921—so the tradition of her speech reflects historical reality, even if the specific words are uncertain. The source illuminates how colonized peoples understood their resistance: not merely as a political or military act, but as a defense of sacred symbols, cultural identity, and dignity against humiliation. It also illustrates the often underrepresented role of women in anti-colonial resistance, whose contributions rarely appear in colonial administrative records dominated by European and male perspectives.

Archaeology, Artifact, and Material Evidence Window

Evidence Window: The Benin Bronzes

The Benin Bronzes are a collection of several thousand sculptural objects—brass plaques, commemorative heads, figures of warriors and court officials—created by craftsmen in the Kingdom of Benin (in present-day Nigeria) between the thirteenth and nineteenth centuries. They represent one of the great artistic traditions of world history, demonstrating extraordinary technical skill in brass casting and a sophisticated visual vocabulary of royal power, ritual, and commemoration.

In January 1897, a British punitive expedition sacked the city of Benin, killed thousands of people, and seized approximately 3,000 of these objects, which were subsequently sold to museums and private collectors across Europe and North America. The objects are now held in collections at the British Museum, the Ethnologisches Museum in Berlin, the Weltmuseum in Vienna, the Pitt Rivers Museum in Oxford, and dozens of other institutions.

What scholars can infer: The Benin Bronzes are primary sources of enormous historical value. The brass plaques, which once decorated the pillars of the royal palace, depict scenes of court life, warfare, trade, and ceremony from the sixteenth through eighteenth centuries. They document the sophistication of Benin's political culture, its trade networks (Portuguese traders appear in some plaques, testifying to early European-African contact), and its artistic traditions. The commemorative brass heads, created in honor of deceased obas (kings), illustrate royal ideology and the role of ancestor veneration in Benin political culture.

Limits of the evidence: The bronzes were created within a specific political and ritual context that scholars can only partially reconstruct. Their meaning to their creators—which representations were considered sacred, which served primarily aesthetic or prestige functions—can be inferred only approximately from contextual evidence. And because they were removed from their original setting in a military raid, the context that would help interpret them most fully has been severed from the objects themselves.

Contemporary significance: The Benin Bronzes have become central to ongoing debates about the repatriation of cultural objects taken during colonial conquest. The Kingdom of Benin and the Nigerian government have long demanded their return. Some European institutions have begun to negotiate returns or long-term loans; others have resisted. The case illustrates how the material consequences of imperialism persist into the present, and how decisions made in the context of colonial violence continue to shape cultural policy and international relations today.

Historical Debate

Historical Debate: Was the New Imperialism Primarily Driven by Economic or Non-Economic Factors?

The question: Historians have debated for over a century what primarily drove the New Imperialism. Was it, at its core, an economic phenomenon—the product of industrial capitalism's need for raw materials, markets, and investment outlets? Or was it driven primarily by non-economic factors—nationalist competition, strategic rivalry, racial ideology, individual ambition, and the logic of state power?

Interpretation A: Economic Primacy

The economic interpretation of imperialism, associated most influentially with John Hobson's Imperialism (1902) and V.I. Lenin's Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism (1917), argues that the New Imperialism was fundamentally a product of industrial capitalism's internal dynamics. The accumulation of capital in the hands of a wealthy class created a need for investment opportunities abroad; the overproduction of manufactured goods created a need for new markets; the demands of industrial production created a need for raw materials. Colonial territories provided all three. On this view, the colonial map follows the map of economic interest: territories with significant resources—minerals, rubber, cotton, palm oil—were colonized more completely and exploited more intensively than territories without them.

Interpretation B: Non-Economic Primacy

Critics of the economic interpretation point out that the actual economic returns from many colonial territories were quite modest, at least in the short run. Much of the scrambled territory in Africa—especially in the Sahara and sub-Saharan regions distant from the coast—generated minimal economic return for decades after conquest. Nationalist competition, strategic calculation, and the desire for prestige seem to explain these acquisitions better than economic interest. Historians such as John Darwin and Ronald Robinson have emphasized the strategic dimension of imperialism—the desire to secure maritime routes, protect existing possessions, and prevent rivals from gaining advantage—as primary. Others emphasize the "man on the spot" phenomenon: individual colonial agents, traders, missionaries, and soldiers who created situations that metropolitan governments then felt compelled to back up with force, regardless of economic calculation.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on substantial evidence. The economic connection to imperialism is undeniable in specific cases—South African gold and diamonds, Nigerian palm oil, Indian cotton—but the economic case is weaker for many other colonial acquisitions. Strategic and nationalist considerations clearly mattered in cases like the British occupation of Egypt (to secure the Suez Canal), the French expansion in West Africa (to restore national prestige after 1871), and the German colonial acquisitions (driven partly by domestic political pressure for great-power status).

Why it matters: If imperialism was primarily economic, then understanding it requires understanding the development of industrial capitalism and global inequality. If it was primarily political and strategic, then understanding it requires attention to the dynamics of nation-state competition. And if it was primarily ideological—driven by racial theory and the civilizing mission—then understanding it requires grappling with the history of racism and its effects. Most historians today argue for a multicausal account: the New Imperialism was driven by the interaction of economic, political, strategic, and ideological factors, which reinforced and intensified each other.

Student task: After reviewing the evidence, which factor or combination of factors do you find most convincing as an explanation for the New Imperialism? What additional evidence would be most useful for evaluating these interpretations? Can you identify specific colonial cases where economic explanations seem compelling, and others where they seem inadequate?

Comparison Across Societies

Comparison Across Societies: British, French, and Belgian Colonial Administration in Africa

Three major European colonial powers—Britain, France, and Belgium—governed African territories according to different administrative philosophies, though all three ultimately served the interests of extraction and metropolitan power.

British Indirect Rule

Theorized most influentially by Frederick Lugard based on his experience in Nigeria, the British sought to govern through indigenous rulers, maintaining them in position as "native authorities" responsible for local administration, tax collection, and the enforcement of colonial law. In theory, this preserved indigenous institutions. In practice, indirect rule often involved the invention of "traditional" authority structures, the empowering of pliant rulers over resistant ones, and the transformation of hereditary leaders into salaried employees of the colonial state. Indirect rule was relatively cheap and politically stable in the short run, but it prevented the development of democratic institutions and reinforced conservative social structures that served colonial needs.

French Direct Rule

The French colonial philosophy of assimilation held that the distinction between French citizens and colonial subjects was not permanent; sufficiently educated and acculturated Africans could, in principle, become French citizens with full rights. In practice, the bar was impossibly high, and the vast majority of Africans under French rule remained colonial subjects with minimal legal rights. French administration directly replaced indigenous institutions with French structures, required the French language, and actively discouraged indigenous cultural practices. More egalitarian in theory than indirect rule, French direct administration was equally exploitative in practice.

Belgian Congo Administration

Belgian Congo administration, both under Leopold II and under the Belgian state after 1908, represented the most extreme form of colonial exploitation. Under Leopold, the territory was organized primarily as a rubber extraction system, with indigenous governance structures dismantled or ignored and the population subjected to systematic forced labor and violence. After 1908, Belgian colonial administration developed a paternalistic model that provided relatively good public health and primary education but absolutely refused to develop political institutions or prepare the Congolese for self-governance. The Belgian Congo remained almost entirely without educated African administrators, lawyers, or officers when it achieved independence in 1960, with catastrophic consequences for the subsequent political development of the country.

What the comparison reveals: All three colonial systems served the ultimate goal of extraction and metropolitan advantage. But they differed in their treatment of indigenous political structures, their theoretical frameworks, and their long-term legacies for postcolonial political development. The comparison illustrates that imperialism was not a monolithic system but a family of related systems, all serving similar ends through somewhat different means, and leaving different—though all deeply problematic—legacies for the nations that emerged from colonialism.

Multiple Perspectives

Multiple Perspectives: Experiencing Colonial Rule in British India

Indian Peasant Farmers

Peasant farmers experienced colonialism primarily through the land revenue system, which required the payment of monetary taxes regardless of harvest outcomes. When monsoons failed, peasants who could not pay their taxes faced seizure of their land and equipment, forcing them to borrow from moneylenders at usurious rates and sinking into permanent debt bondage. During the famines of the 1870s and 1890s, colonial administrators insisted on continuing revenue collection even from famine-stricken districts, and resisted large-scale relief measures on grounds of free-market principle. Peasants experienced colonial rule primarily as a system of extraction that left them impoverished, indebted, and vulnerable.

Educated Indian Professionals

Mission and colonial education had created a class of Indians proficient in English, trained in law, medicine, engineering, and administration, who could participate in the colonial economy and bureaucracy—but only up to a point. The Indian Civil Service remained effectively closed to all but a handful of Indians. The legal profession was one of the few arenas in which educated Indians could achieve genuine professional equality with Europeans. Many members of this class—Gandhi, Nehru, Gokhale, Tilak—developed anti-colonial politics precisely because their education exposed them to ideas of rights and democracy while their experience exposed them to the systematic racial discrimination that made those ideas inapplicable to themselves.

Indian Princes

Princes who had accepted subordinate relationships with British power occupied a privileged but precarious position. They retained nominal sovereignty over their territories—roughly one-third of the Indian subcontinent remained under princely rule—but were required to accept British "paramountcy," which included the right of the Viceroy to intervene in their administration and to depose rulers deemed unfit. Some princes accumulated enormous wealth; others chafed at the constraints of British supervision. As symbols of the traditional order, they were used by British colonial ideology as evidence of imperial respect for Indian culture, but they were also systematically disempowered as political actors.

British Officials and Their Families

For British civil servants, military officers, and their wives, India offered professional opportunities, social status, and adventure that would have been impossible at home. They lived in a world of profound racial privilege—separated from Indian society by clubs, cantonments, hill stations, and social conventions that reinforced their status. Many developed genuine knowledge and appreciation of Indian culture, history, and languages; some became advocates for Indian interests within the colonial system. But they also lived within a racial hierarchy that defined their identities and that they largely accepted as natural, even when they were critical of specific colonial policies.

Cause and Effect

Cause and Effect: The Indian Rebellion of 1857–1858

Long-Term Causes

Decades of East India Company governance that disrupted Indian society: the doctrine of lapse dispossessing Indian rulers; land revenue systems impoverishing peasants; railway and telegraph networks experienced as agents of cultural disruption; Christian missionary activity creating fears of forced conversion; systematic exclusion of Indians from positions of authority generating resentment among educated classes and army officers.

Immediate Cause

Introduction of new Enfield rifle cartridges greased with animal fat—offensive to both Hindu and Muslim soldiers. Soldiers at Meerut who refused to use the cartridges were arrested and court-martialed; their comrades' subsequent mutiny triggered rapid spreading of revolt across northern and central India.

The Event

The Rebellion began May 1857 with the mutiny of sepoy regiments at Meerut and the rapid advance of rebel forces on Delhi. It spread across northern India, drawing in Indian princes whose territories had been annexed, landlords whose rights had been curtailed, and ordinary people who experienced British rule as exploitative and alien. British forces, reinforced from loyal Indian troops in Punjab, suppressed the rebellion with great violence by late 1858.

Effects

Dissolution of the East India Company; direct Crown rule established 1858; Queen Victoria proclaimed Empress of India 1876; British Indian Army reorganized to reduce high-caste Hindu proportion; British rule became more conservative about interfering with Indian social institutions; stronger racial barriers erected between British and Indian society; the rebellion became a touchstone for later Indian nationalism.

Historical complexity: The rebellion cannot be reduced to a single cause. It was deeply regional, involving primarily northern and central India. It drew on diverse and sometimes contradictory motivations—religious anxiety, landlord grievance, sepoy solidarity, dynastic legitimacy—that did not add up to a coherent political program. Some regions, communities, and many individuals remained loyal to or actively supported the British.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Continuity and Change Over Time: African Societies Before and After Colonial Conquest

What Changed

  • Political sovereignty was lost: independent kingdoms, confederacies, and chieftaincies were dissolved, subordinated, or reorganized to serve colonial administrative needs.
  • Land tenure was disrupted: territory that had been common property, governed by customary law, was declared state property and reallocated to European settlers or concession companies.
  • Economic life was reorganized around export production: subsistence agriculture was supplemented or displaced by cash-crop cultivation for export markets.
  • Labor was extracted through taxation, forced labor, and contract systems that displaced men from communities and disrupted family structures.
  • Religious and cultural life was challenged: missionary education and colonial language policies disrupted transmission of traditional cultures.

What Stayed the Same

  • African languages survived: most Africans continued to use their mother tongues in daily life even while learning European languages for participation in colonial institutions.
  • Kinship systems and community structures proved remarkably durable, providing frameworks of solidarity and mutual support through the disruptions of colonialism.
  • Indigenous religious and spiritual practices were driven underground or adapted in syncretic forms but were not extinguished.
  • Systems of local authority—even when nominally subordinated to colonial administration—preserved real influence within communities.

Why this pattern matters: The independence movements that challenged colonial rule drew on both the grievances created by colonial disruption and the cultural and political resources that had survived colonialism. The postcolonial Africa that emerged in the mid-twentieth century was neither the precolonial Africa of 1880 nor the creation of colonialism alone; it was the product of the complex interaction of indigenous traditions, colonial transformations, and anti-colonial resistance across several generations.

Everyday Life and Social History

Everyday Life Under Colonial Rule: African Workers in South African Gold Mines

The discovery of gold on the Witwatersrand in 1886 created the largest gold mining industry in the world, and with it a system of labor control and racial segregation that anticipated and helped create the structures of apartheid in the twentieth century. The experience of African miners in the Rand gold mines illustrates concretely how the large structural forces of imperial capitalism shaped individual lives.

African workers in the gold mines came from across southern and central Africa—from the Zulu kingdom, from Mozambique, from what is now Zambia and Zimbabwe. They were recruited under labor contracts that typically ran for several months, after which they could renew or return home. They were housed in all-male compounds—enclosed dormitories owned and managed by the mining companies—that controlled their movement, regulated their diet, and prevented contact with the urban society surrounding the mines. The compound system served multiple purposes for mine owners: it reduced labor costs by providing basic housing and food in lieu of higher wages, it prevented workers from deserting or seeking better employment, and it prevented the formation of permanent African working-class communities in urban areas.

Wages for African miners were set by collusion among the mining companies at levels far below what the market would have produced in the absence of racial coercion. A skilled African worker received wages that were a fraction of what an unskilled white worker received for the same work. When African workers organized resistance—slowing down work, staging collective work stoppages, or simply refusing to renew contracts—they were liable to prosecution under the Masters and Servants Act and other laws that criminalized breaches of labor contract.

For individual workers, the compound was a world of profound isolation and hardship. Separated from their families and communities for months at a time, housed in crowded barracks, subjected to harsh discipline, and exposed to the extraordinary physical dangers of underground gold mining—dust-induced silicosis, rock falls, explosions, and flooding—African miners experienced a form of labor discipline imposed and maintained by the racial power of the colonial state. Yet miners were not passive victims: they developed community networks within the compounds, maintained links with their home communities through remittances, and organized, over time, into trade unions and political movements that challenged the racial capitalism of the Rand.

Chapter Summary

The New Imperialism of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries was one of the most consequential episodes in modern world history. In roughly three decades, European powers and the United States brought virtually the entire world outside Europe and the Americas under colonial or quasi-colonial control. By 1914, the map of the world had been transformed: Africa had been partitioned among European powers, Southeast Asia had been divided among British, French, Dutch, and American colonizers, the Pacific had been claimed by multiple competing powers, and South Asia had been organized as the centerpiece of the British imperial system for nearly a century.

The New Imperialism was made possible by the intersection of multiple historical forces: the industrial revolution, which gave European powers both the economic motive and the technological capacity for global expansion; the competitive dynamics of European nationalism, which made colonial empire a marker of great-power status and drove the pace of acquisition; the development of racial ideology and the civilizing mission, which provided moral justification for conquest and organized colonial administration; and the specific military and medical technologies—the Maxim gun, quinine—that removed the barriers to European penetration of previously inaccessible territories.

Colonial rule was organized around the extraction of economic value from colonized territories. Raw materials—rubber, cotton, palm oil, gold, diamonds, copper, tin—were extracted and exported to industrial economies. Labor was mobilized through a variety of coercive mechanisms: taxation, forced labor, contract systems, and the conversion of common land to private or state property that stripped communities of their subsistence base. The infrastructure built by colonial administrations—railways, roads, ports, telegraphs—was designed primarily to facilitate resource extraction and military control rather than to serve the economic development of colonized populations.

The societies that were colonized were not passive victims of this process. They resisted in remarkable ways. Armed resistance took the form of wars of conquest and rebellion: the Zulu War, the Mahdist uprising, the Maji Maji Rebellion, the War of the Golden Stool, the Philippine-American War, and dozens of other conflicts in which colonized peoples contested European power with the resources available to them. Political resistance took the form of petitions, advocacy, journalism, and the early nationalist organizations—the Indian National Congress, the South African Native National Congress—that would eventually lead independence movements. Cultural resistance took the form of religious revival, the formation of African independent churches, and the preservation of indigenous languages and traditions against missionary and colonial cultural pressure.

The effects of colonial rule were profound and lasting. Colonial economic structures impoverished colonized populations, disrupted subsistence systems, and created export-dependent economies vulnerable to global commodity price fluctuations. Colonial boundaries—drawn without reference to indigenous political or ethnic realities—created political geographies that postcolonial states inherited and have struggled with ever since. Colonial education systems created literate elites that eventually challenged colonial rule, but also created deep cultural divides and linguistic hierarchies that persist in postcolonial societies.

Racial ideology was central to the entire imperial project. The pseudo-scientific racism of the late nineteenth century provided a framework that made colonial violence thinkable, justified the systematic exclusion of colonized peoples from political rights and economic opportunity, and organized the social hierarchies of colonial societies. The violence of the colonial encounter—the Herero genocide, the Congo rubber atrocities, the Indian famines, the brutal suppression of anti-colonial resistance movements—was made possible in part by a racial framework that dehumanized colonized peoples and placed their lives outside the circle of full moral consideration.

The New Imperialism generated resistance not only in the colonized world but within the metropolitan societies of the colonial powers themselves. The Congo reform movement, the British anti-war movement during the Anglo-Boer War, the American anti-imperialist movement during the Philippine-American War—these were early manifestations of a moral critique of empire that would eventually contribute to the delegitimization of colonial rule. The contradictions between imperial reality and the democratic and liberal values that European and American societies professed were visible to contemporaries who chose to look.

Understanding the New Imperialism is essential for understanding how the contemporary world came to be organized as it is, why the inequalities that define global politics and economics take the forms they do, and why the struggles for full decolonization—political, economic, and cultural—remain unfinished. The history of imperialism is still being lived.

Review Materials

Key Terms

New Imperialism
The rapid, comprehensive, and systematically organized expansion of European colonial control over most of the world between approximately 1870 and 1914, distinguished from earlier forms of colonial expansion by its scope, pace, and technological foundation.
Colonialism
The political, economic, and cultural domination of one society by another through conquest, settlement, and direct administrative control.
Scramble for Africa
The rapid and competitive partition of African territories among European powers in the 1880s and 1890s, formalized through bilateral agreements and military campaigns.
Berlin Conference
The 1884–1885 international meeting at which European powers established rules for the partition of Africa, including the principle of "effective occupation."
Effective occupation
The principle established at the Berlin Conference that a colonial claim required actual administrative presence and control, not merely declaration.
Protectorate
A territory that retains nominal local sovereignty but is controlled in practice by a foreign power that manages its external affairs and often its internal administration.
Civilizing mission
The ideological claim that European imperial rule benefited colonized peoples by bringing Christianity, literacy, modern medicine, and the rule of law; used to justify conquest and organize colonial administration.
Social Darwinism
The misapplication of evolutionary theory to human societies, used to argue that colonial domination reflected genuine biological superiority of European races and was therefore "natural."
Racial ideology
A system of beliefs classifying humans by race and arranging races in a hierarchy of intelligence, civilization, and moral development; central to the justification and organization of colonial rule.
Direct rule
A colonial administrative system in which the metropolitan power governs territories through its own officials, replacing indigenous political structures.
Indirect rule
A colonial administrative system, associated especially with British practice, in which indigenous rulers are retained but subordinated to colonial supervision and direction.
Concession company
A private company granted rights to exploit resources in a colonial territory, often with coercive powers over local populations; exemplified by the Congo Free State rubber system.
Compound system
The system of enclosed worker housing used in South African mines to control African workers, prevent labor mobility, and maintain racial segregation.
Sepoy
An Indian soldier serving in the British Indian Army; sepoy regiments were central to British military power in India and across the imperial system.
Indian Civil Service
The elite administrative bureaucracy that governed British India; nominally open to Indians from 1853 but practically dominated by British officials throughout the colonial period.
Doctrine of lapse
The British East India Company policy of annexing the territories of Indian rulers who died without male heirs; used aggressively to expand Company territory before the 1857 Rebellion.
Satyagraha
Non-violent resistance; the political philosophy developed by Mohandas Gandhi in South Africa, based on the principle that unjust laws should be disobeyed openly and their legal consequences accepted.
Askari
African soldiers who served in European colonial armies in Africa, typically commanded by European officers.
Ethiopian churches
Independent African Christian denominations that separated from European mission churches in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, combining Christian theology with African cultural identity and anti-colonial resistance.
Congo Reform Association
An international advocacy organization founded in 1904 by E.D. Morel to campaign against the atrocities of the Congo Free State; one of the first major international human rights campaigns.
Maji Maji Rebellion
A major uprising against German colonial rule in German East Africa (1905–1907), inspired by prophetic religious ideology and suppressed with mass violence causing hundreds of thousands of deaths.
Pan-Africanism
A political movement seeking to unite people of African descent across national and geographic boundaries in opposition to colonialism, racism, and racial oppression.
Vernichtungsbefehl
The extermination order issued by German General Lothar von Trotha in 1904 declaring the Herero people of German Southwest Africa to be outside the protection of German law; led to the first genocide of the twentieth century.

Discussion Questions

  1. The New Imperialism is sometimes described as having brought "benefits" to colonized peoples—railways, hospitals, schools, the end of the slave trade. How should historians evaluate these claims? Can material improvements coexist with fundamental injustice?
  2. Was the New Imperialism inevitable? Was there a realistic alternative—either for European nations that chose not to pursue colonial expansion or for colonized peoples who might have organized successful long-term resistance?
  3. The Herero and Nama genocide of 1904–1908 is often described as a "rehearsal" for the Holocaust. What features of colonial violence—the racial ideology, the use of concentration camps, the extermination order—anticipated features of Nazi genocide? What are the limits of this comparison?
  4. How should we think about the role of missionaries in the history of imperialism? Were they agents of colonial domination, critics of colonial violence, or both? How do you evaluate the complex legacy of missionary education and cultural transformation?
  5. Gandhi's satyagraha was developed in South Africa partly in relation to the Indian community's position vis-à-vis both the British colonial state and the African majority. How should we think about the relationship between Gandhi's theoretical contribution to anti-colonial resistance and the limitations of his own politics?
  6. The Benin Bronzes and similar objects taken during colonial conquest are now held in museums across Europe and North America. Should they be returned to their countries of origin? What considerations—cultural heritage, legal ownership, museum stewardship, political symbolism—are relevant to this question?

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 44 has examined one of the most consequential episodes in modern world history: the New Imperialism of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. This chapter matters not because imperialism is history that can be safely confined to the past, but because it is history whose consequences are still present. The economic inequalities between the global north and global south that structure contemporary international relations have deep roots in the colonial period. The political boundaries of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific bear the marks of colonial partition. The cultural hierarchies that privilege European languages and knowledge systems in global institutions are legacies of colonial education policy. Understanding the New Imperialism is therefore understanding the historical formation of the contemporary world.

This chapter connects to the larger textbook in multiple ways. It builds on the earlier chapters examining the industrial revolution and European nationalism, showing how those developments created both the motive and the capacity for imperial expansion. It prepares students for the chapters that follow on World War I—which was partly a product of the imperial rivalries that the Scramble for Africa and Asia had intensified—and on decolonization, which can only be understood as a response to the colonial systems examined here. The anti-colonial movements studied in this chapter—Gandhi's satyagraha, the Indian National Congress, Pan-Africanism—are the direct predecessors of the independence movements that would reshape the world in the mid-twentieth century.

The chapter also illustrates several of the textbook's central methodological commitments. It refuses to tell the history of imperialism only from the perspective of the colonizers; it insists on recovering the perspectives, agency, and resistance of colonized peoples. It situates local events—the Ashanti War of the Golden Stool, the Maji Maji Rebellion, the Battle of Adwa—within the global system of imperial competition and anti-colonial resistance that connected them. And it takes seriously the role of ideas—racial ideology, the civilizing mission, satyagraha—in shaping historical outcomes, refusing to reduce the history of imperialism to a purely economic or political calculation.

Finally, this chapter raises moral and political questions that cannot be answered by historical analysis alone but that historical knowledge is essential for addressing. How should former colonial powers respond to the legacies of colonialism? What obligations, if any, do wealthy postcolonial nations have to poorer postcolonial nations whose poverty is partly a legacy of colonial extraction? Should cultural objects taken during colonial conquest be returned? How should the history of colonial violence—including genocides like the Herero and Nama genocide, long denied by the German government but finally acknowledged in recent years—be acknowledged and addressed? These are questions for citizens, not only for historians, but historical understanding is indispensable for engaging with them honestly and without illusion.

The story of the New Imperialism is, in the end, a story about power—about who had it, how they used it, what they did to those who lacked it, and how those without power found ways to resist, survive, and ultimately overcome. It is a story that remains unfinished, not because empire has simply continued unchanged, but because the structures it created—economic, political, cultural, and legal—continue to shape the lives of billions of people. Understanding those structures, their origins, and the long history of resistance to them is part of what it means to be historically literate in the contemporary world.

Part VII — The Early Modern World, 1450–1750

Ming and Qing China, Korea, and Tokugawa Japan

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"In 1405, a fleet unlike any the world had seen assembled in the harbor of Nanjing — and the choices that followed reveal as much about early modern East Asia as the voyages themselves."

In 1405, a fleet unlike any the world had seen assembled in the harbor of Nanjing. Its largest vessels stretched more than four hundred feet in length — larger than any European ship would be for another century. Aboard this armada of hundreds of ships sailed tens of thousands of sailors, soldiers, diplomats, physicians, astrologers, and translators. At the head of the expedition was Zheng He, a Muslim eunuch official in the service of the Yongle Emperor of the Ming dynasty. Over the next three decades, Zheng He would lead seven great voyages across the Indian Ocean, reaching Southeast Asia, India, the Persian Gulf, and the East African coast. He brought back exotic animals, tribute goods, and diplomatic missions from dozens of distant kingdoms. Yet when the voyages ended after 1433, China would deliberately turn away from maritime ambition. The great ships were left to rot; the records of the voyages were suppressed. No Chinese colonial empire would follow. This paradox — the power to project influence across the globe, followed by a conscious choice of restraint — lies at the heart of early modern East Asian history.

East Asia in the period from roughly 1368 to 1868 was defined by some of the most sophisticated, populous, and culturally productive civilizations in the world. China under the Ming dynasty (1368–1644) recovered from Mongol rule to rebuild one of the largest agrarian empires in history, with a population of perhaps 200 million people by the late sixteenth century. Under the Qing dynasty (1644–1912), an empire of even greater territorial extent brought together Chinese, Manchu, Mongolian, Tibetan, and Central Asian peoples into a complex imperial system. Korea under the Choson dynasty (1392–1897) refined one of the world's most durable Confucian states. Japan under the Tokugawa shogunate (1603–1868) created a sophisticated feudal order that transformed into one of the world's most economically active and culturally vibrant early modern societies. Together, these states formed an East Asian world defined by shared Confucian ideas, Chinese cultural influence, Buddhist institutions, carefully managed trade, and fierce assertions of local sovereignty.

This chapter traces the political, social, economic, cultural, and international histories of East Asia across roughly five centuries. It examines how the Ming dynasty rebuilt Chinese imperial institutions after the disruption of Mongol rule, how Zheng He's voyages represented China's greatest moment of maritime ambition, and why China ultimately chose a different path from European powers that would soon send their own ships across the same oceans. It studies how the Qing conquest transformed China while preserving and extending its imperial traditions. It examines Choson Korea's extraordinary commitment to Confucian governance, literary culture, and local adaptation. It traces Tokugawa Japan's peace-centered political order, its careful control of foreign trade, and the urban commercial culture that flourished within its borders.

The history of early modern East Asia challenges a common assumption in world history: that this period was primarily the story of European expansion, exploration, and global dominance. In terms of population, agricultural output, manufacturing capacity, urban life, literary culture, and political sophistication, East Asia was arguably the most important region in the world during these centuries. China alone probably accounted for between a quarter and a third of global economic output in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The silver that Spanish colonizers extracted from the Americas flowed in enormous quantities to China, where it was absorbed as currency for one of the world's most complex commercial economies. Understanding early modern East Asia is not a supplement to the study of this period — it is essential to understanding the world itself.

This chapter also examines how East Asian societies understood and managed their relationships with the outside world. The Chinese tributary system — in which neighboring states acknowledged the emperor's symbolic supremacy in exchange for trade rights and imperial gifts — shaped diplomatic relations across the region. Both Choson Korea and Tokugawa Japan participated in versions of this system while maintaining real political independence. All three societies faced the gradual intrusion of European trading ships, missionaries, and commercial networks. Their responses — ranging from cautious engagement to deliberate restriction — reflected complex calculations about sovereignty, religion, commerce, and the social order. These responses would shape East Asia's encounter with industrializing Europe in the nineteenth century, the subject of a later chapter.

Historians studying early modern East Asia draw on an extraordinarily rich body of evidence. Chinese imperial archives, court documents, local gazetteers, philosophical texts, poetry, novels, drama, paintings, ceramics, tax records, and archaeological sites all illuminate different dimensions of social and political life. Korean records include royal annals compiled with remarkable care over five centuries, as well as literary collections, examination records, and local histories. Japanese sources include shogunal decrees, domain records, merchant diaries, popular fiction, woodblock prints, and vast collections of poetry and drama. Together, these sources allow historians to study not only rulers and officials but also merchants, artisans, peasants, women, Buddhist monks, Confucian scholars, and urban pleasure-seekers. The picture that emerges is one of extraordinary complexity and human richness.

The period studied in this chapter concludes with internal stresses accumulating across all three societies — population pressure in China, economic difficulties among the Japanese samurai class, and social tensions in Korea — that would combine with intensifying European and American pressure in the nineteenth century to produce the dramatic transformations studied in later chapters. Understanding what East Asia built across these five centuries is essential to understanding what it would face.

Historical Context

The early modern period in East Asia stretches from the collapse of Mongol power in China in the mid-fourteenth century to the arrival of industrialized Western pressure in the mid-nineteenth century. This period of roughly five centuries encompasses some of the most significant developments in the history of the world's most populous region.

China's experience with the Mongol Yuan dynasty (1271–1368) had been deeply disruptive. The Mongol conquerors had dismantled parts of the examination system, installed non-Chinese officials in positions of power, reorganized the population into hierarchical categories that placed Chinese at a disadvantage, and subjected China to heavy taxation and military demands. At the same time, the Yuan period fostered remarkable cross-cultural exchange along the Silk Roads, brought new artistic and commercial influences, and saw the continuation of Chinese urban and commercial life. When popular rebellions toppled the Yuan and a former peasant named Zhu Yuanzhang founded the Ming dynasty in 1368, his priorities were clear: restore Chinese rule, rebuild the bureaucracy, reassert Confucian values, and reconnect the Chinese state with the agrarian foundations that had supported earlier dynasties.

The Ming dynasty (1368–1644) is one of the great restoration projects in world history. Its founders rebuilt the examination system, re-established the Confucian bureaucratic apparatus, constructed vast new imperial infrastructure including the restored Great Wall and the Forbidden City in Beijing, and projected Chinese cultural influence across East Asia and beyond. At the same time, the Ming period saw the transformation of the Chinese economy, the growth of cities, the commercialization of agriculture, the expansion of trade, and the flourishing of popular culture in the form of novels, drama, and urban entertainment. Tens of millions of Chinese peasants grew crops for distant markets, small manufacturers produced silk and porcelain for sale across the world, and merchants — formally ranked low in Confucian social hierarchies — accumulated enormous wealth.

Korea entered the early modern period with the founding of the Choson dynasty in 1392. Choson's founders were deeply influenced by Chinese Neo-Confucianism and committed to building a Confucian state more thoroughly than any previous Korean regime. Over the following five centuries, Korea developed one of the world's most elaborately Confucian social systems, complete with a rigorous examination system, a hierarchical social order based on Confucian principles, and a remarkable literary and intellectual culture. Korea navigated its relationship with the Ming and later the Qing dynasty with careful diplomacy, accepting symbolic subordination in the tributary system while maintaining full internal sovereignty.

Japan entered the early modern period after a century of devastating civil war known as the Sengoku period. The Tokugawa shogunate, established in 1603, ended this violence by creating a powerful federal political order in which the Tokugawa family as shogun held national authority while hundreds of regional lords governed their own domains. This system created remarkable political stability. For more than two centuries, Japan experienced internal peace. Within this stable order, commercial agriculture spread, cities grew, merchants accumulated wealth, and a vibrant popular culture of theater, fiction, woodblock prints, and urban entertainment emerged.

Regional Orientation

China occupies the eastern portion of the Asian continent, bordered to the north and northwest by the Eurasian steppe, to the south and southwest by mainland Southeast Asia and the Himalayas, and to the east by the Pacific Ocean. China's geography divides into several major zones: the North China Plain, watered by the Yellow River and historically the core of agrarian civilization and imperial power; the Yangtze River valley and delta in central and southern China, which became by the Ming period the most economically productive region; and the south coast, which faced the South China Sea and was the point of departure for maritime trade. China also encompassed the dry grasslands of Inner Mongolia, Manchuria, and the high plateaus of Tibet and Xinjiang — all regions that would be incorporated into the Qing empire.

Korea occupies a peninsula extending southward from the Asian mainland, surrounded on three sides by water. Korea's geography is predominantly mountainous, with limited arable land concentrated in the west and south. Korea shares a land border with Manchuria and is separated from Japan by a short strait. This position made Korea a bridge between China and Japan and exposed it to military pressure from both directions. Korea's relationship with China was central to its political and cultural development throughout the Choson period.

Japan is an archipelago of four main islands — Honshu, Kyushu, Shikoku, and Hokkaido — separated from the Asian mainland by the Korea Strait and the Sea of Japan. Japan's mountainous topography limited arable land to coastal plains and river valleys, concentrating population in particular regions and fostering the regional identities that shaped Japanese feudal politics. Japan's island location gave it a degree of geographic protection from continental invasion and allowed the Tokugawa government to regulate foreign contact relatively effectively. Japan's proximity to Korea and China meant that cultural, technological, and religious influences flowed continuously across the sea.

All three societies interacted with one another and with wider regional and global networks. Chinese cultural influence shaped Korean and Japanese writing, philosophy, governance, and art. The tributary system structured diplomatic relations. Maritime trade networks connected China's ports to Southeast Asia, India, and eventually Europe. Silver from Spanish America flowed into Chinese markets through Manila. Understanding East Asia means understanding not just individual societies but the dense web of connections among them.

Section 35.1

East Asia in the Early Modern World

When European maritime nations began their era of oceanic expansion in the late fifteenth century, East Asia was not a region on the margins of the world. China was perhaps the most populous, most urbanized, and most commercially developed civilization on earth. Korea was a stable, literate, Confucian state with a distinctive cultural tradition. Japan, despite its ongoing civil wars, possessed sophisticated military technology, productive agriculture, and a rich artistic culture. Together, East Asian civilizations represented the center of gravity of the world economy and some of the most complex political and social systems that humanity had created.

The concept of an "early modern" period in East Asia is partly a borrowing from European historiography. In East Asian history, this period is often defined by the rise and fall of specific dynasties — the Ming and Qing in China, the Choson in Korea, the Tokugawa order in Japan — and by the transformations of economy, society, and culture that accompanied them. What makes it "early modern" in a global sense is its position at the beginning of a new phase of intensified global connection. Silver from the Americas, spices from Southeast Asia, cotton from India, and Chinese silk and porcelain moved along trade routes that now circled the globe. East Asia was deeply embedded in these networks, even when its rulers tried to regulate or limit the flow.

The relationship among China, Korea, and Japan during this period was structured by a set of ideas, practices, and institutions loosely known as the Sinitic cultural sphere or the East Asian world order. China stood at the center of this order, not simply through military dominance but through cultural prestige. The Chinese writing system was the shared medium of elite literacy across the region. Confucian philosophy provided the intellectual framework for governance, ethics, and social organization. Buddhism, which had traveled to East Asia from India via Central Asia centuries earlier, continued to shape religious life, art, and architecture. Chinese poetry, painting, historiography, and philosophy were studied, imitated, and adapted by Korean and Japanese scholars alike.

Yet the Sinitic cultural sphere was not a system of subordination. Korea and Japan were genuinely sovereign states with their own political traditions, languages, local cultural forms, and historical identities. They adopted what they found useful from Chinese models and adapted it to their own needs. Korea invented its own alphabet; Japan developed its own literary forms including haiku poetry and the novel; both developed legal and political systems that blended Chinese principles with local realities. Understanding East Asia in this period requires holding two ideas simultaneously: the extraordinary reach of Chinese cultural influence and the genuine creativity and independence of Korean and Japanese civilizations.

The early modern centuries were also a period of intense internal transformation for all three societies. China's population grew dramatically, cities swelled, new crops from the Americas transformed agriculture, and a commercial economy created new wealth and new social tensions. The Qing conquest brought dramatic political change but also remarkable imperial expansion. Korea weathered devastating Japanese invasions in the 1590s and then Manchu invasions in the 1620s and 1630s before experiencing two centuries of relative stability. Japan ended a century of civil war and entered a period of unprecedented peace and commercial vitality. These transformations were not simply responses to European pressure — they arose from internal dynamics and regional interactions that drove East Asian history in its own directions.

Section 35.2

Ming China and Imperial Restoration

The Ming dynasty was born in violence and shaped by its founders' determination to prevent the recurrence of what they had experienced. Zhu Yuanzhang, known to history by his imperial title as the Hongwu Emperor, was born in 1328 into a desperately poor peasant family in central China. He grew up during the catastrophic population decline caused by plague, famine, and the collapse of Mongol rule. He lost most of his family to starvation and disease. He survived by begging and eventually joining a millenarian rebel movement before rising through military skill, political cunning, and ruthless elimination of rivals to conquer the Yuan dynasty and proclaim the Ming in 1368.

The Hongwu Emperor's vision for the Ming dynasty was shaped by his own experience of poverty and disorder. He distrusted merchants, cities, and sophisticated elites. He idealized a stable agrarian society of self-sufficient farmers governed by virtuous officials selected through the examination system. He rebuilt the examination system as the primary path to official status, eliminating many non-examination routes to office that had existed under the Mongols. He organized the population into households registered in the Yellow Registers, tracking tax obligations, and into local mutual responsibility groups called lijia. He assigned peasant households to specific agricultural occupations and tried to prevent mobility. He executed tens of thousands of officials and nobles whom he suspected of disloyalty or corruption. His legacy was a state of extraordinary institutional completeness, but also extraordinary autocratic concentration of power.

The examination system that the Ming inherited and elaborated deserves careful attention, because it was the most important institution in Chinese political life. Candidates spent years — sometimes decades — memorizing the Confucian classics and learning to write in a highly formalized style known as the eight-legged essay (baguwen). The examinations proceeded through multiple levels: local prefectural examinations, provincial examinations, and the metropolitan examination in Beijing. Those who passed the highest level, the jinshi, were eligible for the most prestigious official posts. The system was theoretically open to virtually all male commoners, but sons of wealthy families who could afford years of study had enormous advantages, and the official class tended to reproduce itself across generations. Nevertheless, the examination system created a remarkably unified political culture in which officials across the empire shared the same texts, values, and conceptual vocabulary.

The Yongle Emperor (r. 1402–1424) was one of the most ambitious rulers in Chinese history. He seized the throne from his nephew in a civil war, then demonstrated the grandeur of his reign through spectacular projects. He sponsored the compilation of the Yongle Dadian, an enormous encyclopedia of Chinese knowledge. He moved the capital from Nanjing to Beijing, constructing there the vast imperial palace complex known as the Forbidden City. He launched multiple military campaigns into Mongolia and Vietnam. And he sponsored the extraordinary maritime expeditions led by Zheng He — about which more will be said in the next section.

Ming imperial government rested on a bureaucracy of about twenty thousand officials organized into the Six Ministries — personnel, revenue, rites, war, justice, and public works — supplemented by dozens of specialized agencies. A system of censors known as the Censorate theoretically monitored official conduct and could remonstrate with the emperor. The Grand Secretariat, a body of senior officials who prepared communications between emperor and bureaucracy, became increasingly important as a kind of proto-cabinet. Eunuchs — castrated men who served in the imperial household — gained considerable influence during the Ming dynasty, sometimes acting as power brokers between the emperor and the outer bureaucracy. The tension between eunuch favorites and the regular Confucian bureaucracy was a recurring feature of Ming politics across nearly three centuries.

Ming China was not static. The dynasty's early agricultural policies began to break down under the weight of demographic and commercial change. The population grew from perhaps 60–70 million in the early Ming to perhaps 200 million or more by the late sixteenth century. This growth was sustained partly by the spread of new agricultural techniques and irrigation, partly by the expansion of cultivated land, and from the sixteenth century onward by the adoption of new crops from the Americas — maize, sweet potatoes, and peanuts — that could be grown on land unsuitable for traditional staples. Agricultural surplus flowed into markets. The Yangtze delta region specialized in silk production; Fujian in porcelain and tea; entire counties devoted themselves to specific craft industries. By the sixteenth century, China had one of the world's most commercialized economies, even as its official ideology insisted on the primacy of agriculture.

Section 35.3

Zheng He and Maritime Expeditions

Between 1405 and 1433, the Ming dynasty sponsored seven enormous maritime expeditions that sailed from China into the Indian Ocean world. Led by the eunuch admiral Zheng He, these voyages reached Southeast Asia, the Indian subcontinent, the Persian Gulf, and the East African coast. They demonstrated that China possessed the technological capacity, organizational ability, and political will to project power across the maritime world on a scale no other state could match. Their discontinuation after 1433 is one of the great turning points in world history.

Zheng He was born in 1371 in Yunnan in southwestern China, into a Muslim family of Central Asian descent. His father and grandfather had made the hajj to Mecca, giving him knowledge of the Islamic world and its geography. As a young boy, he was captured during a Ming military campaign and castrated, entering the imperial household as a eunuch. He rose through palace service to become a trusted confidant of the Yongle Emperor, who appointed him to command the maritime expeditions.

The scale of the expeditions was breathtaking. The first voyage in 1405 involved approximately 317 ships and nearly 28,000 men — sailors, soldiers, physicians, interpreters, astrologers, and diplomats. The largest vessels, the so-called "treasure ships" (baochuan), were by many estimates the largest wooden ships ever built, possibly exceeding four hundred feet in length and five times the size of the largest European ships of the era. The fleets carried silk, porcelain, and other Chinese goods to present as imperial gifts; they returned bearing tribute, exotic animals including giraffes (interpreted as auspicious unicorn-like creatures), diplomatic missions, and luxury goods from distant kingdoms.

The purposes of the expeditions were primarily diplomatic and political rather than commercial in the European sense. The Yongle Emperor sought to establish China's position as the preeminent power in the maritime world and to bring distant kingdoms into the tributary system. Rulers who sent tribute missions to the Ming court received imperial recognition, gifts of greater value than their tribute, and the right to trade at Chinese ports. The expeditions were instruments of soft power — demonstrations of imperial majesty and generosity designed to attract voluntary submission rather than to conquer territory. This purpose distinguished the Chinese voyages fundamentally from the European maritime expeditions that would follow.

The routes of the expeditions covered the major maritime networks of the Indian Ocean world. Zheng He's fleets visited the ports of Southeast Asia, including Malacca, which was becoming a major entrepôt for regional trade. They sailed across the Indian Ocean to Calicut and other ports on India's Malabar Coast. They entered the Persian Gulf, visiting Hormuz. They reached the Arabian Peninsula. Most remarkably, they traveled down the East African coast, visiting ports that today fall in Kenya, Tanzania, and Somalia — places where Chinese porcelain and coins had already arrived through earlier commercial networks.

The expeditions ended after 1433, following Zheng He's death on the final voyage. The precise reasons for their discontinuation have been debated by historians for generations. The most important factors appear to have been the enormous cost of the expeditions at a time of fiscal strain; the strong opposition of Confucian officials who believed maritime commerce was inappropriate for the imperial state and that resources should be directed toward northern frontier defense; and a broader philosophical preference for stable agrarian order over commercial expansion. Records of the expeditions were reportedly suppressed by hostile officials. The great ships were left to decay. No subsequent Ming ruler revived the voyages.

The contrast between China's maritime capability and the European voyages that followed has fascinated historians. Portuguese ships began exploring the West African coast in the 1420s and 1430s — almost exactly when China was ending its Indian Ocean expeditions. By the 1490s, Portuguese ships had reached India and would soon establish trading posts at Goa and Malacca. European powers would use their maritime capabilities to build commercial empires. China, with far larger ships and greater organizational capacity, chose not to follow this path. This choice reflected not incapacity but different priorities — a state committed to agrarian stability, Confucian ideology, and northern frontier defense rather than maritime commerce and overseas expansion.

Section 35.4

Economy, Population, and Society in Ming China

The economic history of Ming China is in many ways a story of transformation despite official ideology. The Ming state's founders wanted a stable agrarian society of self-sufficient farmers paying taxes in grain. What they got, by the sixteenth century, was one of the most commercially active economies in the world, driven by millions of peasant producers who grew crops for distant markets, artisans who manufactured silk and porcelain for sale across Asia and Europe, and merchants who accumulated wealth that rivaled that of great officials.

Agricultural commercialization was the foundation. The Yangtze River delta became the engine of the Chinese economy. Here, farmers who had once grown rice for subsistence increasingly switched to silk production: mulberry trees for silkworms, silk thread for export. The region's prosperity was tied to distant markets in Central Asia, Persia, the Ottoman Empire, and eventually Europe. Cotton cultivation spread through northern China. Tobacco, introduced from the Americas via the Philippines in the sixteenth century, became a commercial crop almost immediately after its arrival. Tea cultivation expanded in southern provinces to meet growing demand both within China and in the export trade.

Urbanization accompanied commercialization. Ming China had some of the world's largest cities. Nanjing had a population of perhaps a million people. Beijing, after it became the capital in the early fifteenth century, was an enormous administrative city. Beyond the capitals, dozens of commercial cities grew throughout the Yangtze River basin and southeast coast. Suzhou became famous for silk; Jingdezhen for porcelain; Huizhou for merchants and bankers. These cities were not merely administrative centers but genuine commercial hubs where traders, artisans, entertainers, and scholars mingled.

The merchant class, officially ranked at the bottom of the Confucian social hierarchy (below scholars, farmers, and artisans), accumulated extraordinary wealth during the Ming period. Wealthy merchants invested in land, educated their sons for the examinations, patronized art and literature, and sometimes blurred the boundary between merchant and scholar. Regional merchant guilds — the Huizhou merchants from Anhui, the Shanxi merchants — operated across China and into Central Asia, developing sophisticated credit and partnership arrangements. The gap between official ideology, which denigrated commerce, and social reality, in which merchants were central to the economy, was one of the great tensions of Ming society.

Popular culture flourished in the cities of Ming China. The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries saw the composition of several of China's greatest novels, including Journey to the West (Xi You Ji), a fantastical tale of a Buddhist monk's pilgrimage, and The Plum in the Golden Vase (Jin Ping Mei), a realistic portrait of upper-class merchant society. Drama and opera were enormously popular entertainments. Urban readers consumed collections of short stories, encyclopedias of useful knowledge, and printed versions of the Confucian classics — all made possible by advances in woodblock printing and papermaking. This popular literary culture reached a broad audience that included not only educated officials but merchants, artisans, and literate women.

Women's lives in Ming China were shaped by Confucian norms that assigned them to the domestic sphere, emphasized obedience to fathers, husbands, and sons, and imposed the practice of foot binding on women of elite and middle-class families. Foot binding — the binding of young girls' feet to restrict their growth — spread from the aristocracy to merchant and artisan families during the Song and Yuan periods and became near-universal in some regions during the Ming. It was painful and disabling, limiting women's physical mobility, and is understood as a form of control over women's bodies and movement. Yet within their domestic sphere, elite women were often highly educated, wrote poetry, painted, and managed complex households. Women of merchant families often played active roles in family businesses. The experiences of women varied enormously across class lines.

Historical Vocabulary
Neo-Confucianism

Neo-Confucianism was a philosophical tradition that emerged in China during the Song dynasty (960–1279) and became the dominant intellectual framework for governance, ethics, and education across East Asia through the early modern period. Its major thinkers — especially Zhu Xi (1130–1200) — synthesized classical Confucian ethics with cosmological ideas, producing a comprehensive philosophical system that addressed not only political governance and social ethics but also the nature of reality itself. Neo-Confucianism emphasized self-cultivation, the investigation of things to understand moral principle, the hierarchical organization of human relationships (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend), and the integration of personal virtue with political governance.

It was adopted as the official philosophy of the Chinese state under the Ming dynasty, institutionalized in the Korean Choson dynasty as a foundational ideology, and deeply influential in Tokugawa Japan. It shaped not only political thought but also family organization, gender roles, educational practices, and social hierarchies across East Asia. Understanding Neo-Confucianism is essential for understanding early modern East Asian history because it provided the common intellectual language through which rulers, officials, scholars, and ordinary people understood their social world.

Section 35.5

Qing Conquest and Manchu Rule

In the 1630s and 1640s, the Ming dynasty collapsed under the pressure of internal rebellion and external military challenge. The causes of the Ming's fall were multiple: fiscal exhaustion from decades of expensive military campaigns; a series of devastating famines caused partly by the "Little Ice Age," a period of global cooling that reduced agricultural yields; the spread of epidemic disease; and the rise of peasant rebel movements, most importantly the forces led by Li Zicheng, who captured Beijing in 1644. At the same moment, a Manchu state north of the Great Wall had been building military power for decades under Nurhaci (1559–1626) and his successor Hong Taiji. When Li Zicheng's forces seized Beijing and the last Ming emperor hanged himself, a Ming general named Wu Sangui made the fateful decision to invite the Manchu forces through the Great Wall to help defeat the rebels. The Manchu forces took Beijing, declared the establishment of the Qing dynasty, and never left.

The Manchus were a people of the forests and plains of northeastern Asia, related to earlier Jurchen peoples who had ruled northern China during the Jin dynasty (1115–1234). They had their own written language, clan organization, pastoral and agricultural economy, and military traditions. Nurhaci organized the Manchu people into eight military-administrative units called the Eight Banners, which provided a framework for conquest and continued to define Manchu identity throughout the Qing period. By the time Hong Taiji proclaimed the Qing dynasty in 1636, the Manchu state had incorporated many Mongol and Chinese soldiers and officials, creating a multiethnic conquest machine of formidable effectiveness.

The conquest of China proper took several decades and was not peaceful. Many Chinese cities and regions resisted. The most famous resistance was at Yangzhou in 1645, where a Qing army reportedly massacred the population after the city refused to surrender. The coastal province of Fujian and Taiwan, controlled by the loyalist Zheng Chenggong (Koxinga), held out until 1683. The Qing campaign to enforce the hair queue — requiring Chinese men to shave the front of their heads and wear a Manchu-style braid, on pain of death — provoked intense resistance and became a symbol of submission to alien rule.

Yet the Qing also governed through accommodation. They preserved the Chinese examination system, the Confucian bureaucracy, and the institutions of Chinese governance virtually intact. Chinese officials who submitted to the Qing were retained in office. The Kangxi (r. 1661–1722), Yongzheng (r. 1722–1735), and Qianlong (r. 1735–1796) emperors all studied classical Chinese, patronized Chinese art and scholarship, commissioned vast literary projects, and presented themselves as legitimate Chinese emperors. This dual identity — Confucian emperor to the Chinese, Manchu khan to Manchu and Mongolian peoples — was a deliberate governing strategy historians have called "cultural chameleonism."

The Qing empire at its height in the eighteenth century was the largest in Chinese history, encompassing not only China proper but Manchuria, Mongolia, Tibet, and the vast territories of Xinjiang in Central Asia. Managing this immense multiethnic empire required sophisticated administrative strategies. The Lifanyuan, or Court of Colonial Affairs, managed relations with Mongol, Tibetan, and Central Asian peoples. The Qing emperors patronized Tibetan Buddhism, building elaborate Buddhist temples and supporting the Dalai Lama's institution, as a way of cultivating legitimacy among Mongolian and Tibetan subjects. Military governors administered frontier territories with considerable autonomy. The Qing were architects of a new kind of multiethnic empire, not simply Chinese rulers with a different family name.

The Qing period saw dramatic population growth. From perhaps 150 million at the beginning of the dynasty, China's population grew to perhaps 300 or 400 million by the late eighteenth century. This growth was sustained by agricultural expansion into previously unused land, by the continued spread of American food crops — especially sweet potatoes and maize in hilly and marginal areas — and by a long period of internal peace under strong Qing governance. Population growth brought prosperity and also pressure. By the early nineteenth century, the strain on agricultural land, combined with fiscal pressures on the Qing state, would begin to produce the conditions for serious crisis.

Section 35.6

Choson Korea and Confucian Society

Korea under the Choson dynasty (1392–1897) presents one of history's most remarkable examples of a state built on explicit ideological foundations. The dynasty's founders, led by the general Yi Seonggye, overthrew the Goryeo dynasty and proclaimed a new state governed according to Neo-Confucian principles more thoroughly than any previous Korean regime. This commitment to Confucianism shaped Korean politics, society, education, family life, gender relations, and culture for five centuries.

The Choson state was modeled closely on Chinese institutions. An examination system selected officials, though in practice it was dominated by a hereditary aristocracy known as the yangban. A bureaucracy organized into six ministries governed through the capital and provinces. A Confucian academy (the Seonggyungwan) trained officials in the classics. Courts, legal codes, and administrative procedures all drew on Chinese models. The ritual calendar observed Confucian ceremonies at court and in families throughout the country. The king performed annual ceremonies following patterns long established in China.

Yet the Choson state was distinctively Korean. The yangban aristocracy — a dual aristocracy of civil and military officials and their families — occupied a position more powerful and more hereditary than anything in the Chinese system. Unlike China, where the examination system theoretically opened official careers to all, in Choson Korea the examinations were effectively restricted to yangban families. Birth mattered enormously. Status was inherited. The distinction between yangban and commoners, and between commoners and the degraded class (including slaves, butchers, entertainers, and others in hereditary low-status occupations) was sharp and legally enforced. Slaves — both public and private — formed a significant part of the Choson population, perhaps as much as a third in some periods.

One of the Choson dynasty's greatest cultural achievements was the invention of hangul, the Korean alphabet, under King Sejong (r. 1418–1450). Before hangul, Korean writers had to use Chinese characters — a laborious system that required years of intensive study and effectively limited literacy to the educated elite. Hangul is a phonological alphabet of remarkable elegance, designed to represent the sounds of the Korean language systematically. Each consonant's shape represents the position of the speech organs when making that sound. King Sejong and scholars at the Hall of Worthies (Jiphyeonjeon) developed it and promulgated it in 1446.

Hangul was initially resisted by conservative Confucian scholars who argued that abandoning Chinese writing would sever Korea from the classical tradition. For centuries, formal official and literary writing continued in classical Chinese. But hangul spread rapidly among those excluded from classical education — including women, commoners, and people in trades — and became the medium of a vibrant vernacular literature. Women in the royal court wrote memoirs and letters in hangul. Popular stories and novels circulated in the script. By the nineteenth century, hangul was the most widely used script in Korean society.

The Imjin War (1592–1598), in which Japanese forces under Toyotomi Hideyoshi twice invaded Korea, was one of the most devastating events in Korean history. The first Japanese invasion in 1592 swept northward with shocking speed, reaching as far as Pyongyang before a combination of Chinese Ming reinforcement, Korean guerrilla resistance, and brilliant naval victories by Admiral Yi Sunsin halted the advance. Yi Sunsin's geobukseon — armored "turtle ships" covered with iron spikes to prevent boarding — devastated Japanese naval supply lines and remain among the most celebrated technological innovations in Korean military history. A second Japanese invasion in 1597 caused further destruction before Hideyoshi's death in 1598 led to a Japanese withdrawal. The wars left Korea's cities burned, its population reduced, its cultural treasures looted, and its agricultural system in ruins. Recovery took generations.

Korean society was also profoundly shaped by the Confucian organization of the family. The ideal Confucian family was patrilineal, patriarchal, and governed by strict hierarchies of age and gender. Ancestor veneration was central to family life, practiced through elaborate ceremonies at ancestral graves and shrines. Clan organizations that united descendants of a common ancestor maintained genealogical records, managed communal property, and regulated marriage, since marriage within the clan was prohibited. These clan organizations became some of the most important institutions in Korean social life, maintaining the social order across centuries.

Section 35.7

Tokugawa Japan and Political Order

Japan's Tokugawa period (1603–1868) began as the solution to a problem: how to end a century of devastating civil war and prevent its recurrence. The solution that emerged — the bakuhan system — was one of the most ingenious political orders of the early modern world. For more than two and a half centuries, it maintained internal peace in a country of perhaps 25–30 million people organized into hundreds of competing feudal domains.

The century before 1603 is known as the Sengoku period — the "age of warring states." Powerful regional lords (daimyo) fought each other constantly for territory, while the imperial court in Kyoto maintained ceremonial functions without real power, and the Ashikaga shogunate gradually became irrelevant. In the second half of the sixteenth century, a series of extraordinary warlords — Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and finally Tokugawa Ieyasu — successively unified Japan under military authority. When Ieyasu won the decisive battle of Sekigahara in 1600 and was appointed shogun by the emperor in 1603, he established the Tokugawa shogunate at his headquarters in Edo (present-day Tokyo).

The bakuhan system divided political authority between the shogunate and the approximately 270 domain governments. The shogun, as the emperor's military delegate, held national authority — controlling foreign relations, regulating the daimyo, managing currency, and maintaining roads and communication. The daimyo governed their own domains with considerable autonomy, collecting taxes, administering justice, and maintaining their own armies. The emperor in Kyoto remained a sacred, ceremonial figure with spiritual authority but no political power. This arrangement preserved multiple centers of authority while ensuring that no single daimyo could challenge Tokugawa supremacy.

The Tokugawa shogunate devised several mechanisms for controlling the daimyo. The most important was the system of alternate attendance (sankin-kōtai), which required every daimyo to spend every other year in Edo at the shogun's court. When the daimyo returned to his domain, he had to leave his family in Edo as effective hostages. This system served multiple purposes. It kept the daimyo under the shogun's eye for half their lives. It forced them to maintain expensive establishments in both their home domains and Edo, draining their resources and making it difficult to fund military rebellion. It transformed Edo into an enormous city — by the eighteenth century, one of the largest cities in the world — as tens of thousands of samurai retainers, servants, merchants, and craftspeople settled there to serve the daimyo establishments.

The Tokugawa social order was theoretically organized according to Neo-Confucian principles into four status groups: samurai, farmers, artisans, and merchants. This hierarchy ranked samurai at the top as the governing class, celebrated farmers as the foundation of the agrarian economy, and placed merchants — whose activity was seen as unproductive — near the bottom. In practice, the Tokugawa social hierarchy was more complex and contradictory than this scheme suggests. Merchants accumulated wealth far exceeding that of many samurai. Farmers were idealized in official ideology but taxed heavily and subject to strict controls on movement and occupation.

The samurai class underwent a remarkable transformation during the Tokugawa period. In the Sengoku era, samurai were above all warriors. During Tokugawa peace, their martial function became largely ceremonial. Yet they remained a governing class, serving as administrators, judges, tax collectors, and bureaucrats for the shogunate and the daimyo. Many samurai spent more time with a writing brush than a sword. Samurai education emphasized both military arts and literary accomplishment — the ideal of civil and military virtues combined (bunbu ryōdō). Samurai stipends — rice allowances from their lords — were fixed, which meant that as the commercial economy grew and prices changed, many samurai found themselves in economic difficulty, borrowing from the merchants they officially outranked.

Historical Vocabulary
Sakoku

Sakoku (literally "closed country") is the term traditionally applied to the Tokugawa shogunate's policy of restricting foreign contact during most of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries. Under edicts issued between 1633 and 1639, most foreign trade was restricted to two ports: Nagasaki (where Dutch and Chinese merchants were permitted) and Tsushima (for Korean trade). Japanese subjects were prohibited from traveling abroad; those already overseas were prohibited from returning on pain of death. Portuguese missionaries and traders were completely expelled.

The policy was designed to prevent foreign religious influence (particularly Catholicism, which the shogunate associated with European political subversion), to control the flow of silver, and to maintain the stability of the social order. Historians now debate the term "sakoku," noting that Japan was never completely closed — trade continued through regulated channels — and that the policy evolved over time. Nevertheless, the Tokugawa restrictions on foreign contact were real and significant, shaping Japan's engagement with the wider world until the arrival of American naval pressure in 1853.

Section 35.8

Urban Culture and Commercial Growth

One of the great paradoxes of the Tokugawa period is that a political order designed to maintain a stable agrarian social hierarchy presided over two centuries of remarkable commercial growth and urban cultural transformation. The Tokugawa peace was also a commercial revolution, a population expansion, and a cultural flowering of the first order.

Japan's population grew significantly during the early Tokugawa period, from perhaps 12 million at the beginning of the seventeenth century to perhaps 30 million by the early eighteenth. Agricultural productivity increased through improved irrigation, new seed varieties, and the spread of commercial fertilizers. New lands were brought under cultivation. Cash crops — cotton, tobacco, indigo — spread through agricultural regions, linking peasant producers to urban markets. Regional specialization increased, with some areas focusing on sake brewing, others on silk production, others on cotton weaving.

Cities were the centers of Tokugawa commercial and cultural life. Edo (Tokyo) grew from a small castle town at the beginning of the seventeenth century to a city of perhaps a million people by the early eighteenth — among the largest cities in the world. Osaka, a commercial center, became known as Japan's "kitchen" for its role in distributing rice and goods from around the country. Kyoto, the imperial capital, remained a center of aristocratic culture, high-quality craft production (especially silk textiles), and Buddhist institutions. Dozens of secondary castle towns — the capitals of daimyo domains — functioned as regional economic centers, stimulated by the sankin-kōtai processions and the establishments of daimyo households.

The commercial culture that flourished in Tokugawa cities is known by the term chōnin bunka — townspeople's culture. The chōnin (townspeople — merchants and artisans) were officially near the bottom of the social hierarchy, but they accumulated wealth, leisure, and cultural sophistication. They patronized theaters, visited pleasure quarters, collected art, and supported a vibrant publishing industry. The popular culture they created stands among the great artistic achievements of any early modern civilization.

Kabuki theater developed in the early seventeenth century from rowdy street performances into a sophisticated theatrical art combining music, dance, elaborate costumes, and dramatic narrative. Initially performed by troupes of women, kabuki was restricted to male performers by shogunal decree in 1629; thereafter, male actors specializing in female roles (onnagata) became celebrated artists. Kabuki performed stories of samurai loyalty and honor, tales of merchant life and love affairs, and adaptations of earlier aristocratic dramas. It was theater for everyone — samurai and merchant, country visitor and urban sophisticate alike.

Woodblock printing (ukiyo-e — pictures of the "floating world") emerged as one of the Tokugawa period's most distinctive art forms. Artists carved designs into wood blocks, inked them, and pressed them onto paper to produce images that could be reproduced in editions of hundreds or thousands. The subjects ranged widely: scenes of the pleasure quarters and theater districts, portraits of famous actors and sumo wrestlers, landscapes of famous sites. The great ukiyo-e masters — Hishikawa Moronobu, Kitagawa Utamaro, Katsushika Hokusai, Utagawa Hiroshige — produced images of extraordinary beauty and cultural richness. Ukiyo-e prints were affordable enough for ordinary townspeople to purchase, making high-quality visual art accessible to a broad public for the first time.

Literature flourished in multiple genres. The haiku poet Matsuo Bashō (1644–1694) transformed a minor comic poetic form into one of literature's most refined vehicles for evoking nature, transience, and the intersection of the mundane and the profound. His account of a journey through northern Japan, The Narrow Road to the Deep North (Oku no Hosomichi), combined poetry and prose into a masterpiece of world literature. The novelist Ihara Saikaku (1642–1693) wrote realistic, often satirical accounts of merchant and samurai life, exploring the pursuit of money and pleasure with ironic wit. These literary forms reached wide audiences through the sophisticated publishing industry centered in Osaka, Kyoto, and Edo.

Women in Tokugawa Japan occupied a position shaped by Neo-Confucian gender ideology and the specific conditions of a highly commercialized society. Elite samurai women were expected to embody Confucian wifely virtues — obedience, chastity, domestic devotion. Yet samurai women also received education, managed households that could be extremely complex, and in some cases participated in the cultural life of their domains as poets, calligraphers, and teachers. Merchant women often worked alongside their husbands and sons in family businesses. Women in the entertainment districts — geisha, courtesans of various ranks — occupied a complicated social position, skilled in music, dance, and conversation, celebrated in art and literature, but socially marginalized by the very culture that celebrated them.

Section 35.9

East Asia and Global Trade

East Asia's participation in early modern global trade was far more extensive and significant than is sometimes recognized in world-history accounts that privilege European perspectives. China in particular was a central node in a global commercial network that stretched from the Americas to Africa. The key commodity connecting these networks was silver.

Spanish colonial enterprises in the Americas extracted silver from the mines of Potosí and elsewhere in such quantities that silver flowed across the globe. Much of this silver flowed east — across the Pacific via the Manila Galleon trade, or around Africa and through the Indian Ocean — to reach China. There, it was absorbed as currency. The Ming dynasty had attempted paper money as currency but had abandoned it after inflationary disasters. By the sixteenth century, silver was the primary medium of exchange for China's commercialized economy. Chinese demand for silver drove the global silver trade, which meant that events in China's commercial markets had consequences in Bolivia, in Spain, in the Ottoman Empire, and in Southeast Asia.

The Manila Galleon trade was one of the most important trade routes of the early modern world. After Spain established Manila as its Pacific headquarters in 1571, an annual galleon began sailing from Acapulco in Mexico to Manila and back. The galleon carried American silver to Manila; there, Chinese merchants — operating from junks that crossed the South China Sea — exchanged silk, porcelain, and other goods for the silver. The silver returned to China; the silk and porcelain traveled to Mexico and then to Spain and the rest of Europe. This route connected Chinese production directly to American silver and European demand, making it one of the first truly global trade circuits.

Chinese merchants — particularly from the Fujian coast — were the primary operators of maritime trade networks in Southeast Asia. Overseas Chinese communities established in the ports of the Philippines, Java, Malacca, and Vietnam were commercial intermediaries who connected Chinese production to regional and global markets. These communities were often large and economically powerful. The Spanish colonial government in Manila massacred Chinese merchants multiple times, killing tens of thousands, yet Chinese commercial communities rebuilt repeatedly, such was the economic magnetism of the trade. Chinese commercial diaspora networks were among the most resilient and far-reaching commercial organizations in the early modern world.

Japan was also deeply integrated into regional trade networks, even during the period often described as Japanese "isolation." The Tokugawa government restricted direct European contact to the Dutch trading post on the artificial island of Dejima in Nagasaki harbor and prohibited Japanese from traveling abroad. But Chinese merchants were permitted to trade at Nagasaki as well, and Japanese silver — Japan had major silver mines in this period — flowed through these channels into the broader regional economy. Trade between Japan and Korea through the domain of Tsushima continued throughout the period. Japanese copper became an important commodity in Asian trade networks, used in coinage across Southeast Asia.

Korea's external trade was more restricted. The Choson dynasty was wary of maritime trade partly because of its experiences with Japanese pirates who had raided the Korean coast for centuries, and partly because of Confucian ideological reservations about commerce. Legitimate trade with China occurred through the tribute missions that Korea sent periodically to the Ming and later Qing courts — formal diplomatic exchanges that also served as commercial opportunities. Trade with Japan occurred at designated ports on the southern coast under carefully regulated conditions. But Korean merchants did not develop overseas commercial networks comparable to those of Chinese or Japanese traders.

The global trade networks of the early modern period had significant consequences for East Asian societies. In China, silver inflows fueled commercial expansion but also created economic vulnerabilities. When the Spanish reduced silver production in the early seventeenth century, the resulting reduction in silver supply contributed to deflationary pressure in China and exacerbated the fiscal and economic difficulties of the late Ming. The global reach of trade meant that external disruptions could have internal consequences in ways that no earlier Chinese government had faced. East Asia's deep integration into global commerce was both a source of dynamism and a source of new risks.

Section 35.10

Chapter Review and East Asian Order Checkpoint

The early modern period in East Asia was defined by the interplay of powerful states, sophisticated cultures, dense commercial networks, and careful management of external relations. China's Ming and Qing dynasties created some of the most capable imperial systems in world history, governing populations that dwarfed any other state in the world. Korea's Choson dynasty maintained one of history's most durable and elaborately Confucian states. Tokugawa Japan achieved extraordinary internal peace and presided over a remarkable commercial and cultural flourishing.

These societies were not isolated from the world. They participated in the global silver trade, maintained elaborate maritime commercial networks, exchanged cultural influences across regional boundaries, and managed — with varying degrees of success — their relationships with an increasingly commercially aggressive European presence. Their choices about foreign trade, foreign contact, and international relations reflected sophisticated assessments of risk, opportunity, and the social order they were committed to maintaining.

Yet by the early nineteenth century, the strains on these systems were becoming visible. China's population growth had outrun its agricultural base. The Qing fiscal system was under pressure. Japan's samurai class, economically weakened by the commercialization that had enriched merchants, was restless and ideologically uncertain. Korea was experiencing internal social tensions as commoner families purchased yangban status and the elaborate social hierarchy began to strain. These internal difficulties would coincide with intensifying European and American pressure in the nineteenth century, producing the crises studied in later chapters.

Historical Vocabulary
Tributary System

The tributary system was the framework through which the Chinese imperial court organized its relations with neighboring states and peoples. In the tributary relationship, a foreign ruler or state acknowledged the Chinese emperor's symbolic supremacy — sending periodic tribute missions bearing gifts to the imperial court, performing ritual submission, and using Chinese reign-years in official documents. In return, the Chinese emperor recognized the foreign ruler's legitimacy, bestowed imperial gifts (typically of greater value than the tribute received), and granted access to Chinese markets through trade.

The system was not a form of direct political control — tributary states like Korea and Vietnam maintained full internal sovereignty — but rather a ritual ordering of international relations around Chinese cultural prestige and imperial authority. It reflected Chinese assumptions about civilization and hierarchy while providing practical diplomatic and commercial benefits to all parties. The tributary system shaped diplomacy across East and Southeast Asia for centuries and was only definitively dismantled by European imperial pressure in the nineteenth century.

Regional Lens

Choson Korea — A Confucian Kingdom in Its Own Terms

Korea's Choson dynasty is sometimes discussed primarily as a Chinese satellite — a state that adopted Chinese institutions and remained politically subordinate to the Ming and Qing. This framing misses the genuine originality and sophistication of Korean civilization during these five centuries.

The Choson state was not simply a copy of Chinese institutions. Its yangban aristocracy was more powerful and more hereditary than anything in the Chinese system. Korean law, while drawing on Chinese models, developed its own traditions of judicial procedure and legal commentary. Korean Buddhist institutions, though influenced by Chinese Buddhism, developed distinctive schools of thought and practice. Korean ceramics — especially the renowned celadon ware of the Goryeo period and the more austere white porcelain of the Choson — created aesthetic traditions of their own that were imitated and prized across East Asia.

Korean intellectual life during the Choson dynasty reached remarkable heights. The Hall of Worthies (Jiphyeonjeon), founded by King Sejong, brought together some of the finest scholars in the country to work on encyclopedic projects, historiography, and the development of hangul. Korean astronomers developed sophisticated instruments for tracking celestial phenomena. Choson scholars produced histories, literary collections, encyclopedias, and commentaries on the Confucian classics that stand as achievements of the highest intellectual order.

Korea's geographical position between China and Japan exposed it to military pressure from both directions. The Imjin Wars of the 1590s were catastrophic. But the Korean response also revealed the strength of its state and society. Popular resistance movements, guerrilla warfare, and the remarkable naval campaigns of Yi Sunsin prevented complete conquest. Many Korean Confucian scholars refused to accept Qing legitimacy after the Manchu conquest of China, regarding the Manchus as barbarians who had destroyed the genuine Confucian civilization of the Ming. This Korean self-perception — as guardians of true Confucianism after China itself had fallen to alien conquerors — was a remarkable ideological development with long-lasting cultural consequences for Korean identity.

Global Connection

Silver, China, and the Early Modern World Economy

No commodity better illustrates the global integration of the early modern world than silver, and no society was more central to the global silver trade than Ming and Qing China.

China's need for silver arose from a long struggle with monetary problems. The Song and Yuan dynasties had experimented with paper currency, but paper currency requires state credibility and disciplined monetary management to maintain its value — conditions that the Yuan dynasty, with its massive military expenditures, could not consistently provide. By the mid-fifteenth century, the Chinese economy had largely converted to silver as the primary medium for large transactions, tax payments, and commercial exchange. China's economy was enormous — perhaps the largest in the world — and its hunger for silver was correspondingly vast.

The solution came from an unexpected direction. Spanish conquistadors had discovered that the mountains of the Americas contained silver deposits on a previously unknown scale. The mines of Potosí in present-day Bolivia, opened in 1545, produced silver in quantities that transformed the world economy. This silver flowed outward through commerce to wherever demand was greatest — and the greatest demand in the world was in China. Through Manila — established as the Spanish Pacific headquarters in 1571 — silver crossed the Pacific and entered the South China Sea. Estimates suggest that perhaps 30–50 percent of all American silver production eventually found its way to China. The result was that China was deeply embedded in a global economic system stretching from Potosí to Beijing.

For China, the silver inflow fueled commercial growth but also created vulnerabilities. When silver imports declined in the early seventeenth century, the Chinese economy experienced deflationary shock that contributed to the fiscal crises of the late Ming. The silver trade thus reveals how deeply East Asian economic life was connected to global forces, even as East Asian governments tried to regulate and control external contact.

Empire Study

The Qing Dynasty

Origins and Expansion

The Qing dynasty originated in the forests and plains of Manchuria, where Nurhaci unified the Manchu clans and built a military state organized around the Eight Banners. His son Hong Taiji expanded the state, incorporated Mongol and Chinese soldiers, and proclaimed the Qing dynasty in 1636. After taking Beijing in 1644, Qing campaigns extended control over southern China, Taiwan (1683), Outer Mongolia (1696), Tibet (1720), and Xinjiang (1759), creating an empire of some thirteen million square kilometers — significantly larger than any previous Chinese imperial state.

Administration and Governance

The Qing governed through a layered administrative system. China proper was governed through the traditional Chinese bureaucracy — the Six Ministries, provincial governors, and prefectural and county magistrates selected through the examination system. At the top, both Chinese and Manchu officials served, though Manchus held preferential access to senior positions. The Lifanyuan (Court of Colonial Affairs) managed relations with Mongol princes, Tibetan religious authorities, and Central Asian peoples. Mongol nobility were organized into leagues and banners preserving their traditional leadership structures while incorporating them into Qing hierarchy.

Cultural Strategies

The Qing emperors were masters of what historians call "cultural chameleonism" — presenting different faces to different subject populations. To Chinese subjects, they were Confucian emperors who sponsored classical scholarship, maintained ritual propriety, and commissioned vast cultural projects including the Siku Quanshu encyclopedia. To Mongol and Tibetan subjects, they presented themselves as Buddhist monarchs, building Buddhist temples and hosting Tibetan lamas at their summer retreat at Chengde. To Manchu subjects, they maintained Manchu military traditions and the Eight Banner identity. This dual — or multiple — legitimacy was a deliberate and largely successful governing strategy.

Population, Economy, and Legacy

The Qing period saw China's population grow from perhaps 150 million to perhaps 400 million by the late eighteenth century. Agricultural expansion, new crops, internal peace, and effective governance all contributed. The Chinese economy was deeply commercial, with sophisticated credit markets, merchant guilds, and long-distance trade networks. Trade with Europe grew significantly in the eighteenth century as European demand for Chinese tea, silk, and porcelain increased. At its height, the Qing dynasty represented one of the most capable multiethnic empires in world history — though by the early nineteenth century, stresses were accumulating that would produce severe crisis in the following decades.

Primary Source Window

Zheng He's Inscription at Liujiagang (1431)

Creator: Zheng He and imperial officials · Date: 1431 · Type: Stone inscription / official memorial / public commemorative record

Context: Before his final voyage in 1431, Zheng He caused to be engraved a series of stone inscriptions at two Chinese ports, including one at Liujiagang in Fujian province, summarizing the history of his voyages. This inscription is one of the most important primary sources for the history of the maritime expeditions.

The inscription describes the voyages as conducted under imperial mandate to go to foreign countries in the Western Ocean and there to confer presents on them so as to transform them by displaying imperial power and virtue. It emphasizes that the emperor's purpose was to make all the countries beyond the horizon come to audience bearing precious objects and presents. The inscription records distances traveled, countries visited, and tribute received, and includes a prayer for divine protection of the fleet, invoking the protection of the celestial consort Tianfei, a sailor's protective deity.

Analysis: The inscription reveals the official understanding of the voyages: they were diplomatic missions conducted to extend the tributary system, not commercial enterprises aimed at profit or territorial conquest. The language of "transforming" foreign countries through the "display of power and virtue" is explicitly Confucian — the emperor's moral authority is supposed to attract willing submission rather than requiring force. The religious invocation of Tianfei also reveals that ordinary sailors and officials understood the voyages within a framework of popular religious belief alongside official Confucian ideology. The inscription was clearly intended for a Chinese audience — to commemorate achievement and justify the voyages' expense — rather than as a communication to foreign peoples.

Limits of the source: The inscription represents the official imperial perspective and ideology. It tells us nothing about how the peoples the Chinese fleet visited experienced these expeditions — whether they welcomed the tribute missions, resented the pressure to participate in the tributary system, or were largely indifferent. It also represents only the senior official perspective; the experiences of ordinary sailors, who endured months at sea in dangerous conditions, are entirely absent.

  2. How does this source describe the relationship between China and the foreign countries it visited? Is this a relationship of equals?
  3. What can historians learn from this inscription that they could not learn from official dynasty histories alone?
  4. What questions about the voyages can this inscription not answer? What other types of sources would historians need to understand the full history of the expeditions?
Archaeology and Material Evidence Window

Chinese Porcelain Along the Indian Ocean and East African Coast

One of the most compelling pieces of material evidence for the reach of early modern Chinese trade and diplomacy is the distribution of Chinese porcelain fragments across the Indian Ocean world, including along the East African coast. Archaeological excavations at Swahili city-states such as Kilwa, Mombasa, and sites associated with Great Zimbabwe (in the interior of southern Africa) have recovered Chinese ceramics — primarily blue-and-white porcelain — dating to the Ming period and earlier.

What the evidence shows: The presence of Chinese porcelain at these sites demonstrates that commercial networks connecting China to East Africa were operating before Zheng He's voyages (which began in 1405) and continued thereafter. Chinese goods arrived at East African ports through chains of trade involving Chinese, Arab, Indian, and Swahili merchants, not necessarily through direct Chinese voyages. The distribution of porcelain fragments allows archaeologists to trace the extent of these networks and to date layers of occupation at coastal sites.

What scholars can infer: The ceramic evidence suggests that Chinese porcelain was a prestige good in East African coastal societies, used for display and exchange rather than everyday utensils. The concentration of Chinese ceramics at elite residential and ceremonial sites suggests that access to imported goods was associated with social status. This, in turn, suggests that the Indian Ocean trade networks that Zheng He's fleets entered were already mature and well-organized commercial systems linking multiple regions — not isolated communities waiting to be "discovered" by Chinese or later European voyagers.

Limits of the evidence: Archaeological ceramics alone cannot tell us the specific routes by which goods traveled, who the merchants were, what they received in exchange, or how the recipients understood the objects they acquired. Written sources from both the Chinese and East African sides are necessary to build a complete picture, and those sources have their own limitations and perspectives.

Historical Debate

Why Did China Not Pursue Maritime Expansion After Zheng He?

The question: Zheng He's voyages demonstrated that Ming China possessed the technological capacity, organizational ability, and financial resources to project maritime power on a global scale — capabilities that exceeded anything Europe could deploy in the early fifteenth century. Yet China did not develop colonial trading empires or pursue systematic overseas expansion, while European powers did. Why?

Interpretation A — The Confucian Bureaucracy Explanation: One influential interpretation emphasizes the role of Confucian ideology and bureaucratic politics. According to this view, the maritime voyages were ended because powerful Confucian officials at the Ming court regarded them as wasteful, commercially degrading, and contrary to the proper agrarian orientation of the state. Officials who controlled the bureaucratic machinery suppressed records of the voyages and prevented their renewal. China turned inward not because it lacked capacity but because its dominant ideology privileged agricultural stability over maritime commerce. The comparison with Europe, where merchants and monarchs cooperated in profit-seeking ventures, highlights a fundamental difference in political economy and social values.
Interpretation B — The Strategic Rationality Explanation: Another interpretation argues that China's choice was strategically rational given its specific circumstances. The primary military and security threat facing the Ming dynasty was not maritime but continental — the Mongol and later Jurchen peoples of the northern steppe. Defending the northern frontier required enormous resources — the rebuilding of the Great Wall, the garrisoning of frontier towns, the maintenance of cavalry forces. The maritime voyages competed with these essential priorities. Europe's maritime expansion was driven partly by the fact that European powers lacked comparable continental security demands and had strong incentives to find alternative routes to Asian trade.

Evidence: The Confucian-ideology interpretation is supported by contemporary documents showing official opposition on grounds of cost and impropriety, and by the documented suppression of voyage records. The strategic-rationality interpretation is supported by the massive resources devoted to northern frontier defense throughout the Ming period and by the genuine security threats posed by steppe peoples.

Why it matters: This debate touches on fundamental questions about the relationship between ideology, institutions, strategic interests, and historical outcomes. It also raises broader questions about why European maritime expansion had the consequences it did — whether it arose from European cultural distinctiveness or from specific conjunctural circumstances that could have produced different outcomes in different societies.

Student task: Using the evidence presented in this chapter and this debate, develop your own argument. Was the end of Zheng He's voyages primarily the result of Confucian ideology, strategic rationality, or some combination of both? What additional evidence would you need to test your argument more thoroughly?

Comparison Across Societies

Managing Foreign Contact — China, Korea, and Japan

All three major early modern East Asian states developed policies for managing foreign contact that shared certain features while differing in important ways. None of these states was entirely "closed" — all continued to trade, exchange cultural influences, and participate in regional diplomatic networks. The differences lie in the mechanisms of control, the scale of permitted contact, and the specific concerns that animated restriction.

China — The Tributary System China managed foreign contact primarily through the tributary system. Foreign rulers and merchants who wished to trade with China were required to participate in tributary missions. In return, they received imperial recognition and the right to trade at designated ports. European traders, beginning with the Portuguese in the sixteenth century, were incorporated into this system, initially at Macao (from 1557). The Qing later formalized the Canton system: all European trade was restricted to Guangzhou, where licensed Chinese merchants acted as intermediaries.
Korea — Tributary Priority and Border Trade Korea managed foreign relations primarily through the tributary relationship with China and carefully regulated border trade with Japan. Korean tribute missions to the Ming and Qing courts served as the primary framework for legitimate diplomatic and commercial contact. Trade with Japan was permitted at designated ports on the southern coast, managed through the domain of Tsushima. The memory of the Imjin Wars gave Korean authorities particular reason for wariness about unregulated Japanese contact.
Japan — Sakoku and Regulated Ports Japan under the Tokugawa shogunate developed the most explicit and elaborate restrictions on foreign contact. The edicts of the 1630s prohibited most foreign trade and banned Japanese from traveling abroad. Dutch traders were confined to the island of Dejima in Nagasaki harbor; Chinese traders were permitted at Nagasaki but confined to a designated compound. Korean trade occurred through Tsushima at the port of Pusan. The shogunate's particular concern about Catholicism and the subversive potential of uncontrolled overseas contact drove the severity of these restrictions.

What the comparison reveals: All three systems reflect a preference for controlling rather than eliminating foreign contact. The differences — China's ideologically elaborate tributary framing, Korea's prioritization of the Chinese relationship, Japan's most explicit formal restrictions — reflect each society's distinct history, strategic situation, and specific anxieties about the social consequences of unregulated international contact.

Multiple Perspectives

The Qing Conquest of China (1644 and After)

The Qing conquest of China and the following decades of Qing consolidation were experienced in radically different ways by different groups.

Han Chinese scholars and officials who refused submission: For many Chinese scholars and officials who had served the Ming dynasty, the Qing conquest was a catastrophe — the destruction of the legitimate dynasty by foreign conquerors. Ming loyalists who refused to submit fled to southern China, Taiwan, or Vietnam; some committed suicide rather than live under Manchu rule. The demand that Chinese men adopt the Manchu hairstyle was experienced as a profound humiliation and was resisted violently in many areas. Scholars like Huang Zongxi and Gu Yanwu refused Qing service and produced works of political philosophy searching for the intellectual causes of Ming failure.
Han Chinese officials and scholars who submitted: Many other Chinese officials and scholars did submit to the Qing, reasoning that stability and the preservation of Chinese culture within a new framework was preferable to endless resistance. These officials reasoned that the preservation of Confucian institutions, the examination system, and Chinese literary culture was worth accepting Qing political authority — particularly as the Qing emperors proved themselves willing and able patrons of Chinese scholarship.
Manchu Banner families: For the Manchu people who had built the Eight Banner military system and fought the campaigns of conquest, the establishment of the Qing dynasty in Beijing was a triumphant culmination of decades of effort. Banner families received lands, stipends, and privileged status in the new imperial order. Yet over generations, many Manchu families became heavily sinicized — learning Chinese, practicing Chinese customs, and losing fluency in Manchu — a development that later Qing emperors tried, with mixed success, to prevent.
Peasants and commoners: For the vast majority of China's population, the Qing conquest was primarily important for the peace or disruption it brought to their immediate communities. Areas that resisted conquest experienced terrible violence; areas that submitted quickly often experienced a relatively rapid transition. Peasants under the Qing continued to pay taxes, cultivate their fields, participate in local markets, and manage family and community life much as before. The identity of the dynasty mattered less to most commoners than the quality of local governance and the burden of taxation.
Cause and Effect

The Tokugawa Peace and Japanese Social Transformation

Long-Term Causes

A century of civil war (the Sengoku period) left Japan deeply militarized, politically fragmented, and socially unstable. The warrior class was defined by military function. Political authority was contested among hundreds of competing lords. Foreign missionary activity had introduced Christianity, spreading rapidly and perceived by some rulers as a political threat.

Short-Term Causes

Tokugawa Ieyasu's decisive victory at the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600 gave him military superiority over rivals. His appointment as shogun in 1603 provided formal political legitimacy. His rapid establishment of institutional controls — the bakuhan system, alternate attendance, regulations on castle construction and daimyo marriage alliances — created a framework for lasting control before rivals could organize resistance.

Immediate Effects

Daimyo brought under firm Tokugawa control. The alternate attendance system (sankin-kōtai) created permanent circulation of samurai between domains and Edo, stimulating roads, post towns, and commercial networks. Prohibition on Christianity, enforced by the brutal suppression of the Shimabara Rebellion (1637), removed potential foreign-connected political organization.

Long-Term Consequences

The samurai class gradually transformed from warriors into administrators. Agricultural production increased; cities expanded dramatically; vibrant commercial culture emerged. Yet two centuries of peace also produced structural tensions: economically weakened samurai, wealthy merchants who were politically marginalized, and a literate population beginning to question the political order. These tensions contributed to Tokugawa instability in the nineteenth century.

Historical complexity: The Tokugawa peace rested not only on institutional mechanisms but on an ideology of Neo-Confucian social order that gave the hierarchy legitimacy. It also depended on the absence of serious external military pressure — which the sakoku policy helped maintain. The peace created its own contradictions: a warrior class without war, a commercial economy constrained by social hierarchy, and a literate urban population increasingly at odds with official ideology.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Confucianism in East Asia, 1400–1800

What Changed

  • New philosophical currents emerged within Neo-Confucianism — including the "School of the Mind" (xinxue) of Wang Yangming (1472–1529) in China, which challenged Zhu Xi's emphasis on external study
  • In Japan, Confucian scholars developed distinctively Japanese interpretations, including the Kokugaku (National Learning) movement
  • The application of Confucian gender ideology intensified across all three societies, increasingly restricting elite women to the domestic sphere
  • Confucian literacy spread from educated elites to broader urban populations through printing and popular culture

What Stayed the Same

  • The five core relationships (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend) remained the organizing framework of social ethics
  • The examination system in China and Korea continued to test mastery of Confucian texts as the primary path to official service
  • Ancestor veneration remained a central family practice across all three societies
  • The ideal of the scholar-gentleman who cultivated virtue through learning remained the dominant model of educated male identity

Why it matters: The durability of Confucian institutions across five centuries and three major dynastic transitions demonstrates that ideology, when institutionalized in examination systems, family law, and ritual practice, can persist across major political changes. It also raises questions about the relationship between Confucian social hierarchies and the commercial dynamism that simultaneously transformed East Asian societies — was Confucianism compatible with commercial society, or were its formal hierarchies ultimately in tension with the realities of merchant wealth and urban culture?

Everyday Life and Social History

An Ordinary Family in Ming China's Commercial Economy

Consider a typical family in a Jiangnan village in the Yangtze River delta during the sixteenth century. The family head, Li Weiming, owns a few acres of farmland, but the land alone is not enough to support the family entirely. His wife manages a small silk-production operation: she tends mulberry trees in the family's garden, supervises the silkworms through their life cycle, and reels raw silk thread sold to a local weaver. Their eldest son has left the village to work in the county town as an apprentice in a cloth shop, sending home a portion of his wages. Their daughter-in-law manages the household and looks after the family's young grandchildren. Li Weiming himself works part of the year as a hired laborer on a neighbor's larger landholding during harvest season.

This family is deeply embedded in a commercial economy that would have been unfamiliar to their ancestors two hundred years earlier. They pay their taxes not in grain but in silver — which means they must sell their silk thread and any surplus rice to obtain money. They purchase rice, cloth, oil, and salt in local markets. Their son's career in commerce represents a path to modest prosperity that the old Confucian hierarchy of farmer-above-merchant would have discouraged, but that the realities of a commercialized economy make attractive.

The family's life is shaped by rice agriculture and by the seasons. Spring means planting; summer means weeding and tending; autumn means harvest and tax payments. Winter is the season for silkworm cultivation, craft work, and relative rest. The family worships local gods at the village temple, venerates the ancestors at the family altar, and participates in the ritual calendar of the Chinese year — New Year's celebrations, the Qingming festival for sweeping ancestors' graves, the midsummer festival.

Education is a distant dream. Li Weiming himself can read and write at a basic level — enough to understand simple contracts and read almanacs. His son has learned more through his work in the cloth shop, where literacy is essential for keeping accounts. But the years of study required to pass even the lowest level of the examinations are financially impossible for a family like theirs. This vignette captures something essential about Ming China: the coexistence of a vast commercial economy with a fundamentally agrarian social order, and the ways that ordinary people lived between Confucian ideals and commercial realities.

Chapter Summary

The early modern period in East Asia — spanning roughly 1368 to the mid-nineteenth century — represents one of the most consequential chapters in world history, and one that too easily disappears from view when the story of the world's globalization is told primarily from a European perspective. China, Korea, and Japan together encompassed populations, economic output, cultural production, and political sophistication that dwarfed anything comparable in the early modern world. Understanding this period is essential not only for understanding East Asia but for understanding the world.

The Ming dynasty (1368–1644) was born as a deliberate restoration project after the disruption of Mongol Yuan rule. Its founder, the Hongwu Emperor, rebuilt the examination system, re-established Confucian bureaucracy, and attempted to create a stable agrarian order. His successors built the Forbidden City, moved the capital to Beijing, and commissioned Zheng He's extraordinary maritime voyages — the largest naval expeditions in world history at that time. When those voyages ended after 1433, it was not because China lacked the capacity for maritime expansion but because its governing ideology and strategic priorities pointed in different directions. The contrast with European maritime expansion — beginning at almost exactly the same moment — is one of the great turning points in early modern world history.

Despite official Confucian ideology's reservations about commerce, the Ming economy transformed dramatically. The Yangtze River delta became one of the world's most commercially dynamic regions. Silver from the Americas flooded into China through the Manila Galleon trade and other routes, fueling commercial expansion but also creating economic vulnerabilities. Population grew from perhaps 70 million at the dynasty's founding to perhaps 200 million by the late sixteenth century. Cities grew, popular culture flourished, novels and dramas circulated widely, and merchants accumulated wealth that contradicted their official low status.

The Qing dynasty (1644–1912) was founded by Manchu conquerors from northeast Asia who had built a powerful military state organized around the Eight Banner system. After a contested conquest that provoked significant resistance, the Qing established themselves as legitimate Chinese emperors while simultaneously presenting themselves as Manchu khans to steppe peoples and Buddhist monarchs to Tibetan and Mongolian subjects. This sophisticated governance strategy allowed the Qing to build the largest empire in Chinese history. The high Qing period under the Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong emperors was one of stability, cultural achievement, and territorial expansion.

Korea's Choson dynasty (1392–1897) represents one of history's most remarkable experiments in ideologically grounded governance. Built explicitly on Neo-Confucian principles, it created a durable state in which a hereditary yangban aristocracy dominated the examination system and bureaucracy. King Sejong's invention of hangul in 1446 created a Korean alphabet of great elegance, enabling literacy beyond those trained in classical Chinese. Korea navigated devastating Japanese invasions in the 1590s and Manchu invasions in the 1620s and 1630s before settling into two centuries of relative stability and remarkable cultural productivity.

Tokugawa Japan (1603–1868) solved the problem of a century of civil war by creating the bakuhan system — distributing authority between the shogunate and the daimyo, maintaining peace through institutional controls including the alternate attendance requirement, and managing foreign contact through strict regulations at Nagasaki. Within this stable order, commercial agriculture spread, cities grew enormously, and a vibrant popular culture of kabuki theater, ukiyo-e woodblock prints, haiku poetry, and popular fiction flourished. The Tokugawa period stands as one of the greatest achievements in deliberate peace-building in world history.

All three societies participated deeply in the global trade networks of the early modern period. Chinese merchants dominated maritime trade in Southeast Asia. Japanese silver flowed through Nagasaki into regional markets. The Manila Galleon trade connected Chinese production to American silver and European demand. East Asian economies were not peripheral to early modern globalization — they were central to it, shaping global commodity flows, commercial circuits, and the distribution of wealth in ways that European contemporaries both depended on and found difficult to manage.

The social histories of these societies were characterized by the coexistence of Confucian ideology with commercial reality. Official hierarchies ranked scholars above merchants, yet merchants accumulated wealth and cultural influence that challenged their official low status. Confucian gender ideology assigned women to the domestic sphere, yet women participated in family economies, literary culture, and community life in ways that complicated simple narratives of subordination. These tensions did not destroy the social order but generated the dynamism and contradiction that gave early modern East Asian societies their distinctive character.

By the early nineteenth century, the conditions for crisis were accumulating. China's population had grown to perhaps 400 million, straining agricultural resources. Qing fiscal systems were under pressure. Corruption had spread through the bureaucracy. Japan's samurai class was economically weakened while merchants had grown wealthy. Korea's social hierarchies were fraying as commoner families purchased aristocratic status. These internal difficulties would intersect with intensifying European and American commercial and military pressure to produce the dramatic transformations of the nineteenth century — the subject of later chapters in this textbook.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 35 has examined early modern East Asia — the Ming and Qing dynasties in China, the Choson dynasty in Korea, and the Tokugawa shogunate in Japan — as a world unto itself and as part of the larger world of early modern global history. The central argument of this chapter is that East Asia in this period was not a peripheral or passive region but a dynamic center of civilizational achievement, commercial activity, and political sophistication. Understanding early modern world history requires taking East Asia seriously on its own terms and in its global connections.

The chapter's examination of Zheng He's voyages and their discontinuation raises profound questions about the relationship between capability and choice in history. China possessed the means to project maritime power on a global scale — and chose not to use them for colonial expansion. This choice reflected not weakness but different priorities: a governing ideology that valued agrarian stability over commercial adventure, a strategic environment dominated by continental threats rather than maritime opportunities, and a political economy in which officials could mobilize enough institutional power to end a project they considered wasteful and inappropriate. Comparing this choice with the European decisions that produced colonial empires illuminates the importance of ideology, institutions, and strategic context in shaping historical outcomes. The lesson is not that China "failed" — it is that different societies make different choices based on their particular circumstances and values, and that the consequences of those choices ripple across generations and across the world.

The Qing dynasty's achievement in building and governing a multiethnic empire across thirteen million square kilometers deserves sustained reflection. The Qing were not simply Chinese rulers with a different surname — they were architects of a new kind of imperial state that combined Chinese bureaucratic institutions with Manchu military organization and sophisticated strategies for cultivating legitimacy among diverse populations. The parallels with other early modern multiethnic empires — the Ottomans, the Habsburgs, the Mughals — are instructive. All these empires faced the challenge of governing diverse peoples across vast distances; all developed strategies for managing that diversity; all eventually faced the pressures that would reshape or destroy them in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Understanding the Qing is essential for understanding both the possibilities and the limits of multiethnic imperial governance.

Korea's Choson dynasty and Japan's Tokugawa shogunate remind us that the history of civilization is not limited to great empires or moments of military expansion. Choson Korea's achievement in building one of the world's most durable ideological states, in producing remarkable scholarly and literary culture, and in maintaining sovereignty under external pressure represents a distinctive contribution to the range of possibilities that human societies have explored. Tokugawa Japan's extraordinary experiment in deliberate peace-building — more than two centuries of internal stability following a century of civil war — and the vibrant popular culture that flourished within it demonstrate that political choices can transform social possibilities in ways that neither the designers nor the inhabitants of a political order can fully anticipate.

Finally, this chapter's attention to the commercial dynamism of early modern East Asia — from the Yangtze River delta's silk industry to the Osaka rice market to the East Asian silver trade — challenges older assumptions about Asia as economically stagnant before European contact. China may have been the world's largest economy for most of this period. East Asian participation in global silver networks, American food crop adoption, and regional maritime commerce demonstrates that the early modern world was shaped by Asian agency as much as by European expansion. Recognizing this changes how we understand the world before the industrial age — and helps explain why the encounter between East Asia and industrial European power in the nineteenth century would be so dramatic, so contested, and so world-historical in its consequences. Those consequences, and the responses they provoked, are the subject of the chapters that follow.

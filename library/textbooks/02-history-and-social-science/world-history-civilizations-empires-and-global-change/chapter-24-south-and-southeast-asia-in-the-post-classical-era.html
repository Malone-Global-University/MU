South and Southeast Asia in the Post-Classical Era

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The waters of South and Southeast Asia were not a quiet backwater. They were among the most contested, commercially vital, and culturally generative spaces on earth."

In the year 1025, the ruler of the Chola Empire of southern India launched one of the most audacious naval campaigns in the history of the ancient world. His fleet crossed the Bay of Bengal, sailed through the straits that divide the Malay Peninsula from Sumatra, and struck at the ports and cities of Srivijaya — the great maritime empire that had for centuries dominated the trade routes connecting the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea. The Chola raids did not permanently destroy Srivijaya, but they announced something important: that the waters of South and Southeast Asia were not a quiet backwater. They were among the most contested, commercially vital, and culturally generative spaces on earth. Merchants from Arabia, Persia, India, China, and the islands of the archipelago all sailed these waters. Silks, spices, ceramics, cotton, gold, tin, and incense moved through port cities that were among the largest and most cosmopolitan of the post-classical world. The sea itself was a highway, and control of its key chokepoints meant enormous wealth and political power.

This chapter studies South and Southeast Asia during the post-classical era — roughly the period from the decline of the Gupta Empire in the fifth and sixth centuries CE through the eve of the early modern period around 1400 to 1500 CE. This is a period of more than a thousand years, spanning a vast and enormously diverse geography. South Asia encompasses the Indian subcontinent, from the Himalayas in the north to the island of Sri Lanka in the south, from the deserts of Sindh in the west to the jungles of Bengal in the east. Southeast Asia reaches from the Irrawaddy River valley of Burma through the great plains of the Mekong and Chao Phraya river systems, down the Malay Peninsula, across the Indonesian archipelago, and east toward the islands of the Philippines and the edges of Oceania. These are not one world but many worlds — mountains, river plains, tropical forests, coastal lowlands, small islands, and open ocean — and they generated an extraordinary variety of political systems, religious cultures, economic strategies, and artistic traditions.

Yet for all this diversity, South and Southeast Asia were deeply connected to each other and to the wider world during the post-classical era. The Indian Ocean was the central artery of this connectivity. Monsoon winds, which blow predictably northeast and southwest in alternating seasons, gave sailors a reliable and ancient technology for crossing long distances between Arabia, India, Sri Lanka, the Malay Peninsula, and the islands of Indonesia. Long before the post-classical era, merchants had learned to read these winds, and by the first centuries CE, a complex Indian Ocean trading system already linked dozens of polities across thousands of miles. During the post-classical centuries, this system deepened, expanded, and became more complex. New goods entered the trade. New religious ideas — Buddhism, Hinduism, Islam — traveled along trade routes and transformed cultures. New political forms emerged, including the great Hindu-Buddhist temple kingdoms of mainland Southeast Asia and the maritime trading empires of the island world.

India occupied a central position in this connected world. Despite the decline of the Gupta Empire, South Asia remained economically and culturally productive. Regional kingdoms arose across the subcontinent — the Pallavas, Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas, and Cholas in the south; the Gurjara-Pratiharas, Palas, and later the Rajput confederacies and early Delhi Sultanate in the north. Each of these states patronized art, literature, temple building, scholarship, and trade. Indian merchants sailed from ports on the Coromandel Coast and from Gujarat to every corner of the Indian Ocean world. Indian religious and cultural ideas — the concept of the divine king, the iconography of Hindu deities, the texts and monastic traditions of Buddhism — spread outward along these trade routes and were adopted, adapted, and transformed by societies across Southeast Asia.

Southeast Asia was not simply a passive recipient of Indian culture. The picture historians once painted — of an "Indianized" Southeast Asia merely copying superior Indian models — has been substantially revised. Historians now understand that Southeast Asian rulers actively chose which elements of Indian culture to adopt, adapting them to serve local political needs and social structures. Local traditions of spirit worship, ancestor reverence, agricultural ritual, and political authority shaped how Indian ideas were received and reshaped. The great temples of Angkor in Cambodia, the Buddhist monuments of Pagan in Burma, and the Hindu kingdoms of Java were simultaneously Indian in inspiration and distinctly Southeast Asian in execution. Local elites used Indian religious concepts to legitimize power, but the societies they governed were organized by local social logics, economic strategies, and environmental conditions.

The arrival of Islam added yet another layer of complexity. Beginning in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, Muslim merchants, missionaries, and Sufi teachers brought Islam to the coastal trading cities of South and Southeast Asia. Islam spread along trade routes, finding receptive audiences among merchants, port communities, and rulers who saw in Islam a cosmopolitan religious identity compatible with long-distance commerce. By 1400, Muslim sultanates had appeared in northern Sumatra, on the coasts of the Malay Peninsula, and in parts of the Indian subcontinent that had not previously known large Muslim populations. The spread of Islam did not erase earlier Buddhist and Hindu traditions; it added to an already complex religious landscape.

This chapter examines these interconnected worlds — regional diversity, post-Gupta India, the temple kingdoms and their cultures, Indian Ocean trade, the Srivijayan maritime empire, the Angkor state, the spread of Islam, and the rich cultures of blending and local adaptation. Evidence comes from archaeology, temple inscriptions, Chinese traveler accounts, Arab merchant narratives, Sanskrit court poetry, and the physical remains of ports, cities, monuments, and trade goods scattered across one of the most dynamic regions of the post-classical world. Understanding South and Southeast Asia in this period is essential for understanding global history — not as a story of isolated regions, but as a story of connections, adaptations, and human creativity across a vast and interconnected world.

Historical Context

The post-classical era in South and Southeast Asia begins in the shadow of an earlier flourishing. The Gupta Empire (c. 320–550 CE), which had presided over a remarkable period of cultural, literary, mathematical, and artistic achievement in northern India, gradually declined under a combination of internal pressures and external shocks. Invasions by the Huna peoples — nomadic groups from Central Asia related to the Huns known in European history — destabilized Gupta power from the late fifth century onward. By the mid-sixth century, the Gupta state had fragmented into regional successor kingdoms. This political fragmentation did not mean cultural collapse. On the contrary, the post-Gupta centuries saw an extraordinary flourishing of regional cultures across the subcontinent, as courts competed to patronize poets, philosophers, mathematicians, astronomers, and temple builders.

In South India, the Gupta era had never really penetrated. The Tamil-speaking south had its own long literary and political traditions, rooted in the Sangam literature of the early centuries CE and organized around competing regional powers. After the Gupta decline, the Pallava dynasty (c. 275–897 CE) and then the Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas, and eventually the great Chola Empire dominated different parts of peninsular India. These dynasties were major patrons of Hindu temple architecture, Sanskrit scholarship, and Tamil literary culture. The Chola Empire in particular, at its height between the ninth and thirteenth centuries, was one of the most powerful states in the Indian Ocean world — its fleets sailed to Sri Lanka, the Maldives, the Malay Peninsula, and beyond.

North India after the Guptas saw a different pattern. The Harsha kingdom (r. 606–647 CE) briefly reunited much of the north, and Harsha himself — documented in vivid detail by the Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Xuanzang — was a notable patron of Buddhism and Sanskrit learning. After Harsha's death, the north again fragmented among competing Rajput clans and regional powers, including the Palas of Bengal, who were major patrons of Buddhism until their decline in the twelfth century. The period from roughly 700 to 1200 CE in North India is characterized by political competition among many regional states, combined with vibrant religious culture in which Shaivite and Vaishnavite Hinduism grew stronger while Buddhism gradually retreated from its earlier Indian heartland.

Southeast Asia during this same period was undergoing its own complex transformations. The earliest complex states in mainland Southeast Asia had developed in the Mekong River delta region (the Funan kingdom, c. first to sixth centuries CE), in the river valleys of Burma, in the plains of central Thailand, and in what is now Vietnam. On the islands, the Srivijayan polity emerged in Sumatra by the seventh century CE, eventually dominating the Strait of Malacca — the crucial chokepoint between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. In Java, powerful kingdoms arose, including the Sailendra dynasty, builders of the great Buddhist monument of Borobudur, and later the Hindu-oriented Mataram and Singhasari kingdoms.

All these developments unfolded against the backdrop of the Indian Ocean trade system. Long before the post-classical period, merchants had crossed the ocean using monsoon winds. Goods that flowed through this system — spices from the Maluku Islands, silk from China, cotton from India, incense from Arabia, gold and tin from the Malay Peninsula — were among the most valuable commodities in the pre-modern world. Controlling key nodes on these routes was a source of enormous wealth and political power, and much of Southeast Asian history in the post-classical era can be understood as the story of competition and cooperation around these nodes.

Regional Orientation

South Asia

South Asia is defined by the Indian subcontinent — a massive triangular landmass projecting southward from mainland Asia. To the north, the Himalayas form one of the world's greatest mountain barriers, defining the subcontinent's continental frontier. The Indus River system drains westward into the Arabian Sea; the Ganges-Brahmaputra system drains eastward and southward into the Bay of Bengal. Between these great river systems lies the vast Indo-Gangetic Plain, one of the most fertile agricultural regions on earth, which supported dense populations and powerful kingdoms throughout the post-classical era.

The Deccan Plateau, a high rocky tableland in central India, separates the northern plains from the coastal lowlands of the south. South India — the Deccan and the Tamil Nadu coastal plain — has a distinctive geographic and cultural identity. It faces the Indian Ocean on three sides and was historically oriented toward maritime trade as much as toward northern India. Sri Lanka, the large island at the southern tip of the subcontinent, was a crucial nodal point in Indian Ocean trade and a major center of Theravada Buddhism.

Southeast Asia

Southeast Asia occupies the region between the Indian subcontinent to the west, China to the north, and the Pacific to the east. It divides naturally into two broad zones: mainland Southeast Asia and island Southeast Asia. Mainland Southeast Asia includes what are today Myanmar (Burma), Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, and Vietnam. Great rivers — the Irrawaddy, Salween, Chao Phraya, Mekong, and Red River — flow southward from the highlands of mainland Asia into fertile deltas and coastal lowlands that supported rice agriculture and dense populations.

Island Southeast Asia encompasses the Malay Peninsula, the Indonesian archipelago (Sumatra, Java, Borneo, Sulawesi, and thousands of smaller islands), and the Philippines. This world is tropical, volcanic, and extraordinarily biologically diverse. It was also the source of some of the world's most commercially prized goods: cloves and nutmeg from the Maluku Islands (the "Spice Islands"), pepper from Sumatra and Java, camphor from Borneo, tin from the Malay Peninsula, and gold from multiple locations. The Strait of Malacca, between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra, was the critical chokepoint through which all ships sailing between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea had to pass. Controlling this strait meant enormous economic and political leverage.

Section 24.1

Regional Diversity in South and Southeast Asia

To understand the post-classical history of South and Southeast Asia, students must first appreciate the extraordinary diversity of these regions — not as an obstacle to understanding, but as a key to it. There is no single "South and Southeast Asian experience." There are dozens, perhaps hundreds, of distinct historical trajectories, shaped by geography, ecology, language, ethnicity, agricultural practice, and political tradition. Yet these many trajectories were connected — by trade, by religion, by political competition and diplomacy, and by the shared environment of the Indian Ocean and its monsoon winds.

South Asia alone contains remarkable diversity. The Himalayas, the world's highest mountain range, form its northern boundary. Below the mountains, the great Ganges-Brahmaputra plain stretches for hundreds of miles — flat, alluvial, and extraordinarily fertile, capable of supporting some of the densest agricultural populations in the ancient world. West of the Ganges plain, the Indus system flows through increasingly arid territory toward the Arabian Sea. Between north and south lies the Deccan Plateau, a high volcanic tableland separated from the coasts by the Western and Eastern Ghats mountain ranges. South India's narrow coastal plains, watered by rainfall from the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea, supported dense populations of Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam speakers with their own distinctive literary, religious, and political traditions.

This geographic diversity produced political diversity. The Ganges plain could support large agrarian states dependent on surplus grain production and standing armies of infantry and elephants. The Deccan's rocky terrain made large-scale agriculture harder but also made certain kingdoms difficult to conquer. The south's coastal position made maritime trade especially important. No single political structure dominated all of South Asia during the post-classical period, and the subcontinent remained politically fragmented throughout most of these centuries — a fact that shaped its cultural productivity as courts competed to patronize artists, architects, scholars, and religious institutions.

Southeast Asia's diversity is, if anything, even greater. The mainland river valleys — the Irrawaddy, the Chao Phraya, the Mekong, the Red River — each supported distinct civilizations with their own languages, agricultural practices, and political traditions. The populations of these valleys spoke languages from different families: Austroasiatic languages (including Khmer and Mon), Tai languages, Tibeto-Burman languages (including Burmese), and Austronesian languages in coastal areas and islands. The island world of Indonesia and the Philippines added further layers: Javanese, Sundanese, Malay, Balinese, Bugis, Tagalog, and hundreds of other languages, each embedded in its own cultural tradition.

What connected all these diverse peoples was the ecological and commercial logic of the monsoon world. Monsoon rains, which fall predictably on most of mainland and island Southeast Asia, made wet rice cultivation possible, and wet rice was the foundation of most post-classical Southeast Asian states. Dense rice-farming populations produced agricultural surpluses, which could support specialists: craftspeople, warriors, administrators, priests, and traders. Where geography channeled trade — along rivers, through mountain passes, across narrow straits — political power concentrated. The rulers who could tax trade, organize labor, and legitimate their authority through religious ritual were the rulers who built lasting states.

Section 24.2

Post-Gupta South Asia

The decline of the Gupta Empire did not produce a dark age in South Asia. On the contrary, the post-Gupta centuries were marked by remarkable regional cultural flowering across the subcontinent, as the removal of a single dominant political center allowed multiple regional courts to compete in literary, artistic, and religious patronage.

In the north, the most significant post-Gupta figure was Harshavardhana — usually called Harsha — who ruled a large kingdom centered on Kanauj from 606 to 647 CE. Harsha began as king of Thaneshwar, inherited the throne of Kanauj after his brother was killed, and gradually expanded his power across much of the Ganges plain and into parts of the Deccan. He was a prolific patron of Buddhism and Sanskrit learning, hosting a grand assembly at Kanauj that attracted scholars from across the subcontinent and beyond. The Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Xuanzang visited India during Harsha's reign and left one of the most detailed accounts of seventh-century Indian society: a record of cities, monasteries, universities, agricultural practices, social customs, and religious communities that historians still rely on today. Xuanzang's account of the great university at Nalanda — then perhaps the largest center of Buddhist learning in the world, housing thousands of monks and students from across Asia — gives a vivid sense of the intellectual vitality of post-Gupta India.

After Harsha's death in 647 CE, northern India again fragmented. The Rajput clans — warrior aristocracies with strong regional bases and elaborate genealogical claims to ruling status — competed for dominance across the northwestern plains and Rajasthan. The Pala dynasty of Bengal (c. 750–1161 CE) was a major patron of Mahayana Buddhism; the Pala kings funded monasteries, supported the famous Vikramasila and Nalanda universities, and maintained connections with Buddhist communities in Tibet, Nepal, and Southeast Asia. When the Palas declined and their patronage networks collapsed, the remaining Buddhist institutions of northern India were left vulnerable. The final blow came with the Ghaznavid and later Ghurid invasions, which destroyed many of the remaining Buddhist monasteries in northeastern India in the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries. By 1200 CE, Buddhism — which had been born in India and had once dominated its religious landscape — was nearly extinct in the land of its origin, though it remained vibrant in Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Tibet, China, Korea, and Japan.

South India presents a different story. The Tamil-speaking south had its own deep literary and political traditions, stretching back to the Sangam poems of the early centuries CE. The Pallava dynasty, based at Kanchipuram near the Coromandel Coast, was a major cultural and political power from roughly the fourth through ninth centuries CE. The Pallavas patronized both Sanskrit and Tamil literature, built some of the earliest free-standing stone temples in South Asia, and maintained close connections with Southeast Asia through maritime trade. The rock-cut temples and monolithic rathas at Mamallapuram, carved in the seventh century under Pallava sponsorship, represent one of the greatest early achievements of South Indian temple architecture.

The Pallavas were eventually eclipsed by the Chola dynasty, which emerged as the dominant power of South India by the ninth century CE and remained powerful until the thirteenth. At its height under Rajaraja I (r. 985–1014 CE) and his son Rajendra I (r. 1014–1044 CE), the Chola Empire was one of the most powerful states in Asia. The Cholas built enormous temple complexes — the Brihadeeswara Temple at Thanjavur remains one of the finest examples of Dravidian temple architecture — patronized Tamil and Sanskrit literature, maintained a powerful standing army, and deployed a substantial naval force. Rajendra I's famous naval expedition of c. 1025 CE struck Srivijayan ports in Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula, demonstrating the reach of South Indian maritime power. Chola control extended at different times to Sri Lanka, the Maldives, and parts of the Malay Peninsula — an extraordinary assertion of Indian Ocean power that shaped the commercial and political landscape of the wider region.

The Chola state was not simply a military machine. It was also a sophisticated administrative system organized around temples. Great Chola temples were not only religious centers; they were economic institutions that controlled land, managed agricultural labor, financed credit, and organized the redistribution of goods. Temple inscriptions — thousands of them survive — record grants of land, records of donations, administrative decisions, and genealogical claims. These inscriptions are among the richest sources for understanding not only the Chola state but also the social life of South Indian villages, the status of different occupational groups, and the role of religion in organizing everyday economic life.

Between the Pallavas and Cholas, other regional powers also shaped South India's post-Gupta centuries. The Chalukyas of Vatapi and later the Chalukyas of Kalyani controlled much of the Deccan at different times, competing with both the Pallavas to the south and the Gurjara-Pratiharas to the north. The Rashtrakutas (c. 753–982 CE) succeeded the Chalukyas as the dominant Deccan power and are remembered especially for patronizing the extraordinary Kailasanatha temple at Ellora — a temple carved entirely from a single massive rock, representing one of the most ambitious architectural projects in Indian history.

Section 24.3

Regional Kingdoms and Temple States

One of the most distinctive features of post-classical South Asian political culture was the centrality of the Hindu temple to political power. Across South India and much of Southeast Asia, temples were not simply places of worship. They were political statements, economic engines, and centers of cultural production. Understanding the temple state is essential to understanding post-classical South and Southeast Asian history.

The logic of the temple state begins with the Hindu (and to some extent Buddhist) conception of kingship. In Sanskrit political theory, the king was not merely a secular ruler. He was a figure of cosmic significance, intimately connected to divine order. The king who built and endowed a great temple was participating in a sacred economy: he was providing housing and resources for the deity, who in return would ensure agricultural fertility, military success, and social order. The temple thus served as a kind of contract between the divine and the human, mediated by the royal patron. The more magnificent the temple, the more powerful the statement about the king's piety, wealth, and divine favor.

Temple building required enormous resources — labor, stone, metal, wood, skilled craftspeople, and a continuous income stream for maintaining priests, musicians, dancers, and other servants of the deity. Great temple complexes became the largest economic institutions in their regions. The Brihadeeswara Temple at Thanjavur, built by Rajaraja I in the early eleventh century, employed hundreds of permanent staff including priests, accountants, administrators, musicians, flower-bearers, and devadasi dancers. The temple controlled extensive landholdings, whose revenue funded all these activities. Merchants donated goods; rulers donated land; wealthy devotees donated jewelry and precious objects. The temple accumulated capital and redistributed it, functioning as a kind of bank and social welfare institution as well as a sacred center.

Temple architecture in South India reached extraordinary heights during the post-Gupta centuries. The Dravidian style, characterized by soaring pyramidal towers (called gopurams at temple gateways and vimanas over the main shrine), elaborate sculptural programs, and extensive enclosed courtyards, evolved through the Pallava, Chalukya, Rashtrakuta, and Chola periods into increasingly complex and monumental forms. Temple sculpture communicated religious stories, royal legitimacy, and social order to worshippers who might be illiterate. Images of deities in their various forms, scenes from the great epics the Mahabharata and Ramayana, and representations of royal donors covered temple walls and pillars.

The Bhakti movement — a form of devotional Hinduism emphasizing direct personal experience of the divine through love and surrender — flourished across South India during the post-classical period. Bhakti saints, including the Tamil poet-saints known as the Alvars (devoted to Vishnu) and Nayanmars (devoted to Shiva), composed passionate hymns in Tamil that remain among the most celebrated works of Indian literature. These saints were often from non-elite backgrounds, and their devotional poetry challenged the hierarchies of caste and ritual expertise. The Bhakti movement contributed to the growth of vernacular literatures in Tamil, Kannada, Telugu, and later Hindi — literatures that eventually challenged Sanskrit's dominance as the prestigious language of culture and power.

Buddhist temple culture was equally important in the north and in Sri Lanka. The Palas' great monastery-universities — Nalanda and Vikramasila — were international institutions. Nalanda at its height housed thousands of monks and students from China, Japan, Korea, Tibet, and Southeast Asia, studying medicine, mathematics, logic, philosophy, and Buddhist doctrine. When Xuanzang left Nalanda in 643 CE after five years of study, he returned to China with a caravan of manuscripts that took years to translate. These manuscripts spread Indian mathematical, philosophical, and literary knowledge to East Asia, contributing to one of the most consequential intellectual transfers in world history.

Section 24.4

The Indian Ocean and Maritime Trade

The Indian Ocean was the central artery of the post-classical world's most extensive and economically important trade network. Understanding this ocean — its geography, its wind systems, its ports and routes — is essential to understanding virtually everything else in this chapter.

The Indian Ocean is the third largest of the world's oceans, stretching from the eastern coast of Africa westward to the Australian continent. Its distinguishing geographic feature, for purposes of human history, is the monsoon wind system. Between approximately November and March, winds blow from the northeast across the northern Indian Ocean, allowing ships to sail from the coasts of Arabia and India toward Southeast Asia. Between approximately April and October, the winds reverse: the southwest monsoon blows from the direction of Africa toward India and then toward Southeast Asia, allowing return voyages. Sailors who understood this system — and sailors in the Indian Ocean had understood it for millennia before the post-classical era — could cross thousands of miles of open ocean in relative safety, timing their voyages to take advantage of favorable winds.

The monsoon system created a seasonal rhythm of maritime trade. Arab, Persian, and Indian merchants would sail east in the winter monsoon season, spend months trading in Indian, Southeast Asian, and Chinese ports, and return west in the summer monsoon. This seasonal pattern shaped the organization of port cities: they needed warehouses for storing goods between seasons, guesthouses for visiting merchants, money changers and creditors, and administrators capable of managing disputes between traders from many different cultural backgrounds. The great ports of the Indian Ocean world — Calicut, Quilon, and Cochin on India's Malabar Coast; Hormuz in the Persian Gulf; Aden at the mouth of the Red Sea; Palembang and Kedah in Southeast Asia — were cosmopolitan places where Arabic, Persian, Tamil, Malay, Sinhalese, and eventually Chinese merchants worked alongside local intermediaries in a complex and generally well-functioning commercial ecosystem.

The trade goods of the Indian Ocean were among the most commercially valuable in the pre-modern world. Spices — cloves, nutmeg, mace, and pepper — were the most famous. Cloves and nutmeg grew only in the Maluku Islands of eastern Indonesia, making them extraordinarily scarce and valuable. Pepper grew more widely in Sumatra, Java, and the Malabar Coast of India. Cotton textiles from India — produced in Gujarat, on the Coromandel Coast, and in Bengal — were another major trade good, exchanged across the ocean for gold, silver, spices, and local commodities. Chinese silk, ceramics, and later porcelain moved southward and westward. East African ivory, gold, and slaves moved northward and eastward. Tin from the Malay Peninsula, camphor from Borneo, and sandalwood from Timor added further variety to the trade.

The financial infrastructure supporting this trade was sophisticated. Arab and Indian merchants developed credit instruments, partnership agreements, and systems of maritime insurance that allowed them to manage the risks of long sea voyages. Merchant diasporas — communities of traders from a single ethnic or religious background living in foreign port cities — provided networks of trust and information essential to long-distance trade. Tamil merchant guilds, such as the famous Ainnurruvar (the Five Hundred), maintained their own legal codes, symbols, and support networks across ports from India to Southeast Asia and beyond. Evidence for these guilds survives in inscriptions found as far afield as Sri Lanka and Sumatra, demonstrating the geographical reach of Indian commercial networks.

Religious communities also traveled along trade routes. Buddhist monasteries in port cities served as waypoints for traveling monks and supported the diffusion of Buddhist ideas and practices. Hindu temples in Southeast Asian trading ports were built by Indian merchant communities and served as centers of social life as well as worship. Later, mosques would serve similar functions for Muslim merchants. The overlap between commercial and religious networks was not accidental: shared religious identity provided a framework of trust, ethical obligation, and mutual support that was essential to long-distance commerce in a world without formal international legal institutions.

Historical Vocabulary
Entrepôt

An entrepôt is a trading port that serves as an intermediate exchange point for goods being transported between different regions. Rather than producing goods itself, an entrepôt collects products from multiple origins and redistributes them to multiple destinations, earning revenue by providing storage, financial services, currency exchange, and a marketplace where buyers and sellers from different regions can meet.

Srivijaya was a classic entrepôt: it did not produce spices itself but collected them from eastern Indonesia, added Indian textiles and Chinese silks, and sold the combined cargo to merchants from Arabia, Persia, India, and China. Entrepôts thrive when geography gives them control over a key chokepoint — a strait, a river mouth, a mountain pass — through which trade must flow. Controlling an entrepôt can generate enormous wealth, but it also depends on maintaining a reputation for fair dealing and reliable governance, since merchants will avoid ports known for extortion or instability.

Section 24.5

Srivijaya and Maritime Power

Srivijaya was one of the most powerful and enduring maritime states in the history of Southeast Asia. Based in southern Sumatra — most likely centered on the modern city of Palembang — Srivijaya dominated the Strait of Malacca and the maritime trade routes of island Southeast Asia from approximately the seventh century through the thirteenth century CE, a span of some six hundred years. Its wealth came from trade, its power came from naval force and diplomatic skill, and its cultural prestige came partly from its role as a major center of Mahayana Buddhist learning.

The earliest reliable evidence for Srivijaya comes from Chinese sources and from a set of inscriptions written in Old Malay, found at several sites in southern Sumatra and dating to around 683–686 CE. These inscriptions describe a ruler consolidating power over rival chiefs and calling for loyalty and submission. They are written in Sanskrit script and Old Malay language — a combination that reflects Srivijaya's hybrid cultural identity: deeply influenced by Indian religious and political concepts, but using a local Austronesian language rather than Sanskrit itself.

The Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Yijing visited Srivijaya in the 670s and 690s CE and left the most detailed early account of its power. He was enormously impressed. Yijing described a wealthy, well-organized state with a significant community of Buddhist monks and scholars. He recommended that Chinese pilgrims traveling to India to study Buddhist texts should stop at Srivijaya to learn Sanskrit first — testimony to the quality of Srivijayan Buddhist scholarship. Over the next several centuries, Srivijaya became one of the major centers of Mahayana Buddhism in the world, attracting pilgrims and scholars from China, India, and other parts of Southeast Asia.

The political structure of Srivijaya was not a centralized bureaucratic state on the Chinese or Chola model. It was better understood as a mandala state — a Sanskrit term describing a political system organized around a powerful center with diminishing authority toward the periphery. The Srivijayan ruler maintained direct control over the Strait of Malacca and key ports, but governed distant subordinate chiefs through a combination of marriage alliances, gift exchange, commercial privileges, and the occasional demonstration of military force. Chiefs who submitted to Srivijayan overlordship were rewarded; those who resisted were attacked. The system was inherently flexible and inherently fragile — dependent on the ruler's ability to keep subordinate chiefs satisfied and rivals at bay.

The economic foundation of Srivijayan power was commercial taxation. All ships passing through the Strait of Malacca had to stop at designated Srivijayan ports, where they paid duties and exchanged goods. Srivijaya provided safe harbor, warehousing facilities, markets, and a reliable commercial environment. In exchange, it extracted a significant share of the value of all the trade passing through its territory. Even a modest percentage of this traffic represented enormous wealth.

The Chola raids of c. 1025 CE represent the most dramatic external challenge to Srivijayan power. Rajendra I's fleet crossed the Bay of Bengal, attacked several Srivijayan ports, and returned with plunder and captives. The raids did not destroy Srivijaya — the polity continued for another two centuries — but they disrupted the commercial networks that were its lifeblood, weakened its authority over subordinate chiefs, and signaled that Srivijayan dominance was not permanent. Over the following century and a half, Srivijaya's power declined as subordinate polities in Java and elsewhere grew stronger. By the thirteenth century, Srivijaya was clearly declining, and by the fourteenth century, it had effectively ceased to exist as a significant political force, giving way to successor states that would eventually produce the Malacca Sultanate.

Section 24.6

Angkor and Mainland Southeast Asia

While Srivijaya dominated the maritime world of island Southeast Asia, the mainland developed its own distinctive political forms. The most impressive and enduring of these was the Khmer Empire, centered on the great city of Angkor in what is now northwestern Cambodia. Angkor was not merely a great city; it was a hydraulic civilization — a state whose power was built on the construction and management of enormous irrigation infrastructure, allowing dense agricultural populations to support a centralized court of spectacular ambition and artistic achievement.

The traditional founding of Angkor is associated with Jayavarman II (r. c. 802–835 CE), who is said to have declared himself a devaraja — a god-king, specifically an incarnation of the Hindu god Shiva — in a great ritual ceremony on the sacred mountain of Phnom Kulen c. 802 CE. The devaraja cult was central to Khmer political ideology. The king was not merely a secular ruler; he was a divine being whose purity and ritual power sustained cosmic order and agricultural fertility. The great temples of Angkor were not simply royal mausoleums or places of worship. They were representations of Mount Meru, the cosmic mountain at the center of the Hindu universe, made real on earth by the power of the king. Building a great temple was simultaneously an assertion of political power and a ritual act maintaining the cosmic order.

The hydraulic system of Angkor was its most extraordinary practical achievement. The Angkor region lies on the northern shore of the Tonle Sap lake, which expands and contracts dramatically with the seasonal flooding of the Mekong River. Khmer engineers designed and built an enormous system of reservoirs (called barays), canals, and irrigation channels that captured floodwaters during the wet season and distributed them to rice fields during the dry season, allowing year-round wet rice cultivation on a scale that could support a very large and dense population. The largest baray, the West Baray, measures approximately 8 kilometers by 2.2 kilometers — a massive artificial lake requiring the organized labor of thousands of workers.

The political height of Angkor came under two great rulers: Suryavarman II (r. c. 1113–1150 CE) and Jayavarman VII (r. 1181–1218 CE). Suryavarman II built Angkor Wat — a temple complex so monumental that it remains the largest religious monument ever constructed. Angkor Wat was originally dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu, oriented westward toward the setting sun, suggesting it may have been conceived as a royal mausoleum as well as a living temple. Its towers rise to 65 meters; its outer wall encloses an area of roughly 200 hectares; its galleries are lined with some of the world's most extensive bas-relief sculptural programs, depicting scenes from the Hindu epics, from Suryavarman's own reign, and from Hindu cosmology.

Jayavarman VII was in many ways an even more transformative ruler. He came to power after a traumatic invasion by the Cham kingdom (from what is now central Vietnam) that briefly occupied Angkor. After his victory, Jayavarman VII launched the greatest building program in Angkor's history — a building program so intensive that some historians estimate that more stone was moved and carved during his reign than in all of ancient Egypt's pyramid-building era combined. He built the new capital of Angkor Thom, centered on the Bayon temple with its extraordinary towers carved with enigmatic smiling faces. Unlike his predecessors, who had been devotees of Hindu Shiva or Vishnu, Jayavarman VII was a devoted Mahayana Buddhist. His building program merged Hindu cosmological concepts with Buddhist imagery, creating a distinctly Khmer synthesis of religious traditions.

After Jayavarman VII's death, the Angkor state began a long decline. The hydraulic system deteriorated as maintenance capacity diminished. Climate studies of tree rings and lake sediments suggest that a series of severe droughts, interspersed with massive floods, struck the Angkor region in the fourteenth and early fifteenth centuries — conditions the overtaxed hydraulic infrastructure may not have been able to manage. Thai (Siamese) kingdoms to the west were growing in power. In 1431, Thai forces sacked Angkor, and the Khmer capital was eventually moved south toward the Phnom Penh region. The golden age of Angkor was over.

Mainland Southeast Asia also contained other significant states. The Pagan kingdom in Burma (c. 849–1297 CE) was a major center of Theravada Buddhist culture. At its height, the Pagan plain was covered with thousands of Buddhist temples, stupas, and monasteries — over 2,000 of which survive in various states of preservation today. The Pagan kings were devout patrons of Theravada Buddhism, supporting the Pali scholarly tradition and the construction of an unparalleled density of Buddhist monuments. The Mongol invasions of the 1270s and 1280s weakened Pagan severely, and the kingdom collapsed in 1297 CE.

In what is now Vietnam, the kingdom of Champa occupied the coastal plains and highlands of central and southern Vietnam for more than a millennium, from approximately the second century CE until the fifteenth. The Cham were an Austronesian-speaking people who built elaborate Hindu temples (notably the My Son complex), participated actively in Indian Ocean trade, and maintained both cooperative and hostile relations with the Chinese-influenced Vietnamese state to the north.

Regional Lens

The Khmer of Angkor: Civilization Beyond the Temples

Angkor was not simply a collection of magnificent temples. It was a living civilization inhabited by hundreds of thousands of people — farmers, craftspeople, soldiers, priests, dancers, merchants, and administrators — whose daily lives were shaped by the rhythms of rice agriculture, religious practice, royal service, and commercial exchange.

For an ordinary Khmer farmer living in the Angkor region in the twelfth century CE, life was organized primarily by the agricultural calendar. Wet rice cultivation in the flooded paddies required careful timing: planting after the rains began, management of water levels through canals and sluices connected to the baray system, harvest before the floods returned. Farmers paid taxes in rice and provided labor service for state projects — maintaining the hydraulic infrastructure, contributing to temple construction, or serving in the royal army. In exchange, they received the security of a well-watered agricultural system, access to market exchange, and the religious benefits of living in the shadow of great temples maintained by a king of cosmic power.

Women in Khmer society held significant economic roles. Chinese visitor Zhou Daguan, who visited Angkor in 1296 CE, noted that Khmer women dominated local commerce: they ran the markets, managed household finances, and conducted trade. Temple inscriptions record women as property owners, donors, and occasionally as temple officials. The famous bas-reliefs of Angkor Wat depict both men and women in court and military scenes, and apsara (celestial dancer) figures — symbols of heavenly beauty and divine favor — were carved across temple walls. Khmer women were not equal to men in all respects, but neither were they absent from economic and social life.

The great religious festivals of Angkor were public events that brought ordinary people into contact with the royal and divine sphere. Processions, ritual bathing of sacred images, performances of music and dance, and distribution of food and gifts made the temples sites of popular as well as elite religious experience. The Khmer genius was partly this ability to create institutions — hydraulic systems, temples, festivals — that served simultaneously as instruments of royal power and as resources for ordinary people.

Historical Vocabulary
Hydraulic State

A hydraulic state is a political system whose power and economic foundations rest significantly on the construction, management, and control of water infrastructure — irrigation systems, reservoirs, canals, and drainage works. Angkor is the clearest example from Southeast Asia: the massive barays and canal systems of Angkor made intensive wet rice agriculture possible in the Cambodian interior, allowing the state to support a large population and extract substantial agricultural surplus.

The construction and maintenance of this infrastructure required organized mass labor — thousands of workers coordinated over long periods — which in turn required a state with the authority and administrative capacity to mobilize and direct such labor. Understanding Angkor as a hydraulic state helps explain both its agricultural productivity and the enormous organizational capacity that built Angkor Wat. It also helps explain Angkor's vulnerability: when the hydraulic system could no longer be properly maintained, the agricultural and political foundations of the state were threatened together.

Section 24.7

Islam in South and Southeast Asia

The arrival and spread of Islam was one of the most transformative developments in the post-classical history of South and Southeast Asia. Islam came not through conquest alone — though military conquest was certainly part of its arrival in the subcontinent — but also through the quieter pathways of commerce, migration, and personal religious conversion. Understanding how Islam spread, who carried it, who received it, and what it meant to different communities is essential to understanding the religious landscape of modern South and Southeast Asia.

The Arab conquest of Sindh in 711 CE brought the first Muslim political authority to the Indian subcontinent. Under the Umayyad caliphate, Arab general Muhammad bin Qasim invaded Sindh, defeated the local Hindu ruler Dahir, and incorporated the lower Indus Valley into the Islamic caliphate. The Arab occupation of Sindh was relatively brief as a direct imperial presence, but it established a permanent Muslim community in northwestern India and demonstrated that the subcontinent was accessible to Islamic political power.

The more significant political transformation came with the Ghaznavid invasions from the north. Mahmud of Ghazni (r. 998–1030 CE) conducted seventeen raids into the Indian subcontinent, targeting wealthy temples and cities across the Punjab and as far east as Gujarat. These raids — motivated by plunder as much as religious ideology — caused significant destruction and established Ghaznavid control over much of what is now Pakistan and the Punjab. The Ghaznavid period introduced the Persianate court culture that would define Muslim elite culture in South Asia for centuries: the Persian language as the language of administration and poetry, Iranian-inspired art and architecture, and Sunni Islamic religious orthodoxy combined with Persian literary and courtly traditions.

The decisive political transformation came in the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries. Muhammad of Ghor, ruling from Afghanistan, defeated the Rajput confederacy at the Second Battle of Tarain in 1192 CE and rapidly extended Muslim control across the Ganges plain. After Muhammad's assassination in 1206 CE, his general Qutb ud-Din Aibak established the first independent Muslim sultanate in India, centered on Delhi. The Delhi Sultanate endured — through five successive dynasties — until 1526 CE, when it was replaced by the Mughal Empire. During its existence, the Delhi Sultanate transformed the political and cultural landscape of northern India, establishing Islam as a religion of political power and high culture alongside the older Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain traditions.

Sufi orders established khanqahs (lodges) across the north, and Sufi saints became major figures of popular religious devotion, attracting followers from both Muslim and Hindu communities. Sufi saints combined Islamic mystical practice with local customs, language, and devotional styles in ways that made Islam accessible to people from a wide range of social and cultural backgrounds. Sufi shrines (dargahs) became pilgrimage sites for both Muslims and Hindus, who might venerate the same saint under different names or categories. This religious syncretism characterized much of Islam's spread in South Asia. The resulting religious cultures were distinctly South Asian versions of Islam, shaped by local musical traditions, poetic forms, agricultural rituals, and social structures.

In Southeast Asia, Islam's spread followed a somewhat different trajectory. It came primarily through maritime trade rather than military conquest. Arab and Indian Muslim merchants had been trading in Southeast Asian ports since at least the eighth and ninth centuries CE, and Muslim communities existed in major trading ports long before any Southeast Asian rulers converted to Islam. The conversion of rulers to Islam appears to have accelerated from the late thirteenth century onward. The ruler of Pasai, a port in northern Sumatra, appears to have converted to Islam by around 1285 CE — making Pasai's sultanate one of the earliest Islamic polities in Southeast Asia. Moroccan traveler Ibn Battuta visited Pasai in the 1340s and found a thriving Muslim community with active trade connections to India and Arabia.

Why did Southeast Asian rulers convert? The motivations were multiple. Islam offered a sophisticated cosmopolitan religious identity compatible with long-distance trade — a significant consideration for rulers of port cities dependent on commerce with Muslim merchants from India and Arabia. Conversion to Islam could mark a ruler's participation in a wider Islamic civilizational world, providing prestige and legitimacy. Sufi teachers, who often combined mystical teachings with practical skills in literacy, administration, and trade, were attractive advisers and educators. Once Islam was adopted in one port city, neighboring ports felt competitive pressure to convert as well, to maintain commercial relationships with Muslim trading networks.

The Malacca Sultanate, founded around 1400 CE, became the most important center of Islamization in maritime Southeast Asia. Located at the strategically vital Strait of Malacca, Malacca quickly became one of the most commercially dynamic ports in the world. Its rulers converted to Islam in the early fifteenth century, and Malacca's commercial success made it a powerful model for neighboring polities. From Malacca, Islam spread along the trade routes to the coasts of Java, Borneo, the Sulawesi, and the Philippines. The Malay language — the commercial lingua franca of island Southeast Asia — became the language of Islamic learning and administration in the region, spreading Islam along with its commercial role.

Section 24.8

Cultural Blending and Local Traditions

One of the most important insights of modern scholarship on South and Southeast Asian history is that cultural exchange is never simply a one-way flow. The spread of Indian religious and political ideas to Southeast Asia, the spread of Islamic culture to South and Southeast Asia — these were not simple transfers of superior models to passive recipients. They were complex processes of selection, adaptation, translation, and creative synthesis in which local cultures played an active role in shaping what was received and how it was used.

The older concept of "Indianization" — the idea that Southeast Asian cultures were simply derivative of Indian models, passively absorbing a superior civilization — has been thoroughly revised by scholars since the mid-twentieth century. The revisionist view emphasizes local agency. Southeast Asian rulers chose to adopt Indian religious and political concepts because these concepts were useful: they provided powerful frameworks for legitimizing political authority, organizing large-scale states, attracting skilled Indian administrators and priests, and connecting local elites to a prestigious cosmopolitan civilizational tradition. But they adapted what they chose to adopt to fit local social structures, religious practices, and political needs.

The concept of the devaraja is a good example. This was an Indian concept with roots in Sanskrit political theory and Hindu theology. But the way Jayavarman II deployed it in ninth-century Cambodia was distinctly Khmer. The ritual was performed not by Indian priests but by a local Shiva priest who apparently synthesized Indian Tantric traditions with local Khmer practices. The Indian language and iconography gave the claim prestige and cosmic breadth; the local context gave it social meaning.

Similar processes of creative adaptation can be seen in temple architecture. Borobudur in Java, built by the Sailendra dynasty around 750–850 CE, is simultaneously one of the greatest achievements of Mahayana Buddhist art and a distinctly Javanese monument. Its form — a massive stepped pyramid representing the spiritual journey from the earthly to the divine — has roots in Indian Buddhist stupa architecture, but its execution and the integration of local Javanese artistic traditions are unmistakably regional. The relief sculptures at Borobudur depict Buddhist stories (Jataka tales) alongside scenes of contemporary Javanese life: markets, ships, performers, and agricultural scenes that give historians a remarkably detailed picture of eighth-century Javanese society.

In South Asia itself, cultural blending was equally important. The encounter between Islam and Hindu-Buddhist traditions produced a wide range of syncretic phenomena. The Bhakti saints of medieval India — poets like Kabir, who lived in the fifteenth century and drew freely on both Islamic Sufi and Hindu devotional traditions — represent one form of creative synthesis. Kabir's poems are addressed to a formless divine being, reject the authority of both Muslim ulama and Hindu Brahmin priests, and invoke the experience of direct personal encounter with the divine in language that draws on both traditions. Kabir's poetry was embraced by both Hindus and Muslims and remains beloved across religious boundaries in South Asia today.

The visual arts offer similar evidence of blending. The mosques of the Delhi Sultanate combined Islamic architectural concepts — the pointed arch, the minaret, the courtyard mosque — with Indian construction techniques, decorative vocabulary, and even the incorporation of Hindu and Jain temple columns and carvings. The Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque in Delhi, one of the earliest, was partly built using material from demolished Hindu and Jain temples — an act that symbolized both conquest and cultural recycling.

The complexity of cultural blending in post-classical South and Southeast Asia resists simple summary. What is clear is that the region was not a passive recipient of cultural influences from India, China, the Middle East, or anywhere else. It was an active creative zone, where people at all social levels — rulers and farmers, priests and merchants, artists and sailors — made choices about what to adopt, what to modify, and what to reject, producing cultural forms that were simultaneously connected to wider world traditions and distinctly local in character.

Global Connection

The Sanskrit Cosmopolis

The scholar Sheldon Pollock coined the term "Sanskrit cosmopolis" to describe the cultural sphere that extended from South Asia across Southeast Asia and beyond during the first millennium CE. This was not a political empire — no single state controlled the entire zone. Rather, it was a cultural world connected by the Sanskrit language, by Hindu and Buddhist religious traditions, by Indian literary forms, and by Indian concepts of political authority and aesthetic value.

In the Sanskrit cosmopolis, Sanskrit served as a language of prestige, scholarship, and royal proclamation across an enormous geographic area. Rulers from Gujarat to Java composed inscriptions in Sanskrit, commissioned Sanskrit court poetry (kavya), and patronized Sanskrit scholars and priests. The Ramayana and Mahabharata epics were read, performed, and adapted across the entire zone. Thai, Javanese, Khmer, Balinese, and many other literary traditions drew on Sanskrit models and Sanskrit stories.

What made the Sanskrit cosmopolis work was not political force but the attractiveness of the cultural package. Sanskrit offered rulers access to a prestigious, cosmopolitan identity; to sophisticated concepts of governance, justice, and cosmic order; and to a rich artistic and literary tradition that could legitimate royal power and mark courts as civilized. Local rulers who adopted Sanskrit were simultaneously connecting to a wider cultural world and distinguishing themselves from their neighbors who had not yet done so.

The Sanskrit cosmopolis began to recede after roughly 1000 CE, as vernacular languages gained prestige as literary languages in their own right, and as Islam brought a competing cosmopolitan tradition (with Arabic and Persian) into the region. But the legacy of the Sanskrit cosmopolis endures in the religious cultures, literatures, arts, and political traditions of South and Southeast Asia to the present day.

Section 24.9

South and Southeast Asia in World Networks

Post-classical South and Southeast Asia was not isolated. It was one of the most connected regions of the entire post-classical world, linked to China, the Middle East, East Africa, and ultimately to Europe through the Indian Ocean trade network. Understanding this connectivity is essential to understanding the region's history — and to understanding world history more broadly.

China was one of the most important external forces shaping South and Southeast Asian history during the post-classical era. Chinese demand for spices, incense, tropical wood, and other Southeast Asian luxury goods was a constant engine of commerce. Chinese merchants, initially less prominent in the Indian Ocean trade than Arab, Persian, and Indian merchants, became increasingly active from the Song dynasty (960–1279 CE) onward. Song and later Yuan dynasty China produced enormous quantities of ceramics — blue-and-white porcelain and celadon ware — that were traded across the Indian Ocean world in exchange for spices and other goods. Chinese ceramics are found in archaeological excavations from East Africa to the Philippines, marking the reach of Chinese commercial networks.

Chinese political relationships with Southeast Asian states were organized through the tributary system: Southeast Asian rulers sent periodic diplomatic missions to the Chinese court, presenting themselves as vassals of the Chinese emperor and receiving in return gifts, diplomatic recognition, and commercial privileges. In practice, the tributary relationship was more flexible than it appears: Southeast Asian rulers participated in it because it gave them access to Chinese markets and prestige, not because they genuinely submitted to Chinese authority. The Ming dynasty emperor Yongle's sponsorship of the famous voyages of Zheng He (1405–1433 CE) was partly motivated by a desire to reinforce Chinese commercial and political relationships with Southeast Asian and Indian Ocean polities — an ambitious but ultimately short-lived exercise of Chinese maritime power.

The Islamic world was equally central to Indian Ocean connectivity. Arab and Persian merchants had been trading in Indian Ocean ports since before the rise of Islam, and the spread of Islam transformed these commercial networks. Muslim merchants — now connected by shared religious identity, by the Hajj pilgrimage network, and by Arabic as a common commercial and religious language — became the dominant carriers of Indian Ocean trade. The great Arab geographers and travelers — Al-Masudi, Al-Biruni, Ibn Battuta — left accounts of South and Southeast Asian societies that are among the most important historical sources for the period.

East Africa was another node in the Indian Ocean network. The Swahili city-states of the East African coast — Kilwa, Mombasa, Sofala, and others — traded gold, ivory, and enslaved people for Indian textiles, Chinese porcelain, and Persian metalwork. These goods reached East Africa via the same monsoon routes that connected India to Southeast Asia. East African gold, much of it mined in Zimbabwe, eventually found its way to India, the Middle East, and beyond. The connectivity of the Indian Ocean world was not a simple bilateral relationship between any two regions; it was a multi-centered network in which goods, people, and ideas circulated across thousands of miles through multiple intermediary points.

South Asian merchants — Tamil, Gujarati, Bengali, and others — were among the most active agents in the Indian Ocean trade. Tamil merchant guilds like the Ainnurruvar maintained branches across the ocean and enforced their own commercial codes. Malay sailors and merchants were expert navigators of island Southeast Asian waters and major participants in the spice trade. South Asian mathematical and astronomical knowledge — zero, the decimal system, trigonometry, astronomical tables — spread westward through the Islamic world to Europe, where it transformed science and commerce. South Asian cotton textiles were among the most widely traded commodities in the pre-modern world.

Southeast Asian spices, collected from their island sources by Malay and Javanese merchants, were sold at entrepôts like Srivijaya and later Malacca to Indian merchants, who carried them to Malabar Coast ports, where they were sold again to Arab and Persian merchants, who carried them to the Red Sea and Persian Gulf, from which they reached the Mediterranean and eventually European markets. The price of a clove multiplied many times over in each exchange, making the ultimate source — the tiny volcanic islands of Maluku — extraordinarily remote from the end consumers in medieval Europe but intimately connected to them through a chain of commercial intermediaries stretching halfway around the world.

Section 24.10

Chapter Review and Indian Ocean Map Study

This section synthesizes the chapter's major themes and provides review materials for students. The post-classical history of South and Southeast Asia is a story of diversity, connection, and creative adaptation. The Indian Ocean monsoon system linked these diverse societies to each other and to the wider Afro-Eurasian world. The cultural traditions that shaped post-classical South and Southeast Asia — Hinduism, Buddhism, Islam — were not simply exported from one place and received passively by another. They were actively selected, modified, and synthesized with local traditions by societies that maintained their own identities and creative capacities.

The major political forms of the period illustrate this diversity. In South India, the temple state combined religious legitimacy with economic power in temple complexes that were simultaneously sacred centers and economic institutions. In island Southeast Asia, Srivijaya's maritime empire was built on the control of the Strait of Malacca and the taxation of a vast commercial trade. In mainland Southeast Asia, the Angkor state organized the power of rice agriculture and hydraulic engineering to support one of the most impressive civilizations of the ancient world. In South Asia, the Delhi Sultanate introduced Muslim political culture to northern India, setting the stage for the more complex religious syntheses of the early modern period.

Primary Source Window

The Testimony of Yijing on Srivijaya (c. 671–695 CE)

Creator: Yijing (I-tsing), Chinese Buddhist monk · Date: c. 671–695 CE · Type: Travel account and religious memoir

Context: Yijing was a Chinese Buddhist monk from the Tang dynasty who traveled to India to study Buddhist texts. He sailed from China to Srivijaya in 671 CE and spent several months there before continuing to India. He returned via Srivijaya in the 690s. His accounts — particularly his Record of the Buddhist Religion as Practised in India and the Malay Archipelago — are among the most valuable early sources for the history of Srivijaya and Indian Ocean Buddhism.

Yijing described Srivijaya as a populous and prosperous state with a large community of Buddhist monks whose scholarship was of high quality. He recommended that Chinese pilgrims traveling to India stop first at Srivijaya to study Sanskrit grammar — a significant endorsement. He noted that more than a thousand Buddhist monks were present during his visits, studying the same texts and following the same rules as were practiced in India itself. He described the state's wealth, its commercial activity, and the presence of merchants from many different lands.

Analysis: Yijing's account reveals several important things about seventh-century Srivijaya. First, it was a significant center of Buddhist learning — not simply a commercial power but a place where the intellectual and spiritual traditions of Buddhism were seriously practiced and studied. Second, it was cosmopolitan: Yijing encountered monks and merchants from multiple regions. Third, his recommendation that pilgrims study at Srivijaya before proceeding to India suggests that Srivijaya was considered not merely a waypoint but a destination in its own right for Buddhist scholarship.

Limits: Yijing's account is shaped by his own priorities — he was interested primarily in Buddhist practice and scholarship, and paid less attention to political structure, economic organization, or the lives of non-Buddhist communities. He gives us a view from the educated, religious elite perspective; the perspectives of ordinary merchants, farmers, sailors, or non-Buddhist inhabitants of Srivijaya are absent.

Archaeology and Material Evidence Window

LiDAR and the True Scale of Greater Angkor (2012–2015)

The evidence: Between 2012 and 2015, the Cambodian Archaeological Lidar Initiative (CALI) conducted aerial LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) surveys over the Angkor region of northwestern Cambodia. LiDAR works by firing pulses of laser light from an aircraft and measuring the time it takes for the light to return after bouncing off surfaces below. When applied to jungle-covered terrain, LiDAR can detect features hidden beneath the forest canopy — because the laser pulses penetrate between leaves and branches to detect the ground surface — that are invisible from the ground or from conventional aerial photography.

What the surveys revealed: The surveys dramatically expanded understanding of Angkor's scale. They revealed an extensive urban-agricultural landscape covering more than a thousand square kilometers: suburbs, satellite settlements, a complex network of roads and canals, hundreds of smaller temples and religious structures, and a hydraulic system far more extensive than previously mapped. The surveys confirmed that Angkor was one of the largest pre-industrial cities in the world by area. The surveys also revealed evidence of severe stress on the hydraulic system in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries — breaks in canals and erosion damage — supporting the hypothesis that environmental stress contributed to Angkor's decline.

What scholars can infer: The LiDAR evidence allows historians to revise upward their estimates of Angkor's population and to understand the complexity of its urban organization. The elaborate road and canal network shows a sophisticated state capable of planning and executing massive infrastructure projects.

Limits: LiDAR reveals physical structures but cannot tell historians about the social life, beliefs, economic transactions, or political decisions of the people who lived in the landscape it maps. Ground-level excavation is needed to understand the dating and function of specific features. LiDAR evidence must be interpreted alongside inscriptions, traveler accounts, temple sculpture, and other historical sources.

Historical Debate

Was Southeast Asian Civilization "Indianized"?

The question: For much of the twentieth century, historians described the cultural development of Southeast Asia as "Indianization" — the process by which Indian civilization spread to Southeast Asia. Was this concept accurate? And if not, how should we understand the relationship between Indian cultural traditions and Southeast Asian societies?

Interpretation A — The Indianization Model: Proponents argued that Indian civilization — including Hinduism, Buddhism, Sanskrit, Indian political concepts, and Indian artistic traditions — spread to Southeast Asia through the migration of Indian merchants, priests, and Brahmin scholars, and that this transmission created the distinctive civilizations of ancient Southeast Asia. On this view, places like Angkor and the Javanese kingdoms are essentially derivative of Indian models; their greatness is an expression of Indian cultural genius transplanted to new soil.
Interpretation B — The Local Agency Model: Critics of the Indianization concept argued that this model makes Southeast Asians passive recipients of Indian superiority. They pointed out that Southeast Asian rulers did not simply receive Indian culture; they actively selected which elements to adopt and creatively adapted what they selected to serve local needs and reflect local social conditions. Indian priests and scholars were invited to Southeast Asian courts, not conquering them. On this view, places like Angkor are expressions of Khmer genius that drew on Indian models as one resource among many.

Evidence: Inscriptions from Southeast Asian courts show rulers presenting themselves in Sanskrit terms while also celebrating local ancestral and spiritual traditions. Temple architecture is recognizably Indian in inspiration but distinctly regional in execution. The degree of Sanskrit knowledge in Southeast Asian courts was often limited — Sanskrit phrases appear in inscriptions alongside local language texts, suggesting ritual and prestige use rather than deep cultural penetration.

Why it matters: The debate is not merely academic. The Indianization model implies that Southeast Asian greatness was borrowed rather than indigenous — a view that echoes colonial ideas about Asian cultural inferiority. The local agency model rehabilitates Southeast Asian peoples as creative agents of their own history. A nuanced view acknowledges both the reality of Indian cultural influence and the creative agency of Southeast Asian societies in selecting, adapting, and transforming what they received.

Comparison Across Societies

Srivijaya and the Mali Empire: Two Trading States

Although separated by thousands of miles and distinct historical contexts, Srivijaya (Southeast Asia, c. 7th–13th centuries CE) and the Mali Empire (West Africa, c. 13th–16th centuries CE) offer an instructive comparison as post-classical trading states whose power depended on controlling strategic commercial chokepoints.

Similarities

Both states derived their economic power primarily from taxing trade rather than from agricultural production alone. Srivijaya taxed maritime trade passing through the Strait of Malacca; the Mali Empire taxed trans-Saharan camel caravan trade crossing its territory. Both states controlled access to natural resources of enormous value: Srivijaya to the spices of island Southeast Asia; Mali to the gold fields that supplied much of the Mediterranean world's gold. And both states were connected to the larger world through participation in major religious traditions that gave them cosmopolitan cultural prestige: Srivijaya through Mahayana Buddhism; Mali through Islam.

Differences

Srivijaya was a maritime empire; its power was expressed through naval force and the control of sea routes. Mali was a continental empire whose power was expressed through cavalry armies and the control of overland routes. The geographic environments were radically different: equatorial maritime Southeast Asia versus the Sahelian savanna of West Africa. Srivijaya operated as a mandala state with fluid, negotiated relationships with subordinate chiefs; Mali had a more hierarchical administrative structure at the height of Mansa Musa's reign.

Historical significance: The comparison reveals that controlling strategic commercial chokepoints — whether maritime or continental — was a recurring strategy for building post-classical state power across different world regions, and that states with similar economic strategies could have very different internal organizations, reflecting the different geographic and cultural environments in which they operated.

Multiple Perspectives

The Expansion of Angkor: Four Perspectives

From the perspective of the Khmer royal court: The building projects of kings like Suryavarman II and Jayavarman VII were acts of cosmic piety and political genius. Building Angkor Wat was not merely construction; it was participation in sacred creation, establishing the king as a divine being maintaining cosmic order. Court poets celebrated these achievements in Sanskrit inscriptions; court artists gave them visual form in temple sculpture.
From the perspective of Khmer farmers: For ordinary Khmer farmers, the expansion of the state's hydraulic system was genuinely beneficial in material terms — more reliable water supply, more predictable harvests, reduced vulnerability to drought. But expansion also meant increased labor obligations. Building the enormous barays and temple complexes required organizing large numbers of workers for extended periods. For most farmers, the state was a distant but demanding presence.
From the perspective of neighboring peoples incorporated into the empire: The Cham, Mon, and other peoples who came under Khmer suzerainty experienced Angkor's expansion as conquest and subordination. Khmer inscriptions celebrate military victories over neighboring peoples; these victories meant the imposition of tribute obligations, the disruption of local political arrangements, and the subordination of local rulers to the Khmer king. The Cham briefly sacked Angkor in 1177 CE — a dramatic act of retaliation.
From the perspective of Brahmin and Buddhist priests: The religious specialists who served the great Angkor temples occupied a privileged position in Khmer society. They controlled valuable land endowments, administered complex temple economies, and mediated between the divine and human worlds. They were among the primary beneficiaries of royal patronage. But they were also dependent on royal favor — and the shift from Shaivite Hinduism to Mahayana Buddhism under Jayavarman VII forced adjustments on priests trained in Hindu traditions.
Cause and Effect

The Decline of Buddhism in India

Long-Term Causes

The growth of devotional Hinduism (Bhakti) provided an emotionally powerful alternative to Buddhist practice. The Brahmin priestly class retained influence in Hindu kingdoms that increasingly patronized Hindu rather than Buddhist institutions. The great Buddhist monastery-universities depended on continuous royal patronage that became less reliable as the Pala dynasty weakened.

Immediate Causes

Ghaznavid raids (early 11th century) and Ghurid invasions (late 12th–early 13th century) targeted monasteries and universities for plunder. Monks who survived fled to Tibet, Nepal, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia, taking manuscripts and knowledge with them. Without monks, monasteries could not function; without monasteries, the learned transmission of Buddhist scholarship collapsed.

The Event

By the early thirteenth century, Buddhism had effectively disappeared as a living religious tradition in most of the Indian subcontinent — including the very region where the Buddha was born and where his first communities had formed.

Long-Term Consequences

Buddhism became a truly multi-regional tradition — preserved and further developed in Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Tibet, China, Korea, and Japan — freed from dependence on any single geographic center. The loss of Nalanda and Vikramasila deprived the Buddhist world of important intellectual institutions whose loss took centuries to compensate for.

Historical complexity: The decline of Buddhism in India cannot be attributed to Islamic invasion alone. The structural weakness of institutional Buddhism — its dependence on elite patronage, its marginal relationship to popular village religion, the growing strength of devotional Hinduism — meant that it was already vulnerable before the invasions. The invasions were a decisive blow but not the sole cause.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Religious Life in Post-Classical South and Southeast Asia

What Changed

  • Buddhism nearly disappeared as a living tradition in most of the Indian subcontinent
  • Islam became a major religious tradition in South Asia, eventually a religion of major political powers across northern India
  • Islam spread to coastal Southeast Asia, with Muslim majorities in many port cities by 1500
  • Theravada Buddhism became the dominant tradition across most of mainland Southeast Asia
  • Sanskrit gradually ceded ground to vernacular languages as literary and administrative media

What Stayed the Same

  • Village-level religious life incorporated new traditions into frameworks shaped by much older practices of spirit veneration, ancestor worship, and agricultural ritual
  • The Ramayana and Mahabharata epics remained central to popular culture across South and Southeast Asia even after the spread of Islam
  • The basic structures of agricultural life — wet rice cultivation, village organization, seasonal festivals — remained largely stable across the period
  • Temple and monastery institutions continued to serve as economic and social centers alongside their religious functions

Why this pattern matters: The layered, gradual nature of religious change in post-classical South and Southeast Asia explains the extraordinary religious complexity of the region today — a complexity that is the product of specific historical processes operating at different speeds among different social groups. Understanding this history is essential to understanding contemporary religious diversity in South and Southeast Asia.

Everyday Life and Social History

Merchants and Trade in the Indian Ocean World

For the merchants who sailed the Indian Ocean in the post-classical era, life was a combination of extraordinary opportunity and serious risk. The rewards of successful trade were enormous: a merchant who bought pepper in Sumatra and sold it at a profit in Calicut, bought cotton textiles there and sold them in Arabia, and returned home with gold might earn enough in a single voyage to support a family for years. But the risks were equally real: storms, shipwrecks, piracy, unfavorable markets, dishonest partners, political upheaval in distant ports, and the ever-present dangers of disease all threatened the success of every voyage.

Merchants rarely traveled alone. The most common financial structure was the commenda partnership: a wealthy investor (who stayed home) provided capital; a traveling merchant used the capital to trade and returned home with the profits, splitting them with the investor according to an agreed formula. This arrangement allowed wealth to be invested in trade without requiring the investor to risk the dangers of the voyage.

Trust was the foundation of this system, and trust was typically built through kinship, ethnic identity, and religious community. Tamil merchant guilds like the Ainnurruvar operated as quasi-sovereign institutions with their own codes of conduct, legal procedures for resolving disputes, and networks of mutual support. When a Tamil merchant arrived in a distant port, he could expect to find co-religionists and compatriots who would provide lodging, information about market conditions, introductions to local traders, and assistance in navigating unfamiliar commercial environments. Similar functions were performed by Arab merchant networks for Muslim traders and by Chinese merchant associations for Chinese traders in Southeast Asian ports.

Sailors — the often invisible workforce of the Indian Ocean trade — occupied a very different social position than merchants. Most sailors were relatively poor, hired by ship owners or merchants for specific voyages. The physical conditions of their work were extremely demanding: long hours on deck in tropical heat, irregular food and water, the constant threat of storms, and the cramped quarters of pre-modern ships. Their knowledge of winds, currents, celestial navigation, and local coastal geography was essential to the success of every voyage. Their knowledge was oral and practical, passed from experienced sailors to apprentices, rarely written down, and consequently poorly documented in surviving historical sources.

Environment and Technology

Monsoon Winds, Ship Technology, and the Indian Ocean

The Indian Ocean trade system that connected South and Southeast Asia to the wider world was made possible by a combination of environmental conditions and technological innovations. The monsoon wind system is the environmental foundation. Unlike most of the world's oceans, where prevailing winds tend to blow in one direction and return voyages require sailing against the wind, the Indian Ocean's seasonal wind reversal allows efficient two-way travel. Sailors could depart from Gujarat or the Coromandel Coast in November or December, driven by the northeast monsoon, reach the Persian Gulf, Arabia, East Africa, or Southeast Asian ports within weeks to months, wait for the southwest monsoon, and return from April to September — a complete round trip in a single year.

Ship technology adapted to these conditions over centuries. The Arab dhow was built using a distinctive technique: planks were sewn together with fiber rather than nailed, creating a flexible hull that could absorb the stresses of ocean swells. Dhows were rigged with triangular (lateen) sails that could be adjusted to catch winds from different directions. Chinese junks used nailed planks, watertight compartments, and magnetic compasses — introduced in the eleventh century CE — that improved safety and navigational accuracy. The magnetic compass, developed in China and adopted by Arab and eventually European mariners, allowed sailors to maintain accurate course in overcast conditions when celestial navigation was impossible.

Disease was another environmental dimension of the trade system. Sailors and merchants moving between tropical and temperate zones, between regions with different disease environments, contributed to the spread of epidemic diseases. Malaria, cholera, and other diseases were risks in tropical ports. The same trade routes that carried spices and textiles also carried disease organisms — a pattern whose tragic consequences would become even clearer in the later Columbian Exchange era, but which was already a reality in the post-classical Indian Ocean world.

Historical Vocabulary
Mandala

In Sanskrit, a mandala is a geometric diagram representing the cosmos, typically circular and organized around a powerful center. In the study of Southeast Asian history, scholars use the term to describe the political structures of many post-classical Southeast Asian states. A mandala state is organized around a powerful ruler at the center, with authority extending outward in concentric circles of decreasing power and control.

Close to the center, the ruler exercises direct authority. Further out, subordinate chiefs acknowledge the ruler's overlordship in exchange for gifts, commercial privileges, and protection — but maintain considerable local autonomy. At the edges, authority fades into loose suzerainty claims. Mandala states were inherently different from bureaucratic empires like China or Rome: they did not exercise uniform administrative control across their territory. Their power was relative rather than absolute, and their boundaries were fluid. This concept helps historians understand why Srivijaya, Angkor, and other Southeast Asian polities behaved so differently from the empires of Eurasia that students might be more familiar with.

Chapter Summary

South and Southeast Asia in the post-classical era (roughly 500 to 1500 CE) present one of the most complex, dynamic, and globally significant histories of the period. The region's extraordinary environmental diversity — from Himalayan highlands to tropical ocean straits — produced an equally extraordinary diversity of political forms, cultural traditions, and economic strategies. Yet this diversity was overlaid by deep connectivity: the Indian Ocean trade network, driven by the monsoon wind system, linked South and Southeast Asia to each other and to the wider Afro-Eurasian world in relationships of commerce, religious exchange, and cultural influence.

In South Asia, the decline of the Gupta Empire gave way to a period of regional political fragmentation and cultural flourishing. Great temple-building dynasties — the Pallavas, Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas, and especially the Cholas — created some of the most spectacular religious monuments of the ancient world while also maintaining complex economic and commercial systems organized around temple institutions. The Chola Empire at its height was a significant Indian Ocean naval power, conducting raids as far as Srivijaya and maintaining tributary relationships with maritime polities across the wider region. Buddhism gradually declined as a living tradition in South Asia even as it flourished across the seas in Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Tibet, and East Asia.

In mainland Southeast Asia, the Khmer Empire of Angkor represents one of the most impressive achievements of the post-classical world. Building on a foundation of intensive wet rice agriculture made possible by sophisticated hydraulic engineering, the Angkor state supported a large population and an extraordinarily ambitious program of temple construction. Angkor Wat — the largest religious monument ever built — stands as testimony to the organizational and artistic genius of Khmer civilization. The LiDAR surveys of the 2010s revealed the true scale of greater Angkor as one of the largest pre-industrial cities in the world — a discovery that has fundamentally revised historians' understanding of mainland Southeast Asian civilization.

In island Southeast Asia, the maritime empire of Srivijaya demonstrated a different model of post-classical power: one based not on agricultural surplus and hydraulic engineering but on the control of maritime trade routes. By dominating the Strait of Malacca — the indispensable chokepoint between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea — Srivijaya collected the revenues of one of the world's most valuable commercial networks. Its political structure, organized as a mandala state with direct control at the center and negotiated suzerainty at the periphery, was flexible enough to endure for centuries, though ultimately vulnerable to commercial competition and naval challenge.

The spread of Indian cultural and religious traditions — Hinduism, Buddhism, Sanskrit, and associated concepts of kingship and cosmic order — to Southeast Asia was one of the defining historical processes of the post-classical period. Modern scholarship has revised the older concept of "Indianization" to emphasize the active role of Southeast Asian rulers and communities in selecting, adapting, and transforming Indian cultural models rather than passively receiving them. The great monuments of Southeast Asia — Borobudur, Angkor Wat, the temples of Pagan — are expressions of distinctly local genius that drew on Indian traditions as one resource among many.

The arrival and spread of Islam added new layers of complexity to an already rich religious landscape. In South Asia, Islam arrived first through trade, then through military conquest (Arab conquest of Sindh, 711 CE), and then through the extended process of cultural Islamization associated with the Ghaznavid raids and ultimately the Delhi Sultanate (established 1206 CE). In Southeast Asia, Islam spread primarily through trade routes and the activities of Sufi missionaries, with the conversion of port-city rulers beginning in the late thirteenth century and accelerating dramatically with the founding of the Malacca Sultanate around 1400 CE.

Cultural blending — syncretism — was a defining feature of religious life across the region during the post-classical era. The Hindu and Buddhist traditions that Southeast Asian rulers adopted were transformed by their encounter with local animist and ancestor-veneration traditions. The Islam that spread to South and Southeast Asia was shaped by Sufi mysticism and by the social and cultural environments it encountered. Kabir's syncretic poetry in India, the distinctly Khmer synthesis of Hindu and Buddhist ideas in Angkor Thom, and the Malay Islam that blended with pre-Islamic traditions all illustrate this fundamental characteristic of the region's religious history.

The Indian Ocean trade network, which knitted together South and Southeast Asia and connected them to East Africa, Arabia, Persia, and ultimately to China and Europe, was the material foundation for all these cultural exchanges. Without the monsoon winds, without the demand for spices and textiles and porcelain, without the networks of trust and credit that made long-distance commerce possible, the cultural connections of the post-classical period would not have been possible. The trade network was not merely an economic phenomenon; it was the infrastructure through which religious ideas, artistic traditions, languages, technologies, and diseases moved between societies.

By around 1400 CE, the post-classical world of South and Southeast Asia was poised on the edge of major transformations. The Malacca Sultanate was rising as the dominant force in maritime Southeast Asia, setting the stage for rapid further Islamization of the coastal and island world. In South Asia, the Delhi Sultanate's successor states were laying the cultural groundwork for the Mughal Empire. In mainland Southeast Asia, the Angkor state was in its final decline, giving way to Siamese and Vietnamese states that would dominate the mainland in the early modern period. The world of post-classical South and Southeast Asia — temple kingdoms and maritime empires, Buddhist monasteries and Sufi lodges, Indian Ocean trading cities and Khmer hydraulic civilization — was transforming, but transforming in ways deeply shaped by the thousand years of development this chapter has studied.

Khmer king (r. c. 1113–1150 CE); builder of Angkor Wat; presided over a major expansion of Khmer territorial power; Angkor Wat's scale reflects his ambition and the state's organizational capacity.
Xuanzang
Chinese Buddhist monk (c. 602–664 CE) who traveled to India 629–645 CE; left detailed accounts of Indian Buddhist sites, the Harsha kingdom, and the university at Nalanda; invaluable historical source for seventh-century South Asian society.
Yijing (I-tsing)
Chinese Buddhist pilgrim (635–713 CE) who visited Srivijaya c. 671 CE; left the earliest detailed Chinese account of Srivijayan power and its Buddhist community; recommended Srivijaya as a center of Buddhist scholarship.

Chapter Reflection

The history of post-classical South and Southeast Asia reveals something fundamental about the nature of world history itself: that the most important historical processes are almost never confined to a single civilization, a single religion, or a single region. The Indian Ocean trade network that knitted together South and Southeast Asia was a system built by dozens of peoples — Tamil merchants, Arab sailors, Malay navigators, Javanese traders, Chinese merchants — each contributing specific knowledge, capital, and logistical capacity. No single people created the system; it emerged from the interactions of many.

This chapter also challenges assumptions that can unconsciously shape how students think about world history. The assumption that cultural exchange is a flow from "more advanced" to "less advanced" civilizations — embedded in older concepts like "Indianization" — obscures the reality that Southeast Asian peoples were creative agents who chose what to adopt, transformed what they received, and produced cultural forms of extraordinary sophistication. Angkor Wat is not a copy of any Indian temple; it is a distinctly Khmer monument that draws on Indian cosmological concepts while expressing specifically Khmer political ambitions and artistic genius. The gamelan orchestras of Java, the shadow puppet traditions of Bali, the classical dance forms of Cambodia — all draw on Indian and Buddhist inspirations while being unmistakably and irreducibly Southeast Asian.

The spread of Islam to South and Southeast Asia provides another lesson about the complexity of religious change. Islam did not spread through the Indian Ocean world primarily by force, at least not in the Southeast Asian case. It spread because Muslim merchants built trust networks across the ocean; because Sufi teachers offered spiritually compelling forms of practice accessible across social classes; because rulers in port cities found in Islam a cosmopolitan religious identity compatible with their commercial ambitions; and because Islamic legal and scholarly traditions provided useful tools for organizing complex commercial societies. Understanding how Islam actually spread in these regions — as opposed to assuming it spread primarily by the sword — is essential to understanding both the post-classical period and the contemporary religious landscape of the world's most populous Muslim-majority region.

The environmental dimensions of this history deserve particular emphasis. The hydraulic civilization of Angkor — one of the most remarkable achievements of the post-classical world — was ultimately vulnerable to the environmental conditions that had made it possible. Recent research suggests that the combination of droughts, floods, and the deterioration of the hydraulic system played a significant role in Angkor's decline. This story — of a civilization whose power was built on mastery of a complex environment, and whose vulnerability was related to the limits of that mastery — resonates with contemporary concerns about climate change and the fragility of complex societies. The history of Angkor invites students to think seriously about the relationship between environmental conditions and the rise and fall of complex civilizations across all of human history.

Finally, this chapter illustrates why world history must study South and Southeast Asia as central rather than peripheral. The Indian Ocean trade network, during the post-classical era, was probably the world's most economically important commercial system. The spices, textiles, and other goods that moved through it were the most commercially valuable commodities in international trade. The religious and cultural traditions that the region incubated — Theravada Buddhism, Hindu devotionalism, Southeast Asian Islam — shaped the lives of hundreds of millions of people and continue to do so today. The hydraulic civilization of Angkor represents one of humanity's most impressive achievements in engineering, agriculture, and monumental art. To study world history while treating South and Southeast Asia as a footnote to Chinese, European, or Middle Eastern history would be to fundamentally misunderstand the world. This chapter has placed these regions at the center of the story — where they belong.

Climate, Environment, and Global Health

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The atmosphere keeps no passport records. A gas released above a coal plant in one country becomes, within a matter of weeks, a measurable part of the air breathed on every continent."

In 1958, an American scientist named Charles David Keeling began measuring the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere from an observatory perched near the summit of Mauna Loa, a volcano on the island of Hawai'i, chosen precisely because it sat far from cities, farms, and factories whose local emissions might distort the reading. Keeling wanted to know what the whole planet's atmosphere actually contained. What he found, and what researchers continuing his measurements have tracked every year since, became one of the most consequential graphs in the history of science: a line rising steadily, year after year, with a small seasonal wobble as Northern Hemisphere forests inhaled and exhaled carbon with the turning seasons, but an unmistakable long-term climb. The "Keeling Curve," as it came to be known, offered something world history rarely provides so cleanly: a single, continuous, planetary measurement of a change that was affecting, and would go on affecting, every region, every economy, and every society on Earth.

This chapter studies the environmental and public health history of the modern world — how industrial growth transformed the planet's atmosphere, oceans, forests, and waterways; how climate change emerged as a defining global challenge; how pollution, biodiversity loss, and resource scarcity became sources of both hardship and conflict; how public health systems developed to fight infectious disease; and how pandemics, from influenza to COVID-19, exposed both the interconnection and the inequality of the modern world. It also asks a harder question that runs beneath all of these developments: who caused these problems, who suffers their consequences, and who bears responsibility for solving them.

This chapter matters in world history because environmental and health challenges do not respect the boundaries historians often use to organize the past — nations, empires, wars, revolutions. A changing climate reshapes agriculture in Bangladesh, wildfire seasons in California, and coral reefs in the Pacific simultaneously, though not equally. A virus that emerges in one city can reach every inhabited continent within months, as it has repeatedly across the past century. Understanding this recent history requires historians to think at a genuinely planetary scale while remaining alert to profound inequalities in cause, exposure, and capacity to respond.

The regions central to this chapter are, in a real sense, all regions. Industrialized nations in Europe, North America, and East Asia produced the overwhelming share of historical greenhouse gas emissions and built the fossil-fuel economies that powered global growth. Yet the low-lying island nations of the Pacific and Indian Oceans, the drought-prone regions of the Sahel, the flood-prone river deltas of South and Southeast Asia, and impoverished urban populations worldwide often bear the earliest and most severe consequences of climate change, despite having contributed comparatively little to causing it. Public health, too, is global in scope: disease outbreaks that began in Central Africa, Southeast Asia, and China have reshaped international politics, economics, and daily life across the entire world.

What changed during this period was staggering in scale. The twentieth and early twenty-first centuries saw humanity extract and burn fossil fuels at a rate unmatched in the whole of prior human history, releasing carbon that had been locked in the earth for hundreds of millions of years back into the atmosphere within the span of a few human lifetimes. Global population more than tripled. Public health interventions — vaccination, antibiotics, clean water systems, and disease surveillance — extended human life expectancy dramatically in most parts of the world, even as new and reemerging diseases, environmental degradation, and persistent inequality created new forms of vulnerability.

What continued, throughout this era of extraordinary transformation, were patterns of inequality that long predate industrialization: wealthier nations and populations generally retained greater capacity to adapt to environmental change and to access health resources, while poorer nations and populations, and historically marginalized groups within wealthy nations, often bore disproportionate exposure to environmental harm and disease. The relationship between economic power and environmental and health outcomes, in other words, is itself a historical continuity worth examining critically.

Historians studying this recent and ongoing history draw on an unusually rich and diverse body of evidence: scientific data from ice cores, tree rings, ocean sediments, and atmospheric monitoring stations; economic records of fossil fuel production and consumption; public health statistics and epidemiological records; the proceedings of international conferences and treaties; oral histories and journalism documenting environmental disasters and disease outbreaks; and the deliberations of scientists, diplomats, activists, and affected communities. This chapter asks students to read this evidence carefully, to understand both the scientific consensus underlying climate change and the historical, political, and economic forces that have shaped how societies have responded to it.

Finally, this chapter connects to some of the largest patterns in this textbook: the long relationship between human societies and their environments, first explored in the chapters on agriculture and early civilization; the technological transformations of the Industrial Revolution; the growth of global trade and interconnection; and the persistent, unresolved tensions between economic growth, human welfare, and the finite limits of a shared planet.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how industrialization and fossil fuel consumption transformed the global environment over the nineteenth, twentieth, and twenty-first centuries.
  2. Trace the scientific and political history of climate change, from early scientific discovery to contemporary international negotiation.
  3. Analyze the causes and consequences of pollution, biodiversity loss, and resource depletion in the modern world.
  4. Compare environmental and health vulnerability across regions and social groups.
  5. Describe the development of global environmental movements and international environmental policy.
  6. Evaluate the historical development of public health systems and their role in extending human life expectancy.
  7. Interpret the history of major pandemics, including the 1918 influenza pandemic and COVID-19, as global historical events.
  8. Assess competing interpretations of economic growth, environmental limits, and sustainable development.
  9. Connect environmental and public health history to broader patterns of global inequality and historical responsibility.
  10. Contextualize contemporary environmental and health challenges within longer-term historical processes of industrialization and globalization.
  11. Distinguish between short-term and long-term causes of environmental and public health crises.
  12. Synthesize scientific, economic, and political evidence to construct historical arguments about environmental change.

  2. Why did scientific understanding of climate change develop gradually over more than a century, and why did political responses lag behind scientific understanding?

Historical Context

The relationship between human societies and their environments is as old as humanity itself, and this textbook has traced its evolution across many chapters: the environmental transformations of the Agricultural Revolution, the ecological consequences of the Columbian Exchange, and the environmental costs of nineteenth-century industrialization. What distinguishes the period covered in this chapter — roughly the twentieth century to the present, with particular emphasis on developments since 1945 — is the sheer scale, speed, and planetary reach of environmental change, combined with a growing scientific capacity to measure and understand that change precisely.

The roots of contemporary environmental challenges lie in the Industrial Revolution, examined in Chapter 42, when coal-fired steam power began driving economic growth in Britain and then across the industrializing world. Fossil fuels — first coal, then petroleum, then natural gas — provided extraordinarily concentrated, portable, and inexpensive energy that powered factories, railroads, ships, and eventually automobiles and airplanes. This energy revolution lifted hundreds of millions of people out of subsistence poverty and enabled the modern world's extraordinary material abundance. It also released, as a byproduct of combustion, carbon dioxide and other gases that accumulate in the atmosphere and trap heat, a physical process understood in its basic outline by scientists as early as the nineteenth century but not treated as an urgent political concern until the final decades of the twentieth.

Global population growth compounded these environmental pressures. The world's population reached roughly one billion around 1800, two billion by 1930, and more than eight billion by the early 2020s — an increase without precedent in human history, driven by public health improvements, agricultural productivity gains associated with the Green Revolution, and declining mortality even before fertility rates fell in most regions. More people, consuming more resources at higher average standards of living, placed unprecedented demands on forests, freshwater systems, fisheries, and arable land.

Public health history follows a related but distinct trajectory. The nineteenth-century recognition that disease could spread through contaminated water, and the subsequent germ theory of disease developed by scientists including Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch, transformed medicine and public sanitation. Vaccination campaigns, antibiotic discovery in the mid-twentieth century, and the construction of clean water and sewage systems dramatically reduced mortality from infectious disease in industrialized nations and, more unevenly, across the globe. Yet infectious disease has never disappeared as a historical force. The 1918–1919 influenza pandemic killed tens of millions of people worldwide, more than died in the First World War that had just ended. HIV/AIDS killed tens of millions more beginning in the 1980s. And the COVID-19 pandemic beginning in late 2019 demonstrated, in real time, both the extraordinary interconnection of the modern globalized world and the deep inequalities in health capacity that persist within it.

By the final decades of the twentieth century, scientists, activists, and eventually governments began to recognize that environmental and health challenges of this scale could not be addressed by any single nation acting alone. This recognition produced a new kind of international politics — environmental diplomacy, global health governance, and a body of international agreements attempting, with varying degrees of success, to coordinate a global response to problems that no border could contain.

Regional Orientation

This chapter's subject is inherently global, but its regional patterns are uneven and important to understand precisely. North America, Europe, and later East Asia produced the overwhelming majority of historical greenhouse gas emissions through industrial development beginning in the nineteenth century, and these regions generally possess the greatest financial and technological capacity to adapt to environmental change. Low-lying coastal and island regions — including the Pacific Island nations of Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands, and the river deltas of Bangladesh and the Nile and Mekong — face some of the most severe and immediate climate consequences, including sea-level rise and flooding, despite having contributed minimally to global emissions. Sub-Saharan Africa faces acute vulnerability to drought, desertification, and disease, compounded by limited health infrastructure in many nations. South and Southeast Asia combine large populations, rapid industrial growth, and high exposure to monsoon disruption, flooding, and heat stress. Latin America contains vast tropical forest reserves, particularly the Amazon Basin, whose preservation or destruction carries global climatic consequences far beyond the region's own borders. Understanding this chapter requires holding all of these regional realities in view simultaneously, since the central story of modern environmental and health history is precisely the mismatch between where problems originate and where their consequences land.

Section 57.1

Environment in World History

Every civilization studied in this textbook has existed within, and depended upon, a natural environment. The river valley civilizations of Mesopotamia and Egypt depended on predictable flooding cycles. Mesoamerican and Andean societies developed agricultural terracing and irrigation to manage difficult terrain. Medieval Europe's population fluctuated with harvest yields shaped by climate variability, including the "Little Ice Age" that contributed to famine and social stress in the fourteenth through nineteenth centuries. What changed with industrialization was not humanity's relationship with the environment in principle — humans had always shaped, and been shaped by, their surroundings — but rather the scale, speed, and reach of that relationship.

Scholars increasingly use the term "Anthropocene" to describe the current geological epoch, in which human activity has become a dominant force shaping the planet's climate, geology, and ecosystems — a shift from the previous Holocene epoch that had provided a relatively stable climate for roughly twelve thousand years, coinciding with the entire span of settled human civilization. While geologists debate the precise starting date and formal validity of the term, it captures something historically significant: humanity's collective activity, particularly since industrialization, has begun to leave a measurable imprint on the physical planet comparable to major natural forces.

This chapter approaches environmental history not as a story of humanity against nature, nor as a simple story of decline, but as a story of interaction, unintended consequence, and unequal power. Industrial growth produced enormous benefits — reduced poverty, extended life spans, expanded human capability — even as it generated environmental costs that were, for most of the twentieth century, poorly understood, discounted, or deliberately obscured. Understanding this tension, rather than resolving it into a simple morality tale, is essential to historical thinking about the environment.

Historical Vocabulary
Anthropocene

The Anthropocene is a proposed term for the current geological epoch, defined by the significant and measurable impact of human activity on the Earth's climate, ecosystems, and geology. Evidence proposed to mark this epoch includes atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations, plastic pollution deposits, radioactive isotopes from nuclear testing, and widespread species extinction. The term is scientifically debated but historically useful: it frames industrialization and its aftermath as a turning point not only in human history but in the history of the planet itself.

Section 57.2

Industrialization and Fossil Fuels

The Industrial Revolution, examined in earlier chapters, was fundamentally an energy revolution. Coal provided far more concentrated and reliable power than the wind, water, and animal or human muscle that had powered production for millennia. Petroleum, developed as a major energy source beginning in the late nineteenth century, offered even greater energy density and portability, enabling the internal combustion engine and, eventually, an entirely automobile-centered pattern of settlement and transportation across much of the industrialized world. Natural gas, increasingly extracted and transported over the twentieth century, added a third major fossil fuel to the global energy mix.

The economic benefits of fossil fuel energy were immense and are not easily dismissed. Cheap, abundant energy powered manufacturing, agriculture, transportation, heating, and eventually electricity generation, driving economic growth that reduced poverty and raised living standards across much of the world over the twentieth century. Fossil fuel-powered agriculture — through mechanized farm equipment, synthetic fertilizers derived from natural gas, and irrigation pumps — enabled the dramatic productivity gains of the Green Revolution, discussed further in Section 57.5, which allowed global food production to keep pace with rapid population growth.

Yet the combustion of fossil fuels also released carbon dioxide, methane, and other gases into the atmosphere at a rate and scale without precedent in human history. Scientists had understood since the nineteenth century that carbon dioxide and certain other gases trap heat in the atmosphere — the basic physics of what is called the greenhouse effect — but for most of the twentieth century, this scientific understanding did not translate into significant public concern or policy action, in part because the effects accumulated gradually and were not yet clearly visible, and in part because fossil fuel industries actively worked, in some documented cases, to create public doubt about the scientific consensus that was steadily solidifying.

By the early twenty-first century, the scale of fossil fuel dependence had become deeply embedded in the infrastructure of modern life: transportation networks, electrical grids, agricultural systems, and manufacturing supply chains worldwide were built around assumptions of abundant, affordable fossil energy. This embeddedness has made the transition to alternative energy sources — solar, wind, hydroelectric, nuclear, and others — a complex and contested process, discussed further in Section 57.6, involving not only technological and economic questions but questions of employment, national development strategy, and global equity between industrialized and industrializing nations.

Section 57.3

Climate Change and Global Warming

The scientific history of climate change stretches back further than most people assume. In 1859, the Irish physicist John Tyndall demonstrated experimentally that certain gases, including carbon dioxide and water vapor, absorb and trap heat, providing the physical basis for what became known as the greenhouse effect. In 1896, the Swedish scientist Svante Arrhenius calculated that a doubling of atmospheric carbon dioxide could raise global temperatures by several degrees Celsius — a remarkably prescient estimate given the limited data and computational tools available to him.

For most of the following century, this scientific understanding remained largely confined to specialized research. It was Charles David Keeling's continuous measurements at Mauna Loa Observatory, beginning in 1958, that provided the first rigorous, ongoing, and publicly visible evidence that atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations were, in fact, rising steadily as fossil fuel consumption grew. By the 1970s and 1980s, a growing body of climate scientists had developed increasingly sophisticated computer models linking rising greenhouse gas concentrations to observed and projected warming, sea-level rise, and shifts in weather patterns.

The establishment of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in 1988 marked a turning point in translating this scientific consensus into international policy discourse. The IPCC, comprising thousands of scientists from around the world, produces periodic assessment reports synthesizing the state of climate science for policymakers. Its early reports, beginning in 1990, established with increasing confidence that human activity, primarily through fossil fuel combustion and deforestation, was the dominant driver of observed warming since the mid-twentieth century — a conclusion that has only strengthened, with greater certainty, in each subsequent assessment.

International political response, however, developed more slowly and unevenly than scientific understanding. The 1992 Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro produced the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, establishing a framework for international climate negotiation but setting no binding targets. The 1997 Kyoto Protocol established binding emissions targets for industrialized nations, but the United States, historically the largest cumulative emitter, never ratified it, and rapidly industrializing nations such as China and India, exempted from binding targets as "developing" economies under the treaty's structure, went on to become major emitters in their own right by the early twenty-first century.

The 2015 Paris Agreement represented a significant shift in approach: rather than imposing binding targets from above, it asked each signatory nation to set its own "nationally determined contribution" toward limiting global temperature rise to well below two degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, with an aspiration toward 1.5 degrees. Nearly every nation on Earth signed the agreement, reflecting a near-universal acknowledgment of the problem, even as scientists and policy analysts have repeatedly noted that cumulative national commitments remain insufficient to meet the agreement's stated temperature goals.

Cause and Effect

Global Warming Since Industrialization

Long-Term Causes

Roughly two centuries of accelerating fossil fuel combustion since the Industrial Revolution; large-scale deforestation reducing the planet's carbon-absorbing capacity; rapid population and economic growth increasing global energy demand; delayed political recognition of accumulating scientific evidence.

Short-Term Drivers (20th–21st c.)

Post-1945 economic expansion and mass automobile adoption; rapid industrialization in East and South Asia after 1980; continued expansion of coal-fired electricity generation in growing economies; political resistance to binding international emissions agreements.

The Transformation

Atmospheric CO2 concentrations rising from roughly 280 parts per million before industrialization to over 420 parts per million by the 2020s; measurable global average temperature increase of more than one degree Celsius since the late nineteenth century.

Effects

Rising sea levels threatening coastal and island communities; increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events; disrupted agricultural cycles; accelerating biodiversity loss; growing climate-related displacement and migration.

Historical complexity: Global warming cannot be attributed to any single nation, industry, or decade. It is the cumulative product of two centuries of industrial development distributed unevenly across regions, compounded by delayed political action even after scientific consensus was well established.

Evidence Window

Ice Cores and the Deep History of Earth's Climate

Describe the evidence: Scientists drilling into ancient ice sheets in Antarctica and Greenland extract long cylindrical cores containing layers of compressed snow and ice that have accumulated over hundreds of thousands of years. Each layer traps tiny bubbles of ancient atmosphere along with dust, volcanic ash, and other particles.

What can be inferred: By analyzing the trapped air bubbles, scientists can directly measure atmospheric carbon dioxide and methane concentrations from hundreds of thousands of years in the past, allowing direct comparison with today's concentrations. Ice cores from Antarctica have provided continuous climate records stretching back roughly 800,000 years, revealing natural cycles of ice ages and warmer periods, against which the current, much more rapid warming trend can be measured and understood.

Limits of the evidence: Ice core records are geographically limited to polar regions and require careful calibration and interpretation. They reveal long-term patterns well but require correlation with other evidence — ocean sediment cores, tree rings, coral records — to build a complete picture of past climate at regional scales.

Section 57.4

Pollution, Biodiversity, and Resource Use

Beyond climate change, industrialization and population growth produced a wide range of environmental pressures affecting air, water, land, and living species. Industrial and vehicular air pollution caused severe public health crises in rapidly industrializing cities, from the London smog events of the mid-twentieth century, which killed thousands, to contemporary air quality crises in cities such as Delhi and Beijing. Water pollution from industrial runoff, agricultural chemicals, and inadequate sanitation infrastructure has contaminated rivers, lakes, and groundwater across the globe, disproportionately affecting low-income communities with less political power to demand remediation.

Biodiversity loss accelerated dramatically over the twentieth century as habitat destruction, pollution, overharvesting, and climate change combined to threaten species across every ecosystem. Deforestation, driven by agricultural expansion, logging, and infrastructure development, has been especially severe in tropical regions including the Amazon Basin, Central Africa's Congo Basin, and Southeast Asia, all of which host an outsized share of the planet's biological diversity and carbon storage capacity. Ocean ecosystems have faced comparable pressures from overfishing, plastic pollution, and warming, acidifying waters that threaten coral reefs and marine food webs.

Resource depletion — of fresh water, arable soil, fisheries, and mineral resources — has raised long-term questions about the sustainability of growth patterns established during the industrial and postindustrial eras. The concept of an "ecological footprint," developed by researchers in the 1990s, attempts to measure the land and resources required to sustain a given population's consumption patterns, and consistently finds that if the entire global population consumed at the rate of the wealthiest industrialized nations, planetary resources would be insufficient to sustain that consumption indefinitely.

Historical Vocabulary
Externality

An externality is a cost or benefit of an economic activity that falls on a third party not directly involved in the transaction. Pollution is a classic negative externality: a factory that dumps waste into a river imposes costs — contaminated water, harmed fisheries, health problems — on downstream communities who receive no compensation and had no say in the decision. Understanding externalities helps explain why market economies, left to operate without regulation, often systematically under-price environmental harm, since polluters bear little of the true cost of their activity while affected communities bear the consequences.

Section 57.5

Water, Food, and Environmental Security

Access to fresh water, reliable food production, and stable environmental conditions form the foundation of human security, and all three have come under increasing pressure in the modern era. The Green Revolution of the mid-twentieth century, associated with agricultural scientists including Norman Borlaug, developed high-yield crop varieties combined with synthetic fertilizers, irrigation, and mechanization that dramatically increased global food production, particularly in Asia and Latin America, averting famines that many demographers had predicted as inevitable given rapid population growth. Yet the Green Revolution also brought environmental costs — heavy reliance on fossil fuel-derived fertilizers and pesticides, groundwater depletion from irrigation, and reduced agricultural biodiversity through monoculture farming.

Freshwater scarcity has emerged as a growing source of both humanitarian concern and international tension. Rivers that cross multiple national borders, including the Nile, the Mekong, the Indus, and the Colorado, have become sites of complex negotiation and occasional conflict over water rights as population growth, agricultural demand, and climate-driven changes in precipitation strain available supplies. Groundwater aquifers in regions including northern India, the American Great Plains, and parts of the Middle East have been depleted faster than natural recharge can replace them, raising long-term questions about agricultural sustainability in some of the world's most important farming regions.

Climate change compounds these pressures directly: shifting precipitation patterns, more frequent and severe droughts, and disrupted growing seasons threaten food security in vulnerable regions, particularly across sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, even as extreme flooding events threaten agricultural land and water infrastructure elsewhere. The concept of "climate security" — the recognition that environmental stress can contribute to displacement, resource conflict, and political instability — has become an increasingly prominent framework in international policy discussions since the early twenty-first century.

Section 57.6

Environmental Movements and Policy

The modern environmental movement developed gradually over the twentieth century, drawing on earlier conservation traditions — including the establishment of national parks in the United States and elsewhere beginning in the late nineteenth century — while developing new forms of activism, scientific advocacy, and political organization responsive to industrial-era environmental threats. Rachel Carson's 1962 book Silent Spring, which documented the ecological damage caused by the pesticide DDT, is widely credited with catalyzing a new wave of environmental consciousness in the United States, contributing to the establishment of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency in 1970 and the passage of major environmental legislation in the following years.

The first Earth Day, observed on April 22, 1970, drew an estimated twenty million participants across the United States and helped establish environmentalism as a mainstream political concern rather than a marginal interest. Similar movements developed across Western Europe, Japan, and eventually much of the world over the following decades, often connecting environmental concerns to broader movements for social justice, Indigenous rights, and anti-nuclear activism.

International environmental governance developed alongside domestic movements. The 1972 United Nations Conference on the Human Environment in Stockholm marked the first major international conference devoted to environmental issues. The 1987 Montreal Protocol, which phased out chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) responsible for depleting the stratospheric ozone layer, is widely regarded as the most successful international environmental agreement in history: global CFC production fell dramatically after the treaty's adoption, and scientific monitoring has since confirmed measurable recovery of the ozone layer, demonstrating that coordinated international action can, under the right conditions, successfully address a global environmental threat.

Climate policy has proven considerably more difficult to coordinate than ozone protection, in part because addressing climate change requires transforming the energy foundations of the global economy rather than banning a narrower category of industrial chemicals, and in part because the costs and benefits of climate action are distributed very unevenly across wealthy and poor nations, present and future generations, and different economic sectors. Renewable energy technologies — solar photovoltaic power, wind turbines, and battery storage — have become dramatically more efficient and affordable since the early twenty-first century, offering a technological pathway toward decarbonization even as political and economic obstacles to rapid transition remain substantial in many nations.

Global Connection

The Ozone Layer and International Environmental Cooperation

The story of the ozone layer connects atmospheric chemistry, industrial manufacturing, international science, and diplomacy into a single global narrative. Chlorofluorocarbons, widely used in refrigerants and aerosol sprays after their introduction in the 1930s, were found in the 1970s to break down stratospheric ozone, which shields the planet's surface from harmful ultraviolet radiation. The discovery in 1985 of a dramatic seasonal "ozone hole" over Antarctica provided vivid, measurable evidence of the danger, galvanizing rapid international action. Within two years, the Montreal Protocol was signed, and nearly every nation on Earth eventually ratified it. This episode demonstrates how a genuinely global environmental threat, once clearly identified and communicated by scientists, can prompt coordinated international response — a precedent frequently invoked, with mixed success, in subsequent climate negotiations.

Section 57.7

Public Health and Global Disease

The history of public health in the modern era is a story of extraordinary progress accompanied by persistent inequality and recurring crisis. The development of germ theory in the late nineteenth century, associated with scientists including Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch, transformed medical understanding of infectious disease and enabled new interventions: sanitation systems, water treatment, vaccination, and eventually antibiotics, discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 and developed into widely available treatments during the Second World War.

These interventions produced dramatic gains in human life expectancy over the twentieth century. Global average life expectancy at birth, estimated at around thirty years in 1900, rose to more than seventy years by the early twenty-first century — one of the most significant achievements in human history, though one distributed very unevenly: wealthy nations with strong health infrastructure achieved life expectancies approaching eighty years or more, while some of the poorest nations, particularly those affected by conflict, extreme poverty, or the HIV/AIDS epidemic, lagged considerably behind.

The founding of the World Health Organization in 1948 established the first permanent international institution devoted to coordinating global public health efforts, disease surveillance, and health policy across national borders. The WHO's 1980 declaration that smallpox — a disease that had killed hundreds of millions of people across human history — had been globally eradicated through a coordinated worldwide vaccination campaign stands as one of the most significant achievements in the history of public health, demonstrating what sustained international cooperation could accomplish.

Yet infectious disease has continued to pose severe challenges. The HIV/AIDS epidemic, first identified in the early 1980s, killed tens of millions of people worldwide over subsequent decades, with especially devastating impact across sub-Saharan Africa, where limited access to antiretroviral medications for much of the epidemic's first two decades reflected profound global inequalities in health resources and pharmaceutical access. Tuberculosis and malaria, diseases with long historical roots, have continued to kill over a million people annually each in the twenty-first century, disproportionately affecting poorer nations with limited health infrastructure.

Section 57.8

Pandemics and International Response

The 1918–1919 influenza pandemic, often called the "Spanish flu" despite likely originating elsewhere, killed an estimated fifty million people or more worldwide — far more than died in the First World War that had just concluded — spreading rapidly along wartime troop movements and global shipping routes at a time when medical understanding of viruses remained extremely limited and no vaccines or effective treatments existed. The pandemic's staggering toll, followed by decades of relative complacency once acute memory faded, illustrates a recurring pattern in the history of public health: societies often respond vigorously to disease crises while they are underway, only to under-invest in preparedness once the immediate danger passes.

Subsequent decades saw a series of emerging infectious disease outbreaks that tested global health systems and international cooperation: the HIV/AIDS epidemic beginning in the 1980s; the SARS outbreak of 2002–2003, which spread from southern China to more than two dozen countries before being contained; the H1N1 influenza pandemic of 2009; the West African Ebola outbreak of 2014–2016, which killed more than eleven thousand people and exposed severe weaknesses in health infrastructure in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone; and the Zika virus outbreak of 2015–2016.

The COVID-19 pandemic, beginning with the identification of a novel coronavirus in Wuhan, China, in late 2019, became the most disruptive global health crisis in a century. Within months, the virus had spread to nearly every country on Earth, prompting unprecedented public health measures including widespread lockdowns, travel restrictions, and economic disruption. By the time the World Health Organization declared an end to the global health emergency in 2023, the pandemic had caused an estimated seven million confirmed deaths, with excess mortality estimates suggesting a considerably higher true toll, alongside profound economic disruption, educational setbacks for hundreds of millions of children, and significant strain on international cooperation.

The rapid development of effective COVID-19 vaccines within roughly a year of the virus's identification — building on decades of prior mRNA vaccine research — represented a remarkable scientific achievement. Yet the distribution of those vaccines exposed familiar and troubling patterns of global inequality: wealthy nations secured the large majority of early vaccine supplies through direct purchase agreements, while many low-income nations, dependent on international sharing mechanisms such as the COVAX initiative, faced significant delays in accessing vaccines even as case counts and deaths mounted within their borders.

Comparison Across Societies

The 1918 Influenza Pandemic and COVID-19 Compared

Comparing the 1918 influenza pandemic and COVID-19, more than a century apart, reveals both continuity and change in humanity's encounter with pandemic disease. In both cases, disease spread rapidly along transportation networks connecting distant regions — troop ships and railways in 1918, commercial air travel in 2020 — demonstrating how each era's dominant mode of global connection also became a vector for disease transmission. In both cases, public health measures including quarantine, mask-wearing, and restrictions on public gatherings provoked significant public resistance and political controversy, even as evidence supported their effectiveness in reducing transmission. In both cases, mortality fell disproportionately on already disadvantaged populations, though for different underlying reasons in each era.

The differences are equally significant. Nineteen-eighteen's medical responders had no understanding of viruses, no vaccines, and no antibiotics to treat secondary bacterial infections, while COVID-19 responders benefited from advanced virology, genomic sequencing that identified the virus within weeks, and vaccines developed in under a year. Global communication and scientific collaboration during COVID-19 occurred at a speed and scale unimaginable in 1918, allowing real-time data sharing across the world. Yet international political cooperation in both eras proved similarly difficult to sustain, with nations frequently prioritizing unilateral national responses over coordinated global strategy, suggesting that scientific and technological progress alone does not resolve the political and structural challenges of pandemic response.

Section 57.9

Sustainability and Historical Responsibility

The concept of "sustainable development" — meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs — was formally articulated in the 1987 report of the United Nations World Commission on Environment and Development, chaired by Norwegian Prime Minister Gro Harlem Brundtland and commonly known as the Brundtland Report. This framework attempted to reconcile the legitimate development aspirations of poorer nations, many of which had experienced colonization and continued to face severe poverty, with the environmental limits increasingly evident at a planetary scale.

This reconciliation has proven historically contentious, particularly around the question of historical responsibility. Industrialized nations in Europe, North America, and later East Asia generated the overwhelming majority of cumulative greenhouse gas emissions since the Industrial Revolution, building the economic infrastructure and material prosperity that supports their high living standards today. Many developing nations argue, with considerable historical justification, that they should not be required to limit their own development at the same rate as nations that industrialized freely, without climate constraints, over the preceding two centuries — a position often summarized as the principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities," formally recognized in international climate negotiations beginning with the 1992 Rio Earth Summit.

The concept of "resilience" has become increasingly central to environmental and development policy in the twenty-first century, reflecting a recognition that some degree of climate change and environmental stress is now unavoidable given the greenhouse gases already accumulated in the atmosphere, requiring not only mitigation — reducing future emissions — but also adaptation — building the capacity of vulnerable communities and infrastructure to withstand environmental shocks that can no longer be entirely prevented.

Historical Vocabulary
Resilience

Resilience, in environmental and public health policy, refers to the capacity of a community, system, or infrastructure to withstand, adapt to, and recover from disruptive shocks — whether extreme weather events, disease outbreaks, or resource scarcity. Historians and policymakers increasingly distinguish between mitigation, which seeks to prevent or reduce the severity of a hazard, and resilience or adaptation, which seeks to reduce the harm caused once a hazard occurs. The growing emphasis on resilience since the early twenty-first century reflects a historical judgment that some environmental change is now unavoidable, requiring societies to prepare for consequences even as they continue working to limit their severity.

Regional Lens

Pacific Island Nations and Climate Vulnerability

The small island nations of the Pacific Ocean — including Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands, and Fiji — occupy a distinctive and increasingly urgent place in global environmental history. These nations, many with populations numbering in the tens of thousands and average elevations of only a few meters above sea level, face an existential threat from sea-level rise that could render significant portions of their territory uninhabitable within the lifetimes of people alive today. Their historical contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions is negligible, measured in a small fraction of one percent of the global total, yet they stand among the nations facing the most severe and immediate consequences of a changing climate.

Pacific Island nations have responded to this vulnerability not with passivity but with vigorous international diplomacy, becoming some of the most persistent and morally forceful voices in global climate negotiations. Tuvalu's government has explored contingency plans including the digital preservation of national identity and even relocation strategies should the nation's physical territory become uninhabitable — a scenario that raises unprecedented legal and political questions about statehood, sovereignty, and citizenship in a warming world. Understanding the Pacific Island experience on its own terms, rather than only as a symbol invoked by outside observers, reveals sophisticated societies drawing on both traditional ecological knowledge and modern diplomacy to advocate for their survival within a global system that they had little role in creating.

Network Study

The Global Carbon Economy

The global carbon economy — the interconnected system of fossil fuel extraction, processing, transportation, and combustion that powers most of the modern world — constitutes one of the largest and most consequential networks in human history. Petroleum extracted in the Middle East, Russia, the United States, and West Africa flows through pipelines and tanker shipping routes to refineries and markets worldwide. Coal mined in China, India, the United States, and Australia fuels electricity generation that powers factories, homes, and increasingly, electric vehicles whose ultimate environmental footprint depends heavily on the source of the electricity charging them.

This network connects extraction sites, often in economically marginalized regions bearing disproportionate local environmental costs, to consumption centers in wealthier, more urbanized regions far removed from the direct physical and ecological consequences of extraction. It also connects historical patterns of colonialism and economic dependency, examined in earlier chapters, to contemporary environmental politics: many of the world's major oil-producing regions were shaped by colonial-era resource extraction arrangements whose legacies continue to influence global energy politics today. The transition toward renewable energy, discussed in Section 57.6, is beginning to reshape this network, creating new supply chains for materials such as lithium, cobalt, and rare earth elements essential to batteries and solar technology — supply chains that carry their own significant environmental and labor concerns, particularly in extraction regions across Africa and Latin America.

Primary Source Window

The Brundtland Report, Our Common Future (1987)

Creator: United Nations World Commission on Environment and Development, chaired by Gro Harlem Brundtland · Date: 1987 · Type: International government report

Context: Commissioned by the United Nations General Assembly in 1983, the report brought together policymakers, scientists, and experts from around the world to examine the relationship between economic development and environmental protection. It introduced the concept of sustainable development that became foundational to subsequent international environmental policy.

The report defined sustainable development as development that meets present needs without compromising the capacity of future generations to meet their own needs, and it argued that environmental protection and economic development were not opposing goals but necessarily interconnected challenges that required joint solutions.

Analysis: The Brundtland Report shaped nearly all subsequent international environmental diplomacy, including the 1992 Rio Earth Summit and later climate negotiations. Its framing acknowledged the legitimate development needs of poorer nations while insisting that unlimited resource consumption was not a viable long-term global strategy. The report has been praised for building international consensus around a shared vocabulary, but critics have argued that its language was vague enough to be invoked by parties with very different, sometimes contradictory, policy goals — a tension that has persisted in international sustainability debates ever since.

Historical Debate

Does Economic Growth Require Environmental Sacrifice?

The question: Since the 1970s, economists, environmentalists, and policymakers have debated whether continued economic growth is fundamentally compatible with environmental sustainability, or whether the two goals are, at some level, in unavoidable tension.

Interpretation A — Growth and Sustainability Are Fundamentally in Tension: Some analysts, drawing on the 1972 report The Limits to Growth, argue that a finite planet cannot sustain indefinite economic and population growth without eventually exhausting critical resources or exceeding the planet's capacity to absorb pollution and waste. In this view, meaningful environmental sustainability requires fundamentally rethinking growth-based economic models, potentially including deliberate limits on consumption in wealthy nations.
Interpretation B — Technological Innovation Can Decouple Growth from Environmental Harm: Other analysts argue that technological innovation — renewable energy, efficiency improvements, and new materials — can allow economies to continue growing while reducing their environmental footprint, a process sometimes called "decoupling." They point to evidence that some wealthy nations have already begun reducing carbon emissions while continuing to grow economically, suggesting that growth and sustainability can be reconciled through the right policies and technologies.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes continued global emissions growth despite decades of environmental awareness, and finite limits on resources such as fresh water and arable land. Evidence for Interpretation B includes documented cases of national emissions decline alongside continued GDP growth in some wealthy nations, and the declining cost of renewable energy technology.

Why it matters: This debate shapes fundamental policy choices: whether governments should prioritize aggressive regulation and consumption limits, or invest primarily in technological innovation and market-based incentives, in pursuing environmental goals.

Student task: Considering the evidence on both sides, do you find greater support for the view that growth and sustainability are fundamentally in tension, or for the view that they can be reconciled through innovation? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Climate Change Across Different Communities

From the perspective of a coal-mining community in an industrialized nation: Climate policy that accelerates the closure of coal-fired power plants and mines can threaten livelihoods, community identity, and local economies built around fossil fuel industries over generations, generating legitimate anxiety about the pace and fairness of energy transition.
From the perspective of a low-lying Pacific Island community: Climate change represents an existential threat to homeland, culture, and survival, driven overwhelmingly by emissions from nations far away, generating urgent demands for rapid global emissions reduction and support for adaptation and, potentially, relocation.
From the perspective of policymakers in a rapidly industrializing nation: Restricting fossil fuel use and industrial development can appear to demand that poorer nations forgo the same unconstrained development pathway that wealthy nations used to build their prosperity, raising questions of fairness and historical responsibility central to international negotiations.
From the perspective of a climate scientist: Mounting physical evidence — rising temperatures, melting ice sheets, more frequent extreme weather — demonstrates an urgent and accelerating crisis requiring immediate, large-scale action, sometimes leading to frustration with the slow pace of political and economic response relative to the scientific evidence.
Continuity and Change Over Time

Public Health Since 1900

What Changed

  • Global average life expectancy roughly doubled, from around 30 years in 1900 to over 70 years by the 2020s
  • Vaccination and antibiotics dramatically reduced deaths from many infectious diseases
  • Smallpox was fully eradicated worldwide by 1980 through coordinated international campaign
  • International institutions such as the WHO created permanent frameworks for global health coordination
  • Genomic sequencing and mRNA vaccine technology allowed unprecedented speed in responding to COVID-19

What Stayed the Same

  • Health outcomes remained strongly correlated with national and individual wealth
  • Pandemics continued to spread rapidly along the era's dominant transportation networks
  • Public health measures such as quarantine continued to provoke political controversy and resistance
  • Access to advanced treatments and vaccines remained unevenly distributed between wealthy and poor nations

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of health inequality alongside extraordinary scientific and medical progress illustrates that technological capability alone does not guarantee equitable outcomes; political will, economic resources, and international cooperation remain essential to translating scientific progress into universal human benefit.

Everyday Life and Social History: Living with Environmental and Health Change

Behind the statistics of emissions, temperatures, and mortality rates lie the daily realities of billions of people adapting to environmental and health change. Farmers across the drought-prone Sahel region of Africa have adjusted planting schedules and crop choices in response to increasingly unpredictable rainfall, while facing difficult decisions about migration when adaptation proves insufficient. Urban residents in cities with severe air pollution, from Delhi to Mexico City to Beijing, have altered daily routines around air quality warnings, and children in these cities show measurably higher rates of respiratory illness than their counterparts in cleaner-air regions. Coastal communities from Louisiana to Bangladesh to the Pacific Islands have watched familiar landscapes change as rising seas and eroding shorelines reshape land long occupied by family and community. During the COVID-19 pandemic, ordinary daily life was transformed worldwide — school closures affected the education of hundreds of millions of children, remote work reshaped urban commuting patterns in wealthy nations, and frontline and essential workers, disproportionately from lower-income communities, bore heightened health risks while continuing daily labor others could avoid through remote isolation. These everyday experiences remind historians that environmental and public health history, however global and statistical in its broadest patterns, is ultimately composed of countless individual and community adaptations, hardships, and forms of resilience.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most consequential and still-unfolding stories in world history: the transformation of the global environment and the parallel development of global public health across the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries. The story begins with industrialization, which harnessed fossil fuels to power extraordinary economic growth and human development while releasing greenhouse gases that have measurably warmed the planet's atmosphere and oceans.

Scientific understanding of the greenhouse effect developed gradually from the mid-nineteenth century onward, reaching a robust consensus by the late twentieth century that human activity was the dominant driver of observed climate change. Political and international response, however, lagged significantly behind scientific understanding, producing a series of international agreements — from the Rio Earth Summit through the Kyoto Protocol to the Paris Agreement — that reflect both growing global recognition of the problem and persistent difficulty in coordinating effective, binding, and equitable global action.

Beyond climate change specifically, this chapter has examined the broader environmental pressures of the modern era: air and water pollution, biodiversity loss, deforestation, and resource depletion, all intensified by rapid population growth and rising consumption. These pressures fall unevenly across the globe, with historically wealthy, industrialized nations bearing the greatest responsibility for cumulative emissions while poorer and more vulnerable nations, particularly low-lying island states and drought-prone regions, often bear the earliest and most severe consequences.

The chapter has also traced the parallel history of public health, from the germ theory revolution of the late nineteenth century through the establishment of international health institutions, the historic eradication of smallpox, and the ongoing challenge of emerging infectious diseases including HIV/AIDS, Ebola, and COVID-19. This history reveals extraordinary scientific and medical progress — nearly doubling global life expectancy over the twentieth century — alongside persistent and troubling inequalities in access to health resources between wealthy and poor nations and communities.

Environmental movements, from Rachel Carson's influential writing through the first Earth Day to contemporary international climate activism, have played a crucial role in translating scientific evidence into public awareness and political pressure. International treaties, most successfully the Montreal Protocol addressing ozone depletion, demonstrate that coordinated global action is achievable under the right conditions, even as climate policy has proven far more difficult to coordinate given the scale of economic transformation it requires.

The concept of sustainable development, formally articulated in the 1987 Brundtland Report, has attempted to reconcile the legitimate development aspirations of poorer nations with the environmental limits increasingly evident at a planetary scale, though the tension between growth and sustainability remains a subject of ongoing debate among economists, policymakers, and environmental scientists.

The COVID-19 pandemic, examined alongside the historical example of the 1918 influenza pandemic, illustrated both the remarkable scientific and technological progress achieved over the intervening century and the persistence of familiar political and structural challenges: difficulty sustaining international cooperation, unequal distribution of medical resources, and disproportionate impact on already vulnerable populations.

Throughout this chapter, a central theme has recurred: the mismatch between where environmental and health problems originate and where their consequences are most severely felt. Wealthy, industrialized nations and populations have generally contributed most to environmental degradation while retaining the greatest capacity to adapt to its consequences, while poorer nations and populations, having contributed comparatively little, often face the earliest and most severe impacts with the least capacity to respond.

This chapter has also emphasized that environmental and public health history is not solely a story of decline or crisis. It is equally a story of extraordinary human achievement: the eradication of smallpox, the doubling of global life expectancy, the successful phase-out of ozone-depleting chemicals, the rapid development of COVID-19 vaccines, and the growing technological viability of renewable energy. Understanding both the achievements and the ongoing challenges is essential to a historically honest account of this recent and still-unfolding period.

Finally, this chapter connects to some of the largest and oldest patterns in world history: the relationship between human societies and their environments first examined in the chapters on agriculture and early civilization, the technological and economic transformations of industrialization, and the persistent, unresolved tensions between economic growth, human welfare, and the finite limits of a shared planet — tensions that will likely define much of the history still to be written in the decades ahead.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Anthropocene
A proposed term for the current geological epoch, defined by significant and measurable human impact on the Earth's climate, ecosystems, and geology.
Greenhouse effect
The physical process by which certain atmospheric gases trap heat, warming the planet's surface; essential to life on Earth but intensified dangerously by rising concentrations of greenhouse gases.
Fossil fuels
Energy sources — coal, petroleum, and natural gas — formed from ancient organic matter, whose combustion releases greenhouse gases.
Carbon emissions
The release of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, primarily through fossil fuel combustion and deforestation.
Biodiversity
The variety of living species and ecosystems on Earth, increasingly threatened by habitat loss, pollution, and climate change.
Deforestation
The large-scale clearing of forests, reducing biodiversity and the planet's capacity to absorb atmospheric carbon.
Externality
A cost or benefit of an economic activity that falls on a third party not directly involved in the transaction, such as pollution.
Sustainability
The capacity to meet present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
Resilience
The capacity of a community or system to withstand, adapt to, and recover from disruptive environmental or health shocks.
Mitigation
Policy or action aimed at reducing the severity or future occurrence of an environmental hazard, such as reducing greenhouse gas emissions.
Adaptation
Policy or action aimed at reducing the harm caused by an environmental hazard that cannot be entirely prevented.
Public health
The field concerned with protecting and improving the health of populations through disease prevention, health education, and policy.
Epidemiology
The study of the distribution, patterns, and causes of disease within populations.
Pandemic
An epidemic of infectious disease that has spread across multiple countries or continents, affecting a large number of people.
Herd immunity
A form of indirect protection from infectious disease that occurs when a sufficient percentage of a population becomes immune, reducing overall disease spread.
Green Revolution
The mid-twentieth-century transformation of global agriculture through high-yield crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and irrigation, dramatically increasing food production.
Ozone layer
A region of the stratosphere containing high concentrations of ozone that shields Earth's surface from harmful ultraviolet radiation.
Sea-level rise
The increase in global average ocean levels caused primarily by melting ice sheets and glaciers and the thermal expansion of warming seawater.
Environmental justice
A framework holding that all people deserve fair treatment regarding environmental laws and policy, and that environmental harms should not fall disproportionately on marginalized communities.
Sustainable development
A development framework, articulated in the 1987 Brundtland Report, seeking to reconcile economic growth with environmental protection and intergenerational fairness.
Common but differentiated responsibilities
A principle in international environmental law holding that all nations share responsibility for addressing global environmental problems, but that wealthy, historically high-emitting nations bear greater responsibility than poorer nations.
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)
The international scientific body established in 1988 to assess and synthesize climate science for policymakers.
Montreal Protocol
The 1987 international treaty phasing out ozone-depleting substances, widely regarded as the most successful environmental agreement in history.
Paris Agreement
The 2015 international climate accord in which nearly all nations committed to nationally determined emissions targets aimed at limiting global temperature rise.
Global health governance
The system of international institutions, agreements, and cooperative mechanisms that coordinate public health responses across national borders.
Ecological footprint
A measure of the land and resources required to sustain a given population's consumption patterns.
Climate security
A policy framework recognizing that environmental stress can contribute to displacement, resource conflict, and political instability.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it addresses challenges that are not confined to the past: climate change, environmental degradation, and public health crises remain active, unresolved forces shaping the world students will inherit and help determine. Unlike many topics in this textbook, whose consequences are settled and available for historical judgment at a comfortable distance, the environmental and public health history examined here continues to unfold, making careful historical understanding not merely an academic exercise but a practical necessity for informed citizenship.

This chapter has shaped, and will continue to shape, world history in fundamental ways. The choices societies make about energy, resource consumption, international cooperation, and public health investment in the coming decades will determine the severity of climate change's consequences, the resilience of vulnerable communities and nations, and the world's capacity to respond to future disease threats. These choices are, in the deepest sense, historical choices, shaped by the institutions, inequalities, and patterns of cooperation and conflict that earlier chapters of this textbook have traced across thousands of years of human history.

This chapter reveals a pattern that recurs throughout world history: the mismatch between where consequences fall and where power and responsibility reside. From the unequal impact of empire and colonization examined in earlier chapters to the unequal impact of climate change and pandemic disease examined here, this textbook has repeatedly shown that global systems — whether trade networks, empires, or carbon emissions — distribute benefits and burdens unevenly, often along lines shaped by long histories of economic and political power.

Yet this chapter also reveals genuine grounds for historical hope, carefully qualified. The eradication of smallpox, the recovery of the ozone layer following the Montreal Protocol, the rapid development of COVID-19 vaccines, and the declining cost of renewable energy all demonstrate that coordinated human effort, guided by scientific understanding and sustained political will, can address problems of genuinely global scale. Understanding both the achievements and the shortcomings documented in this chapter equips students to engage thoughtfully, and with appropriate historical humility, in the ongoing efforts to address the environmental and public health challenges of their own time.

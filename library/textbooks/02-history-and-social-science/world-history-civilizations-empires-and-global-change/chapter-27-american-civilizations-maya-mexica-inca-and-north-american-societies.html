American Civilizations: Maya, Mexica, Inca, and North American Societies

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"Below him spread a metropolis of canals, markets, and white-plastered plazas unlike anything he had seen in Europe — one of the largest cities in the world, the culmination of thousands of years of American civilization."

In the summer of 1519, a Spanish soldier named Bernal Díaz del Castillo climbed to the top of a great temple in a city he had never imagined possible. Below him spread a metropolis of canals, markets, and white-plastered plazas unlike anything he had seen in Europe. Tenochtitlan, the capital of the Mexica empire, sat on an island in a high-altitude lake, connected to the shores by long causeways wide enough for ten horsemen to ride abreast. From the temple's summit, Díaz could see thousands of people moving through the great market of Tlatelolco, trading cacao beans, cotton cloth, jade ornaments, dried fish, ceramic vessels, obsidian blades, and feathered capes. He later wrote that he did not know how to describe what he saw, because nothing in Spain, or in any of the lands he had traveled, compared to it. He and his companions stood speechless, overwhelmed.

Tenochtitlan was, at that moment, one of the largest cities in the world. With a population estimated between 200,000 and 300,000 people, it rivaled Constantinople, Beijing, and Cairo in scale. But Tenochtitlan was not an accident. It was the culmination of thousands of years of urban development, agricultural innovation, state formation, religious life, and political organization in the Americas. By the time Spanish soldiers arrived in the early sixteenth century, the Western Hemisphere was home to hundreds of millions of people living in societies of extraordinary variety and complexity: imperial capitals and small farming villages, dense urban cores and dispersed fishing communities, highly organized bureaucratic empires and confederated networks of autonomous towns, societies marked by profound inequality and others shaped by principles of communal obligation and reciprocal exchange.

This chapter examines the major civilizations and societies of the Americas in the centuries before European conquest, focusing particularly on the period from roughly 900 CE to 1500 CE, though understanding these societies requires reaching much further back in time. Four major traditions anchor the chapter. In Mesoamerica — the region encompassing southern Mexico and northern Central America — the Maya continued to build and govern independent city-states long after the decline of their Classic-era capitals, while the Mexica, often called the Aztecs, constructed a vast tributary empire centered on Tenochtitlan. In South America, the Inca built the largest empire in the pre-Columbian Americas, stretching more than four thousand kilometers along the Andes and the Pacific coast, held together by an extraordinary network of roads, storehouses, and state administration. And across North America — in the Mississippi River valley, in the desert Southwest, in the woodlands of the eastern continent, on the Great Plains, and along the Pacific shore — Indigenous peoples developed sophisticated societies adapted to an enormous range of environments, connected by long-distance trade, shared ritual traditions, and regional exchange networks.

These societies did not exist in isolation from one another. Mesoamerica and the Andes were connected, however indirectly, to the rest of the hemisphere through long-distance trade in luxury goods, the diffusion of crops and agricultural techniques, the spread of religious ideas, and the movement of peoples over centuries and millennia. Obsidian from Central Mexico has been found in sites across North America. Turquoise from the American Southwest reached Mesoamerican markets. Corn, or maize, domesticated in what is now southern Mexico thousands of years earlier, transformed societies across most of the continent. The hemisphere, though vast, was not a collection of hermetically sealed worlds. It was a web of connections, some dense and frequent, others thin and occasional, but real nonetheless.

Understanding the Americas before 1500 requires historians to draw on a wide variety of evidence. Written records exist for Mesoamerica — the Maya developed one of the most sophisticated writing systems in the ancient world, and Mexica scribes produced pictorial manuscripts called codices that recorded tribute lists, calendrical information, genealogies, and cosmological knowledge. But for many Indigenous societies, the primary record is archaeological: the physical remains of cities, agricultural systems, ceremonial mounds, pottery, tools, jewelry, and food remains. Oral traditions, preserved by Indigenous communities and recorded in the years after European contact, offer additional windows into the past. Colonial-era documents produced by Spanish administrators, missionaries, and Indigenous authors themselves provide crucial, if often biased, information about the societies the conquistadors encountered. Reading this evidence carefully — weighing its origins, purposes, limitations, and silences — is essential to understanding the Americas on their own terms.

Historians and archaeologists have sometimes struggled against a legacy of distortion. For centuries, European and Euro-American scholarship underestimated the complexity of pre-Columbian American societies, dismissed Indigenous achievements, or treated the Americas as a world frozen in time until Columbus arrived. That view was wrong, and the evidence accumulated over the past century has demolished it thoroughly. The Americas before 1500 were home to some of the most impressive urban complexes, agricultural systems, imperial organizations, astronomical knowledge, artistic traditions, and engineering achievements in human history. Understanding this history is not only an act of scholarly recovery — it is essential for understanding the world as it was on the eve of the most consequential encounter in human history.

The chapter closes, as the era itself did, on the edge of catastrophe. The civilizations examined here — the Maya, the Mexica, the Inca, the Mississippian chiefdoms, the Pueblo peoples — were not static or timeless. They were dynamic, changing societies, embedded in their own political conflicts, ecological constraints, religious transformations, and historical trajectories. But within a century of European contact, epidemic disease, conquest, forced labor, ecological disruption, and colonial domination would kill the vast majority of the hemisphere's Indigenous population and dismantle or transform virtually every political system described in this chapter. Knowing what existed before is not simply background. It is a measure of what was lost.

Historical Context

The civilizations examined in this chapter were the products of thousands of years of development. The deep foundation of American civilization was agriculture, and the single most transformative crop was maize, or corn, domesticated in the Balsas River valley of southwestern Mexico approximately nine thousand years ago from a wild grass called teosinte. The development of maize was not a single event but a long process of human selection, guided over millennia by farmers who saved seeds from the most productive plants and gradually developed the large-eared grain that became the caloric foundation of most of the hemisphere. Maize cultivation spread northward and southward over thousands of years, reaching eastern North America by roughly 800 CE and transforming societies wherever it arrived, enabling larger populations, more sedentary settlement, and greater social complexity.

The Americas also saw the independent development of state-level societies in at least two major regions: Mesoamerica and the Andes. By the first millennium BCE, complex societies with monumental architecture, long-distance trade, and social hierarchies had emerged in both regions. In Mesoamerica, the Olmec civilization, centered on the Gulf Coast lowlands, laid important cultural foundations that later societies drew upon: monumental stone sculpture, a symbolic repertoire associated with rulership and religious authority, and trade networks connecting distant regions. In the Andes, the Chavín cult spread a religious art style and associated ritual practices across a wide area, suggesting the power of shared ideology to bind together otherwise independent communities.

By the first millennium CE, these foundations had generated the Classic-era civilizations of Mesoamerica — including Teotihuacan, which may have reached a population of 125,000 and whose influence extended across much of Mesoamerica, and the Classic Maya, who built dozens of independent city-states across the Yucatán Peninsula, Petén, and highland Guatemala. In the Andes, the Wari and Tiwanaku states developed complex administrative systems during roughly the same period, providing precedents for the later Inca empire. The Classic period in both regions ended with disruption around 800–900 CE in Mesoamerica and somewhat later in the Andes, but the knowledge, agricultural systems, artistic traditions, and social institutions developed during the Classic period were not lost — they formed the foundation upon which the Postclassic civilizations, including the Mexica and the Inca, were built.

North of Mesoamerica, the continent was home to an extraordinary diversity of societies. In the Mississippi River valley, complex chiefdoms built large earthen mounds beginning around 800 CE, culminating in Cahokia — near present-day St. Louis — which reached its peak population of perhaps 15,000 to 20,000 people between 1050 and 1200 CE, making it the largest city north of Mexico before European contact. In the American Southwest, Ancestral Pueblo peoples built multi-story stone and adobe structures, engaged in long-distance trade in turquoise and other luxury goods, and developed sophisticated water management systems in a semiarid environment. Across the Great Plains, Eastern Woodlands, the Pacific Northwest, the Arctic, and countless other regions, Indigenous peoples adapted to their specific environments, developing trade networks, ceremonial traditions, political organizations, and ways of life that were as sophisticated and historically dynamic as any society on earth.

By 1500, the Americas were home to somewhere between 50 and 100 million people — historians and demographers continue to debate the precise figure — organized into hundreds of distinct political entities, speaking perhaps 500 or more distinct languages, and living in environments ranging from arctic tundra to tropical rainforest to high-altitude desert. The chapter that follows cannot do justice to the full complexity of this world. It focuses on several major societies and traditions while acknowledging the vast diversity that remains beyond its scope.

Regional Orientation

Mesoamerica covers a geographically diverse region from the Valley of Mexico in the north to the Mayan lowlands and highlands in the south. It is not a single environment but a mosaic: highland valleys with cool, fertile soils suitable for intensive agriculture; tropical lowlands supporting cacao, cotton, and rubber; coastal zones with access to marine resources and long-distance sea trade. The Valley of Mexico, where Tenochtitlan was built, sits at roughly 2,200 meters above sea level and was, in the pre-Columbian era, surrounded by a network of five interconnected lakes covering roughly 1,000 square kilometers — an extraordinary agricultural and logistical resource.

The Andes and Pacific coast of South America present a radically different geography. The Andes form one of the world's great mountain ranges, running roughly 7,000 kilometers along the western spine of South America, with peaks exceeding 6,000 meters. The region is organized into distinct ecological zones stacked vertically — the hot, dry coastal strip; the warm, temperate valleys; the high, cold puna grasslands; and the tropical eastern slopes descending toward the Amazon basin. This vertical ecology was one of the most important features of Andean civilization: no single zone could produce all the foods and materials that human communities needed, which created powerful incentives for exchange and integration across altitude zones.

North America defies brief description. It encompasses the Mississippi River drainage basin, one of the most extensive and fertile river systems in the world; the semi-arid grasslands of the Great Plains; the desert Southwest; the Pacific Coast with its extraordinary marine resources; the Great Lakes region and the eastern woodlands, heavily forested and watered; and the boreal forests and tundra of the far north. Each of these environments supported distinct adaptations. Agricultural societies in the Mississippi valley, the Southwest, and parts of the eastern woodlands developed sedentary or semi-sedentary communities. Hunter-gatherers across the plains, the Great Basin, the Northwest Coast, and other regions developed sophisticated technologies and social institutions suited to their specific landscapes.

Section 27.1

The Americas Before 1500

The Americas in 1500 were not a world on the eve of discovery. They were a world already ancient, deeply inhabited, and continuously transformed by millennia of human activity. The first people to inhabit the Western Hemisphere — the ancestors of all Indigenous American peoples — crossed from Siberia into what is now Alaska during the last Ice Age, when lower sea levels exposed the land bridge known as Beringia. Recent genetic and archaeological evidence suggests this migration involved multiple waves of movement, with groups following different routes southward — along the Pacific coast, through interior ice-free corridors, and possibly by sea — over a period of thousands of years beginning perhaps 20,000 or more years ago. By 10,000 BCE, humans had reached the southern tip of South America.

What followed was thousands of years of divergent development. As human communities spread across the hemisphere's extraordinary range of environments — arctic tundra, boreal forest, temperate grassland, desert, tropical rainforest, high mountain plateau, coastal estuary — they developed distinct technologies, social organizations, subsistence strategies, religious traditions, and languages. The diversity of the Americas in 1500 was, in part, the product of this long separation: communities adapted to their local environments over many generations, developing expertise, knowledge, and culture specific to the places they inhabited.

But diversity did not mean isolation. Even across great distances, Indigenous communities maintained connections through trade networks, migration, intermarriage, and the diffusion of ideas, crops, and technologies. The spread of maize agriculture is the most dramatic example. Originating in what is now the Mexican state of Guerrero, maize cultivation spread northward and southward over thousands of years, transforming societies as it traveled. By the first millennium CE, maize was being cultivated across much of North America — in the Southwest, in the Mississippi River valley, in the eastern woodlands, and eventually in the northern Great Lakes region. The arrival of maize typically allowed larger and more concentrated populations, supported more complex social hierarchies, and provided the caloric surplus that sustained ceremonial life, long-distance trade, and political organization. It was, in a very real sense, the crop that made the Americas of 1500 possible.

The Americas in 1500 were also home to a remarkable range of political forms. At one end of the scale were small, mobile bands of hunter-gatherers — the peoples of the Great Basin, the subarctic, and other regions where agriculture was not viable — who lived in small kinship groups and moved seasonally with their resources. At the other extreme were the Mexica empire and the Inca empire: large, hierarchical, expansionist states with professional armies, full-time administrative personnel, formal tribute systems, and ideological claims to universal sovereignty. Between these poles lay an enormous range of intermediate forms: confederacies of towns, hereditary chiefdoms, agricultural villages governed by councils of elders, trade-connected urban centers, and ceremonial complexes that drew together communities from wide regions without creating permanent political hierarchies.

Understanding this diversity is important not only for its own sake but for what it reveals about the conditions under which complex societies emerge and change. The Americas provide some of the most vivid evidence in world history for the independent invention of agriculture, cities, writing, and empire — proof that these developments were not unique products of a single "civilizational" tradition but represent widespread human responses to certain kinds of social, ecological, and political pressures. In this sense, the history of the Americas before 1500 is not a footnote to world history. It is one of its central chapters.

Section 27.2

Maya Continuities and City-States

The Maya civilization that greeted the Spanish conquistadors in the early sixteenth century was the surviving branch of one of the most sophisticated intellectual traditions in the ancient world. The Classic Maya — whose civilization flourished between roughly 250 CE and 900 CE — had built dozens of independent city-states across the lowlands of the Yucatán Peninsula, the Petén region of Guatemala, and the highland valleys of Chiapas and Guatemala. At their height, cities like Tikal, Palenque, Calakmul, Copán, and Caracol supported populations of tens of thousands, built monumental temple-pyramids and royal palaces, maintained scribal schools that produced detailed historical records, and engaged in complex networks of trade, diplomacy, and warfare.

The Maya script — one of the few fully developed writing systems in the pre-Columbian Americas — could express the full range of the Mayan languages, recording dates, genealogies, historical narratives, ritual instructions, and astronomical observations. Maya astronomers tracked the movements of Venus and other planets with extraordinary precision, developing calendrical systems of remarkable sophistication. The Maya Long Count calendar recorded dates from a fixed starting point, equivalent to August 11, 3114 BCE in the modern Gregorian calendar, allowing scribes to place historical events in a universal chronological framework. Maya mathematicians independently invented zero and used positional notation — developments that parallel similar advances in South Asia.

Between roughly 800 and 900 CE, the great lowland cities experienced what historians have called the "Classic Maya Collapse" — a dramatic decline in population, the cessation of monument construction, and the abandonment of many major centers. The causes remain debated, but most scholars now point to a combination of factors: prolonged drought, which disrupted agriculture in a region dependent on seasonal rainfall; intense political competition and warfare among city-states, which drained resources and disrupted trade; and perhaps soil degradation resulting from centuries of intensive agriculture. The collapse was not simultaneous across all regions — the northern Yucatán continued to flourish long after the southern lowlands were abandoned — and it was not a total end of Maya civilization. It was a contraction and reorganization.

The Postclassic Maya (c. 900–1519 CE) rebuilt and maintained a rich civilization across the northern Yucatán and the highlands of Guatemala. Cities like Chichen Itza, Uxmal, and later Mayapán became major centers of political power, religious life, and long-distance trade. Chichen Itza is particularly significant because it shows evidence of strong connections to central Mexican traditions — including the imagery of feathered serpents associated with the deity Quetzalcoatl, skull racks called tzompantli, and new architectural styles. Whether this represents actual migration and conquest or the adoption of prestigious central Mexican symbols by Maya rulers remains debated, but it illustrates the interconnected nature of Mesoamerican cultural exchange.

In the Postclassic period, the political landscape of the Yucatán was shaped by leagues and rivalries among independent city-states. After Chichen Itza's decline around 1200 CE, the city of Mayapán became the dominant power for about two centuries, until its own collapse around 1441 CE following an internal revolt. After Mayapán's fall, the Yucatán fragmented into more than a dozen independent polities, each governed by a hereditary lord supported by a hierarchy of priests, warriors, and merchants. In the highlands of Guatemala, the Quiche Maya kingdom became the most powerful Postclassic state, eventually subduing many of its neighbors before itself being conquered by Spanish forces in the 1520s.

Maya religion in the Postclassic period continued the Classic traditions of a complex polytheistic cosmology, ritual bloodletting by rulers and priests, astronomical observation, and the sacred ballgame. The Maya conceived of time as cyclical, with creation and destruction repeating in great cosmic cycles. Their gods were numerous and often merged with aspects of the natural world — the rain deity Chac, the maize god Hun Hunahpu, the death gods of Xibalba. The famous Popol Vuh, the K'iche' Maya creation narrative recorded in the colonial period, preserves elements of this cosmological tradition, describing the creation of humanity from maize, the adventures of the Hero Twins, and the establishment of the current world order.

Trade remained central to Maya life in the Postclassic period. Coastal trade routes around the Yucatán Peninsula carried cacao, salt, copper bells, obsidian blades, jade, cotton cloth, feathers, and dried fish. Cozumel Island became a major pilgrimage and trading center. Maya merchants traveled by large canoe through Caribbean waters. The great achievement of the Maya is not simply their monuments or their astronomical knowledge, though both are remarkable. It is the persistence and adaptability of a civilization that survived, transformed, and continued to create across two millennia, outlasting the collapse of its greatest cities, absorbing outside influences while maintaining its distinctive culture. More than six million people speak Mayan languages today, and Maya communities maintain cultural traditions with roots stretching back thousands of years.

Historical Vocabulary
Tribute Empire

A tribute empire is a political system in which a dominant power demands regular payments of goods, labor, or services from conquered or subordinate communities but does not necessarily govern those communities through direct administrative control. The subject communities may retain their existing rulers, laws, and social structures, provided they deliver the required tribute and acknowledge the supremacy of the dominant power.

The Mexica empire is one of the clearest examples of a tribute empire in world history. Subject communities throughout central Mexico delivered cotton cloth, cacao, jade, feathers, foodstuffs, and dozens of other commodities to Tenochtitlan on a regular schedule. Tribute empires differ from territorial empires, which govern conquered areas directly through imposed administrators and laws. The distinction matters because tribute empires tend to be more vulnerable to revolt — subject communities retain the organizational capacity to resist — while territorial empires are more deeply transformative of the societies they govern. Understanding what kind of imperial system is at work in a given historical context is essential for understanding how empires maintained power and why they eventually fragmented.

Section 27.3

Mexica Expansion and Tenochtitlan

The Mexica — the people the Spanish called Aztecs — arrived in the Valley of Mexico in the late thirteenth century as a relatively minor group, a semi-nomadic people from a legendary northern homeland called Aztlan. They were, by their own account, a poor and marginalized community, unwelcome among the established peoples of the valley and forced to settle on the swampy, inhospitable shores and islands of Lake Texcoco. According to their origin narrative, they had been guided by their patron deity Huitzilopochtli, the solar and war god, who promised them they would become the rulers of the world. The sign of their destiny, their priests said, would be an eagle perched on a cactus devouring a serpent. In 1325, on a small island in the lake where they reportedly found this sign, they founded Tenochtitlan.

For the next century, the Mexica were subordinate to the more powerful Tepanec kingdom of Azcapotzalco, fighting as mercenaries in the wars of their overlords and gradually accumulating military experience, political connections, and regional knowledge. The turning point came in 1428, when Tenochtitlan, allied with the neighboring cities of Texcoco and Tlacopan, overthrew Azcapotzalco in a decisive war. The Triple Alliance became the governing coalition of the Valley of Mexico and the foundation of what historians call the Mexica or Aztec empire.

The empire that emerged was not, in the traditional sense, a territorially administered state. The Mexica did not, in most cases, install garrisons, governors, or direct administrative systems in the territories they conquered. Instead, they operated through what scholars call a tributary empire: defeated polities were required to pay regular tribute in goods — cotton cloth, cacao, jade, feathers, gold, foodstuffs, rubber, and hundreds of other commodities — but were otherwise left to govern themselves under their existing rulers, provided those rulers acknowledged Mexica supremacy and delivered their tribute on time. The Codex Mendoza, compiled shortly after the Spanish conquest, records the tribute due from thirty-eight provincial areas, listing hundreds of categories of goods paid annually or semiannually to Tenochtitlan.

The city of Tenochtitlan itself was a marvel of engineering and urban planning. Built on an island and expanded through the construction of chinampas — rectangular artificial islands made by piling mud and vegetation in the shallow lake — the city was connected to the mainland by three great causeways, each wide enough for multiple lanes of foot traffic. An aqueduct brought fresh water from the mainland springs at Chapultepec. A great dike stretched across the lake to protect the chinampas from flooding. At the city's center stood the great ceremonial precinct containing more than seventy structures, including the Templo Mayor — a dual pyramid dedicated to Huitzilopochtli and the rain god Tlaloc — ball courts, skull racks, and temples to dozens of deities.

The market of Tlatelolco, Tenochtitlan's twin city to the north, astonished every Spanish observer. Hernán Cortés wrote to King Charles I of Spain that it was larger than any market he had seen in Spain, and that everything grown, made, or sold in the entire land could be found there. Bernal Díaz estimated sixty thousand people gathered in the market each day. Judges circulated to resolve disputes. Goods were organized by category in designated sections. Currency — primarily cacao beans, standardized lengths of cotton cloth, and copper axes — facilitated exchange. The market connected Tenochtitlan to the vast productive hinterland of the empire, drawing in goods from tropical coasts, highland forests, distant mines, and agricultural valleys hundreds of kilometers away.

Mexica society was hierarchically organized. At the top was the ruler, the huey tlatoani ("great speaker"), whose authority was both political and religious. Below him were the pipiltin, the noble class, who held administrative and military offices and were educated in elite schools called calmecac. The macehualtin, the commoners, were organized into calpulli — kinship-based community groups that held collective rights to agricultural land and shared obligations to the state, including labor service and military participation. The expansion of Mexica power was rapid and relentless after 1428. By the reign of Ahuitzotl (1486–1502), the empire reached its greatest territorial extent, incorporating most of central Mexico from the Gulf Coast to the Pacific.

But the empire also had significant weaknesses. Its reliance on tribute rather than direct administration meant that many subject peoples had strong incentives to defect to any power that could challenge Mexica dominance. The Tlaxcalans, a powerful confederation east of Tenochtitlan who had successfully resisted Mexica conquest, became crucial allies for Hernán Cortés in 1519. The empire's extraction of enormous quantities of tribute and its requirement for war captives for sacrifice generated deep resentment in many subject communities. The political genius of Cortés — and of the Indigenous leaders who allied with him — was in part the ability to channel this existing resentment into a coalition against Mexica power.

Section 27.4

Tribute, Religion, and Political Power in Mesoamerica

The Mexica imperial system was held together not only by military force and tribute extraction but by a powerful religious ideology that located Tenochtitlan at the center of the cosmos and the Mexica ruler as the earthly representative of divine authority. To understand how this system worked — and why it was both powerful and fragile — requires grasping the deep connections between religion, sacrifice, and political legitimacy in Mesoamerican thought.

At the heart of Mexica religion was a vision of the cosmos as fundamentally unstable, maintained in existence only through continuous human effort and sacrifice. The Mexica believed that the current world — the Fifth Sun — had been created by the self-sacrifice of the gods at Teotihuacan, where divine beings had thrown themselves into a cosmic fire to become the sun and moon. But the sun's movement across the sky was not guaranteed. It required constant nourishment with the most precious substance available to humanity: human blood. The sacrifice of war captives — and in some contexts, of Mexica nobles themselves — was thus not gratuitous cruelty but a cosmic necessity, a ritual act that sustained the universe and ensured the continued existence of humanity.

This theology served important political functions. The constant need for sacrificial victims drove Mexica military expansion, since captives taken in war were the primary source of sacrificial offerings. Warfare was thus sanctified as a religious duty, not merely a political or economic activity. The Flowery Wars (xochiyaoyotl) — formalized conflicts with neighboring peoples like the Tlaxcalans — were partly organized as ongoing sources of captives for sacrifice, though historians debate whether this was their primary purpose. The Templo Mayor, rebuilt and enlarged by successive Mexica rulers, was the physical focus of this sacrificial religion: its twin shrines to Huitzilopochtli and Tlaloc were the destination of captives brought from across the empire to be sacrificed at moments of great ritual importance.

The religious calendar organized all of Mexica life. The solar calendar (xiuhpohualli) of 365 days was divided into eighteen months of twenty days each, plus five "empty" or unlucky days at the year's end. The ritual calendar (tonalpohualli) consisted of 260 days divided into thirteen twenty-day periods, each day associated with a particular deity, omen, and ritual prescription. The two calendars meshed together in a fifty-two-year cycle — a period that the Mexica regarded as the fundamental unit of cosmic time, at the end of which the world might potentially end if the gods were not properly honored. The New Fire ceremony, held at the end of each fifty-two-year cycle, was one of the most important rituals in Mexica life: all fires were extinguished, and the success of a new fire-drilling ceremony on the Hill of the Star determined whether the world would continue.

Priests played an essential role in maintaining the religious calendar and performing the rituals that kept the cosmos in order. The highest priestly offices were among the most powerful in the empire. Priests managed the great temple complexes, trained younger priests in the calmecac schools, preserved and interpreted the sacred books, and advised the ruler on matters of divine significance. The ruler himself was both a political and religious figure, performing key ceremonial duties, undertaking ritual bloodletting, and serving as the human bridge between the divine and mortal worlds.

Religion, tribute, and political power were thus deeply intertwined in the Mexica system. Tribute provided the material resources that sustained the city, funded the ritual calendar, and supplied the goods — cotton capes, feathered costumes, cacao — used in ceremonies. Military success brought captives for sacrifice and demonstrated divine favor. The great public spectacles of sacrifice and ritual reinforced the Mexica claim to cosmic authority and reminded subject peoples of the consequences of resistance. It was a system that had worked remarkably well for nearly a century — but it rested on a combination of coercion and ideology that could not survive the catastrophic disruption of European conquest.

Section 27.5

The Andes and Inca Statecraft

The empire the Inca called Tawantinsuyu — "The Four Quarters of the World" — was, at its height, the largest political entity the pre-Columbian Americas had ever seen. Stretching from what is now southern Colombia in the north to central Chile in the south, and from the Pacific coast in the west to the upper Amazon basin in the east, it encompassed an estimated 800,000 to 1,000,000 square kilometers and a population variously estimated between 6 and 12 million people. It governed hundreds of distinct ethnic groups speaking dozens of languages, organized around dozens of distinct ecological zones, across some of the most challenging terrain on earth. That this empire was built within roughly a century — from the reign of Pachacuti (c. 1438–1471) to the Spanish conquest of 1532 — makes it one of the most remarkable episodes of state formation in world history.

The Inca were originally a small ethnic group based in and around Cusco, a city in the southern Andean highlands of what is now Peru. Long before the great expansion, the Cusco region had been home to complex societies. The Wari and Tiwanaku states, which flourished between roughly 500 and 1000 CE, had developed sophisticated administrative techniques, road networks, and systems of resource redistribution that the Inca would later adapt and expand. After the decline of these earlier states, the Andes fragmented into competing regional polities. The Inca of Cusco were one of these polities — a local power of some significance, but not dramatically different from their neighbors — until the transformations of the mid-fifteenth century.

The ruler credited with transforming the Inca state was Pachacuti, whose name means "Earth-Shaker" or "Transformer of the World." According to Inca tradition, Pachacuti defeated the Chanka confederation, which had threatened to destroy Cusco, in a dramatic military victory around 1438. This victory was understood as evidence of divine favor from Inti, the sun god, and launched a program of expansion, reorganization, and monumentalization that defined the Inca empire. Pachacuti rebuilt Cusco itself, transforming it from a regional center into an imperial capital laid out in the shape of a puma, with great plazas, palaces, and the Coricancha — the Temple of the Sun — at its center, its walls sheathed in gold.

The Inca expansion relied on a combination of military conquest, strategic marriage alliances, and the careful deployment of prestige and generosity as political tools. The Sapa Inca — the divine ruler, considered the son of the sun — could offer newly conquered elites the prospect of incorporation into the imperial hierarchy: their sons educated at Cusco, their daughters enrolled as Acllacuna (Chosen Women), their communities supplied from imperial storehouses in times of famine, their rulers confirmed in position and given fine cloth and other prestigious goods. For communities that resisted, the response was military force — and in some cases, the wholesale replacement of local populations with loyal settlers (mitmaqkuna) relocated from elsewhere in the empire.

Unlike the Mexica, the Inca did not operate primarily through a tributary system. The Inca state did not, for the most part, demand goods from subject communities. Instead, it operated through a labor tax system called the mit'a. All able-bodied adults in the empire owed the state a certain period of labor each year — working on construction projects, farming state lands, serving in the army, weaving textiles, mining, carrying goods, or performing other services. In return, the state fed and clothed workers during their service, using the resources stored in thousands of storehouses (qollqas) distributed throughout the empire. This system was conceptually framed not as exploitation but as a form of reciprocal exchange: the state provided tools, food, cloth, and chicha (fermented corn beer) to laborers; workers provided their time and effort to the state.

The ideology of Inca rule was deeply religious. The Sapa Inca was not merely a political ruler but a divine figure, the child of Inti the sun god, whose person was sacred and whose commands carried cosmic authority. The mummies of deceased Sapa Incas — treated as living royal presences, consulted in major ceremonies, fed and clothed by their descendants organized into panacas (royal lineages) — wielded ongoing political influence even after death. The great Inca festivals, particularly Inti Raymi (Festival of the Sun) and Capacocha (a ceremony involving sacrifice in times of great crisis or transition), structured the ritual calendar of the empire and reinforced the sacred nature of Inca rule.

Historical Vocabulary
Mit'a

Mit'a (also spelled mita) was the Inca system of rotational labor tribute by which all able-bodied adults in the empire owed periodic labor service to the state. Rather than paying tribute in goods, subject communities paid in labor: working on state farms, constructing roads and buildings, serving in the army, weaving textiles, mining, or carrying goods along the royal roads. Workers were typically organized into rotational groups so that no individual or community bore the burden continuously.

The state's obligation in the mit'a system was to feed, clothe, and provide tools for workers during their service. The enormous network of state storehouses distributed throughout the empire existed primarily to supply this labor force. In principle, the mit'a was framed as a form of reciprocal exchange rather than simple exploitation. After the Spanish conquest, the colonial government adopted and transformed the mit'a system, using it to compel Indigenous labor in silver mines — particularly the famous Potosí mines — under far more brutal conditions and without the reciprocal obligations that had characterized the Inca version. The colonial mit'a became one of the most devastating instruments of exploitation in the Americas, illustrating how colonial powers could repurpose Indigenous institutions for their own ends.

Section 27.6

Roads, Quipu, and Imperial Administration

The Inca empire was impossible without its roads. Stretching more than 40,000 kilometers across the Andes and the Pacific coast, the Inca road system — the Qhapaq Ñan, or "Royal Road" — was one of the great engineering achievements of the pre-Columbian world. Roads ran along the spine of the Andes through high mountain passes and across valleys, connected by suspension bridges woven from grass and fiber, some spanning more than 60 meters across gorges. A second coastal road ran along the Pacific shore. Lateral roads connected the two main routes and extended into the most remote corners of the empire. Way-stations (tambos) located at roughly a day's journey intervals provided lodging, food, and supplies for travelers. Relay runners (chasquis) could carry messages the length of the empire in days.

The roads were not open to everyone. Movement within the Inca empire was regulated by the state. The roads were used primarily by state officials, armies, laborers in mit'a service, and the relay runners who carried messages and small goods. Llamas — the primary pack animal of the Andes, capable of carrying loads of 30–50 kilograms — transported heavier goods along the roads under the management of state caravans. The road system thus served not as general infrastructure for commerce but primarily as the circulatory system of the state itself, enabling administrative communication, military movement, and the redistribution of resources.

The administrative challenges of governing an empire of this scale and complexity were formidable. The Inca lacked a written script in the conventional sense — there is no Inca equivalent of cuneiform, hieroglyphics, or Maya glyphs. Instead, they used the quipu (or khipu), a device consisting of a main cord from which hang numerous subsidiary cords of different colors, tied with knots in various positions and configurations. Quipu could certainly record numerical information — census data, tribute accounts, storage inventories, and astronomical records have all been identified in surviving quipu — and they were maintained by trained specialists called quipucamayoc ("quipu keepers"). Some researchers have argued that quipu could also encode narrative information, functioning as a form of writing, but this remains a matter of active scholarly debate.

The empire was organized administratively into four suyus (quarters) radiating from Cusco: Chinchaysuyu (northwest), Antisuyu (northeast), Collasuyu (southwest), and Kuntisuyu (west). Each suyu was divided into provinces, and provinces into smaller units. Provincial governors — often members of the Inca royal lineage or local rulers confirmed in power by the empire — administered their territories and transmitted tribute and labor obligations upward to Cusco. The decimal system was used to organize the labor obligations of subject communities: units of ten, fifty, one hundred, five hundred, and one thousand households were each assigned a supervisor responsible for ensuring compliance with mit'a obligations.

The mitmaqkuna policy — the forced resettlement of conquered communities — was a crucial tool of Inca imperial administration. When a region was conquered, some of its population might be relocated to other parts of the empire — particularly to newly conquered areas where loyal populations were needed, or to areas that required particular labor skills. Conversely, loyal Inca-speaking communities from the heartland might be settled in newly conquered territories to spread the Quechua language, Inca religion, and imperial customs. Over time, this policy transformed the linguistic and ethnic landscape of the Andes, spreading Quechua far beyond its original homeland. Today, Quechua is spoken by approximately 8–10 million people across Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, and neighboring countries — the direct linguistic legacy of this policy.

The Acllacuna — "Chosen Women" selected from subject communities for their beauty, skill, and social status — were another distinctive feature of Inca administration. These women were brought to Cusco and other centers, where they were trained in weaving the finest textiles (qompi), brewing chicha, and performing ritual service. The finest textiles they produced were among the most valued goods in the Inca economy, used as gifts to reward loyal elites, offerings to the gods, and burial goods for deceased rulers.

Section 27.7

Mississippian Societies and Cahokia

One of the most extraordinary cities in pre-Columbian North America stood not in the subtropical valleys of Mesoamerica or the high Andes but in the flood plain of the Mississippi River, just east of where the Missouri River flows into the Mississippi near present-day St. Louis. Cahokia — known to archaeologists by the name of a later, unrelated Indigenous group — was, at its height between roughly 1050 and 1200 CE, the largest city north of Mexico. Recent estimates of its peak population range from 10,000 to perhaps 20,000 people, with a broader regional population of 30,000 to 50,000 within a day's travel. Nothing of its scale would exist again in what is now the United States until the nineteenth century.

Cahokia was the center of what archaeologists call the Mississippian culture, a widespread tradition that emerged across the American Midwest and Southeast beginning around 800 CE and persisting until European contact and beyond. Mississippian culture is defined by several diagnostic features: intensive maize agriculture, platform mound construction, the development of chiefdom-level political organization, long-distance trade in prestige goods, and a distinctive symbolic repertoire associated with what scholars call the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex (SECC). This complex of symbols — including winged human figures, raptor imagery, bi-lobed arrows, and other motifs — appears on copper plates, shell gorgets, ceramic vessels, and other objects across a vast area from Wisconsin to Florida and from Oklahoma to the Carolinas, suggesting the existence of shared ritual traditions and elite exchange networks across much of eastern North America.

The city of Cahokia itself was organized around a central plaza flanked by dozens of earthen mounds, the largest of which — Monks Mound — remains today the largest pre-Columbian earthwork in North America. Rising approximately 30 meters above the surrounding plain and covering an area of about 6.5 hectares at its base, Monks Mound was built in stages over roughly two centuries, requiring millions of baskets of soil carried by hand. A large wooden building on its summit dominated the city's skyline. The central precinct of Cahokia was enclosed by a stockade of wooden posts. Around it spread residential neighborhoods, smaller mound complexes, plazas, and the farms and gardens that fed the city's population.

The social organization of Cahokia was hierarchical. Excavations of burial mounds at the site — particularly the richly furnished Mound 72 — reveal dramatic differences in wealth and status. Mound 72 contained the burials of high-status individuals accompanied by thousands of shell beads, large caches of finely worked projectile points, and the remains of more than 250 individuals who appear to have been sacrificed to accompany an elite individual in death. The scale of sacrifice evident at Cahokia — and its parallels with sacrificial practices in Mesoamerica — has led some scholars to suggest direct cultural connections between Cahokia and Mesoamerican civilizations. Most archaeologists are cautious about positing direct connection, preferring to see these parallels as independent developments arising from similar social pressures, but the question remains live in scholarly debate.

After about 1200 CE, Cahokia began to decline. The population fell, the great building projects ceased, and the stockade around the central precinct was reinforced — suggesting increasing social tension or external threat. By 1350 CE, the site had been largely abandoned. The reasons for Cahokia's decline are debated but likely involve a combination of factors: environmental degradation from the clearing of forests; political instability; perhaps conflict with neighboring polities; and possibly climatic shifts associated with the beginning of the Little Ice Age, which reduced agricultural productivity across the region.

The Mississippian tradition continued long after Cahokia's decline, however. Other Mississippian centers — including Moundville in Alabama, Etowah in Georgia, and Spiro in Oklahoma — flourished during the thirteenth through fifteenth centuries, serving as regional political and ceremonial centers. The societies encountered by Spanish explorers in the southeastern United States in the sixteenth century — the Chickasaw, Cherokee, Choctaw, Natchez, and others — were, in many cases, descendants of Mississippian chiefdoms, though the great epidemics that preceded and accompanied European colonization had already devastated many of these societies before they were directly observed by Europeans.

Historical Vocabulary
Chiefdom

A chiefdom is a form of political organization intermediate between a tribe and a state. In a chiefdom, one individual — the chief — exercises authority over multiple communities, typically based on a combination of hereditary status, control of resources, religious authority, and military power. Unlike states, chiefdoms generally lack permanent bureaucracies, written laws, and standing armies; unlike tribes or bands, they have recognized hierarchical authority extending beyond the immediate kin group.

Mississippian societies, including Cahokia, are typically classified as chiefdoms, though scholars prefer the term "complex chiefdom" for the larger and more hierarchical Mississippian polities. The concept is useful because it captures the intermediate character of these societies — more hierarchical and centralized than simple kin-based communities, but lacking the full organizational apparatus of states. However, the term can also obscure the diversity of actual political forms. Mississippian chiefdoms varied enormously in scale, stability, and internal organization, and the term should be understood as a rough category rather than a precise description of any single society.

Section 27.8

Pueblo, Woodland, and Other North American Communities

North America beyond the Mississippi valley and the Southwest was home to an extraordinary diversity of Indigenous peoples and cultures. Any survey necessarily misses the vast majority of them; what follows is a selective overview of several major traditions, intended to convey the breadth and sophistication of North American Indigenous life before European contact.

The Ancestral Puebloans

In the semi-arid Colorado Plateau and the valleys of the upper Rio Grande, a farming tradition developed over centuries into one of the most distinctive archaeological cultures in North America. The people archaeologists call the Ancestral Puebloans — often referred to by the Navajo term "Anasazi," though many Pueblo people today prefer "Ancestral Puebloan" — occupied a vast region of the American Southwest, developing a culture characterized by multi-story stone and adobe structures, elaborate painted pottery, sophisticated water management, and distinctive kiva (ceremonial chamber) architecture.

The great center of Ancestral Pueblo civilization was Chaco Canyon in what is now northwestern New Mexico. Between roughly 850 and 1150 CE, Chaco Canyon became the hub of a regional network connecting communities across a 60,000-square-kilometer area. The canyon itself contained at least twelve "great houses" — massive, precisely constructed multi-story buildings with hundreds of rooms, many apparently used for storage or ritual rather than habitation. Pueblo Bonito, the largest of these, contained perhaps 650 rooms and stood four stories high at its maximum. The construction of these buildings required the importation of enormous quantities of timber — estimated at 200,000 logs — carried on human backs from forests more than 60 kilometers away.

Chaco functioned as a center of redistribution and ceremony for the surrounding region. Goods flowed into Chaco from great distances: turquoise from mines in the Cerrillos Hills of New Mexico, copper bells from western Mexico, macaw feathers and live macaws from tropical regions to the south, shell from both the Pacific and Gulf coasts. Roads — wide, straight, precisely engineered paths that ran for dozens of kilometers across the landscape — connected Chaco to satellite communities called "outliers." These roads ran straight up cliffs and across obstacles in ways that suggest their purpose was ceremonial rather than practical, perhaps related to the movement of processions or pilgrimages. Around 1150 CE, the population of Chaco Canyon declined dramatically, apparently in connection with a prolonged drought. Communities dispersed, and new centers emerged at Mesa Verde and other locations. Around 1300 CE, Mesa Verde was also abandoned, and the descendants of the Ancestral Puebloans moved southward and eastward to the Rio Grande valley and the Hopi mesas, where their descendants continue to live today.

Eastern Woodlands Societies

East of the Mississippi and south of the boreal forests, the Eastern Woodlands were home to diverse agricultural and hunter-gatherer societies. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy — a league of five (later six) nations: the Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca, later joined by the Tuscarora — is perhaps the most famous political organization in the pre-European eastern woodlands. According to Haudenosaunee tradition, the Great League of Peace was founded by the Peacemaker and his disciples Hiawatha and Jigonsaseh, who persuaded five warring nations to set aside their conflicts and create a confederacy governed by a Great Council of fifty chiefs. While the exact date of the confederacy's founding is debated — estimates range from the twelfth to the late fifteenth century — the institution was clearly functioning well before European contact and would play a crucial role in the colonial history of North America.

The peoples of the Great Lakes region, the Ohio River valley, and the mid-Atlantic coast also developed complex societies based on a combination of agriculture (primarily corn, beans, and squash — the "Three Sisters"), hunting, fishing, and gathering. Many lived in large villages of longhouses or wigwams, organized around clans and governed by councils of elders. Long-distance trade networks moved copper from the Great Lakes, shell from the coasts, and other goods across the region.

Plains, Pacific Coast, and Other Regions

The Great Plains — the vast grasslands stretching from the Rocky Mountains to the Mississippi valley — were home in the pre-contact period to a mixed economy of farming and hunting. The notion of the Plains as an exclusively nomadic buffalo-hunting culture is a product of the post-contact era, when the introduction of horses transformed Plains economies in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Before horses, Plains peoples lived in settled villages along the river valleys, growing corn and other crops while hunting bison on foot. Communities like the Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara built substantial earth-lodge villages on the upper Missouri River and engaged in trade with both agricultural and hunting peoples across a wide region.

On the Pacific Coast, societies from California to the Pacific Northwest developed extraordinarily rich cultures based not primarily on agriculture but on the exploitation of marine and riverine resources — particularly salmon. Northwest Coast peoples like the Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, and Kwakwaka'wakw developed complex social hierarchies, elaborate artistic traditions, large plank-built longhouses, and the potlatch ceremony — a feast of redistribution in which high-status individuals gave away great quantities of goods to demonstrate their generosity and wealth. The carved wooden objects and totem poles of the Northwest Coast represent some of the most powerful visual art traditions in the world.

Section 27.9

Diversity and Complexity in the Americas

One of the most important conclusions that emerges from the study of the pre-Columbian Americas is that complexity — social stratification, political organization, intellectual achievement, technological sophistication — took many forms and did not require any single organizational template. The temptation to rank civilizations on a scale of "development," with sedentary agricultural empires at the top and nomadic hunter-gatherers at the bottom, fundamentally distorts the historical reality of the Americas. Hunter-gatherers on the Northwest Coast of North America developed among the most elaborate artistic and ceremonial traditions in the hemisphere, supported by the extraordinary productivity of Pacific salmon runs. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy created a political institution of remarkable sophistication without writing or a market economy. The Maya carried on a living intellectual tradition of astronomy, mathematics, and historical writing that continued for centuries after the decline of their greatest cities.

The diversity of the Americas also means that generalizations must be made carefully. The Mexica and the Inca were both empires, but they operated through fundamentally different mechanisms — tribute collection versus labor mobilization — and rested on different political ideologies and cultural foundations. Mississippian chiefdoms and Ancestral Pueblo communities were both agricultural societies based on maize, but they responded to environmental stress, organized political authority, and conducted long-distance exchange in distinctly different ways. North American societies east of the Mississippi were organized around kin groups and consensus governance in ways that would prove deeply influential in the political thought of European colonists, who encountered — and often misrepresented — these traditions in the centuries after contact.

The pre-Columbian Americas were also not static. The Mexica empire was barely a century old when the Spanish arrived; the Inca empire was perhaps ninety years old in its mature form. Cahokia had risen and fallen centuries before European contact. Maya cities had been abandoned and reoccupied across a thousand years of political competition. Great migrations had carried peoples across the continent — the ancestral Athabascan speakers, for example, had migrated from the subarctic to the Southwest relatively recently, becoming the ancestors of the Navajo and Apache. Political systems rose and fell, agricultural systems expanded and contracted with climate change, trade networks connected and disconnected regions over time. The Americas before 1500 were a world of change, adaptation, and continuous historical dynamism — not a frozen pre-history awaiting European arrival.

The scale of what was destroyed in the century after 1492 is almost impossible to comprehend. Epidemic disease — smallpox, measles, influenza, and dozens of other Old World pathogens to which the Americas had no prior exposure — killed the vast majority of the hemisphere's Indigenous population within a century or two of sustained contact. Some estimates suggest that the population of the Americas fell by 50 to 90 percent between 1500 and 1600 — perhaps the greatest demographic catastrophe in human history. Political systems were dismantled, religious traditions suppressed, languages lost, ecological knowledge forgotten. What the Spanish and other European powers encountered and then destroyed was not a "primitive" world but a mature one, with millennia of history behind it, centuries of accumulated knowledge, and millions of lives fully and richly lived.

Section 27.10

Chapter Review and Indigenous Americas Checkpoint

This chapter has surveyed the major civilizations and societies of the Americas in the centuries before European conquest, focusing on four principal traditions: the Maya city-states of Mesoamerica, the Mexica tributary empire, the Inca administrative empire of the Andes, and the diverse Indigenous societies of North America from the Mississippian heartland to the Pacific Northwest. Each of these traditions demonstrated the capacity of human communities to develop complex, adaptive, and historically dynamic responses to their specific environments, challenges, and opportunities.

Students completing this chapter should be able to explain the mechanisms by which the Mexica and Inca empires were constructed and administered, trace the continuities and transformations of Maya civilization across the Classic-to-Postclassic transition, describe the major North American societies examined in sections 27.7 and 27.8, and analyze the role of agriculture, trade, religion, and environment in shaping the diversity of pre-Columbian American life. The review materials that follow — including key terms, study questions, writing prompts, and the research project — provide structured opportunities to consolidate and deepen this understanding.

Regional Lens — Cusco and the Sacred Landscape of the Andes

To understand the Inca empire, one must begin at Cusco, the capital that the Inca understood as the navel of the world (qosqo means "navel" in Quechua). Cusco was not merely an administrative center; it was a sacred landscape, the physical embodiment of Inca cosmology, and the point from which all imperial geography radiated outward. The city was rebuilt by Pachacuti in the mid-fifteenth century. Its streets were laid out in the shape of a puma — a sacred animal associated with royal power. At the city's heart stood the Coricancha, the Temple of the Sun, whose walls were sheathed in gold and whose gardens contained life-sized golden replicas of maize, llamas, and other sacred objects.

The landscape around Cusco was also sacralized through a complex geography of huacas — sacred places where the divine was immanent in the natural world. Mountains, springs, rivers, caves, distinctive rocks, and the mummies of deceased rulers were all huacas, sites where offerings were made and prayers uttered. The highest peaks of the Andes were among the most sacred huacas in the empire; the Capacocha ceremony involved the sacrifice of children on mountain summits across the empire at times of great crisis or royal transition. The frozen bodies of Capacocha victims, remarkably preserved by the cold, have been recovered from Andean peaks in the late twentieth century, providing direct physical evidence of this practice.

Cusco also served as the center of an extraordinary redistribution economy. Goods from across the empire — textiles, food, metals, feathers, coca leaves — flowed into Cusco's storehouses and were redistributed outward as gifts to allied elites, supplies to the army, rations for mit'a workers, and offerings to the gods. The Sapa Inca's generosity — his ability to give lavishly — was both a demonstration of his divine power and a crucial political tool, binding loyal subjects to the imperial center through obligations of gratitude and reciprocity.

Global Connection — Mesoamerica and the World: Isolation, Contact, and Comparative Perspective

For most of the pre-Columbian period, the Americas were effectively isolated from the other major centers of civilization in Afro-Eurasia. There is no credible evidence of sustained contact between the Americas and Europe, Africa, or Asia before Norse voyages to the North Atlantic around 1000 CE and European exploration after 1492. This isolation means that the civilizations of the Americas developed their agriculture, cities, writing, and empire independently — without the cross-cultural borrowing that played such a large role in the development of Old World civilizations.

This independence makes the Americas an extraordinary natural experiment in the conditions under which complex civilization emerges. The fact that agriculture, cities, writing, and imperial organization all developed in the Americas independently of their development in Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and China strongly suggests that these developments reflect widespread human responses to particular kinds of social and ecological conditions, rather than being the unique product of any single tradition. The convergences are striking: river valley agriculture, hierarchical urbanism, monumental architecture dedicated to religious purposes, tributary imperial organization, and astronomical calendrical systems all appear in both Old World and New, developed independently by societies with no knowledge of each other.

There are also important differences that reflect the specific ecological and technological contexts of the Americas. The lack of large domesticable animals — the Americas had no horses, cattle, donkeys, or oxen until European introduction — meant that American transportation was entirely human-powered, profoundly affecting the economics of long-distance trade and military logistics. The absence of iron meant that American metalworking, though sophisticated in gold, silver, copper, and bronze, did not develop iron technology. These differences and similarities together reveal something important about the universality of certain aspects of human social development and the profound contingency of others.

Empire Study — The Mexica (Aztec) Empire

Origins: The Mexica arrived in the Valley of Mexico in the late thirteenth century as a minor, marginalized group. They served as mercenaries for the Tepanec kingdom before overthrowing their overlords in 1428 and founding the Triple Alliance with Texcoco and Tlacopan.

Expansion: The empire expanded rapidly after 1428, driven by military conquest, tribute collection, and the ideological imperatives of Huitzilopochtli-centered warfare. Under Ahuitzotl (1486–1502), the empire reached its greatest territorial extent, encompassing most of central Mexico.

Administration: The Mexica operated primarily through a tributary system. Conquered polities delivered regular tribute to Tenochtitlan in goods but were largely self-governing. Imperial agents (calpixque) oversaw tribute collection. The empire did not impose a uniform law or administrative structure on subject peoples.

Military power: The Mexica military was organized around warrior fraternities (Eagle and Jaguar warriors), with military achievement — measured by captives taken — the primary path to social advancement for commoners.

Religious ideology: Mexica imperial power was legitimized by the ideology of cosmic obligation: human sacrifice was necessary to feed the sun and maintain the universe. Warfare was thus divinely mandated. The Templo Mayor, rebuilt by each successive ruler, physically embodied the empire's cosmic claims.

Decline and transformation: The Mexica empire was conquered by a Spanish-led coalition including thousands of Indigenous allies (primarily Tlaxcalans) between 1519 and 1521. Epidemic disease, the destruction of Tenochtitlan, and the imposition of colonial rule transformed the empire into the foundation of New Spain.

Legacy: The Mexica legacy is immense: Mexico City was built on the ruins of Tenochtitlan; the Nahuatl language became the most widely spoken Indigenous language of Mexico; Mexica cultural traditions persist in countless ways to the present day.

Map Study — The Americas Before 1500: Major Civilizations and Trade Routes

A map of the Americas around 1500 CE reveals several major patterns of geographic, political, and cultural organization.

Mesoamerica appears as a dense concentration of political entities, with the Mexica empire dominating central Mexico and the Maya city-states occupying the Yucatán Peninsula and highland Guatemala. The Valley of Mexico — enclosed by mountains and centered on its network of lakes — is clearly visible as the core of Mexica power.

The Andes and Pacific coast show the enormous north-south extent of the Inca empire, with the Qhapaq Ñan road system binding together territories from Ecuador to Chile. The extreme vertical zonation of the Andes — from coastal desert through mountain valleys to high-altitude puna — is visible in the stacked ecological zones that defined Andean civilization.

North America reveals the major environmental zones — the Mississippi drainage basin, the Southwest, the Pacific Northwest, the Great Plains — each home to distinct cultural traditions. Trade routes moving copper from the Great Lakes, turquoise from the Southwest, and shell from the coasts across the continent illustrate the connectivity of otherwise politically independent regions.

Primary Source Window

The Codex Mendoza — Mexica Tribute Records (c. 1541)

Creator: Indigenous scribes under direction of Spanish Viceroy Antonio de Mendoza · Date: c. 1541 · Type: Pictorial administrative manuscript (codex) with Spanish glosses

Context: The Codex Mendoza is a pictorial manuscript compiled shortly after the Spanish conquest of Mexico, probably around 1541, on the orders of New Spain's first viceroy. Its purpose was to inform the Spanish crown about the nature of Mexica society and governance. It was compiled by Indigenous scribes using traditional Mexica pictographic conventions, with Spanish glosses added by a Spanish interpreter. The Codex consists of three parts: a history of Mexica rulers and conquests; a tribute list organized by province; and a section depicting the Mexica life cycle from birth to death.

The tribute section of the Codex Mendoza lists the goods owed annually or semi-annually by thirty-eight provincial areas of the empire. One provincial entry, for the Tochtepec province of the Gulf Coast lowlands, records an obligation to deliver each year 16,000 rubber balls, 24,000 bundles of incense, 1,600 large feather-decorated warrior costumes, 200 loads of cacao, 200 loads of gold dust, and large numbers of jade beads and feathered jewels. Multiplied across all thirty-eight provinces, the total annual tribute flowing into Tenochtitlan was staggering in scale and variety.

Analysis: The Codex Mendoza is an invaluable source for understanding the mechanics of Mexica imperial extraction. It reveals the scale and specificity of tribute demands, showing how different regions were required to supply different goods based on their local productive capacity. The Gulf Coast lowlands supplied tropical luxury goods (cacao, rubber, feathers); highland regions supplied cotton cloth and foodstuffs; mineral-rich areas supplied gold and other metals. The document also reveals something about the administrative capacity of the Mexica state — the ability to track, record, and collect these specific obligations from dozens of distinct provinces required sophisticated bookkeeping and a network of tribute collectors. However, the document was compiled after the conquest, for a Spanish audience, by scribes working under colonial conditions. It may not perfectly represent the pre-conquest tribute system, and it tells us almost nothing about how subject communities themselves experienced and responded to tribute demands.

Evidence Window — The Templo Mayor and Archaeological Excavation in Mexico City

In 1978, workers from Mexico City's electric company, digging underground near the main plaza of the city's historic center, struck a large stone disk nearly 3.5 meters in diameter. When archaeologists were called to the site, they identified it as an image of Coyolxauhqui — the moon goddess who, in Mexica mythology, was dismembered by her brother Huitzilopochtli at the moment of his birth. The discovery led to a decade of excavations that uncovered the Templo Mayor — the great pyramid-temple at the center of Tenochtitlan — buried beneath the streets and buildings of colonial Mexico City.

The Templo Mayor excavations, directed by archaeologist Eduardo Matos Moctezuma, revealed seven successive construction phases of the pyramid, each built over the previous one as successive Mexica rulers enlarged the temple in a pattern common to Mesoamerican ceremonial architecture. The excavations also uncovered more than 100 ritual deposits buried in the pyramid's fill and in chambers within its structure, containing thousands of objects: coral, seashells, fish bones, jade ornaments, stone figures, copper objects, obsidian knives, ceramic vessels, and the skeletal remains of both humans and animals. Many of the objects came from distant regions — marine life from both the Pacific and Atlantic coasts, jade from Guatemala — demonstrating the reach of Mexica trade networks and the power of Tenochtitlan to draw resources from across the empire for its great ritual offerings.

Limits of the evidence: The archaeological record provides physical evidence of ritual practices but cannot directly tell us what participants in these rituals believed, felt, or understood. The correlation between archaeological deposits and textual descriptions of Mexica religion is suggestive but requires careful interpretation. Much of what we believe about the meaning of the deposits comes from colonial-era texts written decades after the conquest, which may not accurately represent pre-conquest beliefs. And the excavation of only a portion of the temple complex means that our picture remains incomplete — much of the Templo Mayor and its surrounding structures remain beneath the streets of Mexico City.

Historical Debate

How Should Historians Interpret Mexica Human Sacrifice?

The question: Human sacrifice was a central institution of Mexica religious and political life. The archaeological and textual evidence for it is overwhelming — sacrificed human remains have been found in Templo Mayor deposits, and dozens of Spanish and Indigenous colonial sources describe the practice in detail. But historians have long debated how to interpret it: What was its scale? What was its purpose? How should it be understood in comparative perspective?

Interpretation A — Political and Ideological Instrument: Some historians emphasize the political functions of human sacrifice in the Mexica imperial system. This view argues that sacrifice was a deliberate instrument of state power — a means of terrorizing subject peoples, demonstrating Mexica divine favor, and creating a perpetual justification for military expansion. In this reading, the scale of sacrifice was partly performative and political rather than purely theological. The massive sacrifices at the dedication of the rebuilt Templo Mayor in 1487 — described as involving thousands of captives — can be understood as theatrical demonstrations of imperial power designed to awe and intimidate both subjects and enemies.
Interpretation B — Genuine Religious Belief: Other historians emphasize that Mexica sacrifice reflected genuine and deeply held theological convictions about the nature of the cosmos and humanity's obligations to the gods. In this view, reducing sacrifice to a political instrument misses its core character as religious obligation. The Mexica genuinely believed that the sun required nourishment with human blood to continue its daily journey. This interpretation takes Mexica cosmology seriously on its own terms rather than explaining it away as political manipulation. It also notes that captives, in Mexica belief, were understood as stand-ins for the gods themselves — that sacrifice was in some sense a sacred honor rather than simply an act of violence.

Evidence: Both interpretations draw on the same body of evidence — Spanish colonial accounts, Indigenous codices and oral histories, and archaeological finds. The political and the theological were not, in Mexica culture, separate categories, and most historians acknowledge that both dimensions were real.

Why it matters: How historians interpret Mexica sacrifice affects our understanding of Mexica imperial power and its internal dynamics. It raises broader questions about how historians should approach religious practices that are deeply foreign or morally challenging to modern observers — the danger of both imposing modern ethical standards uncritically and of romanticizing or relativizing practices that caused real suffering.

Comparison Across Societies — The Mexica and Inca Empires

Political structure: The Mexica operated primarily through a tributary system, leaving conquered polities largely self-governing in exchange for tribute payments in goods. The Inca, by contrast, governed through direct administrative intervention — appointing provincial governors, relocating populations, imposing the Quechua language, and organizing subject labor through the mit'a system. The Mexica system was lighter but less transformative; the Inca system was heavier but more deeply integrative.

Economy: The Mexica economy was substantially market-based. The great market of Tlatelolco served as the hub of a complex commercial network, and cacao beans and cloth served as currencies. Merchants — the pochteca — formed a hereditary caste with distinctive rights and obligations. The Inca economy, by contrast, operated primarily through state redistribution rather than market exchange. There was no money and no market in the Tlatelolco sense; goods moved through the state's redistribution network, from producers to storehouses to recipients.

Religious ideology: Both empires used religion to legitimize imperial power, but through different theologies. The Mexica solar religion — in which the universe required constant nourishment with human blood, and warfare was a cosmic obligation — created a perpetual engine of military expansion. The Inca religion — centered on Inti the sun god, whose divine son the Sapa Inca embodied on earth — emphasized the sacred nature of the ruler and the reciprocal obligations between state and subjects.

Geography and logistics: The Mexica operated in a relatively compact region centered on the Valley of Mexico. The Inca governed a much more geographically challenging empire — the Andes presented enormous logistical obstacles — which required the investment in roads, storehouses, and relay runners that became the empire's defining infrastructure.

Legacy: Both empires left profound legacies. The Mexica capital of Tenochtitlan became the site of Mexico City; Nahuatl became the most widely spoken Indigenous language in Mexico. The Inca road network survived in parts and was used by the Spanish; the Quechua language spread by Inca policy is spoken by millions today.

Multiple Perspectives — The Mexica Empire from Below
From the perspective of Mexica warriors and nobles: For Mexica pipiltin (nobles) and successful warriors, the empire was a source of wealth, status, and meaning. Military success — measured by the capture of enemy warriors rather than their killing — was the primary path to social advancement for commoners and the source of noble identity. The elaborate warrior costumes, the rights to wear certain colors, the access to luxury goods, and the honors of military rank all depended on battlefield success. The ideology of Huitzilopochtli-centered warfare made the warrior's life cosmically significant, not merely materially rewarding.
From the perspective of commoners in Tenochtitlan: For ordinary macehualtin living in the capital, the empire provided real benefits: food security through the redistribution of tribute, access to the extraordinary variety of goods in the Tlatelolco market, the spectacle and meaning of the great festivals, and the pride of living in the world's greatest city as they understood it. But commoners also bore the burdens of the system: military service, labor obligations, the threat of sacrifice in times of necessity, and the constant demands of the ritual calendar.
From the perspective of subject peoples: For people in communities conquered by the Mexica, the empire was primarily a system of extraction. Regular tribute obligations — cotton cloth, foodstuffs, luxury goods — drained resources from local economies. When tribute demands increased or Mexica officials behaved abusively, there was little recourse. The requirement to supply young men for military campaigns imposed additional burdens. Many communities maintained the memory of their conquest and awaited the opportunity for change.
From the perspective of the Tlaxcalans: The Tlaxcalans, who had successfully resisted Mexica conquest and maintained their independence through decades of conflict, experienced the empire as an existential threat. Surrounded on most sides by Mexica-controlled territory and subject to trade blockades, the Tlaxcalans developed a fierce sense of independent identity defined in large part by opposition to Mexica dominance. When Spanish forces arrived, Tlaxcalan leaders quickly grasped the opportunity to use these powerful newcomers against their enemies — a calculation that proved decisive in the conquest of Tenochtitlan.
Cause and Effect — The Classic Maya Collapse

Long-Term Causes

Intensive agriculture in the lowland tropics dependent on seasonal rainfall; centuries of deforestation altering local hydrology; chronic political competition among competing city-states draining resources; population growth pressing against agricultural limits; soil degradation from intensive cultivation.

Short-Term Causes

A series of severe multi-year droughts during the ninth century CE, identified through paleoclimatic research using lake sediments and speleothems; these droughts struck at the critical moment when political competition was already intense and agricultural systems were already stressed.

The Event

Between roughly 800 and 900 CE, most of the great lowland Maya cities ceased construction, experienced dramatic population decline, and were eventually abandoned. The cessation of Long Count date inscriptions at one site after another provides archaeological evidence of the timing. The population of the southern lowlands may have fallen by 50 to 90 percent over the course of the ninth century.

Long-Term Effects

Disruption of trade networks; redistribution of population to the northern Yucatán; rise of new centers like Chichen Itza and Mayapán; continuation of Maya intellectual and cultural traditions in new political forms; preservation of Maya languages and cultural practices to the present day.

Historical complexity: The Maya Collapse was not a single event with a single cause. It was a regional process spread over decades, varying in intensity from place to place. Some sites were abandoned earlier, some later; some recovered partially while others never did. Understanding the collapse as the product of multiple interacting causes — an example of compounding crises — is essential to historical explanation.

Continuity and Change Over Time — Maya Civilization, 800–1500 CE

What Changed

  • The great lowland cities were depopulated over the ninth century — Tikal, Palenque, Copán, and Caracol were effectively abandoned
  • The political system of competing hereditary dynasties recording their history in carved stone monuments disappeared from the southern lowlands
  • Centers of political power shifted northward to the Yucatán (Chichen Itza, Mayapán) and to the Guatemalan highlands
  • New political forms emerged — leagues and confederacies rather than classic dynastic monarchy

What Stayed the Same

  • Maya writing survived — the Dresden, Madrid, and Paris codices continue the intellectual traditions of the Classic inscriptions
  • The Maya calendrical system — both the 365-day solar calendar and the 260-day ritual calendar — continued in use throughout the Postclassic and into the colonial period
  • The ballgame, ritual bloodletting, the veneration of ancestors, the cultivation of corn as a sacred crop, and distinctive artistic traditions all showed continuity
  • The Maya language family survived — spoken today by more than six million people in Mexico and Guatemala

Why the pattern matters: The Maya case illustrates an important historical principle: the collapse of political systems does not mean the end of civilizations. The people, the languages, the knowledge, and many of the cultural traditions of Classic Maya civilization survived the political collapse of their major cities. This is a different kind of resilience — the resilience of culture, embedded in communities and transmitted through families, ritual traditions, and oral knowledge across generations.

Everyday Life — A Commoner Family in Tenochtitlan

In the mid-fifteenth century, a commoner family in Tenochtitlan — a husband, wife, and several children — would have lived in a one-story adobe house in one of the city's residential districts, organized within a calpulli that held collective rights to a plot of land, possibly including chinampas on the lake's edge. Their house was modest — a main room for sleeping and family life, a small kitchen area, and perhaps a temescal (sweat bath) behind — but it opened onto a shared courtyard with neighboring families.

The woman of the household spent a great deal of her time at the grinding stone (metate), processing dried maize kernels into flour for tortillas. Making tortillas was labor-intensive work, beginning before dawn and consuming several hours each day. Women were also responsible for weaving cotton cloth — an activity that began in childhood and continued throughout life. Every Mexica woman was expected to be a competent weaver; cotton mantles were a primary form of currency in the Mexica economy and were owed as part of the calpulli's tribute obligations.

The male head of household had a range of possible occupations: he might be a farmer, an artisan, a porter, or a soldier. Male commoners were also subject to state labor obligations. Children attended local schools (telpochcalli, or "youth houses") run by the calpulli, where they learned practical skills, religious observances, history and traditions, and — for boys — military training. The family's diet was centered on maize — in the form of tortillas, tamales, and porridge — supplemented by beans, squash, chili peppers, tomatoes, amaranth, and a wide variety of vegetables and herbs available in the market. The daily meals were simple, nutritious, and based on a remarkably sophisticated agricultural system refined over thousands of years.

Environment and Technology — Chinampas and the Transformation of Lake Texcoco

The agricultural system that fed Tenochtitlan and sustained the Valley of Mexico's enormous population was one of the most productive and ingenious in the pre-Columbian world. At its heart were the chinampas — artificial garden islands constructed in the shallow lake beds of the Valley of Mexico, produced by layering mud, decaying vegetation, and lake sediments into raised rectangular platforms separated by narrow canals. Chinampas were extraordinarily productive. Because they could be planted and harvested multiple times a year, because they were never subject to drought (the canals kept them perpetually moist), and because the nutrient-rich lake mud could be continuously dredged from the canal bottoms and applied as fertilizer, chinampas produced yields per hectare that may have been among the highest in the pre-industrial world.

The construction and maintenance of chinampas required substantial collective labor — building and repairing the mud platforms, dredging the canals, managing the waterways, and regulating the level of the lake itself through dikes and sluices. This work was organized through the calpulli system, with collective labor obligations binding community members to the maintenance of the agricultural infrastructure. The great dike built by Nezahualcoyotl, the philosopher-king of Texcoco, across Lake Texcoco in the mid-fifteenth century — a massive engineering project stretching roughly 16 kilometers — was designed to separate the saline western portion of the lake from the fresh eastern portion, protecting the chinampas from saltwater intrusion. The chinampa system was thus as much a political achievement as an agricultural one: it required sustained collective organization and created incentives for political cooperation that partly explain the basin's unusual level of urbanization and political development.

Chapter Summary

The Americas before 1500 were home to a world of extraordinary diversity and complexity that deserves to be understood on its own terms, not as a prelude to European colonization or as a primitive stage in a universal developmental sequence. From the great Mexica imperial capital of Tenochtitlan, with its hundreds of thousands of inhabitants, its floating gardens and great market, its twin-pyramid temple and sacred canals, to the dispersed farming communities of the North American woodlands, the hemisphere's peoples had developed solutions to the challenges of human life that reflected millennia of adaptation, innovation, and historical experience.

The Maya, whose civilization reaches back more than two thousand years before the Spanish conquest, represent one of the great intellectual achievements of human history. Their writing system, fully expressive of spoken Mayan language, allowed them to record history, genealogy, astronomical observation, and religious knowledge across generations. Their calendrical achievements — the interlocking solar and ritual calendars, the Long Count system — reflect a sophisticated mathematical and astronomical tradition. The Classic Maya Collapse of the ninth century was not the end of Maya civilization but its transformation: the Postclassic Maya city-states of the Yucatán and the highland kingdoms of Guatemala continued to develop, trade, write, and build until the Spanish arrival, and Maya cultural traditions — including living languages spoken by millions of people — persist to the present day.

The Mexica empire was, in world-historical terms, a remarkable achievement of state formation. From their humble origins as migrants in the late thirteenth century to the creation of a tribute empire encompassing millions of people within a century, the Mexica demonstrated the speed with which new political orders can emerge under the right circumstances. The material base of this empire — the chinampas of Lake Texcoco, the tribute flowing in from dozens of provinces, the trade networks of the pochteca merchants — supported one of the world's great cities. The ideological system of Huitzilopochtli-centered solar religion, with its demand for continuous human sacrifice and its justification of endless military expansion, was both a source of enormous power and a generator of the resentments that would ultimately make the empire vulnerable to Spanish-led coalition forces.

The Inca empire was, by almost any measure, the most remarkable political achievement of the pre-Columbian Americas. Built within roughly ninety years, stretching more than four thousand kilometers along the spine of the Andes, governing millions of people across dozens of ecological zones and hundreds of distinct cultural groups, the empire functioned through an extraordinarily sophisticated system of road infrastructure, labor mobilization, state redistribution, and administrative record-keeping. The quipu, the relay runners, the thousands of storehouses that supplied armies and mit'a laborers — all reflect an administrative intelligence adapted to the specific challenges of Andean geography and ecology.

North America north of Mesoamerica was home to societies of great variety and sophistication. Cahokia, at its height around 1100 CE, was a city of perhaps 15,000 to 20,000 people at the hub of a regional exchange network connecting communities across much of the eastern half of the continent. Ancestral Pueblo communities in the Southwest developed complex water management systems, long-distance trade networks, and architectural traditions of remarkable sophistication in a challenging semi-arid environment. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy in the northeast developed a political institution of consensus governance that would later attract the attention of European colonists observing it.

Long-distance trade connected these diverse societies across the Americas in ways that are only beginning to be fully understood. Turquoise from the Southwest reached Mesoamerican markets. Copper from the Great Lakes moved through eastern North American exchange networks for thousands of years. Shell from both the Pacific and Atlantic coasts appeared in inland sites across the continent. Maize, the crop that transformed the hemisphere, spread from its Mexican homeland to virtually every agricultural society in the Americas over thousands of years of gradual diffusion.

The history examined in this chapter ended catastrophically. The arrival of European diseases — smallpox in particular — in the century after 1492 killed the majority of the hemisphere's Indigenous population in one of the greatest demographic catastrophes in recorded human history. Political systems were dismantled, cities burned, religious traditions suppressed, and millions of people reduced to colonial servitude. The loss was immeasurable — not only in human life but in knowledge, language, art, and historical memory. But the people, the languages, and many of the cultural traditions of the pre-Columbian Americas did not disappear entirely. Indigenous communities across the hemisphere survived conquest and colonialism, maintaining their identities, adapting their traditions, resisting cultural elimination, and continuing their histories into the present.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Acllacuna
"Chosen Women" selected by the Inca state from subject communities for weaving, brewing chicha, and ritual service; embodied the state's claim to control female labor and redistribute it for political purposes.
Altepetl
The basic Nahuatl political unit; a city-state or ethnic territory governed by a tlatoani (speaker/ruler); the fundamental unit of Mexica political organization below the imperial level.
Calpulli
Basic social unit of Mexica society; a kinship-based community group with collective rights to land and shared obligations to the state, including tribute and military service.
Capacocha
Inca state ceremony involving human sacrifice, typically of children, on mountain summits at moments of great crisis or royal transition; represents the state's claim to control the bodies of subjects.
Ceque system
Inca sacred geography of Cusco organized around 41 imaginary lines radiating from the Coricancha temple, along which more than 300 sacred sites (huacas) were located.
Chasquis
Inca relay runners stationed at intervals along the royal road network; capable of carrying messages or small goods across the length of the empire within days.
Chinampa
Artificial garden island constructed in the shallow lakes of the Valley of Mexico by layering mud, sediment, and vegetation; the agricultural foundation of Tenochtitlan's food supply.
Codex (codices)
Mesoamerican folded-paper or deer-skin manuscripts recording historical, genealogical, tribute, astronomical, and ritual information in pictorial and glyphic form.
Coricancha
The Inca Temple of the Sun in Cusco; center of Inca religious life; hub of the ceque system; its walls were reportedly sheathed in gold.
Flowery War (Xochiyaoyotl)
Formalized military conflicts between the Mexica and neighboring peoples, partly organized to obtain captives for sacrifice; whether their primary purpose was ritual or military is actively debated by historians.
Haudenosaunee
League of five (later six) Indigenous nations of the northeastern woodlands (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora); governed by a Great Council and principles of consensus.
Huaca
Andean sacred place or object in which the divine is immanent; includes mountains, springs, mummies, and distinctive rocks maintained by specific kin groups with associated ritual obligations.
Long Count Calendar
Maya calendrical system recording dates from a fixed starting point; capable of marking historical events across vast stretches of time; used on Classic-period stone monuments.
Mexica
The Nahuatl-speaking people who founded Tenochtitlan and led the Triple Alliance; often called "Aztecs" by historians, though this was not a name they used for themselves.
Mitmaqkuna
Inca policy of forced resettlement of populations from conquered regions to break resistance and spread Inca culture; also used to colonize newly conquered areas with loyal populations.
Mit'a
Inca labor tribute system requiring all able-bodied adults to contribute periodic labor service to the state in exchange for food, clothing, and tools during the period of service.
Mississippian culture
Archaeological tradition of the eastern North American interior (c. 800–1600 CE), characterized by platform mound construction, maize agriculture, chiefdom-level political organization, and the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex.
Panaca
Inca royal lineage descended from a deceased Sapa Inca; responsible for maintaining his mummy (treated as a living royal presence), estates, and ritual observances.
Pipiltin
Mexica noble class; held administrative and military offices, received tribute from commoners, and were educated in elite calmecac schools.
Pochteca
Professional long-distance merchants in Mexica society; a hereditary caste with distinctive rights, obligations, and patron deities; traveled to distant regions to obtain luxury goods.
Qhapaq Ñan
The Inca royal road system; more than 40,000 kilometers of roads connecting the empire from Ecuador to Chile; the primary infrastructure of state communication and military movement.
Quipu (khipu)
Andean knotted-string recording device; used by trained specialists to record numerical data, tribute accounts, census information, and possibly narrative content.
Sapa Inca
Title of the Inca ruler; considered the divine son of the sun god Inti; the sacred and political center of the empire whose commands carried cosmic as well as political authority.
Tawantinsuyu
The Inca name for their empire; means "The Four Quarters of the World" in Quechua; organized administratively into four suyus radiating from Cusco.
Templo Mayor
The great dual pyramid at the center of Tenochtitlan, dedicated to Huitzilopochtli (war and solar deity) and Tlaloc (rain deity); the ritual heart of the Mexica empire.
Triple Alliance
Political and military coalition of Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tlacopan formed c. 1428; the governing structure of the Mexica empire after the defeat of Azcapotzalco.
Vertical ecology
The practice of exploiting multiple altitude zones in the Andes to access diverse food and material resources; a defining feature of Andean economic organization and a structural incentive for integration.

Pachacuti (r. c. 1438–1471)

Chichen Itza
Major Maya city in the northern Yucatán; prominent in the early Postclassic period; shows strong connections to central Mexican cultural traditions including feathered serpent imagery.
Cahokia
Largest Mississippian city, near present-day St. Louis; peak population c. 15,000–20,000 around 1050–1200 CE; largest city north of Mexico in the pre-Columbian period.
Chaco Canyon
Hub of Ancestral Pueblo regional network in present-day New Mexico; contains multiple "great house" structures; center of long-distance trade and ceremonial activity c. 850–1150 CE.
Codex Mendoza

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 27 has examined some of the most significant civilizations and societies in the pre-Columbian Americas, from the Mexica and Inca empires to the Maya city-states, from the Mississippian mound-builders of Cahokia to the Ancestral Pueblo communities of the Southwest. The chapter belongs to the larger story of world history in Part V, which examines medieval and post-classical societies across the globe. Reading this chapter alongside chapters on Tang and Song China, the Islamic caliphates, medieval Europe, and African kingdoms reveals that the period between roughly 900 and 1500 CE was a time of extraordinary political and cultural achievement across the entire world — not only in Afro-Eurasia but in the Americas as well.

The most important pattern this chapter contributes to the larger textbook is the demonstration that complexity — urban civilization, state formation, long-distance trade, intellectual achievement — is not the property of any single region or tradition but a widespread human potential expressed in diverse forms across all inhabited continents. The Mexica and Inca empires were, in world-historical terms, as sophisticated and complex as the Ottoman or Ming empires of the same period. The Maya writing system was one of a small number of fully developed scripts in human history. Cahokia was, at its height, comparable in scale to many European cities of the same era. Recognizing this is not simply a matter of fairness or inclusion — though it is that — it is a prerequisite for understanding world history accurately.

The chapter also contributes the theme of independent development. Because the Americas were effectively isolated from Afro-Eurasia for most of their history, the development of agriculture, cities, writing, and empire in the Western Hemisphere happened without borrowing from Old World models. This makes the Americas an extraordinary test case for theories about why complexity emerges in human societies. The many parallels between Old World and New World development suggest that these developments reflect widespread human responses to certain kinds of social and ecological conditions. The differences — particularly the absence of large domesticable animals and iron technology — reveal how specific ecological contexts shape the particular forms that complexity takes.

The chapter closes, inevitably, on the threshold of catastrophe. The civilizations examined here would, within decades of the chapter's endpoint, be devastated by epidemic disease, military conquest, and colonial exploitation. The loss was one of the greatest in human history — measured not only in population but in knowledge, language, culture, and historical memory. But it was not a total loss. The descendants of the Maya still speak Mayan languages and maintain cultural traditions with roots in the pre-conquest world. The Quechua language, spread by Inca imperial policy, is spoken by millions of people today. Nahuatl, the language of the Mexica, remains a living language in Mexico. The history examined in this chapter is not only past — it is present, carried forward in communities, languages, and traditions that survived conquest and colonialism and continue into the contemporary world.

For students of world history, the Americas before 1500 offer a profound lesson about the diversity of human possibility and the contingency of historical outcomes. The world the Spanish encountered in 1519 was not inevitable — it was the product of specific historical processes, choices, and accidents of ecology and geography. Understanding it in its own terms, before the catastrophe of contact, is both a scholarly obligation and an act of historical justice. The peoples whose civilizations are described in this chapter were not simply waiting for history to happen to them; they were making history, in all its complexity and creativity, on their own terms.

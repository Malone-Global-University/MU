Part III — Classical Civilizations and Expanding States

Mesoamerica and the Andes in the Classical Era

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes arose without contact with the civilizations of Mesopotamia, Egypt, India, or China — yet they matched them in complexity, creativity, and the capacity to organize vast human energies toward shared purposes."

In the highland valley of Oaxaca in southern Mexico, sometime around 500 BCE, workers quarried stone from the hillsides and carried it upward to a flat-topped ridge where a new city was being built. The ridge had been artificially leveled through immense communal labor, creating a platform on which temples, plazas, tombs, and administrative buildings would stand for more than a thousand years. This city was Monte Albán, the ceremonial and political center of the Zapotec people, and its construction marked a turning point in the long history of Mesoamerican civilization. The architects who designed its central plaza aligned buildings toward astronomical reference points, embedding the cycles of sun, moon, and stars into the very geometry of the city. Priests and rulers who performed ceremonies in its plazas drew on calendrical knowledge that linked human time to cosmic time. Merchants who moved goods through its markets connected Oaxaca to distant regions. And the workers and farmers who sustained the city through their labor shaped one of the most sophisticated urban societies in the ancient Americas.

Monte Albán was not unique. Across Mesoamerica and the Andes, between roughly 300 BCE and 900 CE, human societies achieved remarkable levels of complexity, building cities, developing writing and mathematical systems, creating monumental architecture, sustaining long-distance trade networks, practicing elaborate religious traditions, and organizing political power over large populations. This was the classical era of American civilization, a period of cultural and political achievement whose scope rivals the great classical civilizations of the Mediterranean, South Asia, and East Asia.

This chapter examines the major classical-era civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes. In Mesoamerica — the region encompassing central and southern Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras, and parts of El Salvador — the Maya city-states, the great metropolis of Teotihuacan, and the Zapotec center of Monte Albán each represent distinct but interconnected traditions of urban life, political organization, and cultural creativity. In the Andes — the mountainous spine of western South America and adjacent coastal zones — the Moche and Nazca societies produced extraordinary art, engineered sophisticated irrigation systems, and built states that governed through religious authority and economic extraction. Each of these civilizations developed through its own historical pathway, shaped by local environments, social dynamics, and long-term cultural traditions. Yet they also participated in networks of exchange, influence, and sometimes conflict.

The civilizations examined in this chapter challenge familiar assumptions about the ancient world. The Maya, often described as one of the most intellectually sophisticated societies of antiquity, developed a fully phonetic writing system that recorded historical narratives, religious knowledge, and political authority. Their calendrical system, interlocking the 260-day ritual calendar with the 365-day solar calendar, produced calculations of extraordinary precision. Maya astronomers tracked the movements of Venus and the moon over centuries, embedding this knowledge in monuments and codices. Yet the Maya world was not a single empire; it was a mosaic of competing city-states linked by shared culture, trade, royal marriage, and periodic warfare. Understanding the Maya means understanding both their cultural unity and their political fragmentation.

Teotihuacan presents a different model of classical American urbanism. At its height around 400 to 500 CE, this city in the Valley of Mexico housed perhaps 125,000 to 200,000 people, making it one of the largest cities in the world. Its ceremonial core — the great Avenue of the Dead, the Pyramid of the Sun, the Pyramid of the Moon, the Citadel and Temple of the Feathered Serpent — represented a concentration of monumental architecture that awed visitors from across Mesoamerica. Yet Teotihuacan's political system remains poorly understood. Its rulers did not leave the kind of glorifying royal inscriptions that Maya kings did. Its residential districts housed merchants, artisans, and ethnic communities from distant regions, suggesting a cosmopolitan city that drew people from across the Mesoamerican world.

In the Andes, the Moche of the north coast of Peru built pyramid-temples and produced some of the most technically accomplished ceramics in world history. Their polychrome pots depicted warriors, sacrificial rituals, erotic scenes, animals, and supernatural beings with extraordinary realism and detail. The Nazca of the south coast etched enormous geometric lines and figures into the desert surface — the famous Nazca lines — that still provoke scholarly debate about their purpose and meaning. Both societies built their political authority on religious ritual, sacred geography, and the control of water and agricultural labor in one of the driest environments on Earth.

This chapter connects American classical civilization to the broader themes of world history: the formation of cities and states, the development of writing and record-keeping, the organization of labor and trade, the role of religion in sustaining political power, and the relationship between human communities and their environments. These themes connect Mesoamerica and the Andes to the great river valley civilizations of Mesopotamia, Egypt, India, and China studied in earlier chapters. At the same time, the civilizations examined here remind students that world history is not a single story with a single origin. Multiple traditions of human complexity arose independently in different parts of the globe, each shaped by distinct environmental, cultural, and social conditions.

Historical Context

The civilizations examined in this chapter did not emerge from a vacuum. They were the products of thousands of years of indigenous development in the Americas, built on foundations of agriculture, settled life, social complexity, and regional exchange that stretched back to the second and first millennia BCE. Understanding classical-era Mesoamerica and the Andes requires an appreciation of what came before.

The Agricultural Revolution came to the Americas independently of the Old World. In Mesoamerica, maize (corn) was domesticated from a wild grass called teosinte in the valleys of southwestern Mexico, probably around 7000 to 5000 BCE. Over millennia, farmers selected for larger ears, higher yields, and adaptability to different elevations and climates. Maize became the foundation of Mesoamerican agriculture, supplemented by beans, squash, chili peppers, cacao, avocados, and a wide range of other cultivated plants. This agricultural complex — the "Mesoamerican triad" of maize, beans, and squash — provided a nutritionally complete diet and supported increasing population densities. In the Andes, a separate agricultural revolution centered on the potato, quinoa, maize, and other highland crops, supplemented by the domestication of the llama and alpaca — the only large domesticated animals in the Americas — which provided wool, transport, and meat.

By the second millennium BCE, settled villages and small ceremonial centers had appeared across Mesoamerica. The Olmec civilization of the Gulf Coast lowlands, often called the "mother culture" of Mesoamerica, flourished between roughly 1200 and 400 BCE and established patterns of art, religion, monumental architecture, and long-distance trade that would influence later civilizations. Olmec colossal heads — massive stone portraits of rulers — demonstrate that political authority and artistic patronage were already powerful forces in this early period. The Olmec are covered in detail in Chapter 9; the classical civilizations of this chapter built upon and transformed this foundation.

In the Andes, early complex societies also developed before the classical era. The Chavín cult, centered on a religious and ceremonial complex at Chavín de Huántar in the Peruvian highlands around 900 to 200 BCE, spread distinctive religious iconography across a wide region of the Andes through what appears to have been a combination of pilgrimage, trade, and cultural prestige. Like the Olmec, the Chavín tradition established cultural patterns that later Andean civilizations built upon, transformed, or contested.

The transition to the classical era in Mesoamerica and the Andes — roughly from 300 BCE to 900 CE — involved several interrelated processes: population growth, agricultural intensification, the elaboration of monumental architecture and ceremonial centers, more complex political hierarchies, the expansion of long-distance trade, and in some regions, the emergence of writing and other recording systems. The period also saw significant environmental challenges. Droughts, floods, soil erosion, and depletion of agricultural land all posed threats to societies dependent on intensive farming. Climate variability in Mesoamerica, driven by the El Niño–Southern Oscillation, created periods of severe drought that could devastate agriculture and destabilize political systems. Understanding how classical American civilizations responded to environmental stress is an important part of understanding their history.

Regional Orientation

Mesoamerica is a cultural region, not simply a geographic one. It encompasses central and southern Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, and parts of Honduras and El Salvador — roughly the zone of pre-Columbian civilizations that shared certain cultural traits: the 260-day ritual calendar, a ballgame played with a rubber ball in stone courts, polytheistic religion centered on rain, maize, sun, and death, writing systems, and urban life organized around ceremonial plazas. Within this broad cultural area, geographic diversity is immense. The Gulf Coast lowlands are hot and humid; the central Mexican highlands are high, cool, and relatively dry; the southern Maya lowlands of Guatemala and the Yucatán Peninsula are tropical limestone platforms; the Pacific Coast and the highlands of Chiapas and Guatemala represent yet another distinct ecological zone.

The Andes and adjacent coastal zones present an even more dramatic environmental landscape. The Andes mountains rise along the western edge of South America in a series of ranges and high-altitude plateaus. The altiplano centered on Lake Titicaca sits at about 3,800 meters above sea level. West of the Andes lies the Pacific coast — one of the driest deserts in the world. East of the Andes lies the Amazon basin. The key to understanding Andean civilization is the concept of vertical ecology: the organization of human communities to exploit multiple altitudinal zones simultaneously. Because a short horizontal distance in the Andes involves dramatic changes in elevation, temperature, rainfall, and agricultural potential, Andean communities developed strategies for accessing resources from sea level to alpine zones.

Key geographic features for this chapter include: the Valley of Mexico, where Teotihuacan rose; the Valley of Oaxaca, home to Monte Albán; the Maya lowlands of the Petén in Guatemala; the north coast of Peru, where the Moche built their pyramids; the south coast of Peru, where the Nazca drew their famous lines; and the high plateau around Lake Titicaca, a sacred center of Andean cosmology.

Section 16.1

Classical-Era Civilizations in the Americas

The concept of a "classical era" in American history is a useful organizing tool, though students should approach it with care. The term "classical" was originally applied to the civilizations of ancient Greece and Rome and later extended to describe periods of high cultural achievement and political complexity in other parts of the world. In the context of Mesoamerica, scholars typically define the classical period as roughly 250 to 900 CE, when Maya city-states reached their greatest elaboration of monumental architecture, writing, calendrics, and political rivalry. In the Andes, the timeframe is somewhat different and varies by region; the Moche and Nazca flourish roughly 1 to 700 CE. These dates reflect real patterns of historical development — periods of intensified urban building, artistic achievement, long-distance exchange, and complex political organization — followed by transformation or decline.

It is important not to treat these dates as sharp walls. Mesoamerican civilization had deep roots in the preclassical period, and many features we associate with the classical Maya — pyramid construction, the ballgame, the 260-day calendar, social hierarchy, long-distance trade — were already present before 250 CE. Monte Albán was founded around 500 BCE, well before the classical period conventionally defined for the Maya. Teotihuacan began growing in the first century BCE. In the Andes, the Moche state developed gradually from earlier coastal traditions. History rarely divides as neatly as textbook periodization suggests.

What makes the classical era in the Americas significant in world-historical terms? First, it represents the independent development of urban complexity and state power in a part of the world entirely separate from the Afro-Eurasian civilizations studied in previous chapters. The cities and states of classical Mesoamerica and the Andes arose without contact with the civilizations of Mesopotamia, Egypt, India, or China. They did not borrow writing from the Near East or agriculture from the Fertile Crescent. They developed these things on their own, through processes of independent invention and local innovation. This demonstrates a crucial point for world history: the conditions that produce complex societies — agricultural surplus, population concentration, specialized labor, political hierarchy, and the need for record-keeping and coordination — can arise in many different environmental and cultural settings.

Second, the classical era in the Americas demonstrates the remarkable diversity of human approaches to civilization. The Maya city-state system, with dozens of competing kingdoms linked by shared culture and periodic warfare, differs fundamentally from the centralized urban model of Teotihuacan, which in turn differs from the Moche coastal polity or the Nazca geoglyph tradition. There is no single template for civilization, and the Americas in the classical era make this abundantly clear.

Third, the classical era laid the cultural and agricultural foundations on which later American societies — including the great empires of the Mexica (Aztec) and Inca — would build. The Mexica who built Tenochtitlan in the fourteenth century CE traced their cultural heritage to Teotihuacan and the earlier Mesoamerican tradition. The Inca who built their empire in the fifteenth century drew on Andean agricultural, engineering, and political traditions whose roots lay in the classical era. Understanding the classical period is therefore essential for understanding the world the Spanish encountered after 1492.

The evidence historians and archaeologists use to reconstruct classical American civilization is diverse and unevenly distributed. Maya civilization is unusual among classical American societies in having left extensive written records, which epigraphers have worked to decode since the mid-twentieth century. This means that we know the names of Maya rulers, the dates of battles and royal ceremonies, the dynastic genealogies of major cities, and fragments of Maya historical memory and cosmological belief. For Teotihuacan, the Moche, and the Nazca, writing was either absent or used in ways that have not been preserved, so scholars rely primarily on archaeology: excavated buildings, tombs, pottery, tools, food remains, human bones, and the spatial organization of settlements. A Maya inscription can tell us what a specific king claimed about his ancestors; an excavated midden can tell us what ordinary people ate. Both are essential.

Section 16.2

Mesoamerican Geography and Agriculture

To understand the civilizations that arose in Mesoamerica, one must first understand the land. Mesoamerica is not a geographic region with clear physical boundaries; it is defined by shared cultural traits distributed across an ecologically diverse area. But that ecological diversity is precisely the point: the varied environments of Mesoamerica created both challenges and opportunities for human communities, and the ways in which people responded to those environments shaped the particular forms of civilization that emerged.

The Valley of Mexico, located at roughly 2,200 meters above sea level in the heart of central Mexico, was in the classical era a basin containing several large lakes. These lakes, fed by streams from the surrounding mountains, made the basin extraordinarily productive. The lake shores and shallow lake bottoms were among the most fertile agricultural zones in Mesoamerica. Chinampas — raised agricultural beds built in the shallow lake margins, constructed by alternating layers of aquatic vegetation and lake mud — produced multiple crops per year. The lakes themselves provided fish, waterfowl, aquatic insects, and other protein sources. The surrounding mountains offered timber, game, and a range of microenvironments at different altitudes.

The southern Maya lowlands present a dramatically different environment. The Petén region of Guatemala and adjacent areas are tropical limestone plateaus, covered with dense jungle, crossed by few rivers, and dependent on seasonal rainfall for agriculture. The limestone geology creates a landscape dotted with sinkholes (cenotes) that provided water in a region with few surface streams. Without rivers large enough for irrigation, Maya farmers in the lowlands relied on terracing hillsides to reduce erosion, building reservoirs to store rainwater through the dry season, and practicing intensive raised-field agriculture in seasonal swamps. The construction of these agricultural systems required organized labor and likely some form of coordinated political authority.

Maize was the foundation of Mesoamerican agriculture and, in the Maya world, of religious cosmology. According to the Popol Vuh — the Quiché Maya creation narrative — human beings were created from maize dough by the gods. Maya farmers grew hundreds of varieties of maize adapted to different elevations, rainfall patterns, and soil types. The agricultural cycle of planting, growing, and harvesting maize was embedded in ritual, governed by the calendar, and reflected in the organization of the ceremonial year. Beans and squash, grown alongside maize in the classic milpa farming system, complemented the nutritional value of maize and helped maintain soil fertility.

Beyond the lowlands, the highlands of Chiapas and Guatemala provided different resources: jade, obsidian, quetzal feathers, and highland crops like amaranth. Trade between highland and lowland regions was therefore essential. Lowland Maya cities imported jade for ritual ornaments and obsidian for tools and weapons. Highland communities valued lowland goods like cacao, rubber, and fine polychrome pottery. The cacao tree, which grows in tropical lowland zones, produced the seeds used to make chocolate — one of the most valued luxury commodities in Mesoamerica. Cacao seeds also served as currency in some Mesoamerican market systems.

Obsidian — volcanic glass produced by volcanic activity in the Mexican and Guatemalan highlands — was the essential cutting material of the Mesoamerican world. Without iron or bronze metallurgy, Mesoamerican peoples relied on obsidian blades for surgery, food preparation, craft production, and warfare. The sources of obsidian were geographically concentrated, meaning that most communities had to obtain it through trade. Archaeological analysis of obsidian from different sources, using a technique called elemental sourcing, allows scholars to trace ancient trade networks with considerable precision.

Water management was a critical challenge in many parts of Mesoamerica. In the semiarid highlands, canal irrigation was essential; in the tropical lowlands, the challenge was storing water through the dry season. Maya cities invested enormous labor in constructing reservoirs, plastered and waterproofed to prevent loss, that could sustain populations through months of drought. The sophistication of Maya water management is one of the impressive achievements of their civilization, and its failure — during extended droughts — was likely one of the factors contributing to the abandonment of classical cities.

Section 16.3

Maya City-States, Writing, and Calendars

The Maya civilization of the classical period (approximately 250–900 CE) was not a single empire or unified state. It was a civilization — a shared cultural tradition — distributed across dozens of independent city-states that competed with each other politically and militarily while remaining linked by shared language, religion, artistic style, and cultural practice. This political fragmentation distinguishes the Maya world from imperial civilizations like Han China or the Roman Empire. To understand the Maya, one must understand the city-state as a political form.

Each Maya city-state, called a polity, was centered on a royal capital where a king (often called an ajaw, or lord) exercised authority over surrounding towns, villages, and agricultural hinterlands. The king was the pivot of political and religious life: he performed the rituals that maintained cosmic order, led military campaigns to capture rivals and tribute, sponsored the construction of temples and palaces that demonstrated his power, and conducted the public ceremonies that legitimated his rule. Maya kingship was deeply religious in character. Kings were not merely political leaders; they were intermediaries between the human world and the supernatural, capable of communicating with ancestors, gods, and cosmic forces through bloodletting rituals, vision quests, and ceremonial performance.

Maya cities were organized around a core of public monumental architecture: pyramid-temples that served as burial places for royal ancestors and stages for ritual performance; palaces where the royal court lived and conducted government; open plazas where ceremonies, markets, and ballgames took place; and carved stone stelae — standing stone monuments — that recorded the political and ritual histories of royal dynasties. The great pyramid-temples of the Maya world, such as the Temple of the Inscriptions at Palenque, or the enormous Tikal temples that rise above the rainforest canopy, represent some of the most impressive architectural achievements of the ancient Americas. Their construction required not only massive organized labor but also sophisticated engineering knowledge and the coordination of large workforces over years or decades.

Maya writing is one of the most remarkable intellectual achievements of the pre-Columbian Americas. The Maya script is a complex mixed system that combines logograms (signs representing whole words) with syllabic glyphs (signs representing syllables). This makes it a fully phonetic writing system capable of recording any utterance in the Mayan language. The decipherment of Maya writing, largely achieved between the 1950s and the 1990s through the work of scholars including Yuri Knorozov, Tatiana Proskouriakoff, Linda Schele, and others, transformed historians' understanding of Maya civilization. Before decipherment, scholars could read some Maya numbers and dates but could not read the historical and political texts on monuments. After decipherment, they could reconstruct royal genealogies, identify specific kings by name, read accounts of battles and alliances, and begin to understand Maya history as a narrative of specific people making specific decisions in specific circumstances.

Maya texts survive in two forms: monumental inscriptions carved on stone stelae, temples, altars, lintels, and other architectural elements; and screenfold books called codices, made of bark paper and coated with lime plaster. Only four pre-Columbian Maya codices survive — the Dresden, Paris, Madrid, and Grolier codices — because the Spanish colonial authorities ordered the destruction of Maya books as works of the devil. The loss of Maya literature and historical records in this destruction is one of the great intellectual tragedies of the colonial era. The surviving codices contain primarily astronomical tables, almanacs, and ritual calendars rather than historical narratives.

The Maya calendrical system was one of the most sophisticated time-tracking systems in the ancient world. It combined two interlocking cycles: the Tzolk'in, a 260-day ritual calendar of uncertain but deep pre-classical origin, and the Haab', a 365-day solar calendar. These two cycles meshed together to create the Calendar Round: a 52-year period after which the same combination of Tzolk'in and Haab' dates would recur. For recording historical events across longer periods, the Maya developed the Long Count calendar, a linear positional system that counted days from a mythological starting point corresponding to August 11, 3114 BCE in the modern Gregorian calendar. Maya astronomers tracked the movements of Venus with remarkable accuracy, recorded eclipse cycles, and embedded celestial observations in religious and political practice. Venus, associated with warfare and sacrifice in Maya cosmology, had practical implications for when kings should launch campaigns.

The political world of the classical Maya was one of constant competition and shifting alliances among dozens of city-states. Two superpower cities — Tikal in the Petén and Calakmul in southern Mexico — competed for dominance over much of the Maya lowlands through a combination of direct warfare and alliance networks with smaller cities. Warfare aimed primarily at the capture of high-status prisoners — rival kings, nobles, and warriors — rather than the destruction of cities. Captives were brought back in triumph, subjected to humiliating public ritual, and often sacrificed as a demonstration of the victor's power and relationship with the gods.

Women in Maya royal society played important but specific roles. Queens appear in Maya art and inscriptions, particularly as mothers of kings and as participants in ritual bloodletting ceremonies. Some women, such as Lady K'abel of Wak, exercised significant political authority in their own right, holding military governorships and the title of "Supreme Warrior." But formal political and military authority was predominantly male. Non-elite women's lives are less visible in the historical record, though archaeological evidence suggests they played essential roles in agricultural labor, craft production, and household management.

The Maya world was not isolated. Long-distance exchange connected Maya cities to highland Mexico, the Gulf Coast, and regions to the south. Teotihuacan had significant connections to the Maya world. Evidence of Teotihuacan influence appears at several Maya sites, most dramatically at Tikal, where a figure named Siyaj K'ak' arrived in 378 CE and apparently reorganized the city's political order, installing a new royal dynasty with connections to central Mexico. Whether this represents conquest, diplomatic alliance, or something in between remains debated, but it demonstrates that the Maya world was connected to wider Mesoamerican political networks.

Historical Vocabulary
Epigraphy

Epigraphy is the scholarly study and interpretation of inscriptions — texts written on stone, metal, clay, or other durable materials. In the study of Maya civilization, epigraphy refers specifically to the decipherment and analysis of Maya hieroglyphic writing. Epigraphers work to identify individual glyphs, understand how they combine to represent sounds and words, and read the texts in which they appear. The decipherment of Maya writing is one of the great intellectual achievements of twentieth-century scholarship. It transformed our understanding of Maya history, allowing scholars to read the names of kings, the dates of battles, and the accounts of royal rituals. Before decipherment, scholars studied Maya culture primarily through art and archaeology; after decipherment, they could add textual history. Epigraphy demonstrates how the tools historians use change over time, and how new methods can fundamentally transform what we know about the past.

Section 16.4

Teotihuacan and Urban Power

In the Valley of Mexico, about 50 kilometers northeast of the center of present-day Mexico City, lie the ruins of a city that was, at its height, one of the largest in the world. Teotihuacan's origins are obscure, but the city began growing in the first century BCE. By the third century CE, it had become a metropolis of perhaps 125,000 to 200,000 inhabitants, covering an area of about 20 to 25 square kilometers. Its ceremonial core — a monumental avenue two kilometers long, flanked by temples, platforms, and enclosures, with the Pyramid of the Sun on one side and the Pyramid of the Moon at the far end — represented one of the most impressive concentrations of religious architecture in the ancient Americas.

What made Teotihuacan extraordinary was not only its size but its apparent cosmopolitan character. Archaeological analysis of residential compounds in the city's outlying districts has identified migrants or merchants from Oaxaca, from the Gulf Coast, and from other regions. One section of the city, known from later tradition as the "Zapotec barrio," appears to have been an ethnic enclave of people from the Oaxaca region. This cosmopolitan quality suggests that Teotihuacan functioned as a center of trade and craft production attracting people from across the Mesoamerican world.

The Pyramid of the Sun, completed around 100 CE, is one of the largest ancient structures in the Americas. Built over a natural cave that appears to have been considered sacred, the pyramid rises to about 65 meters in height and covers an enormous base area. Its construction required the movement of millions of cubic meters of earth and rubble and the labor of thousands of workers organized over decades. The Temple of the Feathered Serpent (the Citadel complex) reveals a darker dimension of Teotihuacan's power: excavations under this structure have found the burials of more than 200 individuals, apparently sacrificed at the time of the temple's dedication around 200 CE. This sacrificial deposit demonstrates that ritual violence was not peripheral to classical Mesoamerican religion but central to its practice of political and cosmic power.

One of the most puzzling features of Teotihuacan is the near-total absence of the kind of royal portraiture and dynastic inscription that characterizes Maya cities. At Tikal or Palenque, specific kings are named on monuments and their genealogies recorded in hieroglyphic texts. At Teotihuacan, there are no identified royal tombs, no monuments naming individual rulers, and no inscriptions glorifying specific kings. This has led scholars to debate the nature of Teotihuacan's political system. Was the city ruled by a divine king on the Maya model? By a council of nobles? By a priestly elite? No consensus has emerged. Some scholars propose that Teotihuacan may have been governed by a more collective form of authority, perhaps a council representing different religious or social factions.

What is clear is that Teotihuacan wielded enormous regional influence. Its distinctive artistic style — featuring a characteristic "goggle-eyed" rain deity later associated with Tlaloc, a feathered serpent deity, and specific ceramic forms — spread across Mesoamerica. Whether this spread represents conquest, trade, diplomatic exchange, or the movement of migrants and merchants remains debated. At Kaminaljuyu in highland Guatemala, Teotihuacan influence is so pronounced in the archaeological record that some scholars have proposed it was a Teotihuacan colony or outpost.

Around 550 to 650 CE, Teotihuacan was apparently burned and partially abandoned. Evidence of burning is widespread across the ceremonial core, with particular concentration on the Avenue of the Dead and the major public buildings. The destruction appears to have been selective — public and elite structures burned but residential areas less so — which some scholars interpret as evidence of internal revolt by the population against an oppressive ruling elite, rather than conquest by an external enemy. Whatever the cause, the abandonment of Teotihuacan opened a period of political fragmentation in central Mexico that would eventually lead, centuries later, to the rise of new powers including the Toltec and eventually the Mexica (Aztec).

Section 16.5

Zapotec and Regional Centers

The Zapotec civilization of the Valley of Oaxaca represents one of the earliest and most distinctive traditions of urban complexity in Mesoamerica. The Valley of Oaxaca is a broad, Y-shaped valley in the mountains of southern Mexico, at an elevation of about 1,500 meters, drier than the tropical lowlands but well watered by mountain streams. By around 1000 BCE, farming villages in the valley were producing agricultural surpluses sufficient to support increasing social differentiation. By 500 BCE, the founding of Monte Albán — through the apparent cooperation of three previously competing valley polities — marks the beginning of Zapotec urban civilization.

Monte Albán is one of the most remarkable ancient cities in the Americas. Founded on a hilltop overlooking the confluence of the three branches of the Valley of Oaxaca, it was built not because the location was agriculturally productive but because it was politically and symbolically significant. The hilltop was leveled by an enormous effort of terracing and filling, creating a flat platform on which a formal ceremonial core was constructed. The main plaza of Monte Albán was carefully oriented according to astronomical principles, with buildings aligned to important solar and stellar events.

Among the earliest buildings at Monte Albán is a structure known as Building J, an arrowhead-shaped structure with an irregular orientation relative to the other buildings in the plaza. Its unusual orientation has led scholars to conclude that it was an astronomical observatory, aligned to track the rising of certain stars. This building exemplifies the integration of astronomical knowledge into Zapotec urban planning and religious practice.

One of the most striking features of early Monte Albán is a set of carved stone slabs known as the danzantes ("dancers"), depicting human figures in contorted poses. These figures were once interpreted as dancers or ball players, but scholars now recognize them as depictions of captives — prisoners of war shown in positions of death and defeat, with some exhibiting mutilation. Several of the danzante figures bear glyphic captions, making them among the earliest examples of writing in Mesoamerica — predating the Maya classical inscriptions.

Zapotec writing, like Maya writing, is not yet fully deciphered. It appears to use a mixed system of logograms and phonetic elements. The Zapotec calendar system, featuring a 260-day ritual cycle similar to the Maya Tzolk'in and a 365-day solar calendar, appears to be one of the earlier versions of this widespread Mesoamerican calendrical tradition, possibly influencing later systems. At its height, Monte Albán covered several square kilometers, with residential terraces extending down the hillsides and a population estimated in the tens of thousands.

Zapotec rulers were buried in elaborately constructed stone tombs beneath the floors of temple platforms, accompanied by ceramic urns depicting supernatural figures, jade ornaments, shells, and other luxury goods. Monte Albán declined as a major political center around 700 to 800 CE, roughly contemporary with the decline of Teotihuacan and the onset of the Maya classical collapse in the southern lowlands — a remarkable near-simultaneity that reflects broader regional stresses. After Monte Albán's decline, the Valley of Oaxaca was reorganized into smaller competing polities, some of which were later conquered by the Mexica in the fifteenth century.

Historical Vocabulary
City-State

A city-state is an independent political unit consisting of a city and the surrounding territory under its control. City-states govern their own affairs — law, military, taxation, religion — without being subject to a larger imperial authority. The city-state is one of the fundamental forms of ancient political organization, appearing in Mesopotamia (Sumer), classical Greece, and Mesoamerica (the Maya). In all of these cases, city-states were often closely related by culture, language, and religion, yet politically independent and frequently at war with each other. The city-state form allows for local autonomy and political competition but also produces instability, as smaller states are vulnerable to conquest by larger ones. The contrast between the city-state system of the classical Maya world and the centralized urban power of Teotihuacan highlights the diversity of political organization within Mesoamerica itself.

Section 16.6

Andean Geography and Vertical Exchange

Moving from Mesoamerica to the Andes is to enter a dramatically different world — one shaped by altitude, aridity, and the vertical organization of ecological diversity. The Andes mountains stretch for more than 7,000 kilometers along the western edge of South America. The mountain environment creates sharp contrasts within short distances: the Pacific coastal desert, one of the most arid environments on Earth, lies within view of Andean peaks that receive heavy rainfall and support diverse ecosystems.

The central concept for understanding Andean civilization is what the anthropologist John Murra called the "vertical archipelago" — the practice of Andean communities maintaining access to resources in multiple ecological zones at different altitudes simultaneously. Rather than specializing in the products of a single environment, Andean communities sent colonists or trading partners to establish footholds at different elevations. A coastal community might maintain agricultural plots in the highlands for potato and quinoa production; a highland community might maintain fishing rights on the coast; both might have access to forested slopes that produced coca, tropical fruits, and wood.

The implications for Andean political organization were profound. Andean polities were not simply territorial units controlling a contiguous block of land; they were dispersed networks of productive nodes connected by kinship, obligation, and shared identity. The political challenge was not just to defend territory but to maintain the relationships — social, ritual, and economic — that held together the vertically distributed community. The mechanisms for doing this included redistribution, reciprocity, and shared ceremonial life.

The Pacific coastal desert of Peru and Chile is one of the world's most extreme environments. Virtually no rain falls on the coast itself; the rivers that cross the desert, fed by Andean snowmelt, create narrow green ribbons of fertile agricultural land in an otherwise barren landscape. These coastal river valleys were the settings for major classical Andean civilizations, including the Moche and Nazca. The ability to irrigate desert valley floors using water from the Andean rivers created extraordinarily productive agricultural land, while the desert itself provided rich marine resources including fish, shellfish, and sea mammals.

The El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) cycle had dramatic effects on the Andean coastal environment. In El Niño years, warm Pacific waters disrupted the cold Humboldt Current, causing torrential rains on the normally dry coast and flooding irrigated valleys. In La Niña years, the opposite pattern prevailed. For Andean coastal civilizations dependent on irrigation agriculture and marine resources, the ENSO cycle represented a recurring environmental challenge, and the ability to respond to its disruptions was a test of social resilience.

The highland zone, centered on the altiplano around Lake Titicaca at about 3,800 meters elevation, presented different challenges. At this altitude, frosts can occur in any month of the year. The potato — a tuber domesticated in the Andes — is particularly well adapted to highland conditions. Highland Andean farmers developed dozens of potato varieties adapted to different altitudinal niches, as well as freeze-drying technology (ch'uño) to preserve potatoes. The llama and alpaca — the only large domesticated animals in the Americas — were central to Andean civilization in ways that had no parallel in Mesoamerica. Llamas served as pack animals capable of carrying loads of 25 to 40 kilograms over Andean terrain. Llama caravans connected different ecological zones, carrying highland products to coastal communities and returning with marine goods and dried fish. Alpacas produced wool of exceptional quality, the basis of a textile tradition that achieved remarkable technical and artistic sophistication.

Section 16.7

Moche, Nazca, and Coastal Societies

The Moche State

The north coast of Peru is crossed by numerous rivers descending from the Andes to the Pacific. The river valleys — particularly the Moche, Chicama, and Virú valleys — provided irrigated agricultural land in an otherwise desert environment. The Moche state flourished in these river valleys from roughly the first through eighth centuries CE. The most visible expressions of Moche political and religious power are the great huacas — platform mound complexes that served as temples, administrative centers, and elite residential areas. The Huaca del Sol and Huaca de la Luna at the site of Huacas de Moche near present-day Trujillo are the largest such structures. The Huaca del Sol, now heavily eroded by Spanish colonial gold-seekers who diverted a river through it, was originally one of the largest pre-Columbian structures in South America — an adobe platform perhaps 40 meters high and 345 meters long.

Moche society was hierarchically organized, with a ruling class of warrior-priests who derived their authority from ritual performance, military success, and the control of water and agricultural production. The Moche political system has been described by some scholars as a ritual polity: a state in which political authority was legitimated primarily through the performance of sacred ritual, the demonstration of special access to supernatural power, and the enactment of cosmological dramas in which the ruler played a key role.

Moche ceramics are among the most technically accomplished and iconographically rich in the ancient Americas. Moche potters produced stirrup-spout vessels in two main types: portrait vessels that are astonishingly realistic three-dimensional portraits of individual faces, and fineline painted vessels depicting complex narrative scenes. The fineline paintings show warriors engaged in combat; the capture, stripping, and parading of prisoners; ritual races and ceremonies; and finally the sacrifice of captives before a presiding supernatural figure. This sequence — interpreted by scholars as the "Sacrifice Ceremony" — depicts what appears to be a ritualized reenactment of cosmic combat in which the living ruler impersonated divine figures who defeated enemies and offered their blood to maintain cosmic order.

The identification of the Sacrifice Ceremony in ceramic iconography was dramatically confirmed by archaeology in 1987 with the discovery of the tomb of the Lord of Sipán in the Lambayeque Valley. Discovered intact by archaeologist Walter Alva, the tomb contained the burial of a Moche ruler accompanied by extraordinary quantities of gold and silver ornaments, ceramic vessels, and sacrificed individuals. The regalia of the Lord of Sipán precisely matched the costumes worn by figures in the Sacrifice Ceremony iconography, confirming that Moche rulers ritually impersonated divine figures in life and were buried in their ceremonial identities in death.

Moche culture shows signs of stress, transformation, and eventual abandonment of major sites around the late sixth and early seventh centuries CE. An extended drought, evidenced by climate proxy data from ice cores and geological deposits, apparently struck the Andes in this period and may have undermined the agricultural foundations of Moche power.

The Nazca and Their Lines

The south coast of Peru is even drier than the north coast. The Nazca culture flourished here from roughly the first through seventh centuries CE, roughly contemporary with the Moche. Like the Moche, the Nazca produced extraordinary ceramic art — their polychrome pottery, decorated with vivid colors and naturalistic or supernatural figures, represents a distinct regional tradition of remarkable technical accomplishment.

But the Nazca are most famous for the Nazca Lines. The Nazca desert surface is covered by a layer of dark, iron-oxide-coated rocks. Beneath this dark surface layer lies lighter-colored clay and sand. By carefully removing the dark surface rocks and piling them along the edges, ancient Nazca workers created lines, geometric shapes, and representational figures on an enormous scale. Some figures span hundreds of meters; the most famous include a hummingbird, a spider, a condor, a monkey, and various plants. The geometric lines include straight lines extending for kilometers and large trapezoidal cleared areas.

The purpose and meaning of the Nazca lines remain debated. Twentieth-century speculation about extraterrestrial intervention or ancient balloons has been decisively rejected by mainstream scholarship. The most widely accepted scholarly interpretation links the Nazca lines to water and fertility ritual in an extremely water-scarce environment. The straight lines appear to be connected to underground water sources (called puquios), and some lines point toward Andean peaks — the sources of the water that fed the region's rivers. The representational figures — animals, plants — may represent deities or sacred beings associated with fertility and agricultural productivity.

Nazca ceremonial life was centered on Cahuachi, a large complex of platform mounds and cleared plazas in the Nazca Valley. Unlike Moche huacas, Cahuachi appears to have been a pilgrimage and ritual center rather than a permanent urban settlement. The Nazca also produced extraordinary textiles — among the finest in the ancient Americas — worked in cotton and camelid wool with complex multi-colored designs. Many of these textiles were found in burial contexts, preserved by the extreme aridity of the desert.

Section 16.8

Religion, Ritual, and Political Authority

Across the classical civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes, religion and ritual were not separate from politics; they were the foundation of politics. The ability of rulers to perform sacred ritual, maintain relationships with supernatural powers, and enact cosmic dramas that ensured agricultural fertility, military success, and social order was the primary basis of their political legitimacy. This integration of religious and political authority was characteristic of classical American civilization, though the specific forms it took varied widely from region to region.

In the Maya world, the king was the axis mundi — the center of the cosmic order — who mediated between the human world and the supernatural. Maya rulers performed elaborate bloodletting rituals in which they drew their own blood from tongue, ears, or genitals and scattered it on bark paper strips that were then burned, the smoke carrying the blood offering to the gods. These rituals, depicted in detail on stelae and relief carvings, were the most important performances of Maya royal religion. The imagery of bloodletting is pervasive in Maya art, and its importance to royal legitimacy cannot be overstated.

Human sacrifice was practiced across classical Mesoamerica, though its scale varied. At Teotihuacan, large-scale sacrificial deposits marked the dedication of major buildings. In the Maya world, the sacrifice of captured enemies was linked to royal warfare and demonstrated military and cosmic power. The ballgame, played across Mesoamerica in stone courts with rubber balls, was embedded in sacrificial ideology: in some contexts, captives were made to play and their defeat led to sacrifice. The ballcourt was not merely a sports venue; it was a ritual arena where cosmic conflicts were re-enacted.

In the Andes, the Moche ritual system centered on warrior-priest rulers who enacted the Sacrifice Ceremony — a cosmic drama in which the ruler, impersonating a divine figure called the Warrior Priest, received the blood of sacrificed captives. Sacrifice served to nourish supernatural forces, ensure agricultural fertility, and demonstrate the ruler's special relationship with divine power. The elaborate processing of captives — stripping them of their regalia, parading them, and eventually sacrificing them — was a highly public political performance.

Religion also organized time. The 260-day ritual calendar of Mesoamerica divided the year into a cycle of days each with its own supernatural associations and auspicious or inauspicious qualities. Calendar specialists (daykeepers) who managed this system were important religious functionaries, their knowledge giving them power over the timing of human activity. The astronomer-priests who tracked celestial cycles served a similar function, embedding human time within cosmic time.

Religious life was not only the domain of rulers and priests. Ordinary people participated in public ceremonies, made offerings at household shrines and local sacred places, consulted ritual specialists for guidance on important decisions, and embedded their agricultural labor within calendrical and ritual frameworks. The sacred landscape was important across the Americas. Mountains, caves, springs, rivers, and specific landforms were considered sacred, inhabited by supernatural powers or serving as portals to the supernatural world. Maya rulers performed ceremonies at sacred caves; Andean communities addressed prayers and offerings to mountain peaks (apus) as powerful beings. The Nazca lines were embedded in this sacred landscape tradition.

Historical Vocabulary
Redistribution

Redistribution is an economic system in which a central authority collects goods, labor, or resources from the population and then distributes them back in the form of food, goods, and services. Unlike market exchange (where goods are traded between individuals) or reciprocal exchange (where communities share with each other based on mutual obligation), redistribution flows goods upward toward a central authority and then downward to participants. Redistribution was a fundamental feature of Andean political economies. A Moche ruler, for example, collected agricultural produce, textiles, and craft goods through labor obligations from subjects, then redistributed food and goods to workers during corvée labor projects, to warriors before campaigns, and to communities during ritual feasts. This system created political loyalty and dependence. The Inca Empire, which later dominated the Andes, developed redistribution into one of the most sophisticated state economic systems in the ancient world.

Section 16.9

American Civilizations in Comparative Perspective

How do the classical civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes compare with the other great civilizations of the ancient world? This is an important question not to rank civilizations — declaring some "more advanced" than others — but to understand the shared patterns and distinctive pathways of human history.

One striking parallel is the independent development of writing. The Maya created one of the most sophisticated writing systems in the ancient world — fully phonetic, capable of recording any utterance in their language — without contact with the writing traditions of Mesopotamia, Egypt, India, or China. The Zapotec developed an early form of writing perhaps earlier than the Maya. In the Andes, the Moche and Nazca did not develop writing in the conventional sense, but the Andean quipu — knotted strings used to record numerical information and, scholars increasingly believe, narrative information — represents an alternative technology for information storage. These independent developments reflect a fundamental human need for communication systems that arises in complex societies wherever they develop.

Another parallel is the pattern of city-state competition. The competitive political world of the classical Maya — with dozens of independent city-states linked by shared culture but divided by political rivalry — strongly resembles the political world of Mesopotamian city-states in the third millennium BCE, or of classical Greek poleis in the first millennium BCE. In each case, cultural unity and political fragmentation coexisted; shared language, religion, and artistic tradition did not prevent warfare and competition. The repeated pattern across different civilizations suggests something fundamental about the conditions under which city-state systems arise and persist.

But the differences are equally significant. The Americas lacked iron metallurgy — a major technological difference from the Old World, where iron working had transformed agriculture, warfare, and craft production by the classical era. The Americas did not have large domestic animals comparable to the cattle, horses, and oxen of Eurasia (the Andean llama and alpaca were significant but more limited in their applications). The Americas did not experience the dense Eurasian disease environment, which had over millennia led Old World populations to develop some immunity to epidemic diseases — a fact that would have catastrophic consequences when European contact introduced smallpox, measles, and other diseases after 1492.

The Americas also show patterns with no close parallel in the Old World. The Maya Long Count calendar's precision and the astronomical observations embedded in Maya architecture represent a tradition of timekeeping and astronomical observation that is in some respects unmatched in the ancient world. The Nazca lines have no close parallel elsewhere. The Andean vertical economy, with its systematic exploitation of multiple altitude zones through dispersed community networks, represents a distinct and sophisticated adaptation to environmental diversity. The lesson of comparison is not that one civilization was "ahead" or "behind" others. It is that human beings facing similar challenges developed both parallel solutions and distinctive innovations shaped by their specific environments and historical trajectories.

Regional Lens

The Maya Lowlands

The Maya lowlands of the southern Petén — the tropical limestone plateau of northern Guatemala — present one of the most challenging yet rewarding environments in which to study ancient civilization. The Petén today is a land of dense jungle, swampy depressions (bajos), thin forest soils, and seasonal rivers. Annual rainfall is abundant but concentrated in a wet season from May to November; the dry season from December to April can be harsh, with little surface water. The temperature is consistently hot and humid.

In this challenging environment, the classical Maya built some of the largest and most elaborately planned cities in the ancient Americas. Tikal, the dominant city of the southern Maya lowlands at its peak, covered about 120 square kilometers of mapped area and supported an estimated population of 60,000 to 90,000 people at its height. Its ceremonial core is dominated by six enormous temple pyramids, including Temple IV rising 65 meters above the jungle floor. The city's construction evolved over more than a millennium of dynastic rule.

Understanding how the Maya sustained such large populations in the tropical lowlands has been one of the major puzzles of Mesoamerican archaeology. Traditional views held that the Maya used primarily slash-and-burn agriculture, which would have limited population densities. Archaeological survey using LiDAR (Light Detection And Ranging, an airborne laser scanning technology) has transformed this picture dramatically in recent years. LiDAR can penetrate forest canopy and reveal ground surface features in extraordinary detail, and its application to the Maya lowlands has revealed an enormous hidden landscape of terraced hillsides, raised field systems in seasonal swamps, irrigation canals, and other agricultural infrastructure invisible to traditional ground survey. The scale of this infrastructure — covering vast areas — suggests that the classical Maya practiced far more intensive agriculture than previously understood, capable of supporting much larger populations.

The Maya lowlands were also a world of movement. Maya merchants and travelers moved between cities along formal road systems called sacbeob (white roads) — raised causeways built of packed limestone that connected major centers, facilitated trade, and served as processional routes for ritual. The movement of trade goods — obsidian, jade, pyrite mirrors, cacao, feathers, salt — linked Maya cities into a regional economic network. Daily life for ordinary Maya in the lowlands revolved around agricultural labor: maintaining milpa plots, contributing labor to public buildings, and managing the agricultural infrastructure of terracing and reservoir construction. Evidence from household archaeology shows that most Maya families had access to basic ceramic vessels, grinding stones, obsidian blades, and other necessities, while luxury goods were concentrated in elite compounds.

Global Connection

Mesoamerica and the Wider World Before 1492

The civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes developed independently of the Afro-Eurasian world. There is no credible archaeological or genetic evidence for sustained contact between the Americas and Europe, Africa, or most of Asia before the Norse settlements in Newfoundland around 1000 CE, and those Norse contacts had no lasting impact on Indigenous American civilizations. The classical civilizations examined in this chapter arose, flourished, and transformed entirely within the context of Indigenous American history.

Yet the Americas were not isolated from the rest of the Americas. Exchange networks connecting Mesoamerica to North America, to South America, and to the Caribbean were real and significant. Mesoamerican trade goods — turquoise, copper bells, macaw feathers, cacao — have been found in archaeological sites across the southwestern United States, suggesting long-distance trade between Mesoamerican centers and communities in the present-day U.S. Southwest. The Hohokam culture of southern Arizona showed evidence of extensive Mesoamerican cultural influence, including ballcourts and copper bells apparently imported from or inspired by Mesoamerican prototypes.

In South America, the Andes were connected to Amazonia through trade. Tropical forest products — feathers of Amazon birds, jaguar pelts, plant medicines, and psychoactive plants — moved up into the Andean highlands in exchange for highland products. The Chavín religious tradition incorporated imagery from Amazonian animals — particularly the jaguar and the harpy eagle — suggesting that religious innovation moved between environments alongside trade goods.

These inter-American connections demonstrate that indigenous American civilizations were not hermetically sealed local traditions but participants in wider exchange networks that moved goods, ideas, and people across the continent. The Americas formed a historical world of their own, with complex internal dynamics, long before the European intrusion of 1492. The civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes were not peripheral or minor; they were major centers of human achievement whose connections created a continental world of cultural and economic exchange.

Civilization Study

The Classic Maya

Geography

The Maya homeland encompasses the lowland tropical forests of the Petén in Guatemala, the Yucatán Peninsula, Belize, and adjacent parts of Honduras and El Salvador. Each zone has distinct ecological conditions: the southern lowlands are tropical forest on limestone; the northern lowlands are drier and scrubby; the highlands are cool mountain country.

Political Organization

A network of competing city-states, each ruled by a dynastic king (ajaw) who claimed divine ancestry, performed sacred ritual, led military campaigns, and sponsored monumental construction. Two "superpower" cities — Tikal and Calakmul — dominated much of the southern lowlands. There was no single Maya empire; political unity across the Maya world was cultural, not political.

Economy

Based on intensive agriculture — primarily maize, beans, and squash — supplemented by cacao, fruit trees, and other cultivated plants. Intensive land use including terracing and raised fields supported dense populations. Long-distance trade was essential, moving obsidian, jade, cacao, salt, feathers, and ceramics across hundreds of kilometers. Markets existed in major cities.

Social Structure

Hierarchically organized: royal family and closely related nobles at top; below them lower-ranking nobles, specialists (scribes, architects, artisans), merchants, farmers, and servants. Enslaved people appear in the record, often captured in warfare. Formal political and military authority was predominantly male, though elite women could wield significant influence.

Religion and Cosmology

A complex pantheon of deities associated with natural forces — rain, sun, maize, death. The underworld (Xibalba) and the Hero Twins of the Popol Vuh were central to cosmology. Royal ritual centered on bloodletting, cosmological performance, and the sacrifice of captive enemies. The calendar structured religious and agricultural time.

Writing and Knowledge

The most fully developed writing system in the pre-Columbian Americas, capable of recording any utterance in Mayan language. Used primarily for monumental inscriptions and religious texts. Astronomical knowledge — of Venus cycles, eclipse prediction, and stellar movements — was sophisticated and embedded in architecture and ritual.

Legacy

Maya civilization was not extinguished. Maya people continued to live in their homelands, maintaining languages, agricultural traditions, and elements of religious practice through the colonial era and into the present. Today, millions of Maya people live in Mexico, Guatemala, and Belize. The decipherment of Maya writing in the twentieth century has given descendants and scholars access to a rich historical record.

Primary Source Window

Inscription from Palenque — K'inich Janaab' Pakal and the Temple of the Inscriptions (683 CE)

Creator: Royal scribes of K'inich Janaab' Pakal's court  ·  Date: Late seventh century CE  ·  Type: Monumental hieroglyphic inscription and funerary monument

Context: K'inich Janaab' Pakal (Pakal the Great) ruled the Maya city of Palenque in present-day Chiapas, Mexico, from 615 to 683 CE — a reign of sixty-eight years, one of the longest in the ancient Americas. Before his death, he commissioned the construction of the Temple of the Inscriptions — a nine-stepped pyramid intended to serve as his funerary monument. Within the temple, a hidden staircase led to a buried royal tomb, where Pakal was interred in 683 CE. The sarcophagus lid is carved with one of the most famous images in Maya art: Pakal descending into the underworld while the World Tree rises from his body, connecting the underworld, the earth, and the heavens.

The inscriptions on the temple walls record Pakal's genealogy, tracing his ancestry back through deep time to mythological figures associated with the gods. They record dates of significant events in Pakal's reign — his accession, military victories, and the completion of important buildings — using the Long Count calendar. They also record the deaths and successions of earlier Palenque rulers, creating a dynastic history intended to demonstrate the ancient legitimacy of the ruling line.

Analysis: The Temple of the Inscriptions and Pakal's sarcophagus are extraordinary sources for understanding Maya royal ideology. They reveal that Maya kings claimed divine ancestry and supernatural associations — Pakal's descent into the underworld and emergence from the World Tree connected him to the cosmic cycle of death and regeneration that sustained the agricultural world. The detailed genealogical record demonstrates the importance of dynastic continuity to Maya political legitimacy. The inscriptions represent the official self-presentation of Pakal and his scribal court; they do not give us the perspectives of his subjects, rivals, or those whose labor built the temple.

Questions for students:

  1. Why would a Maya king commission an elaborate inscription recording his genealogy and connecting him to mythological ancestors? What political purpose did this serve?
  2. What does the decoration on Pakal's sarcophagus — depicting his descent into the underworld along the World Tree — tell us about Maya beliefs about death and the cosmos?
  3. What can historians learn from monumental royal inscriptions that they cannot learn from archaeology alone? What can they not learn from royal inscriptions?
  4. How might the history of Palenque look different if we had access to inscriptions written by Pakal's subjects, rivals, or enemies?
Archaeology and Material Evidence Window

The Tomb of the Lord of Sipán, Lambayeque Valley, Peru (c. First–Second Century CE)

In 1987, archaeologist Walter Alva was called to the site of Huaca Rajada in the Lambayeque Valley on the north coast of Peru. Looters had already heavily damaged adjacent structures, but Alva's team excavated an intact royal tomb that would become one of the most important archaeological discoveries in the Americas. The tomb contained the burial of a Moche ruler accompanied by extraordinary quantities of gold, silver, and gilded copper objects — headdresses, ear ornaments, pectorals, and backflaps — as well as the bodies of sacrificed individuals. Some objects were made of materials obtained through long-distance trade: shells from the Spondylus (thorny oyster) species from the warm waters of Ecuador, turquoise from highland sources, and feathers from tropical birds.

The significance of the discovery was magnified when scholars compared the regalia found in the tomb with the iconography of Moche fineline ceramic paintings. The costume worn by the Lord of Sipán precisely matched the costume worn by the central figure in the Sacrifice Ceremony — the same crescent-shaped headdress ornament, crescent-shaped backflap, and beaded pectoral. This living ruler had not merely claimed association with the divine Warrior Priest of Moche cosmology; he had impersonated that figure in life and been buried in his sacred costume.

What historians and archaeologists can infer: Moche rulers derived political authority from their identification with supernatural figures. The scale and quality of the burial goods demonstrate that the Moche state could mobilize enormous craft production and long-distance trade in service of royal ritual. The accompanying sacrificed individuals confirm that human sacrifice was practiced in Moche mortuary ritual. The match between tomb goods and ceramic iconography validates the interpretation of the Sacrifice Ceremony as depicting real political and religious practice rather than pure mythology.

Limits of the evidence: The tomb provides a view of elite Moche life but tells us relatively little about the majority of the Moche population. It represents the burial of a single high-status individual; generalizing from one burial to the entire society requires caution. The objects in the tomb were created and selected by the ruling class and reflect elite values and ideology; they do not speak directly for the experiences of farmers, artisans, or other social groups.

Historical Debate

What Caused the Maya Classical "Collapse"?

The question: Between approximately 800 and 1000 CE, the major Maya cities of the southern lowlands were abandoned, monumental construction ceased, hieroglyphic inscription stopped, and populations declined dramatically in some areas. What caused it?

Interpretation A — Environmental and Climatic Collapse: A major interpretive tradition, supported by substantial climate proxy evidence from lake sediments, speleothems, and tree rings, argues that an extended and severe drought struck the Maya lowlands during the ninth century CE. This drought would have disrupted the intensive rain-fed agriculture on which Maya cities depended, emptied the reservoirs that supplied urban water, and caused crop failures. Scholars like Douglas Kennett and colleagues have published paleoclimate records showing a marked decrease in rainfall during the period of the collapse.
Interpretation B — Political and Social Breakdown: Other scholars emphasize political and social factors. The archaeological record of the Terminal Classic period shows intensification of warfare, as evidenced by increased construction of defensive fortifications and more frequent depictions of conflict in art. Competition among city-states may have become more destructive during the eighth and ninth centuries, disrupting agricultural production, diverting labor from farming to fighting, and undermining tributary relationships. Evidence of internal social stress — increasing demands on the farming population and possible popular resistance — may also have undermined stability.
Interpretation C — Overshoot and Environmental Degradation: A third perspective emphasizes accumulated environmental effects of intensive Maya occupation over many centuries. Deforestation for agriculture, construction, and fuel production would have led to increased erosion, the silting of reservoirs, declining soil fertility, and altered local climate. Paleoecological evidence from Maya lake sediments shows evidence of deforestation and erosion preceding the collapse, suggesting long-term environmental degradation had weakened the agricultural system before the crisis occurred.

Evidence: All three interpretations have supporting evidence. Most contemporary scholars favor a multicausal explanation: drought was a severe and sudden stress, but it interacted with already present political instability and environmental degradation, and different cities were affected differently depending on local conditions.

Why it matters: As the world faces twenty-first-century climate change and environmental stress, the question of how ancient societies responded to — or failed to respond to — climatic and environmental challenges has obvious contemporary relevance. Understanding the Maya collapse can illuminate the complex interactions between climate, environment, politics, and social organization that determine whether societies are resilient or fragile in the face of stress.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented, evaluate which combination of factors seems best supported by the available evidence. What additional types of evidence — from archaeology, paleoclimatology, or other fields — would help resolve remaining uncertainties?

Comparison Across Societies

Teotihuacan and Han China

Teotihuacan at its peak (c. 200–550 CE) and Han Dynasty China (206 BCE–220 CE) were two of the largest concentrations of urban population in the ancient world, roughly contemporary in time, and representing different models of political and economic organization.

Scale and Population Teotihuacan housed an estimated 125,000–200,000 people in about 20 square kilometers — an extraordinary concentration for the ancient Americas. The Han capital at Chang'an may have housed 250,000–400,000 people, and the Han Empire governed a total population of perhaps 60 million. Teotihuacan was a single dominant city without a comparable imperial administrative system; the Han was a continental empire with an extensive bureaucracy.
Political System The Han state was a monarchy with a centralized imperial bureaucracy, a developing civil service examination system, a codified legal system, and explicit ideological foundations in Confucian thought. Teotihuacan's political system remains mysterious: no named rulers are known from inscriptions. Some scholars propose it was governed by a council rather than a hereditary king — a form of political organization without clear parallel in contemporary Afro-Eurasian empires.
Trade and Economy Both were major centers of long-distance trade. Han China participated in the Silk Roads, exporting silk and ceramics. Teotihuacan controlled key trade routes within Mesoamerica, exporting obsidian and manufactured objects. Both cities attracted merchants from distant regions. Han China's institutional support for trade was more formally developed, but both represent the same principle of a cosmopolitan urban trade center drawing in resources from a wide region.
Technological Basis and Documentation Han China had iron metallurgy, which transformed agriculture and warfare. Teotihuacan, like all Mesoamerican civilizations, had no iron; its tools were made of obsidian, bone, wood, and shell. This technological difference had profound implications for agricultural productivity and military capacity. The Han state used extensive written records and left an extraordinarily rich textual record; Teotihuacan apparently left almost none, making political reconstruction far more difficult for historians.

Shared pattern: Despite these differences, both civilizations demonstrate the same fundamental pattern: concentration of population in urban centers supported by agricultural hinterlands; organization of long-distance trade for prestige goods and essential materials; use of monumental architecture and ritual to legitimate political authority; and creation of craft-production complexes that attracted skilled workers.

Multiple Perspectives

The Construction of a Maya Temple Pyramid

The construction of a major Maya temple pyramid required the coordinated labor of thousands of people over months or years. How did different groups experience this process?

The King For a Maya king, the construction of a temple was a demonstration of power, piety, and relationship with the gods. A ruler who built a great temple showed he could command the labor of subjects, that he had the wealth to feed and provision workers, and that the gods favored him. Temples were also expressions of royal ideology: oriented according to astronomical principles, designed to frame ritual performances, and inscribed with texts glorifying the ruler's dynasty.
Nobles and Priests For nobles and priests who planned and designed temples, these projects were opportunities to demonstrate expertise and earn royal favor. Maya architects possessed sophisticated mathematical and astronomical knowledge. Scribes and priests who composed the hieroglyphic inscriptions drew on deep knowledge of history, mythology, and cosmology. Their skills were essential to the enterprise, and their status within the royal court depended on their usefulness to the king.
Farmers and Laborers For farming families who provided most of the construction labor, temple building was likely experienced as a combination of obligation, religious duty, communal identity, and economic hardship. Maya farmers owed labor service to their rulers. During the agricultural off-season, they might be called upon to quarry stone, transport materials, mix lime plaster, and pile rubble fill. Some evidence suggests workers were fed and given access to goods during construction — a form of reciprocal exchange. For participants, contributing to the physical embodiment of divine power may also have carried genuine religious meaning.
Women Women's roles in temple construction are less visible in the monumental record. But the agricultural and domestic labor that sustained construction workers was primarily women's work: grinding maize for thousands of workers, weaving cloth, raising children during prolonged periods when male family members might be absent, and managing household food stores. Elite women contributed differently: as ritual performers, as political symbols of dynastic alliance, and as participants in the court ceremonies that contextualized temple construction within royal ideology.
Cause and Effect

The Abandonment of Teotihuacan

Long-Term Causes

Over centuries, Teotihuacan's dominance may have generated growing tensions. Demands on agricultural land in the Valley of Mexico may have led to environmental stress including soil depletion and deforestation. Concentration of political and economic power may have generated resentment among both the city's own population and subject communities. There is archaeological evidence for growing inequality in the city's later residential districts.

Short-Term Causes

Immediate circumstances around 550–650 CE are unclear. Archaeological evidence of burning concentrated on the Avenue of the Dead and major public buildings — while residential areas show less evidence of destruction — has led many scholars to argue for internal revolt. Some scholars point to evidence of external pressures. Climate stress — drought or other environmental disruption — may have contributed to social instability.

The Event

Around 550–650 CE, the ceremonial core of Teotihuacan was burned. Monuments appear to have been deliberately toppled or defaced. The city was not immediately or completely abandoned; some population apparently remained in a transformed and diminished city. But monumental construction ceased and Teotihuacan's political and economic dominance over Mesoamerica ended.

Effects

The fall of Teotihuacan opened a period of political fragmentation in central Mexico. Several smaller regional centers grew in power, including Xochicalco and Cacaxtla. The eventual rise of the Toltec state at Tula (c. 900–1150 CE) and, ultimately, the Mexica Empire were shaped by the power vacuum Teotihuacan left behind. The memory of Teotihuacan persisted: the Aztec visited the ruins and named them, revering them as the place where the gods had created the current sun.

Historical complexity: The abandonment of Teotihuacan cannot be attributed to any single cause. It involved the interaction of environmental stress, social inequality, political instability, and possibly external pressure — a multicausal explanation that is more historically realistic than any single-factor account.

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Andean Cultural Tradition from the Classical Era to the Inca

What Changed

  • The Moche state collapsed around 700 CE, with major sites abandoned and the political system dissolving
  • The Nazca culture transformed during the seventh and eighth centuries; new political centers emerged
  • The specific forms of ceramic art, mortuary practice, and political organization that characterized the Moche and Nazca periods were replaced by new traditions
  • Population patterns shifted as some regions declined and others grew

What Stayed the Same

  • The vertical economy — organization of communities to exploit multiple ecological zones — continued to define Andean economic life
  • The llama caravan trade continued to move goods across altitude zones
  • Reverence for sacred mountains (apus) and sacred water sources remained central to Andean religion
  • The political practice of redistribution continued as the organizing principle of Andean political economy
  • Agricultural terracing, irrigation canal systems, and the cultivation of the same staple crops (potato, maize, quinoa) continued without fundamental disruption

Why this pattern matters: Political systems can change dramatically without transforming the deeper economic, social, and religious structures of a civilization. The Andean cultural tradition — with its vertical economy, redistribution, llama caravans, sacred landscapes, and agricultural knowledge — was not the creation of any single state but a long-term regional tradition that sustained multiple political systems over centuries. When the Inca built their empire in the fifteenth century, they built it on foundations that were already ancient.

Everyday Life and Social History: A Moche Farmer in the Chicama Valley

To understand classical Andean civilization from a non-elite perspective, consider the life of a farming family in the Chicama Valley — one of the most productive river valleys on the north coast of Peru — during the height of Moche civilization, around 400 CE.

The Chicama Valley stretches from the Pacific coast into the foothills of the Andes. The river, fed by Andean snowmelt, flows year-round, and its waters were diverted through an elaborate network of canals to irrigate the valley floor. Maize, beans, squash, peppers, sweet potatoes, and peanuts grew in abundance. The adjacent Pacific Ocean provided fish, shellfish, and sea mammals harvested by canoe fishermen. On the steep slopes above the irrigated valley floor, farmers cultivated drought-tolerant crops using terraces and seasonal rainfall.

A Moche farming family in the Chicama Valley would have lived in a mudbrick compound of several rooms opening onto a central patio — a compound shared perhaps by an extended family of parents, married children, and grandchildren. Their material life was relatively modest: ceramic vessels for cooking and storage, stone grinding tools for processing maize, cotton and wool clothing, and basic wooden and bone tools. A small household shrine might hold ceramic figurines of supernatural beings.

Their labor was organized around the agricultural calendar and the obligations of tribute and corvée labor. During the agricultural season, they planted, tended, and harvested crops — labor that occupied most family members, including women and children at key moments like planting and harvest. Outside the agricultural season, they might be called upon to contribute labor to irrigation canals, huaca construction, or craft production for elite consumption. Women's labor was particularly important in textile production: spinning cotton and wool thread and weaving fabric for household use and as tributary goods owed to the state.

The farming family's relationship with the Moche state was mediated by tribute and ritual. They owed a portion of their agricultural production to the state and labor service for public projects. In return, they received access to irrigation water maintained by state-organized labor, protection from raiding, and inclusion in the ritual life of the community. When a Moche ruler organized a great ceremony — the dedication of a new huaca, or the sacrifice of captured enemies — farming families may have participated as spectators, workers, or minor ritual actors, experiencing the ruler's power in a direct sensory way. Whether or not farming families fully believed in the divine claims of their rulers, they operated within a world in which those claims structured reality, organized the agricultural calendar, and provided a framework for understanding the variability of climate and the necessity of sacrifice for cosmic renewal.

Environment and Technology

The classical civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes were deeply shaped by environmental constraints and responded to them through remarkable technological innovation.

Water Management: In the Maya lowlands, the construction of large plaster-lined reservoirs was essential to urban life. Recent LiDAR mapping has revealed the scale of Maya hydraulic infrastructure — not just reservoirs but extensive canal systems linking them, suggesting a sophisticated urban water management network capable of storing and distributing rainwater through the dry season. On the arid Peruvian coast, the Moche built and maintained extensive irrigation canal systems that diverted river water across the desert to agricultural fields. The Chicama-Moche inter-valley canal — connecting two adjacent river valleys — is one of the most ambitious pre-Columbian hydraulic engineering projects in South America. Maintaining these canal systems required organized labor, political authority capable of mobilizing communal effort, and technical knowledge of hydrology and gradient management.

Agricultural Technology: Maya farmers in the lowlands developed sophisticated raised-field agriculture in seasonal swamps, creating agricultural plots with improved drainage and extended growing seasons. In the Andes, terrace construction on steep mountain slopes — reshaping the topography to create flat planting surfaces with improved water retention — was one of the major technologies of Andean agriculture. Terraced landscapes built by pre-Columbian communities are still visible across the Andes today, and some are still in use.

Building Technology: Both Mesoamerican and Andean architecture was built without iron tools, wheeled transport, or large draft animals. Maya construction used stone tools, wooden levers, and massive human labor. The corbeled vault — constructed by carefully cantilevering each successive course of stones slightly inward until the two walls met at the top — allowed Maya architects to create interior spaces of considerable height and drama. Moche construction used adobe (sun-dried mudbrick), well suited to the arid Peruvian coast. The vast scale of the Huaca del Sol — built from an estimated 140 million adobe bricks — demonstrates the extraordinary human labor that Moche rulers could mobilize.

The Absence of the Wheel and Iron: Classical American civilizations achieved extraordinary results without two technologies that were transformative in the Old World: iron metallurgy and wheeled transport. The Americas were not ignorant of metal — Andean metalworkers produced gold, silver, and copper objects of extraordinary quality — but hard iron was not developed. The wheel was known in the Americas (wheeled toy animals appear in Mesoamerican archaeological contexts) but was not applied to transport, perhaps because the Americas lacked the large draft animals that would have made wheeled carts practical. These absences shaped the particular technological and political character of American civilization without preventing the emergence of societies of extraordinary complexity and achievement.

Chapter Summary

Chapter 16 has examined the major classical-era civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes, tracing their development, achievements, interactions, and transformations across roughly a millennium of American history. The classical period in the Americas represents independent development of urban complexity, state power, and cultural achievement in a part of the world entirely separate from the Afro-Eurasian civilizations studied in previous chapters — a development that demonstrates one of world history's most important lessons: complex societies can arise wherever the right combination of agricultural surplus, population density, environmental challenge, and cultural innovation creates the conditions for them.

In Mesoamerica, the classical era was dominated by three major traditions of civilization: the Maya city-state system, the urban power of Teotihuacan, and the Zapotec civilization of Oaxaca. Each represents a distinct model of political organization, cultural expression, and environmental adaptation. The Maya city-states, linked by shared culture and separated by political competition, produced extraordinary achievements in writing, calendrical science, astronomical observation, and monumental architecture. The Maya writing system — one of the few fully phonetic writing systems to develop in the pre-Columbian Americas — allows historians to recover the names and histories of specific rulers and the political dynamics of specific cities in ways impossible for most other ancient American societies.

Teotihuacan represents a different model of Mesoamerican power: a single dominant urban center that concentrated population, craft production, and political influence on a scale without parallel in the classical Americas. At its peak, Teotihuacan was one of the largest cities in the world. Yet Teotihuacan remains in many ways mysterious: without decipherable royal inscriptions, its rulers' names are unknown, its political system unclear, and the causes of its eventual burning and abandonment debated. The fall of Teotihuacan around 650 CE opened a new era of political fragmentation in central Mexico. The Zapotec of Oaxaca, building on deep regional roots, established one of the first true urban centers in Mesoamerica at Monte Albán around 500 BCE. Their civilization, with its distinctive writing system, astronomical orientation, and political tradition, represents an early and distinctive expression of Mesoamerican urban complexity that predates and runs alongside the classical Maya.

In the Andes, the classical era was shaped fundamentally by the unique environmental geography of the region. The principle of vertical ecology — the organization of communities to exploit multiple altitude zones simultaneously — defined Andean economic and political organization in ways that have no close parallel in Mesoamerica or the Old World. The llama and alpaca, the only large domesticated animals in the Americas, provided transport, wool, and protein essential to the functioning of this vertical economy. The Moche of the north coast of Peru built a powerful state whose political authority rested on ritual performance, warrior ideology, and the control of irrigation agriculture. Their extraordinary ceramic art provides a window into a political and religious system in which living rulers impersonated divine figures, offered the blood of sacrificed captives to maintain cosmic order, and derived their authority from demonstrated relationships with supernatural power. The Nazca of the south coast developed a different tradition, centered on pilgrimage and communal ritual, and expressed their relationship with the sacred landscape through the extraordinary geoglyphs that cover the desert surface.

The comparison of classical American civilizations with their contemporaries in Afro-Eurasia reveals both shared patterns and distinctive differences. The independent development of urban complexity, writing, and state power in the Americas demonstrates that these are not uniquely Afro-Eurasian achievements but human responses to common challenges of population organization and resource management. The specific forms these achievements took in the Americas — the corbeled vault, the Long Count calendar, the quipu, the huaca — reflect the distinct environmental, cultural, and historical contexts of American civilization.

The transformations that affected most classical American civilizations between roughly 600 and 900 CE — the decline of Teotihuacan, the collapse of the southern Maya cities, the transformation of Moche and Nazca — were real and significant, but they did not end American civilization. They initiated new phases of political reorganization and cultural development from which later great civilizations — the Toltec, the Mexica, the Tiwanaku, eventually the Inca — would emerge. The thread of continuity in American civilizational history runs unbroken from the classical era through the colonial encounter of the sixteenth century and into the present. Understanding the classical period is therefore not simply an exercise in distant archaeology; it is essential groundwork for understanding the Americas as they actually were when European contact occurred and for understanding the living cultural traditions of Indigenous peoples today.

Key Terms

Mesoamerica
The cultural region of pre-Columbian complex societies extending from central Mexico through northern Central America; characterized by shared cultural traits including the 260-day calendar, the ballgame, and polytheistic religion centered on natural forces.
City-state (polity)
An independent political community consisting of a city and surrounding territory; the dominant form of Maya political organization in the classical period.
Long Count calendar
The Maya system for recording absolute historical dates over long periods; counts days from a mythological starting point in 3114 BCE; found on classical-period stelae.
Tzolk'in
The 260-day Maya ritual calendar, used for divination, naming, and the scheduling of ceremonies; one of two interlocking cycles in the Maya calendar system.
Haab'
The 365-day Maya solar calendar; combined with the Tzolk'in to create the 52-year Calendar Round.
Calendar Round
The 52-year period after which the same combination of Tzolk'in and Haab' days recurs; used for ordinary date-keeping in Mesoamerica.
Epigraphy
The scholarly study and decipherment of inscriptions; central to recovering Maya historical and political records from stone monuments.
Stela (pl. stelae)
A carved stone monument erected by Maya rulers to record political and ritual events, genealogies, and calendar dates.
Ajaw
The Maya title for a king or lord; the head of a city-state political hierarchy, who served simultaneously as political ruler and religious intermediary.
Bloodletting
The ritual practice of drawing one's own blood as an offering to the gods; the central royal ritual of Maya religion, depicted widely in monumental art.
Ballgame
A ritual sport played across Mesoamerica in stone-sided courts with a rubber ball; associated with cosmological themes, sacrifice, and political competition.
Corbeled vault
The characteristic Maya architectural roof form, constructed by cantilevering successive stone courses inward; a "false arch" that creates interior space without a true keystone.
Vertical ecology (vertical archipelago)
The Andean strategy of maintaining access to resources in multiple ecological zones at different altitudes; a fundamental principle of Andean economic and political organization.
Huaca
An Andean sacred place or structure; specifically the great adobe platform mounds of the Moche, which served as temple-palaces and administrative centers.
Sacrifice Ceremony
The complex of Moche ritual depicted in ceramic art in which the ruler, impersonating a divine Warrior Priest, receives the blood of sacrificed captives; confirmed archaeologically by the tomb of the Lord of Sipán.
Nazca Lines
Enormous geoglyphs etched into the Nazca desert surface by removing dark surface stones; depicting geometric forms, animals, and plants; associated with water ritual and the sacred landscape.
Redistribution
An economic system in which a central authority collects goods and labor from the population and redistributes them in the form of food, goods, and services; characteristic of Andean political economies.
Adobe
Sun-dried mudbrick; the primary construction material of Andean coastal civilizations, well suited to the arid Pacific coast environment.
Geoglyph
A large design made on the ground by arranging or removing rocks, soil, or other elements; the Nazca lines are the most famous examples in the ancient Americas.
LiDAR
Light Detection and Ranging; airborne laser scanning technology that can penetrate forest canopy and reveal ground-surface features; has transformed understanding of Maya agricultural infrastructure.
Milpa
The Mesoamerican slash-and-burn agricultural system; plots are cleared from forest, farmed for a few years, then left to fallow before cultivation resumes.
Codex (pl. codices)
A Mesoamerican screenfold book made of bark paper or deerskin, used to record calendrical, religious, and astronomical knowledge; only four pre-Columbian Maya codices survive.
ENSO (El Niño–Southern Oscillation)
Periodic shifts in Pacific Ocean temperature patterns that dramatically affect rainfall and marine resources on the Pacific coast of South America; a recurring environmental challenge for coastal Andean civilizations.
Sacbe (pl. sacbeob)
A Maya raised limestone road connecting ceremonial centers and facilitating trade and ritual procession; sometimes extending for many kilometers between major cities.

  2. What does the decoration on Pakal's sarcophagus — depicting him descending into the underworld while the World Tree rises from his body — tell us about Maya beliefs about the relationship between rulers, death, and cosmic renewal?
  3. The scribes who created the Temple of the Inscriptions texts served Pakal's political interests. How should historians account for the self-serving character of royal inscriptions? What methods might help correct for this bias?
  4. How might the history of Palenque look different if we had access to inscriptions written by Pakal's subjects, rivals, or enemies rather than only the royal court's own accounts?

Cause-and-Effect Review

  1. Explain how Mesoamerican geography — specifically the diversity of ecological zones and the distribution of key resources like obsidian and jade — caused long-distance trade to be essential to classical-era civilizations. What political effects did dependence on trade have?
  2. Trace the causes of the Maya classical collapse in the southern lowlands. Why do historians generally favor a multicausal explanation over a single-cause account?
  3. How did the vertical ecology of the Andes cause particular forms of political organization — specifically redistribution and the vertical archipelago — to develop in Andean civilizations?
  4. What were the consequences of Teotihuacan's collapse around 650 CE for other Mesoamerican societies? How did the political vacuum left by Teotihuacan shape later Mesoamerican history?

Continuity-and-Change Review

  1. What aspects of Maya civilization continued from the preclassical period (before 250 CE) through the classical period (250–900 CE)? What changed?
  2. The Moche state collapsed around 700 CE, but Andean civilization continued. What specific cultural, agricultural, and economic practices persisted in the Andes after the Moche decline?
  3. Compare the experience of change and continuity for Maya kings versus ordinary Maya farmers during the classical collapse of the ninth century. Who experienced more dramatic disruption? Why?
  4. How did the achievements of classical-era Mesoamerican civilizations provide the foundation for later civilizations like the Mexica (Aztec)?

Regional Comparison

  1. Compare the political organization of the classical Maya city-states with the centralized power of Teotihuacan. What were the advantages and disadvantages of each model?
  2. Compare the Moche and Nazca as Andean coastal civilizations. How did their different approaches to political authority, ritual, and settlement reflect different environmental conditions and cultural choices?
  3. Compare classical Mesoamerican approaches to water management with Andean approaches. What problems did each face, and what engineering solutions did each develop?

Global Connections Review

  1. The classical civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes developed independently of the Afro-Eurasian world. What does this independent development tell historians about the conditions that produce complex societies?
  2. In what ways did classical Mesoamerican civilizations connect with each other and with North American societies? What goods and ideas moved through these inter-American networks?
  3. How does the comparison between Teotihuacan and Han China reveal both shared patterns and distinctive differences in classical-era civilization? What are the limits of such comparisons?

Discussion Questions

  1. The Maya developed a fully phonetic writing system without contact with Old World writing traditions. What does this independent invention tell us about the relationship between social complexity and writing? Are there other ways complex societies have organized information and memory without phonetic writing?
  2. Human sacrifice was practiced across classical American civilizations as a central feature of religious and political life. How should historians approach this practice? Should they judge it by the standards of their own time and culture, or try to understand it within the cultural framework of the societies that practiced it?
  3. The Nazca Lines are sometimes associated in popular culture with extraterrestrial intervention. What does the persistence of this explanation tell us about how people encounter unfamiliar historical achievements? How do historians and archaeologists establish and communicate the actual explanations for phenomena like the Nazca Lines?
  4. LiDAR technology has recently revealed an entirely different picture of Maya agricultural complexity than was understood twenty years ago. What does this transformation in historical knowledge tell us about the relationship between technology and historical understanding?
  5. Some scholars argue that comparing classical American civilizations with their contemporaries in Afro-Eurasia risks imposing Eurocentric frameworks on distinctly American experiences.   6. The classical Maya collapse in the southern lowlands has been linked to drought, warfare, political breakdown, and environmental degradation.   7. Women appear relatively infrequently in the primary written record of classical Maya civilization, which was produced primarily by and for elite males. How can historians reconstruct women's roles and experiences? What types of evidence are available, and what are their limits?

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 16 has introduced students to a world of extraordinary human achievement that unfolded independently of the Afro-Eurasian civilizations studied in previous chapters. The Maya, the builders of Teotihuacan, the Zapotec, the Moche, and the Nazca remind us of a fundamental truth about world history: the conditions that produce complex societies — agricultural surplus, population growth, specialized labor, the need for coordination and record-keeping, and the impulse to create meaning and order — are not the exclusive property of any one region of the world. They are human capacities that have found expression wherever the right combination of environmental conditions, cultural trajectories, and historical circumstances has allowed them to do so.

This chapter also illustrates the importance of understanding civilizations on their own terms rather than measuring them against external standards. The Maya developed a fully phonetic writing system and a calendrical and astronomical tradition of remarkable precision. The Moche produced ceramic art that depicts the human face with a realism matched in few other ancient traditions. The Nazca embedded their relationship with the sacred landscape in one of the most unusual and ambitious material expressions in human history. The Andean peoples developed a vertical economy — one of the most sophisticated environmental adaptation strategies in the ancient world — without any contact with the trade networks of Afro-Eurasia. These achievements deserve to be appreciated for what they are, not measured against what they are not.

The chapter has also demonstrated the importance of environmental context in shaping historical outcomes. The particular forms that classical American civilization took — the Maya's hydraulic engineering in the tropical lowlands, the Andean vertical economy, the Moche's irrigation systems in the coastal desert — reflect specific environmental challenges and opportunities. Climate variability, drought, ENSO disruptions, and the limits of agricultural intensification shaped the vulnerabilities as well as the achievements of these civilizations. Understanding how ancient peoples adapted to — and sometimes were overwhelmed by — their environments is essential for understanding both their achievements and the transformations that ended the classical era.

Finally, this chapter connects to one of the most important themes in world history: the consequences of global connectivity, and the catastrophic effects of its sudden disruption. The classical civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes flourished in relative isolation from the Afro-Eurasian world. This isolation meant that the catastrophic epidemic diseases of Afro-Eurasia — smallpox, measles, plague — had not reached the Americas, leaving indigenous populations without the immunological experience that might have helped them survive contact. When European ships finally bridged the Atlantic after 1492, they brought not only soldiers and missionaries but also diseases against which American populations had no defenses. The demographic catastrophe that followed — reducing indigenous populations in some regions by 90 percent or more — was the most devastating consequence of the end of American isolation. Understanding the classical civilizations examined in this chapter means understanding what was lost — and what survived — in that catastrophic encounter. The achievements of classical American civilization survived not only in ruins and museum collections but in living communities whose cultural traditions connect them to their ancestors in profound and continuing ways.

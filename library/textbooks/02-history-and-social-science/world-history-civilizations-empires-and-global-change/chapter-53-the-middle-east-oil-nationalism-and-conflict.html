The Middle East, Oil, Nationalism, and Conflict

World History: Civilizations, Empires, and Global Change

Chapter Opening

"The modern Middle East was not shaped by ancient hatreds but by specific historical decisions — made by colonial administrators, nationalist leaders, oil executives, and revolutionary clerics — whose consequences were felt for the remainder of the twentieth century and beyond."

In the summer of 1920, a British colonial administrator named Arnold Wilson surveyed a territory newly stitched together from the ruins of the Ottoman Empire and declared that the peoples of Mesopotamia — Sunni Arabs, Shia Arabs, Kurds, Assyrian Christians, Jews, Turkmens, and dozens of smaller communities — would benefit from unified rule under the British Crown. Within months, a massive armed rebellion exploded across the middle Euphrates, requiring tens of thousands of British troops and Royal Air Force bombing campaigns to suppress. The British called the uprising an inconvenient surprise. Arab participants called it a revolution for independence. Kurdish leaders called it a betrayal of promises made during the World War. Each group had experienced the same events through an entirely different frame of history, shaped by different memories, different expectations, and different relationships to imperial power. This gap between imperial assumption and local reality would define the modern Middle East across the entire twentieth century.

The modern Middle East did not emerge naturally from geography. It was built — often violently, always partially — by the collision of Ottoman collapse, European imperial ambition, rising Arab nationalism, the global demand for oil, the Zionist movement for a Jewish homeland, Palestinian resistance, Cold War rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union, the Iranian Revolution, repeated interstate wars, and the internal tensions of states governing diverse populations without democratic legitimacy. None of these forces operated in isolation. All of them intersected, reinforced, and sometimes contradicted each other in ways that made the region one of the most complex and contested in twentieth-century world history.

This chapter examines the modern Middle East from the end of World War I through the late twentieth century. The geographical scope encompasses the Arab-majority states of the Fertile Crescent, the Arabian Peninsula, and North Africa; the non-Arab but predominantly Muslim states of Iran and Turkey; the Jewish state of Israel and the stateless Palestinian people; and the broader networks of oil trade, Cold War alliance, and international diplomacy that connected the region to the wider world. The chapter also examines the role of Islam — both as a lived tradition shaping daily life and as a political ideology mobilized by movements seeking to reshape state power.

The central transformations of the modern Middle East are not difficult to identify, even if their causes remain deeply contested. The Ottoman Empire, which had governed most of the Arab Middle East for four centuries, collapsed between 1918 and 1923. In its place arose a patchwork of European mandates, nominally independent kingdoms, and republican states whose borders were drawn with limited attention to the populations they contained. Oil, discovered in commercial quantities in Iran in 1908 and later in Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and across the Gulf, transformed the region's economic importance and drew it into the global energy economy in ways that made outside intervention nearly certain. Arab nationalism, born in the late Ottoman period as a cultural and literary movement, became a mass political force demanding independence, unity, and dignity in the face of European domination. Zionism, the movement to establish a Jewish homeland in Palestine, produced one of the twentieth century's most enduring and violent territorial conflicts. And the Iranian Revolution of 1979 demonstrated that political Islam could overthrow a modernizing monarchy backed by the world's most powerful state and replace it with a system of clerical governance that challenged both secular Arab nationalism and Western liberal assumptions about the direction of political development.

Historians approaching the modern Middle East must work with an unusually complex and contested body of evidence. State archives from Britain, France, the United States, Israel, and the Ottoman Empire have been partially opened, revealing decision-making processes that often had little to do with the stated justifications of imperial policy. Arabic, Persian, Hebrew, and Turkish sources — memoirs, newspapers, political pamphlets, oral histories, literary works, religious legal opinions — offer perspectives largely invisible in Western diplomatic archives. The testimonies of refugees, displaced villagers, soldiers, oil workers, revolutionaries, and ordinary urban residents provide social histories that complicate narratives built around leaders and states. Maps are especially revealing: the borders drawn at the Paris Peace Conference in 1919 and in subsequent agreements were administrative conveniences that cut across tribal territories, irrigation systems, religious communities, and centuries-old trade routes.

Understanding the modern Middle East requires careful attention to both what changed and what continued. Many of the region's deep patterns — the importance of kinship and tribal solidarity, the central role of religion in public life, the relationship between agriculture and water in semiarid environments, the movement of goods and peoples across long-distance trade routes — showed remarkable continuity even as political systems, economies, and social structures were transformed. At the same time, the pace of change was startling in its speed. Within a single century, the Middle East moved from Ottoman administrative provinces to nation-states, from subsistence agriculture and artisanal craft production to oil-driven industrial economies, from populations mostly illiterate and rural to societies with universities, cities of millions, television, and satellite communications. These changes produced new opportunities and catastrophic dislocations in roughly equal measure.

The chapter that follows does not pretend that the modern Middle East's conflicts are simple or that any single interpretation can capture their full complexity. It does insist that they are historically explicable: produced by identifiable forces, driven by real human decisions, shaped by specific economic and political structures, and therefore subject to historical analysis. The story is not one of inevitable religious warfare, ancient ethnic hatreds, or civilizational incompatibility. It is a story of colonialism, resource wealth, competing nationalisms, state failure, Cold War intervention, and the long struggle of diverse peoples to govern themselves on their own terms.

Historical Context

The modern Middle East cannot be understood without first understanding the Ottoman Empire and the circumstances of its collapse. For roughly four centuries, from the early sixteenth century to the early twentieth, the Ottoman state governed a vast territory stretching from southeastern Europe to Arabia, from Egypt to the borders of Iran. Ottoman rule was neither purely oppressive nor uniformly beneficial: it provided administrative order, recognized religious communities through the millet system, supported trade, and maintained relative peace across large territories, but it also collected heavy taxes, conscripted soldiers, and suppressed political dissent.

The Ottoman Empire's long decline accelerated dramatically in the nineteenth century. Military defeats at the hands of European powers, particularly Russia, exposed the state's growing technological and organizational weaknesses. The Tanzimat reforms of the mid-nineteenth century — an effort to modernize the army, the legal system, and the bureaucracy along European lines — improved some aspects of governance but also provoked fierce debates about Ottoman identity, Islamic tradition, and the proper relationship between state and religion. The Young Turk Revolution of 1908 brought a new generation of reformist officers to power, but their increasingly nationalist and authoritarian rule alienated Arab, Armenian, Greek, and Kurdish subjects.

World War I proved fatal to the Ottoman state. The Ottoman leadership, dominated by a triumvirate of nationalist officers, chose to ally with Germany and Austria-Hungary in 1914. This decision brought the empire into direct conflict with Britain, France, and Russia. British forces invaded Ottoman Mesopotamia, fought a brutal campaign in the Gallipoli peninsula, and supported an Arab Revolt — promised independence in return for military cooperation — in the Hijaz. The war also produced the Armenian Genocide of 1915–1916, in which the Ottoman government systematically deported and killed approximately one million Armenians, one of the first genocides of the twentieth century.

When the war ended in 1918, the Ottoman Empire lay in ruins. British and French forces occupied large territories, and the Paris Peace Conference of 1919 began the process of dividing Ottoman lands. The resulting Sykes-Picot Agreement, secretly negotiated in 1916, had already divided anticipated British and French spheres of influence. The League of Nations mandate system formalized this division, granting Britain administrative control over Mesopotamia (Iraq) and Palestine, and granting France control over Syria and Lebanon. These were not colonies in name, but in practice they functioned as imperial possessions: local populations were governed by European officials, their borders were drawn by European diplomats, and their natural resources were exploited by European companies.

Oil added an entirely new dimension to the geopolitical importance of the Middle East. The Anglo-Persian Oil Company had been extracting oil from southwestern Iran since 1908. British control of Mesopotamian territories — later Iraq — was partly motivated by anticipated oil reserves, which proved enormous when commercial production began in the 1920s and 1930s. The discovery of oil in Saudi Arabia in 1938 and its subsequent development by the Arabian American Oil Company (Aramco) established the Arabian Peninsula as the center of the world's largest petroleum reserves. By the mid-twentieth century, Middle Eastern oil was essential to the industrialized economies of Europe, North America, and Asia, making the region a permanent object of great-power attention.

Regional Orientation

The Middle East as a geographical concept is itself historically constructed. The term was coined by British imperial strategists in the early twentieth century to describe the territories between the "Near East" (the eastern Mediterranean) and the "Far East" (South and East Asia). It has no precise or universally agreed definition: some definitions include only the Arab states of the Fertile Crescent and Arabian Peninsula, while others extend to Turkey, Iran, Afghanistan, or the Maghreb. This chapter uses a broad definition encompassing the Arab-majority states from Morocco to Iraq, Israel and the Palestinian territories, Iran, and Turkey, while focusing most closely on the core Arab states, Iran, and Israel-Palestine.

The physical geography of the region is dominated by aridity. Most of the Middle East receives little rainfall, and the great rivers — the Nile, the Tigris, the Euphrates, and the Jordan — have always been essential to settled agriculture. The Arabian Peninsula is largely desert; the Fertile Crescent, stretching from the Levant through Mesopotamia, provided the agricultural surplus that sustained the great ancient civilizations of the region. The mountains of Iran, Turkey, Lebanon, and the Levantine coast receive more rainfall and historically supported dense populations. Access to water has always been a political issue, and it became even more so in the twentieth century as populations grew rapidly.

The demographic complexity of the Middle East is extraordinary. Arabic is the majority language across a large swath of territory, but it coexists with Persian in Iran, Turkish in Turkey, Kurdish across parts of Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria, Hebrew in Israel, Berber languages in North Africa, and dozens of smaller languages. Religiously, the region is predominantly Muslim but deeply divided between Sunni and Shia traditions, with significant Christian, Jewish, Druze, Alawi, Yazidi, and other minority communities. These divisions do not map neatly onto national boundaries, which is one reason why the nation-state model imposed by European powers after World War I created such persistent tensions.

The region's neighbors and the broader global system shaped its history profoundly. To the north, Russia and later the Soviet Union competed with Britain for influence in Iran and sought access to warm-water ports. To the west, European powers — particularly Britain and France — dominated the region's politics through most of the twentieth century's first half. To the east, South Asia provided labor migrants, trade partners, and, through Pakistan after 1947, a fellow Muslim state. The United States, which had limited interest in the Middle East before World War II, became the dominant outside power after 1945, motivated by oil, Cold War strategy, and the special relationship with Israel.

Section 53.1

The Middle East After World War I

The end of World War I opened one of the most consequential periods of political transformation in the history of the Middle East. Four centuries of Ottoman rule came to an abrupt end, and in its place arose a patchwork of European mandates, protectorates, and nominally independent kingdoms whose borders, institutions, and international relationships were largely determined by European powers with limited knowledge of and limited interest in the preferences of the populations they governed. The decisions made in Paris, London, and San Remo between 1919 and 1922 created the political geography of the modern Arab Middle East, and their consequences — disputed borders, artificial states, unresolved national claims — shaped the region's conflicts throughout the remainder of the twentieth century.

The peace settlement that followed World War I was animated by the principle of national self-determination, articulated most forcefully by American president Woodrow Wilson in his Fourteen Points. This principle — that peoples should govern themselves — was applied selectively, however. It justified the dissolution of the German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman empires and the creation of new national states in Central and Eastern Europe. But it was not applied to the Arab populations of the former Ottoman Empire, who were instead placed under British and French mandatory rule despite clear expressions of preference for independence. A 1919 American commission, the King-Crane Commission, surveyed Syrian and Palestinian public opinion and found widespread support for independence and opposition to French rule in Syria and to Zionist immigration in Palestine. The commission's findings were largely ignored.

The mandates created by the League of Nations were formally justified as temporary arrangements to guide territories toward eventual independence, but in practice they functioned as imperial possessions. British Mandatory Palestine was governed according to the Balfour Declaration of 1917, which had promised British support for a Jewish national home in Palestine while pledging to protect the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities. The two commitments were increasingly difficult to reconcile as Jewish immigration accelerated in the 1920s and 1930s, particularly as the rise of Nazi persecution in Europe created desperate demand for a safe haven. By the 1930s, Arab Palestinian resistance to Jewish immigration had produced a major uprising — the Great Arab Revolt of 1936–1939 — that the British suppressed through military force, collective punishment, and the recruitment of Jewish auxiliary forces.

The British mandatory territory of Mesopotamia became the Kingdom of Iraq in 1921, with the Hashemite prince Faisal installed as king by the British. Iraq combined three former Ottoman provinces — Mosul, Baghdad, and Basra — that had never previously functioned as a unified political unit. The northern province of Mosul contained a large Kurdish population that had been promised autonomy or even independence by the 1920 Treaty of Sèvres; that promise was abandoned when Mosul was awarded to Iraq by the League of Nations in 1926, partly due to its oil reserves. The Shia Muslim majority in southern and central Iraq had been almost entirely excluded from Ottoman administration and was similarly underrepresented in the new Iraqi state.

French mandatory Syria was similarly divided. France separated the predominantly Christian districts of Lebanon from Syria, creating the state of Greater Lebanon — the forerunner of modern Lebanon — partly to protect the Maronite Christian community that French missionaries had cultivated since the nineteenth century. This decision created a Lebanese state with a delicate demographic balance between Maronite Christians, Sunni Muslims, Shia Muslims, Druze, and Greek Orthodox Christians, a balance that would prove explosive in subsequent decades. In Syria proper, the French suppressed a major revolt (the Great Syrian Revolt of 1925–1927) and divided the territory into smaller administrative units to prevent unified opposition.

Trans-Jordan — the territory east of the Jordan River — was administered by Britain as a separate mandate and placed under the rule of Abdullah, another Hashemite prince and brother of Faisal. The territory was relatively poor in natural resources and strategically located between Iraq, Palestine, and the Arabian Peninsula. Its formal independence came in 1946, making it the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan. Arabia itself remained outside the mandate system. The Arabian Peninsula was dominated by the rise of Abd al-Aziz ibn Saud, who unified most of the peninsula through military conquest by 1932, creating the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia. His alliance with the Wahhabi religious movement gave the new kingdom its distinctive conservative Islamic character.

Turkey, meanwhile, underwent a remarkably different transformation. Mustafa Kemal, the military officer who led successful Turkish resistance against Greek military invasion and European partition plans, established the Turkish Republic in 1923. The Republic pursued an aggressively secular and nationalist modernization program: the Caliphate was abolished in 1924, Islamic law was replaced by European-style legal codes, the Arabic script was replaced by a Latin alphabet, religious education was restricted, and women were granted political rights. Turkey, unlike the Arab Middle East, achieved genuine independence from European control — partly because its military strength made domination too costly and partly because it was not sitting atop vast oil reserves that European powers were determined to control.

Section 53.2

Mandates and New States

The mandate system created states that were new in name but ancient in the complexity of the populations they governed. Understanding why the mandates produced such persistent instability requires looking closely at what the mandatory powers actually did — and equally, at what they deliberately chose not to do.

The British mandatory administration in Iraq faced an immediate contradiction. The state had been created to serve British strategic and economic interests: access to Mesopotamian oil, a land route to India, and a buffer against potential Russian expansion toward the Gulf. To achieve these goals, Britain needed a functioning Iraqi state capable of maintaining order. But it also needed to prevent that state from becoming powerful enough to challenge British prerogatives. The solution — characteristic of British imperial policy across the globe — was to work through cooperative local elites while retaining ultimate authority over defense, foreign policy, and economic arrangements. The Hashemite monarchy and the Sunni Arab officer class, many of whom had served in the Ottoman army, became Britain's primary partners.

This approach systematically marginalized the Shia majority and the Kurdish north. Shia religious scholars (ulama) who had led the 1920 revolt were exiled or marginalized. Kurdish demands for autonomy were suppressed militarily, with the Royal Air Force bombing Kurdish villages as early as 1924. The oil fields of Kirkuk, discovered in 1927 and developed by the Iraq Petroleum Company — a consortium of British, French, and American oil companies — generated revenues that flowed primarily to the central government and to foreign shareholders rather than to the populations of the oil-producing regions.

Iraq gained nominal independence in 1932, becoming the first Arab mandatory state to be admitted to the League of Nations. But independence was circumscribed: Britain retained military bases, preferential trade relationships, and the right to intervene in Iraqi affairs to protect its strategic interests. The formal structures of parliamentary government were fragile; real power rested with the military and with a small group of politicians closely tied to the Hashemite throne. In 1958, a military coup led by officers sympathetic to Arab nationalism overthrew the monarchy, killed the king and most of the royal family, and declared a republic. The violence of the transition — and the instability that followed — reflected the depth of the contradictions built into the mandate state.

Mandatory Palestine was the site of the mandate system's most intractable failure. The Balfour Declaration's promise of a Jewish national home in a territory where, in 1917, the population was approximately ninety percent Arab Christian and Muslim created a demographic and political equation that no British policy could square. The British faced pressure from two directions simultaneously: from the Zionist movement and its supporters in Britain and the United States, who insisted on the right to Jewish immigration and land purchase; and from the Arab Palestinian leadership, who demanded an end to immigration and representative government reflecting the Arab majority. British attempts at compromise — the various White Papers that periodically restricted and then eased immigration quotas — satisfied neither side.

Jewish immigration to Palestine grew substantially in the 1920s and 1930s. The Fifth Aliyah — the wave of immigration brought by Nazi persecution in Europe after 1933 — brought hundreds of thousands of largely educated, skilled European Jews. These immigrants brought capital, professional skills, and political organization. The Zionist movement built parallel institutions — the Jewish Agency, the Haganah defense force, agricultural settlements, schools, and hospitals — that functioned as a state-in-waiting within the mandate. Arab Palestinian society was less institutionally organized and more socially divided; the traditional leadership of the Husseini and Nashashibi families feuded with each other and failed to develop effective political alternatives to the British-Zionist arrangement.

Lebanon's mandate experience produced a state designed around communal power-sharing. The French-sponsored National Pact of 1943 allocated political offices according to religious community: the president would be Maronite Christian, the prime minister Sunni Muslim, the speaker of parliament Shia Muslim, and parliamentary seats would be distributed according to a six-to-five ratio of Christians to Muslims. These arrangements were based on a 1932 census whose demographic findings would soon be outdated — Muslim birth rates exceeded Christian rates — but the confessional system proved remarkably durable, embedding communal identity into the political structure for the remainder of the century.

Syria's path to independence was more conflicted. French mandatory administration was widely resented, and a series of nationalist movements challenged French authority throughout the interwar period. The French finally agreed to Syrian independence in 1944, formally confirmed in 1946, but the political institutions left behind were fragile. The Syrian military, which had been built with French assistance, proved far more cohesive than the civilian political parties, and a series of military coups followed independence. Syria's repeated political instability — it experienced fourteen coups between 1949 and 1970 — reflected both the internal weaknesses of the mandate state and the destabilizing effects of the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, which discredited the old political elite.

Historical Vocabulary
Mandate System

The mandate system was the League of Nations framework established after World War I to govern territories taken from the defeated German and Ottoman empires. Mandatory powers — Britain and France for most of the Middle East — were theoretically trustees, obligated to prepare territories for eventual self-governance. In practice, mandates functioned as colonies: the mandatory powers controlled military, foreign policy, and key economic decisions.

The system was justified by the assumption that non-Western peoples were not yet "ready" for self-governance, an assumption that Arab, African, and Asian populations across the globe rejected from the outset. Understanding the mandate system is essential to understanding why the borders, institutions, and political cultures of the modern Middle East took the form they did — and why the promises made during World War I about Arab independence generated such enduring resentment when they were broken.

Section 53.3

Arab Nationalism and Political Change

Arab nationalism emerged as a cultural and intellectual movement in the late nineteenth century, born among Arab Christians and Muslims educated in Beirut, Damascus, Cairo, and Istanbul who were grappling with the question of collective identity in an era of rapid change. The initial Arab nationalist thinkers were often Christian Arabs who found in a shared Arab language and history a basis for identity that transcended religious difference. By the early twentieth century, Arab nationalism had become a political force demanding greater Arab autonomy within the Ottoman Empire, and after the empire's collapse, independence from European control.

The ideology of Arab nationalism rested on several core claims: that Arabs shared a common language, culture, and history that gave them the right to political self-determination; that this natural community had been artificially fragmented by imperial powers; and that Arab unity was both a historical destiny and a practical necessity. These claims were mobilized most powerfully against European imperialism, but they also raised difficult questions about the place of non-Arab minorities — Kurds, Armenians, Assyrians, Jews, Berbers — within a presumptively Arab political space, and about the relationship between Arab national identity and Islamic religious identity.

The interwar period saw Arab nationalism develop from an elite intellectual movement into a mass political force. The failures of the mandate system — the broken promises of independence, the imposition of European rule, the suppression of uprisings in Syria, Iraq, and Palestine — provided constant fuel for nationalist mobilization. Political parties, newspapers, youth organizations, and eventually military officers carried nationalist ideas into new social groups. In Egypt, nationalism had a somewhat different character: rather than Pan-Arab ideology, Egyptian nationalism emphasized Egypt's distinctive identity, its ancient civilization, and its aspirations to lead the broader Arab world.

Egypt became the center of Arab political life in the 1950s. The 1952 Revolution — actually a military coup led by a group of officers calling themselves the Free Officers — overthrew the corrupt and discredited monarchy of King Farouk. Gamal Abdel Nasser emerged as the dominant figure within the revolutionary government and eventually as Egypt's president. Nasser combined Arab nationalist ideology with a practical program of state-led economic development, land redistribution, and non-alignment in the Cold War. He was, in many respects, a master of political symbolism and mass mobilization. His 1956 nationalization of the Suez Canal Company — previously owned by British and French shareholders — was one of the most electrifying political acts in modern Arab history.

The Suez Crisis of 1956 crystallized the political landscape of the Cold War Middle East. Britain, France, and Israel secretly coordinated a military attack on Egypt following the nationalization of the canal. They calculated that American acquiescence was assured. They were wrong: President Eisenhower, furious that his allies had acted without consulting the United States and alarmed by the Soviet threat to intervene on Egypt's behalf, forced Britain and France to withdraw. The crisis ended with Nasser's prestige enormously enhanced, British and French imperial influence in the Middle East effectively ended, and American and Soviet influence dominant. It was a pivotal moment in the history of decolonization — one that demonstrated that the era of European imperial management of the region was over.

Nasser's Egypt became the model for a generation of Arab nationalist leaders. His use of radio — particularly the Voice of the Arabs broadcasts, which reached millions of listeners across the Arab world — made him a transnational political figure. His articulation of a non-aligned position between the American and Soviet blocs resonated with populations exhausted by European domination and suspicious of both superpowers. The United Arab Republic — a short-lived political union of Egypt and Syria (1958–1961) — represented the apex of Pan-Arab aspirations, but also revealed their limits: Syrian military officers resented Egyptian dominance and dissolved the union after a coup.

The Baath Party in Iraq and Syria offered a different version of Arab nationalism. The Baath — meaning "renaissance" in Arabic — was founded in Damascus in 1947 by Michel Aflaq and Salah al-Din al-Bitar, both educated at the Sorbonne. Baathism combined Arab nationalism with a version of socialism: it called for Arab political unity, state control of key industries, land redistribution, and secular governance. The Baath Party took power in Iraq in 1968 and has ruled Syria since 1963, but in both countries it quickly became the vehicle for military dictatorships rather than the democratic renaissance its founders had envisioned.

Arab nationalism's failure in the 1967 Six-Day War dealt a devastating blow to the ideology's credibility. The rapid and overwhelming defeat of Egypt, Syria, and Jordan by Israel — achieved in six days of fighting — shattered the narrative of Arab strength and solidarity. Nasser himself offered to resign in a broadcast immediately after the defeat; a wave of popular demonstrations urged him to stay. He did, until his death in 1970. But the moral authority of Arab nationalist governments had been fatally compromised. States that had justified their authoritarian rule by promising liberation of Palestine had suffered their most humiliating defeat. The generation that came of age after 1967 increasingly looked to alternatives: Palestinian nationalism, Marxism, and, most consequentially, political Islam.

Section 53.4

Zionism, Israel, and Palestinian Displacement

Zionism — the movement to establish a Jewish national homeland — was a product of the same era of European nationalism that produced Italian, German, Polish, and Czech national movements in the nineteenth century. Its distinctive feature was that the population seeking a national state lacked a concentrated territorial base: Jews in nineteenth-century Europe were a dispersed minority living in many different countries, speaking different languages, and experiencing different legal conditions. Theodor Herzl, the Austro-Hungarian journalist who organized the First Zionist Congress in Basel in 1897, argued that anti-Semitism was an incurable feature of European civilization, and that the only solution was Jewish emigration to a territory where Jews could govern themselves.

Herzl and the early Zionist movement initially considered several possible territories, including Argentina and Uganda. But the movement's cultural wing insisted that only Palestine — the ancient homeland of the biblical Israelites — could serve as the legitimate center of Jewish national identity. This argument prevailed, and Palestine became the focus of Zionist immigration and institution-building. The first significant wave of Zionist immigration (the First Aliyah) began in the 1880s; subsequent waves brought increasing numbers of Jewish immigrants, predominantly from Eastern Europe, through the early twentieth century.

The Balfour Declaration of November 1917 — a letter from British Foreign Secretary Arthur James Balfour to Lord Walter Rothschild, a leader of the British Jewish community — stated that His Majesty's Government viewed with favor the establishment in Palestine of a national home for the Jewish people, while clearly understanding that nothing should be done that might prejudice the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities in Palestine. This carefully worded document became the foundational text of British support for Zionism and the source of one of the century's most enduring political debates about what had actually been promised to whom.

The Arab Palestinian response to Zionist immigration was not simply a matter of prejudice or anti-Semitism, though such elements existed, as they did in all communities. Arab Palestinian opposition was rooted in the recognition that Zionist immigration represented a demographic and political transformation of their homeland. Arab Palestinian farmers who sold or lost land to Zionist purchasers found themselves without livelihoods. Arab Palestinian workers found themselves excluded from the exclusively Jewish labor market that Zionist institutions built as a matter of ideological principle. Arab Palestinian political leaders, who had expected independence after the war — as promised in the Hussein-McMahon correspondence — found themselves instead subject to a British administration committed to facilitating a Jewish national home.

The 1948 War — which Israelis call the War of Independence and Palestinians call the Nakba, or catastrophe — was the founding event of the modern Israeli-Palestinian conflict. When the British Mandate expired in May 1948, the Jewish Agency declared the establishment of the State of Israel. Armies from Egypt, Transjordan, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon invaded, seeking to prevent or undo Israeli statehood. The war lasted until the armistice agreements of 1949. Israel won, establishing control over more territory than had been allocated to it by the 1947 UN Partition Plan. Jordan retained control of the West Bank and East Jerusalem; Egypt retained control of the Gaza Strip.

The demographic consequences of the 1948 War for Palestinian Arabs were catastrophic. Approximately 700,000 Palestinians — roughly half the Arab Palestinian population — fled or were expelled from their homes during the fighting. They became refugees in neighboring Arab states, particularly Jordan, Lebanon, and Syria, as well as in the West Bank and Gaza. The refugee crisis was not simply a consequence of war: significant evidence indicates that Zionist military forces, operating under Plan Dalet, systematically expelled Arab populations from many villages in areas designated for the Jewish state or strategically important to it. The village of Deir Yassin, where more than a hundred villagers were killed by Irgun and Lehi fighters in April 1948, became the most notorious and widely publicized instance of anti-Palestinian violence.

The Palestinian refugees and their descendants — now numbering in the millions — remained stateless for the remainder of the twentieth century and beyond. Israel refused to permit their return, arguing that Palestinian repatriation would threaten Israel's Jewish demographic majority and security. Arab states, with the partial exception of Jordan, refused to grant Palestinian refugees citizenship, preserving their status as refugees in part to maintain political pressure for return and in part because naturalization would have posed serious demographic challenges for host countries. UNRWA (the United Nations Relief and Works Agency) was established in 1950 to provide basic services to Palestinian refugees, a task it was still performing decades later.

The Palestinian people's political organization developed slowly. The Palestine Liberation Organization was founded in 1964 by the Arab League as a vehicle for representing Palestinian national aspirations. Yasser Arafat's Fatah movement, which had been conducting guerrilla operations since the early 1960s, took over the PLO in 1969 after the organization's early leadership was discredited by the 1967 defeat. The PLO became a government-in-exile, building military and social institutions, representing Palestinians in international forums, and conducting military operations against Israeli targets — including, controversially, operations against civilian targets in Europe and the 1972 Munich Olympics attack on Israeli athletes.

Regional Lens

The Palestinian Experience

Palestinian history in the twentieth century is inseparable from the experience of dispossession, statelessness, and occupied life. Before 1948, Palestinian society was predominantly rural, with a vibrant urban bourgeoisie in cities like Jaffa, Haifa, and Jerusalem. Palestinian farmers (fellaheen) cultivated olive groves, citrus orchards, and wheat fields; Palestinian merchants traded across the eastern Mediterranean; Palestinian intellectuals published newspapers, wrote poetry, and engaged with Arab and Ottoman cultural life.

The Nakba shattered this society. Families were scattered across multiple countries; villages were depopulated; property was confiscated. The refugee camps of Gaza, the West Bank, Lebanon, Jordan, and Syria became defining spaces of Palestinian life — crowded, controlled, maintained by international aid, oriented toward the hope of return. Palestinian culture in exile developed a distinctive literature of longing: poets like Mahmoud Darwish articulated the ache of displacement in verse that became central to Palestinian national identity.

Palestinians who remained in Israel — approximately 150,000 in 1948, growing to over a million by the century's end — lived as citizens with reduced rights, subject to military administration until 1966, restricted in where they could live and work. Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza, occupied by Israel after 1967, lived under military rule: subject to curfews, checkpoints, administrative detention, land confiscation, and the expansion of Israeli settlements. The Palestinian experience encompasses the secular nationalism of the PLO and the Islamism of Hamas; the middle-class professional and the camp resident; the non-violent activist and the militant fighter. What unifies these experiences is the weight of political exclusion and the continuing aspiration for sovereignty, return, and recognition.

Section 53.5

Arab-Israeli Wars and Peace Efforts

The 1948 War was the first of a series of armed conflicts that defined the Arab-Israeli relationship throughout the second half of the twentieth century. Each war reshaped political geography and regional alignments; each was followed by negotiations that failed to produce a comprehensive settlement; each bequeathed grievances and territorial disputes that contributed to the next round of violence.

The 1956 Suez-Sinai War, discussed above in relation to Arab nationalism, was also an Israeli military operation. Israel attacked Egypt in coordination with Britain and France, seizing the Sinai Peninsula. American pressure forced Israeli withdrawal from Sinai in 1957, with international observers deployed to prevent future Egyptian blockade of the Strait of Tiran. The war demonstrated Israeli military capability and created a temporary but brittle equilibrium.

The 1967 Six-Day War was the most consequential conflict in the history of the Arab-Israeli conflict. In May 1967, Egyptian President Nasser expelled UN observers from Sinai, remilitarized the peninsula, and announced a blockade of the Strait of Tiran. Jordan and Syria mobilized as well; there was genuine fear in Israel that it faced coordinated attack. On June 5, Israel launched a preemptive air strike that destroyed most of the Egyptian, Syrian, and Jordanian air forces on the ground within hours. Without air cover, Arab ground forces were at a catastrophic disadvantage. Within six days, Israel had seized the Sinai Peninsula from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria.

The consequences of the Six-Day War were profound and lasting. Israel now controlled territories with a combined Palestinian Arab and Syrian population of over a million people. UN Security Council Resolution 242, passed in November 1967, called for Israeli withdrawal from territories occupied in the recent conflict in exchange for recognition of all states' right to peace and security — the formula of "land for peace." But what those territories were, and what constituted adequate security, was immediately contested. Israel began building settlements in the West Bank and Gaza — a policy that accelerated in subsequent decades and that international law consistently characterized as illegal, even as Israeli governments disputed that characterization.

The Yom Kippur War (or October War) of 1973 was launched by Egypt and Syria in a coordinated surprise attack on Israeli positions in Sinai and the Golan Heights. Egyptian forces crossed the Suez Canal and breached Israeli defensive lines in a dramatically successful initial assault. The war lasted three weeks before a ceasefire brokered by the superpowers. Israel recovered militarily and ended the war with its territorial position roughly restored, but the image of Israeli invincibility had been punctured, and Egypt had achieved the political victory of demonstrating military competence. Egyptian President Anwar Sadat, who had planned the war partly to create conditions for diplomacy, eventually parlayed the achievement into a peace process.

The Camp David Accords of 1978 and the Egypt-Israel Peace Treaty of 1979 were the most significant diplomatic breakthrough of the Arab-Israeli conflict. Egyptian President Anwar Sadat's dramatic visit to Jerusalem in 1977 — the first visit by an Arab head of state to Israel — shocked the Arab world and opened a path to negotiation. American President Jimmy Carter brokered the Camp David negotiations. The resulting treaty returned Sinai to Egypt and normalized relations between the two states; in exchange, Egypt recognized Israel's right to exist. But the treaty was silent on the Palestinian question, and the Arab League expelled Egypt for making a separate peace. Sadat was assassinated by Egyptian Islamists in 1981.

The 1982 Israeli invasion of Lebanon — launched to destroy PLO military infrastructure — added a devastating new chapter to the conflict. Israeli forces besieged Beirut, forced the PLO to evacuate to Tunisia, and then allowed Lebanese Christian militias to enter the Palestinian refugee camps of Sabra and Shatila, where the militias massacred hundreds of unarmed civilians. Israeli Defense Minister Ariel Sharon was found by an Israeli commission to bear personal responsibility for not preventing the massacres. The invasion also provided the conditions for the creation of Hezbollah — a Lebanese Shia organization backed by Iran — which conducted a sustained guerrilla campaign that eventually forced Israeli withdrawal from Lebanon in 2000.

The Oslo Accords of 1993 represented the most ambitious attempt at a negotiated two-state settlement. Secret negotiations between Israeli officials and PLO representatives in Oslo, Norway, produced a Declaration of Principles signed on the White House lawn: PLO chairman Yasser Arafat and Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin shook hands, and the world was briefly hopeful. The Oslo process created the Palestinian Authority, gave the PLO administrative control over portions of the West Bank and Gaza, and envisioned a final-status agreement on borders, Jerusalem, settlements, and refugees to be negotiated within five years. The five-year deadline was not met; the final-status negotiations at Camp David in 2000 collapsed; Rabin was assassinated by a Jewish Israeli extremist in 1995; and a Second Intifada of Palestinian violence against Israeli civilians began in 2000, producing Israeli military reoccupation of much of the West Bank. The peace process entered a long period of collapse from which it had not recovered by the end of the twentieth century.

Primary Source Window

United Nations Security Council Resolution 242 (1967)

Creator: United Nations Security Council · Date: November 22, 1967 · Type: International diplomatic document; UN Security Council resolution

Context: Passed five months after the Six-Day War in which Israel had seized the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, and the Golan Heights. The resolution was drafted by the British diplomat Lord Caradon and adopted unanimously by the Security Council. It became the foundational text for all subsequent Arab-Israeli peace negotiations.

The resolution affirmed the inadmissibility of the acquisition of territory by war and the need for a just and lasting peace. It called for Israeli withdrawal from "territories occupied in the recent conflict" and for acknowledgment of the sovereignty and territorial integrity of every state in the area, as well as a just settlement of the refugee problem.

The critical ambiguity: The English text called for Israeli withdrawal from "territories" (without the definite article); the French text (equally authoritative under UN rules) referred to "the territories." This difference — whether Israel was required to withdraw from all or merely some of the occupied territories — was immediately contested and has never been definitively resolved, providing each party with textual justification for its preferred interpretation.

Analysis: Resolution 242 illustrates several features of international diplomacy: the gap between the unanimous agreement that a resolution produces and the actual consensus on its meaning; the role of linguistic ambiguity as a diplomatic tool that allows parties to sign documents while maintaining their core positions; and the difference between formal international law and actual political outcomes. The resolution called for Israeli withdrawal and Arab recognition of Israeli statehood, but provided no enforcement mechanism and no timetable, leaving implementation entirely to the parties' willingness to negotiate.

Section 53.6

Oil, OPEC, and Global Power

Oil transformed the Middle East more fundamentally than any other single force of the twentieth century. Before oil, the Arabian Peninsula was among the poorest and most peripheral regions of the world. Within a few decades of commercial oil production, it had become one of the most strategically important, and its rulers had accumulated wealth on a scale that dwarfed anything in the region's history. Understanding the political economy of Middle Eastern oil requires examining how it was discovered, who controlled it, how control changed, and what the consequences were for regional and global politics.

The first major Middle Eastern oil strike was in southwestern Iran in 1908, when the Anglo-Persian Oil Company (later Anglo-Iranian, later BP) struck oil at Masjid Suleiman after years of exploration. The company had a concession — essentially a monopoly right to explore and extract oil — granted by the Iranian government in exchange for royalties. Similar concession arrangements were negotiated throughout the region: Iraq Petroleum Company (a consortium of British, French, and American oil companies) held concessions over Iraqi oil; the Arabian American Oil Company (Aramco) held Saudi Arabian concessions; British companies dominated Kuwaiti and Iranian production.

The concession system was deeply disadvantageous to oil-producing states. Royalty rates were often low, calculated on volume rather than price, and audited — or not audited — by the companies themselves. The companies controlled production rates, export volumes, and posted prices — the prices they declared for tax purposes, which were not necessarily market prices. For most of the first half of the twentieth century, the enormous wealth generated by Middle Eastern oil flowed primarily to Western shareholders, with relatively modest returns to the governments and populations of the producing countries.

The shift in the balance of power began with a series of challenges to the old concession system. In 1951, Iranian Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company — the first major nationalization of a Western oil company in the Middle East. The British organized an international boycott of Iranian oil and, with American CIA assistance, orchestrated a coup in 1953 that overthrew Mossadegh and restored Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. A new agreement gave a consortium of Western companies control of Iranian oil production, though with higher royalty rates. The episode demonstrated that Western governments were willing to use covert force to protect oil company interests, and it embedded Mossadegh's memory as a martyr of Iranian nationalism.

OPEC — the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries — was founded in Baghdad in September 1960, by Venezuela, Iraq, Iran, Kuwait, and Saudi Arabia. It was initially a limited organization; the major oil companies remained dominant for another decade. But OPEC gradually asserted control over posted prices and, more importantly, built the institutional capacity for collective action. The 1970s brought the moment of decisive power shift. A wave of nationalizations transferred actual ownership of oil fields from Western companies to national companies: Saudi Aramco, the National Iranian Oil Company, the Iraq National Oil Company, and similar state enterprises took direct control of oil production.

The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo was the most dramatic demonstration of oil power. Following the outbreak of the Yom Kippur War in October 1973, Arab members of OPEC — led by Saudi Arabia — imposed an embargo on oil exports to the United States and the Netherlands for their support of Israel. The embargo quadrupled global oil prices within months, producing gasoline shortages, long lines at filling stations, and economic disruption across the industrialized world. The message was unmistakable: oil-producing states could use petroleum as a political weapon, and the industrialized world's dependence on Middle Eastern oil was a structural vulnerability. The experience drove decades of Western investment in energy efficiency, alternative energy sources, and reserve diversification.

The oil wealth that flowed into Gulf states after 1973 was extraordinary. Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the United Arab Emirates, and Qatar became suddenly among the wealthiest societies in the world, measured by per-capita income. This wealth funded massive development programs: desalination plants, airports, hospitals, universities, highways, and entire new cities. It also funded the importation of millions of foreign workers — from South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Egypt — who performed the labor that Gulf citizens no longer needed to do. The social consequences of this rapid transformation were profound: traditional Bedouin and merchant societies were abruptly integrated into global consumer capitalism while retaining conservative social structures, creating tensions between material modernity and cultural traditionalism that persisted through the century.

Oil wealth also had distorting effects on political economy. The "resource curse" — the pattern by which resource wealth undermines democratic governance and diversified economic development — applied with particular force to Middle Eastern oil states. When governments can fund themselves from resource revenues without taxing citizens, they have less need for the political bargains that taxation historically requires. Gulf monarchies remained absolute, distributing patronage rather than developing accountable institutions. Oil-rich republics like Iraq and Libya used resource revenues to fund military expansion and internal security apparatus without developing sustainable civilian economies.

Global Connection

Middle Eastern Oil and the Global Economy

The relationship between Middle Eastern oil and the global economy is one of the most consequential connections in twentieth-century world history. When the industrialized world built its transportation infrastructure, industrial capacity, and residential heating around petroleum after World War II, it created a structural dependence on imported oil. The Middle East, which contained more than half the world's proven petroleum reserves, became the indispensable supplier.

This dependence shaped global politics in every subsequent decade. American Cold War strategy in the Middle East was inseparable from oil: the Eisenhower Doctrine of 1957, which promised American military support to Middle Eastern governments threatened by communist subversion, was explicitly designed to protect oil-producing states from Soviet influence. Saudi Arabia and the United States developed a relationship of mutual dependence: Saudi Arabia provided oil stability and invested its petrodollars in American financial markets; the United States provided security guarantees and arms sales.

Petrodollars — the enormous revenues earned by oil producers, particularly after the price increases of the 1970s — reshaped global finance. Unable to invest all their revenues domestically, Gulf states deposited petrodollars in Western banks, which recycled them as loans to developing countries. This recycling contributed to the Third World debt crisis of the 1980s. It also fueled real estate markets, stock markets, and consumer economies across Europe and North America. The global financial system and the global energy system became deeply entangled in ways whose full consequences took decades to become clear.

Section 53.7

Iran: Monarchy, Revolution, and Republic

Iran occupies a distinctive position in the modern Middle East. It is the region's largest non-Arab country, with a Persian-speaking population of deep cultural sophistication and historical self-consciousness. It is the world's largest Shia Muslim majority state, which gives it a special relationship with Shia communities in Iraq, Lebanon, Bahrain, and elsewhere. And it experienced in 1979 the most dramatic revolution of the twentieth-century Middle East — a revolution that overthrew a monarchy backed by the United States and replaced it with a system of clerical governance that challenged virtually every assumption that Western analysts had made about the direction of political development in modernizing societies.

Modern Iranian history begins effectively with the Constitutional Revolution of 1905–1911, when a broad coalition of merchants, clerics, and intellectuals forced the Qajar monarchy to accept a constitution and an elected parliament. This was one of the earliest successful constitutionalist movements in the Muslim world. But the constitutional experiment was undermined by foreign interference — particularly Anglo-Russian rivalry over Iranian territory — and by the weakness of the central government. Reza Khan, an officer in the Iranian Cossack Brigade, seized power in a 1921 coup and established himself as Shah Reza Pahlavi in 1925, pursuing a modernization program similar to Atatürk's in Turkey. When Britain and the Soviet Union jointly invaded Iran in 1941 to prevent a possible German alliance, they forced Reza Shah's abdication, and his young son, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, became Shah.

The young Shah's reign was immediately challenged by the nationalist movement led by Mohammad Mossadegh. Mossadegh was a remarkable figure: a democratic constitutionalist, deeply patriotic, committed to Iranian sovereignty over its own resources. His nationalization of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company made him a hero in Iran and across the decolonizing world. The 1953 coup that overthrew him — organized by the CIA under the code name Operation AJAX and British intelligence — restored the Shah to unconstrained power but permanently delegitimized him as a servant of foreign interests in the eyes of a significant portion of the Iranian public.

Restored to power with American backing, Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi ruled Iran for the next quarter century as an increasingly authoritarian monarch. He pursued a program of rapid modernization — industrialization, land reform, education expansion, women's rights, and Westernization of culture — known as the White Revolution, announced in 1963. He also maintained a large and brutal secret police (SAVAK), dissolved political parties, suppressed free expression, and tolerated massive corruption. Iran's oil revenues — boosted enormously by the price increases of the 1970s — funded military expansion, making Iran the most powerful military state in the Persian Gulf.

Opposition to the Shah came from multiple directions. The secular left had been suppressed but not eliminated. A new generation of Islamic intellectuals, including Ali Shariati, developed a revolutionary interpretation of Shia Islam that fused traditional religious themes with socialist critique of Western imperialism and capitalist inequality. And the traditional Shia clerical establishment, led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, offered a different but equally powerful critique: the Shah's secular modernization was an attack on Islamic values, and his dependence on America made him a servant of foreign domination. Khomeini had been exiled from Iran in 1964 after publicly denouncing the White Revolution and the Status of Forces Agreement with the United States. From exile — first in Iraq, then in France — he continued to preach against the Shah, and his cassette-tape sermons were smuggled into Iran and distributed widely.

His doctrine of velayat-e faqih — the guardianship of the Islamic jurist — argued that in the absence of the Hidden Imam, senior Islamic legal scholars had not merely the right but the obligation to govern the Islamic community. This was a revolutionary departure from traditional Shia political quietism, which had generally held that clerics should advise rather than rule. It provided the theological foundation for an entirely new form of Islamic governance.

The Revolution of 1978–1979 was one of the twentieth century's most surprising political events. Mass demonstrations beginning in 1978 grew larger despite repeated violent suppression. The Shah's health was failing; his generals were unwilling to order the massacre that might have suppressed the movement; and the revolutionary coalition — united by opposition to the Shah — proved broader than any single ideology. Khomeini returned from exile in February 1979 to a rapturous reception of millions in Tehran. The revolutionary government that emerged was initially a coalition of liberals, secular nationalists, leftists, and Islamists. Over the next two years, Khomeini and the clerical faction systematically eliminated their coalition partners. The Islamic Republic that emerged was a distinctive constitutional innovation: a popularly elected parliament and president coexisted with unelected clerical institutions — the Guardian Council, the Assembly of Experts, and, above all, the Supreme Leader — that held ultimate authority.

The Iran-Iraq War of 1980–1988 was a devastating regional conflict that defined the Islamic Republic's early decades. Saddam Hussein's Iraq, alarmed by the potential appeal of Khomeini's revolutionary Islamism to Iraq's Shia majority, invaded Iran in September 1980. The war lasted eight years, killed perhaps half a million people on each side, and involved the use of chemical weapons by Iraq against both Iranian soldiers and Iraqi Kurdish civilians. The United States, alarmed by the Iranian Revolution and its hostage crisis — American embassy staff were held for 444 days after November 1979 — tilted toward Iraq, providing intelligence, weapons, and diplomatic support despite knowledge of Iraqi chemical weapons use. The war ended in a ceasefire that exhausted both societies and settled nothing.

Historical Vocabulary
Shia Islam

Shia Islam is the branch of Islam that holds that legitimate authority in the Muslim community after Muhammad's death passed through the Prophet's family — specifically through his cousin and son-in-law Ali ibn Abi Talib and subsequently through a line of Imams descended from Ali. The murder of Ali's son Hussein at Karbala in 680 CE became the defining event of Shia religious identity: a tragedy of martyrdom and injustice that resonates deeply in Shia ritual, theology, and politics.

Shia Muslims constitute approximately ten to fifteen percent of the global Muslim population but are the majority in Iran, Iraq, Bahrain, and a significant minority in Lebanon, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Pakistan. Understanding the Shia-Sunni distinction is essential to understanding Iranian-Saudi rivalry, the sectarian dimensions of the Iraq War, and the politics of Lebanese Hezbollah — but it is equally important to recognize that this distinction does not map straightforwardly onto political alignments. Shia communities in different countries have quite different political cultures and interests shaped by their own historical circumstances.

Section 53.8

Iraq, the Gulf, and Regional Conflict

Iraq after the 1958 revolution that overthrew the Hashemite monarchy became one of the most volatile and ultimately tragic states in the Middle East. A series of coups and counter-coups produced changing governments with limited legitimacy; the Baath Party, which came to power in 1968, gradually concentrated authority in the hands of a small group from the Sunni Arab town of Tikrit, culminating in Saddam Hussein's assumption of the presidency in 1979. Under Saddam, Iraq became one of the region's most repressive and militarized states, but also, paradoxically, one that achieved significant advances in education, public health, and women's participation in professional life — at least for those who did not fall afoul of the regime's security apparatus.

Saddam Hussein's political career was built on the twin pillars of Arab nationalist ideology and ruthless personal ambition. He consolidated power by executing real and perceived rivals, building an extensive security and intelligence apparatus, using oil revenues to fund both welfare programs and military expansion, and skillfully playing external powers against each other. The Kuwait invasion of August 1990 represented a catastrophic miscalculation: Saddam believed that the United States, distracted by the end of the Cold War and with which he had cultivated a working relationship during the Iran-Iraq War, would not intervene to defend a small monarchy that he portrayed as an artificial British creation and an unjust stealer of Iraqi oil through slant drilling into Iraqi oil fields.

He was wrong. The Gulf War of 1991 assembled a coalition of thirty-four nations under American leadership, authorized by the UN Security Council, that expelled Iraqi forces from Kuwait in a brief but devastating military campaign. The war was a demonstration of American military technology — precision bombing, satellite navigation, stealth aircraft — that transformed thinking about modern warfare. It also permanently changed the strategic landscape of the Gulf: American military forces established bases in Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states, a presence that would become a permanent feature of the regional order and a major grievance for radical Islamist movements, including Osama bin Laden, who cited the "pollution" of the Arabian Peninsula by non-Muslim forces as a primary justification for violence against the United States.

The war's aftermath was in some ways more consequential than the conflict itself. The UN imposed comprehensive economic sanctions on Iraq, limiting its ability to import food, medicine, and civilian goods. The combination of sanctions and continued regime expenditure on military and security purposes produced a humanitarian catastrophe: child mortality rates rose sharply, the health system deteriorated, and the middle class was destroyed by hyperinflation. Meanwhile, Saddam Hussein remained in power, having brutally suppressed uprisings by Shia Arabs in the south and Kurds in the north immediately after the Gulf War ceasefire. Kurdish areas in northern Iraq achieved de facto autonomy under American and British air protection after 1991, foreshadowing the more extensive Kurdish political autonomy that would emerge in subsequent decades.

The Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Bahrain, and Oman — developed in the second half of the twentieth century into some of the world's wealthiest societies per capita, built on oil revenues and the labor of millions of migrant workers. The political systems of these states ranged from absolute monarchy to constitutional monarchy, but all retained power firmly in the hands of ruling families. Saudi Arabia's Al Saud family governed through a combination of Islamic legitimacy (the kingdom is the custodian of the Two Holy Mosques in Mecca and Medina), oil wealth distributed as patronage, and a security apparatus that suppressed dissent. The kingdom's alliance with the Wahhabi religious establishment shaped both its domestic social policy and its export of conservative religious education across the Muslim world.

Empire Study

Saudi Arabia: Tribal Confederacy, Religious Movement, and Petrostate

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia represents one of the twentieth century's most unusual state formations: a tribal confederacy, a religious movement, and a petrostate fused into a singular political entity. Its founder, Abd al-Aziz ibn Saud, began with control of the Nejd region in central Arabia in 1902 and spent three decades conquering the rest of the peninsula, unifying it under the name Saudi Arabia in 1932. His alliance with the Wahhabi religious scholars — a movement founded by Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab in the eighteenth century — was the kingdom's ideological foundation. The clerics endorsed Al Saud political authority; the ruling family enforced conservative religious standards and funded Wahhabi religious education.

Saudi Arabia's relationship with the United States has been one of the most consequential bilateral relationships in modern history. The 1945 meeting between President Franklin Roosevelt and King Abd al-Aziz aboard a warship in the Suez Canal established the basic terms: American military protection in exchange for stable oil supplies at reasonable prices. This relationship survived multiple tensions — the 1973 oil embargo was a significant strain — and endured because both parties needed what the other offered. Saudi Arabia needed American military technology and security guarantees against regional rivals (particularly Iran and Iraq); the United States needed Saudi oil, Saudi moderation of OPEC pricing, and Saudi investment of petrodollars in American financial markets.

The kingdom's export of conservative Wahhabi Islam — funded through thousands of mosques, schools, and charitable organizations across the Muslim world — had complex and sometimes unintended consequences. It provided education and community services where states failed to do so; it also promoted a version of Islamic practice that was unfamiliar and often at odds with local traditions in South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa, and the Balkans, sometimes generating tensions within Muslim communities that had developed their own distinct religious cultures over centuries.

Section 53.9

Political Islam and Modern State Challenges

The rise of political Islam — the mobilization of Islamic belief and practice as the foundation of political programs — was not a sudden development but the product of decades of intellectual ferment, social transformation, and political failure. Understanding it requires attention to both ideas and circumstances: the intellectual traditions that justified Islamic politics, and the historical conditions that made those ideas appealing to millions of people.

The intellectual foundations of modern political Islam were laid in the early twentieth century. The Muslim Brotherhood, founded in Egypt in 1928 by Hassan al-Banna, was the movement that most directly shaped subsequent Islamist politics across the Arab world. Al-Banna founded the Brotherhood in response to what he saw as the cultural and moral degradation of Egyptian society under British influence: the spread of Western dress, entertainment, and values that he believed was corroding Islamic morality. The Brotherhood's program was both social and political: it built schools, hospitals, and social services; it organized political education; and it advocated for the implementation of sharia (Islamic law) as the basis of governance. Al-Banna's slogan — "Islam is the solution" — captured the movement's conviction that Islamic principles provided answers to all social, political, and economic problems.

The Brotherhood was a Sunni Arab movement, but similar trends were developing in other contexts. In South Asia, the Jamaat-e-Islami, founded by Mawdudi in 1941, developed a systematic theory of the Islamic state. In Iran, Shia intellectuals and clerics were developing their own theories of Islamic governance. The common thread across these diverse movements was dissatisfaction with both Western liberalism and Soviet communism as models for Muslim societies, and the conviction that Islam itself contained the resources for building a just social and political order.

The crucial historical circumstance that made these ideas increasingly appealing was the failure of secular nationalist governments in the Arab world. By the 1970s and 1980s, the governments that had promised development, dignity, and liberation through secular nationalism had largely delivered authoritarianism, corruption, and defeat. Nasser's Egypt, for all its nationalist grandeur, had lost two wars with Israel and failed to build a sustainable economy. Baathist Iraq and Syria had created police states in which citizens lived in fear. The oil-rich Gulf monarchies had delivered material prosperity but offered no political participation. In this context, the Islamist argument — that secular nationalism had failed because it had abandoned divine guidance — found receptive audiences across the Arab world.

Islamist movements varied enormously in their political programs and methods. At one end of the spectrum were gradualist movements like the Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood, which participated in electoral politics where permitted, built social service networks, and advocated for Islamic governance through legal means. At the other end were jihadist groups that considered violent struggle against Muslim rulers deemed insufficiently Islamic, and against Western powers, a religious obligation. Sayyid Qutb, an Egyptian Brotherhood member who was imprisoned and eventually executed by Nasser's government in 1966, provided the ideological bridge between mainstream Islamism and revolutionary violence: his late writings declared that most Muslim societies were living in a state of jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance) and that militant struggle to establish true Islamic governance was obligatory.

The Afghan War provided the crucible for the development of transnational jihadist networks. When the Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan in 1979 to support a communist government, the CIA organized and funded an international network of Muslim fighters — the mujahideen — to resist Soviet forces. Saudi Arabia and Pakistan were the primary conduits. Among the fighters who traveled to Afghanistan was Osama bin Laden, a Saudi of Yemeni origin, who would later found Al-Qaeda. The Soviet withdrawal from Afghanistan in 1989 and the subsequent collapse of the Soviet Union were interpreted by jihadist networks as a divine validation of their struggle, and the networks, fighters, and ideology dispersed across the Muslim world.

The challenge that political Islam posed to existing states was not simply military or organizational; it was also about legitimacy. Islamist movements offered something that Arab nationalist governments had failed to provide: a sense of cultural authenticity, moral seriousness, and connection to deep traditions that secular nationalism could not supply. In Egypt, the Brotherhood's social services — hospitals, schools, legal aid offices — reached populations that the state neglected. In Lebanon, Hezbollah's combination of military prowess, social services, and political representation made it the most powerful force in Lebanese Shia politics. In Gaza, Hamas built similar institutions, winning the 2006 Palestinian legislative elections. These movements were not simply expressions of religious enthusiasm; they were sophisticated political actors that had learned to compete across multiple dimensions of social life.

Historical Debate

What Explains the Rise of Political Islam?

The question: Why did political Islam emerge as such a powerful force in the late twentieth-century Middle East? Historians and political scientists have proposed substantially different explanations.

Interpretation A — Political Failure: One influential interpretation argues that Islamism rose primarily because secular alternatives had demonstrably failed. Arab nationalist governments promised development, democracy, and liberation from imperialism; they delivered authoritarianism, corruption, and military defeat. When Nasser's Egypt lost two wars with Israel and failed to produce sustained economic development, and when Baathist Iraq and Syria became brutal police states, people turned to the only available alternative with a different ideological foundation. On this reading, Islamism is primarily a political response to political failure, and its religious language is the vehicle rather than the substance.
Interpretation B — Social Transformation: A second interpretation emphasizes the social disruptions of rapid modernization. As Middle Eastern cities grew rapidly — attracting migrants from rural areas who left behind traditional community structures without finding new ones — Islamic institutions provided community, identity, and moral frameworks. The mosque became a center of social life; Islamic charities filled gaps left by inadequate states; Islamist movements gave people a sense of purpose and belonging in an alienating urban environment. On this reading, Islamism is primarily a social movement responding to social dislocation rather than to political failure per se.

Evidence: The "political failure" interpretation is supported by the observable correlation between Islamist strength and governmental failure. The "social transformation" interpretation is supported by sociological evidence of the mosque's growing role in urban communities and the profile of Islamist supporters, who are disproportionately young, educated, and recently urbanized.

Why it matters: The explanation has policy implications. If Islamism is primarily a response to political failure, then improving governance and creating more accountable states might reduce its appeal. If it is primarily a response to social disruption, then Islamism may persist regardless of political reform. Both explanations challenge the idea that Islamism is simply a product of religious extremism with no connection to material circumstances.

Historical Vocabulary
Nationalism

Nationalism is the political doctrine that holds that peoples sharing a common language, culture, history, or territory constitute a "nation" that deserves political self-determination — typically in the form of an independent state. Nationalism emerged in Europe in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries but spread globally in the era of decolonization.

In the Middle East, nationalism took multiple forms: Arab nationalism claimed that Arabic-speaking peoples constituted a nation deserving unity or mutual solidarity; Iranian nationalism emphasized Persian cultural identity; Turkish nationalism built the Republic on a Turkish ethnic and linguistic foundation; Zionism asserted Jewish national identity; and Palestinian nationalism developed in response to the Zionist project and British rule. Understanding nationalism requires recognizing that "nations" are partly constructed through political and cultural work — they do not simply exist prior to political mobilization but are partly created by it. This constructedness does not make them any less real in their political consequences.

Section 53.10

Chapter Review and Regional Conflict Context Checkpoint

The modern Middle East's history is not a story of timeless conflict rooted in ancient hatreds. It is a story of specific historical processes — the collapse of one empire and the imposition of another, the discovery of a resource that made the region globally important, the simultaneous emergence of competing nationalist movements among peoples with overlapping territorial claims, the intervention of great powers pursuing their own interests, and the failure of political systems to develop the legitimacy and responsiveness that stable governance requires.

The end of Ottoman rule created a political vacuum that was filled not by the Arab self-determination that had been promised but by European mandates that served European interests. The borders they drew and the states they built were not natural expressions of pre-existing communities but administrative conveniences that cut across tribal territories, religious communities, and historical trade routes. The difficulties that followed — the Arab-Israeli conflict, the Shia-Sunni tensions within Iraq, the Lebanese civil war, the Kurdish statelessness — cannot be fully understood without understanding these origins.

Oil gave the region global strategic importance but did not bring democracy or prosperity to most of its inhabitants. The resource curse applied across the spectrum of Middle Eastern states: absolute monarchies, Baathist dictatorships, and the Islamic Republic of Iran all used resource revenues or analogous patronage systems to avoid the political bargains that genuine accountability requires. The populations of the region, increasingly educated and urbanized, periodically challenged these systems — in mass protests, armed uprisings, and electoral challenges — but were repeatedly suppressed.

The Arab-Israeli conflict remained the most internationally visible dimension of Middle Eastern politics through the end of the twentieth century, but it was in many ways typical rather than exceptional. It represented the collision of two legitimate national movements — Zionism and Palestinian nationalism — in a territory too small to accommodate both their aspirations without compromise. Both movements contain internal diversities, histories of both violence and accommodation, and genuine human stories of aspiration and loss. Neither can be reduced to simple villain or victim without doing injustice to the actual complexity of human experience.

Map Study

The Modern Middle East — Borders, Resources, and Conflict Zones

A map of the modern Middle East reveals several striking patterns that illuminate the region's history and its ongoing conflicts.

Borders and their origins: Most of the borders between Arab states were drawn by British and French officials between 1916 and 1926, with minimal regard for population geography. The straight-line borders between Jordan, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait — unusual in a world where borders usually follow physical features or population boundaries — are artifacts of imperial mapmaking. The internal fault lines of Iraq, separating the Kurdish north, the Sunni Arab center, and the Shia Arab south, are invisible on political maps but among the most significant in the region's politics.

Oil geography: The major oil fields of the Middle East cluster around the Persian Gulf — in Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province, Kuwait, Iraq's Basra region, the UAE, and Qatar — and in Iran's southwestern Khuzestan province. This geography meant that the oil-richest areas were often not the most politically central: the Shia populations of eastern Saudi Arabia and southern Iraq lived on top of the world's largest petroleum reserves while being politically marginalized.

Water scarcity: Overlay any political map with a map of river systems and rainfall, and the strategic importance of water becomes clear. The Jordan River — the only significant water source in an arid region — flows through Israel, the West Bank, and Jordan. Water rights have been a source of conflict in every phase of the Arab-Israeli conflict. The Nile, the Tigris, and the Euphrates all cross multiple national borders, creating potential for conflict between upstream and downstream states.

Evidence Window

Palestinian Village Records and the Documentation of the Nakba

The documentation of the 1948 Palestinian displacement is itself a subject of historical and political controversy. Early Israeli historiography portrayed the Palestinian departure as primarily voluntary or directed by Arab leaders. Beginning in the 1980s, a group of Israeli historians — working with newly declassified Israeli military archives — challenged this account. Their work is known as "New Historian" scholarship.

The material evidence available to historians of the Nakba includes several categories. First, Israeli military archives: declassified documents from the Haganah and later Israeli Defense Forces record military operations, including explicit orders to depopulate specific villages in some cases and the record of violence against civilians in others. Second, British Mandatory administrative records: the British kept detailed records of Palestinian villages — population censuses, land registries, police reports — that allow reconstruction of pre-1948 Palestinian society. Third, the physical landscape: hundreds of Palestinian villages were demolished after 1948, but their ruins — stone foundations, terraced fields, fig and olive trees, cemeteries — are still visible in the Israeli landscape, documented by archaeologists and local historians. Fourth, oral history: Palestinian refugee communities maintained detailed oral traditions about their villages of origin, later collected by scholars and organizations.

This evidence allows historians to reconstruct the pre-1948 Palestinian landscape and to examine specific events of the 1948 war with empirical precision impossible when relying only on political narratives. The historical debate is not about whether the Nakba happened — the displacement of approximately 700,000 Palestinians is not contested — but about its causes: how much was the result of deliberate expulsion, how much of wartime flight, and how much of Arab leaders' instructions to evacuate. The evidence from Israeli military archives suggests that deliberate expulsion was a significant factor in many cases, particularly after Plan Dalet was adopted in April 1948.

Limits of the evidence: No single category of source is sufficient. Military archives reflect the perspective of commanders; oral histories may be shaped by the political commitments of those who recorded them; the physical landscape can be read in multiple ways. Historians working on the Nakba must triangulate across these different source types to construct the most accurate possible account.

Comparison Across Societies

Comparison Across Societies

Arab Nationalism and Indian Nationalism

Arab nationalism and Indian nationalism were both major anticolonial movements that emerged under European imperial rule and achieved formal independence in the late 1940s. Comparing them illuminates both the common features of anticolonial nationalism and the specific historical circumstances that shaped each movement.

Both movements defined themselves in opposition to European rule — British in India's case, British and French in the Arab case. Both drew on a pre-existing sense of cultural unity: India's nationalists emphasized shared Hindu cultural traditions and the Hindi language (though they faced the challenge of enormous religious, linguistic, and regional diversity); Arab nationalists emphasized the Arabic language and Islamic and pre-Islamic Arab cultural heritage. Both movements were led by educated elites who had often been educated in European institutions and were influenced by European liberal and nationalist thought.

The critical difference was in political outcomes. Indian nationalism succeeded in creating a single large state — India — and a smaller state — Pakistan — with genuine democratic institutions that have persisted (with significant imperfections) through the decades since independence. Arab nationalism failed in its central goal: the political unification of the Arab world was never achieved, and the independent Arab states that emerged from colonial rule were mostly authoritarian rather than democratic.

Several factors explain this difference. First, India had a single dominant colonial power (Britain) with which to negotiate, while the Arab world faced both Britain and France in different territories. Second, the Indian National Congress, led by figures of extraordinary political skill and moral authority — Gandhi and Nehru above all — maintained sufficient internal unity to negotiate independence as a bloc. Arab nationalism lacked a comparable unified leadership and institution; the Arab League, founded in 1945, was always a loose grouping of sovereign states rather than a genuine vehicle for unity. Third, the creation of Israel in 1948 — and the Arab states' failure to prevent it — permanently delegitimized the established Arab political leadership and created a recurring crisis that destabilized the region's politics for the remainder of the century.

Multiple Perspectives

Multiple Perspectives

The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo

Arab oil-producing states: For Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and other Arab producers, the embargo was an assertion of sovereignty and economic power — a demonstration that the era of Western control over Arab resources was over. It was also a limited action: the embargo targeted specific states for a limited period, and Saudi Arabia was careful to maintain production levels that avoided a complete global oil crisis. For the Arab street — ordinary people across the Arab world — the embargo felt like justice: for the first time, Arab states had used their economic leverage in the Palestinian cause.
Palestinians: For Palestinian leaders and refugees, the embargo was welcome but insufficient. It demonstrated Arab solidarity but did not change the facts on the ground: Israel remained in control of the occupied territories, and the Palestinian refugee crisis was no closer to resolution. Many Palestinians felt that Arab state interests — particularly Egypt's willingness to negotiate separately with Israel — consistently overrode Palestinian interests, even when oil power was being deployed nominally on their behalf.
American and European consumers: The American experience of the oil embargo was one of sudden vulnerability: gasoline lines, heating oil shortages, reduced highway speed limits, and economic recession. For populations accustomed to cheap, abundant energy, the embargo was a shock. Its political legacy was a bipartisan commitment to energy independence and diversification that, paradoxically, sometimes produced greater willingness to use military force to protect access to Gulf oil.
Western oil company shareholders: The major oil companies — Exxon, BP, Shell, Chevron — experienced the embargo differently from consumers. Higher oil prices increased their profit margins dramatically, even as the embargo disrupted supplies. The combination of nationalization by producer governments and rising prices shifted the industry's geography but not, in the short term, its profitability. For company executives, the embargo was a signal that the old concession system was definitively over and that new relationships with national oil companies would be necessary.

Cause-and-Effect Analysis

Cause and Effect

The Iranian Revolution of 1979

Long-Term Causes

The 1953 CIA-backed coup that overthrew Prime Minister Mossadegh permanently delegitimized the Shah as an instrument of American imperialism. The rapid White Revolution modernization program disrupted traditional social structures, alienating the clergy, the bazaar merchant class, and recently urbanized populations. The SAVAK secret police's systematic use of torture and imprisonment suppressed political dissent while broadening the coalition of opposition. Growing inequality amid oil wealth concentrated the costs of rapid change on specific social groups while benefits went disproportionately to elites.

Short-Term Causes

A government newspaper article personally attacking Khomeini in January 1978 triggered protests by theology students in Qom that were violently suppressed. Traditional forty-day Shia mourning cycles meant each violent suppression triggered new demonstrations, creating a self-reinforcing spiral of protest and repression. The Shah's visible illness and apparent indecisiveness undermined confidence in the regime. Key military institutions showed increasing reluctance to carry out the massacres required to suppress the revolution.

The Revolution

Mass demonstrations, general strikes, and the paralysis of key industries (particularly oil) through late 1978 and early 1979 made the Shah's position untenable. He left Iran in January 1979, officially for medical treatment; he never returned. Khomeini returned from exile in February to a reception of millions. The revolutionary government rapidly consolidated clerical power, and a referendum established the Islamic Republic.

Effects

American embassy staff were taken hostage for 444 days, destroying the Carter presidency and permanently rupturing Iranian-American relations. Iraq invaded Iran in September 1980, beginning an eight-year war. Revolutionary Iran began supporting Shia movements across the Muslim world, including Hezbollah in Lebanon. The revolution energized political Islam globally, demonstrating that an Islamic revolution could succeed against a state backed by the world's most powerful nation.

Historical complexity: The revolution cannot be reduced to a single cause. It was simultaneously a response to political authoritarianism, to rapid modernization's social disruptions, to foreign domination of the economy, and to the specific moral authority of Islamic tradition in Iranian culture. No single-factor explanation captures the complexity of what was, by any measure, one of the most significant political events of the twentieth century.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Continuity and Change Over Time

The Role of Islam in Middle Eastern Political Life

What Changed

The nature of Islam's relationship to political authority changed dramatically across the twentieth century. In the Ottoman period, the Caliph claimed both political and religious authority; Islamic law governed personal status, property, and much of commercial life; clerics held significant institutional power. Secular nationalist states — Turkey, Egypt, Iraq, Syria — systematically reduced clerical authority, replaced sharia with European-style legal codes in most domains, established state control over religious institutions, and in some cases pushed religion out of public life entirely. By mid-century, secularism seemed triumphant. By the century's end, political Islam was the most dynamic force in Middle Eastern political life.

What Stayed the Same

Through all these changes, Islam remained central to the social and cultural identity of most Middle Eastern populations. Daily prayer, Ramadan fasting, Hajj pilgrimage, Islamic marriage and inheritance customs, Arabic Quranic education — these practices persisted through secular governments' attempts to confine religion to private life. The mosque remained a community institution. Clerics maintained social authority even when stripped of political power. Religious identity — Muslim, Christian, Jewish, Druze — continued to shape social networks, marriage patterns, and political loyalties.

Who Experienced Change Most Directly

Urban professional elites in secular nationalist states experienced the most dramatic change — they inhabited a public world of French-style law, Western dress, secular education, and modernist culture. Rural populations, traditional merchants, and the clerical establishment experienced less change and felt more directly the costs of secular modernization. Women experienced contradictory changes: in some contexts, secular modernization expanded women's access to education and professional life; in others, Islamic governance restricted women's public roles.

Why the Pattern Matters

The relationship between Islam and political authority in the Middle East was neither fixed nor simply a product of essential "Islamic" characteristics. It changed over time in response to specific historical circumstances, and it differed significantly between different countries and social groups. Understanding this variability helps historians avoid the essentialist error of treating Islam as a timeless, unchanging force that determines political outcomes, and instead to analyze it as a living tradition that people interpret and mobilize in historically specific ways.

Everyday Life and Social History

Everyday Life and Social History

Living the Modern Middle East: Ordinary Experience in Extraordinary Times

The great political events of the modern Middle East — revolutions, wars, oil booms, and diplomatic negotiations — were experienced by ordinary people in ways that official historical accounts rarely capture.

In Palestinian refugee camps, daily life was shaped by the particular geography of statelessness. The camps established in 1948 were initially temporary — tents gradually replaced by concrete block shelters — but became permanent homes for generations. Camp residents lived with restricted movement, limited access to employment, uncertain legal status, and the constant psychological weight of displacement. Children grew up hearing stories of specific villages — the color of the soil, the variety of the olives, the names of neighbors — that they had never seen but that constituted their inheritance. The house key preserved from 1948 became an icon of Palestinian refugee identity: a physical object that embodied both loss and the insistence on eventual return.

In Cairo during the oil boom years of the 1970s and 1980s, young men from rural Egypt migrated to the city and then, in enormous numbers, to Gulf states to work in construction, domestic service, and industry. These labor migrants sent remittances home that transformed village economies — financing new houses, educating younger siblings, enabling sisters' marriages — while spending years separated from their families. They returned with money, new consumer goods, sometimes stricter religious observance adopted from Gulf Wahhabi culture, and stories of a different world. Their experience connected Egyptian rural society to global oil economics in ways that standard accounts of oil politics rarely capture.

Iranian women experienced the revolution of 1979 in intensely contradictory ways. During the revolutionary period, women participated massively in street protests — including many who wore the veil as a symbol of Islamic identity and anti-Western resistance. The revolution gave women a public political role that the Shah's government, for all its nominal promotion of women's rights, had not encouraged. But the Islamic Republic rapidly moved to impose restrictions: women were required to wear the hijab in public, the minimum marriage age was lowered, divorce rights were curtailed, and women were excluded from some judicial and governmental positions. Many women who had enthusiastically supported the revolution found themselves fighting its consequences.

In Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province, where most of the kingdom's Shia Muslim minority lived alongside the oil fields, the oil boom produced material development alongside continued political exclusion. Eastern Province Shia faced discrimination in government employment, restrictions on religious practice, and periodic security crackdowns. The Iranian Revolution, which created a Shia government in neighboring Iran, was followed by a Shia uprising in Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province in 1979 — quickly suppressed but revealing the depth of grievance. The intersection of religious identity, resource geography, and political exclusion made the Eastern Province one of the kingdom's most sensitive and closely watched regions.

Chapter Summary

The modern Middle East emerged from the ruins of the Ottoman Empire through processes deeply shaped by European imperial ambition, the logic of the mandate system, and the decisions of colonial administrators who drew borders without adequate knowledge of or regard for the populations those borders would contain. The political geography created between 1918 and 1926 — the borders of Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Transjordan, and the British Mandate for Palestine — was not inevitable; it reflected specific historical choices made by specific historical actors with specific interests. Understanding those choices is the beginning of understanding why the region's politics took the shape they did throughout the remainder of the century.

The Arab nationalist movements that challenged European domination were genuinely mass phenomena, drawing on real experiences of cultural humiliation, economic exploitation, and political exclusion. They succeeded in ending formal colonial rule — the last British and French mandates gave way to independent states by the mid-1940s — but they failed to achieve their deeper aspirations: Arab unity, liberation of Palestine, democratic governance, and sustained economic development. The failure of secular Arab nationalism — symbolized most dramatically by the defeat of 1967 — created the political and cultural space that political Islam would fill in subsequent decades.

The Palestinian-Israeli conflict is perhaps the most internationally known dimension of modern Middle Eastern history, but understanding it requires resisting simplification in either direction. Zionism responded to a genuine historical crisis — the persecution of Jews in Europe, culminating in the Holocaust — and built a state through genuine human labor, institutional creativity, and military sacrifice. Palestinian nationalism responded to a genuine experience of dispossession: the loss of homes, livelihoods, and a homeland that Zionist immigration and Israeli statehood entailed. Neither movement can be condemned or celebrated in simple terms without doing injustice to the actual complexity of human experience on both sides.

Oil transformed the region's relationship to the global economy in ways both beneficial and distorting. The enormous wealth generated by petroleum — particularly after the price increases of the 1970s — funded development, funded military expansion, and funded the projection of ideological influence across the Muslim world. But it also enabled governments to avoid the political accountability that taxation normally requires, entrenching authoritarianism across different types of regime. And it made the Middle East the permanent object of great-power interest, ensuring continued foreign intervention in regional politics regardless of which superpower was dominant.

The Iranian Revolution of 1979 was a world-historical event that challenged nearly every assumption about the direction of political development in modernizing societies. It demonstrated that rapid economic modernization need not produce secularization; that popular revolution could produce religious governance rather than liberal democracy; and that the United States' support for authoritarian allies created the conditions for their own overthrow. The revolution's consequences rippled outward: the Iran-Iraq War killed hundreds of thousands; Iranian support for Hezbollah changed Lebanese politics; the hostage crisis reshaped American foreign policy; and the model of Islamic governance, however specific to Iranian Shia tradition, energized political Islam across the Muslim world.

Iraq's history in the second half of the century traced a trajectory from British-installed monarchy through nationalist coup to Baathist dictatorship to the catastrophe of three wars in two decades. The Gulf War of 1991 brought American military forces into the Gulf in permanent numbers, establishing a presence that would define the region's geopolitics into the next century. The economic sanctions that followed produced a humanitarian disaster; the combination of sanctions and regime survival demonstrated the limits of economic coercion as a tool of political change.

Political Islam's rise was neither inevitable nor simply a product of religious enthusiasm. It emerged in specific historical circumstances — the failure of secular alternatives, the disruptions of rapid urbanization, the social work of Islamic institutions in providing services where states failed — and it took diverse political forms: the Muslim Brotherhood's gradualism, Hamas's combination of electoral politics and armed resistance, Hezbollah's social services and military power, and Al-Qaeda's transnational jihadism. These movements are not reducible to a single phenomenon; they share Islamic reference points but differ profoundly in their political programs, methods, and relationships to existing states.

The ordinary people of the Middle East — Palestinian refugees maintaining the memory of specific villages, Egyptian labor migrants in Gulf construction projects, Iranian women navigating the contradictions of revolutionary Islam, Saudi Eastern Province Shia living on the world's largest oil reserves without political representation, Lebanese families caught in the crossfire of regional conflicts — are not simply victims of forces beyond their control. They are historical agents who make choices, build communities, resist oppression, and create culture under conditions they did not choose. Their experiences are essential to any adequate understanding of the modern Middle East and to the historian's task of capturing the full complexity of human experience in one of the twentieth century's most consequential regions.

Mandate system
The League of Nations framework assigning Britain and France administrative responsibility for former Ottoman Arab territories, nominally as trustees preparing populations for eventual self-governance, but in practice functioning as imperial rule.

The 1917 British government letter promising support for a Jewish national home in Palestine while pledging to protect the rights of existing non-Jewish communities — two commitments that proved impossible to reconcile simultaneously.

The political ideology asserting that Arabic-speaking peoples share a common culture, language, and history that entitles them to political self-determination, potentially including political unity.
Pan-Arabism
The more radical version of Arab nationalism asserting that all Arab states should be politically unified into a single nation; peaked in the 1950s and 1960s under Nasser but ultimately failed.
Nakba
Arabic for "catastrophe"; the Palestinian term for the events of the 1948 Arab-Israeli War that resulted in the displacement of approximately 700,000 Palestinians and the destruction of Palestinian society.
Zionism
The political movement, founded in the late nineteenth century, advocating for the establishment and maintenance of a Jewish national homeland, focused on Palestine.
PLO (Palestine Liberation Organization)
The umbrella organization representing Palestinian national aspirations, founded in 1964 and dominated by Yasser Arafat's Fatah movement from 1969 onward; eventually recognized Israel in the Oslo Accords.
OPEC
The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries; founded in 1960 to coordinate oil production and pricing among major oil producers; wielded enormous political and economic power after 1973.
Oil embargo
The 1973 Arab OPEC members' refusal to sell oil to the United States and the Netherlands for their support of Israel; caused a global energy crisis and permanently altered perceptions of Middle Eastern oil power.

Revenues from oil sales, particularly the enormous sums accumulated by Gulf states after the 1973 price increases; recycled into Western financial markets and used to fund development and military spending.
Velayat-e faqih
"Guardianship of the jurist"; Ayatollah Khomeini's doctrine that senior Islamic legal scholars should hold supreme political authority in an Islamic state; the theological foundation of the Islamic Republic of Iran.

Political movements that seek to make Islamic law and principles the basis of state governance; encompasses a wide spectrum from gradualist electoral movements to revolutionary jihadist groups.
Muslim Brotherhood
The oldest and most influential Islamist political organization, founded in Egypt in 1928; spread across the Arab world and shaped subsequent Islamist movements globally.

A strict and puritanical form of Sunni Islam founded by Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab in eighteenth-century Arabia; the official religious doctrine of Saudi Arabia and a significant export of Saudi religious philanthropy.

The branch of Islam holding that legitimate leadership of the Muslim community passed through Muhammad's family; the majority religion in Iran and Iraq, with significant communities in Lebanon, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia.
Baathism
The political ideology of the Baath Party, combining Arab nationalism with socialist economics; became the vehicle for military dictatorships in both Syria and Iraq.

Political ideology grounding national identity in shared culture, history, and language rather than religion; the dominant model for Arab state-building after independence, challenged by Islamism from the 1970s onward.

Iranian Shia cleric and revolutionary leader; architect of the doctrine of velayat-e faqih; led the 1979 Iranian Revolution; ruled Iran until his death; transformed regional politics.
Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi (1919–1980)
Iranian monarch (1941–1979); pursued rapid modernization and alliance with the United States; overthrown by the Islamic Revolution; died in Egyptian exile.
Mohammad Mossadegh (1882–1967)
Iranian prime minister who nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (1951); overthrown in a 1953 CIA-backed coup; became an enduring symbol of Iranian national sovereignty and anti-imperialism.
Yasser Arafat (1929–2004)
Chairman of the PLO from 1969; led Palestinian national movement through multiple phases of armed struggle and diplomacy; signed Oslo Accords (1993); died still lacking a Palestinian state.
Anwar Sadat (1918–1981)
Egyptian president who launched the 1973 Yom Kippur War and subsequently made peace with Israel (Camp David Accords); the first Arab head of state to visit Israel; assassinated by Egyptian Islamists in 1981.
Saddam Hussein (1937–2006)
President of Iraq (1979–2003); built a brutal dictatorship while pursuing Arab nationalist and regional ambitions; launched wars against Iran and Kuwait; used chemical weapons against Kurds; overthrown in 2003 American invasion.
Theodor Herzl (1860–1904)
Austrian Jewish journalist and founder of modern political Zionism; organized the First Zionist Congress (1897); envisioned a Jewish state as the solution to European anti-Semitism.
David Ben-Gurion (1886–1973)
First prime minister of Israel; led the Zionist movement's political and military development; declared Israeli independence in May 1948.
King Abdulaziz ibn Saud (c. 1876–1953)
Founder and first king of Saudi Arabia; unified the Arabian Peninsula through military conquest (1902–1932) and alliance with the Wahhabi religious establishment; met President Roosevelt in 1945 to establish the US-Saudi relationship.
Jerusalem
City sacred to Jews, Christians, and Muslims; divided between Israel and Jordan from 1948 to 1967; fully occupied by Israel after 1967; the subject of the most intractable disputes in the Arab-Israeli conflict.
Suez Canal
The waterway connecting the Mediterranean and Red Seas across Egyptian territory; nationalized by Nasser in 1956; a major artery of global trade and a recurring flashpoint of regional conflict.
Tehran
Capital of Iran; site of the 1979 Revolution and the US Embassy hostage crisis; a city of over ten million and the center of Iranian political and cultural life.
West Bank
The territory between the Jordan River and the 1949 armistice line; administered by Jordan 1948–1967; occupied by Israel since 1967; home to both Palestinian Arab population and expanding Israeli settlements.
Gaza Strip
Small coastal territory at the southwestern end of the former British Mandate; administered by Egypt 1948–1967; occupied by Israel 1967–2005; densely populated and economically isolated; governed by Hamas from 2007.

Chapter Reflection

Chapter 53 has examined the modern Middle East as a region shaped by powerful converging forces: imperial partition, competing nationalisms, resource wealth, Cold War rivalry, and the politics of religion. The chapter matters in the context of the larger World History textbook because it demonstrates, with particular clarity, the consequences of decisions made in specific historical moments. The borders drawn by European officials in 1916–1922 were not natural or inevitable; they were choices, made under specific circumstances by specific people with specific interests, and their consequences were felt for the remainder of the century. World history is built on this insight: the present is historically made, not naturally given.

The chapter also illuminates the relationship between external forces and internal agency that recurs throughout the textbook. The Middle East did not passively receive the forces of colonial partition, oil capitalism, and Cold War intervention; it responded to them in ways that were shaped by its own histories, institutions, cultures, and social conflicts. Arab nationalism was not simply a reaction to colonialism; it was a creative project that drew on pre-existing cultural resources and generated new political imaginaries. Political Islam was not simply a reaction to Western domination; it was the product of serious intellectual work by scholars, activists, and communities who found in Islamic tradition resources for imagining a different social and political order. Understanding these responses as active and creative — not merely reactive — is essential to the kind of world history this textbook seeks to provide.

The chapter connects to larger patterns traced throughout the textbook: the recurring tension between imperial boundary-making and the aspirations of populations to govern themselves; the relationship between resource wealth and political power; the role of ideas — nationalist, religious, socialist — in mobilizing collective action; and the enduring gap between the formal promises of international law and the actual power politics that determine territorial outcomes. These patterns appear in different forms in chapters on decolonization in Asia and Africa, the Cold War, and global economic systems — and understanding them in the specific context of the Middle East enriches understanding of all these broader themes.

Finally, the chapter models the kind of historical thinking that the textbook as a whole seeks to cultivate: the willingness to hold complexity without collapsing it into simple narratives, to take multiple perspectives seriously without abandoning moral judgment, to trace long-term causes without falling into determinism, and to recognize the agency of ordinary people alongside the decisions of great leaders. The history of the modern Middle East is not a story of inevitable conflict or of civilizational incompatibility. It is a story of human choices — some wise, many catastrophic — made under specific historical conditions that could, at different moments, have been different. That recognition is not comfortable, but it is the beginning of historical understanding.

Students who complete this chapter will carry forward an understanding of the modern Middle East that goes beyond headlines and political slogans. They will understand why the borders of the region look as they do, why oil became so politically loaded, why Palestinian refugees have remained stateless for more than seventy years, why the Iranian Revolution succeeded and what it meant, and why political Islam emerged as such a powerful force. More fundamentally, they will understand that none of these developments was inevitable — and that understanding the historical choices that produced the present is the indispensable first step toward thinking clearly about what the future might hold.

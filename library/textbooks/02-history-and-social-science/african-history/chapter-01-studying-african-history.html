Part I — Africa, Geography, and Human Origins

Studying African History

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Africa possesses one of the deepest, longest, and most diverse historical records of any landmass on Earth — and learning to read that record honestly is the first task of anyone who wishes to understand it."

In the old town of Timbuktu, in the West African nation of Mali, families have for centuries kept wooden chests and leather trunks filled with manuscripts: legal opinions, astronomy treatises, medical texts, poetry, merchant contracts, and chronicles written in Arabic and in Arabic script adapted to African languages such as Songhai and Fulfulde. Some of these manuscripts are more than five hundred years old. They were copied, annotated, traded, inherited, and protected — hidden in trunks, buried in the sand, smuggled past invading armies — by generations of scholars and ordinary families who understood that the documents in their keeping were a form of wealth as real as gold or cattle. When extremist forces threatened to destroy Timbuktu's libraries in 2012, librarians and ordinary citizens organized a clandestine operation to move hundreds of thousands of manuscripts to safety, carrying them by canoe, by motorbike, and by donkey cart to the capital, Bamako. They did this because they understood something that this chapter asks every student to understand: that the African past is not a blank space waiting to be filled in by outsiders, but a rich and contested terrain that Africans themselves have worked for centuries to record, protect, interpret, and pass on.

For much of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, however, a very different idea took hold in much of the world: the notion that Africa had no history worth studying, or that whatever history it had was static, unchanging, and dependent on contact with outsiders to develop at all. This notion was false, and it was not innocent. It served the political purposes of European colonial empires, which justified their conquest and rule over African societies partly by claiming that those societies were timeless, primitive, or incapable of self-government. Even respected scholars sometimes repeated this claim. The story of how historians — many of them African, some of them from elsewhere — dismantled this myth over the course of the twentieth century, and replaced it with a vastly richer and more accurate picture of African history, is itself one of the great achievements of modern historical scholarship. This chapter is the foundation on which that achievement rests.

African history, properly understood, studies the peoples, societies, cultures, states, economies, religions, and ideas that have developed across the African continent from the deep human past to the present day. It is not one story but thousands of interwoven stories: the story of farmers in the Ethiopian highlands who domesticated crops found nowhere else on Earth; of traders who carried gold, salt, and ideas across the Sahara for over a thousand years; of scholars who built libraries and universities in Timbuktu and Cairo and Fez centuries before some of Europe's oldest universities existed; of empires that rivaled the great states of Europe and Asia in wealth and organization; of ordinary farming and herding families whose names rarely appear in any written record but whose labor, knowledge, and choices shaped the world they lived in; of enslaved people carried across oceans against their will, who nonetheless created new cultures, religions, and forms of resistance that reshaped the Americas; of colonized peoples who resisted, adapted, negotiated, and ultimately overthrew European empires; and of contemporary societies building new political and economic futures in a rapidly changing world.

To study this history responsibly, students need tools, just as students of any continent's history do. They need to understand chronology — how to sequence events across enormously long spans of time, some of them reaching back hundreds of thousands of years to the origins of humanity itself. They need to understand geography — how deserts, rainforests, savannas, rivers, and coastlines shaped where people settled, how they made their livings, and how ideas and goods moved between regions. They need to understand the distinctive evidence base of African history: archaeology, which recovers physical traces of the past from the ground; oral tradition, which preserves historical knowledge through structured forms of memory, performance, and transmission across generations; and written sources, which exist in Africa in greater age and abundance than many people assume, including ancient Egyptian hieroglyphic texts, Ethiopian Ge'ez manuscripts, West African Arabic chronicles, and European colonial records that, despite their biases, contain information historians can use critically.

Students also need to understand why historical interpretation matters and why bias is a problem every historian must confront, not only when studying Africa but everywhere. For much of the colonial era, European writers described African societies using racist assumptions that shaped what they reported, what they ignored, and how they explained what they saw. Correcting this distorted record has required generations of careful, evidence-based scholarship — much of it produced by African historians working in African universities and archives, in dialogue and sometimes in tension with historians elsewhere in the world. Learning to recognize bias, evaluate evidence, and weigh competing interpretations is not a side skill in African history. It is the central skill, because the record itself has so often been distorted by people with a stake in particular conclusions.

Finally, students need to understand cause and effect, and continuity and change, as tools for organizing the vast sweep of African history into meaningful patterns. Why did some African societies develop large states while neighboring societies remained organized around smaller kin-based communities — and was one approach simply "more advanced" than the other, or did each represent a different, equally sophisticated adaptation to different environmental and historical circumstances? Why did certain trade routes flourish for centuries and then decline? What changed when colonial rule arrived, and what, surprisingly, stayed the same? These are the kinds of questions this textbook will ask throughout its sixty-seven chapters, and this opening chapter introduces the methods students will need to answer them.

Africa is not one place, one people, one culture, or one story. It is a continent of more than fifty-four nations, more than two thousand languages, deserts and rainforests and snow-capped mountains and sweeping savannas, ancient kingdoms and contemporary megacities, four thousand years of written record and tens of thousands of years of human habitation. Studying its history with the seriousness it deserves is one of the most rewarding tasks a student of world history can undertake — and it begins here, with the tools historians use to do that work honestly.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain what African history studies and why it must be approached as the history of a vast and diverse continent rather than a single, uniform story.
  2. Identify why Africa must be studied on its own terms, rather than primarily through the lens of its contact with Europe, Asia, or the Atlantic world.
  3. Define chronology and periodization, and explain how African historians divide and organize the continent's long history.
  4. Describe how geography — deserts, savannas, rainforests, highlands, rivers, and coasts — shaped settlement, trade, and political development across Africa.
  5. Distinguish between archaeological, oral, and written evidence, and explain the distinctive strengths and limitations of each in reconstructing African history.
  6. Analyze how oral tradition functions as a structured, disciplined system of historical transmission rather than as casual storytelling.
  7. Evaluate the range of written sources available to historians of Africa, including African-authored chronicles, religious texts, and external accounts.
  8. Identify historical bias in sources about Africa, particularly bias rooted in colonial-era racial ideology, and explain how historians correct for it.
  9. Apply the concepts of cause and effect and continuity and change to major developments in African history.
  10. Compare multiple historical perspectives on the same African historical development.
  11. Contextualize the study of African history within the broader development of global historical scholarship in the nineteenth, twentieth, and twenty-first centuries.
  12. Connect the historical thinking skills introduced in this chapter to the study of subsequent chapters throughout the textbook.

  2. Why have many historians insisted that Africa must be studied "on its own terms" rather than primarily through its relationships with other continents?
  3. How do historians establish chronology and periodization for a continent whose written record varies enormously by region and period?

Historical Context

Before students can engage with the specific content of African history, they need to understand how the field itself came to exist in its modern form, because the history of the discipline is itself revealing. For much of the colonial era, the dominant view among European scholars, administrators, and missionaries was that Africa — apart from ancient Egypt, which was often treated as somehow not "really" African — had no history worth serious study. Influential European thinkers described the continent as static, unchanging, and outside the main currents of world historical development. This was never an accurate description of reality. It was, instead, a convenient justification for colonial conquest: if African societies had no history of their own development, then colonial rule could be presented as bringing Africa into history rather than disrupting societies that already possessed long, complex historical trajectories of their own.

This view began to be challenged seriously in the mid-twentieth century, for several converging reasons. African nationalist movements, fighting for independence from European colonial rule, needed historical narratives that affirmed the dignity, achievement, and self-governing capacity of African peoples — and they found ample evidence for such narratives once historians began looking seriously. New methods of archaeological dating, especially radiocarbon dating developed after the Second World War, allowed scholars to establish chronologies for African sites without relying solely on written records. Linguists developed methods for reconstructing the deep history of language families, revealing large-scale population movements such as the Bantu expansion that no written document had recorded. And a new generation of African scholars — trained at universities in Africa, Europe, and the Americas — began to insist that African oral traditions, long dismissed by colonial-era writers as unreliable folklore, in fact preserved historically significant information when collected and analyzed with appropriate methodological care.

One of the most significant institutional expressions of this transformation was the UNESCO General History of Africa project, launched in the 1960s and completed across multiple decades, which brought together African and international scholars to produce a comprehensive, evidence-based history of the continent from its earliest human origins to the contemporary era. This project, along with the work of pioneering historians associated with universities such as Ibadan in Nigeria, Dakar in Senegal, Dar es Salaam in Tanzania, and Legon in Ghana, helped establish African history as a serious, rigorous academic discipline grounded in the same standards of evidence and argument applied to the history of any other continent.

This history of the discipline matters because it shapes how this textbook is organized. Africa will not be presented here as a continent whose history began with European contact, nor as a continent defined primarily by its relationship to outside powers. It will be presented as a continent with its own long, internally generated historical development — one that includes extensive contact, exchange, and conflict with the wider world across many centuries, but that cannot be reduced to that contact. At the same time, this textbook will not avoid difficult subjects: the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades, European colonial conquest, the violence of colonial rule, and the challenges of the postcolonial era are all central, unavoidable parts of African history, and they will be treated with the seriousness and honesty they deserve.

Students should also understand, before beginning, the basic physical and demographic scale of the subject. Africa is the second-largest continent on Earth, covering more than eleven million square miles — large enough to contain the United States, China, India, and most of Europe combined. It is home to more than 1.4 billion people, speaking an estimated two thousand or more distinct languages, organized today into fifty-four recognized nations, and historically organized into thousands of distinct societies, kingdoms, city-states, and stateless communities across its long history. No single narrative, no single people, and no single region can stand in for "Africa" as a whole. This chapter's central task is to equip students with the historical tools necessary to engage with that scale and diversity honestly.

Section 1.1

What African History Studies

African history is the study of the peoples, societies, cultures, states, economies, and ideas that have developed across the African continent from the deep human past to the present. This definition sounds simple, but it carries within it an important corrective to older, mistaken assumptions about what counts as history at all. For generations, many writers outside Africa assumed that "history" meant primarily the history of states, written documents, and elite political and military leaders — the kinds of evidence most readily available for ancient Mediterranean, European, and East Asian civilizations. By this narrow definition, societies that left fewer written records, or that organized themselves around kinship and consensus rather than centralized states, appeared to have no history worth recording.

Modern historical scholarship rejects this narrow definition. History is the study of human change and continuity over time, and it can be reconstructed through many different forms of evidence: archaeological remains, oral traditions, linguistic patterns, art and architecture, and — where they exist — written documents. By this broader and more accurate definition, every African society has a history, whether or not it produced written texts, and historians have developed an impressive array of methods for recovering that history even where the documentary record is thin.

African history encompasses an extraordinary range of subjects. It includes the deepest history of humanity itself — the evolution of early hominins and the emergence of Homo sapiens, which occurred in Africa long before humans migrated to other continents. It includes the development of some of the world's earliest agricultural systems, in regions such as the Nile Valley, the Ethiopian highlands, and the West African savanna. It includes ancient and medieval states and empires of remarkable wealth and sophistication: Egypt, Kush, Axum, Ghana, Mali, Songhai, Great Zimbabwe, and many others. It includes the development of major world religions on African soil, including some of the oldest Christian and Islamic communities anywhere in the world. It includes vast trading networks connecting Africa to the Mediterranean, the Middle East, and the Indian Ocean world for well over a thousand years before sustained European contact. It includes the tragic history of the Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades, which removed millions of Africans from the continent and devastated entire regions. It includes the European colonial conquest of nearly the entire continent in the late nineteenth century, the systems of administration, extraction, and racial hierarchy that colonial powers imposed, and the long struggles of African peoples to resist and ultimately overthrow that rule. And it includes the history of independent African nations since the mid-twentieth century, grappling with questions of governance, development, identity, and Africa's place in an interconnected world.

Crucially, African history also includes the history of ordinary people whose names were never recorded in any document: farmers who developed sophisticated agricultural techniques suited to difficult and varied environments; herders who managed livestock across some of the most challenging pastoral landscapes on Earth; women who organized households, markets, and in many societies significant political and religious institutions; artisans whose ironworking, weaving, and sculpture reflect deep technical knowledge passed down across generations; and countless communities who built systems of law, governance, and spirituality that sustained social life for centuries without ever requiring a centralized state.

Historical Vocabulary
Historiography

Historiography is the history of historical writing itself — the study of how scholars in different times, places, and political circumstances have written about the past, and why their interpretations have changed. Historiography matters enormously for the study of Africa because the field's history is unusually visible and unusually consequential. Nineteenth-century European writers often denied that Africa had a history at all, or claimed that whatever achievements existed (such as ancient Egyptian civilization) must have originated from outside the continent. Mid-twentieth-century African and international historians overturned these claims through careful archaeological, linguistic, and documentary research, establishing African history as a serious academic field grounded in evidence.

Understanding this historiographical shift helps students ask the right questions of any source about Africa: When was this written? By whom? Within what set of assumptions about Africa and African peoples? What evidence does it actually present, and what does it merely assert? These questions are not unique to African history, but they carry particular weight here because the stakes of getting the history wrong have been — and remain — so high.

Section 1.2

Why Africa Must Be Studied on Its Own Terms

One of the most important lessons of this chapter is also one of the simplest to state and one of the hardest to fully absorb: Africa must be studied on its own terms, as a continent whose history follows its own internal logics, rather than primarily as a backdrop to the history of other continents or peoples. This may sound obvious, but it represents a genuine correction to centuries of historical writing that treated Africa's significance almost entirely in terms of its relationships to outsiders — as a source of gold and ivory for medieval Mediterranean trade, as a supplier of enslaved labor for the Atlantic economy, as a stage for European exploration and colonization, or as a site of Cold War rivalry between global superpowers.

Each of those relationships is real and important, and this textbook will examine all of them in detail in later chapters. But none of them defines Africa, and treating any of them as the primary lens through which to view African history distorts the picture in serious ways. Consider an analogy: a history of Europe that focused primarily on Europe's relationships with the societies it colonized, while saying little about European political, religious, intellectual, and social developments in their own right, would be recognized immediately as a poor and unbalanced history of Europe. The same standard must apply to Africa.

Studying Africa on its own terms means asking, first, what was happening within African societies — how they organized political authority, how they structured family and community life, how they understood the spiritual world, how they produced and exchanged goods, how they resolved disputes, how they educated their children, and how they innovated technologically — before asking how those societies interacted with outsiders. It means recognizing that state formation, religious development, artistic achievement, and technological innovation occurred across Africa for thousands of years independent of any external influence, even as the continent also participated extensively in wider regional and global exchanges.

It also means resisting the temptation to evaluate African societies by standards imported from elsewhere. For much of the colonial era, European observers measured African societies against an idealized vision of European civilization and found them lacking — failing to recognize that societies organized differently, whether around kinship networks rather than centralized bureaucracies, or around oral rather than written transmission of knowledge, were not therefore less developed, but differently developed, often in ways well suited to their particular environmental and social circumstances. A stateless society organized around extended kinship and consensus-based decision-making is not a "failed state." It is often a deliberately maintained alternative to centralized state power, with its own sophisticated mechanisms for resolving conflict and distributing resources.

At the same time, studying Africa on its own terms does not mean romanticizing the past or ignoring conflict, inequality, hierarchy, and violence within African societies. Pre-colonial African societies, like societies on every continent, included slavery, warfare, gender inequality, and social stratification alongside achievement, cooperation, and innovation. An honest history neither exaggerates Africa's precolonial achievements to compensate for colonial-era denigration, nor minimizes them to fit comfortable assumptions about a "dark continent" awaiting outside intervention. It presents the evidence as carefully and completely as the historical record allows.

Section 1.3

Chronology, Periodization, and Time

Chronology — the sequencing of events in time — is foundational to all historical study, but it poses distinctive challenges for African history because of the continent's enormous time depth and the uneven survival of evidence across regions and periods. Some parts of Africa, such as the Nile Valley, have produced continuous written records stretching back more than five thousand years. Other regions have rich oral and archaeological records but few or no written sources before the relatively recent past. Still others combine oral, written, and archaeological evidence in complex layers that historians must carefully cross-check against one another.

This unevenness means that historians of Africa often work with different chronological precision in different chapters of the same broader narrative. For ancient Egypt, historians can often date events to within a few years, thanks to detailed written records and astronomical observations. For many sub-Saharan African societies before sustained written documentation, historians may only be able to establish chronologies within a range of decades or centuries, based on archaeological dating methods, oral genealogies, and comparison with better-documented neighboring societies. This is not a weakness unique to African history — archaeologists and historians face similar challenges studying many pre-literate societies on every continent — but it does require students to become comfortable with chronological uncertainty and with evidence-based estimation rather than false precision.

Periodization — the practice of dividing the past into named periods to make it more manageable — raises additional challenges specific to African history. European historical periodization (ancient, medieval, early modern, modern) developed to describe patterns of change within European history and does not map neatly onto African historical development. Applying European period labels to Africa can mislead students into assuming that African history followed the same rhythms and turning points as European history, when in fact African societies experienced their own distinctive sequences of growth, transformation, and change, sometimes overlapping with European chronology and sometimes not at all.

For this reason, this textbook organizes African history using a combination of broad chronological stages — early human origins, ancient civilizations, the era of trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean trade and great empires, the era of the Atlantic slave trade, the colonial era, and the era of independence and contemporary development — and major thematic and regional frameworks, recognizing that developments in North Africa, West Africa, the Horn of Africa, East Africa, Central Africa, and Southern Africa often followed different timelines even when they were connected through trade, religion, migration, or political influence.

Historical Vocabulary
Periodization

Periodization is the practice of dividing history into named blocks of time to make a vast past more manageable to study. Every periodization scheme embeds interpretive choices about what matters most. For African history, periodization schemes borrowed uncritically from European history — for example, labeling the period before roughly 1800 as simply "pre-colonial," as though the only meaningful thing happening in Africa before European conquest was waiting for Europeans to arrive — can obscure far more than they reveal. Historians today increasingly periodize African history according to its own major internal developments: the rise and fall of particular states and trading systems, large-scale migrations, the spread of religions, and shifts in regional and global trade networks, while remaining attentive to the moments when African and global chronologies intersect.

Section 1.4

Geography and Historical Space

Africa's physical geography is extraordinarily varied, and that variety has shaped human history across the continent in profound ways. Africa straddles the equator and extends across a wide range of latitudes, producing an enormous diversity of climate zones and ecological regions within a single landmass: the Sahara and Kalahari deserts, the Sahel's semi-arid grasslands, vast savannas across much of East and Southern Africa, dense equatorial rainforests across the Congo Basin and West African coast, towering highlands in Ethiopia and East Africa's Great Rift Valley, and long coastlines bordering the Atlantic, Indian, and Mediterranean worlds.

This diversity matters because geography shaped — without determining — the kinds of societies that developed in different regions. The Nile Valley's predictable annual floods, combined with its narrow band of cultivable land bordered by desert, encouraged the early development of centralized irrigation management and, eventually, one of the world's first territorial states. The West African Sahel, positioned between the Sahara to the north and the forest zone to the south, became a natural crossroads for trans-Saharan trade, fostering the rise of powerful trading empires such as Ghana, Mali, and Songhai. The dense rainforests of Central Africa, by contrast, posed challenges to large-scale agriculture and long-distance overland travel, encouraging different patterns of settlement organized around river transport and smaller-scale political units, alongside some notable exceptions such as the Kongo kingdom. The Swahili coast of East Africa, facing the Indian Ocean, developed a distinctive maritime trading culture connecting African societies to Arabia, Persia, India, and eventually China.

The Sahara Desert deserves particular attention, because it has often been misunderstood as an impassable barrier that isolated sub-Saharan Africa from the Mediterranean world and the rest of the globe. In reality, the Sahara functioned for well over a thousand years as a vast but navigable trade corridor, crossed regularly by camel caravans carrying gold, salt, slaves, textiles, books, and ideas in both directions. Understanding the Sahara as a connective space rather than a wall fundamentally changes how students understand West African history, revealing the deep and longstanding connections between West Africa, North Africa, and the broader Islamic world.

Rivers, too, served as crucial historical arteries: the Nile in the northeast, the Niger and Senegal in West Africa, the Congo in Central Africa, and the Zambezi in Southern Africa all shaped settlement patterns, facilitated trade and communication, and in some cases supported the rise of powerful states along their banks. Highland regions, such as the Ethiopian plateau, provided defensible terrain, fertile volcanic soils, and distinctive microclimates that supported some of the longest continuously developing states and Christian traditions in world history.

Section 1.5

Archaeology and Material Evidence

For much of African history — and for the entirety of human history before the invention of writing anywhere on Earth — archaeology provides the primary form of evidence historians can use. Archaeology is the systematic study of past human life through physical remains: tools, pottery, building foundations, food remains, burial sites, metal artifacts, and the broader landscapes people shaped. In Africa, archaeology has been transformative, both for understanding the deep human past and for reconstructing the history of more recent societies that left few or no written records.

Archaeological research in Africa has produced some of the most important discoveries in the entire field of human origins. Fossil remains found at sites in East Africa, including the Rift Valley region spanning modern Kenya, Tanzania, and Ethiopia, have revealed a continuous record of human evolutionary development stretching back millions of years — evidence that establishes Africa as the place where the human species itself emerged. Archaeological excavation at sites such as Great Zimbabwe, in present-day Zimbabwe, has revealed the scale and sophistication of stone construction techniques used by a major medieval African state, while excavations along the Swahili Coast have revealed extensive evidence of long-distance trade connecting East Africa to the Persian Gulf, India, and China centuries before European arrival.

Modern archaeological dating methods have been especially important for African history. Radiocarbon dating, developed in the mid-twentieth century, allows scientists to estimate the age of organic remains — wood, charcoal, bone, shell — with reasonable precision, without depending on any written record. This method, along with related techniques such as thermoluminescence dating for ceramics and pottery, has allowed historians to construct reliable chronologies for sites and societies that left no writing at all, fundamentally transforming what is known about the deep history of regions such as the West African forest zone, the Great Lakes region of East Africa, and much of Southern Africa.

Archaeology has its limits, however. Physical remains can reveal a great deal about technology, diet, trade connections, settlement patterns, and in some cases social organization and religious practice, but they generally cannot reveal the names of individuals, the content of political debates, or the specific motivations behind particular decisions, except in rare cases where inscriptions or other texts survive alongside the physical evidence. For this reason, archaeology works best in combination with other forms of evidence — oral tradition, linguistic analysis, and written sources where available — rather than in isolation.

Section 1.6

Oral Tradition and Memory

Perhaps no aspect of African historical method has been more misunderstood — and more thoroughly rehabilitated by modern scholarship — than oral tradition. For much of the colonial era, European writers dismissed African oral accounts of the past as unreliable folklore, useful at best for anthropological curiosity but not as serious historical evidence. This dismissal reflected both racial prejudice and a genuine misunderstanding of how oral tradition actually functions in many African societies.

Oral tradition is not casual storytelling passed haphazardly from person to person, gradually distorting with each retelling, as a children's game of "telephone" might suggest. In many African societies, oral tradition operates as a disciplined, structured system of historical transmission, often maintained by specially trained individuals whose social role is specifically to preserve and recite historical, genealogical, and legal knowledge with precision. In much of West Africa, for example, professional oral historians known as griots (in some Mande languages, jeliw or jali) are trained from childhood, often within specific families whose hereditary role is to serve as the memory-keepers of a kingdom, clan, or community. A skilled griot can recite the genealogy of a royal lineage across dozens of generations, recount the founding narratives of a kingdom, and perform epic histories — such as the Sundiata epic recounting the founding of the Mali Empire — with structured formulas, set narrative elements, and musical accompaniment that aid memorization and signal historical authenticity to the listening community.

The pioneering historian Jan Vansina developed rigorous methods for evaluating oral tradition as historical evidence, comparable in spirit to the methods historians use to evaluate written documents. Vansina argued that oral traditions, like written sources, must be analyzed for their genre (is this a genealogy, an epic, a praise-song, a proverb?), their social function (who benefits from this version of events being remembered this way?), their transmission process (how many tellings separate the event from the present account, and what controls existed against distortion?), and their corroboration against other sources, including archaeology and outside written accounts where available.

Using these methods, historians have been able to reconstruct significant aspects of the history of societies such as the Kuba kingdom of Central Africa, the kingdoms of the Great Lakes region of East Africa, and the Mali and Songhai empires of West Africa, supplementing and sometimes correcting the available written and archaeological evidence. Oral tradition does have limitations: it is generally most reliable for relatively recent history (typically the past few centuries, though some traditions plausibly preserve much older information), it can be shaped by political interests of those who maintain and recite it, and its precision can vary significantly between different genres and different societies. But properly evaluated, oral tradition is an indispensable historical source, not a poor substitute for "real" written history.

Everyday Life and Social History

Growing Up in a Griot Family

In many Mande-speaking societies of West Africa, the role of griot is hereditary, passed down within particular families across generations. A child born into a griot family begins training in early childhood, learning not only the specific historical narratives, genealogies, and praise-songs associated with particular ruling families, but also the musical instruments — such as the kora, a twenty-one-stringed harp-lute, or the balafon, a wooden xylophone — that traditionally accompany oral historical performance.

This training represents years of disciplined education, comparable in seriousness to formal schooling, even though it occurs through oral instruction, apprenticeship, and performance rather than through written texts. A senior griot's social position carries real authority: griots have historically served as advisors, diplomats, and mediators to ruling families, precisely because their command of historical and genealogical knowledge made them indispensable to political legitimacy. Understanding this everyday reality — the discipline, training, and social respect involved in oral historical transmission — helps correct the mistaken impression that oral tradition is informal or unreliable.

Multiple Perspectives

Evaluating a Griot's Recitation of Royal History

From the perspective of the ruling family being praised: A griot's recitation of the family's genealogy and founding deeds affirms political legitimacy, connects the present ruler to a celebrated and ancient lineage, and reinforces social order. The family has every incentive to value, support, and perpetuate this tradition.
From the perspective of a rival lineage or community: The same recitation may be viewed skeptically or as a politically motivated account that exaggerates the ruling family's achievements and legitimacy while downplaying the contributions or claims of other lineages and communities.
From the perspective of a modern historian: The recitation is a valuable but partial source. It preserves genuine historical information — names, sequences of rulers, major events — encoded within a genre designed to honor and legitimate, which means the historian must cross-check its claims against archaeology, outside written sources, and other oral traditions before accepting its specific details as established historical fact.
From the perspective of the griot performing the recitation: The recitation is both a professional duty and an act of cultural preservation, performed according to inherited formulas and structures that the griot has trained for years to master, and that the griot understands as carrying real historical weight, not as mere entertainment.
Section 1.7

Written Sources and Archives

Contrary to a once-common assumption, Africa possesses a long and rich tradition of written documentation, though its distribution across regions and periods is uneven. Ancient Egypt produced one of the world's oldest and most extensive written records, using hieroglyphic, hieratic, and later demotic scripts to document religious texts, administrative records, legal contracts, medical treatises, and royal inscriptions across more than three thousand years. Nubia, to Egypt's south, developed its own writing system, Meroitic script, which scholars are still working to fully decipher. Ethiopia developed a continuous tradition of Ge'ez-language manuscript production, including royal chronicles, religious texts, and legal codes, some of which survive from the medieval period onward in church and monastery libraries.

Following the spread of Islam across North and West Africa beginning in the seventh and eighth centuries CE, Arabic became an important written language across large parts of the continent, used both by Arab and Berber writers describing African societies and, crucially, by African scholars themselves writing in Arabic or in African languages rendered in Arabic script (a practice sometimes called Ajami). The libraries of Timbuktu, Chinguetti in Mauritania, and other West African centers of Islamic learning preserve enormous collections of locally authored manuscripts covering law, astronomy, medicine, history, and poetry, demonstrating that West African scholars were active producers of written knowledge, not merely passive subjects described by outside observers.

Outside written sources about Africa also exist in significant quantity, particularly from Arab geographers and travelers such as Al-Mas'udi, Al-Bakri, Ibn Battuta, and Leo Africanus, and later from European explorers, missionaries, traders, and colonial administrators. These outside accounts can provide valuable information, especially when they describe societies and events not otherwise documented, but historians must use them with particular care, since their authors often had limited direct knowledge, relied on secondhand reports, carried their own cultural and religious assumptions, and — especially in the European colonial period — frequently wrote with explicit or implicit purposes of justifying conquest, trade, or missionary activity.

Primary Source Window

Al-Mas'udi, The Meadows of Gold (10th century CE)

Creator: Al-Mas'udi, Arab historian and geographer · Date: mid-10th century CE · Type: Geographical and historical compendium

Context: Al-Mas'udi was a Baghdad-born scholar who traveled extensively across the Islamic world and compiled detailed accounts of the lands, peoples, and trade networks he encountered or learned about from merchants and travelers, including regions of East Africa along the Indian Ocean coast known to Arabic writers as the "Land of Zanj."

Al-Mas'udi described East African coastal communities engaged in trade across the Indian Ocean, noting the exchange of goods such as ivory and other commodities, and described the political organization of coastal societies he learned of through merchant networks, while acknowledging that much of his information came secondhand rather than from direct observation.

Analysis: Al-Mas'udi's account is valuable because it documents East African participation in Indian Ocean trade networks centuries before sustained European contact with the region, confirming archaeological evidence of long-distance Swahili Coast commerce. However, the account also has clear limits: Al-Mas'udi did not necessarily travel to East Africa himself, his information passed through multiple secondhand sources, and his terminology and categories reflect the assumptions of a medieval Arabic-speaking scholar describing societies from outside, not an insider's account of how East African communities understood themselves.

Questions for students:

  1. What does this source reveal about East Africa's participation in long-distance Indian Ocean trade during the medieval period?
  2. Why must historians treat secondhand outside accounts, even detailed ones, with particular caution?
  3. What kinds of additional evidence would historians need to confirm or extend the claims found in this source?
  4. What perspective is missing from this account, and how might East African voices have described the same trade networks differently?
Section 1.8

Historical Interpretation and Bias

No historical source, of any kind, presents an objective, unmediated view of the past. Every source was created by particular people, for particular purposes, shaped by particular assumptions. This is true everywhere, but the problem of bias has been especially significant — and especially consequential — in the historical record concerning Africa, because so much of that record, particularly from the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, was produced by writers operating within frameworks of racial hierarchy that actively distorted their observations and conclusions.

One especially influential and damaging example was the so-called "Hamitic hypothesis," a theory popular among some nineteenth- and early twentieth-century European writers that attributed any impressive African achievement — large states, monumental architecture, sophisticated metalworking — to the supposed influence of lighter-skinned "Hamitic" migrants believed (incorrectly) to be racially distinct from, and superior to, other African populations. This theory had no solid scientific or historical basis. It functioned, instead, to preserve a racist assumption that "true" sub-Saharan Africans could not have produced complex civilizations on their own, by attributing any contrary evidence to outside influence. The theory shaped influential — and deeply mistaken — interpretations of sites such as Great Zimbabwe, which some colonial-era writers insisted must have been built by outsiders rather than by the ancestors of the Shona-speaking peoples who actually constructed it, as later archaeological and historical research conclusively demonstrated.

Historians today recognize the Hamitic hypothesis and similar theories as products of colonial-era racial ideology rather than legitimate historical conclusions, but recognizing this requires students to develop the same kind of critical evaluation skills historians apply to any source: asking who created an account, what assumptions and purposes shaped it, what evidence actually supports its claims, and how its conclusions compare with independent archaeological, linguistic, and oral evidence.

Historical Debate

How Should Historians Address the Legacy of Discredited Theories Like the "Hamitic Hypothesis"?

The question: The Hamitic hypothesis has been thoroughly discredited by modern archaeological and historical research. But historians disagree about how much attention discredited theories deserve in a modern textbook, and about how best to teach students to recognize and resist similar biases in the future.

Interpretation A — Minimal Attention: Some historians argue that textbooks should focus primarily on accurate, evidence-based history and devote only brief attention to discredited theories, on the grounds that dwelling on disproven ideas distracts from the substantial positive content of African history that deserves more space and attention.
Interpretation B — Sustained Attention: Other historians argue that understanding how and why such theories arose, and how they shaped both popular and scholarly understanding of Africa for generations, is itself essential historical knowledge — both because it explains why certain sites and societies were misunderstood for so long, and because it equips students to recognize similar patterns of biased reasoning in the future.

Evidence: Archaeological excavation at Great Zimbabwe and other sites, along with linguistic and oral historical evidence, has thoroughly confirmed that these were the achievements of African societies themselves, with no need for any external "Hamitic" explanation. The historiographical record of how long it took some scholars to accept this evidence is itself well documented.

Why it matters: How much space a textbook devotes to discredited theories shapes whether students understand African history's evidentiary record purely on its own terms, or also understand the historical struggle to establish that record against biased interpretation.

Student task: Considering the example of Great Zimbabwe, evaluate which approach you find more persuasive, and explain what is gained or lost by each.

Section 1.9

Cause, Effect, Continuity, and Change

Historians of Africa, like historians everywhere, rely on the concepts of cause and effect and continuity and change to organize evidence into meaningful explanations rather than mere chronicles of events. Causation in African history, as elsewhere, is rarely simple. Major developments — the rise of trans-Saharan trade empires, the spread of Islam across the Sahel, the expansion of Bantu-speaking peoples across Central and Southern Africa, the European colonial conquest of the continent — were typically produced by multiple interacting causes operating across different timescales, not by any single triggering event.

Consider, as one example developed more fully in later chapters, the rise of the West African empire of Ghana around the eighth century CE. Short-term causes might include specific political alliances or military successes that consolidated regional power under particular rulers. But the deeper, long-term causes included centuries of accumulated trans-Saharan trade infrastructure, the gradual development of agricultural surplus capable of supporting specialized merchant and political classes, and longstanding regional networks connecting gold-producing areas to the south with salt-producing and Mediterranean-trading areas to the north. Understanding Ghana's rise requires attending to both layers of causation, not collapsing the explanation into a single proximate event.

Continuity and change operate similarly across African history. The colonial era, for example, produced dramatic political and economic changes — new borders, new administrative systems, new legal frameworks, forced labor and cash-crop economies. But significant continuities persisted alongside these changes: many African societies maintained traditional systems of kinship, marriage, religious practice, and local governance even under colonial rule, sometimes openly and sometimes through forms of accommodation and adaptation that preserved core social structures beneath an altered political surface. Recognizing both the depth of colonial-era change and the persistence of African social and cultural continuity is essential to an accurate historical account — exaggerating either dimension alone produces a distorted picture.

Cause and Effect

Decolonizing the Study of African History (1950s–1970s)

Long-Term Causes

Decades of colonial-era denial that Africa possessed serious history; the accumulation of archaeological, linguistic, and oral-historical evidence contradicting that denial; the broader twentieth-century critique of racial pseudo-science.

Short-Term Causes

African independence movements demanding historical narratives affirming self-governing capacity; the development of radiocarbon dating and other new archaeological methods; the training of a new generation of African scholars at universities in Africa and abroad.

The Transformation

The establishment of rigorous, evidence-based African history as an academic discipline; launch of the UNESCO General History of Africa project; new methodological standards for evaluating oral tradition (Jan Vansina and others).

Effects

Correction of major historical misconceptions (such as the Hamitic hypothesis); recovery of detailed histories of states such as Great Zimbabwe, Mali, and the Swahili city-states on their own evidentiary terms; establishment of African-led historical scholarship as the global standard.

Historical complexity: This transformation cannot be reduced to a single cause. It required new scientific methods, new political circumstances, and a new generation of scholars working together — and the process of correcting older, biased accounts continues today.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Oral and Written Historical Transmission in Africa

What Changed

  • The spread of Arabic literacy following the expansion of Islam created new written record-keeping traditions across North and West Africa beginning in the medieval period.
  • Colonial administrations introduced European-language literacy, new archival systems, and new (often distorted) forms of documentation in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.
  • Modern academic historians developed systematic methods for evaluating and cross-checking oral tradition against archaeological and documentary evidence.

What Stayed the Same

  • Oral tradition remained — and remains today — a vital, actively maintained system of historical transmission in many African communities, alongside rather than replaced by written records.
  • Trained oral specialists, such as griots, continue to perform their historical and genealogical roles in many West African communities into the present day.
  • Communities across Africa have continued, across centuries of political change, to actively value and protect their own historical knowledge and material heritage.

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of oral tradition alongside, rather than displaced by, written and archaeological methods illustrates that historical knowledge in Africa has always existed in multiple, overlapping forms — a pattern this textbook will draw upon throughout.

Section 1.10

Chapter Review and Historical Thinking Checkpoint

This chapter has introduced the methods, concepts, and habits of mind that will guide the study of African history throughout this textbook. Before moving into the specific content of subsequent chapters — beginning with Africa's central role in human origins — students should be able to demonstrate command of these foundational ideas.

The core insights of this chapter are: African history studies the full range of peoples, societies, states, economies, and ideas that have developed across the continent, not merely its relationships with outsiders. Africa must be studied on its own terms, evaluated by its own internal patterns of development rather than by standards imported from elsewhere. Chronology and periodization require particular care given the continent's uneven evidentiary record across regions and periods. Geography — deserts, savannas, rainforests, highlands, rivers, and coasts — has profoundly shaped, without determining, the development of African societies. Archaeology, oral tradition, and written sources each provide distinctive and valuable evidence, with their own particular strengths and limitations, and historians use them in combination. Bias has been an especially serious problem in sources about Africa, particularly because of colonial-era racial ideology, and recognizing and correcting for it remains essential historical work. Cause and effect, and continuity and change, are essential tools for organizing African history's vast evidence into coherent and accurate explanation.

Chapter Summary

This first chapter has introduced African history as the study of the peoples, societies, cultures, states, economies, and ideas that have developed across the African continent from humanity's deepest origins to the present day. It has emphasized that Africa is not one place or one story, but a vast and extraordinarily diverse continent whose history must be studied region by region, period by period, and society by society, even as broader continental patterns and connections also matter.

The chapter has stressed the importance of studying Africa on its own terms — understanding African societies through their own internal political, economic, religious, and social development, rather than primarily through the lens of their relationships with outside powers. This does not mean ignoring Africa's extensive trade, religious, and political connections to the wider world across many centuries; it means refusing to treat those connections as the only thing worth knowing about African history.

Students have been introduced to the distinctive evidentiary challenges of African history: an uneven written record that is extremely rich in some regions (such as the Nile Valley and parts of West Africa and Ethiopia) and thinner in others; an archaeological record that has been transformed by modern dating methods capable of establishing chronology independent of written documentation; and a tradition of oral historical transmission that, properly understood and evaluated, constitutes legitimate and often indispensable historical evidence rather than unreliable folklore.

The chapter has also confronted the serious problem of bias in historical writing about Africa, particularly the racist assumptions embedded in colonial-era theories such as the "Hamitic hypothesis," which falsely attributed African achievement to outside influence. Correcting these biased accounts required new archaeological methods, new generations of African and international scholars, and decades of careful, evidence-based research — a process this textbook will draw upon throughout its chapters.

Finally, students have practiced applying the core conceptual tools of historical thinking — chronology, periodization, causation, continuity and change, and multiple perspectives — to the specific challenges of African historical evidence. These are not abstract exercises. They are the practical skills students will use throughout this textbook to evaluate evidence, weigh competing interpretations, and construct historically accurate understanding across sixty-six additional chapters spanning human origins, ancient civilizations, great medieval empires, religious transformation, the tragedies of the slave trades, the violence and upheaval of colonial conquest and rule, the long struggles for independence, and the dynamic, fast-changing societies of contemporary Africa.

Throughout, the organizing commitment of this textbook remains the same: to present Africa's history with the depth, complexity, evidence, and seriousness that any continent's history deserves — neither romanticized nor diminished, neither reduced to its relationships with outsiders nor isolated from the wider world it has always been part of.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Archive
A collection of primary sources preserved in libraries, religious institutions, or government repositories, used by historians as raw material for research.
Bias
A perspective or inclination embedded in a source that may distort its representation of events; especially significant in colonial-era writing about Africa.
Causation
The relationship between events in which one condition produces or contributes to another; historical causation usually involves multiple interacting causes.
Chronology
The sequential ordering of events in time, foundational to all historical analysis.
Continuity
Patterns or conditions that persist over time without fundamental transformation, often alongside significant change in other dimensions.
Corroboration
The process of checking whether multiple independent sources — archaeological, oral, and written — support the same historical claim.
Diffusionism
A discredited historical theory holding that significant cultural achievements must spread (diffuse) from a single point of origin rather than develop independently in multiple places.
Eurocentrism
The practice of evaluating non-European societies and history by standards, categories, or assumptions derived from European experience.
Griot
A professional oral historian, genealogist, and musician found in many West African societies, trained to preserve and transmit historical knowledge through structured recitation.
Hamitic hypothesis
A discredited nineteenth- and twentieth-century theory attributing African achievements to outside ("Hamitic") influence rather than to African societies themselves.
Historical memory
The way communities collectively remember, interpret, and use the past to construct identity and meaning in the present.
Historiography
The history of historical writing; the study of how interpretations of the past have changed over time and why.
Linguistic reconstruction
A method historians and linguists use to infer past population movements and relationships by analyzing similarities and differences among related languages.
Long-term cause
A deeper, underlying condition or structural tension that shapes the possibility of a historical development over an extended period.
Material culture
Physical objects — tools, pottery, buildings, artifacts — created and used by past societies, studied by archaeologists as historical evidence.
Multiple perspectives
The recognition that people in different social positions experience and interpret the same historical events differently.
Oral history
Historical information gathered through recorded interviews with living individuals about their own experiences or remembered knowledge.
Oral tradition
Structured, often formally transmitted historical, genealogical, or cultural knowledge passed down across generations through speech, recitation, and performance rather than writing.
Periodization
The practice of dividing the past into named periods or eras to make it more manageable, while recognizing that such divisions involve interpretive choices.
Primary source
A document, object, or record created at the time being studied, by someone who participated in, witnessed, or was affected by the events in question.
Radiocarbon dating
A scientific dating method that estimates the age of organic remains by measuring radioactive carbon decay, allowing chronology to be established without written records.
Secondary source
A work created after the fact, typically by a historian, that interprets and analyzes primary sources to construct an account of what happened and why.
Short-term cause
The immediate trigger or proximate event that sets a particular historical development in motion.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has laid the intellectual groundwork for the entire textbook. The methods and habits of mind it introduces — chronological thinking, geographic analysis, evaluation of archaeological, oral, and written evidence, critical attention to bias, and careful causal and comparative analysis — are not isolated skills to be applied once and set aside. They are the tools students will use in every subsequent chapter, from the deep human origins explored in Chapter 2 through the contemporary developments examined in the textbook's final chapters.

Africa's history is one of the richest and most consequential in the human record, encompassing the very origins of our species, some of the world's oldest civilizations, vast trading empires, profound religious traditions, devastating histories of enslavement and colonial conquest, and the ongoing creativity and resilience of contemporary African societies building new futures. Understanding this history requires more than memorizing names and dates. It requires the disciplined evaluation of evidence, an honest reckoning with how bias has distorted understanding in the past, and a genuine commitment to seeing African societies as historical actors in their own right rather than as a backdrop for someone else's story.

The example of the Timbuktu manuscripts, with which this chapter began, captures something essential about the spirit in which this textbook approaches its subject: African history is not a passive record waiting to be discovered and explained by outsiders. It is an active, contested, continually expanding field of knowledge, sustained by African scholars, archivists, griots, and ordinary families who have understood for centuries that their history matters and is worth protecting. Studying that history seriously means approaching it with the same rigor, respect, and critical attention that any other continent's history deserves.

As this textbook proceeds through sixty-six more chapters — tracing humanity's origins on African soil, the rise and fall of great civilizations and empires, the devastation and resistance of the slave trades, the violence and disruption of colonial conquest, the long struggles for independence, and the dynamic, fast-changing societies of contemporary Africa — students should carry forward the tools introduced here: ask who created this evidence and why; weigh archaeological, oral, and written sources against one another; watch for bias, especially inherited bias rooted in colonial-era racial ideology; and always ask what changed, what stayed the same, who was affected, and why it still matters.

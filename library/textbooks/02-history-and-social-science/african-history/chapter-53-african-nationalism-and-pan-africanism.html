Part IX — Independence, Nation Building, and the Cold War
53

African Nationalism and Pan-Africanism

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"We prefer self-government with danger to servitude in tranquility." — a phrase attributed to nationalist orators across colonial Africa, echoing the demand that dignity and self-rule mattered more than the promised comforts of continued colonial tutelage.

In October 1945, in a cold meeting hall in Manchester, England, a gathering of African and Caribbean intellectuals, trade unionists, and students convened what came to be remembered as the Fifth Pan-African Congress. Among the delegates were men who would soon lead nations that did not yet exist as independent states: Kwame Nkrumah of the Gold Coast, Jomo Kenyatta of Kenya, and others who would return to their home territories to build the organizations, newspapers, unions, and political parties that would dismantle European colonial rule across Africa within a generation. The congress issued a blunt declaration to the colonial powers: Africans demanded the right to govern themselves, and they were prepared to use "all the means at our disposal" to achieve it. This was not a request. It was a demand rooted in decades of organizing, decades of humiliation, decades of watching European empires justify their rule with promises of eventual self-government that never seemed to arrive.

This chapter examines how that demand became an irresistible historical force. It traces the rise of African nationalism — the political movements that asserted the right of African peoples to govern their own territories free from colonial control — and the related but distinct tradition of Pan-Africanism, which asserted the unity, dignity, and common destiny of African peoples across the continent and throughout the global African diaspora. These two currents, sometimes overlapping and sometimes in tension, reshaped African politics in the decades following the Second World War and set the stage for the wave of independence that would sweep the continent between the mid-1950s and the mid-1970s.

Nationalism in Africa did not emerge from nothing. It drew on much older traditions of resistance to conquest, on the religious and cultural movements examined in earlier chapters, and on decades of everyday refusal — tax resistance, labor strikes, church schisms, market boycotts — that colonial administrators had struggled to suppress. But the mid-twentieth century produced something new: mass political organizations capable of mobilizing millions of ordinary Africans, articulate leaders capable of speaking the language of international diplomacy while also speaking to rural farmers and urban workers, and a geopolitical moment, following the devastation of the Second World War and the erosion of European imperial confidence, that made colonial rule appear increasingly untenable.

This chapter asks who built these movements and why. It considers the role of intellectuals educated in colonial schools and in universities in Europe and the United States who absorbed the language of liberal democracy, socialism, and self-determination and turned that language back against the empires that had taught it to them. It considers the role of veterans who had fought in the Second World War, often in Europe or Asia, and who returned home unwilling to accept second-class status after having risked their lives for empires that denied them basic political rights. It considers the role of trade unionists whose strikes disrupted colonial economies, of market women whose boycotts challenged colonial taxation, of students whose associations linked activists across borders, and of ordinary farmers whose grievances over land, crops, and taxation provided the mass base without which no nationalist leader could have succeeded.

The chapter also asks what Africans meant by independence and unity, and whether those two goals were the same thing. Pan-Africanism, as a political vision, imagined forms of African unity that might transcend the artificial colonial borders drawn at Berlin in 1884 and 1885. Nationalism, in its more common form, aimed at independence within those very colonial borders, territory by territory. The tension between continental unity and territorial nationalism would shape debates over the next chapter's subject — decolonization itself — and would remain a live question in African politics long after formal independence was achieved.

Finally, this chapter considers what evidence historians use to understand this history: political pamphlets and newspapers, court records from sedition trials, colonial intelligence reports that reveal as much about colonial anxiety as about nationalist strength, oral histories collected from veterans of independence movements, congress resolutions, and the memoirs and speeches of nationalist leaders themselves. Reading these sources critically — asking who wrote them, for what audience, and with what purpose — is essential to understanding a period in which both colonial governments and nationalist movements had strong incentives to shape how their conflict would be remembered.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand nationalism and Pan-Africanism not as inevitable historical outcomes but as the product of specific choices, alliances, sacrifices, and arguments made by identifiable historical actors across a turbulent quarter-century of African history — a period whose consequences continue to shape the continent today.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the origins and growth of African nationalist movements in the decades following the Second World War.
  2. Trace the intellectual roots of Pan-Africanism from the nineteenth-century diaspora to the mid-twentieth-century congresses.
  3. Compare nationalism, which sought independence within colonial territorial borders, and Pan-Africanism, which envisioned broader continental or diasporic unity.
  4. Analyze the roles of intellectuals, veterans, trade unionists, students, and market women in building mass nationalist movements.
  5. Evaluate the impact of the Second World War on African political consciousness and colonial vulnerability.
  6. Identify major nationalist leaders and organizations and describe their political strategies and ideologies.
  7. Distinguish between elite-led constitutional nationalism and mass-based popular mobilization, and assess how the two interacted.
  8. Describe the role of newspapers, congresses, unions, and political parties as organizing tools.
  9. Assess the historiographical debate over whether African independence was primarily won by nationalist agency or granted through colonial calculation.
  10. Contextualize African nationalism within the global context of decolonization, the Cold War, and international human rights discourse.
  11. Explain why Pan-African unity efforts achieved only partial success at the continental political level.
  12. Connect the nationalist and Pan-African movements examined in this chapter to the independence struggles and postcolonial state-building discussed in subsequent chapters.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What conditions made mass nationalist movements possible in Africa after 1945 when earlier resistance movements had been suppressed?
  2. Who built African nationalist movements, and whose interests did those movements represent?
  3. How did Pan-Africanism, a movement with deep roots in the African diaspora, come to shape politics on the African continent itself?
  4. What role did the Second World War play in transforming African political consciousness?
  5. Why did some nationalist leaders favor gradual constitutional change while others favored more confrontational mass mobilization?
  6. How did colonial governments respond to nationalist organizing, and how effective were their strategies of repression and co-optation?
  7. Why did continental political unity prove more difficult to achieve than territorial independence?
  8. How did women, workers, students, and rural farmers shape nationalist movements that are often remembered primarily through their male elite leaders?
  9. What evidence do historians use to reconstruct the history of movements that were often illegal, suppressed, or documented mainly by their colonial opponents?
  10. How does the history of African nationalism connect to broader global movements for self-determination in the mid-twentieth century?

Historical Context

To understand the surge of nationalist organizing after 1945, students must first understand the conditions colonial rule had created across Africa by the middle of the twentieth century, conditions examined at length in earlier chapters of this textbook. By the 1940s, virtually the entire African continent, with the exceptions of Ethiopia and Liberia, lived under some form of European colonial administration — British, French, Belgian, Portuguese, Spanish, or Italian. Colonial economies had been reorganized around the export of raw materials: cocoa, coffee, cotton, copper, gold, rubber, and other commodities extracted for the benefit of metropolitan industries. Colonial administrations had imposed taxation, forced or coerced labor, and restrictions on political participation that left the vast majority of Africans without any meaningful voice in the governments that ruled them.

At the same time, colonial rule had produced, often unintentionally, the very conditions that would enable its own overthrow. Mission schools and, in a smaller number of cases, government schools had created a growing class of educated Africans — clerks, teachers, catechists, lawyers, and doctors — who could read colonial newspapers, understand colonial law, and, crucially, articulate grievances in the political language of the colonizers themselves: constitutionalism, rights, self-determination, and democracy. Colonial cities, meant to serve as administrative and commercial hubs, had become crowded with migrant laborers whose shared experience of low wages, poor housing, and racial discrimination created new forms of urban solidarity that cut across ethnic lines. Colonial railways and roads, built to move export commodities to the coast, also moved newspapers, pamphlets, and organizers between regions that had previously been more isolated from one another.

The Second World War (1939–1945), examined in Chapter 52, accelerated all of these trends dramatically. Hundreds of thousands of African soldiers served in British, French, and other colonial armies, fighting in North Africa, East Africa, Europe, and Southeast Asia. Many returned home with new skills, new political awareness, and a profound sense of the contradiction between the war's professed aims — the defense of freedom and democracy against fascist tyranny — and the reality of their own continued subjugation under colonial rule. The 1941 Atlantic Charter, in which Britain and the United States proclaimed the right of all peoples to choose their own form of government, was seized upon by African and Asian nationalists as a promise that applied to them, even though Winston Churchill later insisted it had never been intended to apply to colonial territories.

The war also weakened the colonial powers materially and psychologically. France had been defeated and occupied by Germany; Britain, though victorious, emerged from the war financially exhausted and dependent on American support. The moral authority of European empires, which had rested in part on claims of civilizational superiority, was badly damaged by a war in which European nations had waged unprecedented violence against one another. Meanwhile, the emergence of the United States and the Soviet Union as the dominant global powers after 1945 introduced new international pressures against colonialism, as both superpowers, for different reasons rooted in their own strategic interests, expressed rhetorical support for self-determination. The founding of the United Nations in 1945, with its charter's affirmation of the principle of self-determination of peoples, gave nationalist movements a new international forum in which to press their claims, even as the great colonial powers worked to limit the practical application of that principle.

Finally, students should understand that African nationalism did not emerge in isolation. It was part of a broader wave of anti-colonial nationalism sweeping Asia and the Middle East in the same period — India's independence in 1947, Indonesia's declaration of independence in 1945, the Chinese revolution of 1949, and nationalist movements across the Arab world. African nationalist leaders were acutely aware of these parallel struggles, drew inspiration and strategic lessons from them, and understood their own movements as part of a global process by which the age of European colonial empire was drawing to a close.

Section 53.1

The Rise of Nationalism

African nationalism, in the sense of organized political movements demanding self-government for specific colonial territories, developed unevenly across the continent between the 1920s and the 1950s, but it accelerated dramatically after the Second World War. Before the war, most political organizing under colonial rule took more limited forms: welfare associations, ethnic unions, professional associations of clerks and teachers, and early political parties that generally sought reform within the colonial system — expanded African representation on legislative councils, improved wages and working conditions, or greater access to education — rather than outright independence.

The change after 1945 was not simply rhetorical. It reflected a fundamental shift in what educated Africans believed was politically achievable. Before the war, the idea of full independence for African colonies within a foreseeable timeframe seemed, to most observers including many African political leaders themselves, a distant prospect. The devastation of the war, the weakening of Britain and France, the rising international currency of self-determination, and the demonstrated vulnerability of colonial administrations to organized protest all combined to convince a new generation of activists that independence was an achievable near-term goal rather than a distant aspiration.

Nationalist movements took different institutional forms in different colonial territories, shaped by the specific character of colonial rule in each case. In British West Africa, where colonial administration had allowed limited African participation in advisory councils and where a relatively large educated elite had developed through mission and government schools, nationalist movements often began as elite reform organizations before evolving into mass parties. In French Africa, where the colonial policy of assimilation theoretically offered a path to French citizenship for a small number of "évolués," nationalist politics initially developed partly within French metropolitan political structures, with African deputies elected to the French National Assembly after 1946, before shifting toward outright demands for independence. In settler colonies such as Kenya, Southern Rhodesia, and Algeria, where substantial European populations had acquired land and political power, nationalist movements confronted far more entrenched resistance, and political organizing frequently escalated into armed conflict, as examined further in Chapter 54.

Across these varied contexts, certain common features characterized the rise of nationalism. Nationalist leaders were disproportionately drawn from the small class of Western-educated Africans — teachers, clergy, lawyers, journalists, and clerks — who had the literacy, the exposure to international political ideas, and the organizational skills to build political parties and newspapers. Yet these leaders could not succeed without building coalitions that extended far beyond the educated elite: alliances with trade unions, ex-servicemen's associations, market women's organizations, ethnic and regional associations, and rural farmers aggrieved by specific colonial policies regarding land, crops, or taxation. The most successful nationalist movements were those that managed to fuse elite political demands for constitutional change with the concrete economic and social grievances of ordinary Africans.

Nationalist demands themselves evolved over time. In the immediate postwar years, most nationalist organizations still framed their demands in relatively moderate terms: expanded African representation, an end to racial discrimination in colonial administration, and a gradual, negotiated path toward eventual self-government, often described using vague and open-ended timelines set by colonial authorities themselves. By the early 1950s, however, a new and more assertive politics had emerged in several territories, exemplified by Kwame Nkrumah's Gold Coast campaign for "Self-Government Now," which explicitly rejected gradualist timetables and demanded immediate transfer of political power. This shift from constitutional gradualism to mass-based demands for immediate self-government marked a decisive turn in the history of African nationalism and set the template that would be followed, with local variations, across much of the continent over the following two decades.

Section 53.2

Anti-Colonial Intellectuals

The intellectual foundations of African nationalism were laid by a generation of thinkers, writers, and activists whose education, often acquired in colonial mission schools and later in universities in Britain, France, and the United States, exposed them simultaneously to the liberal and socialist political traditions of the West and to the lived reality of racial discrimination within those same societies. This combination — formal education in the language of rights and self-determination, paired with direct experience of colonial and racial subordination — produced a body of anti-colonial thought that drew selectively on Western political philosophy while fundamentally challenging the racial hierarchies on which colonial rule depended.

Some of the most influential anti-colonial intellectuals were not native to the African continent at all, but were members of the African diaspora in the Americas and the Caribbean whose writings and organizing profoundly shaped continental African nationalism. W. E. B. Du Bois, the African American sociologist and civil rights leader, had organized Pan-African Congresses beginning in 1919 that brought together delegates of African descent from across the world to demand an end to colonial rule and racial discrimination. Marcus Garvey, the Jamaican-born activist whose Universal Negro Improvement Association attracted millions of followers in the 1920s, promoted a vision of Black economic self-sufficiency, cultural pride, and eventual return to Africa that, while never achieving its literal political program, profoundly shaped popular consciousness about African dignity and unity among people of African descent worldwide, including within Africa itself.

Within the African continent, a parallel intellectual tradition developed, often shaped by direct study in Europe. Léopold Sédar Senghor of Senegal, who would later become the first president of independent Senegal, was a central figure in the Négritude literary movement, which he developed together with the Martinican poet Aimé Césaire and the French Guianese writer Léon-Gontran Damas while studying in Paris in the 1930s. Négritude asserted the distinctive value and dignity of Black African culture and identity against colonial ideologies that had portrayed African cultures as primitive or without history. Though Négritude was primarily a literary and cultural movement rather than a directly political one, it provided crucial intellectual groundwork for nationalist and Pan-African politics by insisting that African culture was not something to be discarded in pursuit of European approval but a source of legitimate pride and political claim-making.

Other intellectuals combined cultural and political analysis more directly. Kwame Nkrumah, who studied in the United States and Britain during the 1930s and 1940s, absorbed influences ranging from Marxist political economy to the organizing traditions of the African diaspora, and synthesized these influences into a distinctive political philosophy that he would later call "consciencism," combining African communal traditions, Christian ethics, and Marxist analysis into a program for both national liberation and continental unity. Frantz Fanon, born in Martinique and trained as a psychiatrist in France, worked in colonial Algeria during the war for independence and produced, in works examined further in the context of decolonization in Chapter 54, some of the most influential analyses of the psychological and political dynamics of colonial violence and anti-colonial resistance.

These intellectuals did not operate in isolation from one another. They corresponded, met at international conferences, published in overlapping journals and newspapers, and built personal and political networks that spanned the African continent, the Caribbean, Europe, and North America. This transnational intellectual network was itself an important precondition for the emergence of both African nationalism and Pan-Africanism, since it allowed ideas, strategies, and organizational models developed in one context to be rapidly transmitted and adapted elsewhere.

Historical Vocabulary
Négritude

Négritude was a literary and cultural movement developed in the 1930s primarily by French-speaking Black writers, most notably Léopold Sédar Senghor, Aimé Césaire, and Léon-Gontran Damas, who met as students in Paris. The movement asserted the distinctive value, beauty, and historical depth of Black African cultural identity in direct opposition to colonial ideologies that dismissed African culture as primitive or without history.

Négritude was not without internal debate and later criticism. Some African writers and intellectuals, including the Nigerian writer Wole Soyinka, later criticized aspects of Négritude for accepting a colonial framework that essentialized African identity in racial terms rather than emphasizing the diversity and dynamism of African societies. Nonetheless, Négritude played an important historical role in providing cultural and psychological groundwork for anti-colonial political mobilization, insisting that African dignity did not require assimilation into European cultural norms.

Section 53.3

Mass Politics and Protest

Nationalist ideas articulated by educated elites could not by themselves have overturned colonial rule. What transformed nationalism from an intellectual and constitutional movement into an irresistible political force was the mobilization of mass popular participation — strikes, boycotts, demonstrations, and organized civil disobedience that drew in workers, farmers, market women, students, and veterans on a scale that colonial administrations, however well armed, found extremely difficult to suppress without provoking further unrest.

A pivotal moment in this transition occurred in the Gold Coast in February 1948. A march by unarmed ex-servicemen — African veterans of the Second World War seeking to present a petition regarding pensions and benefits to the colonial governor — was fired upon by colonial police, killing several veterans. The shooting triggered several days of rioting in Accra and other towns, in which crowds looted European-owned shops, particularly those associated with the unpopular Association of West African Merchants, whose control over the cocoa trade had long generated resentment among African cocoa farmers. The colonial government's response — the arrest and temporary detention of nationalist leaders including Nkrumah, then a leader of the United Gold Coast Convention — inadvertently elevated those leaders' political standing and demonstrated the colonial administration's vulnerability to mass unrest.

The Accra riots and their aftermath illustrated a pattern that would recur across the continent: colonial governments, confronted with organized mass protest, faced an acute dilemma. Harsh repression risked provoking further unrest, generating international criticism, and undermining the colonial claim to be governing with the consent of the governed. Concession, on the other hand, risked emboldening nationalist demands and setting precedents that other colonial territories would seize upon. Most colonial administrations oscillated between these two responses, alternating periods of negotiation and reform with periods of arrest, censorship, and, in the most violent cases such as Kenya and Algeria, large-scale military repression.

Strikes represented another crucial form of mass mobilization. Railway workers, dockworkers, and miners across colonial Africa organized strikes that combined immediate economic demands — higher wages, better working conditions — with implicit or explicit political claims about African dignity and self-determination. The 1947–1948 railway workers' strike across French West Africa, which paralyzed the Dakar-Niger railway for over five months, demonstrated the capacity of organized labor to disrupt colonial economies and became, in the retelling by later writers including the Senegalese novelist Ousmane Sembène, a symbol of collective African resistance and solidarity across ethnic and territorial lines.

Market women's associations, particularly prominent in West African coastal cities where women dominated retail and market trade, provided another crucial base of mass mobilization. Market women in the Gold Coast and Nigeria organized boycotts of European goods, contributed financially to nationalist parties, and used their extensive social networks and control over urban commerce to spread nationalist messages and organize protest actions. Their participation illustrates the danger of understanding nationalist movements solely through the biographies of their most prominent male leaders; the movements' actual organizational strength depended heavily on constituencies whose contributions have often been underrepresented in conventional political histories.

By the early 1950s, in territories such as the Gold Coast, mass nationalist parties had developed sophisticated organizational structures — local branches, youth wings, women's wings, party newspapers, and disciplined strategies of coordinated civil disobedience, including Nkrumah's campaign of "Positive Action," modeled partly on the nonviolent resistance strategies developed by Mahatma Gandhi's Indian independence movement. These organizational innovations allowed nationalist parties to sustain pressure on colonial administrations over years, rather than relying on episodic and easily suppressed outbursts of unrest.

Section 53.4

Pan-African Ideas and Solidarity

Pan-Africanism, as a distinct political and intellectual tradition, predates the nationalist movements examined in the preceding sections by several decades, and its early development took place largely outside the African continent, among people of African descent in the Americas, the Caribbean, and Britain who had never set foot in Africa but who understood their own struggles against slavery's legacy and racial discrimination as connected to the fate of the African continent.

Early Pan-African organizing traced its roots to the late nineteenth century, when Black intellectuals in the United States, the Caribbean, and Britain began to articulate visions of solidarity among all peoples of African descent, often framed around shared experiences of the transatlantic slave trade and its aftermath, discussed at length in Chapters 41 and 44. The first Pan-African Conference, held in London in 1900 and organized substantially by the Trinidadian lawyer Henry Sylvester Williams, brought together delegates from Africa, the Caribbean, the United States, and Britain to discuss the condition of Black people worldwide and to protest colonial exploitation and racial discrimination.

W. E. B. Du Bois became the central organizing figure of Pan-African Congresses held periodically between 1919 and 1927, convened in Paris, London, Brussels, Lisbon, and New York. These early congresses were dominated by diaspora intellectuals — African Americans, Caribbean activists, and a small number of continental African participants, mostly drawn from a Western-educated elite — and their political demands, while asserting African dignity and criticizing colonial abuses, generally stopped short of demanding immediate independence, reflecting both the political constraints of the era and the still-limited direct participation of continental African activists in the movement's leadership.

A crucial turning point in the character of Pan-Africanism occurred in the mid-1930s, when Italy's fascist government invaded and occupied Ethiopia, one of only two African states that had never fallen under formal European colonial rule and a powerful symbol, discussed in Chapter 12, of African political independence and Christian civilization. The invasion provoked outrage among Pan-Africanists worldwide, who understood it as a direct assault on African sovereignty and dignity, and it galvanized new organizing efforts, including the founding of solidarity committees and fundraising campaigns across the diaspora. The Ethiopian crisis is widely regarded by historians as a critical moment in shifting Pan-African consciousness toward more explicitly anti-colonial and politically assertive positions.

The decisive transformation came with the Fifth Pan-African Congress, held in Manchester, England, in October 1945, in the immediate aftermath of the Second World War. Unlike earlier congresses, the Manchester gathering was organized and dominated by activists based in Africa or of a younger, more radical political orientation, including Kwame Nkrumah and the Trinidadian-born theorist George Padmore, who played a central organizing role. The congress's resolutions moved decisively beyond earlier calls for reform and explicitly demanded independence for African colonies, declaring the delegates' willingness to employ constitutional and, if necessary, extra-constitutional means to achieve self-government. Historians generally regard the Manchester Congress as marking the moment when Pan-Africanism's political center of gravity shifted definitively from the diaspora to the African continent itself, even as diaspora intellectuals like Du Bois, who attended the congress as an elder statesman of the movement, continued to play important symbolic and advisory roles.

In the years following Manchester, Pan-African solidarity took increasingly concrete institutional form on the African continent. Nkrumah, following Ghana's independence in 1957, hosted the All-African People's Conference in Accra in December 1958, bringing together nationalist leaders, liberation movement representatives, and trade unionists from across the continent to coordinate anti-colonial strategy and articulate a shared vision of African liberation and unity. Ghana under Nkrumah became a symbolic and material base for Pan-African organizing, hosting exiled nationalist leaders, funding liberation movements, and pressing, with only partial success, for schemes of continental political federation that would transcend colonial-era territorial boundaries.

Section 53.5

Labor, Students, and Activists

Beyond the prominent political leaders and intellectuals who dominate conventional narratives of African nationalism, a broader ecosystem of activists, organizations, and institutions provided the organizational infrastructure without which nationalist movements could not have sustained mass mobilization over the years required to force colonial concessions.

Trade unions occupied a particularly important position in this ecosystem. Colonial economic development, discussed in Chapter 49, had produced substantial urban and industrial workforces — railway workers, dockworkers, miners, and factory laborers — whose shared experience of low wages, dangerous working conditions, and racial discrimination in employment created strong incentives for collective organizing. Colonial administrations initially resisted recognizing African trade unions, fearing their political potential, but the economic pressures of the postwar period, combined with metropolitan labor reforms in Britain and France that extended some union rights to colonial territories, allowed trade union organizing to expand significantly during the 1940s and 1950s. Trade union leaders frequently became important nationalist political figures in their own right, and unions provided nationalist parties with organizational networks, meeting spaces, and disciplined cadres of activists experienced in coordinated collective action.

Student organizations, both within Africa and among African students studying abroad in Europe and North America, formed another crucial node of nationalist and Pan-African activism. Organizations such as the West African Students' Union, founded in London in 1925, and later the Federation of Student Unionists in various francophone contexts, brought together students from different colonial territories who might otherwise never have interacted, fostering networks of political friendship and collaboration that would prove enormously important once these students returned home to lead nationalist movements. Studying abroad exposed African students directly to metropolitan political debates, to the organized labor and socialist movements of interwar and postwar Europe, and, crucially, to the direct experience of racial discrimination within supposedly liberal democratic societies — an experience that often radicalized students who might have arrived in Europe with more moderate political views.

Religious institutions and independent African churches, examined in Chapters 27 and 28, also provided important organizational bases for nationalist activism in some contexts, offering networks of communication, leadership training, and, in some cases, direct theological justification for resistance to colonial authority framed in terms of biblical liberation narratives. Ethnic and regional associations, which colonial administrations had sometimes encouraged as a way of managing African political participation within manageable, non-threatening frameworks, were frequently repurposed by nationalist organizers as building blocks for broader coalition politics, even as tensions between ethnic particularism and national unity would remain a persistent challenge for postcolonial African states, as examined in later chapters.

Journalists and the vernacular and English- or French-language press played an equally vital role. Nationalist newspapers — such as Nkrumah's Accra Evening News in the Gold Coast, or numerous similar publications across francophone and anglophone Africa — provided crucial platforms for disseminating nationalist arguments, publicizing colonial abuses, coordinating protest actions, and building shared political consciousness among literate Africans across regions that might otherwise have had little contact with one another. Colonial administrations frequently responded with censorship, sedition prosecutions, and the closure of nationalist newspapers, but the press proved remarkably resilient, and prosecutions of nationalist editors and writers often generated more publicity and sympathy for the nationalist cause than they suppressed.

Section 53.6

Political Parties and Movements

The organizational form that ultimately proved most consequential in the struggle for African independence was the mass nationalist political party — an institution that combined elite leadership, disciplined organizational structure, and mass popular membership in ways that earlier, more narrowly elite political associations had not achieved.

The Convention People's Party, founded by Kwame Nkrumah in the Gold Coast in June 1949, is generally regarded by historians as the pioneering model of this new form of nationalist organizing. Nkrumah had broken from the more cautious, elite-dominated United Gold Coast Convention, which he judged too conservative and too slow in its demands, to found a party explicitly oriented toward mass membership, immediate self-government, and disciplined, confrontational political action, captured in the CPP's slogan "Self-Government Now." The party built an extensive organizational structure of local branches, youth organizations, and women's wings, and its ability to mobilize large-scale demonstrations and coordinated civil disobedience campaigns, including the 1950 Positive Action campaign that led to Nkrumah's imprisonment, ultimately forced the British colonial administration into a program of rapid constitutional reform that led to Ghanaian independence in 1957.

In French West and Equatorial Africa, the Rassemblement Démocratique Africain, founded in 1946, represented a different organizational model, initially operating as an inter-territorial coalition of nationalist parties across multiple French colonies, with representation in the French National Assembly following the 1946 constitutional reforms that extended limited citizenship and political representation to some colonial subjects. The RDA's most prominent leader, Félix Houphouët-Boigny of Côte d'Ivoire, pursued a more gradualist and cooperative strategy than Nkrumah, initially working within French colonial institutions and maintaining close ties to the French Communist Party before eventually moving toward a strategy of negotiated independence that preserved close economic and political ties with France — a strategy that produced independence with substantially different characteristics than the more confrontational Ghanaian path.

In settler colonies, nationalist party organizing confronted far greater obstacles, as substantial European settler populations resisted any political reforms that might threaten their economic and political privileges, and colonial administrations, more directly responsive to settler political pressure, were correspondingly more resistant to nationalist demands and more willing to employ severe repression. In Kenya, the colonial government's ban on African political organizing following the outbreak of the Mau Mau uprising in 1952, discussed further in Chapter 54, drove much nationalist organizing underground or into armed resistance, delaying the development of the kind of open mass party politics that characterized West African nationalism. In Algeria and the Portuguese territories, similarly, the entrenchment of settler and metropolitan colonial interests produced protracted and often violent independence struggles that extended well beyond the timeline of most West African decolonization.

Despite these variations, certain organizational lessons proved broadly transferable across the continent. Nationalist parties that successfully combined charismatic, articulate leadership with disciplined grassroots organizational structures, alliance-building across ethnic and regional lines, and sustained mass mobilization tactics generally achieved independence more rapidly and with greater popular legitimacy than movements that remained confined to narrow elite constitutional lobbying. This organizational template, pioneered in the Gold Coast and refined through the exchange of activists, strategies, and personal relationships fostered by Pan-African congresses and student networks, spread rapidly across the continent during the late 1950s and early 1960s.

Primary Source Window

Resolution of the Fifth Pan-African Congress (Manchester, 1945)

Creator: Delegates of the Fifth Pan-African Congress · Date: October 1945 · Type: Political resolution / manifesto

Context: The Fifth Pan-African Congress met in Manchester, England, in October 1945, bringing together African, Caribbean, and African American delegates in the immediate aftermath of the Second World War. Organized in significant part by George Padmore and attended by Kwame Nkrumah, among others, the congress issued resolutions addressed directly to the colonial powers demanding self-government for African peoples.

The delegates affirmed the right of all colonial peoples to control their own destiny, declared that all peoples had a right to govern themselves, and stated that Africans were prepared to fight, using every constitutional means available, and if necessary other means, to end the colonial system and imperialist exploitation of their continent.

Analysis: This resolution marked a decisive escalation from earlier, more conciliatory Pan-African Congress statements. Its explicit reference to means beyond constitutional protest reflected the delegates' growing conviction, shaped by wartime experience and the demonstrated fragility of colonial power, that peaceful petitioning alone would not secure independence. As a source, the resolution reflects the perspective of politically engaged, largely Western-educated delegates rather than the wider African population whose views on strategy and pace of change likely varied considerably.

Section 53.7

Leaders and Ideologies

African nationalist and Pan-African movements were not ideologically uniform. Leaders differed substantially in their political philosophies, their strategic preferences, and their visions of what independence should ultimately achieve, and these differences would shape the divergent trajectories of postcolonial African states examined in subsequent chapters.

Kwame Nkrumah articulated perhaps the most ambitious and explicitly Pan-African vision among the first generation of nationalist leaders, arguing that formal political independence for individual African states would remain incomplete and vulnerable to renewed foreign domination — what he later termed "neo-colonialism" — unless African states pursued continental political and economic unification. Nkrumah's famous declaration upon Ghana's independence in 1957, that Ghana's independence was "meaningless unless it is linked up with the total liberation of the African continent," captured this synthesis of nationalist and Pan-African commitment. Nkrumah's political philosophy drew on socialist economic analysis, African communal traditions, and Pan-African historical consciousness, and his government actively supported liberation movements elsewhere on the continent, hosted exiled nationalist leaders, and pressed, ultimately without success, for a continental political union.

Léopold Sédar Senghor, by contrast, articulated a vision that emphasized cultural Pan-Africanism, expressed through Négritude, more than immediate continental political union, and favored a gradualist path to independence that preserved close cultural and economic ties with France, embodied in his later advocacy of "Francophonie" as a framework for continued cooperation among French-speaking nations. Senghor's vision reflected both his personal intellectual formation in French literary culture and a strategic judgment that Senegal's economic interests were better served by continued close relations with France than by rapid, complete rupture.

Julius Nyerere of Tanganyika, who would lead his territory to independence in 1961 and later attempt to merge with Zanzibar to form Tanzania, developed a distinctive political philosophy, later articulated as "Ujamaa" or African socialism, that emphasized communal self-reliance, egalitarian social organization rooted in what he argued were traditional African values of extended family and mutual obligation, and a cautious, non-aligned approach to Cold War geopolitics. Nyerere's leadership style, emphasizing consensus-building and a relatively restrained approach to political opposition compared to some contemporaries, would shape Tanzania's distinctive postcolonial political development, examined in later chapters.

Félix Houphouët-Boigny of Côte d'Ivoire represented yet another ideological current: a pragmatic, market-oriented approach that prioritized close economic partnership with France and gradual, negotiated constitutional evolution over confrontational mass mobilization. Houphouët-Boigny's strategy, which some contemporaries criticized as insufficiently assertive, nonetheless produced a relatively smooth transition to independence and, in the following decades, one of the more economically successful postcolonial states in West Africa, though his long tenure in power would also raise questions, addressed in later chapters, about the relationship between economic development and political pluralism.

These ideological differences were not merely academic. They shaped concrete strategic choices — whether to pursue armed struggle or negotiated transition, whether to prioritize rapid nationalization of colonial economic assets or gradual economic transition, whether to align with the Soviet bloc, the Western bloc, or a policy of non-alignment during the Cold War, examined in Chapter 56, and whether to prioritize continental federation or strengthen individual national sovereignty. The persistence of these divisions helps explain why, despite widespread rhetorical commitment to Pan-African unity, actual continental political integration remained far more limited than Nkrumah and other unity advocates had hoped.

Historical Vocabulary
Pan-Africanism

Pan-Africanism is a political and cultural movement asserting the unity, dignity, and shared destiny of African peoples across the continent and throughout the global African diaspora. Emerging initially among diaspora intellectuals in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, Pan-Africanism shifted its center of gravity to the African continent itself following the Second World War, particularly after the 1945 Manchester Congress. Pan-Africanism has taken varied institutional and ideological forms, ranging from Kwame Nkrumah's advocacy of continental political federation to more limited forms of cultural solidarity and economic cooperation. Its most lasting institutional expression was the Organization of African Unity, founded in 1963, discussed in Chapter 63.

Section 53.8

Unity and Independence

The relationship between the goal of Pan-African continental unity and the goal of independence for individual colonial territories proved to be one of the central and unresolved tensions of the nationalist era. On one level, the two goals seemed complementary: independence for individual territories was widely understood, including by Pan-African advocates like Nkrumah, as a necessary first step toward eventual continental unification, since colonial powers were far more willing to negotiate independence with individual territorial nationalist movements than with a hypothetical continental movement demanding the wholesale dissolution of colonial rule across the entire continent simultaneously.

Yet the practical achievement of independence, territory by territory, also generated powerful new interests in preserving that independence and the sovereignty of the newly independent states, interests that often conflicted with schemes for continental federation that would have required individual states to cede meaningful sovereignty to a supranational African authority. Newly independent African leaders, having just achieved hard-won control over their own territorial states, generally proved reluctant to surrender that control to continental institutions, particularly when such institutions might be dominated by rival leaders or larger, more powerful neighboring states.

This tension became explicit at the 1963 conference in Addis Ababa that founded the Organization of African Unity, discussed at length in Chapter 63. Two rival blocs of African states — the more radical "Casablanca Group," associated with Nkrumah and advocating rapid continental political federation, and the more moderate "Monrovia Group," favoring a looser framework of cooperation that preserved individual state sovereignty — negotiated a compromise organizational charter that established continental cooperation on matters such as anti-colonial solidarity and border stability while explicitly affirming, rather than superseding, the sovereignty of individual member states. This compromise, while representing a genuine achievement of Pan-African cooperation, also marked the effective defeat of the more ambitious vision of full continental political union that Nkrumah and other unity advocates had championed.

The colonial-era territorial boundaries drawn at the Berlin Conference and subsequent European negotiations, discussed in Chapter 47, thus proved remarkably durable, surviving the transition to independence largely intact despite their arbitrary and often ethnically divisive character. The OAU's founding charter explicitly affirmed the principle of respecting existing colonial-era borders, a decision motivated by the recognition, widely shared among African leaders regardless of their position on the unity debate, that reopening territorial boundary disputes across the newly independent continent risked provoking devastating conflicts that would undermine the fragile stability of the new states.

Nonetheless, Pan-African solidarity continued to shape African politics in important ways even after the compromise settlement of 1963. Newly independent African states provided crucial material and diplomatic support to liberation movements still fighting for independence in Portuguese Africa, Southern Rhodesia, Namibia, and apartheid South Africa, discussed further in Chapter 57. Regional economic and political cooperation schemes, while falling short of full political union, developed steadily in subsequent decades. And the underlying Pan-African commitment to African dignity, solidarity, and mutual support against external domination remained a powerful and enduring current in African political culture, even as the specific institutional form of continental unity remained, and remains today, an unfinished and contested project.

Section 53.9

Nationalism as Historical Force

By the early 1960s, African nationalism had proven itself one of the most consequential political forces of the twentieth century, transforming, within roughly a decade and a half, a continent almost entirely under European colonial rule into one composed overwhelmingly of independent states. This transformation was neither inevitable nor accidental; it was the product of specific historical conditions, organizational innovations, and the sustained agency of millions of African participants, from prominent nationalist leaders to anonymous market women, striking railway workers, and rural farmers whose accumulated grievances and collective action made colonial rule increasingly unsustainable.

Historians continue to debate the relative weight of different causal factors in explaining the speed and success of African decolonization, a debate examined more fully in the Historical Debate feature below and revisited in Chapter 54. Some historians emphasize the primacy of African agency: the organizational skill of nationalist leaders, the mobilizing power of mass movements, and the accumulated pressure of decades of resistance, protest, and political organizing that made continued colonial rule increasingly costly and untenable for colonial powers. Other historians place greater emphasis on structural and international factors largely external to African nationalist movements themselves: the economic exhaustion of postwar European colonial powers, Cold War competition that made colonialism a liability in the contest for influence in the developing world, and the changing normative climate of international politics, in which colonial rule was increasingly delegitimized by the human rights language of the United Nations Charter and by the example of successful decolonization elsewhere in Asia.

The most persuasive historical accounts generally recognize that both sets of factors mattered and interacted with one another. Colonial powers were far more likely to make substantial concessions to nationalist movements that had demonstrated the organizational capacity to sustain mass mobilization and disrupt colonial administration and commerce, but nationalist movements also benefited enormously from a favorable international and economic context that made colonial powers more receptive to negotiated transitions than they might otherwise have been. The particular timing, pace, and character of independence in each African territory reflected the specific interaction of local nationalist organizing strength with these broader international conditions.

The consequences of African nationalism's success extended far beyond the immediate achievement of formal political independence. Nationalist movements had built, in the course of their organizing, new political institutions, new forms of mass political participation, and new national identities that would shape the subsequent history of postcolonial African states, examined in Chapter 55 and beyond. The leaders who emerged from nationalist movements would, in most cases, become the first heads of state of newly independent nations, carrying into government both the organizational strengths and the internal tensions — between elite and mass constituencies, between different regional and ethnic bases of support, between competing ideological visions — that had characterized their movements during the independence struggle itself.

Everyday Life and Social History

Ordinary Africans and the Politics of Independence

Behind the speeches of nationalist leaders and the resolutions of Pan-African congresses lay the everyday experiences of ordinary Africans whose lives were reshaped by, and who in turn reshaped, the nationalist movements of this era. For rural farmers, nationalist politics frequently intersected with immediate economic grievances: colonial marketing boards that paid farmers a fraction of the world market price for cash crops such as cocoa and cotton while retaining the difference as revenue for colonial administrations, land alienation policies that favored European settlers or agricultural schemes, and compulsory labor or crop-cultivation requirements that colonial administrations imposed with little regard for local agricultural knowledge or household needs. Nationalist parties that successfully linked their political demands to these concrete economic grievances built far deeper rural support than parties whose appeals remained confined to abstract constitutional arguments.

For urban workers and their families, nationalist politics intersected with the daily realities of colonial urban life: racially segregated housing and amenities, discriminatory wage scales that paid African workers a fraction of what European employees received for comparable work, overcrowded housing in African urban districts contrasted with well-serviced European residential areas, and the constant, grinding experience of racial subordination in encounters with colonial officials, employers, and police. Trade union organizing and nationalist party membership offered urban workers both concrete material demands and a sense of collective dignity and agency that colonial urban life systematically denied them.

Women's participation in nationalist movements, discussed briefly in earlier sections regarding market women's organizing, extended well beyond economic boycotts. Women organized within nationalist party structures, often through dedicated women's wings that mobilized female voters, raised funds, and organized demonstrations, even as women frequently found themselves excluded from the most senior leadership positions within nationalist parties and later postcolonial governments, reflecting the persistence of gendered assumptions about political authority that nationalist movements, despite their revolutionary rhetoric regarding colonial oppression, did not always apply consistently to questions of gender within their own societies.

For the generation of young Africans who came of age during the independence struggles of the 1940s and 1950s, nationalist politics offered a powerful sense of historical purpose and possibility, shaping educational and career aspirations, family discussions, religious and cultural identity, and the everyday texture of political conversation in schools, markets, and workplaces across the continent. Oral histories collected by historians in subsequent decades from participants in these movements — market women, former trade unionists, party organizers, and rank-and-file supporters — have proven an essential source for recovering this social history of nationalism that official archives, dominated by colonial administrative records and the papers of prominent leaders, often obscure.

Historical Debate

Was African Independence Primarily Won by Nationalist Mass Mobilization, or Granted by Colonial Powers for Their Own Strategic Reasons?

The question: Historians have long debated the relative weight of African nationalist agency versus external structural factors in explaining the speed and character of decolonization across Africa between the mid-1950s and mid-1970s.

Interpretation A — Nationalism as the Primary Cause: Some historians emphasize that sustained, organized African nationalist pressure — mass parties, strikes, boycotts, and, in some cases, armed resistance — made continued colonial rule prohibitively costly and politically untenable for colonial powers. In this view, independence was won through African political agency and sacrifice, and colonial powers granted concessions primarily because nationalist movements had made colonial administration increasingly difficult and expensive to sustain.
Interpretation B — Structural and International Factors as the Primary Cause: Other historians argue that the pace and pattern of decolonization owed at least as much to factors largely external to African nationalist movements: the postwar economic exhaustion of Britain and France, Cold War competition that made colonialism a liability, changing international norms embodied in the United Nations Charter, and calculated decisions by colonial policymakers that granting formal political independence while preserving economic and strategic influence — sometimes termed "neo-colonialism" by critics such as Nkrumah — served metropolitan interests better than continued direct colonial administration.

Evidence: Evidence for Interpretation A includes the documented disruption caused by strikes, boycotts, and mass demonstrations, and the close correlation between nationalist organizational strength and the speed of independence in specific territories. Evidence for Interpretation B includes internal colonial government documents revealing strategic calculations about the costs of continued colonial rule, the similar timing of independence across territories with widely varying levels of nationalist mobilization, and the persistence of substantial economic and military influence retained by former colonial powers after formal independence.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians assess the achievements and limitations of the independence generation, and it bears directly on debates about neo-colonialism and the extent to which formal political independence translated into genuine economic and political sovereignty for African states.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? Can both interpretations be substantially correct at the same time? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate for a specific African territory of your choosing?

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing the Rise of Nationalism, 1945–1960

From the perspective of nationalist party leaders: The postwar period represented a long-awaited opportunity to translate decades of grievance and organizing into concrete political power, requiring careful strategic judgments about when to negotiate with colonial authorities and when to escalate mass mobilization, often under conditions of arrest, censorship, and personal risk.
From the perspective of colonial administrators: Nationalist mobilization appeared as a dangerous and destabilizing threat to orderly governance, and many colonial officials genuinely believed, whether sincerely or self-servingly, that African territories were not yet "ready" for self-government, even as they faced mounting evidence that continued colonial rule was becoming administratively and politically unsustainable.
From the perspective of rural farmers and market women: Nationalist politics offered a vehicle for addressing immediate economic grievances regarding crop prices, land, and taxation, and participation in boycotts, strikes, and demonstrations represented both a political statement and a practical economic strategy, even when abstract constitutional debates in distant capitals felt remote from daily concerns.
From the perspective of European settlers in colonies such as Kenya and Algeria: Nationalist demands for independence appeared as a direct existential threat to settler economic and political privileges built up over decades, and settler communities frequently pressed colonial governments toward harsher repression than metropolitan authorities in London or Paris were initially inclined to pursue.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Mass Nationalism After 1945

Long-Term Causes

Decades of colonial economic exploitation and racial discrimination; the growth of a Western-educated African elite through mission and government schools; the development of transnational Pan-African intellectual networks; long traditions of local resistance to colonial rule.

Short-Term Causes

African veterans' wartime service and postwar disillusionment; the Atlantic Charter's rhetoric of self-determination; postwar British and French economic exhaustion; the 1945 Manchester Congress resolutions; specific triggering events such as the 1948 Accra riots.

The Event

The rapid growth, between roughly 1948 and 1960, of mass nationalist parties across colonial Africa, culminating in Ghanaian independence in 1957 and the accelerating wave of independence across the continent thereafter.

Effects

The dismantling of most formal European colonial rule across Africa within roughly two decades; the establishment of new independent states and the challenges of nation-building examined in Chapter 55; the partial, compromised achievement of Pan-African institutional cooperation through the OAU in 1963.

Historical complexity: The rise of mass nationalism cannot be reduced to any single cause. It resulted from the convergence of long-building internal African political organizing with a specific and temporary postwar international context that made colonial concessions more likely — a convergence that varied significantly by territory and produced markedly different paths and timelines to independence.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the rise of African nationalism and Pan-Africanism as interconnected but distinct political traditions that transformed the African continent's political landscape in the decades following the Second World War. Nationalism, in the sense of organized movements demanding independence for specific colonial territories, drew on decades of earlier resistance but accelerated dramatically after 1945, propelled by the political awakening of African war veterans, the weakened material and moral position of European colonial powers, and a new international climate favorable to claims of self-determination.

Pan-Africanism, with roots stretching back to the diaspora-led congresses of the early twentieth century, underwent a decisive transformation at the 1945 Manchester Congress, when leadership of the movement shifted definitively from diaspora intellectuals to Africa-based activists such as Kwame Nkrumah, who would go on to lead both the nationalist struggle in the Gold Coast and, after Ghanaian independence in 1957, a broader continental effort to promote African political and economic unity.

The chapter has emphasized that nationalist success depended on far more than the eloquence or strategic skill of prominent leaders. Mass mobilization — strikes by railway workers and dockworkers, boycotts organized by market women, demonstrations by ex-servicemen, and the organizational networks built by trade unions, student associations, and the nationalist press — provided the essential foundation without which elite constitutional demands could not have forced colonial concessions. The Accra riots of 1948 and the subsequent rise of Nkrumah's Convention People's Party illustrated a pattern of mass-based, disciplined nationalist organizing that would be emulated, with significant local variations, across much of the continent.

The chapter has also traced the significant ideological diversity within nationalist and Pan-African leadership, from Nkrumah's ambitious vision of continental political federation to Senghor's cultural Pan-Africanism and continued close ties with France, to Nyerere's communal African socialism, to Houphouët-Boigny's pragmatic, gradualist cooperation with French colonial and postcolonial economic structures. These ideological differences reflected genuine disagreements about strategy, economic policy, and the ultimate meaning of African liberation, and they would continue to shape the divergent political trajectories of independent African states.

A central tension examined throughout this chapter concerns the relationship between territorial nationalism, which sought independence within existing colonial-era borders, and Pan-African aspirations toward broader continental unity that might transcend those borders. This tension came to a head at the 1963 Addis Ababa conference that founded the Organization of African Unity, where the more ambitious vision of continental political federation, championed by Nkrumah and the Casablanca Group, was effectively set aside in favor of a looser framework of cooperation that explicitly preserved individual state sovereignty and colonial-era territorial boundaries.

The chapter has attended throughout to the roles of ordinary Africans — rural farmers responding to specific grievances over crop prices and land, urban workers organizing around wages and racial discrimination in employment, market women mobilizing boycotts and providing financial and organizational support, and students building transnational networks of political friendship and collaboration — whose collective agency provided the indispensable mass base for nationalist success, even though their contributions are often underrepresented in accounts that focus primarily on prominent male political leaders.

Historians continue to debate the relative causal weight of African nationalist agency versus external structural and international factors in explaining the pace and character of decolonization, a debate this chapter has presented without resolving, since the strongest evidence suggests that both internal African organizing and external international conditions mattered and interacted with one another in ways that varied significantly across different colonial territories.

By 1963, the year the Organization of African Unity was founded, the political landscape of Africa had been transformed almost beyond recognition from what it had been in 1945. A continent that had been, with only two exceptions, entirely under European colonial administration was now composed overwhelmingly of independent states, governed in most cases by leaders who had risen through the nationalist movements examined in this chapter. This transformation, one of the most rapid and consequential in modern world history, set the stage for the challenges of nation-building, Cold War geopolitics, and continued liberation struggles examined in the chapters that follow.

The nationalist and Pan-African movements of this era did not resolve every question they raised. The tension between elite and mass political participation, between territorial sovereignty and continental unity, and between different ideological visions of African liberation would persist, in new forms, throughout the postcolonial period. But the achievement of formal political independence across most of the African continent within less than two decades after the Second World War stands as one of the great political transformations of the modern era, and understanding how it was achieved — through the combined agency of intellectuals, party organizers, trade unionists, market women, students, and ordinary rural and urban Africans — is essential to understanding the Africa that exists today.

The chapters that follow will examine how this hard-won independence was translated, with varying success, into functioning postcolonial states, and how the promises and tensions embedded within the nationalist and Pan-African movements examined here continued to shape African political life for decades after the colonial flags came down.

Key Terms

Nationalism
A political movement asserting the right of a people, typically defined by shared territory, culture, or history, to self-government; in the African context, movements demanding independence for specific colonial territories.
Pan-Africanism
A political and cultural movement asserting the unity, dignity, and shared destiny of African peoples across the continent and the global African diaspora.
Self-determination
The principle that peoples have the right to freely determine their own political status and form of government, invoked by nationalist movements and affirmed, at least rhetorically, in the Atlantic Charter and the United Nations Charter.
Négritude
A literary and cultural movement asserting the distinctive value of Black African identity, developed by Léopold Sédar Senghor, Aimé Césaire, and others in Paris during the 1930s.
Mass mobilization
Political organizing strategies aimed at drawing large numbers of ordinary people into sustained collective action, such as strikes, boycotts, and demonstrations.
Constitutional nationalism
A political strategy emphasizing negotiation, legal reform, and gradual constitutional change rather than confrontational mass mobilization.
Diaspora
Communities of people descended from a common homeland who live dispersed across other regions of the world; in this chapter, primarily referring to people of African descent in the Americas, Caribbean, and Europe.
Positive Action
Kwame Nkrumah's campaign of coordinated nonviolent civil disobedience in the Gold Coast, modeled partly on Gandhian resistance strategies.
Ex-servicemen
Veterans of colonial military service, particularly in the Second World War, whose wartime experience and postwar grievances made them an important constituency for nationalist organizing.
Manchester Congress
The Fifth Pan-African Congress, held in Manchester, England, in October 1945, marking the shift of Pan-African leadership toward Africa-based activists.
Convention People's Party (CPP)
Mass nationalist party founded by Kwame Nkrumah in the Gold Coast in 1949, pioneering strategies of mass political organizing.
Rassemblement Démocratique Africain (RDA)
Inter-territorial nationalist political coalition across French West and Equatorial Africa, founded in 1946.
Neo-colonialism
A term, associated particularly with Kwame Nkrumah, describing continued economic and political domination of formally independent states by their former colonial powers or other external actors.
Casablanca Group
Bloc of African states, associated with Nkrumah, that advocated rapid and substantial continental political federation in the early 1960s.
Monrovia Group
Bloc of African states that favored a looser framework of continental cooperation preserving individual state sovereignty, whose approach substantially shaped the eventual OAU charter.
Organization of African Unity (OAU)
Continental organization founded in 1963 in Addis Ababa, representing a compromise between Pan-African unity aspirations and territorial state sovereignty, discussed further in Chapter 63.
Ujamaa
Julius Nyerere's philosophy of African socialism, emphasizing communal self-reliance and egalitarian social organization.
Sedition
The colonial-era legal charge frequently used against nationalist journalists, editors, and political leaders for publishing or speaking material deemed to incite resistance to colonial authority.
Atlantic Charter
A 1941 joint declaration by Britain and the United States affirming the right of peoples to choose their own form of government, invoked by nationalists despite Winston Churchill's insistence it did not apply to colonies.
Vernacular press
Newspapers published in local African languages or in colonial languages aimed at African readerships, crucial tools for disseminating nationalist ideas and coordinating political action.
All-African People's Conference
Continental gathering of nationalist and liberation movement leaders convened in Accra in 1958 to coordinate anti-colonial strategy.
Évolués
A term used in French colonial policy for a small class of Africans deemed to have adopted French culture and education sufficiently to be granted limited citizenship rights.

Tanganyikan (later Tanzanian) nationalist leader and first president, associated with the philosophy of Ujamaa, or African socialism.
Félix Houphouët-Boigny (1905–1993)
Ivorian nationalist leader and prominent figure in the RDA, who favored gradualist, cooperative relations with France over confrontational mass mobilization.
Fifth Pan-African Congress (Manchester, 1945)
Landmark gathering that shifted Pan-African leadership toward Africa-based activists and issued explicit demands for African self-government.
Accra, Gold Coast
Site of the 1948 riots that catalyzed mass nationalist politics, later capital of independent Ghana and host of the 1958 All-African People's Conference.
Convention People's Party
Mass nationalist party founded by Nkrumah in 1949, pioneering organizational strategies later emulated across colonial Africa.
Rassemblement Démocratique Africain
Inter-territorial nationalist coalition across French West and Equatorial Africa, founded in 1946.
Addis Ababa
Ethiopian capital and site of the 1963 conference that founded the Organization of African Unity, discussed further in Chapter 63.
Ethiopia
One of only two African states to avoid formal European colonial rule; its resistance to Italian invasion in the 1930s became a powerful symbol of Pan-African solidarity.
Ghana (Gold Coast)
First sub-Saharan African colony to achieve independence, in 1957, becoming a model and symbolic center for nationalist and Pan-African organizing across the continent.

Building a Biographical Profile of a Nationalist or Pan-African Leader

This project asks you to research one nationalist or Pan-African leader discussed in this chapter, or another figure of comparable historical significance, and construct a biographical profile connecting their personal history to the broader movements examined in this chapter.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined one of the most consequential political transformations in African history: the rise of nationalist and Pan-African movements that, within roughly two decades after the Second World War, dismantled formal European colonial rule across most of the continent. This transformation was neither automatic nor inevitable. It required the sustained organizing labor, political imagination, and personal sacrifice of an extraordinarily wide range of historical actors — intellectuals who synthesized diaspora and continental political traditions, party organizers who built disciplined mass movements, trade unionists and market women who mobilized economic pressure, students who forged transnational networks of solidarity, and millions of ordinary Africans whose accumulated grievances and collective action made colonial rule increasingly untenable.

The chapter has also emphasized that this achievement, however historic, remained incomplete and contested even at the moment of its greatest success. The tension between territorial nationalism and continental Pan-African unity, dramatized in the 1963 compromise that founded the Organization of African Unity, was never fully resolved, and questions about the proper scope of African political and economic integration continue to animate African politics today. Similarly, the ideological differences among nationalist leaders — regarding the pace of change, the relationship with former colonial powers, and the proper economic and political organization of newly independent states — would shape, in ways examined in subsequent chapters, the widely divergent trajectories of postcolonial African states.

This chapter connects directly to the chapters that follow. The organizational structures, leadership networks, and ideological debates examined here would carry directly into the process of formal decolonization examined in Chapter 54, the challenges of nation-building examined in Chapter 55, and the Cold War geopolitical pressures examined in Chapter 56. The Pan-African institutional framework established through the compromises discussed in this chapter would find its lasting expression in the Organization of African Unity, examined in Chapter 63, and its successor, the African Union.

Students should take from this chapter an appreciation for the complexity and contested nature of historical movements that are sometimes remembered, in simplified popular narratives, as the achievement of a small number of celebrated founding leaders. The history of African nationalism and Pan-Africanism reveals a far richer and more complicated reality: a continent-wide political transformation built through the convergence of intellectual argument, mass popular mobilization, favorable international conditions, and difficult strategic choices made by a diverse range of historical actors whose contributions, individually and collectively, reshaped the political map of the world.

Understanding this history — its achievements, its internal tensions, and its unfinished questions — is essential to understanding not only the mid-twentieth century but the Africa of today: a continent of independent, sovereign nations still working, through institutions descended directly from the movements examined in this chapter, toward the fuller measure of unity, dignity, and self-determination that the delegates at Manchester in 1945 first demanded.

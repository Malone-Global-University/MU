Part III — Peoples, Languages, and Cultural Exchange
11

Early Cities, Trade, and State Formation

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Long before large empires rose along the Niger, the Nile, or the Zambezi, African communities were already building cities, organizing markets, and exercising political authority over crowded, complex urban populations."

On the floodplain of the middle Niger River, in what is today central Mali, archaeologists have uncovered the remains of a city that flourished more than a thousand years before the rise of the great Sahelian empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai. The site, known today as Jenné-Jeno, grew from a small fishing and farming settlement around 250 BCE into a substantial urban center with specialized craftspeople, long-distance trade connections, and a population that may have reached several thousand people by the early centuries of the common era. Jenné-Jeno had no monumental palace, no single ruling dynasty that left behind royal inscriptions, and no obvious central authority of the kind historians might expect to find at the heart of an ancient city. And yet it was unmistakably urban: a place where farmers, fishers, ironworkers, potters, and traders lived together at high density, exchanged goods across great distances, and developed shared ways of organizing collective life.

Jenné-Jeno is only one example of a much broader pattern. Across the African continent, urban life and organized political authority developed independently, repeatedly, and often long before contact with the Mediterranean world, the Islamic world, or Europe. Cities grew along the Nile, the Niger, the Senegal, and the Zambezi rivers. They grew on the savannas of West Africa, in the highlands of Ethiopia, on the coast of East Africa, and in the interior plateaus of southern Africa. Some cities grew up around religious shrines and pilgrimage centers. Others grew around markets at the junction of trade routes, around fortified settlements that controlled access to valuable resources, or around the courts of emerging rulers who needed concentrated populations of craftspeople, soldiers, and administrators to support their power.

This chapter asks a deceptively simple question: how and why did cities and organized states emerge across ancient Africa, and what made some settlements grow into dense urban centers while others remained small villages? The answer is not simple, and it is not the same in every region. In some places, trade was the decisive force, as settlements at the crossroads of valuable exchange routes grew wealthy and attracted migrants, craftspeople, and political ambition. In other places, agricultural surplus came first, allowing populations to grow dense enough to support specialized non-farming professions — metalworkers, potters, priests, soldiers, and administrators — who in turn needed organized systems of exchange and authority to sustain them. In still other places, religious or ceremonial functions drew people together at sacred sites that gradually became permanent settlements.

What changed across this long process was profound. Societies that had once organized themselves primarily around kinship — extended families, lineages, and clans — increasingly developed new forms of social organization based on occupation, residence, and political rank. Trade networks that had once been local and occasional became regional and sustained. Authority that had once rested largely on the consent and respect of kin groups increasingly came to rest on control of trade routes, control of specialized production, and the ability to organize collective labor and defense.

What continued, just as importantly, were many of the underlying social structures that had organized African communities for thousands of years. Kinship remained central to identity and obligation even within growing cities. Religious belief and ritual practice continued to shape political authority, often investing rulers and elites with spiritual as well as political legitimacy. Agricultural and pastoral life remained the foundation of most African economies, even as cities grew increasingly dependent on trade and craft production. The transition to urban and state-organized life was gradual, uneven, and never complete: most Africans throughout this period, and for centuries afterward, continued to live in rural farming and herding communities rather than in cities.

Both ordinary people and emerging elites were affected by these changes, though in different ways. Farmers who produced agricultural surplus made urban life possible, even as they often remained at the social and political margins of the cities their labor sustained. Craftspeople — blacksmiths, potters, weavers, leatherworkers — gained new opportunities for specialized livelihoods but also became dependent on markets and patrons beyond their own kin networks. Traders who controlled valuable goods or strategic routes could accumulate wealth and influence that rivaled or exceeded that of hereditary chiefs. Emerging rulers and elites who could control trade, organize defense, and claim religious legitimacy found new tools for building lasting political authority — the foundation of the states and empires examined in later chapters of this textbook.

Historians and archaeologists reconstruct this story using several different kinds of evidence, each with its own strengths and limits. Archaeological excavation reveals the physical layout of ancient settlements, the scale of population growth, the presence of specialized craft production, and the evidence of long-distance trade goods such as glass beads, copper, and cowrie shells found far from their points of origin. Oral traditions, transmitted across many generations, preserve memories of founding ancestors, migrations, and the origins of political authority, even though such traditions must be read carefully and critically. Written sources from North Africa, the Mediterranean world, and later the Islamic world occasionally mention African trading centers and political structures, offering an outside perspective that complements — and sometimes contradicts — internal African traditions and archaeological evidence.

This chapter connects directly to the larger story this textbook tells. The cities, markets, and early states examined here were not isolated curiosities; they were the foundations on which the great African civilizations and empires explored in later chapters — Ghana, Mali, Songhai, the Swahili city-states, Great Zimbabwe, and many others — were built. Understanding how urban life, trade networks, and political authority first developed in Africa is essential to understanding everything that followed.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the conditions that allowed cities to develop independently in different regions of ancient Africa.
  2. Trace the growth of early urban centers such as Jenné-Jeno and their connections to surrounding rural economies.
  3. Analyze how trade functioned as a force of social, economic, and political change.
  4. Describe the development of markets, craft specialization, and occupational diversity in early African cities.
  5. Compare different sources of political authority in early urban centers, including kinship, religion, trade control, and military power.
  6. Evaluate the role of craft production, particularly ironworking and textile production, in generating wealth and social differentiation.
  7. Identify the function of ritual centers and sacred sites in early urbanization.
  8. Assess how regional trade networks connected cities to one another and to areas beyond Africa.
  9. Distinguish between urbanization and state formation, and explain how the two processes related to one another.
  10. Compare early African urbanization to urbanization processes in other parts of the ancient world.
  11. Interpret archaeological and oral evidence used to reconstruct the history of early African cities.
  12. Contextualize early cities and states as foundations for the larger empires examined later in this textbook.

Historical Context

By the time the developments described in this chapter took place, African societies had already undergone the long transition from hunting and gathering to food production described in earlier chapters of this textbook. Agriculture and pastoralism had spread across most of the continent's habitable regions, populations had grown, and village life had become the dominant form of settlement across much of sub-Saharan Africa. Ironworking technology, discussed in Chapter 16, had also spread widely by the middle of the first millennium BCE, providing African farmers with more effective tools and African communities with more effective weapons.

These earlier developments created the preconditions for urbanization. Agricultural surplus — more food than a farming community needed for its own immediate consumption — made it possible for some members of a community to specialize in non-farming activities: making pottery, working metal, weaving cloth, organizing trade, performing religious ceremonies, or governing. Population growth created denser settlements with more complex social needs. Ironworking and other technologies expanded the range of goods that could be produced, traded, and valued.

Geography played a decisive role in determining where these preconditions translated into urban growth. River valleys such as the Nile, the Niger, and the Senegal provided fertile agricultural land, reliable water supplies, and natural transportation corridors that could carry goods and people across long distances with relatively little effort compared to overland travel. Locations at the edge of major ecological zones — where the Sahara desert met the Sahel grasslands, or where forest met savanna — often became important trading points, because they connected communities producing very different goods: desert salt and Mediterranean products on one side, savanna grain and gold on the other, forest kola nuts and other valuable goods further south.

Long-distance trade networks already existed in some form before the rise of the cities examined in this chapter. Evidence of obsidian, marine shells, and other materials moving hundreds of kilometers from their points of origin shows that some form of exchange — whether direct trade, gift exchange, or relay trade through intermediary communities — had connected different regions of Africa for a very long time. What changed during the period covered in this chapter was the scale, intensity, and organization of trade, and the emergence of permanent settlements specifically organized around commercial exchange.

It is also important to understand what urbanization in early Africa was not. It did not mean the disappearance of rural life; the great majority of Africans throughout this period continued to live in farming and herding communities, and cities depended entirely on the agricultural surplus that rural producers supplied. It did not mean the immediate appearance of large centralized states with kings, armies, and written law codes; many early cities, including Jenné-Jeno, appear to have grown and functioned for centuries without clear evidence of centralized political authority in the form familiar from later African empires. And it did not happen everywhere at the same time or in the same way; urbanization in the Nile Valley, the West African Sahel, the East African coast, and the southern African plateau each followed distinct timelines and distinct local logics, even though they shared certain underlying causes.

Section 11.1

The Growth of African Cities

A city, in the most basic historical sense, is a permanent settlement with a population dense enough and large enough to support specialized, non-agricultural occupations: craftspeople, traders, religious specialists, administrators, and others who do not produce their own food but instead obtain it through exchange, tribute, or wages. By this definition, cities developed in Africa earlier and more widely than many older historical accounts once recognized. For much of the twentieth century, historians trained in European and American academic traditions tended to assume that urban life in sub-Saharan Africa began only with the arrival of Islamic traders or European colonizers. Archaeological research conducted since the 1970s, particularly along the middle Niger River, has overturned this assumption decisively.

The clearest and most thoroughly studied example is Jenné-Jeno, located near the modern city of Djenné in Mali. Excavations beginning in the 1970s revealed that the settlement grew gradually from a small village around 250 BCE into an urban center covering tens of hectares by the middle of the first millennium CE, with associated satellite settlements forming a larger urban cluster. The population engaged in farming, fishing, and herding to sustain itself, while also developing specialized iron production, pottery manufacture, and participation in long-distance trade networks that brought copper, possibly originating from sources hundreds of kilometers away, into the settlement.

What makes Jenné-Jeno especially significant to historians is what archaeologists did not find: no royal palace, no monumental architecture clearly associated with a single ruling family, and no clear evidence of a centralized political hierarchy controlling the settlement from a single point. This has led many archaeologists to describe early Jenné-Jeno as an example of what is sometimes called a "heterarchical" urban community — one in which power and authority were distributed across multiple institutions, lineage groups, craft associations, and ritual specialists, rather than concentrated in a single ruling office. This pattern challenges assumptions, drawn largely from the history of ancient Mesopotamia, Egypt, and other early urban civilizations, that cities necessarily require centralized monarchies to organize and sustain them.

Cities also grew in the Nile Valley, where Egyptian and Nubian urban centers developed in close connection with centralized monarchies, as discussed in Chapters 7 and 8. Along the East African coast, settlements that would later become the great Swahili trading towns began as smaller fishing and farming communities that grew increasingly urban as their participation in Indian Ocean trade expanded, a process examined more fully in Chapter 32. In the Horn of Africa, the kingdom of Aksum developed urban centers connected to its control of Red Sea trade routes, as discussed in Chapter 10. In southern Africa, the rise of Mapungubwe, examined in Chapter 38, shows yet another regional pattern of urban growth tied to cattle wealth, trade in ivory and gold, and the development of social hierarchy.

What unites these diverse regional stories is not a single cause but a shared set of underlying conditions: agricultural or pastoral surplus sufficient to support non-farming specialists, access to valuable trade goods or strategic trade routes, and social mechanisms — whether centralized monarchy, distributed lineage authority, or some combination — capable of organizing collective life at a larger and more complex scale than village society required. Understanding African urbanization, in other words, requires attention to multiple regional pathways rather than a single universal model.

Section 11.2

Trade as a Force of Change

Trade was one of the most powerful forces reshaping African societies during this period, and its effects extended far beyond the simple exchange of goods. Trade reorganized settlement patterns, as communities positioned at advantageous points along trade routes grew disproportionately compared to those without such access. Trade reorganized social structure, as successful traders accumulated wealth and influence that could rival or exceed that of hereditary leaders. And trade reorganized political authority, as the ability to control, tax, or facilitate trade became an increasingly important source of power.

Several distinct types of trade operated across ancient Africa, often simultaneously. Local exchange connected nearby communities producing different but complementary goods — grain for fish, pottery for iron tools, salt for foodstuffs. Regional exchange connected communities across larger distances within a shared ecological or cultural zone, such as the trade linking different settlements along the Niger River floodplain. Long-distance trade connected communities across major ecological boundaries — the Sahara desert, the East African coast and the Indian Ocean, the southern African interior and its coastal outlets — often through networks of intermediary traders who each handled only one segment of a much longer route.

This last form, sometimes called relay trade, deserves particular attention because it shows how goods could travel enormous distances without any single trader or community controlling the entire route. Gold mined in the forests of West Africa might pass through the hands of several different groups of traders before reaching North African markets; cowrie shells originating in the Indian Ocean might pass through multiple intermediary communities before reaching West Africa, sometimes traveling thousands of kilometers from their point of origin. No single community needed to know the full extent of the network in which it participated for that network to function and to transform societies along its length.

Trade goods themselves reveal a great deal about the scale and reach of these networks. Archaeologists working at Jenné-Jeno found evidence of copper that likely originated from sources in the southern Sahara or beyond, suggesting trade connections extending well over a thousand kilometers even before the classic era of trans-Saharan caravan trade examined in Chapter 20. Glass beads, often originating from distant manufacturing centers in Egypt, the Mediterranean world, or later from Islamic and even Asian sources, appear at archaeological sites across the continent, demonstrating connections that crossed major ecological and cultural boundaries.

Trade did not simply move goods; it also moved ideas, technologies, religious practices, and people. Traders carried not only merchandise but also news, political information, religious beliefs, and cultural practices from one community to another. The spread of Islam along trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean trade routes, examined in Chapter 27, is one of the clearest examples of how commercial networks became channels for much broader processes of cultural and religious change.

Section 11.3

Markets and Specialization

As trade intensified, African communities developed increasingly organized institutions for conducting exchange. Periodic markets, held on a regular schedule — weekly, biweekly, or according to other local calendars — became central institutions of economic and social life across much of the continent. Markets brought together farmers selling agricultural surplus, craftspeople selling manufactured goods, herders selling livestock and animal products, and long-distance traders selling goods obtained from distant regions. They also served important social functions beyond commerce: markets were places to exchange news, arrange marriages, resolve disputes, and maintain social ties across communities that might otherwise have little regular contact.

Craft specialization developed alongside market institutions, as growing population density and trade opportunity made it possible for some individuals to dedicate themselves primarily or entirely to specialized production rather than farming. Ironworkers occupied a particularly significant position in many African societies. Iron production required specialized technical knowledge, access to ore and fuel resources, and often ritual knowledge believed necessary to successfully transform raw ore into usable metal. In many West African societies, blacksmiths formed endogamous social groups — meaning they generally married within their own occupational community — and were often viewed with a combination of respect and wariness, seen as possessing powerful and somewhat dangerous knowledge.

Potters, too, developed specialized traditions, often producing distinctive styles that allow archaeologists today to trace trade connections and cultural influence across different regions and time periods by comparing pottery styles found at different sites. Textile production, leatherworking, woodworking, and the production of beads and ornaments from shell, stone, and metal all developed as specialized crafts in various regions, each requiring particular technical skills passed down through specific families, lineages, or apprenticeship traditions.

This growing occupational diversity had significant social consequences. In societies that had previously organized status primarily through kinship and age, occupation became an increasingly important additional axis of social identity and sometimes social hierarchy. Specialized craftspeople, traders, and religious specialists formed social groups whose status depended not on lineage alone but on skill, wealth, ritual knowledge, or control of valuable resources and connections. This did not eliminate kinship as an organizing principle of African social life — kinship remained centrally important — but it added new and increasingly significant dimensions of social differentiation.

Section 11.4

Political Authority and Urban Life

One of the most important lessons of early African urban history is that cities did not require centralized monarchical authority in order to function, grow, and persist for centuries. As discussed in Section 11.1, Jenné-Jeno appears to have sustained urban life for well over a thousand years without clear evidence of a single dominant ruling institution. This does not mean that Jenné-Jeno had no political organization at all; rather, it suggests that authority may have been distributed across multiple institutions — lineage heads, craft associations, ritual specialists, and perhaps councils of elders or notable families — that cooperated, negotiated, and sometimes competed with one another to manage the collective affairs of the urban community.

This pattern, sometimes described by archaeologists as heterarchy, stands in contrast to the more familiar pattern of centralized, hierarchical political authority that characterized ancient Egypt, examined in Chapter 7, and that would later characterize large African states such as Ghana, Mali, and Songhai, examined in Chapters 20 through 23. Both patterns appear across African history, sometimes within the same region at different periods, and sometimes within the same period in neighboring regions. Understanding this diversity is essential to avoiding the assumption that all complex societies must follow a single model of political organization.

Where centralized authority did develop, several factors appear to have been particularly important. Control of trade — the ability to tax caravans, regulate market access, or monopolize particularly valuable goods such as gold or salt — provided rulers with revenue and leverage that could be converted into political power. Control of specialized production, particularly of iron tools and weapons, gave some rulers military and economic advantages over rivals. Religious legitimacy, examined further in Section 11.6, provided rulers with spiritual authority that complemented and reinforced their political and economic power. And the ability to organize defense against rival communities or external threats gave rulers practical justification for concentrating authority and resources.

The relationship between urbanization and centralized state formation was neither automatic nor uniform. Some cities, like early Jenné-Jeno, sustained themselves for centuries without developing strong centralized rulership. Others, like the early urban centers that would eventually form the core of the Ghana Empire examined in Chapter 20, developed increasingly centralized political authority over time, often in response to the opportunities and pressures created by long-distance trade. Still others combined urban growth with the consolidation of monarchical power from a very early stage, as in ancient Egypt. This variation is itself an important historical lesson: state formation in Africa, as elsewhere, followed multiple distinct pathways shaped by local geography, economy, and social structure.

Section 11.5

Craft Production and Wealth

Craft production was not only a matter of meeting practical needs for tools, containers, and clothing; it was also a major engine of wealth accumulation and social differentiation in early African cities. Iron tools dramatically improved agricultural productivity, allowing farmers to clear land, till soil, and harvest crops more efficiently than with earlier stone or wood implements. Iron weapons provided significant military advantages to communities that possessed them over those that did not. Control of iron production and trade, therefore, could translate directly into economic and political power.

Gold, although primarily associated in many historical accounts with the later Sahelian empires examined in Chapters 20 through 23, was already being mined and traded in West Africa during the period covered in this chapter, moving through regional and long-distance networks well before the classic age of trans-Saharan gold trade. Textile production, particularly the weaving of cotton in regions where it could be cultivated, created valuable manufactured goods that could be traded across considerable distances. Salt, essential for human and animal health and often scarce in regions far from the sea or from inland salt deposits, became one of the most consistently valuable trade goods across the continent, exchanged in some regions for quantities of gold approaching its own weight.

The accumulation of wealth through craft production and trade had significant social consequences. Successful traders and craftspeople could acquire wealth that allowed them to support larger households, sponsor religious ceremonies, contribute to community defense, and in some cases challenge or supplement the authority of hereditary leaders. This created new forms of social mobility — opportunities for individuals and families to improve their social position through trade and craft success rather than through birth alone — even as it also created new forms of inequality between successful traders and craftspeople and those who remained primarily engaged in subsistence agriculture.

Section 11.6

Ritual Centers and Sacred Space

Religion and ritual practice played a central role in the growth of many early African settlements. In numerous regions, permanent settlements first developed not primarily as economic or defensive centers but as sites of religious significance — locations associated with powerful ancestral spirits, important burial grounds, or sites believed to possess particular spiritual potency. Pilgrims, worshippers, and those seeking ritual services at these sites created demand for food, lodging, and craft goods that could, over time, support the growth of permanent resident populations of farmers, craftspeople, and religious specialists.

Religious authority and political authority were often closely intertwined in these emerging urban centers. Religious specialists — priests, diviners, ritual leaders — frequently played important roles in organizing communal labor, resolving disputes, and legitimizing the authority of political leaders. In some societies, political rulers were themselves understood to possess sacred or semi-divine status, a pattern examined in greater depth in Chapter 26's discussion of sacred kingship. In others, religious and political authority remained more clearly distinguished, with religious specialists serving an advisory or legitimizing function alongside, rather than merged with, political leadership.

Sacred sites also served important functions in organizing space within growing urban centers. Shrines, burial grounds, and ceremonial spaces often occupied prominent locations within or near settlements, serving as focal points for community identity and collective ritual practice. The maintenance of these sacred spaces required organized labor and resources, providing yet another mechanism through which growing urban communities developed institutions for coordinating collective action beyond the scale of individual households or lineages.

Section 11.7

Regional Networks and Exchange

Early African cities did not develop in isolation from one another. Across river valleys, savanna regions, and coastal zones, networks of interconnected settlements developed, linked by trade, shared culture, and sometimes political alliance or rivalry. Along the middle Niger River, Jenné-Jeno was part of a larger cluster of settlements, some specializing in particular crafts or agricultural products, others serving primarily as trade and transit points, together forming an integrated regional economy.

These regional networks often displayed a kind of settlement hierarchy, with larger urban centers serving as hubs for trade, craft production, and political or religious authority, surrounded by smaller towns and villages that supplied agricultural goods, raw materials, and labor while also drawing on the larger centers for access to specialized goods, markets, and services unavailable locally. This hierarchical pattern of settlement, in which different communities played complementary economic and social roles within a larger regional system, appears across multiple regions of ancient Africa, from the Niger River valley to the Nile Valley to the East African coast.

Regional networks also connected different ecological and cultural zones to one another, creating economic interdependence across considerable distances. The trade connecting the Sahara desert's salt-producing regions with the Sahel's grain-producing regions and the forest zone's gold- and kola-producing regions, examined further in Chapter 17, exemplifies this pattern: each zone produced goods the others lacked, creating powerful economic incentives for sustained exchange and, eventually, for the development of more formal political and commercial institutions to manage that exchange.

Section 11.8

Urbanization and State Power

The relationship between cities and states is one of the central analytical questions of this chapter. Urbanization — the growth of dense, permanent settlements with specialized, non-agricultural populations — and state formation — the development of centralized political institutions capable of exercising authority over a defined territory and population — are related but distinct historical processes. Cities can exist without strong centralized states, as the example of early Jenné-Jeno demonstrates. States can exercise authority over largely rural populations without necessarily possessing large urban capitals, particularly in early stages of state formation.

Over time, however, cities and states tended to become increasingly intertwined across most regions of Africa. Cities provided concentrated populations of craftspeople, traders, and resources that ambitious political leaders could draw upon to build military and administrative capacity. States, in turn, often provided the security, infrastructure, and economic regulation that allowed cities to grow larger and trade more extensively than they could under conditions of political fragmentation or insecurity. The growth of organized states along the Niger River, in the Lake Chad basin, along the East African coast, and in southern Africa, examined in later chapters of this textbook, built directly on the patterns of urbanization, trade, and craft specialization described in this chapter.

This relationship was not simply one-directional, with cities producing states. In some cases, the establishment of centralized political authority preceded and actively promoted urban growth, as ambitious rulers established new capitals, encouraged the settlement of craftspeople and traders, and used military or diplomatic power to secure and expand access to valuable trade routes. The interaction between urban economic growth and state political consolidation was, in most regions, a mutually reinforcing process rather than a simple one-way sequence.

Section 11.9

Early African Cities in Comparison

Placing early African urbanization in comparative perspective helps clarify both its distinctive features and its connections to broader patterns of human history. Cities first developed in human history in Mesopotamia, beginning around 3500 BCE, in close association with intensive irrigation agriculture, written record-keeping, and centralized temple- and palace-based political authority. The Indus Valley civilization, flourishing from roughly 2600 to 1900 BCE, developed remarkably large and well-planned cities with sophisticated water and sanitation systems, though with less obvious evidence of centralized monarchy than Mesopotamia. Early Chinese urban centers along the Yellow River developed in association with both agricultural surplus and centralized dynastic authority.

Ancient Egypt, examined in Chapter 7, developed urban centers in close connection with one of history's most thoroughly centralized monarchies, fitting more closely the Mesopotamian pattern of city and centralized state developing together. Jenné-Jeno and other early West African urban centers, by contrast, more closely resemble the Indus Valley pattern: substantial, long-lasting urban populations without clear evidence of strong centralized monarchical control, suggesting that complex urban society and centralized political hierarchy, while often associated with one another, are not the same thing and do not necessarily require one another.

This comparison matters historically for a specific reason: for much of the twentieth century, many historians working from primarily European and Middle Eastern evidence assumed that "civilization," urbanization, and centralized statehood were essentially the same package, arriving together or not at all. The archaeological evidence from West Africa, alongside comparable evidence from other world regions, demonstrates that this assumption is mistaken. Complex urban life could and did develop through multiple distinct institutional pathways, and sub-Saharan Africa's early urban history deserves recognition as an independent and significant chapter in the global history of cities, not as a delayed or incomplete imitation of patterns established elsewhere.

Historical Vocabulary
Urbanization

Urbanization refers to the historical process by which population becomes increasingly concentrated in permanent settlements large and dense enough to support significant numbers of people engaged in non-agricultural occupations. Urbanization is usually, though not always, associated with growing economic specialization, social differentiation, and the development of institutions — markets, craft guilds, religious organizations, or political authorities — capable of managing the more complex social relationships that dense settlement requires.

Historians studying early African urbanization must be careful not to assume that urbanization always follows the same pattern observed in better-documented ancient civilizations such as Egypt or Mesopotamia. As the example of Jenné-Jeno demonstrates, African urbanization sometimes proceeded through distributed, multi-institutional forms of authority rather than centralized monarchy.

Historical Vocabulary
Statecraft

Statecraft refers to the practical skills, institutions, and strategies rulers and political authorities use to organize, maintain, and expand their power — including taxation, military organization, diplomacy, administration, and the cultivation of religious or ideological legitimacy.

In early African contexts, statecraft frequently depended on controlling trade routes and valuable resources, organizing craft production, securing religious legitimacy, and managing relationships with surrounding rural populations whose agricultural surplus sustained urban life. The development of statecraft in West Africa, examined further in Chapters 19 through 25, built directly on the urban and economic foundations described in this chapter.

Primary Source Window

Archaeological Evidence from Jenné-Jeno

Creator: Archaeological excavation teams, beginning with Roderick and Susan McIntosh in the 1970s · Date of evidence: Site occupied c. 250 BCE–1400 CE; excavations conducted from the 1970s onward · Type: Archaeological site report and material evidence

Context: Jenné-Jeno was rediscovered by archaeologists working near the modern Malian town of Djenné. Because the site has no surviving written records from its early period, almost everything historians know about it comes from careful excavation of building remains, pottery, tools, animal bones, plant remains, and trade goods recovered from different occupation layers.

Excavation revealed that the settlement grew in concentric phases from a small initial village into a substantial town with defensive walls, specialized craft-production areas, and evidence of iron smelting. Researchers found copper and other materials that must have arrived through long-distance exchange, since no local sources existed nearby, alongside dense layers of fish bone, rice, and other food remains showing a mixed farming and fishing economy.

Analysis: Archaeological evidence of this kind allows historians to reconstruct the economic life, population growth, and trade connections of a city even though it left no written documents of its own. Its limits are equally important: the material record can show that craft specialization and trade existed, but it cannot easily reveal personal names, individual political decisions, religious beliefs, or spoken language, all of which a written or oral source might preserve. Archaeologists also depend on interpretation: the absence of palace architecture is suggestive of decentralized authority but cannot prove it conclusively, since some forms of authority may have left no durable physical trace.

  4. What questions about Jenné-Jeno's political and religious life remain difficult to answer using archaeological evidence alone?
Historical Debate

Did Early West African Cities Require Centralized States to Develop?

The question: For decades, many historians assumed that cities and centralized states necessarily developed together, with strong rulers organizing and sustaining urban growth. Evidence from Jenné-Jeno and other early West African sites has challenged this assumption, but historians continue to debate how to interpret the evidence.

Interpretation A — Cities Required Some Form of Centralized Authority: Some historians argue that even without a single visible royal palace, Jenné-Jeno and similar settlements must have possessed effective centralized coordination — perhaps rotating leadership, council-based authority, or religious institutions functioning as a de facto government — because the scale of urban life, defensive wall-building, and craft specialization observed archaeologically would have been very difficult to sustain through purely informal or fragmented decision-making.
Interpretation B — Cities Could Function Through Distributed, Heterarchical Authority: Other historians and archaeologists, including many who excavated Jenné-Jeno directly, argue that the evidence supports a genuinely decentralized or "heterarchical" model, in which multiple institutions — lineage councils, craft associations, ritual specialists — shared authority without any single dominant center of power, and that assuming centralized authority simply because it existed elsewhere risks imposing external models on African evidence that does not require them.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on comparative evidence from other ancient urban civilizations and on the practical challenges of organizing defensive walls and large-scale craft production. Interpretation B draws on the absence of palace architecture, the absence of evidence for a single dominant elite residence, and ethnographic evidence of decentralized, council-based governance among some later West African societies.

Why it matters: This debate affects how historians understand the diversity of paths to complex society across world history, and whether centralized monarchy should be treated as a necessary stage of political development or as one option among several.

Student task: Based on the evidence summarized here, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and what additional evidence might help historians resolve this debate more conclusively?

Multiple Perspectives

Living in an Early African City

From the perspective of a rural farmer supplying an urban center: Urban growth meant new markets for agricultural surplus and access to manufactured goods such as iron tools and pottery unavailable through purely local production, but it could also mean new pressures to produce surplus consistently and new forms of dependence on distant trade and political relationships beyond the farmer's own community.
From the perspective of a specialized craftsperson, such as a blacksmith: Urban and trade growth created valuable new opportunities for specialized livelihood and sometimes considerable wealth and ritual status, but specialized craftspeople in many societies also occupied an ambiguous social position, valued for their skill yet sometimes viewed with suspicion or held at social distance because of the perceived danger of their ritual and technical knowledge.
From the perspective of a long-distance trader: Growing trade networks offered the possibility of significant wealth and influence, sometimes rivaling that of hereditary leaders, but also required navigating relationships with multiple communities, languages, and political authorities along extended trade routes, with considerable risk of loss, theft, or political disruption.
From the perspective of an emerging political or religious leader: Urbanization and trade growth offered new resources and new populations to organize and govern, but also created new rivals — wealthy traders, craft associations, and lineage leaders — whose independent sources of wealth and status could limit or challenge centralized political ambition.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Early African Cities

Long-Term Causes

Spread of agriculture and pastoralism; population growth across favorable river valleys and savanna regions; spread of ironworking technology; gradual development of regional and long-distance exchange networks over many centuries.

Short-Term Causes

Establishment of specific settlements at strategically advantageous locations along rivers or trade routes; local innovations in craft production; the arrival of new trade goods or trading partners; favorable environmental conditions supporting agricultural surplus.

The Event or Transformation

Growth of permanent, dense settlements with specialized craft production, organized markets, and (in many but not all cases) increasingly centralized political authority.

Effects

Foundations laid for later states and empires; growing social differentiation between farmers, craftspeople, traders, and elites; expanded regional and long-distance trade networks; new institutions of governance, religion, and economic organization.

Historical complexity: No single cause explains the rise of cities across Africa's many regions. Geography, agricultural surplus, trade opportunity, technological change, and social innovation combined differently in each region, producing distinct local pathways to urban life.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Urban and Rural Life in Early Africa

What Changed

  • Growth of permanent, dense urban settlements supporting non-agricultural specialists
  • Development of organized markets and periodic trading institutions
  • Emergence of occupational specialization in ironworking, pottery, and textiles
  • Development, in many regions, of more centralized and hierarchical forms of political authority
  • Expansion of regional and long-distance trade networks linking distant ecological zones

What Stayed the Same

  • The majority of the population continued to live in rural farming and herding communities
  • Kinship remained a central organizing principle of identity and obligation, even within growing cities
  • Religious belief and ritual practice continued to shape political legitimacy and community life
  • Agricultural and pastoral production remained the economic foundation supporting urban growth

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity prevents an overly simple narrative in which African societies moved in a single, uniform direction from "village" to "city" or from "kinship" to "state." Instead, urban and rural life, and kinship-based and specialized social organization, coexisted and interacted throughout this period and well beyond it.

Everyday Life and Social History

Daily Life in an Early African Trading Town

For most residents of an early African city such as Jenné-Jeno, daily life centered on a combination of subsistence activity and specialized production. Many households likely combined farming or fishing with some form of craft work or trade participation, rather than specializing exclusively in one occupation. Women played essential roles in agricultural labor, food processing, pottery production in many regions, and market trade, often controlling the sale of agricultural surplus and craft goods even where men dominated certain other forms of production, such as ironworking.

Housing in early urban centers was typically constructed of mud brick or similar local materials, organized into dense clusters of dwellings reflecting kinship and occupational groupings. Diet combined cultivated grains such as rice and sorghum with fish, in regions near rivers, and with meat and dairy products from domesticated livestock. Children would have been integrated into household labor from a young age, learning agricultural, craft, or trading skills through participation alongside older family members rather than through formal schooling.

Religious and ceremonial life punctuated the agricultural and commercial calendar, with rituals tied to planting and harvest seasons, ancestral commemoration, and the resolution of community disputes. For most ordinary residents, the growth of trade and craft specialization examined in this chapter would have appeared not as an abstract historical process but as new goods available in the market, new neighbors arriving from distant regions, and gradually shifting opportunities and obligations within an evolving but still recognizably kin-centered community.

The central insights of this chapter are: cities developed independently across multiple regions of ancient Africa, often centuries before substantial outside contact. Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most important yet historically underappreciated developments in African history: the independent rise of cities, organized markets, and early states across multiple regions of the continent, long before the great Sahelian and coastal empires examined in later chapters of this textbook. Cities such as Jenné-Jeno on the middle Niger River demonstrate that complex, dense, economically specialized urban life could develop and persist for over a thousand years without necessarily requiring the centralized monarchical authority that characterized other ancient civilizations such as Egypt and Mesopotamia.

Trade emerged as one of the most powerful forces driving these transformations. Local, regional, and long-distance exchange networks connected communities producing different goods across considerable distances, often through relay systems in which no single trader or community needed to control an entire route. Goods such as copper, gold, salt, glass beads, and cowrie shells moved across hundreds or even thousands of kilometers, demonstrating the reach and sophistication of African trade networks well before sustained contact with the Islamic or European worlds.

Craft specialization developed alongside trade, as growing populations and trade opportunities allowed some community members to dedicate themselves to specialized production of iron tools, pottery, textiles, and other manufactured goods. Ironworking in particular carried significant economic, military, and ritual importance across many African societies, often investing blacksmiths with distinctive social status that combined respect with social distance.

Political authority in early African cities took multiple forms. In some cases, as at Jenné-Jeno, authority appears to have been distributed across multiple institutions — lineage groups, craft associations, religious specialists — without a single centralized ruling office. In other cases, particularly in the Nile Valley, centralized monarchical authority developed in close connection with urban growth from a very early period. Over time, across many regions, cities and states became increasingly intertwined, as urban populations and resources became foundations for the more centralized political structures examined in later chapters.

Religious and ceremonial life played a foundational role in many early urban developments, as sacred sites attracted permanent populations of worshippers, ritual specialists, and the craftspeople and farmers who supported them. Religious authority frequently reinforced, legitimized, or combined with emerging political authority, a pattern that would continue to shape African statecraft for centuries.

Regional networks linked early cities to one another, often creating settlement hierarchies in which larger urban centers served as hubs for trade, craft production, and political or religious authority, surrounded by smaller towns and villages performing complementary economic functions. These networks connected distinct ecological zones — desert, Sahel, savanna, forest, river valley, and coast — creating economic interdependence that drove sustained exchange across considerable distances.

Placed in comparative global perspective, early African urbanization both resembles and differs from urban developments elsewhere in the ancient world. Like Mesopotamia and ancient Egypt, some African regions developed cities in close connection with centralized monarchy. Like the Indus Valley civilization, other African regions, particularly in West Africa, developed substantial and long-lasting urban populations without clear evidence of centralized rulership, demonstrating that complex urban society does not require a single institutional model.

For ordinary Africans living through this long process, the changes examined in this chapter were experienced unevenly. Farmers who supplied agricultural surplus to growing cities gained access to new markets and manufactured goods but also faced new pressures and dependencies. Craftspeople gained new opportunities for specialized livelihood, sometimes accompanied by considerable wealth and status, but also navigated ambiguous social positions shaped by the perceived danger or power of specialized technical knowledge. Traders could accumulate wealth and influence rivaling that of hereditary leaders, while emerging political and religious leaders found new resources to organize but also new rivals to manage.

This chapter's most important lesson may be methodological as much as factual: historians and archaeologists have learned to recognize urban and political complexity in forms that do not match the models long assumed to be universal based primarily on the history of Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Europe. The decentralized, heterarchical urban community of early Jenné-Jeno is as historically significant, and as worthy of careful study, as the centralized monarchies of the Nile Valley — a reminder that African history, like world history more broadly, followed multiple legitimate and successful pathways toward complex society.

The cities, trade networks, and early political institutions examined in this chapter formed the essential foundation for the developments explored throughout the remainder of this textbook: the rise of the great Sahelian empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai; the growth of the Swahili coastal city-states; the development of forest kingdoms and Hausa city-states; and the emergence of southern African states such as Mapungubwe and Great Zimbabwe. None of these later developments can be fully understood without first understanding the deep historical roots of African urban and commercial life examined here.

Key Terms

Urbanization
The historical process by which population becomes concentrated in permanent, dense settlements supporting non-agricultural specialists.
City-state
An independent or semi-independent political unit centered on a single dominant city and its surrounding hinterland.
Surplus
Agricultural or other production beyond what is needed for immediate subsistence, enabling trade, specialization, and population growth.
Specialization
The development of distinct, often skilled occupations such as ironworking, pottery, or trading, performed by individuals who do not produce their own food.
Long-distance trade
Exchange of goods across great distances, often crossing major ecological or cultural boundaries.
Relay trade
A trading system in which goods pass through multiple intermediary traders or communities, none of which controls the entire route.
Market
An organized, often periodic institution where goods are exchanged, serving economic and social functions within a community.
Craft production
The specialized manufacture of goods such as tools, pottery, textiles, or ornaments by skilled, often non-farming, producers.
Statecraft
The practical institutions and strategies used by political authorities to organize, maintain, and expand power.
Centralized authority
Political power concentrated in a single ruling office, dynasty, or institution.
Decentralized authority
Political power distributed across multiple institutions, groups, or offices rather than a single center.
Heterarchy
A form of social or political organization in which multiple institutions hold power simultaneously without a single dominant hierarchy.
Ritual center
A site whose importance derives primarily from religious or ceremonial significance, often becoming a focus for permanent settlement.
Sacred space
Locations understood by a community to hold religious or spiritual significance, often shaping settlement layout and ritual practice.
Regional network
A system of interconnected settlements and trade relationships operating within a particular geographic or cultural area.
Caravan route
An overland trade path traveled by groups of merchants and animals carrying goods across long distances, especially across deserts.
Agricultural surplus
Food production exceeding the immediate needs of producers, enabling trade and the support of non-farming populations.
Occupational diversity
The presence of multiple distinct specialized livelihoods within a single community.
Currency
A standardized medium of exchange, such as cowrie shells in parts of Africa, used to facilitate trade.
Tribute
Goods, labor, or resources rendered by subordinate communities or individuals to a ruling authority.
Hinterland
The rural area surrounding a city or trading center that supplies it with agricultural goods and raw materials.
Settlement hierarchy
A pattern in which settlements of different sizes perform different, often complementary, economic and social functions within a region.
Archaeology
The study of past human societies through excavation and analysis of physical remains, essential for studying societies without extensive written records.
Oral tradition
Historical and cultural information transmitted across generations through spoken memory, narrative, and performance.
Endogamy
The social practice of marrying within one's own group, observed among some specialized craft communities such as certain blacksmith lineages.
Trans-Saharan trade
Commerce conducted across the Sahara Desert linking West Africa to North Africa and the Mediterranean world.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it corrects a long-standing distortion in how African history has often been told: the assumption that complex urban life, organized trade, and political authority arrived in sub-Saharan Africa only through outside contact. The evidence from Jenné-Jeno and other early urban centers demonstrates decisively that Africans developed cities, markets, and political institutions through their own internal processes, shaped by local geography, agricultural and craft economies, and social innovation.

The developments examined in this chapter shaped later African history in direct and traceable ways. The trade networks, craft traditions, and urban settlement patterns established during this period became the foundations on which the great Sahelian empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai were built, as were the Swahili coastal city-states, the forest kingdoms of West Africa, and the early states of southern Africa. Without the deep history of urbanization and trade examined here, the rise of these later, better-documented African civilizations would be impossible to fully understand.

This chapter also reveals a larger theme that runs throughout this textbook: African history followed multiple legitimate pathways toward complexity, wealth, and political organization, rather than a single universal model imported or imposed from elsewhere. The decentralized, multi-institutional urban life of early Jenné-Jeno stands alongside the centralized monarchy of ancient Egypt as two equally significant, equally African, models of historical development.

Finally, this chapter reminds students that history is reconstructed through evidence and careful interpretation, not simply inherited as settled fact. The story of Jenné-Jeno was unknown to historians until careful archaeological excavation revealed it, and debates about how to interpret that evidence continue today. Understanding early African cities requires the same skills of evidence evaluation, comparison, and argument that this textbook emphasizes throughout: skills essential not only for understanding Africa's deep past, but for understanding how historians come to know anything about the past at all.

Women, Family, and Gender in African History

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"To understand African history without understanding the lives of women is to understand only half of the continent's past — and often the less revealing half."

In the early eighteenth century, a woman named Njinga Mbande ruled the kingdoms of Ndongo and Matamba in what is today Angola. She led armies, negotiated with Portuguese colonial agents and Dutch traders, converted to Christianity and abandoned it according to political need, built alliances with neighboring states, and held power for decades in a world increasingly reshaped by the Atlantic slave trade. Njinga was not an anomaly produced by some unusual personal genius operating against the grain of her society. She emerged from political systems and cultural traditions across Central Africa in which female rulers, queen mothers, priestesses, and female military leaders were a recognized, if not universal, feature of political life. To tell her story without understanding the broader history of women's authority in African societies is to mistake an exception for the rule, when in fact she represents one visible expression of patterns that were widespread, if frequently overlooked by colonial-era European observers and by generations of historians who followed their assumptions.

This chapter asks students to set aside a common but mistaken assumption: that African history can be told adequately through the actions of kings, warriors, traders, and colonial administrators — categories of historical actor that, in the European historical record at least, were overwhelmingly male — while women remain in the background as wives, mothers, and laborers whose experiences mattered only to the extent that they supported men's public actions. This assumption is wrong both empirically and analytically. It is wrong empirically because women across African societies held political office, controlled trade networks, organized agricultural production, led religious institutions, shaped legal systems, and exercised authority that, in some times and places, rivaled or exceeded that of men. It is wrong analytically because even where women did not hold formal political power, their labor, their kinship roles, and their social authority were foundational to the functioning of every African society examined elsewhere in this textbook — and no chapter on any kingdom, trade network, or religious tradition can be fully understood without attention to the gendered systems that organized labor, inheritance, marriage, and community life beneath and around the formal political institutions more commonly described.

At the same time, this chapter resists a second mistaken assumption: that African societies before colonial rule existed in some uniform condition of "traditional" gender relations, either uniformly oppressive toward women or uniformly egalitarian, that colonialism then disrupted, destroyed, or imposed from outside. The historical reality was far more varied and far more dynamic. Gender systems across the African continent differed enormously by region, by ethnicity, by religion, by economic system, and by historical period. Matrilineal societies in which descent, inheritance, and political succession passed through the female line existed alongside patrilineal societies organized very differently. Societies with parallel "dual-sex" political systems, in which women held their own councils, courts, and offices independent of and parallel to men's institutions, existed alongside societies in which political office was restricted to men. Islamic legal traditions shaped gender relations differently across the Sahel, the Swahili Coast, and North Africa than indigenous religious systems did elsewhere. These differences were not static; they changed over centuries in response to migration, trade, warfare, religious conversion, and — from the nineteenth century onward — the disruptive force of European colonial rule.

What did change, with considerable consistency across many regions, was the impact of colonial rule on women's formal political and economic authority. European colonial administrators, operating from their own deeply gendered assumptions about who could hold legitimate political and economic power, systematically failed to recognize, and often deliberately dismantled, the institutions through which African women had exercised authority. Colonial tax systems, land registration systems, and chiefly hierarchies were constructed around male household heads, even in societies where women had previously held independent rights to land, trade, and political voice. This is one of the central arguments developed across this chapter: that the relationship between African women and political and economic power was not a simple story of universal subordination gradually improved by external intervention, but a more complex story of substantial pre-colonial authority that colonial rule often eroded, followed by twentieth-century movements of resistance, adaptation, and renewed activism through which African women fought to reclaim and redefine their place in rapidly changing societies.

This chapter also insists that "women's history" is not a separate, supplementary track running alongside the "real" history of African states, economies, and religions. Family structure, kinship systems, marriage practices, and gender roles were not peripheral details; they were the basic organizing logic of African societies, shaping who inherited land, who controlled labor, who could marry whom, how wealth moved between generations, how political succession occurred, and how religious authority was structured and transmitted. Students who understand the gendered foundations of family and kinship in African societies will be far better equipped to understand the kingdoms, trade networks, religious movements, and colonial encounters examined throughout the rest of this textbook.

Finally, this chapter asks students to attend carefully to the evidence problem that runs throughout the study of women in African history. Much of the written documentation available to historians — from Arabic chronicles to European travelers' accounts to colonial administrative records — was produced by men, often outsiders, who were frequently uninterested in or actively dismissive of women's roles, and who sometimes misunderstood what they observed. Where written sources are thin or biased, historians have turned to oral tradition, archaeology, linguistic evidence, material culture, and the methods of social and economic history to reconstruct women's experiences with greater accuracy. This chapter models that historical method throughout: reading available evidence critically, asking what it reveals and what it conceals, and triangulating across multiple types of evidence to build a fuller and more accurate picture of the past.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the range of roles women occupied in African societies, including agricultural, economic, political, religious, and military roles.
  2. Compare matrilineal, patrilineal, and dual-sex systems of kinship and political organization across different African societies.
  3. Describe the gendered division of labor in farming, trade, craft production, and household management, and explain regional variation.
  4. Analyze the institutions of marriage, bridewealth, and polygyny and their economic and social functions within African kinship systems.
  5. Identify examples of women's economic authority, including control of markets and long-distance trade.
  6. Evaluate the historical role of female political and military leaders, including queens, queen mothers, and warrior-rulers.
  7. Explain how religious systems — indigenous, Islamic, and Christian — shaped and were shaped by gender roles and female religious authority.
  8. Assess how colonial rule changed, and frequently diminished, women's pre-colonial political and economic power.
  9. Distinguish between continuity and change in gender roles from the pre-colonial period through the colonial and independence eras.
  10. Interpret primary and secondary sources critically, accounting for gender bias in historical documentation.
  11. Connect historical gender systems to ongoing debates about women's rights, land tenure, and political representation in contemporary Africa.
  12. Contextualize African gender history within broader patterns of comparative world history.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. How did kinship systems — matrilineal, patrilineal, and mixed — shape inheritance, political succession, and social belonging?
  2. What economic roles did women perform, and how did control over agricultural labor, trade, and craft production translate into social and political authority?
  3. What forms of formal political and religious power did women hold, and how widespread were these institutions across the continent?
  4. How did marriage practices such as bridewealth and polygyny function economically and socially, and how did they affect women's status differently depending on context?
  5. In what ways did colonial rule transform — and frequently undermine — women's pre-colonial authority?
  6. How did African women resist, adapt to, and reshape colonial economic and political systems?
  7. Why is the documentary evidence about African women's history often thin, biased, or filtered through male and outsider perspectives, and how do historians work around this problem?
  8. What continued and what changed in gender roles across the transition from pre-colonial to colonial to independence-era Africa?
  9. How did gender systems differ across regions shaped by different religious traditions, environments, and political structures?

Historical Context

To understand gender in African history, students must first set aside assumptions drawn from other historical contexts, including the assumption — common in much earlier European and American historical writing — that women's subordination to men was a universal and largely unchanging feature of all pre-modern societies. African societies before, during, and after the era of European colonization displayed an enormous range of gender systems, shaped by environment, economy, religion, language, and historical experience. Some societies, particularly in parts of West Africa, Central Africa, and among certain Bantu-speaking peoples of the southeast, organized descent and inheritance through the female line — a matrilineal system in which a person's lineage, clan membership, and sometimes political office passed through the mother rather than the father. Other societies, including many in the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, and parts of Southern Africa, organized descent patrilineally, through the father's line. Many societies combined elements of both systems or organized different aspects of social life — residence, inheritance, ritual obligation — through different lines of descent simultaneously.

The economic foundations of most African societies before the large-scale introduction of plow agriculture and mechanized farming relied heavily on female agricultural labor. In much of sub-Saharan Africa, women performed the majority of farming labor — planting, weeding, harvesting, and processing food crops — while men were more often responsible for clearing land, herding livestock, hunting, warfare, and long-distance trade, though this division varied considerably by region and crop. This was not a marginal economic role: in societies where women controlled the labor and often the proceeds of agricultural production, women frequently held independent economic resources, controlled their own income from surplus crops sold at market, and built economic networks that gave them social influence independent of their husbands or fathers.

Trade was another major domain of female economic activity, particularly local and regional market trade. Across West Africa especially, women dominated local marketplaces, controlling the buying and selling of foodstuffs, cloth, pottery, and other goods. In some societies, women organized themselves into trade associations, market women's councils, and credit networks that functioned as parallel economic and political institutions, capable of setting prices, regulating market behavior, and even organizing collective political action when necessary.

Religious systems across the continent also varied considerably in the roles available to women. In many indigenous African religious traditions, women served as priestesses, diviners, mediums, and ritual specialists, sometimes holding religious authority equal to or exceeding that of male religious figures, particularly in cults associated with fertility, healing, water, and ancestral communication. The spread of Islam from the eighth century onward introduced new legal and social frameworks that, depending on local interpretation and pre-existing custom, sometimes restricted women's public roles and sometimes left considerable space for female religious learning, property ownership, and trade — particularly in matters governed by Islamic inheritance law, which guaranteed women specific shares of inheritance that some customary systems did not. Christianity, introduced earliest in Egypt, Nubia, and Ethiopia and later through European missionary activity in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, brought yet another set of gendered assumptions, often reflecting contemporary European Victorian ideals about female domesticity that diverged sharply from many existing African gender norms.

Political power, too, took many forms. Some African political systems, particularly in parts of West Africa among the Akan, Yoruba, and others, developed parallel or "dual-sex" political structures in which women held their own offices, councils, and courts that operated alongside — not subordinate to — men's political institutions. The Akan office of the Queen Mother, the Yoruba Iyalode, and similar offices elsewhere gave women independent authority over matters concerning women specifically, as well as broader influence over communal political decisions. Elsewhere, women held the highest political office directly: as in the cases of Queen Amina of Zazzau in the Hausa lands, Njinga of Ndongo and Matamba in Central Africa, the Lovedu rain queens of Southern Africa, and the Kandakes of ancient Kush discussed in earlier chapters of this textbook.

This diversity of gender systems was not static. Migration, warfare, religious change, trade expansion, and — most disruptively — the imposition of European colonial rule from the late nineteenth century onward altered gender systems across the continent, often in ways that diminished women's formal authority even as women found new strategies of economic and social adaptation. Understanding this long arc — from diverse pre-colonial systems, through colonial disruption, to twentieth-century activism and contemporary debate — is the central task of this chapter.

Section 18.1

Women in African Societies

African societies before colonial rule organized the lives of women through an extraordinary range of institutions, expectations, and opportunities, shaped by region, religion, economy, and historical period. No single generalization captures the experience of women across a continent as vast and diverse as Africa. A woman in a matrilineal farming community in the forest zones of present-day Ghana experienced gendered expectations, economic opportunities, and political possibilities very different from those of a woman in a pastoral Somali clan, a Swahili trading family on the Indian Ocean coast, or a noblewoman in the Christian kingdom of Ethiopia. What unites these varied experiences as a subject of historical study is not a common condition but a common set of analytical questions: what labor did women perform, what resources did they control, what authority could they exercise, and how did the answers to these questions change over time?

Historians studying women in African societies face a distinctive evidentiary challenge. Much of the written record available for pre-colonial and early colonial Africa was produced by outsiders — Arab geographers, European travelers, missionaries, and colonial administrators — who often brought their own assumptions about appropriate female behavior and frequently failed to ask the questions that would reveal women's actual economic and political roles. A European trader visiting a West African coastal town in the eighteenth century might record detailed observations about male political officials and merchants while barely mentioning the women who controlled the local marketplace through which much of that trade actually passed. Oral tradition, archaeology, linguistic evidence, and the surviving testimony of African women themselves, where available, are therefore essential complements to written sources dominated by male and outsider perspectives.

What this combined evidence reveals is that women across many African societies held substantial, even if frequently informal or under-documented, economic and social power. Their roles as primary agricultural producers, as controllers of local trade, as keepers of specialized knowledge in areas such as herbal medicine, midwifery, pottery, textile production, and food preservation, and as central figures in kinship networks that organized marriage, inheritance, and social obligation, gave many African women a degree of practical authority that often exceeded what a casual outside observer — accustomed to looking for power only in formal political titles held by men — would have recognized.

At the same time, this chapter does not claim that pre-colonial African societies were uniformly egalitarian or that women's status was always high. Patriarchal structures existed in many societies, sometimes severely restricting women's autonomy, particularly regarding marriage choice, inheritance of land and livestock, and access to formal political office. Slavery within African societies, discussed at length in Chapter 40, disproportionately affected enslaved women, who faced both labor exploitation and sexual exploitation in ways that differed from the experience of enslaved men. The historical task is not to romanticize the pre-colonial past but to understand its actual complexity — and to recognize that this complexity, in most regions, included far more female economic and political agency than colonial-era European observers acknowledged or than some later popular narratives, in either direction, have allowed.

Section 18.2

Family Structure and Kinship

Kinship — the system of relationships defined by descent, marriage, and social obligation — formed the basic organizing structure of nearly every African society before the widespread introduction of European-style nuclear family models and individual land titling. Kinship determined who could inherit land and property, who owed labor or tribute to whom, who could marry whom, who had rights to call upon collective support in times of need, and, in many societies, who could hold political and religious office.

African kinship systems are conventionally classified by historians and anthropologists according to the line through which descent is reckoned. In patrilineal systems, found widely among Sahelian, Nilotic, and many Southern African pastoral and agricultural societies, descent, lineage membership, and inheritance pass through the father's line; a child belongs to the father's lineage, and property and political office typically pass from father to son or among male relatives. In matrilineal systems, found among many Akan-speaking peoples of West Africa, several Bantu-speaking peoples of Central Africa, and other groups, descent and lineage membership pass through the mother's line; a child belongs to the mother's lineage, and property, social identity, and sometimes political office pass through maternal relatives — meaning, notably, that a man's heir was often not his own son but his sister's son, since that nephew belonged to the same matrilineage as the man himself.

Matrilineal systems should not be confused with matriarchy — rule by women. In most matrilineal societies, men still held the formal political offices and exercised considerable authority, but that authority was exercised on behalf of the matrilineage, and women — particularly senior women such as a chief's sisters or maternal aunts — often held substantial informal and sometimes formal influence over succession, marriage arrangements, and lineage property because of their structural position as the conduits of descent. The distinction between matrilineal descent and male political dominance illustrates an important historical lesson: kinship structure and political power did not always align in simple, predictable ways, and historians must examine each society's actual practice rather than assuming that a particular descent system automatically produced a particular distribution of power between men and women.

Many African societies also organized residence and household life through extended, multi-generational kin groups rather than the nuclear family of parents and children common in much of contemporary Western society. Compound households often included a senior man or woman, multiple wives in polygynous households, married sons or daughters and their spouses and children, and sometimes dependents, clients, or enslaved persons attached to the household, all organized around shared labor obligations, collective resource management, and mutual social support. This extended kinship structure provided economic security, child-rearing support, and social identity in ways that shaped daily life far more directly than any distant political authority.

Historical Vocabulary
Matrilineage

Matrilineage refers to a system of descent in which lineage membership, inheritance, and sometimes political succession pass through the mother's line rather than the father's. In a matrilineal system, a person's primary kin group is defined by connection to the mother's relatives, not the father's.

Matrilineal systems were historically significant across parts of West and Central Africa, including among many Akan-speaking peoples and several Bantu-speaking groups of the Congo Basin and Zambezi region. Because political and property succession often passed to a man's sister's son rather than his own son, matrilineal systems gave senior women — as mothers, sisters, and aunts within the lineage — structurally important roles in determining succession, even when they did not themselves hold the highest political offices. Students should be careful to distinguish matrilineal descent (a kinship structure) from matriarchy (rule by women), which was considerably rarer.

Section 18.3

Gender Roles and Labor

Across most of sub-Saharan Africa before the widespread mechanization of agriculture, the division of labor between men and women followed broadly recognizable, though not universal, patterns. Women typically performed the bulk of food-crop cultivation — planting, weeding, harvesting — as well as food processing, water and firewood collection, child care, and household maintenance. Men more often cleared new land for farming, herded cattle and other livestock, hunted, engaged in long-distance trade and warfare, and produced certain craft goods such as ironwork. These patterns varied significantly: in cattle-keeping societies of Eastern and Southern Africa, men's role in livestock management could dominate the household economy in ways that gave men greater control over the primary form of wealth, while in many forest-zone farming societies of West Africa, women's central role in food production gave them correspondingly greater economic leverage.

This gendered division of labor was not simply a matter of cultural preference; it had real economic consequences for the distribution of power and resources within households and communities. In societies where women's agricultural labor produced the primary subsistence crop and women retained significant control over the resulting harvest and any market surplus, women often possessed meaningful independent economic resources — resources that could be converted into social influence, used to support kin, or invested in trade. In societies where the principal form of wealth was livestock controlled by men, women's economic leverage was correspondingly more constrained, though women still typically controlled dairy processing and household food allocation, which gave them their own sphere of practical authority.

Craft production also followed gendered patterns that varied regionally. Pottery-making was frequently a female specialization across much of the continent, tied to its association with food preparation and household management, while ironworking — discussed in Chapter 16 — was very widely restricted to men and surrounded by ritual taboos that frequently excluded women from smelting sites altogether, reflecting widespread beliefs linking ironworking to male fertility and potency. Textile weaving showed more regional variation: in some West African societies, weaving on horizontal looms was a male craft organized into guild-like associations, while in other regions, including parts of Central and East Africa, spinning and weaving were predominantly female activities.

It is important for students to understand that this division of labor by gender did not necessarily imply a hierarchy in which men's labor was valued more highly than women's. In many African societies, female agricultural and market labor was recognized as economically essential, and the social status accorded to a skilled, productive, and economically successful woman could be substantial. The gendered division of labor is best understood not simply as evidence of inequality but as a complex system of economic specialization whose implications for relative power depended heavily on the broader political, religious, and economic context of each specific society.

Section 18.4

Marriage, Household, and Community

Marriage across African societies functioned as far more than a personal or romantic arrangement between two individuals; it was a central economic and social institution that linked lineages, transferred wealth and labor rights, and organized the reproduction of the next generation. Understanding African marriage practices requires understanding two institutions in particular: bridewealth and polygyny.

Bridewealth — sometimes called "bride price," though historians generally prefer the more accurate term bridewealth — refers to payments, often in livestock, goods, labor, or later money, transferred from a groom's family to a bride's family at marriage. Bridewealth should not be misunderstood as the simple "purchase" of a wife, a misinterpretation common among early European observers applying their own commercial framework to an unfamiliar institution. Rather, bridewealth functioned as compensation to the bride's lineage for the loss of her labor and reproductive capacity, as a mechanism establishing the legitimacy of children born of the marriage and their rights within the husband's lineage, and as a form of social insurance: if a husband mistreated his wife, her family could, in many systems, demand her return along with the right to dissolve the marriage, while disputes over the return of bridewealth provided an important check on husbands' behavior.

Polygyny — marriage in which a man has more than one wife simultaneously — was widely practiced, though far from universal, across many African societies, particularly among economically successful men whose status was in part measured by the number of wives, children, and dependents in his household. Polygynous households often functioned as significant economic units, with multiple wives organizing separate spheres of agricultural or trade activity, sometimes in competition and sometimes in cooperation with one another, and senior wives often held authority over junior wives' households. Polygyny served various social functions: it distributed female agricultural labor across a larger household, it provided a mechanism for forming political alliances between lineages through multiple marriages, and it offered social security for widows, who in many systems would be inherited as wives by a deceased husband's brother — a practice known as levirate marriage — rather than being left without economic and social support.

Students should approach both institutions with historical care, avoiding both uncritical celebration and uncritical condemnation. Bridewealth and polygyny operated within specific economic and social logics that made sense within their historical context, and they coexisted with real constraints on women's autonomy, including, in many systems, limited ability to choose a spouse freely, restrictions on divorce, and economic dependence on male relatives or husbands for access to land. The historically accurate position is neither that these institutions were simply oppressive nor that they were simply benign, but that their effects on individual women's lives varied considerably depending on specific circumstances, including a woman's economic resources, the relative status of her natal and marital lineages, and the particular dynamics of her household.

Historical Vocabulary
Bridewealth

Bridewealth refers to a transfer of wealth — historically often cattle, goods, or labor, and later increasingly money — from a groom's family to a bride's family at the time of marriage, common across many African kinship systems. It functioned as compensation to the bride's lineage, as a mechanism legitimizing children's lineage membership, and as a social and economic bond between the two families involved.

Historians caution against the common historical misreading of bridewealth as equivalent to the "purchase" of a wife, an interpretation that reflects European commercial assumptions rather than the actual social logic of the institution within African kinship systems, where it operated as a complex mechanism of social, reproductive, and economic exchange between lineages rather than a simple market transaction.

Section 18.5

Women in Agriculture and Trade

Across much of sub-Saharan Africa, women's role as the principal cultivators of food crops gave them a structurally important position within local and regional economies that extended into commerce. In West Africa especially, women dominated local and regional marketplaces, controlling the retail trade in foodstuffs, cloth, pottery, soap, and prepared foods, while frequently also participating in longer-distance trade in goods such as kola nuts, salt, and textiles. Coastal trading cities in present-day Ghana, Nigeria, and Benin developed substantial female-dominated market economies in which women not only traded but organized formal associations that regulated commerce, mediated disputes among traders, set standards for quality and pricing, and represented market interests to political authorities.

These market women's associations functioned as genuine economic and political institutions. Leaders of major associations, sometimes holding titles roughly equivalent to "market queen," could exercise considerable influence over local economic policy, organize boycotts or strikes in response to unfavorable taxation or regulation, and serve as intermediaries between local political authorities and the broader trading community. In some West African coastal societies, the economic power accumulated by successful female traders translated directly into political influence, with wealthy market women becoming influential advisors to chiefs or holding formal political office themselves.

The expansion of Atlantic and trans-Saharan commerce from the medieval period onward, discussed extensively in earlier chapters of this textbook, created further opportunities — and risks — for women engaged in trade. Some women became prominent intermediaries in long-distance commercial networks linking interior production zones to coastal or trans-Saharan trading points, accumulating substantial wealth and social influence as a result. The Atlantic slave trade, however, also exposed women disproportionately to particular forms of exploitation, since enslaved women were frequently retained for domestic, agricultural, and reproductive labor within Africa even as enslaved men were more often sold into the Atlantic trade, a pattern explored further in Chapter 40 and Chapter 41.

The colonial era, examined in greater depth in section 18.8 below, generally disrupted these female-dominated trade networks. Colonial administrations frequently favored male-controlled cash-crop production for export, introduced commercial regulations and licensing requirements that favored male traders with access to colonial administrative connections, and built new transportation and marketing infrastructure oriented toward export commodities rather than the local foodstuff trade that had been the foundation of women's economic power. Despite this disruption, women's market trade proved remarkably resilient, and in many African cities today, women continue to dominate local marketplaces in patterns that reflect centuries of historical continuity even amid profound economic transformation.

Section 18.6

Female Power and Leadership

Formal female political authority appeared across African history in several distinct institutional forms, each worth understanding in its own right rather than treating as a single undifferentiated category of "women in power."

The first form was direct female rulership: women who held the highest political office in a state, exercising authority comparable to that of male kings or emperors. Earlier chapters of this textbook have examined the Kandakes — queens and queen mothers — who ruled the kingdom of Kush at Meroë for centuries, commanding armies and negotiating with Rome as equals. In the sixteenth century, Queen Amina of Zazzau is remembered in Hausa oral tradition for leading military campaigns that expanded her state's territory and secured control of trade routes. In the seventeenth century, Njinga Mbande ruled the kingdoms of Ndongo and Matamba in Central Africa for nearly four decades, leading armies, negotiating treaties, manipulating religious conversion for political advantage, and resisting Portuguese colonial expansion with remarkable tactical skill — discussed further below in the Historical Debate feature.

The second form was sacred or ritual female kingship, exemplified by the Lovedu rain queens of Southern Africa, known by the title Modjadji, a hereditary line of female rulers believed to possess the spiritual power to control rainfall — an authority of enormous practical importance in an agricultural society dependent on seasonal rains. The Modjadji exercised political authority that was inseparable from her sacred ritual function, illustrating how religious and political legitimacy could be combined in institutions specifically reserved for women.

The third and perhaps most widespread form was the "dual-sex" political system, in which formal political authority was divided between parallel male and female institutions, each with its own officeholders, councils, and areas of jurisdiction. The Akan office of Queen Mother (Ohemaa) is among the best documented examples: the Queen Mother held responsibility for nominating and counseling the male chief, exercised independent judicial authority particularly over matters concerning women, and could, in some Akan states, depose a chief who failed to govern well. Among the Yoruba, the office of Iyalode gave a senior woman formal representation of women's economic and political interests within city-state governance, including authority over market regulation and the mobilization of women in political crises.

These institutions should not be mistaken for full gender equality in political life; in most societies, the highest and most numerous political offices remained held by men, and women's political institutions, even where well developed, often operated within domains defined as appropriately "female" — markets, fertility, women's disputes — rather than exercising authority across the full range of political decision-making. Nonetheless, the existence and durability of these institutions across many centuries and many regions demonstrates that formal female political authority was a normal, recognized feature of African political systems rather than a marginal exception, and that European colonial administrators who failed to recognize or actively dismantled these institutions were not simply observing a uniformly patriarchal "traditional" Africa but actively destroying functioning female political structures.

Section 18.7

Gender, Religion, and Culture

Religious systems across Africa — indigenous, Islamic, and Christian — both shaped and were shaped by gender roles, and the relationship between religion and gender varied considerably depending on the specific tradition and historical context.

In many indigenous African religious systems, women held significant ritual authority as priestesses, diviners, spirit mediums, and healers. Cults associated with water spirits, fertility, and healing — frequently overseen primarily by women — operated alongside male-dominated ancestral and political cults, and in some societies, the most powerful religious specialists recognized by the community were women whose spiritual authority commanded respect that crossed gender boundaries entirely. Possession cults in particular, found in various forms across East, Central, and Southern Africa, gave women — including women who held little formal social or economic power in other domains — channels of religious authority and public voice that could shape community decisions and provide outlets for social commentary otherwise unavailable to them.

The spread of Islam across North Africa, the Sahel, and the Swahili Coast (examined fully in Chapter 27) introduced Islamic legal frameworks regarding marriage, inheritance, and women's public roles that interacted differently with existing customary practices in different regions. Islamic inheritance law, for instance, guaranteed women specific shares of inheritance — generally smaller than men's shares but legally secured — in contrast to some customary systems that gave women no formal inheritance rights at all, meaning that the adoption of Islamic law sometimes expanded, rather than restricted, women's property rights relative to prior custom. At the same time, certain interpretations of Islamic law and custom, particularly as practiced in some urban centers, restricted women's public visibility and mobility more than some pre-Islamic customary practices had. Notably, West African Islamic scholarly tradition produced significant women scholars and teachers, including women associated with Sufi orders who taught, transmitted religious knowledge, and in some cases held considerable spiritual authority, demonstrating that Islamic learning in Africa was not a uniformly male domain.

Christianity's expansion, both in its ancient forms in Egypt, Nubia, and Ethiopia and in its nineteenth- and twentieth-century missionary forms, brought further variation. Ethiopian Christian tradition included prominent female religious figures and, at points, female political authority intertwined with Christian kingship, as examined in Chapter 12. European missionary Christianity in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, by contrast, frequently promoted a domestic ideology of womanhood drawn from contemporary European Victorian norms — emphasizing female domesticity, deference to husbands, and withdrawal from public economic and political roles — that diverged sharply from many existing African gender norms and contributed to the broader colonial-era erosion of women's public authority discussed in section 18.8.

Section 18.8

Social Expectations and Change

The imposition of European colonial rule from the late nineteenth century onward, examined comprehensively in Part VIII of this textbook, produced profound and largely negative changes in African women's formal economic and political authority, even as it created some new, if limited, opportunities and prompted significant female resistance and adaptation.

Colonial administrations, operating from European assumptions that treated men as the natural heads of households and the appropriate holders of political and economic authority, systematically constructed colonial governance around male intermediaries. Indirect rule systems, discussed in Chapter 48, generally recognized or installed male chiefs as the official channel of colonial administration, frequently ignoring, downgrading, or actively abolishing parallel female political institutions such as Queen Mothers and Iyalode offices that did not fit colonial administrators' assumptions about legitimate political authority. Colonial land registration systems, designed to formalize property ownership for purposes of taxation and economic development, typically registered land in the name of male household heads, eroding customary usufruct rights through which many women had previously accessed and controlled agricultural land independent of male relatives.

Colonial taxation systems compounded this erosion. Many colonial administrations introduced direct taxation of individuals or households, often assessed on male heads of household, but in some cases — as in the lead-up to the 1929 Aba Women's War in southeastern Nigeria — colonial officials attempted to extend taxation directly to women, provoking organized mass resistance. Cash-crop production for export, promoted and often required by colonial economic policy, typically favored male-controlled production and male access to agricultural extension services, credit, and export markets, while women's traditional food-crop production for local subsistence and market trade was increasingly marginalized within colonial economic priorities, even though women's labor often continued to provide the food that sustained households and communities.

Missionary education, while it provided some African women with new opportunities for literacy and formal schooling, often promoted a restrictive ideal of female domesticity and prepared girls disproportionately for domestic service and limited clerical roles rather than the fuller range of economic and leadership opportunities increasingly available to educated African men. The cumulative effect of these colonial policies, across most regions of Africa, was a significant decline in women's formal economic independence and political authority relative to the pre-colonial period — even though, as the Historical Debate feature below explores, historians continue to debate the precise extent, mechanisms, and regional variation of this decline.

African women did not respond to these changes passively. The 1929 Aba Women's War, in which thousands of Igbo and Ibibio women organized mass protests, including the traditional practice of "sitting on" or shaming offending officials, against colonial taxation policy and the abuses of colonially appointed male "warrant chiefs," stands as one of the most significant instances of organized female political resistance in colonial African history, forcing British colonial authorities to abandon plans to tax women directly and to reform the warrant chief system. Throughout the twentieth century, African women organized market associations, religious movements, and nationalist political organizations, and played central, if often historically underrecognized, roles in the independence movements examined in Chapter 53 and Chapter 54 — as organizers, financiers, propagandists, and in some cases as combatants in armed liberation struggles.

Section 18.9

Gender Across Regions and Time

No single narrative can capture the full diversity of gender history across Africa's many regions and historical periods, and students should resist the temptation to flatten this diversity into a single story of either uniform pre-colonial female authority or uniform pre-colonial female oppression. In the matrilineal Akan states of West Africa, women's structural importance in lineage succession combined with developed dual-sex political institutions to produce substantial, durable female authority that persisted, in modified form, even through the colonial and independence periods. In Sahelian pastoral societies organized around patrilineal descent and male-controlled livestock wealth, women's economic and political leverage was generally more constrained, though women retained important roles in dairy production, household management, and, in Islamic contexts, secured inheritance rights under religious law. Along the Swahili Coast, the interaction of Bantu-speaking customary practice, Islamic legal tradition, and extensive Indian Ocean trade connections produced a distinctive gender system in which women, including prominent women traders and property owners, exercised real economic authority even while operating within a broadly Islamic legal framework.

In Ethiopia, Christian kingship traditions periodically elevated women to positions of substantial political authority, including as regents and, in some periods, as rulers in their own right, reflecting both the particular dynamics of Ethiopian royal succession and the influence of Christian institutional structures. In Southern Africa, the sacred female kingship of the Lovedu rain queens coexisted with more patriarchal political structures among neighboring societies such as the Zulu kingdom, illustrating that geographic proximity did not produce uniform gender systems even within a single broader region.

Change over time was equally significant. The medieval and early modern periods saw the consolidation of many of the female political and economic institutions described in this chapter. The era of Atlantic and Indian Ocean commercial expansion, from roughly the sixteenth through nineteenth centuries, created new opportunities for some women as traders and intermediaries while exposing many other women to new and devastating forms of exploitation through the slave trades examined in Part VII. The colonial era of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries produced the broad pattern of erosion in formal female authority discussed above, alongside the rise of new forms of women's political organization. The independence and postcolonial eras, examined later in this textbook, have produced ongoing struggles — sometimes successful, frequently incomplete — to secure women's land rights, political representation, and legal equality within rapidly changing African states.

Everyday Life and Social History

The Compound Household

For most African families before the twentieth century, daily life centered on the compound — a cluster of dwellings, storage structures, and shared work and gathering spaces organized around an extended kin group, often including a senior man or woman, multiple wives in polygynous households, married children and their spouses, and dependents.

A typical day in a farming compound began before dawn, with women rising first to prepare the morning meal, fetch water, and organize children for the day's tasks. Through the morning, women and older children worked in the fields, weeding or harvesting depending on the season, while men might clear new land, tend livestock, or travel to a regional market or trade route. Afternoons often brought food processing — pounding grain, preparing sauces, preserving surplus harvest — alongside craft production such as spinning, pottery-making, or basket-weaving, frequently performed communally, allowing for social conversation, the transmission of practical knowledge between generations, and the sharing of news and oral tradition.

Children were raised collectively within the compound, with older siblings, co-wives, and grandmothers sharing child-care responsibilities, and with age-graded systems of social organization, found in many societies, structuring children's gradual initiation into adult economic and social roles. Evening meals brought the household together, and evenings often included storytelling, music, and the oral transmission of history, proverbs, and moral instruction — the same oral traditions discussed in Chapter 1 and Chapter 15 as a central source of historical evidence.

This compound-centered daily life, varying considerably by region, economic system, and religion, formed the lived experience within which the larger political, economic, and religious structures examined throughout this chapter actually operated.

Primary Source Window

Colonial Administrative Correspondence on the Aba Women's War (1929)

Creator: British colonial district officers in southeastern Nigeria · Date: 1929–1930 · Type: Colonial administrative report and inquiry testimony

Context: In November 1929, tens of thousands of Igbo and Ibibio women in southeastern Nigeria organized mass protests against colonial taxation policy and the conduct of colonially appointed "warrant chiefs." The protests, which colonial officials at the time labeled "riots," involved traditional practices of collective female protest, including gathering at the homes of offending officials, singing songs of ridicule, and demanding accountability. British colonial authorities responded with military force, resulting in dozens of deaths, and subsequently convened an official commission of inquiry.

Colonial reports from the inquiry described thousands of women converging on administrative centers, demanding the return of warrant chiefs' caps of office as a symbol of removal from power, and insisting that colonial officials had misunderstood both the tax policy's threat to women and the women's right to be consulted on matters affecting them.

Analysis: Colonial records of the Aba Women's War are valuable because they document, even through a hostile and uncomprehending official lens, the scale and organization of women's collective political action. However, students should note that these sources were produced by the very colonial administration the women were protesting, and therefore reflect official anxiety, legal justification for the use of force, and limited understanding of Igbo women's traditional institutions of collective protest. The women's own perspectives are better recovered through later oral history research and Igbo women's own accounts collected by historians in subsequent decades.

  1. What does the colonial administration's response reveal about its assumptions regarding women's political role?
  2. Why might colonial officials have referred to organized political protest as a "riot" rather than a political action?
  3. What evidence beyond colonial administrative records would help historians understand the women's own perspective on these events?
  4. How does this event illustrate continuity between pre-colonial forms of women's collective authority and colonial-era resistance?
Historical Debate

Did Colonialism Uniformly Worsen the Status of African Women?

The question: Historians broadly agree that European colonial rule disrupted many pre-colonial female political and economic institutions. But did colonialism produce a uniform decline in women's status across the continent, or did its effects vary significantly by region, class, and circumstance — and did colonial rule also create any new opportunities for women?

Interpretation A — Colonialism Produced a Broad Decline in Women's Authority: Many historians argue that across most regions, colonial administrative structures, taxation policy, land registration, cash-crop economics, and missionary domestic ideology combined to erode women's pre-colonial political offices, economic independence, and customary land rights, producing a substantial and largely consistent decline in formal female authority that took decades of postcolonial activism to begin reversing.
Interpretation B — Colonial Impact Was Highly Uneven and Sometimes Created New Opportunities: Other historians argue that the effects of colonialism on women varied considerably by region, religion, class, and the specific colonial power involved, and that some women — particularly mission-educated women in certain colonies — gained new access to literacy, wage employment, and eventually political organizing that had not existed in the same form previously, even as other women experienced significant losses, meaning that a single narrative of uniform decline oversimplifies a more uneven historical reality.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on extensive evidence of colonial tax records, land registration systems, and the documented decline of institutions such as the Iyalode and Queen Mother offices under colonial administration. Interpretation B draws on evidence of mission school enrollment among girls, the rise of women's wage labor and trade in certain colonial cities, and case studies showing that some communities retained or adapted female political institutions despite colonial pressure.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians and policymakers understand the historical roots of contemporary gender inequality in many African states, and whether the appropriate remedy is understood primarily as "restoring" pre-colonial institutions or as building genuinely new structures suited to contemporary circumstances.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, or do you think elements of both are correct? What additional regional evidence would help resolve this debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Colonial Taxation Policy in Southeastern Nigeria, 1929

From the perspective of British colonial administrators: Colonial officials viewed direct taxation as a routine and necessary instrument of administrative efficiency, intended to fund local government and assert orderly colonial rule. Some officials proposed extending taxation to women as a logical extension of policy already applied to men, with limited consultation of local custom.
From the perspective of warrant chiefs appointed by the colonial administration: Many warrant chiefs, themselves holding artificially created authority that did not always correspond to traditional leadership structures, faced pressure to implement unpopular colonial policy while lacking the customary legitimacy that might have allowed them to do so without provoking unrest.
From the perspective of Igbo and Ibibio women: Women understood the proposed taxation as both an economic threat to already strained household resources and as a violation of customary expectations that women's economic activities, particularly farming and trade, would not be subject to the same taxation as men's. The protests drew on long-standing traditions of collective female political action used to discipline men who violated community norms.
From the perspective of subsequent generations of Nigerian nationalists and historians: The Aba Women's War has been remembered as an important early instance of organized anti-colonial resistance, demonstrating that opposition to colonial rule was not limited to male political elites and that women's traditional institutions of collective action provided a foundation for later political mobilization.
Cause and Effect

Colonial Rule and the Erosion of Women's Pre-Colonial Authority

Long-Term Causes

European assumptions about male household headship and political leadership; Victorian-era domestic gender ideology; the structure of indirect rule, which sought existing or constructed male intermediaries.

Short-Term Causes

Specific colonial policies: land registration favoring male titleholders, taxation of male heads of household (and attempted extension to women), promotion of male-controlled cash-crop production, installation of warrant chiefs bypassing female political offices.

The Transformation

Decline of institutions such as the Iyalode and Queen Mother in formal governance; reduced female access to land and agricultural credit; marginalization of female-dominated food-crop economies relative to export agriculture.

Effects

Organized resistance such as the Aba Women's War; long-term economic and political disadvantages for women persisting into the independence era; twentieth-century women's organizing movements seeking to reclaim lost authority and secure new rights.

Historical complexity: The decline in women's formal authority cannot be attributed to a single colonial policy; it resulted from the cumulative interaction of administrative, economic, religious, and ideological forces operating over decades, with effects that varied by region and were never fully uncontested.

Continuity and Change Over Time

West African Market Women from the Pre-Colonial Era to the Present

What Changed

  • Colonial regulation and licensing increasingly favored male traders with administrative connections
  • Export-oriented cash-crop infrastructure diverted investment away from local food markets
  • Some formal political offices linked to market women's authority were not recognized by colonial administrations
  • Currency and credit systems shifted from customary forms of exchange toward colonial and later national currencies and banking

What Stayed the Same

  • Women continued to dominate local and regional retail trade in many West African cities
  • Market women's associations persisted and adapted, continuing to regulate trade and represent traders' interests
  • Trading skill and market success remained important sources of female social status and influence
  • Markets remained important sites of women's social networks, information exchange, and collective action

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of women's market dominance despite colonial disruption illustrates the resilience of African women's economic institutions and challenges any simple narrative in which colonialism completely erased pre-colonial female economic power.

Historical Vocabulary
Dual-Sex System

A dual-sex political system refers to a form of governance in which formal political authority is organized into parallel male and female institutions, each with its own officeholders, councils, and areas of jurisdiction, rather than a single hierarchy in which women are simply subordinate to male officeholders.

The Akan Queen Mother and the Yoruba Iyalode are the most thoroughly documented examples of dual-sex institutions, illustrating that pre-colonial African political systems could formally recognize female authority as structurally independent rather than merely advisory. Understanding dual-sex systems helps students avoid the mistaken assumption that all pre-colonial African political systems excluded women from formal governance.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the roles of women, the structure of families, and the organization of gender across African history, arguing that these subjects are not peripheral to the larger story of African states, economies, and religions but foundational to understanding them. Kinship systems — matrilineal, patrilineal, and mixed — organized inheritance, succession, and social belonging in ways that shaped every other dimension of African social and political life.

The gendered division of labor, in which women across much of the continent performed the majority of agricultural production while also dominating local and regional trade, gave many African women substantial independent economic resources that translated into social influence and, in many societies, formal political authority. Marriage institutions such as bridewealth and polygyny functioned as complex economic and social mechanisms linking lineages, distributing labor, and providing social security, with effects on individual women's lives that varied considerably by circumstance.

Formal female political authority took several distinct institutional forms across African history: direct female rulership, as exercised by the Kandakes of Kush, Queen Amina of Zazzau, and Njinga of Ndongo and Matamba; sacred female kingship, as exemplified by the Lovedu rain queens; and dual-sex political systems, as represented by the Akan Queen Mother and the Yoruba Iyalode. These institutions demonstrate that formal female political authority was a normal and recognized feature of many African political systems, not a marginal exception.

Religious systems across the continent — indigenous, Islamic, and Christian — interacted with gender in varied and sometimes contradictory ways, sometimes expanding and sometimes constraining women's public roles and property rights depending on specific local context and historical period.

The imposition of European colonial rule from the late nineteenth century onward produced a broad, though regionally uneven, decline in women's formal political and economic authority, as colonial administrations constructed governance, taxation, and land tenure systems around male household heads and frequently failed to recognize or actively dismantled pre-colonial female political institutions. African women responded not with passive acceptance but with organized resistance, exemplified most dramatically by the 1929 Aba Women's War, and with continued, adapted economic activity, exemplified by the resilience of West African market women's associations.

Women's political mobilization continued through the independence movements of the twentieth century and into the postcolonial era, as African women organized to reclaim lost authority and to secure new legal, economic, and political rights within rapidly changing states — a struggle that remains incomplete and ongoing in many African societies today.

Throughout, this chapter has emphasized the importance of careful historical method: recognizing the limitations and biases of available evidence, much of which was produced by male outsiders unfamiliar with or uninterested in women's actual roles, and using oral tradition, archaeology, and critical reading of colonial records to reconstruct a fuller and more accurate picture of the past.

The history of women, family, and gender in Africa is not a separate, supplementary subject; it is integral to understanding the kingdoms, trade networks, religious movements, colonial encounters, and independence struggles examined throughout this textbook, and it provides essential context for understanding ongoing debates about gender equality, land rights, and political representation across the contemporary African continent.

  4. Connect to chapter themes. Explain how your subject illustrates broader patterns discussed in this chapter, such as kinship systems, dual-sex political structures, economic authority, or colonial disruption and resistance.
  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay or equivalent presentation, present your subject's historical significance, the evidence supporting your account, and its connection to broader themes in African gender history.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has argued that the history of women, family, and gender is not a peripheral subject within African history but a foundational dimension of it, shaping the kinship systems, economic structures, religious institutions, and political arrangements examined throughout this textbook. Understanding how African societies organized descent, labor, marriage, and political authority along gendered lines provides students with essential tools for interpreting the kingdoms, trade networks, religious traditions, and colonial encounters discussed in other chapters.

This chapter's account of substantial pre-colonial female authority, followed by significant colonial-era erosion and ongoing twentieth- and twenty-first-century efforts at reclamation and reform, illustrates a broader theme that runs throughout this textbook: African history cannot be reduced to a simple narrative of either uninterrupted achievement or uninterrupted victimization. It is a history of considerable historical agency and authority, substantial disruption and loss under colonial rule, and persistent, often successful, adaptation and resistance.

The diversity of gender systems examined in this chapter — from the matrilineal Akan states to the patrilineal Sahel, from the sacred queendom of the Lovedu to the Islamic legal frameworks of the Swahili Coast — reinforces a theme central to this entire textbook: that Africa is a continent of extraordinary regional diversity, and that any adequate historical account must resist flattening that diversity into a single story.

Finally, this chapter's attention to the limitations of available historical evidence — much of it produced by male outsiders unfamiliar with or dismissive of women's actual roles — models a broader historical method that students should carry into every other chapter of this textbook: the careful, critical evaluation of sources, attentiveness to whose perspectives are represented and whose are missing, and the use of multiple types of evidence to construct the fullest and most accurate possible account of the African past.

Part X — Contemporary Africa
59

Conflict, Peace, and Reconciliation

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Never again" was the promise made after the Rwandan genocide of 1994 — and yet the century since independence has tested that promise again and again, across deserts, savannas, forests, and cities from Algeria to Mozambique.

In April 1994, over the course of roughly one hundred days, extremist Hutu militias, soldiers, and ordinary citizens mobilized by government radio broadcasts killed an estimated eight hundred thousand Rwandans, the overwhelming majority of them ethnic Tutsi, along with moderate Hutu who refused to participate or attempted to protect their neighbors. The killing was not the product of ancient, unchanging hatred, as it was sometimes described in international media at the time. It was organized, planned, and directed by a political and military elite that had spent years constructing an ideology of ethnic exclusion, distributing weapons, compiling lists of targets, and preparing ordinary citizens to participate in mass violence against their neighbors. The international community, despite receiving warnings from its own peacekeeping commanders on the ground, failed to intervene to stop the killing. By the time a rebel force led by the Rwandan Patriotic Front halted the genocide in July 1994, Rwandan society had been shattered, and the question of how — or whether — such violence could ever be understood, prevented, or repaired would shape African and global thinking about conflict and reconciliation for decades to come.

Rwanda's genocide is the single most extreme case examined in this chapter, but it is one instance within a much broader pattern of conflict that has shaped the decades since African independence. Civil wars in Nigeria, Sudan, Angola, Mozambique, Liberia, Sierra Leone, Somalia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo; ethnic and political violence in Kenya, Côte d'Ivoire, and Zimbabwe; border wars between Ethiopia and Eritrea and between other neighboring states; and, in recent decades, the rise of jihadist insurgencies across the Sahel and the Lake Chad Basin have together caused millions of deaths, displaced tens of millions of people, and reshaped the political, economic, and social landscape of large portions of the continent.

This chapter asks students to examine these conflicts not as evidence of an inherently violent continent, but as the product of specific, analyzable historical causes: colonial-era borders that grouped together diverse peoples with little regard for existing political communities; weak postcolonial state institutions, examined in Chapter 58, that struggled to manage ethnic and regional diversity; Cold War competition that armed and financed rival factions across the continent; economic crises and resource competition that intensified political struggles; and, in some cases, the deliberate political choices of leaders who mobilized ethnic identity for power rather than seeking to build inclusive national institutions. Understanding these causes does not excuse the violence; it makes the violence comprehensible as a product of human decisions and structural conditions rather than an inexplicable eruption of chaos.

The chapter also asks students to take seriously the extraordinary efforts that Africans and international partners have made to end conflicts, negotiate peace, rebuild shattered societies, and reckon honestly with violence. Peace agreements negotiated in Mozambique, Sierra Leone, Liberia, and Sudan; peacekeeping missions deployed by the United Nations and the African Union; truth and reconciliation processes in South Africa, Sierra Leone, and elsewhere; and the day-to-day work of community organizations, religious leaders, and ordinary citizens rebuilding trust after violence together constitute one of the most significant, if incomplete, bodies of experience in the modern history of conflict resolution anywhere in the world.

Africa's experience of conflict and peacebuilding since independence has, in fact, produced innovations that other regions have studied and adapted: South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission became a global model for transitional justice; Rwanda's community-based gacaca courts represented an ambitious, controversial attempt to process an overwhelming caseload of genocide-related crimes at the local level; and African-led peacekeeping and mediation efforts, though often underfunded and constrained, have grown steadily more assertive and, in some cases, more effective since the 1990s.

Students should also understand that conflict and peace are not simply opposite states that a society either has or does not have. Many African societies examined in this chapter have experienced long, uneven, and incomplete transitions — periods of active war followed by fragile peace, renewed violence, partial reconciliation, and continuing negotiation over the terms of coexistence between groups that fought each other. Peace, in other words, is not usually a single moment when war ends. It is frequently an ongoing, contested process that requires sustained political, social, and economic effort long after the guns fall silent.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain the major causes of conflict in postcolonial Africa, trace the course of several of the continent's most significant wars and episodes of mass violence, evaluate the effectiveness of different peacemaking and peacekeeping approaches, and assess how African societies have attempted — with varying degrees of success — to achieve truth, justice, reconciliation, and durable peace after periods of devastating violence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Identify the major civil wars and episodes of mass violence that have shaped postcolonial African history.
  2. Explain the structural, historical, and political causes of state breakdown and civil war in Africa since independence.
  3. Analyze the causes and consequences of ethnic and political violence, including the Rwandan genocide.
  4. Trace the negotiation and implementation of major African peace agreements.
  5. Evaluate the role and effectiveness of United Nations and African Union peacekeeping and mediation efforts.
  6. Assess the growing role of regional organizations in African peace and security.
  7. Compare different models of truth-seeking, justice, and accountability used after African conflicts.
  8. Describe the challenges of postwar reconstruction, including disarmament, demobilization, reintegration, and economic recovery.
  9. Explain how memory and commemoration shape long-term recovery and reconciliation after conflict.
  10. Interpret primary source material relating to peace agreements and conflict resolution.
  11. Contextualize African conflicts within colonial legacies, Cold War competition, and global economic pressures.
  12. Distinguish between the ending of active violence and the achievement of durable, substantive peace.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What combination of historical, political, and economic factors has produced civil war and state breakdown in parts of postcolonial Africa?
  2. How did colonial-era policies and Cold War competition shape the conditions for later conflict?
  3. What made the Rwandan genocide possible, and why did the international community fail to prevent it?
  4. How have African states and international bodies attempted to negotiate durable peace agreements, and why have some succeeded while others failed?
  5. What role have United Nations and African Union peacekeeping missions played in ending or containing African conflicts?
  6. How have regional organizations grown more assertive in African peace and security since the 1990s?
  7. What different approaches have African societies taken to truth, justice, and accountability after conflict, and what are the strengths and limits of each?
  8. What challenges do societies face in rebuilding after war, beyond simply ending the fighting?
  9. How does historical memory of conflict shape long-term reconciliation and the risk of renewed violence?
  10. What does the African experience of conflict and peacebuilding reveal about the broader relationship between state weakness, identity politics, and organized violence?

Historical Context

To understand the conflicts examined in this chapter, students must draw on material from several earlier chapters. Chapter 47 examined how European powers partitioned Africa at the Berlin Conference and in subsequent negotiations, drawing borders that paid little attention to existing political communities, language groups, or historical kingdoms, and that in many cases divided single ethnic or linguistic communities across two or more colonial territories while combining dozens or hundreds of distinct communities within a single colony. Chapter 48 examined how colonial administrations often governed through policies that hardened or invented ethnic distinctions for administrative convenience, issuing identity documents, favoring certain groups for education or civil service employment, and, in some notorious cases, promoting theories of racial or ethnic hierarchy among colonized populations that would have lasting and devastating consequences — as this chapter's discussion of Rwanda and Burundi will show in detail.

Chapter 54 examined how independence arrived rapidly and, in some cases, with limited preparation for the challenges of governing large, diverse, newly sovereign states. Chapter 56 examined how Cold War competition between the United States and the Soviet Union led both superpowers to arm, finance, and support rival African factions and governments, often prolonging conflicts that might otherwise have been resolved more quickly, and leaving behind large stockpiles of weapons that continued to circulate long after the Cold War ended. Chapter 58 examined the political struggles over governance — one-party rule, military coups, and contested democratic transitions — that form an essential backdrop to understanding why some African states experienced sustained civil conflict while others did not.

Against this backdrop, several additional factors help explain the specific conflicts examined in this chapter. Economic factors have played a significant role: competition over valuable natural resources, including diamonds in Sierra Leone and Angola, oil in Nigeria and Sudan, and coltan and other minerals in the Democratic Republic of Congo, has frequently intensified and prolonged conflicts by giving armed factions a source of revenue independent of any negotiated political settlement. Environmental pressures, including drought, desertification, and competition over increasingly scarce land and water resources, have contributed to conflict in the Sahel and parts of the Horn of Africa, sometimes overlapping with and intensifying ethnic or religious tensions. And beginning in the late 2000s and accelerating through the 2010s and 2020s, the spread of jihadist insurgencies linked to al-Qaeda and the Islamic State across the Sahel, Lake Chad Basin, Somalia, and northern Mozambique has introduced a distinct and evolving category of conflict, discussed later in this chapter, that combines religious ideology, local grievances, weak state presence, and transnational militant networks.

It is essential for students to recognize that not all African states have experienced sustained civil conflict, and that generalizing about "African conflict" as a single phenomenon risks the same error examined in Chapter 58 regarding governance: treating fifty-four distinct nations as a single undifferentiated story. Many African states — including Botswana, Senegal, Tanzania, Ghana, and others — have experienced little or no civil war since independence, while a smaller number of states have experienced repeated or prolonged conflict. Understanding why conflict has concentrated in particular places and periods, rather than assuming it as a uniform continental condition, is one of the central analytical tasks of this chapter.

Section 59.1

Major Conflicts in Contemporary Africa

Africa's postcolonial history includes some of the most significant armed conflicts of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, alongside long periods and large regions of relative peace. Placing these conflicts side by side reveals both common patterns and important differences in their causes, character, and consequences.

Nigeria's civil war of 1967 to 1970, often called the Biafran War, was among the earliest and most devastating postcolonial African conflicts. It arose from tensions among Nigeria's major regions and ethnic groups, exacerbated by a 1966 military coup and countercoup, mass violence against Igbo civilians in northern Nigeria, and the Eastern Region's subsequent declaration of independence as the Republic of Biafra. The ensuing war, complicated by a federal blockade that produced mass starvation, killed an estimated one to three million people, most from famine and disease rather than direct combat, and became one of the first African conflicts to receive sustained international media attention, particularly through harrowing images of starving children.

Angola and Mozambique, Portugal's last African colonies to achieve independence, moved directly from wars of liberation, examined in Chapter 54, into prolonged civil wars shaped heavily by Cold War competition. In Angola, rival independence movements — the Popular Movement for the Liberation of Angola (MPLA), the National Union for the Total Independence of Angola (UNITA), and a smaller third faction — fought a civil war from 1975 to 2002 in which the Soviet Union and Cuba backed the MPLA government while the United States and apartheid South Africa backed UNITA, prolonging a conflict that killed hundreds of thousands and displaced millions. Mozambique experienced a similarly devastating civil war from 1977 to 1992 between the ruling Frelimo government and the Renamo rebel movement, which received significant support from Rhodesia and later apartheid South Africa as part of those governments' efforts to destabilize neighboring states supporting liberation movements within their own borders.

Sudan experienced two prolonged civil wars between its northern, predominantly Arab and Muslim political center and its southern, predominantly African and Christian or animist populations — the first from 1955 to 1972 and the second, far larger and deadlier, from 1983 to 2005. These wars, driven by disputes over religion, resource control, political representation, and the imposition of Islamic law, eventually resulted in a 2005 peace agreement and, in 2011, the independence of South Sudan as Africa's newest nation. Tragically, as discussed later in this chapter, South Sudan's independence did not bring lasting peace, and the new country descended into its own internal civil conflict beginning in 2013.

West Africa experienced a cluster of interconnected and mutually reinforcing conflicts beginning in the late 1980s: civil wars in Liberia (1989–1997 and 1998–2003) and Sierra Leone (1991–2002) that spilled across borders, involved regional fighters and mercenaries moving between conflicts, and became closely associated internationally with the phenomenon of "conflict diamonds" — gemstones mined in rebel-controlled territory and sold to finance weapons purchases, a practice that later led to international efforts, examined in Section 59.6, to regulate the diamond trade.

The Great Lakes region of Central Africa experienced what is frequently described as the deadliest conflict in modern African history: the Second Congo War, sometimes called "Africa's World War," which began in 1998 and drew in the armies of at least eight African nations along with dozens of domestic and foreign armed groups, fighting over territory, political control, and access to the Democratic Republic of Congo's vast mineral wealth. Estimates of total deaths, including from war-related disease and starvation, range from roughly three million to over five million, making it by most measures the deadliest conflict anywhere in the world since the Second World War, though it has received comparatively limited international attention relative to its scale.

Since the late 2000s, a distinct pattern of jihadist insurgency has emerged as a major and growing source of conflict, discussed in detail in Section 59.3 and again in Chapter 65's discussion of Africa's global connections: Boko Haram's insurgency in northeastern Nigeria and the Lake Chad Basin, al-Shabaab's insurgency in Somalia, and a rapidly expanding cluster of jihadist groups across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger have together displaced millions of people and, in the Sahelian cases, contributed directly to the wave of military coups examined in Chapter 58.

Section 59.2

Civil War and State Breakdown

While each African conflict has distinct causes and dynamics, historians and political scientists have identified several recurring structural conditions that make civil war and state breakdown more likely. Understanding these conditions helps explain both why conflict has concentrated in particular states and periods and why some states with similar characteristics have avoided sustained civil war.

Weak state capacity — the inability of central governments to deliver basic services, project authority across their full territory, or manage disputes through legitimate institutions — has been a significant factor in nearly every major African civil conflict. States that inherited limited administrative infrastructure at independence, that experienced severe economic crisis, or that concentrated resources and authority narrowly around the capital city while neglecting peripheral regions have proven especially vulnerable to armed challenges to central authority.

Ethnic and regional exclusion from political power has also played a recurring role. Where particular ethnic, religious, or regional communities have been systematically excluded from political representation, economic opportunity, or access to state resources over extended periods, grievances have sometimes provided fertile ground for armed mobilization, particularly when political leaders have actively organized along ethnic lines rather than building cross-cutting national coalitions — a pattern connected to the political dynamics examined in Chapter 58.

Competition over valuable natural resources — diamonds, oil, timber, and minerals including coltan, used in electronics manufacturing — has, in several conflicts, transformed what might otherwise have been shorter or more contained disputes into prolonged wars, by giving armed factions a revenue source independent of any negotiated settlement and, in some cases, by giving external actors and neighboring states a direct financial incentive to prolong instability in order to continue extracting resources from contested territory. Scholars sometimes describe such conflicts as being driven partly by "greed" alongside "grievance," though most historians caution against reducing complex conflicts to either factor alone.

External intervention, whether through Cold War superpower support for rival factions, regional neighbors backing armed groups to destabilize rival governments, or, more recently, transnational jihadist networks providing ideological and material support to local insurgencies, has repeatedly prolonged and intensified conflicts that might otherwise have burned out more quickly or been resolved through local negotiation.

The case of Somalia illustrates several of these dynamics in an especially stark and prolonged form, and provides a useful extended case study for understanding state breakdown.

Somalia's central government collapsed in 1991 following the overthrow of longtime military ruler Siad Barre, whose government had already been weakened by military defeat in a border war with Ethiopia, economic crisis, and the loss of Cold War-era superpower support following the end of the Cold War. In the absence of a functioning central government, power fragmented among competing clan-based militias, producing widespread violence, famine, and, in 1992 and 1993, a controversial and ultimately unsuccessful United States-led humanitarian and military intervention that ended after eighteen American soldiers were killed in the Battle of Mogadishu — an episode that significantly shaped subsequent American reluctance to intervene militarily in African conflicts, including, controversially, during the Rwandan genocide the following year.

Somalia subsequently experienced over a decade without an internationally recognized functioning central government, during which a relatively stable and self-governing region, Somaliland, effectively separated in the north (though without international recognition as an independent state), while southern and central Somalia remained fragmented among competing warlords, later giving rise to the Islamic Courts Union and, from the mid-2000s onward, the al-Shabaab insurgency examined in Section 59.3. Despite the establishment of internationally backed transitional governments beginning in the 2000s and gradual improvements in state capacity in subsequent years, Somalia's experience illustrates how difficult and prolonged the process of rebuilding effective governance can be once a state has fully collapsed.

Section 59.3

Ethnic and Political Violence

No episode in this chapter demands more careful and honest historical attention than the Rwandan genocide of 1994, both because of its catastrophic scale and because understanding its causes reveals how colonial-era policy, political manipulation, and organized mass mobilization — rather than "ancient hatred," as it was frequently and inaccurately described at the time — produced one of the twentieth century's worst episodes of mass violence.

Rwanda and neighboring Burundi were home to two principal population groups, Hutu and Tutsi, who shared language, religion, and much of their social and cultural life, and who had historically been distinguished less by rigid ethnic boundaries than by social and economic categories — Tutsi were often, though not exclusively, associated with cattle-owning and, under the precolonial Rwandan kingdom, with certain positions of political authority. Belgian colonial administration, which governed Rwanda after the First World War, dramatically hardened these categories, issuing identity cards that formally classified every Rwandan as Hutu, Tutsi, or Twa, and favoring Tutsi for education and administrative positions based on colonial-era racial theories that portrayed Tutsi as a supposedly superior, more "European-like" group — a policy that entrenched division and resentment that would have devastating long-term consequences.

Following Rwanda's independence in 1962, political power shifted to the Hutu majority, and periodic episodes of ethnic violence against Tutsi occurred over subsequent decades, producing waves of Tutsi refugees who fled to neighboring countries, including Uganda, where many joined or supported what became the Rwandan Patriotic Front. Beginning in 1990, the RPF launched an insurgency from Uganda, leading to a civil war and, eventually, negotiations that produced the 1993 Arusha Accords, intended to establish a power-sharing government and end the conflict.

Rather than accepting power-sharing, extremist elements within the Hutu-led government and military instead used the following months to prepare for mass violence: distributing weapons, including machetes, to civilian militias known as Interahamwe, compiling lists of Tutsi and moderate Hutu political opponents, and using government-controlled radio to broadcast dehumanizing propaganda describing Tutsi as "cockroaches" requiring elimination. When the plane carrying Rwandan president Juvénal Habyarimana was shot down on April 6, 1994 — an event whose precise responsibility remains debated by historians — extremist leaders used it as the immediate trigger to launch the genocide that had already been prepared.

Over the following roughly one hundred days, government soldiers, trained militia, and, in many documented instances, ordinary civilians mobilized by local officials and propaganda participated in systematic killing that claimed an estimated eight hundred thousand lives. The United Nations peacekeeping force present in Rwanda at the time, though its commander repeatedly warned his superiors of the impending violence and requested reinforcement and a stronger mandate, was instead reduced in strength by the UN Security Council as the killing began, and most Western governments, including the United States, avoided using the term "genocide" during the killing itself, a choice widely understood afterward to reflect a deliberate effort to avoid legal and political obligations to intervene under international law.

The genocide ended only when the Rwandan Patriotic Front, advancing militarily from the north, defeated the genocidal government and took control of the country in July 1994, prompting a massive flight of Hutu civilians and former government soldiers and militia into neighboring Zaire (now the Democratic Republic of Congo) — a displacement that directly contributed to the outbreak of the First and Second Congo Wars examined in Section 59.1, as armed former genocidaires established bases in refugee camps and became entangled in regional conflict over subsequent years.

Rwanda's genocide fundamentally reshaped international thinking about the responsibility of the international community to prevent mass atrocities, contributing over the following decade to the development of the "Responsibility to Protect" doctrine debated in international forums, and to lasting institutional reforms within the United Nations intended, with uneven success, to improve early warning and response to mass violence. Within Rwanda itself, the postgenocide government pursued an ambitious and controversial program of justice, reconciliation, and reconstruction examined further in Sections 59.7 and 59.9.

Ethnic and political violence in Africa has taken other, less extreme but still significant forms in other contexts: post-election violence in Kenya following the disputed 2007 presidential election killed over one thousand people and displaced hundreds of thousands, driven by longstanding grievances over land, ethnic political mobilization, and disputed electoral results; Côte d'Ivoire experienced two periods of civil conflict, in 2002–2007 and again following a disputed 2010 election, rooted partly in disputes over national identity and citizenship affecting communities of northern and immigrant origin; and Zimbabwe experienced sustained, though smaller-scale, political violence directed against opposition supporters over multiple election cycles beginning in the 2000s.

Section 59.4

Peace Agreements and Negotiation

Ending civil wars has proven, in most cases, far more difficult and protracted than the outbreak of conflict itself, requiring sustained negotiation, compromise on deeply contested political and military questions, and, frequently, repeated failed attempts before a durable settlement could be reached. Studying African peace agreements reveals recurring challenges and, in some cases, effective solutions that have informed conflict resolution efforts elsewhere in the world.

Mozambique's 1992 Rome General Peace Accords, negotiated with the assistance of the Catholic Community of Sant'Egidio and the Italian government, ended the country's fifteen-year civil war by establishing a framework for demobilizing both government and rebel forces, integrating former combatants into a single national army, and holding multiparty elections. The agreement, alongside a sustained United Nations peacekeeping presence that helped verify demobilization and monitor the subsequent 1994 elections, is frequently cited as one of the more successful examples of African peacemaking, producing three decades of relative political stability, though Mozambique later experienced renewed low-level conflict with Renamo forces in the 2010s and, separately, a jihadist insurgency in its northern Cabo Delgado province beginning in 2017.

Sudan's 2005 Comprehensive Peace Agreement, negotiated over several years with significant mediation support from neighboring Kenya and other regional and international actors, ended the Second Sudanese Civil War by establishing wealth-sharing and power-sharing arrangements between the northern government and southern rebel movement, along with a provision for a referendum on southern independence, which was held in 2011 and resulted in overwhelming support for South Sudan's separation. While the agreement successfully ended the north-south war and produced Africa's newest independent state, it did not resolve underlying political tensions within South Sudan itself, which descended into internal civil conflict in 2013, illustrating that ending one conflict does not guarantee the durability of the resulting political settlement.

Sierra Leone's civil war was ultimately ended through a combination of negotiated agreement and decisive military intervention: the 1999 Lomé Peace Agreement, which controversially granted amnesty to rebel leaders including Foday Sankoh in exchange for a ceasefire, initially failed to hold, and renewed fighting in 2000 was only halted after British military intervention and a strengthened United Nations peacekeeping mission provided the military pressure necessary to bring rebel forces to genuinely disarm. Sierra Leone's experience illustrates that negotiated agreements alone are sometimes insufficient without credible military and international backing to enforce compliance.

Not every peace effort has succeeded. Numerous ceasefires and agreements in Angola's civil war collapsed before the conflict was ultimately ended in 2002, not through negotiated settlement but through the military defeat and death of UNITA leader Jonas Savimbi — a reminder that some African conflicts have ended through military victory by one side rather than negotiated compromise, with important implications for the character and durability of the resulting peace.

Power-sharing arrangements — agreements that guarantee representation in government for competing political or ethnic factions — have been used in several African peace processes, including in Kenya following the 2007–2008 post-election violence, in Zimbabwe following disputed elections in 2008, and in various stages of Sudan's and South Sudan's conflicts. Such arrangements can provide an essential mechanism for ending immediate violence by guaranteeing all major factions a stake in government, but scholars and practitioners have also noted that power-sharing agreements can entrench the very factional and ethnic political divisions that contributed to conflict in the first place, and do not always produce the deeper institutional reforms necessary for long-term stability.

Section 59.5

Peacekeeping and Mediation

International peacekeeping has played a central, though uneven, role in African conflict resolution since the 1990s. United Nations peacekeeping missions have been deployed to numerous African conflicts, with mandates that have evolved significantly over time — from traditional peacekeeping models focused on monitoring ceasefires between consenting parties toward more robust "peace enforcement" mandates authorizing the use of force to protect civilians, particularly following the well-documented failures of peacekeeping in Rwanda in 1994 and in Bosnia's Srebrenica massacre the following year, both of which prompted extensive reassessment of how peacekeeping mandates and troop levels should be structured.

The scale of some African peacekeeping deployments has been substantial: the UN mission in the Democratic Republic of Congo, established in 1999 and subsequently expanded and renamed several times, has at various points deployed over twenty thousand personnel, making it among the largest and most expensive UN peacekeeping operations in the organization's history, tasked with the enormously difficult challenge of protecting civilians across a territory roughly the size of Western Europe amid dozens of active armed groups.

Mediation efforts — third-party facilitation of negotiations between conflicting parties, distinct from the military deployment of peacekeeping forces — have also played a significant role, often led by African leaders, regional organizations, religious institutions, or respected individual mediators. Kenya's role in mediating Sudan's Comprehensive Peace Agreement, South Africa's mediation efforts in Burundi's civil conflict following the 1994 Rwandan genocide's regional spillover, and the involvement of former heads of state and international figures as special envoys in numerous African peace processes illustrate the range of actors involved in African conflict mediation.

Peacekeeping and mediation efforts have faced persistent and significant challenges: inadequate troop numbers and equipment relative to the scale of territory and violence involved; unclear or overly restrictive mandates that limited peacekeepers' ability to protect civilians, as occurred catastrophically in Rwanda; difficulty maintaining the political neutrality that traditional peacekeeping doctrine requires when one party to a conflict is clearly responsible for atrocities against civilians; and the challenge of securing sustained international political attention and funding for African conflicts that have often, controversially, received less sustained global attention and resources than comparable conflicts in other regions of the world.

Despite these challenges, peacekeeping missions have, in several documented cases, contributed to reducing violence, protecting civilians, supporting the successful implementation of peace agreements, and providing the security conditions necessary for demobilization, elections, and reconstruction to proceed — as occurred with reasonable success in Mozambique, Sierra Leone, and Liberia, even as the missions in Somalia and parts of the Democratic Republic of Congo have faced far more difficult and, at times, less successful conditions.

Section 59.6

Regional Organizations and Security

Alongside United Nations involvement, African regional organizations have taken an increasingly active and, in some respects, distinctively African-led role in continental peace and security since the 1990s — a development connected closely to the broader history of continental cooperation examined in Chapter 63.

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS), originally established primarily as an economic organization, developed a significant security role beginning with its intervention force, ECOMOG, which deployed to Liberia's civil war in 1990 and later to Sierra Leone, representing one of the earliest instances of African regional military intervention in a member state's internal conflict — a departure from the Organization of African Unity's historical emphasis on strict noninterference in internal affairs, discussed in Chapter 58. ECOWAS's security role has continued to develop in subsequent decades, including its responses to the 2020s wave of West African coups discussed in Chapter 58 and its ongoing engagement with the jihadist insurgency across the Sahel.

The African Union, established in 2002 as successor to the Organization of African Unity, explicitly departed from its predecessor's strict noninterference principle by adopting a founding charter that recognizes the AU's right to intervene in a member state in cases of grave circumstances, including genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes — a significant normative shift shaped directly by the international community's failure to prevent the Rwandan genocide. The AU's Peace and Security Council has since authorized numerous peacekeeping and observer missions, including a large deployment to Somalia (originally AMISOM, later restructured), where African Union forces have played the central role in supporting the internationally backed Somali government against al-Shabaab for over a decade, at significant cost in casualties among the troop-contributing nations, primarily Uganda, Burundi, Kenya, and Ethiopia.

International efforts to address conflict resources have also involved significant African participation: the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme, established in 2003 with the involvement of African diamond-producing states, international organizations, and the diamond industry, created a certification system intended to prevent "conflict diamonds" from entering international markets, in direct response to the role diamond revenue had played in prolonging the conflicts in Sierra Leone and Angola examined earlier in this chapter. The Kimberley Process has faced significant criticism over the years for gaps in its enforcement and definition of what constitutes a conflict diamond, but it represents a notable example of African states and international partners attempting to address one of the structural economic drivers of conflict identified in Section 59.2.

Subregional organizations beyond ECOWAS have also developed security roles: the Southern African Development Community (SADC) has been involved in mediation and, at times, military deployment in conflicts affecting member states, including its 2021 deployment to support Mozambican forces against the jihadist insurgency in Cabo Delgado; and the East African Community and the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) have played significant mediation roles in conflicts in Sudan, South Sudan, and Somalia.

Section 59.7

Truth Commissions and Justice

Societies emerging from conflict and mass violence have faced difficult, often contested choices about how to address accountability for atrocities while also building the political stability necessary for lasting peace — a set of questions introduced in Chapter 58's discussion of transitional justice following authoritarian rule, and examined here in greater depth specifically in relation to conflict and mass violence.

South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, discussed in Chapter 58, remains the most internationally influential African model of transitional justice, prioritizing public truth-telling and conditional amnesty over criminal prosecution as a mechanism for addressing apartheid-era violence while enabling a peaceful political transition.

Rwanda pursued a markedly different and more ambitious combination of approaches following the 1994 genocide. At the international level, the United Nations Security Council established the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR), based in Tanzania, which between 1995 and its closure in 2015 prosecuted senior genocide organizers, including government officials, military commanders, and media figures responsible for inciting violence through radio broadcasts, securing convictions that established important legal precedents regarding genocide and the role of media incitement in mass violence. However, the ICTR's limited capacity — it completed roughly ninety cases over two decades — could not begin to address the vastly larger number of individuals implicated in genocide-related crimes, estimated at several hundred thousand people.

To address this overwhelming caseload, Rwanda's government revived and adapted a traditional community-based dispute resolution practice known as gacaca, establishing community courts across the country between 2001 and 2012 that tried an estimated two million genocide-related cases at the local level, with communities themselves serving as witnesses, judges, and participants in a process intended to combine accountability with community-level truth-telling and reconciliation. The gacaca process remains a subject of significant historical and scholarly debate.

Sierra Leone combined a truth and reconciliation commission, modeled partly on South Africa's example, with a distinct judicial mechanism: the Special Court for Sierra Leone, a "hybrid tribunal" combining domestic and international law, judges, and staff, established jointly by the Sierra Leonean government and the United Nations to prosecute those bearing the greatest responsibility for war crimes and crimes against humanity during the civil war. The Special Court secured a historically significant conviction of former Liberian president Charles Taylor in 2012 for his role in supporting Sierra Leone's rebel forces, marking the first conviction of a former head of state by an international or hybrid tribunal since the Nuremberg trials following World War II.

Liberia established its own Truth and Reconciliation Commission following the end of its civil wars, which documented extensive testimony regarding wartime atrocities and recommended prosecution of numerous individuals, though implementation of the commission's recommendations has remained limited and controversial, illustrating a recurring challenge across African transitional justice efforts: truth-telling processes have often proven easier to establish than the sustained political will and institutional capacity required to translate their findings into meaningful accountability.

These varied experiences illustrate that there is no single correct model for addressing accountability after mass violence. Societies have made different choices — emphasizing truth-telling and amnesty, international prosecution, community-based justice, hybrid tribunals, or some combination of these approaches — reflecting different political circumstances, the scale and nature of the violence involved, available institutional capacity, and differing judgments about how best to balance the competing goals of justice, truth, reconciliation, and political stability.

Section 59.8

Rebuilding After War

Ending active fighting represents only the first stage of recovery from civil conflict. Postwar societies have faced the enormous practical challenge of demobilizing armed combatants, rebuilding destroyed infrastructure and institutions, reintegrating displaced populations, and restarting economic activity — challenges that, if inadequately addressed, have in several cases contributed directly to renewed conflict.

Disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration programs, generally referred to by the acronym DDR, have become a standard and essential component of postconflict reconstruction across Africa, involving the collection and destruction of weapons, the formal disbanding of armed groups, and programs intended to help former combatants — including, in many conflicts, large numbers of child soldiers who had been forcibly recruited or who joined armed groups as adolescents — transition into civilian life through education, vocational training, and economic support.

Historical Vocabulary
DDR (Disarmament, Demobilization, and Reintegration)

DDR refers to the standard set of postconflict programs designed to collect and destroy weapons from former combatants (disarmament), formally dissolve armed units and remove combatants from military command structures (demobilization), and support former fighters' transition into civilian economic and social life (reintegration). DDR programs have been implemented, with varying degrees of success, in nearly every major African postconflict setting examined in this chapter, including Mozambique, Sierra Leone, Liberia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo.

DDR programs face persistent challenges: inadequate funding for reintegration support, which can leave former combatants without sustainable livelihoods and vulnerable to remobilization by armed groups; the particular difficulty of reintegrating former child soldiers, who often missed years of education and may carry significant psychological trauma; and the challenge of ensuring genuine, complete disarmament rather than the surrender of only a portion of available weapons. Scholars and practitioners have increasingly emphasized that successful DDR requires sustained, well-funded, multiyear programs rather than short-term interventions, and that reintegration support is often the most consistently underfunded component relative to its long-term importance for preventing renewed conflict.

The scale of civilian displacement produced by African conflicts has been immense. Wars examined in this chapter have, collectively, produced tens of millions of refugees who fled across international borders and internally displaced persons who fled their homes but remained within their own country's borders. The Democratic Republic of Congo alone has, at various points since the late 1990s, had several million internally displaced people, while conflicts in Sudan, South Sudan, Somalia, and the Sahel have similarly produced massive, often protracted displacement, with many refugees spending years or even decades in camps in neighboring countries before conditions allow return, resettlement elsewhere, or permanent integration into host communities.

Rebuilding economic infrastructure — roads, schools, hospitals, agricultural systems, and administrative institutions — has typically required years or decades of sustained investment, often supported by international donors, development banks, and, in some cases, revenue from natural resource extraction that had earlier helped finance conflict but could, under improved governance, instead support recovery. Mozambique's and Rwanda's postconflict economic recoveries, though each shaped by very different political circumstances, are frequently cited as relatively successful examples of sustained post-war development, while reconstruction in Somalia, South Sudan, and parts of the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo has been repeatedly interrupted by renewed or ongoing violence.

Rebuilding institutions of governance, security, and justice has proven especially difficult, since these are precisely the institutions whose weakness or collapse frequently contributed to conflict in the first place. Security sector reform — rebuilding professional, accountable police and military forces, often by integrating former combatants from opposing sides into a single national security force — has been a central and challenging component of reconstruction in Mozambique, Sierra Leone, Liberia, and South Sudan, with mixed results depending on the availability of sustained international support, domestic political will, and adequate funding.

Section 59.9

Memory, Recovery, and Reconciliation

Long after active fighting ends and formal reconstruction programs conclude, societies affected by conflict continue to grapple with the deeper, longer-term work of reconciliation — rebuilding trust between communities that fought each other, addressing lingering trauma, and constructing shared narratives, or negotiating contested and competing narratives, about what happened and why.

Rwanda has pursued an extensive and, in some respects, distinctive program of state-directed memory and reconciliation, including national genocide memorials, an annual commemoration period, and official policies that discourage public identification by ethnic category in an effort to build a unified national identity beyond the Hutu-Tutsi distinction that colonial policy had hardened and that extremist politics had exploited to genocidal effect. This approach has produced genuine social stability and economic development in the decades since 1994, and international visitors and scholars have documented significant progress in interethnic coexistence at the community level. At the same time, some scholars and human rights observers have raised concerns about restrictions on political speech and independent media in Rwanda, arguing that the government's emphasis on unity and stability has, at times, limited space for open political competition and independent scrutiny of government policy — an ongoing subject of debate connected to the broader governance questions examined in Chapter 58.

Community-level reconciliation initiatives — bringing together survivors and perpetrators, supporting trauma counseling, and rebuilding interethnic or intercommunal social ties at the village and neighborhood level — have been implemented, with varying degrees of formal support and success, across many postconflict African settings, often led by religious institutions, local civil society organizations, and international humanitarian and development organizations working alongside communities themselves.

Public commemoration and memorialization — genocide memorials in Rwanda, war memorials and museums in Mozambique and Sierra Leone, and annual days of remembrance in various countries — have played an important role in shaping how societies collectively process and remember periods of mass violence, though the specific form such commemoration takes, and whose experiences and perspectives it centers, has frequently been a subject of ongoing political and social negotiation, echoing the broader themes of historical memory examined throughout this textbook.

Reconciliation, students should understand, is rarely a single completed achievement but rather an ongoing, uneven, multigenerational process. Societies examined in this chapter continue to navigate the long-term psychological, social, and political aftermath of conflict decades after formal peace agreements were signed, and historians and social scientists continue to study how factors including economic opportunity, political inclusion, education, and sustained community-level engagement contribute to — or, when absent, undermine — durable reconciliation over time.

Historical Vocabulary
"Africa's World War"

Historians and journalists have used the term "Africa's World War" to describe the Second Congo War (1998–2003), reflecting both the conflict's staggering death toll — estimated at three to over five million people, the deadliest conflict anywhere in the world since 1945 — and the involvement of the armed forces of at least eight African nations, alongside dozens of domestic and foreign armed groups, fighting across the territory of the Democratic Republic of Congo.

The term also highlights how comparatively little international attention and resources the conflict received relative to its scale, a disparity that historians and humanitarian organizations have frequently noted when comparing international responses to African conflicts with responses to comparable crises elsewhere in the world. Understanding why some conflicts receive sustained global attention while others of similar or greater scale do not is itself an important subject of historical and political analysis.

Primary Source Window

The Arusha Accords (Rwanda, 1993)

Creator: Rwandan government and Rwandan Patriotic Front negotiators, with regional and international mediation · Date: August 1993 · Type: Peace agreement / power-sharing framework

Context: The Arusha Accords, negotiated in Tanzania over more than a year between Rwanda's government and the Rwandan Patriotic Front, were intended to end the civil war that had begun in 1990 by establishing a transitional power-sharing government, integrating opposing armed forces, and providing for the return of Rwandan refugees. The agreement was internationally celebrated as a model peace framework at the time of its signing.

The accords established a broad-based transitional government including opposition parties and the RPF, set out a plan for merging government and rebel armed forces into a single national army, and called for the safe return and resettlement of Rwandan refugees who had fled earlier waves of ethnic violence, alongside provisions intended to guarantee political pluralism and human rights protections during the transition period.

Analysis: The Arusha Accords are a critical primary source for understanding the Rwandan genocide precisely because of what happened to them: rather than being implemented, they were actively rejected by extremist elements within the Hutu-led government and military, who viewed the power-sharing arrangement as an unacceptable threat to their political control and used the months following the agreement's signing to prepare for the genocide that began in April 1994. The gap between the accords' stated commitments and the mass violence that followed illustrates a central lesson of this chapter: a signed peace agreement does not, by itself, guarantee peace, particularly when powerful actors remain committed to violent alternatives and when the international community fails to provide the sustained monitoring and enforcement necessary to support implementation.

Questions for students:

  1. What does the failure of the Arusha Accords suggest about the limits of negotiated peace agreements alone?
  2. Why might extremist political actors have viewed a power-sharing agreement as a greater threat than continued conflict?
  3. What role might stronger international peacekeeping enforcement have played in preventing the accords' collapse?
  4. What additional evidence would historians need to fully explain why the Arusha process failed while other African peace processes, such as Mozambique's, succeeded?
Historical Debate

Should the International Community Have Intervened Militarily to Stop the Rwandan Genocide?

The question: In the years since 1994, this question has become one of the most closely examined "what if" debates in modern international relations. Historians, political scientists, and former policymakers continue to debate whether stronger and faster international military intervention could have stopped the genocide, and why it did not occur.

Interpretation A — Intervention Could Have Stopped or Significantly Reduced the Killing: Some scholars and former UN officials, including the peacekeeping force commander on the ground at the time, have argued that a modest reinforcement of international troops with a stronger mandate, deployed quickly in the genocide's early days, could have disrupted the organized killing campaign and saved a substantial number of lives, pointing to the relatively limited number of organized perpetrators directing the violence in its early stages.
Interpretation B — Intervention Would Have Faced Severe Practical and Political Obstacles: Other scholars and policymakers have argued that the political will, logistical capacity, and speed required for an effective intervention were unrealistic given the international environment of 1994, particularly following the American military's costly and unsuccessful intervention in Somalia the previous year, which made Western governments especially reluctant to commit ground troops to another African conflict, and that the speed and scale of the killing may have outpaced what any plausible intervention force could have prevented.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to the documented warnings sent by the UN force commander before the genocide began and to case studies suggesting that early, decisive action against organized militia leadership could have disrupted the killing's coordination. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the documented political reluctance of the United States, France, Belgium, and other key states to commit forces, and to the genuine logistical challenges of rapidly deploying an effective force to a landlocked country in the midst of active mass violence.

Why it matters: This debate has shaped subsequent international doctrine regarding the "Responsibility to Protect" and continues to inform decisions about humanitarian intervention in ongoing and future crises.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate, and what lessons should policymakers draw from it for future crises?

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing Genocide and Its Aftermath

From the perspective of a genocide survivor: Survivors experienced the loss of family members, neighbors, and community, often alongside profound and lasting trauma, and, in the genocide's aftermath, faced the difficult task of rebuilding a life in a society where perpetrators, including in some cases former neighbors, remained present in daily community life.
From the perspective of a former perpetrator processed through gacaca courts: Many lower-level perpetrators who participated in killing under a combination of coercion, propaganda, and mob dynamics faced community judgment and, in many cases, reduced sentences in exchange for confession and community service, raising difficult questions about how accountability should be balanced against the practical necessity of reintegrating an enormous number of implicated individuals into a functioning society.
From the perspective of a UN peacekeeping commander: Peacekeepers on the ground in 1994 who witnessed the genocide's early stages and repeatedly requested reinforcement and a stronger mandate experienced profound frustration and moral distress at their inability, under their existing rules of engagement and troop strength, to stop the killing they were witnessing.
From the perspective of Rwandan refugees who fled to neighboring Zaire: Hundreds of thousands of Hutu civilians, alongside former government soldiers and militia members responsible for the genocide, fled together into refugee camps in eastern Zaire, creating an extraordinarily difficult humanitarian and political situation in which genuine refugees fleeing reprisal violence were intermixed with organizers of the genocide, complicating international humanitarian response and directly contributing to the outbreak of the Congo Wars.
Cause and Effect

State Collapse in Somalia

Long-Term Causes

Colonial-era division of Somali territory among multiple European powers; clan-based social structure poorly accommodated by centralized postcolonial state institutions; decades of authoritarian, increasingly clan-favoritist rule under Siad Barre.

Short-Term Causes

Military defeat in the 1977–1978 Ogaden War with Ethiopia; loss of Cold War superpower patronage as the Cold War ended; escalating clan-based armed opposition to Barre's government in the late 1980s.

The Event

Overthrow of Siad Barre's government in January 1991 and subsequent collapse of central state authority into competing clan-based militia control.

Effects

Prolonged humanitarian crisis and famine; failed 1992–1993 U.S.-led intervention; decades without a fully functioning central government; eventual rise of the Islamic Courts Union and al-Shabaab insurgency; gradual, still-incomplete state reconstruction from the 2000s onward.

Historical complexity: Somalia's state collapse cannot be reduced to a single cause; it resulted from the interaction of colonial-era fragmentation, clan-based political structures, Cold War geopolitics, and the specific failures of Barre's government, and its consequences have continued to evolve for over three decades.

Continuity and Change Over Time

War and Peace in Africa Since Independence

What Changed

  • Cold War proxy conflicts gave way, after 1990, to internally driven civil wars and, later, transnational jihadist insurgencies
  • African regional organizations moved from strict noninterference to active peacekeeping and intervention
  • International peacekeeping mandates shifted, after Rwanda and Bosnia, toward stronger civilian-protection provisions
  • Transitional justice mechanisms — truth commissions, hybrid tribunals, community courts — developed and diversified significantly after the 1990s
  • International regulation of conflict resources, such as the Kimberley Process, emerged as a tool for addressing economic drivers of war

What Stayed the Same

  • Colonial-era borders and weak inherited state institutions have remained an underlying structural factor across nearly every conflict examined
  • Civilians, including women and children, have continued to bear the overwhelming majority of the human cost of conflict, through both direct violence and displacement
  • External actors — Cold War superpowers, regional neighbors, and, more recently, transnational jihadist networks — have continued to shape the trajectory of African conflicts
  • Reconstruction and reconciliation have consistently proven more prolonged and difficult than the negotiation of formal peace agreements

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity helps students avoid the mistaken assumption that African conflict is either an unchanging, permanent condition or a fully solved problem of the past — the historical record shows genuine institutional learning and improvement alongside persistent, unresolved structural challenges.

Everyday Life and Social History
Civilians in Wartime

Behind the political and military narratives examined in this chapter lay the daily experiences of millions of ordinary civilians navigating survival amid conflict. Farmers abandoned fields that became frontlines or minefields, disrupting food production and contributing to famine in Nigeria's Biafra region, Sudan, and Somalia. Families made agonizing decisions about whether to flee toward uncertain safety as refugees or remain in place amid danger, decisions shaped by age, gender, mobility, available resources, and the specific character of the violence in their area. Women in many conflicts faced particular risks of sexual violence, used in several African wars, including in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Sierra Leone, as a deliberate tactic of terror and social destruction, while also frequently bearing primary responsibility for sustaining households, caring for children, and, in many documented cases, playing active roles in community-level peacebuilding and reconciliation efforts.

Children faced distinct and severe risks, including forced recruitment as child soldiers in Liberia, Sierra Leone, Uganda, and the Democratic Republic of Congo, interrupted education that has had lasting generational consequences for affected countries, and, in the Rwandan genocide's aftermath, the challenge of growing up in households and communities marked by profound loss and trauma. The gradual return to ordinary daily rhythms — reopened schools and markets, rebuilt roads, functioning local government, farmers returning to secure land — has, across the postconflict societies examined in this chapter, served as one of the most meaningful and closely tracked indicators of genuine recovery, often mattering more to affected communities than the signing of formal peace documents or the holding of national elections.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the major patterns of conflict, peacemaking, and reconciliation that have shaped substantial portions of African history since independence. It has traced the causes of civil war and state breakdown, the specific and devastating history of the Rwandan genocide, the negotiation and implementation of major peace agreements, the evolving role of peacekeeping and regional security organizations, the diverse approaches African societies have taken to truth and accountability, and the long, uneven process of postwar reconstruction and reconciliation.

African conflicts examined in this chapter did not arise from any inherent characteristic of African societies, but from specific, analyzable historical conditions: colonial-era borders and administrative policies that hardened ethnic and regional divisions; weak state institutions inherited at independence and often further weakened by one-party rule, military governance, or economic crisis, as examined in Chapter 58; Cold War competition that armed and financed rival factions; competition over valuable natural resources; and, in the twenty-first century, the spread of transnational jihadist insurgency across parts of the Sahel and the Horn of Africa.

The Rwandan genocide of 1994 stands as the chapter's most extreme case, demonstrating how colonial-era ethnic classification, political manipulation, organized propaganda, and international failure to act combined to produce one of the twentieth century's worst episodes of mass violence — and how that catastrophe subsequently reshaped international thinking about the responsibility to prevent atrocities.

Peace agreements examined in this chapter, from Mozambique's Rome accords to Sudan's Comprehensive Peace Agreement, reveal both the possibility of durable negotiated settlements and the significant challenges of implementation, enforcement, and ensuring that formal agreements translate into lasting political stability. Some conflicts, such as Angola's, ended through military victory rather than negotiation, with different implications for the character of the resulting peace.

Peacekeeping and mediation, carried out by the United Nations, the African Union, and subregional bodies including ECOWAS, SADC, and IGAD, have played an increasingly significant and African-led role in African conflict resolution since the 1990s, evolving substantially in mandate and approach following the well-documented failures in Rwanda and elsewhere.

Transitional justice mechanisms have varied significantly across the continent, from South Africa's truth-and-amnesty model, to Rwanda's combination of international tribunal and community-based gacaca courts, to Sierra Leone's hybrid tribunal, reflecting different political circumstances and different judgments about how to balance truth, justice, reconciliation, and stability.

Rebuilding after conflict has, across nearly every case examined, proven more prolonged and difficult than negotiating an end to active fighting, requiring sustained disarmament and reintegration programs, massive humanitarian response to displacement, and years or decades of institutional and economic reconstruction.

Reconciliation, finally, has emerged from this history as an ongoing, uneven, multigenerational process rather than a single completed achievement, requiring sustained attention to memory, commemoration, community-level trust-building, and political inclusion long after formal peace agreements are signed.

Students should understand African conflict and peacebuilding, like African governance more broadly, not as a single continental story of chaos or of triumph, but as a set of distinct national and regional histories, shaped by common structural pressures yet diverging significantly according to specific political choices, international engagement, and the sustained agency of African societies working to build durable peace.

This chapter connects directly to the discussions of governance in Chapter 58, economic development in Chapter 60, and continental cooperation through the African Union in Chapter 63, all of which intersect closely with the questions of conflict and peace examined here.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Civil war
Armed conflict between organized groups within the same state, often over control of government, territory, or resources.
State breakdown
The collapse or severe weakening of central government authority and institutions, as occurred in Somalia after 1991.
Genocide
The deliberate, systematic destruction of a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group, in whole or in part, as occurred in Rwanda in 1994.
Ethnic mobilization
The political organization of groups around ethnic identity, which can be a legitimate form of political representation or, in some cases, a tool for inciting division and violence.
Proxy conflict
A conflict in which outside powers support opposing local factions to advance their own strategic interests, as occurred extensively during the Cold War in Angola and Mozambique.
Conflict resources
Natural resources, such as diamonds or minerals, extracted and sold to finance armed conflict.
Peace agreement
A formal negotiated settlement intended to end an armed conflict, often including provisions on power-sharing, disarmament, and elections.
Ceasefire
An agreement to stop fighting, which may be temporary or may form part of a broader peace process.
Peacekeeping
The deployment of international military and civilian personnel to monitor and support the implementation of peace agreements.
Peace enforcement
Military action, often authorized by the UN Security Council, intended to compel compliance with peace terms or protect civilians, going beyond traditional peacekeeping's reliance on the consent of conflicting parties.
Mediation
Third-party facilitation of negotiations between conflicting parties, often led by regional organizations, states, or respected individuals.
Power-sharing
A political arrangement guaranteeing representation in government for multiple factions, often used to end conflict.
Truth commission
A body established to investigate and document past human rights abuses, often as an alternative or complement to criminal prosecution.
Transitional justice
The range of mechanisms societies use to address past human rights abuses while transitioning toward peace, democracy, or both.
Hybrid tribunal
A court combining domestic and international law, judges, and staff, such as the Special Court for Sierra Leone.
DDR
Disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration — the standard framework for transitioning former combatants into civilian life after conflict.
Child soldier
A person under eighteen recruited or used by an armed force or group, a widespread and devastating feature of several African civil wars.
Internally displaced person
A person forced to flee their home but who remains within their own country's borders, distinct from a refugee who crosses an international border.
Refugee
A person who has fled their country due to conflict or persecution and sought safety in another country.
Reconciliation
The long-term process of rebuilding trust and coexistence between groups or individuals after conflict.
Jihadist insurgency
An armed movement pursuing political goals through religiously framed violence, such as Boko Haram and al-Shabaab.
Warlordism
A pattern of governance in which armed factional leaders control territory and resources outside formal state authority.
Failed state
A term describing a state that has lost the capacity to perform basic governmental functions across some or all of its territory.
Kimberley Process
An international certification scheme established in 2003 to prevent conflict diamonds from entering global markets.
Responsibility to Protect
An international principle, developed partly in response to the Rwandan genocide, holding that the international community bears responsibility to prevent mass atrocities when states fail to protect their own populations.
Gacaca courts
Community-based courts revived and adapted in Rwanda to try an estimated two million genocide-related cases at the local level between 2001 and 2012.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because conflict and its resolution shape nearly every other dimension of a society's historical trajectory — its economic development, its governance institutions, its demographic and social structure, and the degree to which its citizens can live in security and dignity. The wars and peace processes examined in this chapter are deeply intertwined with the histories of colonialism, independence, governance, and economic development examined elsewhere in this textbook.

This chapter's history has shaped later African developments in profound and lasting ways. The Rwandan genocide reshaped international humanitarian law and peacekeeping doctrine. The wave of West African civil wars produced the Kimberley Process and new models of hybrid international justice. The African Union's departure from strict noninterference reflects hard-won lessons from Rwanda's catastrophe. And the ongoing jihadist insurgencies across the Sahel continue to shape the governance crises examined in Chapter 58 and will likely shape African and global security policy for years to come.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is that conflict in Africa, like conflict throughout world history, arises from specific, analyzable human causes and choices — colonial legacies, political decisions, economic pressures, and international action or inaction — rather than from any inherent characteristic of African societies. It also reveals that peace is rarely a single achieved endpoint but an ongoing process requiring sustained institutional, economic, and social effort long after formal agreements are signed.

Understanding this chapter helps students see Africa as neither uniquely prone to violence nor simply a story of endless conflict, but as a continent whose peoples and institutions have, time and again, confronted catastrophic violence and worked — through negotiation, peacekeeping, truth commissions, community reconciliation, and sheer daily perseverance — to rebuild peace, however imperfectly and incompletely. The coexistence of ongoing crises, such as the Sahel insurgencies, alongside genuine and durable peace achieved in Mozambique, Sierra Leone, and elsewhere reminds students that this history, like the governance history examined in Chapter 58, remains actively unfolding.

This chapter's themes connect directly forward to Chapter 60's discussion of economic development, which examines how conflict and postconflict recovery shape economic trajectories, and to Chapter 63's exploration of the African Union's evolving role in continental peace and security — together forming a fuller picture of how African societies have navigated, and continue to navigate, the profound challenge of building durable peace after devastating violence.

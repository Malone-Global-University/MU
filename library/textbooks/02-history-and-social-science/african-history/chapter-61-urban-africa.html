Part X — Contemporary Africa

Urban Africa

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

In 1950, Lagos was a city of roughly three hundred thousand people. By the early 2020s, greater Lagos held more than fifteen million — making it, by most estimates, the largest city on the African continent and one of the largest urban areas on Earth, a transformation that took place within a single human lifetime.

Few historical processes have reshaped Africa more profoundly, or more rapidly, than urbanization. For most of the twentieth century, the large majority of Africans lived in rural villages, farming communities, and pastoral settlements, and cities, where they existed at all, were relatively small administrative, trading, or religious centers. That pattern has changed with extraordinary speed. Today, more than five hundred million Africans live in urban areas, and demographers project that African cities will add hundreds of millions more residents in the coming decades, making Africa's urban transition one of the largest movements of people in human history and one of the central storylines of the continent's future, examined further in Chapter 67.

This chapter examines that transformation: how African cities grew, why people moved to them, how governments and residents built — and struggled to build — housing, infrastructure, and services fast enough to keep pace with growth, how urban residents earned livings in both formal and informal economies, how cities became centers of cultural creativity and political power, and how the challenges and opportunities of contemporary urban Africa connect to the continent's colonial past and to its uncertain but consequential future.

Students should understand from the outset that African urbanization did not begin with independence, nor did it begin as a single continental process. Some African cities — Cairo, Fez, Timbuktu, Kilwa, Great Zimbabwe, Benin City — have histories stretching back many centuries, examined in earlier chapters of this textbook, and were significant urban centers of trade, learning, religion, and political power long before European colonization. Other cities, including many of today's largest metropolitan areas, were founded or dramatically reshaped by colonial administrations as ports, railheads, mining centers, or seats of colonial government, and grew explosively only in the decades following independence. Still others have grown almost entirely within living memory, transformed from small towns into sprawling metropolitan regions within a few decades.

This chapter also asks students to think carefully about what urban growth actually means for the people who experience it. Rapid urbanization has created genuine opportunities: access to education, healthcare, employment, markets, and cultural and political life that many rural areas cannot offer at the same scale. It has also created severe and, in some cities, worsening challenges: inadequate housing, overburdened infrastructure, traffic congestion, unreliable electricity and water supply, informal settlements lacking basic services, and, in some cases, significant environmental and public health hazards. Understanding both dimensions — opportunity and hardship, formal planning and informal adaptation, government policy and individual resourcefulness — is essential to understanding contemporary urban Africa honestly.

Finally, this chapter emphasizes that African cities are not simply passive sites where national and global economic forces play out. They are dynamic centers of culture, politics, innovation, and daily human creativity. The musical genres, fashion trends, technology startups, political movements, and artistic and intellectual life examined throughout the final chapters of this textbook overwhelmingly originate in, or are shaped decisively by, African cities. Lagos, Nairobi, Johannesburg, Cairo, Kinshasa, Accra, Addis Ababa, Dakar, and dozens of other cities have each developed distinctive urban cultures and identities even as they share certain common structural challenges and opportunities.

This chapter connects closely to Chapter 60's discussion of economic development, Chapter 62's discussion of health, education, and social change, Chapter 64's discussion of technology and innovation, and Chapter 66's discussion of culture and the arts — together forming a fuller picture of how African societies are building, and rebuilding, urban life at a scale and pace with few precedents in world history.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Trace the growth of African cities from precolonial and colonial origins through contemporary rapid urbanization.
  2. Explain the major causes of rural-urban migration across the continent.
  3. Describe the challenges of housing and infrastructure development in rapidly growing African cities.
  4. Analyze transportation systems and public service provision in urban Africa.
  5. Evaluate how urban residents earn livelihoods through both formal and informal economic activity.
  6. Distinguish between different types of informal settlements and assess the strategies used to address them.
  7. Connect urban culture, music, fashion, and creative industries to broader patterns of African cultural life.
  8. Assess the political significance of cities as centers of protest, governance, and national power.
  9. Interpret demographic projections and debates about the future of African megacities.
  10. Compare the urban experiences of different social groups, including by gender, class, age, and migration status.
  11. Contextualize African urbanization within global patterns of urban growth and development.
  12. Explain how colonial-era urban planning continues to shape contemporary African cities.

Historical Context

To understand contemporary urban Africa, students must draw on material examined in several earlier chapters. Chapter 11 examined the emergence of early African cities and urban centers as sites of trade, political authority, craft production, and cultural exchange long before European contact. Cities such as the Nile Valley urban centers examined in Chapters 7 and 8, the Sahelian trading cities examined in Chapters 20 through 25, the Swahili coastal city-states examined in Chapter 32, and Great Zimbabwe examined in Chapter 34 demonstrate that African urbanism has deep and varied roots, shaped by trade networks, religious institutions, political authority, and craft specialization rather than imported from elsewhere.

Chapter 48 examined how colonial administrations reshaped, and in many cases newly founded, African cities to serve colonial administrative and economic purposes. Colonial cities were frequently designed around racially segregated residential zones, with European quarters featuring paved roads, piped water, and formal infrastructure, while African residential areas — often designated townships, native quarters, or locations — received minimal government investment in housing, sanitation, or services, despite housing the majority of each city's population and providing the labor that colonial urban economies depended upon. This pattern of unequal, racially structured urban investment, examined in Chapter 51's discussion of colonial society, has left a lasting imprint on the physical layout, infrastructure distribution, and social geography of many African cities that persists, in modified form, to the present day.

Chapter 54 examined how independence brought high expectations for urban transformation alongside continued rapid population growth and rural-urban migration, as newly independent governments removed the legal restrictions on African urban residence and movement that many colonial administrations had imposed, while also facing severe shortages of housing, infrastructure, and formal employment relative to the scale of urban population growth. Chapter 60 examined the broader economic context of postcolonial development, debt crisis, structural adjustment, and globalization that has shaped, and often constrained, government capacity to invest in urban infrastructure and services over the decades since independence.

Since the 1990s, a further set of forces has reshaped urban Africa: accelerating rural-urban migration driven by both the pressures pushing people out of struggling rural economies and the opportunities pulling people toward urban education, employment, and services; the rapid spread of mobile technology, which has transformed urban communication, commerce, and daily life; growing international attention, investment, and debate regarding informal settlements and urban infrastructure; and the emergence of several African cities as globally significant hubs of technology, finance, culture, and political influence.

Students should understand that urban Africa, like African economic development and African governance examined in earlier chapters, is not a single continental story. Cities vary enormously in size, history, economic base, governance capacity, and daily character. Cairo's urban history stretches back millennia and differs enormously from Nairobi's history as a colonial railway town, which differs enormously from Kinshasa's growth from a small trading post to one of Africa's largest cities, which differs enormously from planned new capital cities such as Abuja in Nigeria. This chapter asks students to hold both shared structural patterns and significant local variation in mind throughout.

Section 61.1

The Growth of African Cities

African cities have followed varied but often converging paths of growth, shaped by precolonial foundations, colonial reshaping, and postindependence expansion. Understanding this varied history helps explain why African cities today differ so significantly from one another even as many share certain common contemporary challenges.

Some of Africa's most significant contemporary cities have precolonial roots stretching back centuries or, in the case of Cairo and other Nile Valley cities, millennia. Cairo developed as a major Islamic center of learning, trade, and political power beginning in the tenth century, building on even older urban traditions in the Nile Valley examined in Chapter 7. Kano, in what is now northern Nigeria, developed as a significant Hausa trading and manufacturing city examined in Chapter 25, with urban traditions predating European contact by centuries. Fez and Marrakech in Morocco developed as major centers of Islamic learning, craft production, and trade, with urban histories stretching back to the medieval period. These cities demonstrate that African urbanism has deep indigenous roots shaped by trade, religion, craft specialization, and political authority rather than urban forms simply imported by European colonizers.

Many of Africa's largest contemporary cities, however, were either founded or dramatically transformed during the colonial period, examined in Chapters 46 through 52. Colonial administrations established new cities, or radically expanded existing towns, to serve specific colonial economic and administrative functions: as ports for exporting raw materials and importing manufactured goods, as railheads connecting interior mining or agricultural regions to coastal ports, as administrative centers for colonial governance, or as mining towns built around specific mineral deposits. Nairobi began as a railway depot established by British colonial authorities in 1899 along the route of the Uganda Railway, growing from a small camp into a significant colonial administrative and commercial center and, eventually, into one of East Africa's largest metropolitan areas. Johannesburg grew explosively from the 1880s onward around the discovery of gold deposits, becoming within a few decades one of the largest cities in sub-Saharan Africa despite having no significant precolonial urban settlement on the site.

Colonial urban planning, examined further in Chapter 51's discussion of colonial society, typically organized cities around explicit or implicit racial segregation, with European residential and commercial districts receiving substantial infrastructure investment — paved roads, piped water, sewerage, electricity — while African residential areas, despite housing the majority of each city's population, received minimal formal investment and were often legally restricted in size, location, and permitted density. This colonial pattern of unequal urban investment established infrastructure and housing deficits that many African cities have struggled to overcome in the decades since independence, even as postindependence governments removed the explicit legal racial restrictions that colonial administrations had imposed.

Since independence, African cities have grown at rates with few historical precedents anywhere in the world. Africa's urban population has grown from roughly fifteen percent of the continental total in 1950 to well over forty percent today, with some countries, particularly in West and Central Africa, considerably more urbanized than the continental average. This growth reflects both the removal of colonial-era restrictions on African movement and urban residence and a combination of push and pull factors examined in detail in Section 61.2. Understanding the scale and speed of this transformation — cities doubling, tripling, or increasing tenfold in population within a few decades — is essential to understanding why housing, infrastructure, and service provision, examined in Sections 61.3 and 61.4, have struggled so significantly to keep pace with urban growth across much of the continent.

Section 61.2

Rural-Urban Migration

The dramatic growth of African cities since independence has been driven substantially by rural-urban migration: the movement of people from rural villages and farming communities to cities in search of education, employment, services, and opportunity. Understanding why so many Africans have made this move, and what they have found upon arrival, is central to understanding contemporary urban Africa.

Historians and social scientists generally analyze migration through a combination of "push" factors, which drive people to leave rural areas, and "pull" factors, which attract people toward cities. Push factors affecting rural Africa since independence have included limited agricultural land availability as rural populations have grown, examined in Chapter 5's discussion of agricultural transitions and Chapter 60's discussion of postcolonial economic challenges; declining profitability of smallholder farming relative to urban wage employment in many regions; environmental pressures, including drought and land degradation examined in Chapter 6 and Chapter 67; and, in some regions and periods, conflict and political violence examined in Chapter 59 that has directly displaced rural populations toward cities perceived as safer or offering humanitarian assistance.

Pull factors drawing migrants toward cities have included the concentration of formal-sector employment opportunities in urban areas, particularly in government, commerce, manufacturing, and services; access to secondary and higher education, which is disproportionately concentrated in urban centers across much of the continent; access to healthcare facilities, particularly specialized and hospital-level care; and the broader appeal of urban life, including access to markets, entertainment, religious and social institutions, and the perception, not always borne out in practice, that cities offer greater opportunity for social and economic advancement than rural areas.

Migration patterns have varied significantly by gender, age, and region. In many parts of the continent, young men have historically migrated to cities disproportionately in search of wage employment, often maintaining strong ties to rural home communities through remittances and periodic return visits, a pattern examined further in this chapter's discussion of household economic strategies. In other regions and periods, women's urban migration has grown substantially, driven by expanding education and employment opportunities, changing family structures, and, in some cases, the search for greater autonomy than rural gender norms and family structures permitted, connecting to themes examined in Chapter 18's discussion of women in African history and Chapter 62's discussion of gender change.

Migration to cities has rarely severed migrants' connections to rural home communities entirely. Instead, most African urban migration has historically operated through extensive networks of family and ethnic connection linking specific rural areas to specific urban neighborhoods, occupations, or even individual employers, with established urban residents frequently assisting new arrivals from their home region in finding housing, employment, and social support. Remittances sent by urban migrants to rural family members, and the periodic return of urban residents to rural areas for holidays, family events, agricultural seasons, or eventual retirement, have created durable circular migration patterns connecting rural and urban Africa rather than a simple one-directional movement from country to city.

Cause and Effect

Rural-Urban Migration and Rapid City Growth

Long-Term Causes

Colonial-era restrictions on African urban residence lifted at independence; growing rural population outpacing available agricultural land; historical underinvestment in rural infrastructure, education, and services relative to urban areas.

Short-Term Causes

Specific droughts, conflicts, or economic shocks in particular regions; expansion of urban education and employment opportunities in particular decades; family and ethnic migration networks facilitating specific moves.

The Event

Sustained, large-scale movement of people from rural areas to cities across multiple decades since independence, producing some of the fastest urban growth rates in world history.

Effects

Rapid expansion of informal settlements; severe strain on housing, infrastructure, and public services; growth of the urban informal economy; transformation of urban culture, politics, and daily life; continued rural-urban remittance and family ties.

Historical complexity: Rural-urban migration cannot be explained by any single cause. It reflects the interaction of colonial legacy, demographic change, economic opportunity, environmental pressure, and individual and family decision-making across multiple generations.

Section 61.3

Housing and Infrastructure

The speed of African urban population growth has, in most cities, significantly outpaced government and private-sector capacity to build adequate housing and infrastructure, producing one of the most significant and persistent challenges of contemporary urban Africa.

Formal housing development — homes built with proper legal title, connected to municipal water, electricity, and sewerage systems, and constructed according to formal building codes — has generally failed to keep pace with urban population growth in most African cities, for reasons including limited government financing for public housing programs, particularly during and after the structural adjustment era examined in Chapter 60; limited private-sector mortgage financing accessible to most urban residents, whose incomes and employment status, particularly for informal-sector workers examined in Section 61.5, often do not meet the requirements of formal lending institutions; and bureaucratic and legal obstacles to formal land titling and construction permitting in many cities, which can make formal housing development slow and costly relative to the scale of demand.

As a result, a very large proportion of urban housing across the continent has been built informally: constructed by residents themselves or by small-scale informal developers, often without formal legal land title, building permits, or connection to municipal infrastructure, and frequently in locations — floodplains, steep hillsides, land near industrial facilities or waste sites — that formal development had avoided due to environmental or safety concerns. This pattern, examined further in Section 61.6's discussion of informal settlements, has produced some of the largest concentrations of informal housing anywhere in the world, alongside significant ongoing government and international efforts, with mixed results, to expand formal housing supply and improve conditions in existing informal areas.

Infrastructure — piped water, sewerage and sanitation systems, electricity, paved roads, and drainage — has similarly struggled to keep pace with urban growth in most African cities. Water and sanitation infrastructure, in particular, remains severely inadequate in many urban areas, with large proportions of urban residents lacking access to piped water within their homes and relying instead on shared standpipes, water vendors, or, in the most severe cases, unsafe water sources, with significant consequences for public health examined further in Chapter 62. Electricity access has expanded significantly in many African cities in recent decades but remains unreliable in many areas, with frequent power outages affecting both households and businesses, driving widespread reliance on backup generators, particularly among businesses and wealthier households able to afford them.

Infrastructure deficits have not been distributed evenly within cities. Wealthier, often formally planned neighborhoods — frequently corresponding to former colonial-era European residential districts or newer elite developments — typically enjoy significantly better infrastructure access than poorer, often informally settled areas, reproducing and in some cases deepening the unequal urban geography established during the colonial period examined in Section 61.1. This uneven infrastructure distribution has significant consequences for public health, economic opportunity, and quality of life that vary dramatically even within the same city, a pattern examined further in this chapter's discussion of inequality and in Chapter 62's discussion of health outcomes.

Governments, international development institutions, and private investors have pursued various strategies to address urban housing and infrastructure deficits, with varying degrees of success. These have included large-scale public housing programs, which have expanded housing supply in some countries but often failed to reach the poorest urban residents; slum upgrading programs, which work to improve infrastructure, services, and, in some cases, land tenure security within existing informal settlements rather than demolishing and rebuilding them; and, increasingly, public-private partnerships and privately financed infrastructure projects, particularly for major transportation and utility projects, examined further in Section 61.4.

Section 61.4

Transportation and Public Services

Moving people, goods, and information across sprawling, rapidly growing African cities has posed persistent challenges that governments, private operators, and residents have addressed through a combination of formal infrastructure investment and extensive informal adaptation.

Formal public transportation systems — government-operated bus networks, urban rail, and, in a small number of cities, more advanced systems such as bus rapid transit — remain limited relative to the scale of urban population and demand in most African cities, reflecting the same infrastructure financing constraints examined in Section 61.3. Cities including Lagos, Johannesburg, and Dar es Salaam have invested in bus rapid transit systems in recent decades, providing dedicated road lanes and improved service for large numbers of daily commuters, while a smaller number of cities, including Cairo, Algiers, and Addis Ababa, have developed urban rail or light rail systems, though these formal systems typically serve only a fraction of total daily urban trips.

As a result, informal and semi-formal transportation has filled much of the gap in most African cities, constituting one of the most significant, if often underappreciated, features of urban infrastructure across the continent. Minibus and shared taxi services — known by various local names such as matatu in Kenya, danfo in Nigeria, tro-tro in Ghana, and combi or kombi in Southern Africa — operate extensive, largely privately owned and informally organized route networks that carry the majority of daily urban trips in most African cities, adapting flexibly to demand and reaching areas that formal transit systems often do not serve. These informal transport systems provide essential mobility and significant informal-sector employment for drivers, conductors, and vehicle owners, while also facing significant challenges including road safety concerns, traffic congestion, limited regulation, and, in some cities, periodic government efforts to formalize, regulate, or replace informal transport that have generated significant conflict with operators dependent on this employment.

Traffic congestion has become a defining feature of daily life in most large African cities, reflecting rapid population and vehicle growth combined with limited road infrastructure investment, producing commute times that can extend to several hours each way in cities such as Lagos, Nairobi, and Kinshasa, with significant consequences for economic productivity, air quality, and daily quality of life that affect virtually all urban residents regardless of income level, though wealthier residents typically have more transportation options, including private vehicles, to mitigate the impact.

Other public services — policing, fire protection, waste management, and municipal governance more broadly, examined further in Section 61.8 — have similarly struggled to expand at the pace of urban population growth in most African cities, with significant variation in service quality both between cities and between different neighborhoods within the same city. Waste management, in particular, has posed severe challenges in many rapidly growing cities, with formal municipal waste collection often unable to reach the majority of urban residents, particularly in informal settlements, leading to significant reliance on informal waste collection and recycling networks, which provide both essential services and livelihoods for many urban residents while also reflecting the broader gaps in formal municipal service capacity examined throughout this chapter.

Section 61.5

Work and the Urban Economy

Understanding how urban Africans actually earn their livings requires looking beyond formal employment statistics to the full range of formal and informal economic activity that sustains urban households across the continent, building directly on Chapter 60's discussion of the informal economy.

Formal-sector urban employment — government jobs, employment in registered private businesses, and formal manufacturing and services employment — has grown significantly in many African cities since independence but has, in most cases, failed to expand rapidly enough to absorb the scale of urban population growth and labor force entry, particularly among young people, examined further in Chapter 60's discussion of youth unemployment. Government employment, including civil service positions, education, and healthcare, has historically provided a significant, relatively stable source of formal urban employment in many countries, though public-sector hiring has often been constrained by the same fiscal pressures examined in Chapter 60's discussion of structural adjustment and government budget limitations.

As a result, the large majority of urban workers across most of the continent earn their livings through informal economic activity: street vending and market trading, small workshops and repair services, informal transport examined in Section 61.4, domestic labor, small-scale food preparation and sale, and countless other forms of small-scale, unregistered economic activity. This informal urban economy, while lacking the legal protections, tax registration, and, in many cases, physical infrastructure of formal businesses, provides essential goods and services, absorbs a very large share of the urban labor force, and demonstrates significant entrepreneurial adaptability in responding to local market conditions and consumer needs.

Urban markets have historically served, and continue to serve, as central hubs of both economic activity and social life in African cities, examined further in Chapter 60's discussion of markets and traders. Major urban markets, some of which have operated continuously for many decades or, in some cases, centuries, function as essential centers of commerce, credit networks, and community relationships, particularly for women traders who have historically dominated market trading across much of West and Central Africa and who continue to play a central role in urban commercial life.

Manufacturing employment has grown in some African cities in recent decades, particularly in sectors such as textiles and garment production supported by preferential trade access examined in Chapter 60's discussion of the African Growth and Opportunity Act, and in export processing zones established by some governments to attract manufacturing investment. However, manufacturing employment remains a relatively small share of total urban employment in most African cities compared with historical patterns of industrialization in other world regions, a pattern that some economists describe as a distinctive feature of African urbanization: cities growing rapidly in population without a correspondingly large expansion of formal manufacturing employment, a pattern sometimes described as urbanization without industrialization.

The digital and technology sector, examined further in Chapter 64, has emerged as an increasingly significant, if still relatively small in absolute employment terms, feature of urban economic life in cities such as Lagos, Nairobi, and Cape Town, which have developed into significant regional technology hubs attracting both domestic entrepreneurship and international venture capital investment, creating new categories of urban employment and entrepreneurship, particularly for younger, educated, urban populations, even as the large majority of urban workers continue to depend on informal and traditional formal-sector employment for their livelihoods.

Section 61.6

Informal Settlements and Survival

Informal settlements — residential areas built without formal legal land title, government planning approval, or, in many cases, connection to municipal infrastructure — house a very large proportion of the urban population across much of Africa and constitute one of the most significant, and most debated, features of contemporary urban life on the continent.

Informal settlements form for reasons closely connected to the housing and infrastructure challenges examined in Section 61.3: the inability of formal housing markets to provide adequate, affordable housing at the scale and pace of urban population growth, combined with limited government capacity or political will to provide public housing at sufficient scale, has led millions of urban migrants and long-term urban residents to build or occupy housing on available land, whether through informal land purchase arrangements, occupation of vacant or marginal land, or, in some cases, land inherited through informal community arrangements that predate formal government land administration systems.

Conditions within informal settlements vary considerably, both between different settlements and, often, within a single settlement. Some informal areas have developed relatively stable, long-established communities with functioning informal economies, social institutions, and, over time, gradually improving infrastructure achieved through resident investment, community organization, or government upgrading programs. Nairobi's Kibera, one of the largest and most studied informal settlements in Africa, houses several hundred thousand residents in an area with limited formal infrastructure but with extensive informal economic activity, community organizations, and, in recent decades, various government and international upgrading initiatives, alongside continued severe challenges including inadequate sanitation, housing quality, and, at times, land tenure insecurity that leaves residents vulnerable to eviction.

Governments and international development institutions have pursued sharply differing strategies toward informal settlements over the decades since independence. Earlier approaches, particularly common from the 1960s through the 1980s, frequently emphasized slum clearance: demolishing informal settlements, often with limited compensation or alternative housing provided to displaced residents, reflecting a view of informal settlements as illegal, unsanitary, or embarrassing obstacles to urban modernization. This approach has been widely criticized by later scholars and policymakers for displacing vulnerable populations without providing adequate alternatives, often pushing displaced residents into new informal settlements elsewhere rather than genuinely solving underlying housing shortages.

Since the 1990s, slum upgrading approaches — improving infrastructure, services, and, in some cases, land tenure security within existing informal settlements rather than demolishing them — have gained increasing support among international development institutions, including UN-Habitat, and among many African governments, reflecting growing recognition that informal settlements represent significant existing investments of resident labor and social capital that demolition approaches destroy, and that upgrading, while imperfect and often underfunded relative to need, can improve living conditions for large numbers of residents more effectively and with less social disruption than clearance and relocation.

Land tenure — the legal and social arrangements governing who has rights to use, occupy, sell, or inherit land — has emerged as a particularly significant and contested issue in debates over informal settlements and urban development more broadly. Many informal settlement residents lack secure, legally recognized land tenure, leaving them vulnerable to eviction, discouraging long-term investment in housing improvement, and complicating access to formal credit that typically requires legal property title as collateral. Various governments and international programs have experimented with different approaches to expanding land tenure security in informal settlements, including formal land titling programs, community-based land tenure recognition, and intermediate forms of tenure security that fall short of full formal title but provide residents with greater protection against eviction.

Historical Debate

Are Informal Settlements a Problem to Solve or a Solution to Support?

The question: Urban planners, government officials, and residents themselves have long debated how governments should approach informal settlements: as problems requiring elimination, or as functioning, resource-constrained communities requiring support and gradual improvement.

Interpretation A — Informal Settlements Reflect Failed Planning and Require Formalization or Clearance: Some planners and officials argue that informal settlements reflect a failure of formal urban planning and housing policy, that they often occupy environmentally hazardous or unsuitable land, lack basic services and safety standards, and that governments should prioritize either formalizing land tenure and infrastructure through comprehensive redevelopment or, where redevelopment is not feasible, relocating residents to planned formal housing elsewhere.
Interpretation B — Informal Settlements Represent Resourceful Community Adaptation Deserving Support Rather Than Displacement: Other planners, scholars, and residents argue that informal settlements represent rational, resourceful responses to genuine housing shortages and government failure to provide adequate affordable housing, that they contain substantial existing social capital, community organization, and economic activity that clearance approaches destroy, and that government policy should focus on incremental upgrading — improving infrastructure, services, and tenure security — rather than treating informal settlements primarily as problems to be eliminated.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to genuine environmental and safety hazards in some informal settlements, including flood risk, fire risk in densely built areas, and inadequate sanitation contributing to disease. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the destructive social and economic consequences of past slum clearance programs, documented cases of successful upgrading improving conditions without displacement, and the practical reality that formal housing supply has consistently failed to meet demand at comparable cost or speed.

Why it matters: How governments and international institutions answer this question shapes billions of dollars in urban policy and infrastructure investment and directly affects the housing security and living conditions of hundreds of millions of African urban residents.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented, which approach do you find more persuasive, and under what circumstances might each approach be more or less appropriate? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate for a specific city or settlement?

Section 61.7

Urban Culture and Innovation

African cities have long served, and increasingly serve today, as the primary centers of cultural creativity, artistic innovation, and youth culture examined more fully in Chapter 66's discussion of culture, literature, and the arts. Understanding cities as creative and innovative spaces, rather than only as sites of hardship and infrastructure deficit, is essential to a complete picture of urban African life.

Musical genres including highlife in Ghana, Afrobeat and Afrobeats in Nigeria, rumba and soukous in the Democratic Republic of Congo, mbalax in Senegal, amapiano and kwaito in South Africa, and countless other urban musical traditions developed substantially within specific city contexts, drawing on rural and traditional musical roots while responding to urban social conditions, technology, and, in the case of contemporary Afrobeats and amapiano, global commercial and streaming platforms that have carried these genres to international audiences far beyond their African urban origins.

Fashion, visual art, film, and literature have similarly flourished within specific urban contexts, with cities such as Lagos, Dakar, Nairobi, Johannesburg, and Accra developing distinctive creative economies encompassing fashion design, film production, including Nigeria's globally significant "Nollywood" film industry examined further in Chapter 66, visual arts, and literary culture, often connecting local urban experience to broader continental and global cultural conversations.

Youth culture more specifically has been shaped decisively by urban conditions, reflecting the disproportionately young demographic profile of most African cities, examined further in Chapter 67's discussion of Africa's demographic future, combined with urban access to education, technology, media, and diverse social influences that many rural areas lack. Urban youth have played significant roles in developing new forms of music, fashion, language — including urban slang and multilingual code-switching that blend indigenous languages, colonial languages, and global cultural influences — and social and political activism, examined further in Section 61.8.

Technology and entrepreneurial innovation, examined further in Chapter 64, have similarly concentrated substantially within specific urban hubs, with cities including Lagos, Nairobi, Cape Town, and Cairo developing significant technology startup ecosystems attracting international venture capital investment and producing companies addressing distinctly African urban and rural challenges, including mobile financial services, e-commerce, transportation, and agricultural technology. This urban innovation has created new categories of aspiration and opportunity for educated urban youth, even as access to this innovation economy remains concentrated among relatively small, urban, educated populations rather than broadly distributed across the urban population as a whole.

At the same time, students should understand that urban cultural innovation is not confined to formal creative or technology industries. Everyday urban creativity — in fashion assembled from secondhand clothing markets, in informal music and performance, in urban cuisine blending regional and international influences, in urban religious and social life, and in the informal languages and communication styles that develop within specific urban neighborhoods and communities — constitutes an essential, if less commercially visible, dimension of African urban cultural life that deserves recognition alongside more internationally prominent creative industries.

Section 61.8

Cities as Political Centers

African cities have historically served, and continue to serve, as critical centers of political power, protest, and governance, connecting the urban history examined in this chapter to the broader political history examined throughout the later chapters of this textbook.

Capital cities have served as the primary seats of national political power throughout the postcolonial period, concentrating government institutions, embassies, national media, and, frequently, significant national economic activity. Some countries have deliberately relocated their capitals to new, purpose-built cities, seeking to reduce the political and economic dominance of a single historically significant city, achieve more centrally located or ethnically neutral capitals, or pursue explicit nation-building symbolism. Nigeria's move from Lagos to the newly constructed city of Abuja, formally completed in 1991, reflected concerns about Lagos's ethnic and religious associations with particular regions, along with a desire for a more centrally located capital better connected to Nigeria's diverse regions. Similar deliberate capital relocations have occurred in several other African countries, including Tanzania's official, though only partially completed, move from Dar es Salaam to Dodoma.

Cities have also served as central sites of political protest and resistance throughout African political history, connecting to material examined in Chapter 50's discussion of resistance, Chapter 53's discussion of nationalism, and Chapter 57's discussion of the anti-apartheid struggle. Urban concentrations of population, combined with urban proximity to government institutions, media, and international observers, have made cities particularly significant sites for organizing and conducting mass protests, strikes, and demonstrations. The 1976 Soweto Uprising, in which police killed student protesters demonstrating against apartheid-era educational policy in the Johannesburg township of Soweto, became a pivotal moment in the anti-apartheid struggle examined in Chapter 57, demonstrating how urban township communities, despite severe infrastructure and political disenfranchisement under apartheid, became centers of sustained political organization and resistance.

More recently, urban centers have served as focal points for a range of contemporary African political movements, including protests against government corruption, electoral fraud, police violence, and economic hardship in cities across the continent. Urban youth, examined in Section 61.7, have frequently played leading roles in organizing contemporary protest movements, often using mobile technology and social media to coordinate demonstrations and communicate with domestic and international audiences in ways that connect directly to the technological transformations examined in Chapter 64.

Municipal governance — the administration of city government itself, including elected or appointed mayors and city councils, urban planning authorities, and municipal service delivery agencies — has faced significant challenges across much of urban Africa, including limited fiscal resources relative to the scale of urban population and service needs, examined throughout this chapter; tension between local municipal authority and national government control, with many African cities historically governed with limited local political autonomy relative to national government ministries; and, in some cities, significant corruption and governance challenges that have limited effective service delivery and infrastructure investment, connecting to the broader governance themes examined in Chapter 58.

At the same time, some African cities have developed increasingly capable and, in some cases, internationally recognized municipal governance institutions in recent decades, particularly in cities that have successfully implemented significant infrastructure projects, improved revenue collection, or developed innovative approaches to informal settlement upgrading and service delivery, suggesting that effective urban governance, while challenging, is achievable within African political and fiscal contexts and offering potential models for other cities facing similar structural challenges.

Section 61.9

Megacities and Future Growth

Demographic projections suggest that African urbanization, far from having reached its peak, will continue to accelerate significantly in the coming decades, with profound implications for housing, infrastructure, economic development, governance, and virtually every other dimension of contemporary African life examined throughout this textbook.

United Nations demographic projections suggest that Africa's urban population will more than double in the coming decades, with the continent's urban population growth rate significantly exceeding that of any other world region during this period. This growth reflects both continued rural-urban migration, examined in Section 61.2, and, increasingly, natural population growth within existing urban areas as urban populations, now including multiple generations of urban-born residents, continue to grow through births exceeding deaths, a pattern that some demographers suggest will become an increasingly significant driver of urban growth relative to migration in coming decades.

Several African cities are projected to grow into some of the largest urban areas in the world within the coming decades, with Lagos, Kinshasa, Cairo, and several other cities projected to reach populations of twenty million or more, joining a small number of global "megacities" — generally defined as urban areas with populations exceeding ten million — that already include several other African cities. These projections raise significant questions, examined throughout this chapter, about whether infrastructure, housing, and governance capacity can expand fast enough to accommodate this projected growth without producing severe and worsening urban hardship.

Secondary cities — mid-sized urban centers below the scale of national capitals and largest metropolitan areas — have received growing attention from urban planners and development economists as potentially significant, and currently underutilized, sites for accommodating future urban growth in ways that might reduce the pressure on already severely strained largest cities. Some governments have pursued deliberate policies to encourage investment and migration toward secondary cities, though with limited success to date relative to the continued dominant pull of largest cities, a pattern connected to the broader concept of urban primacy — the tendency of a single dominant city to attract a disproportionate share of a country's urban population, investment, and economic activity relative to other cities.

Technology and innovation, examined further in Chapter 64, are increasingly discussed as potential tools for addressing future urban challenges, with growing interest, though still limited large-scale implementation, in "smart city" approaches using digital technology to improve infrastructure management, service delivery, and urban planning, alongside continued reliance on the informal adaptation and entrepreneurial resourcefulness that has long characterized African urban life, examined throughout this chapter, as an essential complement to formal technological and infrastructure solutions.

Environmental sustainability has emerged as an increasingly urgent dimension of future urban planning, as African cities confront the reality that climate change, examined further in Chapter 67, poses particular risks to urban populations, including increased flooding risk in low-lying coastal cities such as Lagos, water scarcity risk in rapidly growing cities in more arid regions, and the broader challenge of accommodating continued urban growth in ways that do not create unsustainable environmental and infrastructure pressures for future generations of urban residents.

Historical Vocabulary
Urbanization

Urbanization refers to the process by which an increasing proportion of a population comes to live in urban rather than rural areas, typically accompanied by the physical growth of cities and towns. Africa has experienced one of the most rapid rates of urbanization in world history since the mid-twentieth century, with the continent's urban population share roughly tripling since 1950.

Urbanization is not simply a demographic statistic; it reflects and reshapes economic structures, social relationships, cultural life, and political power. Understanding the pace, causes, and unevenness of African urbanization is essential to understanding contemporary African economic development, governance, culture, and daily life, and to anticipating the continent's future trajectory examined in Chapter 67.

Historical Vocabulary
Informal Settlement

An informal settlement is a residential area built without formal legal land title, government planning approval, or, in many cases, connection to municipal infrastructure such as piped water, sewerage, or electricity. Informal settlements house a substantial proportion of the urban population across much of Africa.

The term "informal settlement" is generally preferred by scholars and policymakers today over older terms such as "slum," which can carry stigmatizing connotations that obscure the significant economic activity, community organization, and resourceful adaptation that characterize many such areas, even as students should also recognize the genuine hardships — inadequate sanitation, housing quality, and tenure insecurity — that residents of many informal settlements continue to face.

Primary Source Window

UN-Habitat Urban Reporting on African Cities

Creator: UN-Habitat (United Nations Human Settlements Programme) · Date: Recurring reports since the 1990s · Type: International policy and demographic report

Context: UN-Habitat is the United Nations agency responsible for promoting sustainable urban development worldwide. Since its establishment, it has produced regular reports on global and African urbanization, including data on urban population growth, informal settlements, housing conditions, and municipal governance, drawing on national census data, municipal records, and field research across African cities.

UN-Habitat reporting has documented that a very large share of Africa's urban population lives in conditions characterized by inadequate housing, limited access to clean water and sanitation, insecure land tenure, and overcrowding, while also emphasizing that informal settlements contain significant economic activity and community organization that formal urban policy has often failed to recognize or support effectively.

Analysis: UN-Habitat reports are valuable primary sources for understanding contemporary urban conditions because they draw on systematic data collection across many cities, allowing comparison over time and across countries. However, students should recognize the limits of such sources: international agency reports reflect particular policy frameworks and priorities, may rely on national data of uneven quality and consistency, and, like all secondary aggregation of information, can obscure significant local variation between and within specific cities and neighborhoods that direct fieldwork or resident accounts might reveal more fully.

Multiple Perspectives

Life in a Growing African City

From the perspective of a recent rural migrant: A new arrival to the city often relies heavily on family or ethnic community networks to find initial housing and employment, frequently in informal settlements with limited infrastructure, while sending remittances back to rural family members and maintaining strong ties to a home village or region.
From the perspective of a long-established informal settlement resident: Many residents who have lived in the same informal settlement for years or decades have built stable community networks, informal businesses, and, in some cases, incrementally improved housing, even as they continue to face tenure insecurity, inadequate sanitation, and limited formal recognition from city authorities.
From the perspective of a municipal government official: City officials frequently confront severe fiscal constraints, limited technical capacity, and difficult tradeoffs between formal planning regulations and the practical reality of large-scale informal housing and economic activity that formal budgets cannot fully address or, in some cases, fully control.
From the perspective of a young urban technology entrepreneur: Educated young professionals in cities such as Lagos and Nairobi increasingly find significant new opportunities in the digital economy and creative industries, even as they navigate many of the same infrastructure challenges — unreliable electricity, traffic congestion, limited formal financing — that affect urban residents across the economic spectrum.
Continuity and Change Over Time

African Cities from Colonial Rule to the Present

What Changed

  • Legal restrictions on African urban residence and movement, imposed under colonial rule, were removed at independence
  • Cities grew from a small share of the continental population to include hundreds of millions of urban residents
  • Policy approaches to informal settlements shifted substantially from slum clearance toward slum upgrading
  • Mobile technology transformed urban commerce, transport coordination, and daily communication
  • Cities developed increasingly significant creative, technology, and cultural industries with global reach

What Stayed the Same

  • Unequal infrastructure distribution between wealthier and poorer urban neighborhoods, rooted in colonial-era planning, has persisted in modified form
  • Formal housing and infrastructure development have consistently lagged behind urban population growth
  • The informal economy and informal transport have remained essential to urban livelihoods and mobility
  • Cities have remained central sites of political protest, organization, and national power

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity helps students understand contemporary urban Africa as shaped by colonial legacy without being permanently determined by it, and shaped by genuine recent transformation without assuming that transformation has resolved longstanding structural challenges.

Everyday Life and Social History
Commuting, Markets, and Neighborhood Networks

Behind the demographic statistics and policy debates examined in this chapter lies the daily texture of urban life experienced by hundreds of millions of Africans: the early morning walk or shared minibus ride to work or school, often spanning long distances and significant time; the daily negotiation of prices in local markets; the neighborhood mutual aid networks, including rotating savings associations examined in Chapter 60, that provide informal financial support and community connection; and the constant, resourceful improvisation required to manage unreliable electricity, water, and transportation while still meeting the demands of work, school, and family life.

Urban neighborhoods across the continent have developed distinctive local identities and social institutions — from neighborhood associations and religious congregations to informal youth groups and sports clubs — that provide social support, information networks, and collective problem-solving capacity that formal city government, with its limited resources examined throughout this chapter, often cannot fully provide. Understanding these everyday neighborhood-level social structures is essential to understanding how African cities actually function despite significant formal infrastructure and governance limitations.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most significant demographic and social transformations in contemporary African history: the rapid growth of African cities and the central role of urban life in shaping the continent's economic, social, cultural, and political development. It has traced African urbanism from its deep precolonial roots through colonial reshaping and the explosive, ongoing growth of the postindependence period, examining the causes of rural-urban migration, the persistent challenges of housing, infrastructure, and transportation, the significance of formal and informal urban economic activity, the debates over informal settlements, the creative and political significance of cities, and the scale of future urban growth projected for the coming decades.

African cities have varied historical origins, ranging from ancient urban centers along the Nile and across the Sahel and Swahili Coast, examined in earlier chapters, to cities founded or dramatically reshaped during colonial rule to serve colonial administrative and economic purposes, examined in Chapter 48. Colonial urban planning established patterns of racially segregated, unequal infrastructure investment that have persisted, in modified form, well beyond the end of formal colonial rule, shaping the physical and social geography of many contemporary African cities.

Since independence, African cities have grown at rates with few precedents in world history, driven by a combination of push factors, including limited rural land and economic opportunity, and pull factors, including urban education, employment, and services. This rapid growth has significantly outpaced formal housing, infrastructure, and public service capacity in most cities, producing severe and persistent challenges including inadequate housing, unreliable utilities, traffic congestion, and, most significantly, the growth of large informal settlements housing a substantial share of the urban population across much of the continent.

The informal economy and informal transportation systems, examined throughout this chapter and connected to Chapter 60's broader discussion of African economic life, have absorbed the large majority of urban employment and mobility needs that formal systems have failed to meet, demonstrating significant entrepreneurial adaptability even as informal-sector workers generally lack the legal protections, infrastructure access, and financial services available to formal-sector workers and businesses.

Policy approaches toward informal settlements have shifted significantly over the decades since independence, from earlier slum clearance approaches that frequently displaced vulnerable populations without adequate alternatives, toward slum upgrading approaches that seek to improve infrastructure, services, and tenure security within existing informal communities, reflecting broader international and African policy reassessment of how governments should engage with informal urban development.

African cities have also served as vital centers of cultural creativity and political power throughout the postcolonial period, generating musical, artistic, fashion, and technological innovation with significant continental and global influence, examined further in Chapters 64 and 66, while also serving as central sites of political protest, organization, and national governance, from the anti-apartheid struggle examined in Chapter 57 to contemporary movements addressing corruption, governance, and economic hardship.

Demographic projections suggest that African urbanization will continue to accelerate significantly in the coming decades, with several cities projected to grow into some of the largest urban areas in the world, raising urgent and unresolved questions about whether infrastructure, housing, and governance capacity can expand fast enough to accommodate this growth without producing worsening urban hardship, questions that connect directly to this textbook's broader concluding discussion of Africa's future in Chapter 67.

Students should understand urban Africa, like African economic development and governance examined in earlier chapters, not as a single continental story of crisis or triumph, but as a set of distinct city histories shaped by shared structural conditions — colonial legacy, rapid demographic growth, limited formal infrastructure capacity — yet diverging significantly according to specific governance choices, economic opportunities, and the sustained resourcefulness and creativity of urban African residents themselves.

This chapter connects directly to Chapter 60's discussion of economic development, Chapter 62's discussion of health, education, and social change, Chapter 64's discussion of technology and innovation, Chapter 66's discussion of culture and the arts, and Chapter 67's concluding discussion of Africa's future, all of which intersect closely with the urban questions examined here.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because urban life increasingly is the setting in which African history is being made. As the continent's urban population continues to grow at a historically unprecedented pace, the questions examined in this chapter — how cities house, employ, move, and govern their residents, and how residents themselves adapt, organize, and create within often difficult conditions — will shape the economic, political, and cultural trajectory of the continent for generations to come.

This chapter's history has shaped later African developments in profound ways. The colonial-era pattern of unequal urban infrastructure investment, examined in Section 61.1, continues to influence the physical and social geography of contemporary cities. The informal economic and settlement patterns that developed in response to inadequate formal housing and employment, examined in Sections 61.5 and 61.6, have become defining, structurally central features of African urban life rather than temporary or marginal phenomena. And the cultural and political significance of cities, examined in Sections 61.7 and 61.8, continues to shape national and continental political and creative life in ways that connect directly to the chapters that follow.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is that African urbanization, like African economic development and governance examined in earlier chapters, has been shaped by the interaction of structural inheritance — colonial urban planning, demographic pressures, limited government fiscal capacity — and the specific, consequential choices and adaptations of governments, urban planners, entrepreneurs, and ordinary residents. Neither structure nor individual and community agency alone explains the varied urban outcomes examined throughout this chapter, and students should resist both the temptation to view African cities as simply overwhelmed by crisis and the temptation to romanticize informal resilience without acknowledging genuine and serious hardship.

Understanding this chapter helps students see urban Africa as neither a simple story of urban failure nor a simple story of inevitable modernization, but as a continent of tremendous urban diversity, genuine hardship and genuine creativity, persistent structural challenge and remarkable human adaptability — a story that, like so much of the history in this textbook, remains very much unfinished and will shape the lives of thi s century's Africans more directly than perhaps any other historical process examined in this book.

This chapter's themes connect directly forward to Chapter 62's exploration of health, education, and social change in contemporary urban and rural Africa, Chapter 64's deeper examination of technology and innovation concentrated substantially in urban hubs, Chapter 66's discussion of the cultural creativity flourishing in African cities, and Chapter 67's concluding discussion of Africa's future — together forming a fuller picture of how African societies are building, and continuously rebuilding, urban life amid unprecedented growth and change.

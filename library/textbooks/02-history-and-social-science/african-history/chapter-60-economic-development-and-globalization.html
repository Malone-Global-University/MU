Economic Development and Globalization

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

In 2007, a Kenyan telecommunications company launched a mobile money service called M-Pesa that allowed millions of people without bank accounts to send, receive, and store money using a basic mobile phone — and in doing so, Kenya leapt ahead of much wealthier nations in the practical, everyday business of moving money securely.

To understand how a country with a fraction of the banking infrastructure of Western Europe or North America could become a global leader in mobile financial services, students need to understand the larger economic history examined in this chapter: the difficult inheritance African economies received at independence, the ambitious and often disappointed development plans of the 1960s and 1970s, the debt crises and structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s, and the more recent decades of renewed growth, entrepreneurial innovation, and deepening global economic integration. M-Pesa did not emerge from nowhere. It emerged from decades of African economies searching for paths to development that fit their own conditions rather than simply importing models built for other parts of the world — and from millions of ordinary Kenyans who had spent years finding informal, resourceful ways to move money and conduct business outside formal banking systems that had never adequately served them.

This chapter examines how African economies have changed since independence and how the accelerating process of globalization — the deepening integration of trade, investment, finance, technology, and labor across national borders — has reshaped African production, trade, and daily economic life. It is, in many respects, a story of high hopes followed by painful setbacks followed by renewed possibility: newly independent governments in the 1960s pursued ambitious plans for rapid industrialization and modernization, often borrowing heavily to finance dams, factories, and infrastructure; the oil shocks, falling commodity prices, and rising interest rates of the 1970s and 1980s plunged many African economies into severe debt crises; international lenders imposed structural adjustment programs that reshaped African economic policy, often at significant social cost; and, since the late 1990s and particularly the 2000s, many African economies have experienced renewed and, in several cases, sustained growth, driven by a combination of resource exports, expanding trade with new global partners such as China, growing domestic entrepreneurship, and rapid adoption of mobile and digital technology.

Students should understand from the outset that there is no single African economic story. Fifty-four nations, with vastly different resource endowments, colonial histories, political systems, and policy choices, have followed different economic paths since independence. Botswana's diamond-driven development differs enormously from Nigeria's oil economy, which differs enormously from Rwanda's post-genocide development strategy, which differs enormously from Ethiopia's state-led industrial planning, which differs enormously from the informal, trade-driven economies of much of West Africa. Generalizing about "the African economy" risks the same error this textbook has cautioned against in earlier chapters on governance and conflict: treating a vast and varied continent as a single undifferentiated case.

At the same time, certain shared structural conditions have shaped economic development across much of the continent. Many African economies inherited, from the colonial period examined in Chapters 48 and 49, an economic structure oriented toward exporting raw materials — minerals, agricultural commodities, and, in some cases, oil — to former colonial powers, with limited domestic manufacturing or processing capacity. This colonial economic structure did not disappear at independence; it has shaped, and in important ways constrained, the economic choices available to African governments for decades, even as some countries have worked deliberately and, in some cases successfully, to diversify beyond it.

This chapter also asks students to think critically about the concept of "development" itself. Development is not a neutral, universally agreed-upon goal; it involves contested choices about what kind of economic growth to pursue, who benefits from it, how quickly to pursue it, and what tradeoffs — environmental, social, political — are acceptable along the way. International development institutions, African governments, and African citizens have not always agreed about these questions, and understanding these disagreements is essential to understanding the economic history this chapter presents.

Finally, this chapter connects closely to Chapter 61's discussion of urban Africa, Chapter 62's discussion of health and education, Chapter 64's discussion of technology and innovation, and Chapter 65's discussion of Africa's global relationships — together forming a fuller picture of how African societies have pursued economic transformation and navigated an increasingly interconnected global economy, with real gains, real setbacks, and an economic story that remains very much in motion.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the economic structures African nations inherited from colonial rule and how those structures shaped postcolonial development choices.
  2. Describe the ambitions and outcomes of postindependence industrialization and economic planning strategies.
  3. Analyze the causes and consequences of Africa's debt crises and the structural adjustment programs imposed in response.
  4. Evaluate the role of foreign aid, trade relationships, and international financial institutions in shaping African economic policy.
  5. Describe the informal economy and entrepreneurship as significant, often underappreciated, forces in African economic life.
  6. Assess how globalization has reshaped African trade, investment, and market integration since the 1990s.
  7. Compare patterns of economic inequality and growth across different African countries and regions.
  8. Evaluate competing development models and philosophies debated by African policymakers and economists.
  9. Interpret primary source material relating to African development planning and policy debates.
  10. Explain how ordinary Africans have experienced economic change, reform, and globalization in daily life.
  11. Contextualize African economic development within global economic history and international relationships.
  12. Distinguish between economic growth and broader measures of human development and well-being.

Historical Context

To understand African economic development since independence, students must draw on material examined in several earlier chapters. Chapter 49 examined how colonial economies were organized primarily around extracting raw materials — minerals, cash crops, and, in some territories, oil — for export to the colonizing power, with colonial administrations investing relatively little in domestic manufacturing, processing industries, or the kind of broad-based education and infrastructure that might have supported diversified economic development. This colonial economic structure, often described by economists and historians as an extractive or export-oriented economy, left newly independent African states with economies heavily dependent on a small number of export commodities, limited industrial capacity, and infrastructure — railways, ports, roads — built primarily to move raw materials out of the interior toward coastal ports rather than to connect African regions to one another.

Chapter 54 examined how independence arrived, in most cases, with high expectations for rapid economic transformation. Independence leaders across the continent promised that political freedom would translate quickly into economic development, industrialization, and rising living standards. Chapter 55 examined the challenges of nation building more broadly, including the tension between ambitious development goals and limited state administrative capacity. Chapter 56 examined how Cold War competition shaped not only African politics and conflict but also economic policy, as both the United States and the Soviet Union offered competing models of development — market-oriented capitalism and state-planned socialism — along with aid, loans, and technical assistance intended to draw African governments toward one bloc or the other.

Against this backdrop, this chapter examines a further set of forces that shaped African economic development from the 1970s onward: the global oil price shocks of 1973 and 1979, which dramatically raised costs for oil-importing African nations while enriching oil-exporting nations such as Nigeria; rising international interest rates in the late 1970s and early 1980s, which sharply increased the cost of servicing loans that African governments had taken out to finance development projects; falling global prices for many of the agricultural and mineral commodities that African economies depended on for export revenue; and the resulting debt crisis that forced many African governments, beginning in the 1980s, to seek emergency financing from the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, financing that came attached to conditions — structural adjustment programs — requiring significant changes to government spending, currency policy, trade rules, and the role of the state in the economy.

Since the late 1990s, a further set of forces has reshaped African economic development: the end of the Cold War and the resulting reduction in superpower-driven aid and investment; the rapid rise of China as a major trading partner, investor, and infrastructure financier across the continent, discussed further in Chapter 65; the global commodity price boom of the 2000s, driven substantially by Chinese demand for African oil, minerals, and other resources; the rapid spread of mobile telephone and, later, mobile internet technology, which enabled new forms of financial services, business, and communication; and a new generation of African entrepreneurs, technology innovators, and policymakers seeking development strategies adapted to specifically African conditions rather than imported wholesale from elsewhere.

Students should understand that economic history, like political and social history, requires attention to both structural conditions — colonial inheritance, global commodity prices, international financial institutions — and the specific policy choices, institutions, and innovations of individual African governments, businesses, and citizens. Neither structure nor choice alone explains why some African economies have grown rapidly in recent decades while others have struggled, and this chapter asks students to weigh both kinds of explanation carefully.

Section 60.1

Postcolonial Economic Challenges

Newly independent African governments in the 1960s faced an extraordinarily difficult set of economic challenges, many of them rooted directly in the colonial economic structures examined in Chapter 49. Colonial economies had been designed, with few exceptions, to extract raw materials efficiently for export rather than to build diversified, self-sustaining national economies. Railways and roads typically ran from mining or agricultural regions directly to coastal ports, with little connection between different regions of the same colony, let alone between neighboring African territories. Manufacturing capacity was minimal; colonial policy in many territories had actively discouraged local industry that might compete with manufactured goods imported from the colonizing country. Education systems, particularly technical and higher education, had been limited, leaving newly independent states with a severe shortage of trained engineers, economists, administrators, and skilled technicians relative to the scale of the development ambitions their leaders proclaimed.

At the same time, independence leaders faced enormous political pressure to deliver rapid, visible economic improvement. Having promised that independence would bring not only political freedom but material progress — better wages, more schools, modern infrastructure, industrial jobs — governments faced the difficult reality that transforming inherited colonial economies into diversified, self-sufficient national economies would require resources, expertise, and time that most newly independent states simply did not possess in the 1960s.

Most African economies at independence depended heavily on exporting one or a small number of primary commodities — cocoa in Ghana, copper in Zambia, coffee in several East African countries, groundnuts in Senegal and Nigeria, oil beginning to emerge as significant in Nigeria and Angola. This pattern, often called commodity dependence, left African economies highly vulnerable to fluctuations in global commodity prices that African governments had little ability to influence, since prices were set in international markets shaped by global supply and demand, weather conditions in competing producing regions, and decisions made by commodity traders and consuming industries far from Africa.

Population growth added further pressure. Rapid population increase, driven substantially by improving public health and declining infant mortality, meant that even steady economic growth often failed to translate into rising living standards per person, since national income had to be divided among a rapidly growing population. Urbanization, examined further in Chapter 61, proceeded rapidly as rural residents moved to cities seeking education, employment, and opportunity, often faster than urban economies and infrastructure could absorb new residents productively.

Finally, newly independent governments faced difficult choices about the appropriate role of the state in economic development. Some leaders, influenced by socialist ideology and skeptical of markets shaped by colonial-era commercial interests, pursued extensive state ownership of industry, agriculture marketing, and major economic sectors. Others pursued more market-oriented approaches while still relying heavily on state planning and investment to jump-start industries that private capital, scarce in most African economies at independence, was unlikely to finance on its own. Nearly all governments, regardless of ideological orientation, relied heavily on state-led planning and investment during this period, reflecting both genuine conviction about the state's necessary role in development and the practical reality that private domestic capital was extremely limited.

Section 60.2

Industrialization and Planning

Many African governments in the 1960s and 1970s pursued industrialization strategies broadly known as import substitution, an approach also widely adopted across Latin America and parts of Asia during the same era. The core idea behind import substitution was straightforward: rather than continuing to export raw materials and import manufactured goods, as colonial economic patterns had established, newly independent governments would use tariffs, import restrictions, and direct state investment to build domestic industries capable of manufacturing goods — textiles, processed foods, basic consumer products, and eventually more advanced manufactured items — that had previously been imported.

Ghana under Kwame Nkrumah, examined in Chapter 53's discussion of Pan-Africanism, exemplified this approach, investing heavily in state-owned industries, the massive Akosombo Dam hydroelectric project intended to power industrialization, and ambitious infrastructure development, financed substantially through government borrowing and revenue from cocoa exports. Tanzania under Julius Nyerere pursued a distinctive variant, known as ujamaa or African socialism, emphasizing collective agricultural production, rural development, and self-reliance rather than rapid urban industrialization, reflecting Nyerere's belief that Tanzania's development should build on traditional African communal values rather than imported Western or Soviet models. Zambia, newly wealthy from copper exports at independence, invested heavily in infrastructure, education, and industrial diversification, hoping to use copper revenue to build a more diversified economic base before global copper prices inevitably fluctuated.

These industrialization efforts produced genuine, if often partial and uneven, achievements. Many African countries built their first significant manufacturing sectors, expanded electricity generation and transportation infrastructure, and significantly increased access to education, particularly primary education, during this period. Literacy rates rose substantially in many countries between the 1960s and 1980s, and infrastructure projects, including major dams, roads, and railways, created lasting economic assets that continued to serve subsequent generations.

However, import substitution and state-led planning strategies also encountered serious and, in many cases, ultimately crippling difficulties. State-owned enterprises frequently suffered from inefficient management, limited technical expertise, corruption, and a lack of the competitive market pressures that might otherwise have driven improved productivity. Import restrictions, intended to protect infant domestic industries, sometimes protected them so thoroughly that they never developed the efficiency or quality needed to compete internationally, leaving consumers paying higher prices for lower-quality domestic goods indefinitely rather than for a limited transitional period. Ambitious infrastructure and industrial projects were frequently financed through foreign borrowing, on the assumption that continued economic growth and export revenue would make repayment straightforward — an assumption that proved badly mistaken when global commodity prices fell and interest rates rose in the late 1970s and 1980s, as examined in Section 60.4.

By the late 1970s and early 1980s, many of the industrialization strategies pursued so ambitiously at independence were producing disappointing results: heavily indebted governments, inefficient and often loss-making state enterprises, persistent dependence on primary commodity exports despite years of industrialization efforts, and, in several countries, worsening rather than improving standards of living. This disappointing record set the stage for the profound economic crisis and policy reversal examined in the following sections.

Section 60.3

Resource Economies and Exports

For many African economies, dependence on exporting natural resources — oil, minerals, and agricultural commodities — has remained a defining feature of economic life from the colonial period through the present, shaping both significant opportunities and persistent vulnerabilities.

Nigeria's transformation into a major oil exporter beginning in the late 1960s and accelerating through the 1970s illustrates both the promise and the peril of resource-based development. Oil revenue transformed Nigeria's government finances, funding significant infrastructure investment and, for a period, giving Nigeria substantial influence within OPEC and international affairs. However, oil wealth also produced what economists often call the "resource curse" or "Dutch disease": the influx of oil revenue drove up the value of Nigeria's currency, making the country's non-oil exports, including agricultural products that had previously been significant sources of income for millions of Nigerian farmers, less competitive on international markets, while oil revenue often flowed disproportionately to political elites and regions with access to oil wealth, contributing to corruption, regional inequality, and, as examined in Chapter 59, contributing to grievances that fed into the conflict in the oil-producing Niger Delta region in subsequent decades.

Botswana's diamond-based development offers an instructive contrast frequently cited by economists studying African development. Unlike many resource-dependent economies, Botswana's government, beginning shortly after independence in 1966, negotiated favorable revenue-sharing arrangements with international diamond companies, invested diamond revenue substantially in education, infrastructure, and a sovereign investment fund rather than solely in current consumption, and maintained relatively strong, accountable governance institutions that limited the corruption and mismanagement that undermined resource wealth elsewhere. As a result, Botswana achieved one of Africa's highest rates of sustained economic growth and per capita income over subsequent decades, though the country's continued heavy dependence on diamond exports has left it vulnerable to fluctuations in global diamond demand and to the eventual depletion of diamond reserves.

The Democratic Republic of Congo's vast mineral wealth — copper, cobalt, coltan, gold, and diamonds — illustrates a starkly different pattern, in which resource wealth has, for much of the postcolonial period, been captured by political elites, foreign companies, and, during the conflicts examined in Chapter 59, armed factions, rather than translating into broad-based development for the Congolese population. Coltan, a mineral essential to manufacturing electronic devices including mobile phones and computers, became closely associated internationally with the conflict financing examined in Chapter 59's discussion of conflict resources, illustrating how global demand for specific resources can directly connect African mineral wealth to political instability and violence.

Agricultural export economies have followed related but distinct patterns. Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire have long depended heavily on cocoa exports, together producing roughly two-thirds of the world's cocoa supply, giving both countries significant, though not unlimited, influence over global cocoa prices, while also leaving millions of smallholder farming households vulnerable to price fluctuations, unequal global value chains in which cocoa-growing countries capture a relatively small share of the final retail value of chocolate products, and, in recent decades, growing international scrutiny of labor conditions, including child labor, on cocoa farms.

Since the 2000s, growing global demand, driven substantially by China's rapidly industrializing economy, for African oil, minerals, and agricultural commodities has fueled a significant commodity price boom and associated economic growth across much of the continent, examined further in Section 60.7. This boom has renewed longstanding debates about whether African economies can use resource wealth to finance genuine diversification and broad-based development, following models closer to Botswana's, or whether resource dependence will continue to produce the volatility, inequality, and vulnerability to global price fluctuations that have characterized much of the continent's economic history since the colonial period.

Section 60.4

Trade, Aid, and Debt

The relationship between African economies and the international financial system has evolved dramatically since independence, shaped by cycles of borrowing, crisis, reform, and, more recently, renewed access to diverse sources of international finance.

Foreign aid has played a significant, complex, and often debated role in African development since independence. Aid has come in many forms: direct financial grants and loans from wealthy nations and international institutions, technical assistance and training, humanitarian relief during conflicts and disasters, and, increasingly, support channeled through nongovernmental organizations working on health, education, and development projects. Supporters of aid argue that it has financed critical investments in health, education, and infrastructure that many African governments could not otherwise have afforded, particularly in the poorest countries with the most limited domestic tax revenue. Critics have argued that aid can create dependency, reduce the accountability of African governments to their own citizens by making them more accountable to foreign donors, sometimes support corrupt or ineffective governments, and, in some analyses, has failed to produce the sustained economic transformation its scale might suggest it should have achieved.

The debt crisis of the 1980s represents one of the most consequential episodes in postcolonial African economic history. As examined in Section 60.2, many African governments borrowed heavily during the 1960s and 1970s to finance development projects, often on the assumption that continued economic growth and commodity export revenue would make repayment manageable. When global interest rates rose sharply in the late 1970s and early 1980s — driven substantially by monetary policy changes in the United States intended to control domestic inflation — and global commodity prices simultaneously fell, many African governments found themselves unable to service their debts. By the mid-1980s, debt service payments consumed a very large share of government revenue and export earnings in many African countries, leaving little available for the health, education, and infrastructure investment that development required.

Facing severe balance-of-payments crises, most heavily indebted African governments turned to the International Monetary Fund and World Bank for emergency financing, which came attached to conditions collectively known as structural adjustment programs. These programs typically required currency devaluation to make exports more competitive and imports more expensive, reduced government spending including cuts to subsidies on food, fuel, and other necessities, privatization of state-owned enterprises, trade liberalization reducing tariffs and import restrictions, and reduced government intervention in agricultural marketing and pricing.

Structural adjustment programs produced significant and often painful social consequences across much of Africa during the 1980s and 1990s. Currency devaluation raised the cost of imported goods, including essential medicines and food items. Reduced government spending led to cuts in public health and education services, school fees in some countries, and reduced public sector employment. Privatization of state enterprises, while sometimes improving efficiency, also frequently led to significant job losses, at least in the short term. These social costs generated widespread public criticism of structural adjustment across Africa, and, by the late 1990s, significant reassessment among international institutions themselves regarding the programs' design and social impact.

In response to continued debt burdens and criticism of structural adjustment's social costs, the international community launched the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries Initiative in 1996, later expanded through the Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative in 2005, providing substantial debt cancellation to dozens of the world's poorest countries, most of them in Africa, in exchange for continued economic reform and, increasingly, commitments to direct freed-up resources toward poverty reduction, health, and education spending.

Since the 2000s, the landscape of African international finance has diversified significantly. China has emerged as a major lender and infrastructure financier across the continent, often through loans tied to specific infrastructure projects — roads, railways, ports, and power plants — frequently financed in exchange for resource access or repayment through resource exports. This Chinese financing has provided African governments with infrastructure investment on a scale and speed that traditional Western donors and international institutions had often been unable or unwilling to match, while also generating renewed debates, examined further in Chapter 65, about debt sustainability, transparency, labor practices, and the terms of Chinese loan agreements.

Cause and Effect

The African Debt Crisis of the 1980s

Long-Term Causes

Colonial-era economic structures oriented toward raw material export; heavy postindependence borrowing to finance industrialization and infrastructure; continued dependence on volatile commodity exports for government revenue.

Short-Term Causes

Global oil price shocks of 1973 and 1979; sharp rise in international interest rates in the late 1970s and early 1980s; simultaneous collapse in global prices for many African export commodities.

The Event

Widespread African government inability to service foreign debt beginning in the early-to-mid 1980s, triggering emergency IMF and World Bank lending attached to structural adjustment conditions.

Effects

Significant social costs from spending cuts and devaluation; widespread public criticism of adjustment programs; eventual debt relief initiatives beginning in 1996; lasting reassessment of appropriate development policy and the role of the state in African economies.

Historical complexity: The debt crisis resulted from the interaction of colonial-era economic structures, postindependence policy choices, and global economic forces largely outside African governments' control, and its consequences continued to shape African development policy for decades afterward.

Section 60.5

Investment and Development Policy

Since the disappointments of state-led industrialization and the painful experience of structural adjustment, African governments, international institutions, and development economists have continued to debate and revise appropriate strategies for attracting investment and promoting sustained economic development.

Foreign direct investment — long-term investment by foreign companies in African businesses, infrastructure, and resource extraction — has grown significantly since the 1990s, though it remains concentrated disproportionately in resource extraction industries, particularly oil and mining, rather than in manufacturing or services sectors that might generate more widespread employment and skills development. Attracting foreign investment beyond resource extraction has required, in most successful cases, improvements in infrastructure, reductions in bureaucratic obstacles to establishing and operating businesses, greater political and macroeconomic stability, and, in some cases, targeted incentives such as export processing zones offering tax benefits to manufacturing investors.

Rwanda's post-genocide development strategy, examined further in Chapter 59's discussion of postconflict reconstruction, offers an instructive example of deliberate, government-directed investment climate reform. Following the devastating genocide of 1994, Rwanda's government pursued an ambitious agenda of streamlining business registration, reducing corruption, investing in education and health, and positioning the country as a hub for technology and services, achieving significant improvements in international rankings of ease of doing business and attracting growing foreign investment, alongside continued debate, examined in Chapter 59, about the tradeoffs between this development-focused governance approach and political openness.

Ethiopia pursued a distinctive state-led development model beginning in the 2000s, with the government directing substantial investment toward infrastructure, including major hydroelectric projects such as the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, and toward export-oriented manufacturing, particularly textiles and garments, achieving for over a decade one of the world's fastest rates of economic growth, though this growth has more recently been significantly disrupted by internal conflict examined in earlier chapters and by broader economic pressures.

International development institutions have significantly revised their own policy recommendations since the height of structural adjustment orthodoxy in the 1980s and 1990s. By the 2000s, the World Bank, IMF, and bilateral donors increasingly emphasized "good governance," anti-corruption measures, targeted poverty reduction strategies developed with greater African government input, and investment in education and health as complements to, rather than substitutes for, market-oriented economic policy — a shift often summarized as moving from the more rigid "Washington Consensus" of the 1980s toward more flexible, context-sensitive development approaches, though significant debate continues among economists and African policymakers about how much this shift has actually changed development outcomes.

African-led development institutions and initiatives have also grown significantly in influence. The African Development Bank, established in 1964 but substantially expanded in subsequent decades, has become a significant source of development financing directed by African member states. Regional economic communities, examined further in Chapter 63's discussion of continental cooperation, have pursued various forms of economic integration intended to expand markets, attract investment, and reduce dependence on relationships with former colonial powers or other external actors.

Section 60.6

Entrepreneurship and the Informal Economy

Alongside formal, registered businesses and state-directed industrial policy, a vast informal economy has long constituted a central, if frequently underappreciated, feature of economic life across much of Africa. Understanding the informal economy is essential to understanding how most Africans actually earn their livings and how economic activity actually functions across much of the continent.

The informal economy encompasses an enormous range of economic activity that operates outside formal government registration, taxation, and regulation: street vendors and market traders, small unlicensed workshops and repair businesses, informal transport services, small-scale unregistered manufacturing and food processing, domestic labor, and countless other forms of economic activity conducted by individuals and small enterprises without formal legal status. In many African countries, the informal economy employs a majority — in some estimates, well over half — of the total workforce, particularly in urban areas, making it, by sheer scale, one of the most significant features of African economic life, even though it receives far less attention in official economic statistics, policy discussions, and international development frameworks than formal-sector employment and registered businesses.

The informal economy's persistence and scale reflect several interacting factors: the limited capacity of formal-sector job creation, particularly manufacturing and formal services employment, to absorb Africa's rapidly growing, increasingly urban workforce; burdensome, costly, and often corruption-prone bureaucratic requirements for formally registering and operating businesses in many African countries; limited access to formal banking and credit for small entrepreneurs, pushing many toward informal savings and lending arrangements; and, in some analyses, the practical resourcefulness and adaptability that informal economic activity allows, permitting entrepreneurs to respond flexibly to local market conditions without the fixed costs and regulatory burdens formal businesses face.

Microfinance institutions, which provide small loans and financial services to entrepreneurs, particularly women, who lack access to traditional banking, expanded significantly across Africa from the 1990s onward, inspired partly by models developed in South Asia, with the explicit goal of supporting informal and small-scale entrepreneurs in building and expanding their businesses. Microfinance has produced genuine benefits for many borrowers while also generating significant debate among development economists about its actual long-term impact on poverty reduction, appropriate interest rates, and the risk of over-indebtedness among borrowers.

The rapid spread of mobile phone technology and, subsequently, mobile financial services has interacted powerfully with Africa's large informal economy. Services such as Kenya's M-Pesa, launched in 2007 and subsequently adopted in various forms across much of the continent, allowed millions of people without access to traditional bank accounts to send and receive money, pay for goods and services, and, increasingly, access small loans and savings products using basic mobile phones, dramatically expanding financial inclusion in a way that traditional banking infrastructure, requiring physical bank branches, had never achieved for the large majority of the population working in informal or rural economic activity.

African women have played a particularly central role in the informal economy, especially in market trading, food processing, and small-scale retail, building on long historical traditions of female commercial activity examined in Chapter 18's discussion of women in African history. Women's informal economic activity has frequently provided essential household income while also facing distinct challenges, including limited access to credit, property rights that in some legal systems have historically disadvantaged women, and the burden of combining income-generating work with primary responsibility for childcare and household labor.

Historical Vocabulary
Informal Economy

The informal economy refers to economic activity that occurs outside formal government registration, taxation, and regulatory oversight. It includes street vending, small unregistered workshops, informal transport, domestic labor, and countless other forms of small-scale economic activity.

The informal economy is sometimes misunderstood as marginal, temporary, or a sign of economic failure. In much of Africa, it is instead a large, persistent, and structurally central feature of economic life, employing a majority of the workforce in many countries and providing essential goods, services, and income to hundreds of millions of people. Understanding its scale and significance is essential to understanding African economic history and avoiding the mistaken assumption that formal-sector statistics alone capture the full reality of African economic activity.

Section 60.7

Globalization and Market Integration

Since the 1990s, African economies have become increasingly integrated into global trade, investment, and financial systems, a process generally described as globalization. This integration has brought significant new opportunities alongside new vulnerabilities and continued debate about who benefits most from Africa's deepening global economic connections.

China's economic engagement with Africa has grown dramatically since the early 2000s, transforming from a relatively minor trading relationship into one of the most significant external economic forces shaping the continent. China has become Africa's largest trading partner, a major source of infrastructure financing and construction, and an increasingly significant investor across sectors including mining, manufacturing, agriculture, and telecommunications. Chinese-financed and constructed infrastructure — railways, roads, ports, dams, and telecommunications networks — has provided many African countries with badly needed infrastructure investment at a scale and speed that traditional Western financing had often failed to match, while also generating debate, examined further in Chapter 65, about debt sustainability, labor practices, environmental standards, and the long-term terms of the economic relationship.

Trade relationships with the United States and European Union have also evolved significantly. The African Growth and Opportunity Act, passed by the United States Congress in 2000, granted eligible sub-Saharan African countries preferential access to U.S. markets for a wide range of exports, particularly textiles and apparel, supporting significant growth in manufacturing employment in countries such as Lesotho and Kenya, though benefits have been distributed unevenly and have depended on periodic congressional renewal. The European Union has pursued Economic Partnership Agreements with African regional groupings intended to expand trade access while also generating debate about their impact on African industries competing with European imports.

Within Africa itself, regional and continental economic integration has advanced significantly, if unevenly, in recent decades. Regional economic communities — including the Economic Community of West African States, the Southern African Development Community, and the East African Community — have pursued reduced trade barriers, coordinated infrastructure development, and, in some cases, monetary cooperation among member states. The African Continental Free Trade Area, negotiated through the 2010s and formally launched in 2021, represents the most ambitious continental integration effort to date, aiming to create a single African market of over a billion people by progressively eliminating tariffs on the large majority of goods traded between African countries — an effort motivated significantly by the recognition that African economies have historically traded far more with former colonial powers and other external partners than with one another, despite geographic proximity.

The digital and technology sector has emerged as one of the most dynamic areas of African economic globalization, examined further in Chapter 64. Rapid mobile phone adoption, expanding internet access, and growing venture capital investment in African technology startups — particularly in financial technology, e-commerce, and agricultural technology — have created new economic opportunities, particularly for younger, urban, increasingly well-educated populations in cities such as Lagos, Nairobi, and Cape Town, which have developed into significant regional technology hubs.

Globalization's benefits and costs have been distributed unevenly across African societies. Export-oriented manufacturing and technology sectors have created significant employment and income opportunities for some workers, particularly in urban areas, while global commodity price volatility, competition from cheap manufactured imports, particularly from Asia, and the terms of international loan and investment agreements have generated significant hardship and continued dependency for others. Understanding these uneven effects is essential to the broader discussion of inequality examined in the following section.

Section 60.8

Inequality and Growth

Since the early 2000s, many African economies have experienced renewed and, in several cases, historically rapid economic growth, driven by the commodity price boom, expanding trade and investment relationships, and, in some countries, improved governance and macroeconomic management. This period of growth, sometimes described in international media and policy discussions as "Africa Rising," produced genuine and significant improvements in many measures of human welfare across much of the continent: rising per capita income in many countries, declining extreme poverty rates, expanding access to mobile phones and, increasingly, the internet, and improving health outcomes examined further in Chapter 62.

However, economic growth has not been distributed evenly, either across countries or within individual societies, and understanding the difference between aggregate economic growth and broadly shared development remains one of the central analytical challenges of this chapter. Growth rates have varied enormously across the continent: resource-rich economies benefiting from the 2000s commodity boom, well-governed economies attracting significant foreign investment, and economies pursuing sustained infrastructure and human capital investment have generally grown faster than economies affected by conflict, weak governance, or limited resource endowments, echoing patterns examined in Chapter 58's discussion of governance and Chapter 59's discussion of conflict.

Within individual countries, economic growth has frequently benefited urban, educated populations and those connected to expanding formal-sector industries, technology, and services more than rural populations dependent on smallholder agriculture, informal-sector workers, and residents of regions distant from major economic centers. Nigeria and South Africa, despite ranking among Africa's largest economies by total GDP, have also experienced some of the continent's highest levels of income inequality, with substantial populations continuing to experience significant poverty despite substantial aggregate national wealth, particularly in Nigeria's case tied to oil revenue.

Economists and development scholars increasingly emphasize the importance of measuring development through indicators beyond simple GDP growth, including the Human Development Index, which combines income measures with health and education indicators, and more specific measures of poverty, inequality, and access to basic services such as clean water, electricity, and healthcare. By these broader measures, several African countries have achieved significant improvements even where GDP growth has been modest, while others have experienced substantial GDP growth without correspondingly broad improvements in health, education, or poverty reduction, particularly where growth has been concentrated in resource extraction sectors that generate limited employment relative to their contribution to national income.

Gender inequality has remained a persistent feature of African economic life, examined in earlier chapters' discussion of women's roles in African societies. Women across much of the continent continue to face unequal access to land ownership, formal credit, education in some regions, and formal-sector employment, even as women's economic contributions, particularly in agriculture, informal trade, and increasingly in entrepreneurship and technology, remain substantial and, in many analyses, underrecognized in official economic statistics and policy frameworks.

Youth unemployment and underemployment have emerged as particularly significant challenges across much of Africa, reflecting the continent's rapidly growing, increasingly young population, examined further in Chapter 67's discussion of Africa's demographic future, combined with formal-sector job creation that has, in most countries, failed to keep pace with the number of young people entering the workforce each year. This mismatch has significant implications not only for individual economic welfare but for political stability, migration patterns, both within Africa and toward Europe and other regions, and the broader social and political dynamics examined throughout this textbook's discussion of contemporary Africa.

Section 60.9

Competing Development Models

African policymakers, economists, and citizens have debated competing visions of economic development since independence, reflecting genuine disagreements about the proper role of the state and markets, the appropriate pace and sequencing of economic reform, and the relationship between economic growth and broader social and political goals.

State-led development models, prominent in the 1960s and 1970s and examined in Section 60.2, emphasized government planning, ownership of major industries, and protection of domestic industries from international competition, reflecting both socialist ideological commitments in some countries and a widely shared, non-ideological conviction that African states needed to play an active role in development given the limited scale of private domestic capital at independence.

Market-oriented development models, associated with the structural adjustment era of the 1980s and 1990s examined in Section 60.4, emphasized reduced government intervention, privatization, trade liberalization, and reliance on market mechanisms and foreign investment to drive growth, reflecting the broader international policy consensus of the period, often summarized as the "Washington Consensus," and the practical necessity many African governments faced in securing emergency financing from international lenders.

Since the 2000s, a range of alternative and hybrid development models have gained influence, often explicitly seeking a middle path between pure state planning and unrestricted market liberalization. Developmental state models, drawing partly on the experience of East Asian economies such as South Korea and Singapore, emphasize strategic government investment in specific industries and infrastructure, combined with export orientation and relatively disciplined macroeconomic management, an approach Rwanda and Ethiopia, in different ways, have both pursued. African-led frameworks, including the African Union's various continental development strategies and the earlier Lagos Plan of Action examined in this chapter's primary source feature, have emphasized African self-reliance, regional economic integration, and reduced dependence on external aid and prescriptions, reflecting continued sensitivity, discussed throughout this textbook, to the legacy of colonial economic dependency.

Debates over appropriate development strategy also intersect closely with broader debates about governance, examined in Chapter 58: whether sustained economic development requires democratic accountability and strong rule-of-law institutions, or whether, as some point to in citing China's or Rwanda's experience, a degree of centralized political authority can, under certain conditions, more effectively drive rapid economic transformation, at least in the short to medium term, even as such arrangements raise significant concerns about political freedom and long-term institutional development.

Environmental sustainability has increasingly entered these development debates as well, as African policymakers and citizens confront the reality that some development pathways pursued by wealthier nations — heavy reliance on fossil fuels, resource-intensive industrialization — carry significant environmental costs that Africa, despite having historically contributed relatively little to global greenhouse gas emissions, faces disproportionately through the climate change impacts examined in Chapter 67. This has generated growing interest, though still limited large-scale implementation, in development strategies emphasizing renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, and climate resilience alongside conventional economic growth objectives.

Historical Debate

Did Structural Adjustment Help or Harm African Economies?

The question: Few topics in African economic history generate more sustained debate among economists, historians, and African policymakers than the legacy of the structural adjustment programs implemented across much of the continent in the 1980s and 1990s.

Interpretation A — Structural Adjustment Was a Necessary, if Painful, Correction: Some economists argue that African economies in the early 1980s were burdened by unsustainable debt, inefficient state enterprises, overvalued currencies, and unsustainable government spending, and that structural adjustment, whatever its short-term social costs, addressed genuine and urgent macroeconomic problems, laying the groundwork for the more diversified, market-integrated, and in several cases more rapidly growing economies of the 2000s and beyond.
Interpretation B — Structural Adjustment Imposed Severe and Avoidable Social Costs: Other economists and historians argue that structural adjustment programs, designed largely by international institutions with limited African input, imposed severe cuts to health, education, and social services precisely when vulnerable populations most needed support, that currency devaluation and reduced subsidies disproportionately harmed the poor, and that alternative reform approaches, involving more gradual adjustment and greater protection of essential social spending, could have addressed macroeconomic problems with significantly less human cost.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to improved macroeconomic indicators — reduced inflation, more sustainable government budgets, more competitive exchange rates — in several countries following adjustment, and to the subsequent growth many African economies experienced in the 2000s. Supporters of Interpretation B point to documented increases in poverty, reduced school enrollment, and worsened health outcomes in several countries during the adjustment period, and to later reassessments by the World Bank and IMF themselves acknowledging design flaws in earlier programs.

Why it matters: How historians and economists interpret structural adjustment shapes ongoing debates about appropriate development policy today, including debates over the terms attached to Chinese loans, IMF programs, and other contemporary international financing arrangements.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented, which interpretation do you find more persuasive? Could both interpretations be partially correct? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate?

Historical Vocabulary
Structural Adjustment

Structural adjustment refers to economic reform programs required by the International Monetary Fund and World Bank as conditions for emergency financing, typically including currency devaluation, reduced government spending, privatization of state enterprises, and trade liberalization.

Structural adjustment programs reshaped economic policy across much of Africa during the 1980s and 1990s and remain one of the most historically significant and contested episodes in postcolonial African economic history. Understanding both the macroeconomic problems these programs were designed to address and their significant social costs is essential to understanding subsequent debates over African development policy.

Primary Source Window

The Lagos Plan of Action for the Economic Development of Africa (1980)

Creator: Organization of African Unity heads of state and government · Date: April 1980 · Type: Continental development policy framework

Context: Adopted at an OAU summit in Lagos, Nigeria, the Lagos Plan of Action represented African leaders' own collective attempt to chart a development strategy for the continent, in explicit response to growing concern that African economies remained too dependent on former colonial trading partners and vulnerable to external economic shocks. The plan was developed partly with technical support from the United Nations Economic Commission for Africa.

The plan called for African nations to pursue greater self-reliance, reduce dependence on imported manufactured goods and external aid, expand intra-African trade and regional economic cooperation, and invest in food self-sufficiency and industrial diversification rather than continuing to rely primarily on raw material exports to markets outside the continent.

Analysis: The Lagos Plan of Action is historically significant both for what it proposed and for how limited its actual implementation proved to be. Many of its specific goals — increased intra-African trade, reduced commodity dependence, greater industrial self-sufficiency — were not achieved in the following decade, partly because the debt crisis and subsequent structural adjustment programs examined in this chapter pushed African economic policy in a different direction shortly after the plan's adoption. Nonetheless, the plan's emphasis on African self-reliance and regional integration anticipated themes that would resurface prominently decades later in initiatives such as the African Continental Free Trade Area, illustrating both the persistence of certain development aspirations across generations of African leadership and the significant gap that has often existed between development plans and their actual implementation.

Multiple Perspectives

Living Through Economic Reform and Globalization

From the perspective of a public sector worker during structural adjustment: Government employees, including teachers and healthcare workers, frequently experienced wage freezes, delayed pay, or job losses as governments reduced spending under structural adjustment conditions, even as the cost of living rose due to currency devaluation and reduced subsidies.
From the perspective of an informal-sector market trader: Many informal traders, largely operating outside formal taxation and regulation, experienced economic reform indirectly through changing consumer purchasing power, currency fluctuations affecting the cost of imported goods they sold, and, in recent decades, new opportunities created by mobile money services that simplified transactions and access to credit.
From the perspective of a technology entrepreneur in a growing urban hub: Young, often well-educated entrepreneurs in cities such as Lagos, Nairobi, and Cape Town have found significant new opportunities in globalization and digital technology, attracting international venture capital and building businesses connected to global markets, even as they navigate persistent infrastructure challenges and limited access to capital compared to entrepreneurs in wealthier regions of the world.
From the perspective of a smallholder cocoa or coffee farmer: Farmers producing export commodities have experienced globalization primarily through volatile international prices largely outside their control, unequal global value chains that capture only a small share of final consumer prices for their products, and, in some cases, new certification and fair-trade programs intended to improve their income and working conditions.
Continuity and Change Over Time

African Economies from Independence to the Present

What Changed

  • State-led industrialization gave way to market liberalization under structural adjustment, and later to more hybrid development models
  • African economies diversified their international economic partners, particularly through the rise of China as a major trading and lending partner
  • Mobile technology and digital financial services dramatically expanded financial inclusion, especially for previously unbanked populations
  • Regional and continental economic integration advanced significantly, culminating in the African Continental Free Trade Area
  • Development policy shifted from purely GDP-focused metrics toward broader human development measures

What Stayed the Same

  • Many African economies have remained heavily dependent on exporting a small number of primary commodities
  • The informal economy has continued to employ a majority of workers in many countries throughout the period
  • Inequality, both between and within African countries, has persisted despite periods of aggregate economic growth
  • External economic forces — global commodity prices, international lending terms, foreign investment decisions — have continued to significantly shape African economic conditions

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity helps students avoid two mistaken extremes: assuming African economies are permanently trapped by colonial-era structures, or assuming recent growth has fully resolved longstanding structural challenges. The historical record shows genuine transformation alongside persistent, unresolved structural conditions.

Everyday Life and Social History
Markets, Traders, and Households

Behind the macroeconomic policies and global trade statistics examined in this chapter lay the daily economic lives of hundreds of millions of African households navigating markets, work, and household budgets. Urban and rural markets — from small daily village markets to enormous urban trading centers such as Lagos's Balogun Market or Nairobi's Gikomba Market — have long served not only as centers of commerce but as vital social spaces, particularly for women traders who have historically dominated market trading across much of West and Central Africa, building businesses, credit networks, and community relationships that formal economic statistics rarely capture.

Households across the continent have developed sophisticated informal strategies for managing economic uncertainty, including rotating savings and credit associations — known by various names such as susu in West Africa or chama in East Africa — in which community members pool and rotate access to savings, providing a form of informal banking and mutual support long before formal microfinance institutions or mobile banking services existed. The arrival of mobile money has, in many communities, layered new digital tools atop these long-established informal financial practices rather than replacing them entirely. Remittances sent home by family members working in cities, other African countries, or abroad have also long constituted an essential, if often statistically underappreciated, component of household income across much of the continent, connecting local household economies directly to broader patterns of migration and the African diaspora examined in earlier chapters.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the major patterns of economic development and globalization that have shaped African economies since independence. It has traced the difficult colonial economic inheritance most African nations received at independence, the ambitious and often disappointed industrialization strategies of the 1960s and 1970s, the severe debt crises and painful structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s, the significant role of the informal economy and entrepreneurship throughout this period, the deepening globalization of African trade and investment since the 1990s, persistent patterns of inequality alongside periods of significant growth, and the ongoing debate over appropriate development models.

African economies at independence inherited economic structures, examined in Chapter 49, oriented primarily toward exporting raw materials rather than supporting diversified domestic development, alongside limited industrial capacity, limited technical education, and infrastructure built to serve colonial extraction rather than national economic integration. Newly independent governments pursued ambitious industrialization and development planning strategies intended to overcome this inheritance, achieving genuine, if partial and uneven, gains in infrastructure, education, and initial industrial development.

The global economic shocks of the 1970s and rising interest rates of the early 1980s exposed the vulnerabilities of heavily indebted, commodity-dependent African economies, triggering a severe debt crisis that forced most African governments into structural adjustment programs required by international lenders. These programs, whatever macroeconomic problems they addressed, imposed significant and often painful social costs, generating lasting debate among historians and economists about their overall balance of costs and benefits.

Throughout this period and continuing to the present, the informal economy has constituted an enormous, structurally central, and frequently underappreciated feature of African economic life, employing a majority of workers in many countries and demonstrating significant entrepreneurial resilience and adaptability, particularly among women traders and small-scale entrepreneurs, even as formal government policy and international development frameworks have often focused primarily on formal-sector economic activity.

Since the 2000s, African economies have become significantly more integrated into global trade, investment, and financial systems, driven substantially by China's rapidly expanding economic engagement with the continent, renewed commodity demand, growing foreign investment, and the rapid spread of mobile and digital technology. This globalization has produced genuine and significant economic growth and improvements in human welfare across much of the continent, while also generating new debates about debt sustainability, unequal benefit distribution, and appropriate terms of international economic engagement.

Economic growth since the 2000s has not been distributed evenly, either across African countries or within individual societies, with urban, educated, and formally employed populations generally benefiting more than rural, informal-sector, and less-educated populations, and with significant variation in growth and development outcomes depending on governance quality, resource endowments, and policy choices across different countries.

African policymakers and economists continue to debate competing development models, ranging from state-led developmental approaches to market-oriented liberalization to hybrid frameworks emphasizing African self-reliance and regional integration, reflecting genuine, unresolved disagreements about the proper role of government and markets, the pace of economic reform, and the relationship between economic growth and broader social, political, and environmental goals.

Students should understand African economic development, like African governance and conflict examined in earlier chapters, not as a single continental story of failure or triumph, but as a set of distinct national and regional economic histories, shaped by shared structural conditions yet diverging significantly according to specific policy choices, governance quality, resource endowments, and the sustained economic agency and innovation of African entrepreneurs, workers, and communities.

This chapter connects directly to Chapter 59's discussion of how conflict shapes and is shaped by economic conditions, Chapter 61's discussion of urban Africa, Chapter 62's discussion of health and education, Chapter 64's discussion of technology and innovation, and Chapter 65's discussion of Africa's global relationships, all of which intersect closely with the economic development questions examined here.

Key Terms

Development
The process of improving economic, social, and institutional conditions within a society, encompassing but not limited to economic growth.
Import substitution
A development strategy emphasizing domestic manufacturing of goods previously imported, protected through tariffs and import restrictions.
Industrialization
The process of developing manufacturing and industrial capacity within an economy.
Structural adjustment
Economic reform programs required by the IMF and World Bank in exchange for emergency financing, including devaluation, spending cuts, privatization, and trade liberalization.
Debt crisis
A situation in which a government is unable to meet its debt repayment obligations, as occurred widely across Africa in the 1980s.
Commodity dependence
Economic reliance on exporting a small number of raw materials, leaving an economy vulnerable to global price fluctuations.
Diversification
The process of expanding an economy's productive base beyond a narrow reliance on a small number of sectors or exports.
Foreign direct investment
Long-term investment by foreign entities in businesses, infrastructure, or resource extraction within a country.
Informal economy
Economic activity occurring outside formal government registration, taxation, and regulation.
Microfinance
Small-scale financial services, including loans and savings products, provided to entrepreneurs and individuals lacking access to traditional banking.
Trade liberalization
The reduction of tariffs, quotas, and other barriers to international trade.
Privatization
The transfer of ownership of enterprises from government to private hands.
Currency devaluation
A reduction in the official value of a country's currency relative to other currencies, often intended to make exports more competitive.
GDP growth
The rate of increase in a country's gross domestic product, a common but incomplete measure of economic development.
Human Development Index
A composite measure combining income, education, and health indicators to assess overall human development.
Globalization
The deepening integration of trade, investment, finance, technology, and labor across national borders.
Value chain
The sequence of activities, from raw material production to final consumer sale, through which a product gains value; African producers often capture a small share of total value chain profits.
Resource curse
The paradox in which resource-rich countries sometimes experience slower, less equitable development than resource-poor countries, due to currency effects, corruption, and reduced incentives for diversification.
Inequality
Uneven distribution of income, wealth, or opportunity within or between societies.
Entrepreneurship
The activity of starting and building businesses, often involving risk-taking and innovation.
Digital economy
Economic activity conducted through digital technology, including mobile financial services, e-commerce, and technology startups.
Regional integration
The process of reducing economic barriers and increasing cooperation among neighboring countries or regions.
Aid dependency
A condition in which a country relies heavily on foreign aid to finance government spending or development, raising concerns about sustainability and accountability.

1980 OAU development framework emphasizing African self-reliance and regional cooperation.
Washington Consensus
A set of market-oriented economic policy prescriptions widely promoted by international financial institutions during the 1980s and 1990s.
Developmental state
A government model emphasizing strategic state investment and coordination to drive rapid industrialization and export growth.
African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)
Agreement launched in 2021 aiming to create a single African market by reducing intra-African trade barriers.

  2. Research its colonial economic inheritance. Identify the primary export commodities, infrastructure, and economic structures the country inherited at independence.
  3. Trace its development strategy. Identify major development plans, industrialization efforts, and, if applicable, its experience with debt and structural adjustment.
  4. Evaluate its recent economic trajectory. Research the country's economic growth, trade relationships, and development challenges since the 2000s.
  5. Write your findings. In a 4–6 page essay, present the country's economic history, connecting it to the broader continental patterns discussed in this chapter while identifying what is distinctive about its particular path.

Library resources to use: The MGU Library's Africa economic development reference collection, World Bank and African Development Bank country reports, IMF country assessments, and scholarly journal databases on African economic history.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because economic conditions shape nearly every other dimension of African life examined throughout this textbook — governance and political stability, conflict and peace, health and education, urban growth, and Africa's evolving relationships with the rest of the world. The economic history examined here is deeply intertwined with the histories of colonialism, independence, governance, and conflict examined elsewhere in this book.

This chapter's history has shaped later African developments in profound ways. The disappointments of state-led industrialization and the painful experience of structural adjustment reshaped a generation of African economic policy thinking. The debt crisis and subsequent debt relief initiatives continue to inform debates over the terms of contemporary international lending, including the significant Chinese financing examined in Chapter 65. The resilience and scale of the informal economy continue to challenge policymakers and development institutions to rethink frameworks built primarily around formal-sector activity. And the rapid rise of mobile technology and digital financial services continues to open new economic possibilities that few observers anticipated even twenty years ago.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is that African economic development, like African political and social history more broadly, has been shaped by the interaction of structural inheritance — colonial economic patterns, global commodity markets, international financial institutions — and specific, consequential choices made by African governments, businesses, and citizens. Neither structure nor choice alone explains the continent's varied economic outcomes, and students should resist both the temptation to view African economies as permanently determined by colonial legacy and the temptation to ignore how deeply that legacy has shaped the choices available to African policymakers.

Understanding this chapter helps students see Africa's economic story as neither a simple tale of perpetual crisis nor a simple tale of inevitable rising prosperity, but as a continent of enormous economic diversity, genuine achievement and genuine hardship, persistent structural challenge and remarkable entrepreneurial innovation — a story, like so much of the history in this textbook, that remains very much unfinished and actively unfolding.

This chapter's themes connect directly forward to Chapter 61's exploration of urban Africa's economic dynamism and challenges, Chapter 64's deeper examination of technology and innovation, and Chapter 65's discussion of Africa's evolving global economic relationships — together forming a fuller picture of how African societies have pursued, and continue to pursue, economic transformation within an increasingly interconnected and rapidly changing global economy.

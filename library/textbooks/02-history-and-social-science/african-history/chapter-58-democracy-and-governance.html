Part IX — Independence, Nation Building, and the Cold War
58

Democracy and Governance

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"We have tasted freedom, and now we must decide who governs us, and how, and for how long." — a phrase echoed in national conferences, constitutional assemblies, and street protests across Africa in the decades after independence.

In February 1990, delegates gathered in Benin's capital, Cotonou, for what became known as a "national conference" — a sprawling, televised assembly of politicians, trade unionists, students, religious leaders, and ordinary citizens convened to decide the country's political future. For ten days, speaker after speaker rose to describe years of one-party rule, economic mismanagement, and political repression under President Mathieu Kérékou, who had ruled Benin since a 1972 coup. To the astonishment of many observers, the conference declared itself "sovereign," suspended the existing constitution, and set the country on a path toward multiparty elections. Kérékou, who had governed as a Marxist-Leninist strongman for nearly two decades, addressed the delegates and, in a moment that became iconic across the continent, publicly asked forgiveness for the abuses of his government. Benin's national conference did not simply change a government. It became a model that other African countries — Congo-Brazzaville, Niger, Togo, Mali, Zaire, and beyond — would study, adapt, and in some cases attempt to replicate.

This chapter is about the question that the Benin conference forced into the open: how should African nations govern themselves after independence? It is a question with no single answer, because Africa's fifty-four nations have followed dramatically different paths since the era of decolonization examined in earlier chapters. Some newly independent states experimented briefly with multiparty parliamentary systems inherited from colonial constitutions, only to see those systems collapse within a few years into one-party states or military rule. Others maintained a single dominant party for decades, arguing that unity was essential for nation building and economic development. Still others experienced repeated military coups, with soldiers seizing power in the name of ending corruption or chaos, only to entrench their own rule. Beginning in the late 1980s and accelerating through the 1990s, a wave of political change swept across the continent, as citizens, opposition movements, and international pressure combined to force many governments to accept multiparty elections, written constitutions with enforceable limits on power, and greater space for independent media and civil society.

None of these transformations occurred smoothly or completely. Many African states that adopted multiparty constitutions in the 1990s saw those constitutions bent, amended, or ignored by incumbent leaders determined to stay in power. Elections were held regularly in many countries, but not all elections were free, fair, or peaceful; some produced disputed results, violence, or entrenched one-party dominance under a multiparty label. Military coups, which many observers believed had become a relic of an earlier era by the 2000s, returned to several West African and Sahelian states in the 2020s, raising difficult questions about whether earlier gains in democratic governance were durable or fragile.

Understanding this history requires moving beyond simple judgments of success or failure. It requires asking who benefited from different systems of governance and who was excluded; what political leaders promised and what citizens actually experienced; how colonial-era institutions shaped postcolonial political possibilities; and how ordinary Africans — voters, activists, journalists, students, market women, soldiers, and civil servants — participated in, resisted, or endured the political systems under which they lived. It requires attending to both formal institutions, such as constitutions and electoral commissions, and informal practices, such as patronage networks, ethnic mobilization, and the personal authority of individual leaders, that often mattered as much or more than the written rules.

This chapter also asks students to resist two common oversimplifications. The first is the assumption that Africa has been uniquely prone to authoritarianism or instability, as though the continent's political struggles were rooted in some inherent characteristic of African societies. In fact, one-party states, military coups, and contested elections have occurred on every inhabited continent, and Africa's postcolonial political struggles must be understood in the context of colonial-era institutions, Cold War interference, weak state capacity inherited at independence, and global economic pressures — not as evidence of an essential African condition. The second oversimplification is the opposite temptation to treat every African government's departure from multiparty democracy as simply illegitimate or the product of "big man" greed, without examining the genuine governance challenges — ethnic diversity, weak infrastructure, external debt, and Cold War pressure — that shaped the choices leaders and citizens made.

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to trace the major patterns of African governance since independence: the initial experiments with inherited parliamentary systems, the widespread turn toward one-party and military rule during the 1960s through 1980s, the wave of democratization in the 1990s, and the more complicated and uneven record of the twenty-first century, including democratic backsliding, coups, and the ongoing struggle of civil society movements to hold governments accountable. Students should come away understanding that African governance is not a single story but fifty-four overlapping and distinct stories, unified by common historical pressures but diverging according to national circumstance, leadership, and the persistent agency of African citizens demanding a voice in how they are governed.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain why many African states abandoned inherited multiparty parliamentary systems in the first decades after independence.
  2. Describe the structure and justification of one-party rule as practiced across postcolonial Africa.
  3. Analyze the causes and consequences of military coups and military governments in African political history.
  4. Trace the development of written constitutions in Africa and their role in structuring or limiting political power.
  5. Evaluate the significance of the "third wave" of democratization that swept Africa beginning in the late 1980s.
  6. Assess the role of civil society organizations, churches, trade unions, and student movements in demanding political reform.
  7. Identify key human rights and accountability mechanisms developed in postcolonial African states, including truth commissions and constitutional courts.
  8. Compare the experiences of different African states in navigating the tension between central state authority and popular political pressure.
  9. Distinguish between formal democratic institutions and substantive democratic practice.
  10. Interpret primary source material relating to political transitions, national conferences, and constitutional change.
  11. Contextualize African governance struggles within global patterns of decolonization, Cold War politics, and international economic pressure.
  12. Assess ongoing debates about the durability, limits, and future of democratic governance across the African continent.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. Why did many African states move away from multiparty systems shortly after independence, and what arguments did leaders offer to justify one-party rule?
  2. What conditions made military coups a recurring feature of African political life, and how did military governments justify their seizure of power?
  3. What role did constitutions play in African governance, and why were they so often suspended, amended, or ignored?
  4. What combination of internal and external pressures produced the wave of democratic transitions in the 1990s?
  5. How effective have multiparty elections been at producing accountable government in different African states?
  6. What role have civil society organizations — churches, trade unions, student groups, and human rights organizations — played in demanding political reform?
  7. How have African states attempted to hold former leaders and officials accountable for human rights abuses, and with what success?
  8. What is the difference between formal democratic institutions and substantive democratic practice, and why does this distinction matter?
  9. Why have some African states experienced democratic backsliding or renewed military rule in the twenty-first century after earlier progress toward multiparty democracy?
  10. What does the history of African governance since independence reveal about the broader relationship between state power and popular pressure?

Historical Context

To understand the politics examined in this chapter, students must recall the circumstances under which African states became independent, discussed in earlier chapters on decolonization and nation building. Most African countries gained independence between 1957 and 1975, inheriting political institutions designed by departing colonial powers — parliaments, written constitutions, multiparty electoral systems, and independent judiciaries modeled loosely on British, French, Belgian, or Portuguese practice. These institutions were often introduced hastily in the final years of colonial rule, sometimes only months or a few years before independence itself, and were frequently poorly suited to the social and economic realities of the societies in which they were implanted.

Colonial rule had, in nearly every African territory, deliberately limited the development of broad-based political participation, national civil service capacity, and experience with representative government among the colonized population. Colonial administrations relied on small educated elites, indirect rule through traditional or appointed authorities, and coercive systems of taxation and labor control rather than democratic consent. When independence arrived, new African governments inherited weak state institutions, armies and police forces often more experienced at suppressing anticolonial protest than defending constitutional order, and societies divided by colonially reinforced ethnic and regional distinctions that political parties often mobilized for electoral advantage.

New leaders also faced enormous practical challenges: building functioning economies dependent on a handful of export commodities, providing education and healthcare to populations that colonial administrations had systematically underserved, managing artificial borders that grouped together dozens or hundreds of distinct ethnic and linguistic communities, and asserting genuine sovereignty in a world still shaped by former colonial powers' economic and military influence. The Cold War, examined in Chapter 56, added another layer of pressure, as the United States and the Soviet Union competed for influence across the continent, often supporting African leaders — regardless of their domestic human rights record — who aligned with their geopolitical interests.

Within this context, many African leaders concluded that multiparty competition, as inherited from colonial constitutions, threatened rather than supported national unity and development. Political parties in the immediate postindependence period often organized along ethnic or regional lines, and leaders argued that competitive multiparty politics risked entrenching those divisions rather than building a shared national identity. Some leaders sincerely believed that centralized, single-party government was necessary to mobilize resources for development, suppress ethnic conflict, and consolidate fragile new states. Others used these arguments as justification for entrenching their own personal power, suppressing opposition, and controlling state resources for the benefit of themselves and their allies.

By the late 1960s, a majority of African states had adopted some form of one-party rule, either through constitutional amendment, through the banning of opposition parties, or through military coups that suspended constitutional government altogether. This pattern would dominate African governance for roughly two decades before the pressures examined in this chapter began to produce a different political landscape.

Section 58.1

Postcolonial Political Systems

When African states achieved independence, most began with political systems modeled on those of their former colonial rulers. Former British colonies typically adopted Westminster-style parliamentary systems, with an elected legislature, a prime minister drawn from the majority party, and, in many cases, a ceremonial head of state. Former French colonies adopted constitutions influenced by the French Fifth Republic, often granting significant power to a directly elected president. Former Belgian and Portuguese colonies faced particularly difficult transitions, since colonial administrations had done even less to prepare local political institutions or an educated administrative class for self-government.

These inherited systems rested on assumptions that did not always match the social and political realities of postcolonial African states. Parliamentary and presidential systems modeled on European practice presumed a degree of national cohesion, administrative capacity, and shared political culture that many new states did not yet possess. Political parties that formed to contest early postindependence elections frequently organized around regional, ethnic, or religious constituencies rather than programmatic policy differences, a pattern shaped in part by colonial administrative practices that had already grouped populations along ethnic lines for purposes of taxation and indirect rule.

In several states, these tensions produced political crisis within the first years of independence. Nigeria's First Republic, established in 1960, struggled to manage the competing interests of its three (later four) major regions, each dominated by a different ethnic group, and collapsed into a military coup in January 1966, followed by a countercoup and, within a year, the outbreak of civil war (see Chapter 59 for extended discussion of postcolonial conflict). The Congo, granted rapid and poorly prepared independence by Belgium in 1960, descended into political chaos within days, as regional secession movements, a mutiny within the army, and Cold War intervention combined to produce years of instability.

Not every new state faced this level of crisis. Some, including Tanzania, Zambia, and Kenya, maintained relative political stability in their first decades, even as they moved away from multiparty competition toward various forms of centralized single-party or dominant-party rule. Botswana stood as a notable exception, maintaining regular multiparty elections and a functioning parliamentary system from independence in 1966 onward, aided by relatively strong pre-colonial governance traditions, a small and relatively homogeneous population, and the responsible management of diamond revenue discovered shortly after independence.

Across the continent, the two decades following independence saw a broad shift away from the multiparty systems introduced at independence and toward more centralized forms of governance — a shift examined in detail in the following section.

Section 58.2

One-Party Rule and Centralization

By the late 1960s and through the 1970s, a majority of independent African states had adopted formal or de facto one-party rule. This occurred through several different paths: some governments formally amended their constitutions to establish a single legal party, banning all others; some allowed opposition parties to exist in theory but used state resources, intimidation, and control of the media to prevent them from competing effectively; and some simply absorbed or suppressed rival parties until a single ruling party remained dominant in practice.

Leaders who established one-party systems typically offered several justifications. First, they argued that competitive multiparty politics, inherited from colonial constitutions, risked entrenching ethnic and regional divisions rather than building a unified national identity — a concern that, given the artificial nature of colonial borders discussed in Chapter 47, was not without foundation. Julius Nyerere of Tanzania, one of the most articulate proponents of one-party rule, argued that a single national party could accommodate vigorous internal debate and competition for leadership positions without the destabilizing effect of parties organized along ethnic lines competing for state power itself. Under Tanzania's system, candidates from the ruling Tanganyika African National Union (TANU, later Chama Cha Mapinduzi) competed against one another in local and parliamentary elections, providing a degree of internal political competition even without inter-party competition.

Second, many leaders argued that centralized political authority was necessary to mobilize scarce resources for economic development, education, and infrastructure — priorities that, they argued, could be undermined by the delays and compromises of competitive party politics. This argument drew on development theories popular during the 1960s and 1970s, which often emphasized strong state direction of the economy, and was reinforced by the examples of centralized planning in the Soviet Union and China, which some African leaders viewed as models, at least in part, for rapid development.

Third, and less openly acknowledged, one-party rule served the interests of incumbent leaders and their political allies, providing a legal framework for suppressing opposition, controlling state resources, and remaining in power indefinitely without the risk of electoral defeat. Not all leaders who adopted one-party systems were equally sincere in their stated justifications, and the practical effect in many states was the concentration of political and economic power in the hands of a president and a relatively small circle of allies, officials, and, in some cases, family members.

One-party states varied considerably in their character. Some, like Tanzania under Nyerere, combined centralized political authority with genuine efforts at social development, egalitarian rhetoric, and relatively limited use of political violence against domestic opponents, though the government did suppress dissent and imprison critics. Others, such as Malawi under Hastings Banda or Zaire under Mobutu Sese Seko, combined one-party rule with extensive use of surveillance, imprisonment, and violence against political opponents, alongside extensive personal enrichment of the ruling elite — a pattern political scientists later termed neopatrimonialism, in which formal state institutions coexisted with, and were often subordinated to, informal networks of personal loyalty and patronage centered on the ruler.

Historical Vocabulary
Neopatrimonialism

Neopatrimonialism is a term political scientists and historians use to describe political systems in which formal bureaucratic and constitutional institutions exist alongside, and are often subordinated to, informal networks of personal loyalty, patronage, and reciprocal obligation centered on a ruler or a small ruling elite. In neopatrimonial systems, state resources — jobs, contracts, licenses, development projects — are distributed not primarily according to impersonal bureaucratic rules but according to personal and political loyalty, often reinforced by ethnic, regional, or clientelist networks.

Scholars have applied this concept widely to postcolonial African states, though they caution against treating it as a uniquely African phenomenon; similar patterns of personalized rule and patronage politics have existed throughout history on every continent. Understanding neopatrimonialism helps explain why formal constitutional structures — parliaments, courts, electoral commissions — sometimes had limited practical effect on how power actually operated in many African states during the one-party era, and why simply changing formal rules through new constitutions or elections did not always change underlying patterns of governance.

The economic consequences of centralized one-party rule were mixed and varied by country. In some states, centralized planning supported real gains in education, healthcare, and infrastructure during the 1960s and early 1970s. In others, mismanagement, corruption, and the diversion of state resources toward political patronage contributed to economic stagnation, particularly as global commodity prices declined and external debt burdens grew during the 1970s and 1980s — developments examined further in Chapter 60. By the 1980s, many one-party states faced severe economic crisis, a factor that would prove significant in the political transitions of the following decade.

Section 58.3

Military Governments and Coups

Alongside one-party civilian rule, military intervention became one of the most common paths to power in postcolonial Africa. Between 1960 and 1990, African states experienced dozens of successful military coups, along with many more failed attempts. Some countries, such as Nigeria, Ghana, and Benin, experienced multiple coups and countercoups over the course of a few decades, with military and civilian governments alternating in power.

Military officers who seized power typically justified their action by pointing to civilian government failures: corruption, economic mismanagement, ethnic favoritism, or political instability. Coup leaders frequently promised to restore order, root out corruption, and eventually return the country to civilian, constitutional rule. In some cases, these promises were kept, at least in part; in others, military rulers who initially presented themselves as caretakers remained in power for decades, banning political parties, ruling by decree, and, in some cases, eventually organizing carefully managed elections that confirmed their own continued rule under a civilian guise.

Several structural factors help explain the frequency of military coups in postcolonial Africa. First, the armies inherited by new states were often disproportionately powerful relative to weak civilian institutions, having been built up by colonial powers primarily for internal security and, in some cases, having gained further training, equipment, and political confidence through participation in the world wars examined in Chapter 52. Second, weak civilian governments, often lacking broad-based legitimacy, struggled to establish clear civilian control over the military, and disputes over military pay, promotion, and resources frequently triggered unrest within the ranks that escalated into coups. Third, Cold War competition often meant that foreign powers were willing to support military governments that aligned with their strategic interests, regardless of how those governments had come to power or how they treated their own citizens.

Not all military governments were alike. Some, such as the government that came to power in Ghana under Jerry Rawlings after two separate coups in 1979 and 1981, initially combined military rule with populist rhetoric against corruption and eventually oversaw a transition to a multiparty constitution under which Rawlings himself was twice elected president in the 1990s. Others, such as the government of Idi Amin in Uganda from 1971 to 1979, combined military rule with extreme violence against political opponents and ethnic minorities, resulting in the deaths of an estimated hundreds of thousands of Ugandans before Amin was overthrown following a war with neighboring Tanzania. Still others, such as Mobutu Sese Seko's government in Zaire, combined military origins with an elaborate one-party civilian political structure and extensive personal enrichment, remaining in power for over three decades.

The prevalence of military rule declined significantly during the 1990s and 2000s, as the pressures for democratization examined later in this chapter took hold and as regional and international bodies increasingly refused to recognize governments that came to power through unconstitutional means. However, as discussed in Section 58.9, military coups returned as a significant feature of West African and Sahelian politics in the early 2020s, raising renewed questions about the underlying conditions that make military intervention in politics more or less likely.

Section 58.4

Constitutions and Political Change

Written constitutions have played a central, if often contested, role in African governance since independence. Nearly every African state adopted a formal constitution at independence, and most have adopted new constitutions, or extensively amended existing ones, at least once since then — in some cases many times. Constitutions have defined the structure of government, the powers of the executive, legislature, and judiciary, the rights of citizens, and, crucially, the rules governing how political power may be gained, exercised, and transferred.

The historical record shows that constitutions in many African states functioned less as fixed, enforceable limits on power and more as documents that could be, and frequently were, amended to serve the interests of those already in power. A common pattern involved incumbent presidents seeking constitutional amendments to remove or extend term limits, expand executive authority, or restrict the activities of opposition parties. Scholars and civil society organizations have tracked dozens of such amendments across the continent since the 1990s, and the removal or attempted removal of presidential term limits has become one of the most closely watched indicators of democratic backsliding in African politics.

At the same time, constitutions have also served as important tools for reform and accountability. The negotiated constitutions that emerged from national conferences and transition processes in the early 1990s, discussed in Section 58.5, frequently introduced stronger protections for political pluralism, judicial independence, and human rights than the constitutions they replaced. South Africa's 1996 constitution, drafted through an extensive and inclusive negotiation process following the end of apartheid, is widely regarded as one of the most progressive constitutional documents in the world, with an entrenched bill of rights, an independent Constitutional Court, and detailed protections against discrimination.

Constitutional courts and judiciaries have, in some African states, played an increasingly assertive role in constraining executive power, striking down unconstitutional actions, and, in a small number of high-profile cases, invalidating disputed presidential election results — as Kenya's Supreme Court did in 2017, when it annulled the results of that year's presidential election and ordered a new vote, citing irregularities in the electoral process. Such judicial interventions remain relatively rare, however, and judiciaries in many African states continue to face pressure, underfunding, or political interference that limits their practical independence.

Section 58.5

Elections and Multiparty Politics

The late 1980s and early 1990s produced one of the most significant shifts in African political history: a widespread movement away from one-party and military rule toward multiparty elections. Political scientists often describe this period as part of a global "third wave of democratization," a term coined to describe the spread of democratic transitions across Southern Europe, Latin America, East Asia, Eastern Europe, and Africa beginning in the mid-1970s and accelerating dramatically after 1989.

Historical Vocabulary
Third Wave of Democratization

The third wave of democratization refers to a global pattern, identified by political scientist Samuel Huntington, of transitions from authoritarian to democratic government occurring in successive regional clusters from the mid-1970s through the 1990s. In Africa, the third wave arrived somewhat later than in Southern Europe or Latin America, gathering force especially after 1989–1990, and produced multiparty elections, new constitutions, and expanded political freedoms in dozens of African states within a few years.

Historians caution against treating the third wave as a single uniform process. The pace, depth, and durability of democratic change varied enormously by country, and in many cases the "wave" produced formal multiparty competition without necessarily producing substantive changes in how power actually operated — a distinction examined further in Section 58.9.

Several factors converged to produce this wave of change in Africa specifically. The end of the Cold War, symbolized by the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989, removed much of the superpower incentive to prop up authoritarian African allies regardless of their domestic governance, and Western donor governments and international financial institutions increasingly tied foreign aid and loans to conditions requiring political liberalization alongside the economic reforms discussed in Chapter 60. Severe economic crises in many one-party and military states during the 1980s undermined the legitimacy of governments that had justified their rule partly on the basis of delivering development. And, crucially, sustained pressure from within African societies themselves — from trade unions, churches, student movements, lawyers' associations, and increasingly organized opposition politicians — created domestic political momentum for change that external pressure alone could not have produced.

Benin's national conference of February 1990, described in the chapter opening, became an influential model, particularly for Francophone African states. Similar national conferences were convened in Congo-Brazzaville, Niger, Togo, Mali, and Zaire (though with varying degrees of success — Togo's and Zaire's conferences, for example, ultimately failed to produce lasting political change, as incumbent leaders Gnassingbé Eyadéma and Mobutu Sese Seko each managed to retain power despite the conferences' initial momentum). In Zambia, sustained pressure from a coalition of trade unions, businesspeople, and civil society organizations led by former union leader Frederick Chiluba forced President Kenneth Kaunda, who had ruled since independence in 1964, to accept multiparty elections in 1991, which Chiluba's Movement for Multi-Party Democracy won decisively.

By the mid-1990s, the great majority of African states had adopted multiparty constitutions and held at least one competitive election. The quality and consequences of these elections, however, varied enormously. In some countries, multiparty elections produced genuine transfers of power between rival parties, increasingly robust institutions, and meaningful political competition. In others, ruling parties retained power through a combination of legitimate popular support, incumbency advantages, control of state media and resources, and, in some cases, outright manipulation or fraud — producing what political scientists term "hybrid regimes," discussed further in Section 58.9.

Section 58.6

Civil Society and Reform

The democratic transitions of the 1990s were not simply granted from above by governments responding to external pressure; they were, in most cases, actively demanded by organized groups within African societies. Trade unions, particularly in states with significant formal-sector employment such as Zambia and South Africa, mobilized workers around both economic grievances and demands for political liberalization. Christian and Islamic religious leaders and institutions, which had often maintained a degree of independence from state control even under authoritarian governments, provided organizational infrastructure, moral authority, and physical space for opposition organizing in many countries. Roman Catholic bishops, for example, played prominent roles in Benin's and Zaire's national conferences.

University students and faculty were frequently at the forefront of protest movements demanding political change, continuing a tradition of student activism that stretched back to the anticolonial movements examined in Chapter 53. Student protests in Kenya, Zambia, Malawi, and elsewhere during the late 1980s and early 1990s repeatedly challenged government authority, often at significant personal risk, and were met in many cases with violent government crackdowns, arrests, and campus closures.

Independent and opposition media, though frequently subject to censorship, harassment, and violence, played an increasingly important role in exposing government corruption and mismanagement and providing platforms for opposition voices. The expansion of independent newspapers, and later radio and television stations, in the 1990s marked a significant departure from the state-controlled media environments typical of the one-party era. Human rights organizations, both African and international, documented abuses and advocated for political prisoners, contributing to both domestic and international pressure for reform.

Women's organizations, though not always centered in mainstream political histories of this period, played significant roles in many countries' reform movements, organizing around both specific political demands and broader questions of representation and rights. In several states, including Rwanda and South Africa, postconflict and post-transition constitutional processes explicitly incorporated gender quotas or representation requirements, producing some of the highest rates of female parliamentary representation in the world by the early twenty-first century — Rwanda's parliament, notably, became the first in the world to have a majority of women members.

Civil society's role did not end with the initial transitions of the 1990s. In subsequent decades, election monitoring organizations, anti-corruption watchdogs, legal aid societies, and advocacy groups have continued to press for government accountability, sometimes achieving significant victories — such as successful legal challenges to disputed elections or corrupt government contracts — and sometimes facing renewed repression as governments sought to limit the space available for independent political activity.

Section 58.7

Human Rights and Accountability

The transitions of the 1990s raised difficult questions about how new governments should address human rights abuses committed under previous regimes. Some countries pursued formal accountability mechanisms; others prioritized political stability and national unity over pursuing accountability for past abuses, sometimes granting amnesty to former officials in exchange for their cooperation with the transition or their peaceful relinquishment of power.

South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), established in 1995 under the leadership of Archbishop Desmond Tutu, became the most internationally influential model of transitional justice to emerge from Africa. The TRC held public hearings in which victims of apartheid-era violence could testify about their experiences, and it offered conditional amnesty to perpetrators who fully disclosed politically motivated crimes committed during the apartheid era. The commission's approach — prioritizing truth-telling and reconciliation over criminal prosecution — was controversial, with critics arguing that it denied many victims meaningful justice, while supporters argued that it made possible a peaceful transition that might otherwise have been derailed by fear of prosecution among the outgoing white minority government and security establishment.

Other African states adopted different approaches. Rwanda, following the 1994 genocide examined in Chapter 59, pursued both international prosecution through the United Nations-established International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda and a massive domestic community justice process known as gacaca, which tried hundreds of thousands of genocide-related cases at the local level. Sierra Leone and Liberia, following their civil wars, each established truth and reconciliation commissions alongside, in Sierra Leone's case, a hybrid international-domestic court that prosecuted the most serious offenders.

Accountability efforts have faced significant limits and controversies across the continent. Former heads of state have rarely faced meaningful prosecution for human rights abuses committed while in office, and the International Criminal Court, based in The Hague, has faced sustained criticism from many African governments and the African Union for what critics describe as a disproportionate focus on African defendants relative to alleged perpetrators elsewhere in the world — a controversy examined further in Chapter 63. Domestic anti-corruption and human rights institutions established in many states following the transitions of the 1990s have often struggled with inadequate funding, political interference, and, in some cases, direct pressure from the governments they were meant to hold accountable.

Section 58.8

State Power and Popular Pressure

Underlying the specific institutional developments examined in this chapter is a broader and enduring tension in African political history: the relationship between the power of the central state and the pressure exerted by ordinary citizens, organized groups, and local communities. This tension predates independence — it shaped African responses to colonial rule, examined in Chapter 50 — and it has continued to shape governance in the decades since.

African states inherited from colonial rule administrative structures oriented primarily toward extraction and control rather than responsiveness to popular demands, and many postcolonial governments, whether one-party, military, or nominally multiparty, continued to rely on centralized authority, patronage networks, and, when necessary, coercion to maintain power. At the same time, African citizens have consistently found ways to exert pressure on those in power, whether through formal channels such as voting, litigation, and organized advocacy, or through informal channels such as strikes, protests, boycotts, migration, and the cultivation of alternative sources of authority and support outside the formal state — including religious institutions, traditional leadership structures, and community organizations.

This dynamic helps explain why formal changes in governance structure — the adoption of a new constitution, the holding of a multiparty election — have not always produced correspondingly deep changes in how power actually operates. Where underlying patterns of patronage, informal authority, and weak state capacity for delivering public services remain largely intact, new formal institutions may coexist uneasily with older informal practices, producing outcomes that are neither fully authoritarian nor fully democratic in practice, whatever the formal constitutional label suggests.

At the same time, this dynamic also explains the genuine and repeated success of popular pressure in producing real political change across the continent's history — from the anticolonial movements of the mid-twentieth century, through the civil society-led transitions of the 1990s, to more recent protest movements demanding accountability, respect for term limits, or an end to specific abusive practices. The relationship between state power and popular pressure in African governance is not fixed; it has shifted repeatedly over the decades in response to economic conditions, international pressure, and the organized action of African citizens themselves.

Section 58.9

Democratic Transitions and Limits

The democratic gains of the 1990s did not follow a single, linear trajectory toward consolidated multiparty democracy across the continent. By the early 2000s, scholars of African politics increasingly distinguished between states that had achieved relatively consolidated democratic systems — with genuine competition, peaceful transfers of power between rival parties, and reasonably independent institutions — and states that had adopted the formal apparatus of multiparty democracy, including regular elections, while maintaining, in practice, dominant-party or personalized rule through incumbency advantages, control of state resources and media, weak opposition, and, in some cases, targeted repression or electoral manipulation.

Ghana, Benin, Senegal, Botswana, Mauritius, South Africa, and Zambia are frequently cited among the continent's relatively successful cases of democratic consolidation, each having experienced at least one peaceful transfer of power between rival political parties through elections since the 1990s. Other states, including several with regular multiparty elections, have experienced far more limited genuine political competition, with ruling parties or individual leaders remaining continuously in power for decades under a multiparty label.

The removal or extension of presidential term limits has become a particularly closely watched indicator of democratic backsliding. Since the 1990s, dozens of African leaders have sought constitutional amendments to remove or extend term limits that would otherwise have required them to leave office, and a significant number have succeeded, sometimes provoking significant popular protest. In a smaller number of cases — including Burkina Faso in 2014 and Senegal in 2012 — sustained popular protest successfully blocked attempted term-limit extensions, forcing incumbent presidents from power or from seeking a prohibited additional term.

Beginning around 2020, a distinct and troubling pattern reemerged: a wave of military coups across West Africa and the Sahel, including in Mali (2020 and 2021), Guinea (2021), Burkina Faso (2022, twice), Niger (2023), and Gabon (2023). These coups were often initially met with significant domestic popular support, reflecting widespread frustration with corruption, economic hardship, and, in the Sahelian cases, the failure of civilian governments to contain jihadist insurgencies (examined in Chapter 59). Regional bodies, particularly the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS), condemned the coups and imposed sanctions, and in Niger's case threatened military intervention to restore the elected government, though ultimately did not carry out that threat. The wave of coups renewed a long-standing debate among historians and political scientists about the durability of Africa's post-1990 democratic gains and the underlying conditions — weak state capacity, security crises, economic hardship, and public frustration with perceived elite corruption — that continue to make military intervention in politics a recurring possibility in parts of the continent.

At the same time, other regions of the continent have continued to see meaningful democratic consolidation, competitive elections, and, in several cases, the peaceful removal of long-serving leaders through electoral defeat rather than coup or protest. The overall picture of African governance in the twenty-first century, in other words, remains sharply uneven — a pattern that resists simple continent-wide generalization and requires attention to specific national and regional circumstances.

Primary Source Window

Declaration of a National Conference (Composite Example, Francophone Africa, 1990–1991)

Creator: Delegates to a national sovereign conference · Date: 1990–1991 · Type: Political declaration / transitional governing document

Context: Between 1990 and 1991, several Francophone African states — including Benin, Congo-Brazzaville, Niger, Togo, and Mali — convened large national conferences bringing together government officials, opposition politicians, trade unionists, religious leaders, and civil society representatives to negotiate a transition away from one-party or military rule. These conferences frequently declared themselves "sovereign," meaning they claimed authority superior to the existing government for the duration of the transition process, and produced declarations outlining new transitional governing arrangements and timelines for constitutional reform and elections.

Such declarations typically affirmed the conference's sovereign authority for the transition period, established an interim government or prime minister independent of the existing head of state, set a timeline for drafting a new constitution establishing multiparty competition, and called for the organization of free and fair elections within a specified period, often accompanied by a general amnesty for political prisoners and a commitment to freedom of the press and of political association.

Analysis: These declarations reveal both the ambitions and the fragility of Africa's early-1990s democratic transitions. They demonstrate the remarkable degree of coordinated civil society and political organization that produced them, bringing together groups — trade unions, churches, students, opposition politicians, and sometimes even members of the outgoing government — that had often previously been suppressed or divided. At the same time, the fact that some national conferences (such as those in Togo and Zaire) ultimately failed to produce lasting political change, while others (such as Benin's) succeeded, illustrates that the declarations themselves guaranteed nothing; their success depended on the ongoing balance of political power, international pressure, and continued civic mobilization after the conference concluded.

Questions for students:

  1. What does the phenomenon of a "sovereign national conference" suggest about how political legitimacy was understood during this transitional period?
  2. Who was included in these conferences, and why might that inclusiveness have mattered for their outcomes?
  3. Why might some national conferences have succeeded in producing lasting political change while others failed?
  4. What additional evidence would historians need to evaluate how effectively these declarations were implemented in practice?
Historical Debate

Should Newly Independent African States Have Prioritized Democracy or Development First?

The question: Many of Africa's early postcolonial leaders argued that centralized, one-party governance was more likely to produce rapid economic development and national unity than competitive multiparty politics, which they associated with instability, ethnic mobilization, and slow decision-making. Other leaders, activists, and later historians have argued that centralized rule undermined both accountability and long-term development by removing checks on corruption and mismanagement. Historians continue to debate which approach, if either, better served African development and stability.

Interpretation A — Centralized Rule as a Necessary Development Strategy: Some historians and political scientists argue that the weak state capacity, ethnic diversity, and colonial-era divisions inherited by new African states made competitive multiparty politics genuinely destabilizing in the short term, and that centralized authority allowed some governments — Tanzania under Nyerere, for example — to mobilize resources for education and infrastructure more effectively than fragmented multiparty competition might have permitted.
Interpretation B — Centralized Rule as a Barrier to Accountability and Development: Other historians argue that centralized, unaccountable rule removed the checks — a free press, opposition scrutiny, independent courts — that might have constrained corruption and mismanagement, and that many one-party and military states used development rhetoric to justify what was, in practice, entrenchment of personal and elite power at the expense of broader economic and social progress.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to real gains in education and infrastructure in some centralized states during the 1960s and 1970s, and to the instability that afflicted several states that maintained more open political competition in this period. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the severe economic crises many one-party and military states experienced by the 1980s, the extensive corruption documented in states like Zaire, and the relative success of more consistently democratic states like Botswana.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians evaluate the choices made by Africa's first postindependence generation of leaders, and it continues to inform contemporary debates about the relationship between governance structure and development outcomes.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and why? Can elements of both interpretations be true for different states or different time periods?

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing One-Party Rule in Postcolonial Africa

From the perspective of a ruling party official: One-party rule represented a necessary and legitimate strategy for building national unity out of ethnically diverse populations, mobilizing resources for development, and avoiding the destabilizing effects of politicized ethnic competition. Many officials sincerely believed centralized governance served the national interest, even as it also served their own political and economic advantage.
From the perspective of an opposition politician or activist: One-party rule represented the suppression of legitimate political competition, the silencing of dissent, and, in many cases, imprisonment, exile, or violence directed against those who challenged the ruling party. Opposition figures frequently argued that claims of national unity masked the entrenchment of a narrow governing elite's power and access to state resources.
From the perspective of an ordinary rural farmer: For many rural citizens, the practical experience of one-party rule was shaped less by abstract debates over political theory than by concrete questions: whether roads, schools, and clinics reached their community; whether local officials demanded bribes or favoritism; and whether the state's presence in their daily lives felt supportive or extractive. Experiences varied enormously depending on the specific government, region, and period.
From the perspective of an international Cold War power: Both the United States and the Soviet Union frequently prioritized the strategic alignment of African governments over their domestic governance practices, providing military and economic support to one-party and military governments — of varying ideological orientations — that served their geopolitical interests, regardless of how those governments treated their own citizens.
Cause and Effect

Africa's Democratic Transitions of the Early 1990s

Long-Term Causes

Decades of accumulated frustration with corruption, repression, and economic mismanagement under one-party and military rule; growth of educated urban populations, trade unions, and civil society organizations; long tradition of student and labor activism dating to the anticolonial period.

Short-Term Causes

End of the Cold War and collapse of superpower patronage for authoritarian allies; severe debt and economic crises of the 1980s; donor pressure tying aid to political liberalization; Benin's 1990 national conference providing a widely emulated model.

The Event

Wave of national conferences, constitutional reforms, and multiparty elections across dozens of African states between roughly 1990 and 1994, alongside South Africa's negotiated transition from apartheid.

Effects

New constitutions with expanded rights and, in some cases, term limits; expanded space for independent media and civil society; some genuine transfers of power between parties; but also, in many states, the emergence of "hybrid regimes" and, decades later, renewed coups in parts of West Africa and the Sahel.

Historical complexity: The democratic wave of the 1990s cannot be reduced to a single cause — domestic pressure, Cold War geopolitics, and economic crisis all interacted, and the resulting political changes proved far more durable in some states than in others.

Continuity and Change Over Time

African Governance from Independence to the Present

What Changed

  • Most states moved from inherited multiparty systems to one-party or military rule by the 1970s
  • Beginning around 1990, most states adopted new multiparty constitutions and held competitive elections
  • Independent media, civil society organizations, and judicial review expanded significantly after the 1990s in many states
  • Truth commissions and other accountability mechanisms emerged in several post-transition states
  • A renewed wave of military coups affected several West African and Sahelian states beginning in 2020

What Stayed the Same

  • Patronage networks and informal authority have continued to shape how power actually operates in many states, regardless of formal constitutional structure
  • Weak state capacity to deliver public services has remained a persistent challenge across much of the continent
  • Tension between central state authority and popular pressure from below has remained a defining feature of African political life across every period examined in this chapter
  • External actors — from Cold War superpowers to contemporary donor governments, international financial institutions, and regional bodies — have continued to shape the incentives facing African governments

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing both change and continuity helps students avoid both excessive optimism about the permanence of democratic gains and excessive pessimism about the possibility of meaningful political reform — both of which are contradicted by the actual historical record.

Everyday Life and Social History
Citizens and the State in Daily Life

Behind the large-scale political transformations examined in this chapter lay the daily experiences of millions of ordinary Africans navigating relationships with the state. Under one-party and military governments, obtaining a government job, a business license, a place at a state university, or even basic services like water or electricity connections often depended as much on personal or political connections as on formal entitlement — a lived reality of neopatrimonial governance discussed in Section 58.2. Market women, farmers, teachers, and civil servants developed practical strategies for navigating bureaucracies that were often underfunded, unpredictable, and shaped by informal expectations of favor-trading or small bribes, sometimes termed "the fees for services that are supposed to be free."

The expansion of multiparty politics and independent media after the 1990s changed daily political life in tangible ways for many citizens: newspapers and radio stations openly criticized government officials in ways that would have been dangerous a decade earlier; citizens could, in many though not all states, join opposition parties or civil society organizations without automatic fear of arrest; and election days became genuine moments of political choice rather than ritual confirmation of a predetermined outcome. At the same time, many of the daily frustrations of navigating an underfunded and sometimes arbitrary state bureaucracy persisted across the political transitions examined in this chapter, reminding students that formal political change does not automatically transform the everyday texture of citizens' relationship with government institutions.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the evolution of African governance from the inherited multiparty constitutions of the independence era through the widespread turn toward one-party and military rule during the 1960s through 1980s, the remarkable wave of democratic transitions beginning around 1990, and the uneven and continuing struggle to consolidate accountable, responsive governance in the decades since.

Newly independent African states inherited political institutions poorly matched to the social, economic, and administrative realities left by colonial rule. Weak state capacity, ethnically organized political competition, and Cold War pressures combined to push most African governments away from the multiparty systems established at independence and toward centralized one-party or military rule by the 1970s. Leaders justified this centralization through appeals to national unity and development, though centralized power also served the personal and political interests of ruling elites, producing patterns of governance that scholars term neopatrimonial.

Beginning in the late 1980s and accelerating dramatically after 1989, a combination of domestic civil society pressure — from trade unions, churches, students, and opposition politicians — and international factors, including the end of the Cold War and donor pressure for political liberalization, produced a wave of democratic transitions across the continent. Benin's 1990 national conference became an influential model, and by the mid-1990s the great majority of African states had adopted multiparty constitutions and held competitive elections.

The record of these transitions has been genuinely mixed. Some states achieved meaningful democratic consolidation, including peaceful transfers of power between rival parties, expanded press freedom, and increasingly independent judiciaries. Others adopted the formal apparatus of multiparty democracy while maintaining, in practice, dominant-party or personalized rule through incumbency advantages and, in some cases, manipulation or repression — a pattern political scientists describe as hybrid regimes.

Accountability mechanisms, including South Africa's influential Truth and Reconciliation Commission and various national and international courts, have attempted to address human rights abuses committed under previous authoritarian governments, with mixed success and considerable ongoing controversy, particularly regarding the International Criminal Court's relationship with African states.

Beginning around 2020, a renewed wave of military coups in Mali, Guinea, Burkina Faso, Niger, and Gabon has raised serious questions about the durability of Africa's post-1990 democratic gains, reflecting persistent underlying challenges of weak state capacity, security crises, and public frustration with perceived governmental failure.

Throughout this history, a persistent tension between centralized state authority and popular pressure from below has shaped African governance across every period examined. This tension is not unique to Africa, though it has taken particular forms shaped by the continent's colonial history, ethnic diversity, Cold War experience, and economic circumstances.

Students should understand African governance since independence not as a single continental story of either progress or failure, but as fifty-four distinct national trajectories, shaped by common historical pressures yet diverging significantly according to national leadership, economic circumstance, civil society strength, and international context.

The history of democracy and governance in Africa remains, as this chapter's title suggests, an unfinished story — one in which the outcomes of the 2020s coup wave, ongoing struggles over term limits and electoral integrity, and the continued growth of civil society and independent institutions will shape the continent's political future in ways historians cannot yet fully assess.

This chapter connects directly to the discussions of conflict and peace in Chapter 59, economic development in Chapter 60, and continental cooperation through the African Union in Chapter 63, all of which intersect closely with the governance questions examined here.

Review Materials

Key Terms

One-party state
A political system in which a single political party is legally or effectively the only party permitted to hold power, common across Africa from the late 1960s through the 1980s.
Multiparty system
A political system in which multiple political parties legally compete for power through elections.
Coup d'état
The sudden, illegal seizure of political power, typically by military officers, often justified by claims of restoring order or ending corruption.
Military government
A government led by military officers who have seized power outside constitutional processes, often ruling by decree.
Constitutionalism
The principle that government power should be structured, limited, and made accountable through a written constitution and the rule of law.
National conference
A large assembly of political and civil society representatives convened in several Francophone African states around 1990 to negotiate a transition from authoritarian to multiparty rule.
Civil society
Organizations and associations — trade unions, churches, student groups, human rights organizations — operating independently of the state and often advocating for political reform.
Neopatrimonialism
A governance pattern in which formal state institutions coexist with, and are often subordinated to, informal networks of personal loyalty and patronage.
Patronage
The distribution of jobs, favors, or resources by those in power in exchange for political loyalty or support.
Third wave of democratization
A global pattern of transitions from authoritarian to democratic government occurring in regional clusters from the mid-1970s through the 1990s, including in Africa.
Hybrid regime
A government that maintains formal multiparty elections and democratic institutions while limiting genuine political competition in practice.
Incumbency advantage
The political benefits available to a sitting government, including control of state resources and media, that make it easier for incumbents to win elections regardless of performance.
Electoral commission
A body responsible for organizing and overseeing elections; its independence is often a key indicator of electoral fairness.
Term limits
Constitutional restrictions on how long an official may serve in a particular office, frequently a focal point of political conflict in African governance.
Truth and Reconciliation Commission
A body established to investigate past human rights abuses, often offering conditional amnesty in exchange for truthful disclosure, most famously used in post-apartheid South Africa.
Human rights
Rights considered fundamental to all people, including protections against arbitrary imprisonment, torture, and denial of political participation.
Accountability
The principle that those who hold power should be answerable for their actions, whether through elections, courts, media scrutiny, or other mechanisms.
Popular sovereignty
The principle that legitimate political authority derives from the consent and will of the people.
Unconstitutional change of government
A term used by regional bodies such as the African Union and ECOWAS to describe coups and other seizures of power outside constitutional processes.
Democratic backsliding
A gradual erosion of democratic institutions and practices, often through legal or quasi-legal means such as constitutional amendment or electoral manipulation.
Personal rule
A pattern of governance in which political authority is concentrated in and dependent upon a single leader rather than institutionalized structures.
State capacity
The ability of a government to effectively implement policy, deliver services, and enforce laws across its territory.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because governance shapes nearly every other dimension of a society's historical experience — its economic development, its capacity to prevent or manage conflict, its protection or violation of human rights, and the degree to which ordinary citizens can meaningfully participate in decisions that affect their lives. The struggles over democracy and governance examined in this chapter are not separate from the histories of colonialism, independence, the Cold War, and economic development examined elsewhere in this textbook; they are deeply intertwined with all of them.

This chapter's history shaped later African developments in profound ways. The patterns of governance established during the one-party and military eras left institutional legacies — weak checks on executive power, patronage-based political systems, underdeveloped independent judiciaries — that continued to shape politics even after formal democratic transitions in the 1990s. Conversely, the civil society organizations, independent media, and constitutional protections that emerged from those transitions have provided crucial tools for the ongoing struggles over accountability, term limits, and electoral integrity examined in the chapter's final sections.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is the persistent, unresolved tension between concentrated state power and popular demands for voice and accountability — a tension found throughout world history but shaped in Africa by the particular legacies of colonial rule, Cold War geopolitics, and the challenge of building durable political institutions in enormously diverse societies within a matter of decades. This tension does not resolve neatly in favor of either centralization or democratization; it continues to play out differently across the continent's fifty-four nations.

Understanding this chapter helps students see Africa as neither uniformly authoritarian nor uniformly democratic, neither a story of hopeless political dysfunction nor one of simple linear progress. It is, instead, a continent of diverse and actively contested political struggles, in which citizens, civil society organizations, political leaders, and international actors continue to shape outcomes that remain genuinely open and unresolved. The coup wave of the 2020s and the enduring examples of democratic consolidation in states like Ghana, Botswana, and South Africa exist side by side, reminding students that the history examined in this chapter is very much still being written.

This chapter's themes connect directly forward to Chapter 59's examination of conflict and peace, Chapter 60's discussion of economic development, and Chapter 63's exploration of the African Union's evolving role in responding to governance crises — together forming a fuller picture of how African nations have navigated the challenges of building durable, accountable, and responsive political institutions since independence.

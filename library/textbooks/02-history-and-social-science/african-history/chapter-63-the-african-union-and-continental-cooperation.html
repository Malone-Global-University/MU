The African Union and Continental Cooperation

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"We are Africans first, and it is only as Africans that we can hope to solve our problems." — a conviction repeated by African leaders across generations, embodying the ideal that would eventually take institutional form in the Organization of African Unity and the African Union.

On May 25, 1963, thirty-two heads of state and government gathered in Addis Ababa, Ethiopia, to sign a charter creating the Organization of African Unity. Many of the nations they represented had won independence only months or a few years earlier. Others were still fighting for it. The delegates who assembled in the newly built Africa Hall came from states with different colonial histories, different languages of administration, different economic systems, and, in some cases, deep and unresolved border disputes with one another. Yet they shared a conviction that had been building for decades among African intellectuals, exiles, trade unionists, and political leaders scattered across Africa, the Caribbean, and the Americas: that the peoples of Africa, having endured centuries of the slave trade and generations of colonial partition, needed institutions that could speak and act for the continent as a whole, and that African unity was not simply a sentimental aspiration but a practical necessity for defending hard-won sovereignty against renewed external domination.

This chapter examines that idea and the long, difficult, and still unfinished effort to build institutions capable of realizing it. It traces the intellectual roots of Pan-Africanism in the writings and activism of thinkers such as W. E. B. Du Bois, Marcus Garvey, George Padmore, and Kwame Nkrumah; the creation of the Organization of African Unity in 1963 as a compromise between competing visions of unity; the OAU's decades of work on decolonization, apartheid, and non-interference; the organization's frequent inability to stop wars, coups, and humanitarian catastrophes on the continent it claimed to represent; and the transformation of the OAU into the African Union in 2002, an institution with a more ambitious mandate, new instruments for intervention, and new aspirations for economic integration.

The chapter also examines the regional economic communities — ECOWAS in West Africa, SADC in Southern Africa, the East African Community, ECCAS in Central Africa, IGAD in the Horn, and others — that have often done more of the practical work of African cooperation than continental bodies, mediating conflicts, deploying peacekeepers, and building frameworks for trade. It considers the African Union's peace and security architecture, its development frameworks such as the New Partnership for Africa's Development and Agenda 2063, and the ambitious African Continental Free Trade Area launched in the early twenty-first century. It asks what continental cooperation has actually achieved, where it has failed, and why.

Understanding this history requires holding two truths together. The first is that African unity has always been more than a bureaucratic project. It has been a moral and political response to centuries of external domination — the slave trade, colonial partition, and the persistence of foreign economic and military influence after independence — and a genuine expression of solidarity among peoples who share overlapping histories of dispossession and resistance. The second is that continental institutions have consistently struggled to overcome the very sovereignty of the states that created them. Newly independent African governments, having just won the right to govern their own territories, were often unwilling to surrender meaningful authority to a supranational body, even one built in the name of African solidarity. This tension between unity and sovereignty runs through the entire history examined in this chapter.

Students should also understand that the story of African continental cooperation is not a story of steady progress toward an inevitable goal. It has included real achievements: the near-total elimination of colonial rule and apartheid from the continent, the establishment of continental human rights instruments, the deployment of African-led peacekeeping missions, and the creation of a free trade area encompassing more than fifty countries. It has also included real failures: the OAU's reluctance to intervene in the Rwandan genocide of 1994, the difficulty African institutions have faced in preventing coups and civil wars, and the persistent gap between the ambitious language of continental summits and the resources and political will available to act on it.

Finally, this chapter connects continental cooperation to the everyday lives of ordinary Africans. Decisions made in Addis Ababa about free movement of people, trade tariffs, currency union, and peacekeeping deployments affect traders crossing borders, families divided by colonial boundaries, refugees fleeing conflict, and workers seeking opportunity beyond their home countries. The African Union is not only a diplomatic institution; it is an attempt, however imperfect, to reorganize the political and economic space of an entire continent in ways that shape daily life.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand both the aspirations behind African continental cooperation and the structural obstacles that have limited its achievements — and should be equipped to evaluate, using historical evidence, how far Africa has come toward the unity its founders imagined in 1963.

Historical Context

The idea that Africans and people of African descent shared a common destiny and needed to organize collectively did not begin in Addis Ababa in 1963. It emerged over the preceding century, largely outside the African continent itself, among Black intellectuals and activists in the Americas, the Caribbean, and Europe who were grappling with the aftermath of slavery, the persistence of racism, and the ongoing colonization of Africa. This movement came to be known as Pan-Africanism, and its development is essential background for understanding the institutions this chapter examines.

In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, figures such as Henry Sylvester Williams, a Trinidadian lawyer, organized the first Pan-African Conference in London in 1900, bringing together delegates to protest colonial exploitation and racial discrimination. The American sociologist and historian W. E. B. Du Bois organized a series of Pan-African Congresses beginning in 1919, which called for greater self-government for African colonies and an end to racial discrimination worldwide. The Jamaican activist Marcus Garvey built the largest Black nationalist movement of the era through the Universal Negro Improvement Association, promoting Black pride, economic self-sufficiency, and, eventually, a vision of African redemption that inspired millions across the diaspora, even though Garvey himself never set foot on the African continent.

The 1945 Pan-African Congress held in Manchester, England, marked a decisive shift. Organized in the aftermath of the Second World War, it was dominated for the first time by African participants rather than diaspora intellectuals, including a young Gold Coast activist named Kwame Nkrumah and the Kenyan nationalist Jomo Kenyatta. The Manchester Congress explicitly demanded independence for African colonies, not merely reform of colonial administration, and it helped launch the generation of leaders who would achieve independence across the continent over the following two decades.

When Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African colony to achieve independence in 1957, Nkrumah declared that Ghana's independence was "meaningless unless it is linked up with the total liberation of the African continent." He became the most visible advocate of rapid, far-reaching continental unification — including, in his most ambitious formulations, a single continental government with a unified military and currency. Nkrumah hosted the first Conference of Independent African States in Accra in 1958 and the All-African Peoples' Conference later that year, both of which brought together African leaders and liberation movements to coordinate strategy.

By the early 1960s, as more African states achieved independence, sharp disagreements emerged over what form unity should take. These disagreements crystallized into two loose blocs. The "Casablanca Group," led by Ghana, Guinea, Mali, Egypt, Morocco, and Libya, favored rapid and far-reaching political federation, a common African defense force, and a unified approach to economic planning. The "Monrovia Group," a larger and more diverse coalition that included Nigeria, most of the former French colonies, Ethiopia, and Liberia, favored a looser association of sovereign states that would cooperate on specific issues — decolonization, economic development, cultural exchange — without surrendering national sovereignty to a supranational authority.

This division reflected deeper realities. Many of the new states, having just emerged from colonial rule and often possessing fragile institutions, weak economies, and borders drawn by European powers with little regard for African political geography, were unwilling to cede newly won sovereignty to another external or supranational authority, even one composed of fellow Africans. Leaders governing multi-ethnic states assembled from arbitrary colonial borders worried that rapid continental federation might destabilize their own fragile national unity before it had been consolidated. Ethiopia's Emperor Haile Selassie, whose country had never been colonized and whose diplomatic weight was considerable, played a central role in brokering a compromise between the two blocs at the founding conference in Addis Ababa in May 1963.

The organization that emerged from that conference — the Organization of African Unity — reflected the Monrovia vision far more than the Casablanca vision. It was built around principles of sovereign equality, non-interference in internal affairs, and voluntary cooperation rather than supranational authority. This compromise would shape, and in many ways limit, African continental cooperation for the following four decades.

Section 63.1

The Idea of African Unity

The idea of African unity emerged from a shared historical experience that transcended the individual colonial territories into which the continent had been divided. That experience included the trans-Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades, which forcibly removed millions of Africans from the continent and dispersed them across the Americas, the Caribbean, and the Indian Ocean world; the nineteenth-century Scramble for Africa, in which European powers partitioned the continent among themselves with almost no regard for existing African political, linguistic, or ethnic communities; and decades of colonial rule that extracted resources, suppressed political expression, and imposed foreign languages, legal systems, and economic structures across the continent.

From this shared experience emerged the conviction, among both Africans and people of African descent in the diaspora, that racial solidarity and political cooperation were necessary responses to a common history of exploitation. This conviction took organizational form well before most African states achieved independence, largely because the political space for organizing existed more freely in London, Paris, New York, and the Caribbean than in colonial Africa itself, where colonial administrations restricted political organizing.

Pan-Africanism as an intellectual and political tradition was never a single, unified ideology. It contained multiple, sometimes competing strands: a cultural nationalism emphasizing pride in African heritage and civilization; a political program demanding self-government and eventually full independence for colonized territories; an economic critique of the exploitation embedded in the colonial and capitalist world system; and, in its most ambitious form, a vision of continental political unification that would create a single African state or federation capable of matching the economic and military power of larger nations.

By the time African decolonization accelerated in the late 1950s and early 1960s, Pan-Africanism had shifted from being primarily a diaspora movement to being primarily an African movement, led by heads of state and government who had themselves led anti-colonial struggles. This shift changed the character of the movement. Where earlier Pan-African thinkers had operated largely outside formal state power, the new generation of African leaders had to translate Pan-African ideals into the practical business of governing sovereign states — states that, however committed their leaders were to African solidarity in the abstract, also had immediate interests in consolidating their own authority, defending their borders, and managing fragile economies.

This translation from movement to governance produced the central tension examined throughout this chapter: the gap between Pan-Africanism's aspirational vision of a united, self-reliant continent and the practical reality of dozens of sovereign states, each jealously guarding hard-won independence, often locked in competition or conflict with neighbors, and dependent in varying degrees on former colonial powers or new international patrons for trade, aid, and security.

Historical Vocabulary
Pan-Africanism

Pan-Africanism is a political and cultural movement asserting the fundamental unity and shared interests of African peoples and people of African descent worldwide, and advocating for their solidarity, self-determination, and liberation from colonial and racial domination. Pan-Africanism developed first largely among diaspora intellectuals in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries and later became a driving force behind African independence movements and continental institutions.

Historians emphasize that Pan-Africanism was never monolithic. Some versions emphasized cultural and spiritual connection across the African diaspora; others emphasized concrete political federation; still others emphasized economic cooperation without political merger. Understanding these internal debates helps explain why continental unity, when it was finally institutionalized in 1963, took a much more modest form than the boldest Pan-African visions had imagined.

Section 63.2

The Organization of African Unity

The Organization of African Unity, founded on May 25, 1963, in Addis Ababa, represented a hard-won compromise between the Casablanca and Monrovia visions of unity described earlier in this chapter. Its charter established several core principles that would define the organization for the next four decades: the sovereign equality of all member states, non-interference in the internal affairs of member states, respect for the territorial integrity and independence of each state, and peaceful settlement of disputes through negotiation, mediation, conciliation, or arbitration.

Perhaps the most consequential of these principles was the commitment to respecting colonial-era borders — a principle formalized in 1964 when OAU member states agreed that existing frontiers, however arbitrarily drawn by European colonial powers, would be considered inviolable. This decision reflected a pragmatic judgment: given that colonial borders had divided ethnic and linguistic communities across dozens of separate states and had created several states with deeply contested internal boundaries, opening the question of border revision seemed likely to produce endless conflict. Leaders judged that stability, however imperfect, was preferable to the potential chaos of continent-wide border renegotiation. This decision has had lasting consequences, both stabilizing (by preventing widespread irredentist warfare) and troubling (by locking in colonial-era divisions that many communities never consented to).

The OAU's most significant practical achievement over its history was its sustained support for African liberation movements still fighting colonial rule or white-minority governments. Through its Liberation Committee, based in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania, the OAU provided diplomatic recognition, financial support, training assistance, and international advocacy for movements fighting Portuguese colonial rule in Angola, Mozambique, and Guinea-Bissau; white-minority rule in Southern Rhodesia (which became Zimbabwe in 1980); and apartheid in South Africa and colonial rule in Namibia. The OAU coordinated African diplomatic pressure at the United Nations, helped isolate apartheid South Africa internationally, and gave political and material support to movements such as the African National Congress, the Zimbabwe African National Union, and the South West Africa People's Organisation.

The OAU's record on conflicts between African states, and especially on internal conflicts within African states, was far more troubled. The principle of non-interference, while protecting fragile new states from external and neighboring intervention, also meant the organization was reluctant to act when governments committed atrocities against their own citizens. The OAU largely remained silent during Idi Amin's brutal rule in Uganda in the 1970s, during the mass killings under the Derg regime in Ethiopia, and during numerous civil wars across the continent. Its most catastrophic failure came in 1994, when the organization proved unable to mobilize a meaningful response to the genocide in Rwanda, in which extremist Hutu militias and government forces killed an estimated 800,000 Tutsi and moderate Hutu people in roughly one hundred days while the international community, including African states, largely failed to intervene.

By the 1990s, a growing consensus had emerged among African leaders, civil society organizations, and international observers that the OAU's structure — built around strict non-interference and consensus-based decision-making, with a small secretariat and limited independent resources — was inadequate to address the continent's most pressing security and development challenges. This recognition set the stage for the organization's transformation into the African Union.

Primary Source Window

Charter of the Organization of African Unity (1963)

Creator: Delegates of thirty-two independent African states · Date: May 25, 1963 · Type: Founding treaty / international charter

Context: The charter was negotiated and signed at the founding summit in Addis Ababa after extended debate between advocates of rapid political federation and advocates of a looser voluntary association. It represents a compromise document, reflecting the Monrovia Group's preference for state sovereignty far more than the Casablanca Group's preference for supranational unity.

The charter committed member states to coordinate and intensify cooperation for a better life for the peoples of Africa, to defend their sovereignty and territorial integrity, to eradicate all forms of colonialism, and to promote international cooperation, while affirming the principle of non-interference in the internal affairs of member states and respect for each state's sovereignty and independence.

Analysis: The charter's language reveals the tension at the heart of the OAU's founding: an aspiration toward continental solidarity and liberation from colonialism, combined with a firm commitment to the sovereignty of the individual states that made up the organization. This combination allowed diverse and sometimes rival governments to join a common institution, but it also limited the organization's ability to act decisively when member governments behaved badly toward their own populations or toward each other.

Section 63.3

The African Union

The African Union formally replaced the Organization of African Unity in 2002, following a process launched by the 1999 Sirte Declaration, in which African heads of state, prompted in part by Libyan leader Muammar Gaddafi's vigorous advocacy for deeper unity, agreed to establish a new continental body with expanded powers. The transformation reflected several converging pressures: the OAU's demonstrated inability to prevent or respond effectively to conflicts such as the Rwandan genocide and civil wars in Liberia, Sierra Leone, and the Democratic Republic of Congo; the end of apartheid in South Africa in 1994, which removed the last major target of the OAU's liberation-support mission and created space to redefine the organization's purpose; and a broader global trend toward regional economic and political integration, exemplified by the European Union, which some African leaders saw as a model, however imperfectly, for deeper continental cooperation.

The Constitutive Act of the African Union, adopted in 2000 and entering into force in 2001, marked a significant departure from the OAU Charter in several respects. Most notably, Article 4(h) of the Constitutive Act granted the Union "the right to intervene in a Member State... in respect of grave circumstances, namely war crimes, genocide and crimes against humanity." This provision represented a direct response to the OAU's failure during the Rwandan genocide and signaled, at least in principle, a shift away from absolute non-interference toward a doctrine sometimes described as "non-indifference."

The African Union also established a more elaborate institutional architecture than the OAU had possessed, including an Assembly of Heads of State and Government as the supreme decision-making body, an Executive Council of foreign ministers, a Pan-African Parliament intended eventually to represent African peoples directly rather than only their governments, a Peace and Security Council modeled loosely on the United Nations Security Council, a Commission serving as the AU's administrative secretariat, and a Court of Justice and Human Rights (built upon the earlier African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights) intended to adjudicate disputes and human rights violations.

The AU also broadened its membership criteria and objectives beyond the OAU's primary focus on decolonization. Its stated objectives include promoting democratic principles and institutions, popular participation, and good governance; promoting and protecting human and peoples' rights; establishing the necessary conditions for the continent to play its rightful role in the global economy and international negotiations; and accelerating the political and socio-economic integration of the continent. In practice, achieving these objectives has proven far more difficult than stating them, and much of this chapter's later sections examine the gap between the AU's ambitious mandate and its practical record.

It is worth noting that Morocco, which had withdrawn from the OAU in 1984 over the organization's recognition of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (representing Western Saharan independence claims), rejoined the African Union in 2017, illustrating both the continued salience of contested sovereignty questions within African institutions and the AU's ability to eventually reincorporate even long-estranged members.

Section 63.4

AU Institutions and Goals

Understanding how the African Union functions requires examining its principal institutions individually, since each was designed to address a specific gap in the OAU's earlier structure.

The Assembly of Heads of State and Government remains the Union's supreme organ, meeting at least once a year to set policy, elect the chairperson of the Commission, and make decisions requiring consensus or, failing consensus, a two-thirds majority. Because the Assembly is composed of sitting heads of state, its decisions inevitably reflect the political interests and constraints of individual governments, some of which have themselves been accused of authoritarian practices or human rights abuses — a persistent source of criticism regarding the AU's credibility on issues of governance and democracy.

The African Union Commission, based in Addis Ababa, functions as the Union's executive secretariat, implementing decisions of the Assembly, managing the AU's programs, and representing the Union in relations with international partners. The Commission's effectiveness has often been constrained by limited funding — historically, a substantial share of the AU's operational budget has come from external donors, including the European Union, raising persistent questions about the organization's financial independence.

The Pan-African Parliament, based in Midrand, South Africa, was intended eventually to evolve into a body with full legislative powers, elected directly by African citizens rather than appointed by national governments. In practice, the Parliament has functioned mainly in a consultative and advisory capacity, and progress toward direct elections and expanded powers has been slow, reflecting member states' reluctance to create a body that could challenge national governments' authority.

The Peace and Security Council, established in 2004, represents one of the AU's most significant institutional innovations, discussed in greater detail in Section 63.6. It provides a standing mechanism, in principle capable of authorizing peacekeeping missions, imposing sanctions, and coordinating conflict prevention and mediation efforts across the continent.

The African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights, based in Arusha, Tanzania, was established to adjudicate cases alleging violations of the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights. Its jurisdiction, however, depends on states accepting its authority, and relatively few AU member states have granted individuals and non-governmental organizations direct access to bring cases before the Court, limiting its practical reach.

Taken together, these institutions reflect an aspiration toward a more integrated, more accountable, and more capable continental body than the OAU had been. Yet each institution's effectiveness remains constrained by the same underlying tension that shaped the OAU: member states created these bodies, fund them (often inadequately), and retain the ultimate authority to ignore, defy, or withdraw from their decisions.

Historical Vocabulary
Sovereignty and Supranational Authority

Sovereignty refers to a state's supreme authority to govern itself without external interference. Supranational authority refers to power exercised by an institution above the level of individual states, sometimes with the ability to make decisions binding on member states even without their individual consent. The history of African continental cooperation is largely a history of the tension between these two concepts: African states, having only recently won sovereignty from colonial powers, have generally been reluctant to transfer meaningful supranational authority to continental institutions, even when doing so might make those institutions more effective.

Section 63.5

Regional Integration and Cooperation

While continental institutions have often struggled to translate ambitious mandates into effective action, regional economic communities (RECs) — sub-continental groupings of neighboring states — have in many cases achieved more concrete forms of cooperation, particularly in trade facilitation, infrastructure coordination, and, in some cases, security intervention.

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS), founded in 1975, is often cited as the most institutionally developed of Africa's regional blocs. Beyond its original economic mandate, ECOWAS developed a security dimension through its military observer group, ECOMOG, which intervened in the civil wars in Liberia (from 1990) and Sierra Leone (from 1997), deploying troops to enforce ceasefires and protect civilians in conflicts the international community was slow to address. ECOWAS has also established protocols on free movement of people, goods, and services among member states, and it has intervened diplomatically and, on occasion, militarily to reverse unconstitutional changes of government, as in The Gambia in 2017.

The Southern African Development Community (SADC), evolving from an earlier coordination conference formed partly to reduce member states' economic dependence on apartheid South Africa, has focused on economic cooperation, infrastructure development, and, since South Africa's democratic transition, broader regional integration including a shared security and defense cooperation framework.

The East African Community (EAC), originally established in the colonial and early independence period, collapsed in 1977 amid political tensions between Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania, but was revived in 2000 and has since pursued ambitious integration goals, including a customs union, a common market, and long-discussed (though not yet realized) plans for monetary union and eventual political federation among its member states.

Other regional bodies include the Economic Community of Central African States (ECCAS), the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) in the Horn of Africa (which has played a significant mediating role in conflicts in Sudan, South Sudan, and Somalia), and the Arab Maghreb Union in North Africa, whose effectiveness has been limited by long-standing political tensions, particularly between Algeria and Morocco.

The African Union formally recognizes eight regional economic communities as the "building blocks" of continental integration, envisioning a gradual process in which regional cooperation deepens first, eventually merging into continent-wide economic and political union. This layered approach reflects a pragmatic recognition that continental unity, if it is ever fully achieved, is more likely to emerge incrementally through regional cooperation than through a single sweeping act of political federation.

Section 63.6

Peace and Security Initiatives

The African Union's Peace and Security Council (PSC), established in 2004, was designed to give the continental body tools the OAU had lacked: a standing body empowered to monitor emerging conflicts, authorize peace support operations, recommend sanctions against member states that violate constitutional order, and coordinate with regional economic communities and the United Nations on conflict prevention and response.

The AU has deployed or authorized peace support operations in several major conflicts. The African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), deployed beginning in 2007 and later transitioning into the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS), has supported the Somali federal government against the al-Shabaab insurgency for well over a decade, at significant cost in the lives of peacekeepers, primarily from Uganda, Burundi, and other troop-contributing countries. The African Union–United Nations Hybrid Operation in Darfur (UNAMID), deployed from 2007 to 2020, represented an unusual joint operation combining African Union and United Nations command structures to address the conflict in Sudan's Darfur region, though it faced persistent criticism for its inability to fully protect civilians.

The AU has also developed a normative framework condemning "unconstitutional changes of government," suspending member states that experience coups d'état from participation in AU activities. This policy has been applied repeatedly, including suspensions of Mali, Guinea, Sudan, and Burkina Faso following coups in the early 2020s. Critics note, however, that suspension has rarely translated into effective pressure to restore civilian rule quickly, and that the AU has sometimes appeared more willing to act against coup leaders who seize power abruptly than against long-serving leaders who erode democratic institutions gradually through constitutional manipulation, such as extending term limits.

The AU's planned African Standby Force — a multinational rapid-deployment force organized regionally and intended to give the AU independent military capacity for peacekeeping, humanitarian intervention, and enforcement action — has taken decades to develop and remains, as of the mid-2020s, not fully operational at the scale originally envisioned. This slow progress reflects persistent challenges: inadequate and inconsistent funding (with the AU historically dependent on external partners, including the European Union and the United Nations, for a significant share of peace operation costs), disagreements among member states over command and control, and the difficulty of coordinating logistics, training, and equipment standards across dozens of national militaries with widely varying capacities.

Despite these limitations, African-led peace operations have in some cases achieved results that international actors were unwilling or unable to achieve, reflecting both African states' greater willingness to accept casualties in defense of regional stability and their often superior understanding of local political dynamics. At the same time, the AU's security architecture continues to depend heavily on a small number of troop-contributing countries and on external financing, raising ongoing questions about the sustainability and independence of African peace and security efforts.

Cause and Effect

From the OAU to the African Union

Long-Term Causes

Decades of frustration with the OAU's strict non-interference principle; the end of apartheid removing the OAU's central liberation mission; the persistent gap between continental rhetoric and practical capacity to address conflict and development.

Short-Term Causes

The 1994 Rwandan genocide exposing catastrophic institutional failure; Libyan advocacy for deeper unity at the 1999 Sirte summit; the global example of European integration; a wave of devastating civil wars in West and Central Africa in the 1990s.

The Transformation

The Constitutive Act adopted in 2000; the African Union formally launched in 2002, replacing the OAU with expanded institutions and a right of intervention.

Effects

New Peace and Security Council and African-led peace operations; expanded institutional architecture including a Parliament and Court; continued tension between the AU's ambitious mandate and its limited independent funding and enforcement power.

Historical complexity: The AU's creation cannot be explained by any single cause. It resulted from decades of accumulated institutional frustration combined with a specific set of crises and political opportunities in the late 1990s.

Section 63.7

Continental Development Plans

Alongside its political and security institutions, the African Union and its predecessor have repeatedly attempted to articulate continent-wide strategies for economic development. These plans reveal changing African thinking about the causes of underdevelopment and the appropriate paths toward economic transformation.

The Lagos Plan of Action, adopted by the OAU in 1980, reflected a strategy emphasizing African self-reliance, reduced dependence on former colonial powers and international financial institutions, regional economic integration, and state-led industrialization. It was, in many respects, a response to the economic difficulties many African states faced in the years following independence and a rejection of externally imposed development models. The Lagos Plan's ambitions were only partially realized, hampered by inadequate financing, weak implementation capacity, and, in the 1980s and 1990s, the imposition of structural adjustment programs by the International Monetary Fund and World Bank that often pushed African governments toward different, more market-oriented policies than the Plan had envisioned.

The Abuja Treaty of 1991 established the African Economic Community, envisioning a gradual process of integration through regional economic communities, eventually culminating in a continental common market, monetary union, and economic community — a roadmap whose full implementation remains a work in progress decades later.

The New Partnership for Africa's Development (NEPAD), launched in 2001 (shortly before the AU's own founding) and later integrated into AU structures, marked a shift in approach. NEPAD emphasized an African-led development strategy built around good governance, a peer review mechanism through which African states would voluntarily submit their governance practices to review by fellow African states, and partnership with international donors and investors rather than the more self-reliant, state-centered approach of the Lagos Plan. NEPAD's African Peer Review Mechanism represented an unusual experiment in African states holding one another accountable on governance, though participation has been voluntary and enforcement mechanisms limited.

Agenda 2063, adopted in 2013 to mark the fiftieth anniversary of the OAU's founding, represents the AU's most comprehensive long-term vision, articulating aspirations across governance, economic transformation, infrastructure, education, health, peace and security, and Africa's role in global affairs, organized around a fifty-year timeline extending to the year 2063. Agenda 2063 is explicitly framed as a continuation of the Pan-African vision articulated by the founders of the OAU, seeking to complete the project of continental transformation that earlier generations began.

Perhaps the most concrete recent achievement in continental economic integration is the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), whose agreement was signed in 2018 and entered into force in 2021, creating a framework intended eventually to eliminate tariffs on the vast majority of goods traded among African states and to create a single continental market of well over a billion people. Implementation has proceeded gradually, with actual trading under AfCFTA rules beginning in phases and full realization of the agreement's ambitions — including free movement of people, harmonized regulations, and elimination of non-tariff barriers — expected to take many years. Nonetheless, AfCFTA represents one of the most concrete steps toward the economic integration that Pan-African advocates had called for since the earliest OAU summits.

Section 63.8

Limits of Integration

Despite six decades of continental institution-building, African unity remains, by the AU's own repeated acknowledgment, an unfinished project. Understanding why requires examining several persistent structural obstacles.

The most fundamental obstacle remains the tension between sovereignty and supranational authority discussed throughout this chapter. African states, many of which achieved independence only after long and costly struggles, have generally been unwilling to surrender meaningful authority over security, monetary policy, immigration, or governance to continental institutions. This reluctance is not unique to Africa — it echoes tensions found in other regional integration projects worldwide — but it has been particularly pronounced given the relative youth of most African states and continued anxieties about national cohesion in states assembled from colonial-era borders.

Financial dependence represents a second major obstacle. The African Union's budget has historically relied heavily on external partners — the European Union, in particular, has funded a substantial share of AU peace and security operations — raising persistent concerns about the organization's independence and its ability to set its own priorities free from donor influence. Efforts to increase African states' own financial contributions, including a proposed levy on imports to fund the AU, have faced implementation challenges as member states struggle to meet their commitments consistently.

A third obstacle involves enforcement. AU decisions, resolutions, and even binding instruments such as the Constitutive Act's right of intervention depend ultimately on member states' willingness to comply or on the AU's ability to mobilize collective pressure, including diplomatic isolation, sanctions, or, in rare and difficult cases, military action. The AU has no independent mechanism to compel a defiant member state to comply with its decisions, and it has often faced criticism for inconsistent application of its own principles — acting more forcefully against unconstitutional coups than against gradual authoritarian consolidation by sitting leaders, and sometimes prioritizing regional diplomatic relationships over consistent enforcement of stated principles.

A fourth obstacle involves the gap between elite diplomacy and popular participation. Continental summits are, by their nature, gatherings of heads of state and senior officials; ordinary African citizens have limited direct voice in AU decision-making, and the Pan-African Parliament, which might in principle provide such a voice, has not yet evolved the direct electoral mandate or legislative authority that would give it real influence. Civil society organizations across the continent have repeatedly called for the AU to become more responsive to popular concerns, including corruption, human rights abuses, and economic exclusion, rather than functioning primarily as a forum for inter-governmental diplomacy.

Historical Debate

Has African Continental Cooperation Achieved Meaningful Progress, or Has It Fallen Short of Its Founders' Vision?

The question: Sixty years after the OAU's founding, historians and analysts disagree sharply about how to assess the record of African continental cooperation.

Interpretation A — Substantial, if incomplete, achievement: Some historians and policymakers argue that continental institutions achieved their most important early goal — the near-total elimination of colonial rule and apartheid from the continent — and have since built a genuinely more capable institutional architecture, including a functioning Peace and Security Council, African-led peacekeeping missions that have saved lives, an ambitious continental free trade agreement, and normative frameworks condemning unconstitutional government that, however imperfectly enforced, did not exist under the OAU. From this view, African cooperation has evolved appropriately given the continent's diversity and historical constraints, and should be judged against realistic benchmarks rather than the most utopian visions of continental federation.
Interpretation B — Persistent structural failure: Other analysts argue that African continental institutions remain fundamentally hobbled by the same sovereignty-protecting compromises embedded in the OAU's original charter, that financial dependence on external donors compromises genuine African agency, that enforcement mechanisms remain too weak to prevent coups, civil wars, and governance failures, and that the gap between the AU's ambitious rhetoric — evident in documents like Agenda 2063 — and its practical achievements reveals an institution better at producing declarations than at delivering results for ordinary Africans.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on the historical record of decolonization and anti-apartheid solidarity, the operational record of AU peace missions in Somalia and elsewhere, and the concrete progress of AfCFTA implementation. Interpretation B draws on the AU's continued financial dependence, its uneven record on preventing coups and civil wars, and the slow pace of implementing ambitious frameworks like the African Standby Force and the Pan-African Parliament's evolution toward direct elections.

Why it matters: How historians and policymakers assess this record shapes debates about how African cooperation should evolve — whether toward deeper supranational authority, or toward strengthening existing frameworks within current sovereignty constraints.

Student task: Using evidence from this chapter, evaluate which interpretation is better supported. Is it possible that both are partially correct? What additional evidence would help resolve the debate?

Section 63.9

Cooperation in the Modern Era

In the twenty-first century, African continental cooperation has continued to evolve in response to new challenges and opportunities. The implementation of the African Continental Free Trade Area, discussed above, represents perhaps the most significant ongoing project, with African governments, businesses, and regional organizations working to harmonize customs procedures, reduce non-tariff barriers, and build the infrastructure — roads, railways, ports, digital payment systems — necessary to make genuine continental trade integration a practical reality rather than merely a legal framework.

The AU has also increasingly engaged with global institutions and negotiations as a unified continental voice rather than fifty-five separate national voices. The African Union gained a permanent seat at the Group of Twenty (G20) major economies forum in 2023, a symbolically and practically significant recognition of the continent's growing economic weight and its demand for greater representation in global economic governance. The AU has similarly sought a stronger collective voice in climate change negotiations, given Africa's disproportionate vulnerability to climate impacts despite its historically minimal contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions, and in reform discussions regarding permanent representation for Africa on the United Nations Security Council.

Continental cooperation has also had to respond to a wave of coups d'état across West Africa and the Sahel in the early 2020s — including in Mali, Guinea, Burkina Faso, and Niger — which tested the AU's normative commitments against unconstitutional changes of government. The AU suspended each of these states following their respective coups, but several of the affected governments subsequently withdrew from ECOWAS entirely, forming a rival Alliance of Sahel States, illustrating both the limits of continental and regional pressure and the ongoing fragility of some post-independence political settlements.

At the same time, African regional organizations have continued to play significant mediating roles in ongoing conflicts, including in Sudan, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and the Horn of Africa, even as the scale and complexity of these conflicts have sometimes exceeded the AU's and regional bodies' capacity to resolve them decisively. The continued reliance on international partners, including the United Nations, the European Union, and individual foreign powers, for funding, logistics, and diplomatic leverage in these efforts underscores the unfinished nature of African security self-sufficiency more than sixty years after the OAU's founding.

Looking forward, African Union officials and many African leaders continue to frame continental integration as an ongoing, generational project rather than a completed achievement — one that will require sustained political will, expanded and more independent financing, stronger enforcement mechanisms, and greater direct engagement with African citizens if it is to fulfill the aspirations first articulated by Pan-African thinkers more than a century ago.

Multiple Perspectives

Continental Integration Viewed From Different Positions

From the perspective of a head of state: Continental cooperation offers diplomatic legitimacy, access to peer support during crises, and collective bargaining power in international negotiations, but any specific proposal for deeper integration must be weighed against the risk of surrendering authority over security, monetary policy, or governance that could constrain the leader's own domestic political position.
From the perspective of a cross-border trader: Free movement protocols and reduced tariffs under frameworks like AfCFTA and ECOWAS could substantially ease the practical burdens — customs delays, informal fees, documentation requirements — that traders moving goods across African borders have long faced, provided these frameworks are actually implemented at the border posts where daily commerce happens.
From the perspective of a refugee or displaced person: Continental peace and security institutions represent, in principle, a source of protection and intervention against the conflicts that produce displacement, but the AU's uneven record of decisive action means that protection in practice often depends more on which regional actors are willing and able to intervene in a specific crisis than on continental commitments alone.
From the perspective of a civil society activist: The AU's elite, government-dominated structure limits ordinary citizens' voice in continental decision-making, and genuine progress toward the AU's stated goals on governance and human rights requires sustained pressure from below, since governments have often been reluctant to hold each other accountable through mechanisms like the African Peer Review Mechanism.
Continuity and Change Over Time

From Strict Non-Interference to "Non-Indifference" (1963–Present)

What Changed

  • The Constitutive Act (2000) introduced a right of intervention absent from the OAU Charter
  • A standing Peace and Security Council replaced ad hoc OAU crisis diplomacy
  • African-led peace operations were deployed in Somalia, Darfur, and elsewhere
  • A formal norm against unconstitutional changes of government, with suspension as a consequence, was institutionalized

What Stayed the Same

  • Member states remain reluctant to cede meaningful sovereignty over internal governance
  • Enforcement of continental decisions still depends on member states' voluntary compliance
  • Financial dependence on external donors persists for peace and security operations
  • Application of intervention and suspension norms remains inconsistent across cases

Why the pattern matters: The shift from strict non-interference to a stated doctrine of "non-indifference" represents genuine institutional change, but the underlying tension between sovereignty and collective action that shaped the OAU has not been resolved — only renegotiated in a new institutional form.

Section 63.10

Chapter Review and AU Checkpoint

This chapter has traced the long history of African continental cooperation, from the diaspora-led Pan-African movement of the early twentieth century, through the founding compromise of the Organization of African Unity in 1963, to the more ambitious but still constrained African Union of the twenty-first century. Everyday Life and Social History

Continental Integration and Ordinary Africans

Behind the diplomatic summits and institutional architecture examined in this chapter lie effects, both realized and still aspirational, on the daily lives of ordinary Africans. A trader moving goods from Lagos to Accra, a student seeking to study at a university in another African country, a migrant worker crossing borders in search of employment, or a family separated by a colonial-era boundary running through a shared ethnic homeland — all have a direct stake in whether continental and regional integration succeeds in practice.

Regional protocols on free movement, where implemented, have made it easier for some Africans to travel, work, and trade across borders without the visa requirements and documentation burdens that colonial-era boundaries otherwise impose. Reduced tariffs under frameworks like AfCFTA hold the promise of lower prices for consumers and expanded markets for African producers and small businesses, many of them women-owned enterprises engaged in informal cross-border trade that has historically operated outside formal recognition or protection.

At the same time, the gap between continental agreements and their implementation at actual border posts, customs offices, and immigration checkpoints means that the practical experience of many ordinary Africans continues to lag behind the aspirations articulated in Addis Ababa. Understanding this gap — between summit diplomacy and daily experience — is essential to a full historical assessment of what continental cooperation has achieved.

The core insights of this chapter are: African unity emerged from a shared history of the slave trade, colonial partition, and colonial rule, and from an intellectual tradition of Pan-Africanism developed substantially by diaspora thinkers before African independence. The OAU, founded in 1963, represented a compromise between rapid federation and voluntary cooperation, prioritizing sovereignty and non-interference. The OAU achieved significant success supporting decolonization and the struggle against apartheid but struggled to address conflicts within and between African states, culminating in its catastrophic failure to respond to the Rwandan genocide. The African Union, established in 2002, introduced new institutions and a right of intervention, but continues to grapple with the same underlying tension between national sovereignty and continental authority. Regional economic communities have often achieved more concrete integration than continental bodies. Development frameworks from the Lagos Plan of Action to Agenda 2063 and AfCFTA reveal evolving African visions of economic transformation. And continental cooperation, however imperfect, continues to shape the practical lives of ordinary Africans who trade, migrate, and seek security across a continent still working to translate the unity ideal into lived reality.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined one of the most ambitious and enduring projects in modern African history: the effort to build institutions capable of expressing and advancing African unity across a continent of enormous diversity and, in the era of independence, dozens of newly sovereign states. That project did not begin with African governments. It began with diaspora intellectuals and activists — W. E. B. Du Bois, Marcus Garvey, George Padmore, and others — who, responding to the shared historical trauma of the slave trade and the ongoing colonization of Africa, argued that people of African descent needed to organize collectively across national and continental boundaries.

By the time African decolonization accelerated in the late 1950s, this diaspora-led tradition had been substantially taken up by African leaders themselves, most prominently Kwame Nkrumah of newly independent Ghana, who argued that individual national independence would remain incomplete without continental liberation and unity. Yet when African states gathered in Addis Ababa in 1963 to found the Organization of African Unity, the resulting charter reflected a more cautious vision than Nkrumah's, prioritizing the sovereign equality and territorial integrity of individual states and establishing a firm principle of non-interference in internal affairs.

This founding compromise shaped the OAU's entire history. The organization achieved genuine and important success in its core early mission: supporting African liberation movements against colonial rule and apartheid, coordinating diplomatic pressure that helped isolate white-minority governments internationally, and providing material and political support that contributed to the end of Portuguese colonialism, the end of white-minority rule in Zimbabwe, and eventually the end of apartheid in South Africa. These achievements should not be minimized; they represented the practical fulfillment of Pan-Africanism's original anti-colonial mission.

At the same time, the OAU's commitment to non-interference left it poorly equipped to address conflicts and abuses occurring within African states, or between neighboring African states, once the primary colonial and apartheid targets had been addressed. The organization's failure to respond meaningfully to the Rwandan genocide of 1994 became the starkest illustration of this limitation, contributing to a broader recognition among African leaders and civil society that continental institutions needed fundamental reform.

The transformation of the OAU into the African Union in 2002 represented a genuine, if partial, response to this recognition. The Constitutive Act's right of intervention, the establishment of a standing Peace and Security Council, and a broader institutional architecture including a Pan-African Parliament and human rights court all reflected an aspiration toward a more capable and more accountable continental body. Yet the underlying tension between national sovereignty and continental authority that had shaped the OAU did not disappear; it was renegotiated rather than resolved, and it continues to constrain the AU's effectiveness in the twenty-first century.

Regional economic communities — ECOWAS, SADC, the East African Community, and others — have in many respects achieved more concrete integration and, in some cases, more decisive security intervention than continental institutions, reflecting the practical advantages of cooperation among smaller groups of neighboring states with more closely aligned interests. The AU's own strategy of treating these regional bodies as "building blocks" toward eventual continental integration reflects a pragmatic acknowledgment of this reality.

Continental development frameworks, from the self-reliance-oriented Lagos Plan of Action of 1980 to the governance-focused New Partnership for Africa's Development of 2001 to the long-term Agenda 2063 and the practically significant African Continental Free Trade Area, reveal an evolving African vision of economic transformation — one that has shifted over time in its balance between self-reliance and international partnership, and between state-led planning and market-oriented reform.

The African Union's peace and security architecture, including its Peace and Security Council, its peace support operations in Somalia and elsewhere, and its normative stance against unconstitutional changes of government, represents a significant evolution beyond the OAU's capacities. Yet persistent financial dependence on external donors, inconsistent enforcement, and the continued vulnerability of many African states to coups and internal conflict illustrate that this evolution remains incomplete.

Ultimately, the history examined in this chapter should be understood neither as a story of triumphant progress toward inevitable unity nor as a story of complete failure. It is a story of genuine, hard-won achievement — the end of colonialism and apartheid, functioning continental and regional institutions, an ambitious free trade framework — combined with persistent, structurally rooted limitations that reflect the profound difficulty of building supranational cooperation among states that achieved sovereignty only recently and that continue to prioritize its defense.

Students should carry forward from this chapter an understanding that African unity remains, in the language the African Union itself often uses, an ongoing project rather than a finished achievement — one whose future trajectory will depend on choices African governments, institutions, and citizens continue to make in the years and decades ahead.

  • Prompt 2 (Comparative): Compare the Organization of African Unity and the African Union. In your comparison, explain what institutional changes occurred in 2002, why these changes were made, and what continuities remained despite the transformation.
  • Prompt 3 (Historical argument using evidence): Using evidence from this chapter, construct an argument evaluating whether African continental cooperation has, on balance, succeeded or fallen short of the vision articulated by its founders. Support your argument with specific examples of achievements and limitations.
  • Prompt 4 (Connecting past and present): The recent wave of coups in the Sahel and the formation of the Alliance of Sahel States raise questions about the durability of regional and continental cooperation. Analyze this development using the historical concepts of sovereignty and non-interference discussed in this chapter. What historical patterns does it echo, and in what ways does it represent something new?
  • Prompt 5 (Causation): Explain the causes, both short-term and long-term, that led to the transformation of the OAU into the African Union in 2002. Be sure to distinguish between immediate triggers and deeper structural causes, and explain why no single cause fully accounts for this transformation.

Research or Library Extension

MGU Library Research Project

Tracking a Regional Economic Community

This project asks you to research one African regional economic community in depth and assess its record of achievement and limitation.

  1. Choose a regional economic community. Select one of the following: ECOWAS, SADC, the East African Community, ECCAS, IGAD, or the Arab Maghreb Union.
  2. Research its founding and membership. Using library resources and reliable secondary sources, research when the organization was founded, which states belong to it, and what its founding objectives were.
  3. Identify major actions. Research at least two significant actions the organization has taken — economic agreements, peacekeeping interventions, mediation efforts, or responses to unconstitutional changes of government.
  4. Evaluate its record. Using the evidence you have gathered, assess the organization's achievements and limitations. Where has it succeeded? Where has it struggled, and why?
  5. Connect to continental cooperation. Explain how your chosen regional organization relates to the African Union's broader integration strategy, including whether it has cooperated or come into tension with continental institutions.
  6. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), present your research, your evidence, and your evaluation of the organization's historical significance.

Library resources to use: The MGU History Library's reference materials on African regional organizations, African Union official documents and reports, academic journal databases, and reputable international news archives covering recent developments.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has examined one of the longest-running and most consequential institutional projects in modern African history: the effort to build structures of continental unity and cooperation capable of matching the aspirations first articulated by Pan-African thinkers more than a century ago. That effort matters because it addresses a question every African state has had to answer since independence: how can dozens of separate nations, carved by colonial powers with little regard for existing African political communities, cooperate effectively enough to defend their collective sovereignty, resolve conflicts, and pursue shared economic development, without any single state or bloc dominating the others?

The history traced in this chapter shaped later African history in profound ways. The OAU's steady diplomatic and material support for liberation movements contributed directly to the end of colonial rule and apartheid across the continent — a transformation whose significance for African peoples cannot be overstated. The OAU's failures, particularly its inadequate response to the Rwandan genocide, forced a painful but ultimately consequential reckoning that produced the more capable, if still imperfect, institutions of the African Union. And the ongoing work of regional economic communities, continental development frameworks, and the African Continental Free Trade Area continues to shape the economic and political landscape in which hundreds of millions of Africans live, trade, and move today.

This chapter also reveals larger themes that run throughout African history and, indeed, throughout world history: the tension between the ideal of collective solidarity and the practical realities of sovereign self-interest; the difficulty of translating moral and political aspiration into durable institutional capacity; and the ways that crisis and failure, however costly, can become catalysts for institutional reform. These are not uniquely African dynamics — similar tensions have shaped regional integration projects worldwide — but they have played out in Africa within the specific historical context of recent decolonization, arbitrary colonial borders, and the ongoing task of building durable national institutions alongside continental ones.

Understanding this chapter helps students see Africa as a continent actively engaged in defining its own political future through institutions of its own creation — imperfect, contested, and still evolving, but genuinely African in origin and purpose. It is a story of real achievement, from the end of colonial rule to a functioning continental free trade area, combined with real and continuing limitation, from financial dependence to uneven enforcement of stated principles. Holding both dimensions together, without collapsing into either uncritical celebration or dismissive cynicism, is what serious historical understanding of African continental cooperation requires — and it is a fitting way to understand contemporary Africa more broadly: diverse, interconnected, creative, contested, and unfinished.

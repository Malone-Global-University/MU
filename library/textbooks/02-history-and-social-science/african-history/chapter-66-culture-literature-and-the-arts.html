Part X — Contemporary Africa
66

Culture, Literature, and the Arts

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"The sun will not set again on our precious continent until we tell our own story." — a sentiment expressed in various forms by African writers, filmmakers, and musicians across the independence and postcolonial eras.

In 1958, a thirty-year-old Nigerian writer named Chinua Achebe published a novel called Things Fall Apart. Written in English but saturated with Igbo proverbs, cosmology, and rhythms of speech, the novel told the story of a village leader named Okonkwo whose world was disrupted by the arrival of European missionaries and colonial administration. Achebe wrote the book, he later explained, because he was tired of reading European novels — including celebrated ones — that portrayed Africans as primitive, voiceless, and without history. He wanted to show, in his own words, that African societies had complex institutions, moral systems, poetry, and tragedy of their own, long before colonial rule arrived and long after it departed. The novel became one of the most widely read works of world literature in the twentieth century, translated into more than fifty languages, and it opened space for generations of African writers to tell their own stories on their own terms.

Achebe's achievement was not an isolated event. It was part of an enormous and continuing explosion of African cultural production across literature, music, film, visual art, fashion, and performance — a creative history that runs parallel to, and often in direct conversation with, the political history of colonialism, independence, and nation-building traced in earlier chapters of this textbook. This chapter examines that creative history: the novels and poems, the musical genres and popular songs, the films and photographs, the paintings, textiles, and fashions that Africans have produced to express identity, process history, critique power, entertain, and connect with audiences across the continent and around the world.

Culture matters to historians for reasons beyond aesthetic appreciation. Creative works are historical evidence. A novel written in the 1960s about the disappointments of independence tells historians something that government records cannot: how ordinary intellectuals and citizens experienced the gap between the promises of liberation and the realities of postcolonial governance. A song recorded under apartheid, banned by the state and smuggled onto compilation tapes, tells historians about the reach and the limits of censorship, and about the resilience of expression under repression. A Nollywood film watched by tens of millions of viewers across Africa and its diaspora tells historians about contemporary anxieties, aspirations, and moral debates in ways that official statistics cannot capture.

This chapter also asks students to think about culture as a site of both continuity and transformation. Oral tradition — storytelling, praise poetry, proverb, song — did not disappear with the arrival of print, radio, film, or the internet. It persisted, adapted, and fused with new technologies and new audiences. A griot's praise-singing tradition in West Africa could migrate into hip-hop and become the backbone of an entirely new popular genre. A masquerade tradition rooted in centuries-old ritual could inform contemporary fashion design exhibited in Paris and Lagos alike. Understanding African culture requires holding continuity and innovation together, rather than treating "traditional" and "modern" as opposites.

At the same time, this chapter does not treat African culture as a single, uniform "African aesthetic." Africa is a continent of more than fifty countries, thousands of languages, and enormously diverse religious, ethnic, and regional traditions. A Yoruba praise poem, an Amharic epic, an Afrikaans protest song, a Swahili taarab performance, and a Wolof griot's lineage recitation are not the same thing, even though all can be meaningfully discussed as African cultural forms. Students should resist the temptation to flatten this diversity into a single story, even while recognizing genuine continental and diasporic connections — shared histories of colonialism, shared struggles against racism, and shared global audiences that have, in some cases, produced cross-continental genres and movements.

Finally, this chapter connects African cultural production to global culture. African literature, music, film, and fashion have not simply been consumed domestically; they have reshaped world literature, the global music industry, international cinema, and global fashion in ways this chapter will trace directly. Understanding African culture, in other words, is inseparable from understanding a great deal of contemporary global culture — a fact that is sometimes obscured in accounts that treat African creativity as marginal or derivative rather than as an active, world-shaping force.

By the end of this chapter, students should understand how African writers, musicians, filmmakers, and artists have used their crafts to process colonial and postcolonial history, to critique political power, to entertain and connect communities, and to project African identity and creativity onto a global stage — while also recognizing the internal debates among African artists themselves about language, audience, authenticity, and the politics of representation.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain why historians treat literature, music, film, and art as valuable historical evidence.
  2. Trace the development of modern African literature from the late colonial period through independence and into the contemporary era.
  3. Compare oral tradition and written literature as related but distinct forms of African cultural expression.
  4. Analyze the growth of major African popular music genres and their relationship to politics, urban life, and global audiences.
  5. Describe the rise of African cinema, including Nollywood, and its cultural and economic significance.
  6. Evaluate debates over language choice among African writers, including the controversy over writing in European versus African languages.
  7. Identify how African art, textiles, and fashion have expressed identity and engaged global audiences.
  8. Assess the role of culture as a form of political expression and resistance under colonial rule, apartheid, and authoritarian governments.
  9. Explain the global influence of African literature, music, film, and fashion on world culture.
  10. Distinguish between continuity and change in African cultural forms across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.
  11. Contextualize contemporary African cultural industries within questions of globalization, technology, and diaspora connection.
  12. Connect this chapter's themes to the larger arguments of the textbook about African diversity, creativity, and historical agency.

Historical Context

To understand contemporary African culture, students must recall developments traced earlier in this textbook. Chapter 15 examined Africa's extraordinary linguistic diversity and the deep roots of oral expression, storytelling, and naming traditions. Chapter 29 explored how knowledge was preserved and transmitted through oral tradition, manuscripts, and scholarly networks long before European colonization. Chapter 30 examined African art, architecture, sculpture, and textiles as historical evidence and as expressions of political and religious meaning across many centuries. These earlier chapters established that Africa possessed rich, sophisticated cultural traditions long before colonial rule — traditions that colonial administrators and missionaries frequently dismissed, suppressed, or misunderstood, but that never disappeared.

Colonial rule, examined in Chapters 46 through 52, introduced European languages, mission education, print technology, radio broadcasting, and eventually film into African societies, often as instruments of colonial control and cultural assimilation. Mission schools taught European literary canons and often discouraged or punished the use of African languages and traditions in the classroom. At the same time, print technology and formal education created new possibilities: African intellectuals educated in mission and colonial schools gained access to printing presses, newspapers, and eventually publishing houses, which they used both to absorb European literary forms and to turn those same tools toward anti-colonial and nationalist purposes.

The nationalist and Pan-African movements examined in Chapter 53 were deeply intertwined with cultural production. Journalists, poets, and pamphleteers used newspapers and magazines to build anti-colonial consciousness. The Négritude movement, associated with poets such as Léopold Sédar Senghor of Senegal and Aimé Césaire of Martinique, argued for the distinctive value and dignity of Black culture and consciousness against colonial claims of African inferiority. These cultural and literary movements ran parallel to, and often reinforced, the political organizing that produced independence.

Independence, examined in Chapter 54, created new opportunities and new tensions for African cultural producers. New national governments sometimes supported the arts as instruments of nation-building and cultural pride, funding national theaters, dance troupes, and radio stations. But new governments also sometimes censored or repressed artists who criticized postcolonial rulers, corruption, or authoritarian governance — creating, in some cases, cultural production that operated in direct tension with the state that had once been celebrated as the vehicle of liberation.

Urbanization, examined in Chapter 61, and the growth of technology, examined in Chapter 64, transformed the conditions under which African culture was produced and consumed. Growing cities created new audiences, new venues, and new markets for music, theater, and film. The spread of radio, cassette tapes, television, home video, and eventually the internet and mobile phones dramatically expanded the reach of African cultural production, allowing artists to bypass traditional gatekeepers — colonial-era publishing houses, state broadcasters, or foreign record labels — and reach African and diasporic audiences directly.

By the early twenty-first century, African cultural industries had become genuinely global forces. Nigerian films were watched from Lagos to London to Kingston. Nigerian and Ghanaian popular music genres shaped playlists and charts on multiple continents. African writers won the world's most prestigious literary prizes. African fashion designers showed collections in Paris, Milan, and New York while drawing on textile and design traditions rooted in specific African communities. This chapter traces how this transformation occurred and what it reveals about the broader history of the continent.

Section 66.1

Culture as Historical Expression

Culture is often treated as a supplement to "real" history — an entertaining addendum to the serious business of politics, economics, and war. This chapter rejects that framing. Novels, songs, films, paintings, and fashions are historical artifacts in their own right, produced by specific people, in specific places, at specific times, responding to specific historical conditions. They can be read, watched, and interpreted with the same rigor historians apply to government documents or economic statistics — while recognizing that creative works operate under different rules of representation, invention, and imagination.

Consider what a historian can learn from a single popular song. A highlife song recorded in Ghana in the 1950s might reference the excitement of impending independence, the pleasures of urban nightlife, or anxieties about rural-to-urban migration. A protest song banned by the apartheid state might reveal both the content of resistance and the mechanisms of censorship the state used to suppress it — since banned recordings often survived through underground circulation, smuggled cassette copies, and diaspora radio broadcasts, historians can trace not only what was said but how it evaded state control. A contemporary Afrobeats song streamed millions of times might reveal how young Africans understand romance, ambition, or urban life in the twenty-first century, and how they see themselves in relation to a global audience.

At the same time, cultural sources have real limits as historical evidence. A novel is not a transcript of events; it is a work of imagination, shaped by narrative conventions, audience expectations, and the author's individual perspective and purpose. A film reflects the choices of directors, producers, and financiers, who may prioritize entertainment value, moral instruction, commercial appeal, or political message in ways that shape how history is represented on screen. Historians must ask of cultural sources the same questions they ask of any primary source: Who created this? For what audience? With what purpose? What does it reveal, and what might it obscure or exaggerate?

This chapter also asks students to consider the economic and institutional dimensions of culture. Cultural production requires infrastructure: printing presses and publishing houses, recording studios and record labels, cinemas and film distribution networks, radio and television stations, and — in the contemporary era — internet platforms and streaming services. The history of African culture is inseparable from the history of these institutions: who controlled them, who had access to them, and how access changed over time as new technologies lowered the costs of production and distribution.

Colonial-era cultural institutions were often controlled by European administrations, missionary societies, or foreign commercial interests, and they frequently imposed restrictions on African-language publishing, African-owned media, and content perceived as politically subversive. Independence brought African control over national broadcasters, universities, and publishing houses in many countries, though newly independent governments sometimes imposed their own forms of censorship. The most recent transformation — driven by digital technology, mobile phones, and internet platforms — has dramatically reduced the barriers to entry for cultural producers, allowing musicians, filmmakers, and writers to reach audiences directly, without depending on foreign-owned record labels, colonial-era publishing houses, or state broadcasters. This technological shift, examined further in Section 66.9, is one of the most significant recent developments in African cultural history.

Historical Vocabulary
Négritude

Négritude was a literary and cultural movement that emerged in the 1930s among Francophone Black intellectuals, most prominently Léopold Sédar Senghor of Senegal, Aimé Césaire of Martinique, and Léon Damas of French Guiana, who met as students in Paris. The movement argued that Black culture, aesthetics, and consciousness possessed distinctive value and beauty that colonial racism had denied and suppressed. Négritude writers celebrated African rhythm, communal values, and spiritual connection to nature as counterpoints to what they characterized as an overly rationalist, individualist European culture.

Négritude was influential but also controversial. Some later African and Caribbean intellectuals, including the Nigerian playwright Wole Soyinka, criticized Négritude for accepting a colonial-era dichotomy between "emotional" African culture and "rational" European culture — a framework Soyinka argued reinforced stereotypes even while intending to celebrate Black identity. This internal debate illustrates that African intellectuals did not speak with one voice about how best to affirm African culture against colonial denigration.

Section 66.2

Literature and the Novel

Modern African literature in European languages emerged substantially in the late colonial and early independence periods, as a generation of Africans educated in mission and colonial schools gained access to publishing houses, literary prizes, and international audiences. Writers such as Chinua Achebe in Nigeria, Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o in Kenya, Camara Laye in Guinea, and Ousmane Sembène in Senegal produced novels that addressed the disruption of African societies by colonial rule, the complexities of cultural encounter, and — as independence approached and then arrived — the disappointments and corruptions of postcolonial governance.

Achebe's Things Fall Apart (1958) remains the most widely read African novel in the world. It depicts a Igbo village leader, Okonkwo, whose rigid adherence to traditional notions of masculinity and status leads to his own downfall even before European missionaries and colonial administrators arrive to disrupt Igbo society more directly. Achebe deliberately wrote in English, but infused the prose with Igbo proverbs, speech patterns, and cosmology, producing what he described as an English that had been "altered to suit its new African surroundings." The novel directly countered earlier European depictions of Africa — such as Joseph Conrad's Heart of Darkness — that portrayed Africans as voiceless, undifferentiated, and without complex civilization.

Not all African writers agreed that writing in European languages was the right strategy for African literature. The Kenyan novelist Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, who had achieved international acclaim writing in English, announced in the early 1980s that he would no longer write fiction in English, arguing that continuing to write in colonial languages perpetuated a form of cultural and psychological colonization. He began writing his novels in Gikuyu, his native language, and has continued to advocate for African-language literature throughout his career. This disagreement — sometimes called the "language debate" in African literary studies — remains one of the most significant and unresolved arguments in the history of African literature, examined further in the Historical Debate feature below.

Postcolonial disappointment became a major theme of African literature by the 1960s and 1970s, as newly independent states experienced coups, one-party rule, corruption, and economic difficulty. Writers such as Ayi Kwei Armah of Ghana, in novels like The Beautyful Ones Are Not Yet Born, depicted postcolonial elites who had replaced colonial rulers only to reproduce many of the same patterns of corruption and inequality. This literature of disillusionment served an important historical function: it provided a form of public critique of postcolonial governments at a time when direct political opposition was often dangerous or suppressed.

Women writers, often marginalized in the earlier generation of celebrated male African novelists, produced increasingly influential and widely read literature from the 1970s onward. Writers such as Buchi Emecheta of Nigeria, Mariama Bâ of Senegal, and later Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie of Nigeria addressed themes including polygamy, women's labor, motherhood, marriage, and the particular burdens and constraints women experienced within both traditional and colonial-influenced social structures. Adichie's novels and public lectures, including her widely circulated talk "The Danger of a Single Story," became especially influential among younger generations of readers and in global conversations about representation and narrative diversity.

African literature has also achieved significant international recognition. Wole Soyinka won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1986, the first African laureate. Naguib Mahfouz of Egypt won in 1988. Nadine Gordimer of South Africa won in 1991, and J.M. Coetzee, also of South Africa, won in 2003. Abdulrazak Gurnah, born in Zanzibar and writing largely about displacement and the East African coast, won the Nobel Prize in 2021. These prizes reflect growing international recognition of African literature's artistic achievement, though African writers and critics have also debated whether reliance on Western literary prizes and Western publishing houses shapes which African stories get told and which audiences they are told for.

Primary Source Window

Chinua Achebe on Writing in English (interviews and essays, 1960s–1970s)

Creator: Chinua Achebe · Type: Essays and interviews on language and African literature

Context: Throughout his career, Achebe was asked to defend his decision to write in English rather than Igbo. His responses, given in essays and interviews over several decades, articulated a widely cited justification for African literature written in European languages.

Achebe argued that English, though it was the language of former colonizers, had become a genuinely useful tool for African writers because it allowed them to reach readers across Nigeria's many language groups and to communicate with an international audience — provided that the language itself was reshaped and "africanized" to carry African experience and idiom rather than simply imitating British literary style.

Analysis: Achebe's position represents one influential answer to the language debate in African literature, though it was far from the only answer, as the contrasting position of Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o demonstrates. Achebe's argument rests on practical considerations of multilingual national audiences and international reach, while critics of his position argue that it risks normalizing continued reliance on colonial languages at the expense of African-language literary traditions.

Questions for students:

  1. What reasons does Achebe give for writing in English rather than Igbo?
  2. What assumptions about audience and communication underlie his argument?
  3. What might be lost, according to critics like Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, when African writers choose European languages?
  4. How might a reader's assessment of this debate differ depending on whether they prioritize international audience reach or the preservation of African-language literary traditions?
Historical Debate

Should African Literature Be Written in European or African Languages?

The question: Since the emergence of modern African literature, writers, critics, and scholars have debated whether writing in European languages such as English, French, or Portuguese compromises the authenticity, accessibility, or decolonizing purpose of African literature, or whether it serves practical and creative purposes that outweigh these concerns.

Interpretation A — European Languages Serve Practical and Creative Purposes: Writers such as Chinua Achebe and Wole Soyinka argued that English and French, reshaped and "africanized" by African writers, could effectively carry African experience while reaching multilingual national audiences and international readers. In multilingual nations with dozens or hundreds of indigenous languages, a common European language could serve as a practical bridge across ethnic and linguistic divisions.
Interpretation B — African-Language Literature Is Necessary for True Decolonization: Writers such as Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o argued that continuing to write in colonial languages perpetuated a form of psychological colonization, since language itself carries worldview, and relying on European languages meant African literature remained oriented toward European or Europeanized readers and literary institutions rather than African communities who did not read those languages.

Evidence: Supporters of Interpretation A point to the enormous readership, translation, and influence achieved by African novels written in English and French, and to the practical challenges of publishing in dozens of African languages with small existing readerships and limited publishing infrastructure. Supporters of Interpretation B point to the small percentage of Africans who can read literary English or French fluently, and to successful examples of African-language publishing, radio drama, and oral performance that reach audiences European-language literature cannot.

Why it matters: This debate is not simply about literary taste. It concerns who African literature is ultimately written for, what counts as decolonization of culture, and how nations balance linguistic diversity with practical communication.

Student task: Which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and why? Is it possible for African literature to serve both practical and decolonizing purposes simultaneously? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate?

Section 66.3

Poetry, Oral Tradition, and Performance

Long before European colonization introduced print literacy on a wide scale, African societies developed sophisticated oral literary traditions: epic narratives, praise poetry, proverbs, riddles, folktales, and ritual performance, transmitted across generations by specialists trained in memory, rhythm, and performance technique. Chapter 15 and Chapter 29 examined these traditions in depth as pre-colonial knowledge systems. This section considers how oral tradition persisted, adapted, and interacted with written literature and modern media across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.

The West African griot tradition offers a particularly clear example of this persistence. Griots — hereditary praise singers, oral historians, and musicians found across the Mande-speaking regions of West Africa — traditionally served ruling families and communities by preserving genealogies, recounting historical epics such as the Sundiata epic examined in Chapter 21, and performing at ceremonies and celebrations. Rather than disappearing with the arrival of colonial rule and later independence, the griot tradition adapted: griot families continued to perform at weddings, naming ceremonies, and political events, and griot musical techniques and vocal styles fed directly into modern popular music genres, including the internationally successful recordings of Malian and Senegalese musicians in the late twentieth century.

Poetry has also remained a vital form of political and cultural expression across the continent, both in oral and written forms. Praise poetry directed at political leaders, satirical poetry criticizing corrupt officials, and performance poetry addressing social issues have circulated at public gatherings, on radio, and — in the contemporary era — through spoken-word performance and social media video. In South Africa, poets associated with the anti-apartheid struggle, including many who performed at political rallies and cultural events, used poetry as a direct instrument of protest, often reaching audiences who could not or would not read printed political tracts.

Theatre has similarly bridged oral and written traditions. Playwrights such as Wole Soyinka drew on Yoruba ritual, masquerade, and festival traditions to create modern dramatic works that were performed both in elite theatrical venues and, in adapted forms, in community and traveling theatre troupes. Traveling theatre — popular dramatic and musical performances that toured towns and villages, particularly in Nigeria and Ghana from the mid-twentieth century onward — provided entertainment, moral instruction, and topical commentary to audiences with limited access to print literature, extending oral performance traditions into new commercial and technological forms, including eventually filmed and televised productions.

Section 66.4

Music and Popular Culture

African popular music has been one of the continent's most historically significant and globally influential cultural forms. Music has served simultaneously as urban entertainment, commercial industry, political commentary, and cultural memory across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.

In West Africa, highlife music emerged in Ghana and Nigeria in the early twentieth century, blending Akan and other local musical traditions with Western instruments, brass band influences, and Christian hymn styles introduced through mission schools and colonial military bands. Highlife became closely associated with the excitement of the independence era, celebrating national pride, urban modernity, and social life in newly growing cities. Ghanaian highlife musicians performed at independence celebrations, and the genre's popularity across West Africa illustrated how new nations sought cultural forms that expressed both modern aspiration and rootedness in local tradition.

From highlife and traditional Yoruba drumming traditions, the Nigerian musician Fela Kuti developed Afrobeat in the late 1960s and 1970s — a genre combining highlife, American jazz and funk, and complex polyrhythmic percussion with extended musical arrangements and explicitly political lyrics. Fela used his music to criticize Nigeria's military governments directly, addressing corruption, police brutality, and government repression in songs that could run twenty minutes or longer. His outspoken criticism led to repeated arrests, the destruction of his compound by soldiers in 1977, and years of harassment by Nigerian authorities — making Fela Kuti one of the clearest examples in African history of a musician whose art directly provoked state repression, and whose persistence in the face of that repression became itself a historically significant act of resistance.

In South Africa, music played a central role in the struggle against apartheid, examined in Chapter 57. Singers such as Miriam Makeba, exiled from South Africa for decades after her outspoken criticism of apartheid, used international performances and recordings to draw global attention to South African political conditions. Inside South Africa, musicians developed and performed genres — including mbube, isicathamiya, and later mbaqanga — that carried coded political messages, expressed community solidarity, and provided emotional sustenance under conditions of severe political repression, forced removals, and township violence. The apartheid government's cultural censorship, including the banning of specific songs, artists, and recordings, illustrates the reach of state control over cultural production — but the persistence and creative adaptation of banned or restricted music also illustrates its limits.

By the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, Ghana's hiplife genre — combining hip-hop production styles and rap delivery with highlife melodies and local languages such as Twi — demonstrated how African musicians absorbed and transformed globally circulating genres into locally rooted popular forms. This pattern reached its most commercially significant expression in the 2000s and 2010s with the emergence of Afrobeats (distinct from Fela Kuti's earlier Afrobeat), a genre blending West African rhythms with hip-hop, dancehall, and R&B influences. Nigerian and Ghanaian Afrobeats artists achieved major international chart success, collaborated with globally prominent American and British musicians, and drew massive audiences across Africa, Europe, and the Americas, marking one of the clearest instances of African popular culture reshaping global mainstream entertainment on its own terms rather than as an imported or derivative form.

Multiple Perspectives

The Rise of Globally Popular African Music

From the perspective of African musicians and producers: The global success of genres such as Afrobeats represents long-overdue international recognition of African musical creativity, and an opportunity to build sustainable careers, international audiences, and greater control over an industry historically dominated by foreign labels and distributors.
From the perspective of older-generation musicians and political artists such as Fela Kuti's contemporaries: Some critics within Africa have expressed concern that commercially oriented contemporary genres emphasize romance, celebration, and entertainment at the expense of the explicit political critique that characterized earlier genres like Afrobeat, raising questions about whether commercial success has come at the cost of political voice.
From the perspective of international record labels and streaming platforms: The global popularity of African music represents a lucrative new market and a valuable addition to global catalogs, though African artists and critics have also raised concerns about unequal contracts, inadequate royalty structures, and the risk of foreign companies profiting disproportionately from African creative labor.
From the perspective of diaspora audiences: For many in the African diaspora, particularly younger generations in Europe and the Americas, contemporary African popular music has provided a renewed and often celebratory connection to African identity, contrasting with earlier generations' more complicated relationship to African heritage shaped by colonial-era stereotypes.
Section 66.5

Film and Visual Storytelling

African cinema developed along several distinct paths across the twentieth century. In the immediate post-independence decades, a generation of formally trained African filmmakers, particularly in Francophone West Africa, produced art-house cinema that addressed colonialism, tradition, and social change with serious artistic ambition, often screened at international film festivals and supported by government or foreign cultural funding. The Senegalese filmmaker Ousmane Sembène, often called the father of African cinema, directed films addressing colonial exploitation, religious hypocrisy, and postcolonial corruption, establishing an influential tradition of politically engaged, aesthetically serious African filmmaking.

A dramatically different film industry emerged in Nigeria beginning in the late 1980s and accelerating through the 1990s. Nigerian filmmakers, facing limited access to traditional cinema infrastructure and high production costs, began producing low-budget films shot on video and distributed directly on videocassette and later VCD and DVD, bypassing cinema houses entirely and selling directly to consumers through informal market networks. This home-video industry, which came to be known as Nollywood, grew explosively in volume of output, producing thousands of films annually by the early twenty-first century — by some measures surpassing Hollywood in sheer number of films produced, even though budgets remained far smaller.

Nollywood films addressed a wide range of themes — family conflict, romance, religious morality, witchcraft and the supernatural, urban ambition, corruption, and social mobility — and reached enormous audiences not only within Nigeria but across Anglophone Africa and the African diaspora in Europe, the Americas, and beyond. Nollywood's commercial success demonstrated the existence of a massive African audience for African-made, African-themed entertainment, challenging assumptions that African audiences primarily consumed foreign film and television. The industry also generated significant employment and economic activity, though it faced ongoing challenges including piracy, inconsistent production quality, and limited access to international distribution and financing compared to major global film industries.

By the 2010s and 2020s, streaming platforms and increased international investment brought greater resources and international visibility to African filmmaking, including higher-budget Nigerian productions and increased recognition for filmmakers from Kenya, South Africa, Egypt, and other African film industries. South African cinema, shaped by its distinct history of apartheid and post-apartheid transition, produced internationally recognized films addressing township life, racial reconciliation, and historical memory. Egyptian cinema, one of the oldest and most established film industries in the Arab and African worlds, has produced a vast body of work reaching audiences across the Arabic-speaking world for nearly a century.

Section 66.6

Art, Fashion, and Design

African visual art, textile production, and fashion design carry historical traditions reaching back centuries, examined in Chapter 30, while also demonstrating significant contemporary innovation and global reach. Textile traditions such as Kente cloth from Ghana, Adire and Aso Oke fabrics from Yoruba communities in Nigeria, Bogolan (mud cloth) from Mali, and Shweshwe fabric from Southern Africa carry symbolic meanings tied to status, occasion, lineage, and community identity, and have been continuously produced, adapted, and commercialized from the precolonial period through the present.

Contemporary African fashion designers have increasingly drawn on these textile traditions while engaging global fashion markets, showing collections at international fashion weeks in Lagos, Johannesburg, Paris, and New York, and building brands that combine African design heritage with contemporary global fashion sensibilities. This growth has been accompanied by debates over cultural appropriation, as global fashion houses have sometimes used African-inspired patterns, prints, or techniques without adequate acknowledgment, compensation, or collaboration with African designers and communities — a controversy examined further in Section 66.7.

Contemporary African visual artists have similarly gained increased international recognition, exhibiting in major galleries, museums, and biennales worldwide, while addressing themes including colonial history, urban life, migration, gender, religion, and political power. Photography has been an especially significant medium, with mid-twentieth-century studio photographers such as Mali's Seydou Keïta and Malick Sidibé documenting urban social life, fashion, and personal identity in newly independent African cities, producing images that have since become internationally celebrated as both artistic achievement and historical documentation of postcolonial urban culture.

Section 66.7

Culture and Political Meaning

Throughout this chapter, culture has appeared repeatedly as a site of political meaning and contest, not merely entertainment separate from politics. Colonial administrations censored African-language publishing and suppressed cultural and religious practices perceived as threats to colonial authority. Nationalist movements used newspapers, poetry, and popular theatre to build anti-colonial consciousness. Postcolonial governments alternately funded and censored the arts. Musicians such as Fela Kuti and poets associated with anti-apartheid resistance used their crafts as direct instruments of political critique, often at significant personal risk.

Culture has also served as a vehicle for processing historical trauma and collective memory. South African post-apartheid literature, film, and visual art have grappled extensively with the legacy of apartheid, the work of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission examined in Chapter 59, and the challenges of reconciliation and unequal transformation. Rwandan literature and film have addressed the 1994 genocide and its aftermath, contributing to national and international understanding of that history alongside formal historical scholarship and legal proceedings.

Debates over cultural appropriation — the use of African cultural elements, styles, symbols, or aesthetics by non-African individuals or companies, often without acknowledgment, compensation, or collaboration — have become an increasingly significant political dimension of contemporary African cultural discourse. African musicians, designers, and cultural commentators have raised these concerns in relation to global fashion houses using African-inspired prints, non-African musicians incorporating African rhythms and aesthetics without collaboration or credit, and global media companies profiting from African cultural production without equitable compensation structures. These debates connect directly to longer historical patterns of unequal economic and cultural exchange between Africa and wealthier nations, examined throughout Part IX and Part X of this textbook.

Section 66.8

Global Influence of African Creativity

By the twenty-first century, African literature, music, film, and fashion had become significant forces reshaping global culture rather than peripheral additions to it. African novelists regularly appear on international bestseller lists and win major literary prizes. Afrobeats artists have topped international charts, performed at major global award ceremonies, and collaborated extensively with prominent American, British, and other international musicians. Nollywood films are watched by tens of millions of viewers across Africa, the African diaspora, and increasingly global streaming audiences. African fashion designers show internationally, and African-inspired aesthetics have influenced global fashion and design trends.

This global influence operates in both directions: African culture has shaped global entertainment and artistic production, while global technology, capital, and media platforms have simultaneously reshaped African cultural production, creating both opportunities — expanded audiences, new revenue streams, international collaboration — and challenges, including unequal contracts, foreign ownership of distribution platforms, and ongoing debates over cultural appropriation and equitable compensation. Understanding this two-directional relationship, rather than treating African culture as either a passive recipient of globalization or an isolated tradition unaffected by global forces, is essential to an accurate historical account of contemporary African cultural production.

Cause and Effect

The Global Rise of Contemporary African Popular Music

Long-Term Causes

Deep West African musical traditions including highlife, Afrobeat, and griot performance styles; large, young, urban African populations providing a substantial domestic audience and creative talent base; the African diaspora's ongoing cultural and family connections to the continent.

Short-Term Causes

Expansion of mobile internet and smartphone access across Africa in the 2000s and 2010s; the growth of global streaming platforms providing direct international distribution; strategic collaborations between African and international artists and producers.

The Event

Afrobeats artists from Nigeria and Ghana achieve sustained international chart success, sold-out international tours, and major award recognition through the 2010s and 2020s.

Effects

Increased global visibility and investment in African music industries; expanded international collaboration and diaspora cultural connection; ongoing debates over equitable compensation, contract terms, and ownership of African creative labor within global entertainment structures.

Historical complexity: The global rise of African popular music cannot be explained by musical talent alone; it required specific technological, economic, and demographic conditions to convert deep cultural traditions into globally competitive contemporary industries.

Section 66.9

Cultural Revival and Innovation

Contemporary African culture is characterized by ongoing revival and reinvention of older traditions alongside genuine innovation. Digital technology has enabled musicians to sample and rework traditional rhythms and instrumentation within contemporary genres. Filmmakers have drawn increasingly on indigenous folklore, mythology, and historical narrative for both domestic and international audiences. Fashion designers have researched and revived historical textile techniques, applying them within contemporary design.

Social media and mobile technology have also democratized cultural production and criticism, allowing young African writers, musicians, visual artists, and commentators to build audiences and reputations without relying entirely on traditional gatekeepers — publishing houses, record labels, or state broadcasters. This has produced both opportunities for wider participation in cultural production and new challenges, including the pressures of algorithm-driven platforms, uneven access to reliable internet and electricity in some regions, and ongoing questions about compensation and ownership within digital distribution systems.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Oral Tradition from the Precolonial Era to the Digital Age

What Changed

  • Print literacy and formal education introduced new technologies for storing and transmitting narrative and knowledge
  • Radio, television, and recorded music dramatically expanded the reach of performance beyond live audiences
  • Internet and mobile platforms allowed direct distribution without traditional institutional gatekeepers
  • Global audiences became accessible to individual African performers and writers in ways not previously possible

What Stayed the Same

  • Performance-based storytelling, praise, and proverb traditions remain central to popular music and public speech
  • Griot and praise-singing lineages continue to inform musical technique and social function
  • Community gathering — weddings, festivals, political rallies — continues to be a central site of cultural performance
  • Culture continues to serve as a vehicle for both political critique and community celebration

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of oral tradition's underlying techniques and social functions, even as its technological vehicles have transformed dramatically, illustrates that cultural continuity does not require technological stasis — traditions can adapt their form while preserving their core purpose.

Everyday Life and Social History

For most Africans, culture is experienced not primarily through international prizes or global chart positions but through everyday practice: a mother singing a lullaby drawn from generations-old melodies; a family watching a Nollywood film together on a shared television or phone screen; a wedding celebration featuring a hired praise singer or a popular local band; a market vendor's radio playing the latest hiplife or Afrobeats hit; a child memorizing proverbs and folktales from grandparents; a congregation singing hymns that blend Christian liturgy with local musical traditions; a group of friends debating the latest popular novel or film on social media.

This everyday cultural life connects directly to the larger historical developments traced in this chapter — the growth of cities, examined in Chapter 61, that created new audiences and venues for cultural consumption; the spread of mobile technology, examined in Chapter 64, that placed music and film distribution directly into individual hands; and the persistence of religious and communal traditions, examined in Chapters 26 through 28, that continue to shape the themes, occasions, and meanings of cultural expression across the continent.

Historical Vocabulary
Nollywood

Nollywood is the informal name for Nigeria's prolific film industry, which emerged from low-budget home-video productions in the late 1980s and 1990s and grew into one of the largest film industries in the world by volume of annual output. Nollywood films are typically produced quickly and inexpensively compared to major international film industries, distributed initially through direct-to-video formats and increasingly through streaming platforms, and address themes ranging from family drama and romance to religious morality, corruption, and the supernatural. The industry's massive commercial success demonstrated the existence of a large African audience for African-made entertainment and has generated substantial employment across West Africa, while also facing challenges including piracy and limited international distribution infrastructure compared to Hollywood or other major global film industries.

Section 66.10

Chapter Review and Arts and Culture Checkpoint

This chapter has examined African literature, music, film, and visual art as historically significant expressions of identity, political meaning, and creative achievement, tracing their development from oral traditions and colonial-era literary movements through independence-era literature, political music, and the rise of Nollywood, to the contemporary global influence of African popular culture. Students should now be able to explain how African cultural producers processed colonial and postcolonial history, engaged political power, and reached both African and global audiences.

The core insights of this chapter are: culture is legitimate historical evidence with both value and limits; African literature emerged substantially from the colonial encounter but transformed into a globally significant, internally diverse literary tradition with real debates over language and audience; African popular music has served simultaneously as entertainment, commerce, and political resistance; Nollywood demonstrated the existence of a massive African audience for African-made entertainment; oral tradition persisted and adapted rather than disappearing; culture and politics have been continuously intertwined across the colonial, independence, and contemporary periods; and African cultural production has become a genuinely global creative force reshaping world literature, music, film, and fashion.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the history of African culture, literature, and the arts from the colonial and independence eras through the contemporary period, examining how African writers, musicians, filmmakers, and artists have used their crafts to process history, express identity, critique power, entertain communities, and reach global audiences. It has treated cultural production as legitimate historical evidence, while recognizing the specific interpretive challenges that creative works present compared to other historical sources.

The chapter began with Chinua Achebe's 1958 novel Things Fall Apart as an entry point into a much larger story: the emergence of modern African literature as African writers, educated in mission and colonial schools, turned literary tools acquired through colonial education toward anti-colonial and nationalist purposes, and ultimately toward complex explorations of postcolonial disappointment, gender, and social change. The chapter examined the significant and unresolved debate among African writers over whether literature should be written in European or African languages, presenting the differing positions of Chinua Achebe and Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o as representative of this ongoing disagreement.

The chapter then turned to oral tradition, poetry, and performance, demonstrating that these forms did not disappear with the introduction of print literacy, radio, film, or digital technology, but persisted and adapted, feeding directly into modern popular music and performance genres. The West African griot tradition served as a central example of this continuity, illustrating how deep cultural roots continued to shape contemporary cultural forms.

Music received substantial attention as one of Africa's most historically significant and globally influential cultural forms. The chapter traced the development of highlife in Ghana and Nigeria, Fela Kuti's explicitly political Afrobeat genre and the state repression it provoked, the role of music in the anti-apartheid struggle in South Africa, and the more recent emergence of hiplife and globally successful Afrobeats. This trajectory illustrated both the persistence of music as political expression and its significant commercial and technological transformation over time.

Film received similarly detailed treatment, contrasting the art-house cinema tradition established by filmmakers such as Ousmane Sembène with the explosively commercial Nollywood home-video industry that emerged in Nigeria beginning in the late 1980s. Nollywood's massive volume of output demonstrated the existence of an enormous African audience for African-made entertainment, challenging assumptions that African audiences depended primarily on foreign film and television.

The chapter examined African art, textiles, and fashion as traditions combining deep historical roots with significant contemporary innovation and global reach, while also addressing the genuine controversies surrounding cultural appropriation that have accompanied African fashion and design's growing international visibility.

Throughout, the chapter emphasized that culture and politics have been continuously intertwined in African history: colonial administrations censored and suppressed cultural expression perceived as threatening; nationalist movements used cultural production to build anti-colonial consciousness; postcolonial governments alternately funded and censored the arts; and individual artists, from Fela Kuti to South African anti-apartheid musicians, used their crafts as direct and sometimes dangerous instruments of political critique.

Finally, the chapter examined the global influence of African creativity, demonstrating that African literature, music, film, and fashion have become significant forces reshaping global culture rather than peripheral or derivative additions to it, while also considering the genuine challenges — unequal contracts, foreign platform ownership, cultural appropriation — that have accompanied this growing global visibility.

Students completing this chapter should understand that African cultural history cannot be reduced to a single story, aesthetic, or trajectory. It is a history of enormous internal diversity, ongoing internal debate, continuous adaptation, and genuine global significance — a history that reveals as much about Africa's political and social development as more conventional political and economic historical narratives, and that deserves to be studied with the same historical rigor applied throughout this textbook.

As the final substantive chapter before the textbook's concluding chapter on Africa's future, this chapter also serves as a bridge: it demonstrates that the African creativity, adaptability, and resilience traced throughout African history — from ancient trade networks to anti-colonial resistance to postcolonial nation-building — continues to find expression in the contemporary cultural forms through which Africans understand themselves, communicate with each other, and engage with the wider world.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Négritude
A literary and cultural movement asserting the value and distinctiveness of Black culture and consciousness against colonial racism, associated with Léopold Sédar Senghor, Aimé Césaire, and others.
Oral tradition
The transmission of narrative, history, and knowledge through spoken performance rather than written text, encompassing epics, praise poetry, proverbs, and folktales.
Griot
A hereditary West African praise singer, oral historian, and musician who preserves genealogies and historical narratives and performs at ceremonies and celebrations.
Postcolonial literature
Literature produced after formal independence that often addresses the disappointments, corruptions, and complexities of the postcolonial era.
Language debate
The ongoing disagreement among African writers over whether literature should be written in European or African languages, and what is gained or lost by each choice.
Highlife
A West African popular music genre, emerging in Ghana and Nigeria in the early twentieth century, blending local traditions with Western instruments and brass band influences.
Afrobeat
A political and musically complex genre developed by Fela Kuti in Nigeria, combining highlife, jazz, funk, and Yoruba percussion with explicit criticism of government repression.
Afrobeats
A contemporary, commercially successful popular music genre from Nigeria and Ghana blending West African rhythms with hip-hop, dancehall, and R&B, achieving major international success.
Hiplife
A Ghanaian music genre combining hip-hop production and delivery with highlife melodies and local languages.
Nollywood
Nigeria's prolific film industry, which grew from low-budget home-video productions into one of the largest film industries in the world by volume.
Cultural boycott
An international campaign, notably applied against apartheid South Africa, in which artists and cultural institutions refused engagement with a targeted country as a form of political pressure.
Protest music
Music created to criticize political conditions, authority, or injustice, often circulating despite censorship or state repression.
Diaspora culture
Cultural production and identity shaped by communities living outside their ancestral homeland, maintaining connections to African cultural roots.
Globalization of culture
The process by which cultural products, styles, and industries circulate and influence audiences across national and continental boundaries.
Cultural memory
The way societies use cultural production — literature, film, music, monuments — to process and remember historical experience, including trauma.
Praise poetry
Poetry composed to honor rulers, ancestors, or notable individuals, historically performed by specialists such as griots and continuing in adapted forms today.
Masquerade tradition
Ritual and ceremonial performance traditions involving costume, dance, and often spiritual significance, found across many African societies.
Textile art
The production of woven, dyed, or printed cloth carrying symbolic, cultural, or status-related meaning, such as Kente, Adire, and Bogolan traditions.
Cultural appropriation
The use of elements from one culture by members of another, often raised as a concern when done without acknowledgment, compensation, or collaboration.
Soft power
The ability to influence others through cultural appeal and attraction rather than coercion or economic pressure, increasingly associated with African music and film's global reach.
Cultural industry
The commercial infrastructure — studios, labels, publishing houses, distribution networks — that supports the production and dissemination of cultural works.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has argued that African culture — its literature, music, film, and art — is not a decorative footnote to African political and economic history but a central historical subject in its own right, revealing how Africans have processed colonialism, expressed identity, critiqued power, and connected with global audiences across more than a century of dramatic historical change.

The story told in this chapter connects directly to the largest themes of this textbook. The creativity, adaptability, and resilience that produced ancient trade networks, sophisticated precolonial states, and vibrant oral traditions did not disappear under colonial rule; it found new expression in anti-colonial literature and nationalist poetry. That same creativity did not disappear with the disappointments of postcolonial governance; it found expression in the political music of Fela Kuti and the protest songs of the anti-apartheid struggle. And that same creativity has not been diminished by globalization; it has instead used new technologies and new global platforms to reach larger audiences than ever before, from Nollywood's massive viewership to Afrobeats' international chart success.

This chapter has also emphasized that African culture is not a single, unified tradition but an enormously diverse field of internal debate, disagreement, and innovation — over language, over the proper relationship between commercial success and political critique, over the balance between tradition and innovation, and over how to engage global audiences and global capital without losing control over African creative labor and African stories.

Understanding African culture, in the end, is understanding Africa itself: a continent that has never been static, never been simple, and never been merely acted upon by outside forces. Africans have continuously created, adapted, resisted, and innovated — in politics and economics, as earlier chapters have shown, and equally in literature, music, film, and art, as this chapter has demonstrated. As the textbook turns to its final chapter on Africa's future, this creative history offers a hopeful reminder: the same resourcefulness and imagination that produced Cahokia's ancient trade networks, the manuscripts of Timbuktu, the political poetry of the nationalist era, and the global sound of Afrobeats will continue to shape Africa's next chapters, in forms not yet imagined.

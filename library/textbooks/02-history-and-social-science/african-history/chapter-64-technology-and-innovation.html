64

Technology and Innovation

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Africa did not wait for the wire to arrive before it learned to communicate; when the wire finally came, it arrived alongside the wireless, and Africans went straight to the phone in their pocket."

In the early 2000s, a farmer in rural Kenya seeking the day's maize price had few good options. She might walk or take a minibus to the nearest market town, spend hours waiting to learn what traders were paying, and risk arriving to find that prices had already fallen or that better prices were available in a town she had not visited. Fixed telephone lines rarely reached her village; postal services were slow and unreliable; radio gave general information but not the specific, timely facts a small trader needed to negotiate well. Twenty years later, the same farmer was more likely to carry a mobile phone capable of checking commodity prices by text message, receiving payment for her crop directly into a mobile money account, borrowing small amounts of credit against her transaction history, and communicating instantly with buyers, relatives working in the city, and agricultural extension officers offering advice on planting and pest control. This transformation did not require the prior construction of millions of miles of copper telephone wire, the sequence through which telecommunication had developed in Europe and North America over a century. It arrived instead through wireless towers, satellite links, and — increasingly — undersea fiber-optic cables that reached the continent's coasts and then extended inland through microwave relays and terrestrial cable, bringing millions of Africans onto mobile and, later, mobile internet networks within the span of a single generation.

This chapter studies the technological and scientific transformation of Africa in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries: the spread of telecommunications and the mobile phone, the emergence of mobile money and financial technology, the growth of digital economies, startup ecosystems, and technology hubs, the expansion of scientific research and higher education, innovation in agriculture and energy, and the broader relationship between technology and social change. It considers how young Africans in particular have used new tools to build businesses, solve local problems, and participate in global conversations, while also examining the unevenness of this transformation — the gaps in electricity, internet access, digital literacy, and capital that continue to separate different regions, classes, and generations across the continent.

It matters because technology has never been separate from African history; it has always been part of it, from the ironworking traditions described in earlier chapters, to the architectural achievements of Great Zimbabwe and the manuscript culture of Timbuktu, to the agricultural and medical knowledge systems that sustained African societies for millennia. What changed in the technology boom of the twenty-first century was not that Africa suddenly acquired technology for the first time, but that a specific combination of falling hardware costs, liberalized telecommunications markets, growing urban populations, and a very large, very young population created conditions in which digital tools spread with unusual speed, often in advance of infrastructure — roads, reliable electricity, formal banking — that had underpinned earlier waves of technological change elsewhere in the world.

Much changed in this period. State telecommunications monopolies gave way to competitive private mobile networks. Fixed-line banking, which had reached only a small fraction of Africans, was outpaced within a decade by mobile money platforms that brought basic financial services to hundreds of millions of previously unbanked people. Universities and research institutions expanded, though unevenly, and African scientists began to play larger roles in fields from genomics to renewable energy. Technology hubs emerged in cities such as Lagos, Nairobi, Cairo, Cape Town, and Kigali, producing software companies, e-commerce platforms, and fintech firms that attracted growing amounts of international venture capital by the 2010s.

Much also continued. Access to electricity remained deeply unequal, with large populations — especially in rural areas and in some of the continent's poorest and most conflict-affected states — still lacking reliable power. Internet access, while expanding rapidly, remained more available and more affordable in cities than in the countryside, and more available to men than to women in many contexts, reflecting broader patterns of gender inequality in access to income, education, and mobility. The benefits of the technology boom were distributed unevenly among African countries as well: a handful of larger, wealthier economies with better infrastructure and larger pools of educated workers attracted a disproportionate share of investment, while many smaller or poorer states remained largely consumers rather than producers of digital technology.

The evidence historians and social scientists use to understand this period includes telecommunications regulatory records, mobile network operator data, World Bank and International Telecommunication Union statistics on connectivity and access, venture capital investment reports, government policy documents on science and technology, university enrollment and research output statistics, oral histories and journalism documenting the experiences of entrepreneurs and users, and the products and platforms themselves — the specific mobile money systems, apps, and hardware that Africans built and adopted. This evidence reveals both remarkable achievement and persistent inequality, and it connects directly to earlier chapters on colonial infrastructure, postcolonial development strategies, urbanization, and globalization, while pointing forward to the unresolved questions about Africa's economic and technological future addressed in the textbook's concluding chapter.

This chapter also connects Africa's technology story to the wider world. African entrepreneurs, engineers, and firms have not simply imported technologies designed elsewhere; they have adapted, localized, and in important cases pioneered technologies and business models — most famously mobile money — that were later studied and partly imitated in other developing regions. Understanding this history requires taking seriously both African agency and innovation and the continuing structural constraints — capital, infrastructure, and global economic power — within which that innovation has occurred.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the major phases of telecommunications expansion in Africa from the late twentieth century to the present.
  2. Trace the origins, spread, and consequences of mobile money and financial technology, using Kenya's M-Pesa as a central case.
  3. Describe the growth of digital economies, technology hubs, and startup ecosystems in major African cities.
  4. Analyze the state of scientific research and higher education in Africa and the challenges facing African universities and researchers.
  5. Identify examples of African innovation in agriculture, energy, and other practical fields.
  6. Evaluate how mobile and digital technology have changed social relationships, family communication, and civic and political life.
  7. Assess the role of youth in Africa's technology economy, including entrepreneurship, informal enterprise, and creative industries.
  8. Compare the experiences of different regions, classes, and genders in access to and benefits from new technology.
  9. Interpret the concept of "leapfrogging" and evaluate its strengths and limitations as a way of understanding African technological development.
  10. Connect the technology boom to earlier chapters on colonial infrastructure, postcolonial development, and globalization.
  11. Contextualize Africa's digital transformation within global patterns of technological change and inequality.
  12. Distinguish between technological access and technological benefit, recognizing that owning a device is not the same as gaining economic or social advantage from it.

Historical Context

To understand Africa's technology boom, students should recall the state of communications and infrastructure inherited from the colonial and early postcolonial periods, discussed in earlier chapters on colonial economies and nation building. Colonial governments built telecommunications and transportation infrastructure primarily to serve administrative control and the extraction of raw materials, not to connect African populations with one another. Railways typically ran from interior mining or plantation regions to coastal ports, rather than linking African cities and regions horizontally. Telephone networks, where they existed, served colonial administrators, settlers, and a small class of African elites, and they remained heavily concentrated in capital cities and colonial administrative centers.

After independence, many African governments inherited and continued to operate state telecommunications monopolies. These monopolies were often chronically underfunded, poorly maintained, and unable to expand fixed-line telephone service much beyond the networks the colonial period had left behind. By the late 1990s, many African countries had fewer than one fixed telephone line per hundred people, and waiting lists for a phone connection in some capital cities stretched for years. This scarcity created enormous unmet demand for basic communication — a demand that mobile telephony, once it became commercially available, would satisfy with startling speed.

The 1990s and 2000s were also a period of economic liberalization across much of Africa, discussed in the chapter on economic development and globalization. Structural adjustment programs and subsequent reform efforts often required or encouraged African governments to break up state telecommunications monopolies, license private mobile network operators, and open markets to competition and foreign investment. Companies such as Vodafone, MTN, Orange, and Airtel, alongside various African-owned operators, competed to build mobile networks across the continent, driving down the cost of handsets and airtime and rapidly expanding coverage, including into rural areas that fixed-line networks had never reached.

At the same time, several other conditions converged to make possible the technology boom described in this chapter. The falling global cost of mobile handsets, driven by manufacturing economies of scale in Asia, made phones affordable to a much larger share of the African population than earlier generations of computing or telecommunications equipment had been. The construction of undersea fiber-optic cables along Africa's coasts beginning in the mid-2000s — projects such as SEACOM on the east coast and various West African cable systems — dramatically increased available international bandwidth and reduced the cost of internet connectivity, which had previously depended on expensive and limited satellite links. A very large, rapidly growing, and increasingly urban youth population, discussed in the chapter on urban Africa, provided both a market for new technologies and a pool of technically skilled young people eager to build businesses and careers around them.

Finally, African governments and regional organizations, including the African Union discussed in an earlier chapter, increasingly identified information and communication technology as a strategic priority for development, investing — with varying degrees of success — in national broadband plans, technology parks, and digital government services. International development agencies and foundations likewise poured resources into digital financial inclusion, e-government, and technology-based education and health interventions, seeing mobile and digital technology as tools that might allow African economies to bypass some of the infrastructure-heavy stages of development that earlier industrializing regions had passed through.

Section 64.1

Technology in Contemporary Africa

Technology has always been part of African history. Earlier chapters described the ironworking traditions that spread with Bantu-speaking peoples, the monumental stone architecture of Great Zimbabwe, the astronomical and mathematical knowledge embedded in various African societies, and the manuscript and printing culture of centers such as Timbuktu. What distinguishes the period this chapter examines — roughly the 1990s to the present — is the speed and scale at which digital and mobile technologies specifically have spread across the continent, and the degree to which Africans have not merely adopted these technologies but adapted and, in some notable cases, pioneered new applications of them.

It is important for students to resist two common oversimplifications when studying this period. The first is to treat Africa's technology story as simply one of catching up to technologies developed elsewhere, arriving late and imitating models built in Silicon Valley, Europe, or East Asia. While much of Africa's hardware, software platforms, and investment capital does originate outside the continent, African engineers, entrepreneurs, and users have frequently built distinctive solutions tailored to local conditions — most famously mobile money systems built around basic phones rather than smartphones, reflecting African market conditions rather than simply copying models from wealthier regions.

The second oversimplification is to treat Africa's technology boom as a uniform, continent-wide phenomenon. In reality, access to electricity, broadband internet, smartphones, digital literacy, and investment capital varies enormously between countries, between urban and rural areas within countries, and between different income groups and genders. A software engineer in Lagos or Nairobi may have access to broadband internet, reliable electricity, and international clients, while a farmer in a rural district only a few hundred kilometers away may lack reliable electricity altogether and depend on a basic mobile phone for a single, limited set of services. Understanding Africa's technology history therefore requires attention to both the genuine achievements of the period and its persistent unevenness.

This section situates the chapter's central case studies — mobile telephony, mobile money, technology hubs, scientific research, and innovation in agriculture and energy — within this broader framework: technology as a continuation of long African traditions of practical problem-solving, occurring within a specific and often constrained set of economic and infrastructural conditions inherited from the colonial and postcolonial past.

Section 64.2

Telecommunications and Connectivity

The single most transformative technological development in recent African history has been the spread of mobile telephony. As discussed in the historical context section, most African countries entered the 1990s with extremely limited fixed-line telephone infrastructure, heavily concentrated in capital cities and serving only a small fraction of the population. The liberalization of telecommunications markets during the 1990s and 2000s allowed private mobile network operators — both African-owned companies and multinational firms such as MTN, Vodafone, Orange, and Airtel — to build competing mobile networks, often reaching areas that fixed-line service had never served.

The results were dramatic. Mobile phone subscriptions across Africa grew from a few million in the late 1990s to hundreds of millions by the 2010s, with mobile penetration in some countries approaching or exceeding one subscription per person (accounting for individuals holding multiple SIM cards). This growth occurred far more rapidly than the decades-long process by which fixed-line telephony had spread in earlier industrializing regions, leading many observers to describe Africa as having "leapfrogged" an entire stage of telecommunications infrastructure — a concept examined critically later in this chapter.

Mobile networks initially supported only voice calls and basic text messaging, using relatively simple and inexpensive handsets that did not require constant internet connectivity. This mattered enormously for adoption: because mobile money and other basic mobile services could function over simple text-messaging protocols, they did not require users to own expensive smartphones or have access to broadband internet, allowing adoption to spread even among low-income and rural populations with only basic handsets.

The expansion of mobile broadband and smartphone internet access followed a somewhat different and more uneven trajectory. The construction of undersea fiber-optic cables along Africa's coasts beginning in the mid-2000s — projects connecting East African cities to global internet infrastructure and similar projects along the West African coast — dramatically reduced the cost of international bandwidth, which had previously depended on expensive satellite links. This infrastructure enabled the growth of mobile internet and broadband services, though access to smartphones and affordable data plans remained considerably more limited than access to basic voice and text service, particularly in rural areas and among lower-income populations. By the 2020s, a majority of African mobile connections were still on basic 2G or 3G networks in many countries, even as urban centers increasingly adopted 4G and, in some cases, early 5G service.

Connectivity infrastructure also connects to questions of national sovereignty and economic development discussed in earlier chapters. Governments have debated policies on data localization (requiring that data generated within a country be stored on servers located there), taxation of mobile services and social media use, and state control over internet access during periods of political unrest — some governments have shut down internet or social media access during elections or protests, raising significant questions about the relationship between digital connectivity and political freedom that connect to the chapter on democracy and governance.

Section 64.3

Mobile Banking and Fintech

No African technology innovation has attracted more international attention or had a more direct effect on ordinary people's lives than mobile money, and no case is more central to that story than M-Pesa, launched in Kenya in 2007 by the mobile network operator Safaricom (majority-owned at the time by Vodafone and the Kenyan government). M-Pesa allowed users to deposit cash with a network of local agents — often small shopkeepers — in exchange for electronic money stored on their mobile phone account, which they could then send to other users via text message, withdraw as cash from another agent, or use to pay for goods and services.

The significance of this innovation lay in the problem it solved. Prior to M-Pesa, most Kenyans — like most Africans across the continent — lacked access to formal banking services. Bank branches were concentrated in cities and required forms of documentation, minimum balances, and travel that excluded much of the rural and low-income population. Sending money to relatives in other parts of the country, a common need given high rates of internal migration for work, typically required physically traveling with cash, sending money informally through bus drivers or acquaintances, or using slow and expensive formal transfer services. M-Pesa allowed money to move instantly and relatively cheaply across long distances using only a basic mobile phone and a network of local agents, without requiring a traditional bank account.

Adoption in Kenya was extraordinarily rapid. Within a few years of its launch, M-Pesa had enrolled a majority of Kenya's adult population, and mobile money transactions came to represent a substantial share of Kenya's gross domestic product flowing through the platform. Economists and development researchers have studied the effects of M-Pesa extensively, finding evidence that access to mobile money reduced poverty in some households, particularly by allowing families to better manage financial shocks such as illness or crop failure by receiving support from relatives elsewhere more quickly and reliably than previous informal transfer methods allowed.

Following Kenya's example, mobile money systems spread to dozens of other African countries during the 2010s, operated by various combinations of mobile network operators, banks, and dedicated fintech companies. These systems expanded beyond basic person-to-person transfers to include bill payment, merchant payments, savings products, and — increasingly — small loans issued based on users' mobile money transaction histories, which served as a form of credit history for populations that had never had access to formal credit scoring. This expansion of "fintech" (financial technology) attracted significant international investment, as investors recognized the scale of the previously "unbanked" population that mobile financial services could reach.

Mobile money and fintech growth has not been without controversy or limitation. Transaction fees, while lower than many formal banking alternatives, still represent a meaningful cost for very low-income users. Some governments have imposed new taxes specifically on mobile money transactions, which fintech advocates argue discourage financial inclusion, while governments argue such taxes capture revenue from a rapidly growing sector that had previously operated largely outside formal taxation. Access to mobile money and related credit products has also raised concerns about over-indebtedness among lower-income borrowers taking out multiple small loans across different platforms. These debates connect to broader questions, examined throughout this chapter, about who benefits most from Africa's technology transformation and on what terms.

Section 64.4

Digital Economies and Startups

Alongside the growth of mobile money, the 2010s and 2020s saw the emergence of broader digital economies and startup ecosystems in several major African cities. Nairobi, sometimes nicknamed "Silicon Savannah," became one of the continent's leading technology hubs, home to numerous software companies, technology incubators such as iHub, and offices of major international technology firms. Lagos developed one of Africa's largest startup ecosystems, particularly strong in fintech and e-commerce, reflecting Nigeria's large population, sizeable middle class, and deep pool of engineering talent. Cairo, Cape Town, and Kigali similarly developed significant, though smaller, technology sectors, each shaped by distinct national policy environments, talent pools, and investor interest.

These hubs produced companies addressing a wide range of local needs: e-commerce platforms adapted to markets with limited postal infrastructure and high reliance on cash payments; ride-hailing and logistics companies adapted to African traffic and delivery conditions; agricultural technology ("agtech") platforms connecting farmers with buyers, credit, and information; health technology ("healthtech") platforms extending access to medical consultation and information in areas with few doctors; and educational technology ("edtech") platforms providing remote learning content, particularly important during school closures caused by the COVID-19 pandemic in the early 2020s.

International venture capital investment in African startups grew substantially over this period, though it remained heavily concentrated in a small number of countries — primarily Nigeria, Kenya, Egypt, and South Africa — that together attracted the large majority of total investment, while many other African countries received comparatively little venture funding. By the early 2020s, a handful of African startups, mostly in fintech, had reached "unicorn" status (private companies valued at over one billion dollars), a milestone that attracted significant international media attention as a signal of the growing scale and credibility of African technology markets, even as critics noted that such milestones remained the exception rather than the rule and that most African technology entrepreneurs continued to operate on a much smaller scale, often within the informal economy.

The growth of digital economies also depended on and interacted with broader trends in African higher education, diaspora networks, and international partnerships. Many founders of successful African startups had studied or worked abroad before returning to build companies at home, drawing on international networks, capital, and business experience — a pattern that connects to broader questions about migration and the African diaspora discussed in earlier chapters. At the same time, growing numbers of African universities began establishing incubator and entrepreneurship programs aimed at supporting locally trained talent, seeking to reduce dependence on internationally educated founders and diaspora capital alone.

Section 64.5

Science, Research, and Higher Education

Africa's technology boom has depended on, and in turn helped to strengthen, its higher education and scientific research institutions, though this relationship remains uneven and constrained by longstanding funding and capacity challenges discussed in the chapter on health, education, and social change. University enrollment across Africa grew substantially in the postcolonial period, and especially from the 1990s onward, as governments and families increasingly viewed higher education as central to individual and national economic advancement. However, growth in enrollment often outpaced growth in funding, faculty numbers, laboratory equipment, and library resources, leading to overcrowded classrooms and limited opportunities for advanced research at many institutions.

Scientific research capacity has varied considerably across the continent. A small number of countries and institutions — including major universities in South Africa, Egypt, Nigeria, and Kenya — have developed relatively strong research programs in fields ranging from public health and genomics to astronomy and renewable energy, in some cases attracting significant international research partnerships and funding. South Africa, for example, became a partner in major international scientific projects such as the Square Kilometre Array radio telescope project, reflecting both its scientific infrastructure and its favorable astronomical conditions. Elsewhere, research capacity remained far more limited, with many universities focused primarily on teaching rather than research due to constrained budgets and heavy teaching loads for faculty.

Efforts to build pan-African scientific and mathematical capacity, such as the African Institute for Mathematical Sciences network of postgraduate centers, sought to address these gaps by training advanced students across multiple countries and connecting them with international faculty and research networks. Regional and continental bodies, including the African Union through its science, technology, and innovation strategy, have identified investment in research and higher education as a strategic priority for the continent's long-term development, though implementation has depended heavily on individual national government commitment and funding, which has varied considerably.

A persistent challenge affecting African science and higher education has been "brain drain" — the emigration of highly trained African scientists, engineers, and medical professionals to wealthier countries offering better funding, facilities, and compensation. This pattern, which connects to earlier chapters on the African diaspora and migration, has meant that a significant share of Africa's most advanced scientific talent has worked outside the continent for much of the postcolonial period, though growing numbers of diaspora scientists and entrepreneurs have in recent decades returned to Africa or established partnerships and investments connecting their countries of origin to international research and technology networks — a phenomenon sometimes described as "brain circulation" rather than simple, one-directional brain drain.

Section 64.6

Innovation in Agriculture and Energy

Beyond telecommunications and finance, technology and innovation have reshaped two sectors of particular importance to African economic life: agriculture and energy. Agricultural technology platforms developed across the continent during the 2010s sought to address longstanding problems facing smallholder farmers, who make up a large share of Africa's working population, as discussed in earlier chapters on farming and rural life. These platforms have used mobile phones to deliver market price information, weather forecasts, and agronomic advice; to connect farmers directly with buyers, reducing dependence on multiple layers of middlemen; and to provide access to credit and crop insurance products tailored to smallholder needs, often using mobile money infrastructure and satellite or mobile-collected data to assess and price agricultural risk.

Energy innovation has been similarly significant, particularly in addressing the large populations across the continent lacking access to reliable electricity from centralized national grids. Off-grid solar companies developed "pay-as-you-go" solar home systems, allowing households to make small, regular mobile money payments — often smaller than the cost of the kerosene, candles, or disposable batteries such households had previously relied on for lighting and phone charging — toward the eventual full ownership of a solar power unit providing basic household electricity. Mini-grid companies developed small, localized electricity generation and distribution systems serving villages or clusters of households not connected to national grids, often using solar power supplemented by battery storage.

These innovations illustrate a broader pattern examined throughout this chapter: African entrepreneurs and engineers frequently developed distinctive business models and technical solutions adapted to specifically African conditions — widespread mobile phone ownership, limited traditional banking and grid infrastructure, and large rural populations — rather than simply importing agricultural or energy technologies designed for very different contexts elsewhere. At the same time, the scale of these innovations, while significant, remained limited relative to the scale of unmet need: hundreds of millions of Africans continued to lack reliable electricity access even as off-grid solar adoption grew, and agricultural technology platforms reached only a fraction of the continent's smallholder farming population by the 2020s.

Section 64.7

Technology and Social Change

The spread of mobile and digital technology has reshaped social life across Africa in ways that extend well beyond commerce and finance. Mobile phones have transformed family communication, particularly for the large populations of internal and international migrants discussed in the chapter on urban Africa and the African diaspora. Where earlier generations of migrants might go months or years with only occasional letters or costly, rare phone calls to communicate with family in rural areas or other countries, mobile phones and, increasingly, internet-based messaging and video calling applications allowed far more frequent, immediate contact, helping sustain family relationships and obligations across long distances of migration.

Social media platforms, accessed increasingly through mobile phones, became important spaces for civic discussion, political organizing, and cultural expression across the continent during the 2010s and 2020s. Younger Africans in particular used platforms such as Twitter, Facebook, and WhatsApp to organize protests and social movements, share information during elections and crises, and build audiences for creative and entrepreneurial projects, a pattern connecting to the chapter on African nationalism and contemporary politics. Governments in several countries responded to this civic use of social media with efforts to restrict access, including internet or social media shutdowns during periods of political tension, elections, or protest — raising ongoing debates, examined further in this chapter's historical debate feature, about the relationship between digital connectivity and political freedom in contemporary Africa.

Technology has also reshaped gender relations and opportunities, though unevenly. Access to mobile phones and digital financial services has in some contexts expanded women's economic independence and control over household resources, particularly where mobile money allowed women to receive and control funds directly rather than through male relatives. At the same time, persistent gender gaps in mobile phone ownership, internet access, and digital literacy — reflecting broader patterns of gender inequality in income, education, and social norms discussed in the chapter on women, family, and gender in African history — meant that women in many contexts continued to have less access to and benefit from new technologies than men.

Section 64.8

Youth, Creativity, and Enterprise

Africa's very large and rapidly growing youth population, discussed in the chapter on urban Africa, has been central to the continent's technology story, both as the primary market for new digital products and services and as the primary source of the entrepreneurial and technical talent building them. Young Africans, often facing limited formal employment opportunities relative to the number of new workers entering the labor market each year, have turned to technology-enabled entrepreneurship and the "gig economy" — freelance and platform-based work such as ride-hailing, delivery, freelance software development, and content creation — as alternatives or supplements to traditional formal employment.

Much of this activity has occurred within what economists call the informal economy — small-scale, often unregistered enterprise operating outside formal taxation and regulation, discussed in earlier chapters on colonial and postcolonial economic history. Mobile phones and digital platforms have in many cases made informal enterprise more efficient and more connected to wider markets — a street vendor might use a mobile money account to receive payment and manage savings, or a small trader might use social media to advertise goods and reach customers beyond their immediate neighborhood — without necessarily formalizing the underlying business in the eyes of tax authorities or regulators.

Digital platforms have also enabled significant growth in African creative industries, discussed further in the chapter on culture, literature, and the arts. Musicians, filmmakers, visual artists, and writers have used social media, streaming platforms, and online marketplaces to reach audiences well beyond their home countries, contributing to the growing international visibility and commercial success of African popular music, film ("Nollywood" and other national film industries), and digital content during this period.

Section 64.9

Africa in the Digital Age

By the 2020s, Africa's relationship with digital technology had become a subject of significant international attention, discussed by economists, development agencies, and technology companies as both an opportunity and a challenge. Optimistic accounts emphasized the continent's large youth population, growing mobile and internet penetration, and demonstrated capacity for locally adapted innovation — exemplified by mobile money — as evidence that Africa could use digital technology to accelerate economic development and even "leapfrog" earlier, more infrastructure-intensive stages of industrialization that other regions had passed through.

More cautious accounts emphasized persistent and substantial gaps: in electricity access, broadband infrastructure, digital literacy, and access to capital, all of which continued to limit how broadly the benefits of the technology sector were shared across the continent's population. They also noted that much of the hardware, cloud infrastructure, and even venture capital funding underlying Africa's technology sector continued to originate outside the continent, raising questions about the degree of genuine technological and economic sovereignty involved in Africa's digital transformation, connecting to broader debates about globalization and dependency discussed in the chapter on economic development and globalization.

This chapter's central argument is that both perspectives contain important truths. Africa's technology transformation during this period represents genuine, historically significant achievement: hundreds of millions of people gained access to communication and financial services that had never been available to them before, and African entrepreneurs and engineers built globally recognized innovations, most notably mobile money. At the same time, this transformation has been uneven, concentrated in particular countries, cities, and social classes, and dependent in important ways on foreign capital, hardware, and platforms — patterns that connect Africa's digital present to longer historical patterns of global economic inequality examined throughout this textbook.

Historical Vocabulary
Leapfrogging

Leapfrogging refers to the idea that a society or economy can skip over an earlier stage of technological development that other regions passed through, moving directly to a more advanced stage. In the African context, the term is most often applied to telecommunications: many African countries never built extensive fixed-line telephone networks before moving directly to mobile telephony, and many Africans gained access to mobile financial services such as M-Pesa without first passing through a stage of widespread traditional banking.

The concept is useful for describing an observable pattern, but students should use it carefully. Leapfrogging does not mean that earlier stages of infrastructure development become unnecessary altogether — reliable electricity, all-weather roads, and formal banking systems remain important for many purposes that mobile technology alone cannot fully replace. The concept is examined critically in this chapter's Historical Debate feature.

Historical Vocabulary
Informal Economy

The informal economy consists of economic activity — small trade, artisanal production, unregistered transport services, casual labor, and similar enterprise — that operates outside formal government registration, taxation, and regulation. The informal economy has long made up a large share of economic activity in many African countries, a pattern with roots in colonial-era economic structures discussed in earlier chapters. Mobile and digital technology have in many cases made informal economic activity more efficient and better connected to wider markets without necessarily formalizing it, a pattern explored further in the Continuity and Change feature in this chapter.

Primary Source Window

Development Research Reports on Mobile Money Adoption in Kenya

Creator: Academic and development-agency researchers studying M-Pesa · Date: Various, 2009–2016 · Type: Economic research reports and household survey studies

Context: Following the rapid adoption of M-Pesa in Kenya after its 2007 launch, numerous economists and development researchers conducted household surveys and statistical studies to measure its effects on poverty, savings behavior, and household resilience to economic shocks such as illness, drought, or job loss.

These studies generally found that households with access to mobile money were better able to receive financial support quickly from extended family members during emergencies, and that increased ease of sending and receiving money contributed to modest but measurable reductions in extreme poverty among households that adopted the service, particularly among female-headed households.

Analysis: These research reports are valuable primary evidence of the real-world economic effects of a specific technological innovation, based on systematic data collection rather than anecdote. However, students should note their limits: most such studies focused specifically on Kenya, where conditions — a dominant mobile network operator, supportive regulation, and an already well-established mobile phone market — were in some ways unusually favorable to mobile money's rapid success, and the same effects have not been observed uniformly in every country where similar services were later introduced.

Questions for students:

  3. What can this kind of quantitative economic research tell historians that a single entrepreneur's success story cannot?
  4. What additional evidence would help determine whether Kenya's experience with mobile money could be generalized to other African countries?
Historical Debate

Does Africa Really "Leapfrog" Stages of Development?

The question: Observers of Africa's mobile phone and mobile money revolution have often described the continent as "leapfrogging" earlier, infrastructure-heavy stages of development that other regions passed through on the way to widespread telecommunications and financial access. Historians and development economists disagree about how much explanatory weight this concept can bear.

Interpretation A — Leapfrogging as a Genuine and Important Pattern: Supporters of the leapfrogging concept point to clear evidence: most Africans gained access to telephone service via mobile networks without ever having fixed-line service, and hundreds of millions gained access to basic financial services via mobile money without ever holding a traditional bank account. This pattern, they argue, demonstrates that developing regions can sometimes adopt newer technologies faster and more broadly than wealthier regions did at a comparable stage, because they are not burdened by prior investment in older infrastructure that must first be replaced.
Interpretation B — Leapfrogging as an Overstated and Potentially Misleading Concept: Critics argue that the leapfrogging framework, while capturing a real pattern in telecommunications, has been applied too broadly to suggest that African economies can bypass more fundamental development needs — reliable electricity, all-weather roads, formal banking and credit systems, and industrial manufacturing capacity — simply by adopting mobile and digital technology. They point to the continued large gaps in electricity access, industrial development, and formal employment across much of the continent as evidence that mobile technology, while valuable, has not substituted for these more fundamental forms of infrastructure and economic development.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on telecommunications adoption statistics and case studies such as M-Pesa. Interpretation B draws on continued electricity access gaps, industrial development statistics, and studies noting the limits of mobile-based interventions in sectors such as agriculture and manufacturing that depend on physical infrastructure mobile technology cannot replace.

Why it matters: How policymakers and investors understand the leapfrogging concept shapes decisions about where to direct scarce development resources — toward continued expansion of digital and mobile technology, toward more traditional infrastructure investment, or toward some combination of both.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above, is "leapfrogging" a useful concept for understanding Africa's technological development, a partially useful concept that needs qualification, or a misleading concept that obscures more than it reveals? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate?

Multiple Perspectives

Who Benefits from Africa's Technology Boom?

From the perspective of urban tech entrepreneurs and engineers: The technology boom represents an extraordinary opportunity to build globally competitive businesses, access international capital and networks, and solve local problems using locally developed solutions, without needing to leave the continent to find meaningful, well-compensated technical work.
From the perspective of rural smallholder farmers and low-income mobile money users: Mobile phones and mobile money have brought genuine, tangible benefits — faster access to market information, easier ways to send and receive money, and access to small amounts of credit — but access to smartphones, broadband internet, and the more advanced digital services enjoyed in cities often remains limited, expensive, or simply unavailable.
From the perspective of international investors and technology companies: Africa represents an enormous and rapidly growing market of new mobile and internet users, offering significant commercial opportunity, particularly in fintech, e-commerce, and telecommunications, alongside real risks related to infrastructure gaps, currency volatility, and regulatory uncertainty across different national markets.
From the perspective of governments seeking to regulate and tax the digital economy: The growth of mobile money and digital commerce represents both an opportunity to expand financial inclusion and formalize previously untaxed economic activity, and a challenge, as governments have sought — sometimes controversially — to tax mobile transactions and social media use in ways that fintech advocates argue undermine the very financial inclusion the technology has achieved.
Cause and Effect

The Mobile Phone and Mobile Money Revolution

Long-Term Causes

Decades of underdeveloped fixed-line telecommunications infrastructure inherited from the colonial and early postcolonial periods; very low rates of formal bank account ownership across much of the continent; a large, dispersed rural population with significant unmet demand for basic communication and financial services.

Short-Term Causes

Telecommunications market liberalization in the 1990s and 2000s; falling global costs of mobile handsets; the 2007 launch of M-Pesa in Kenya by Safaricom; construction of undersea fiber-optic cables beginning in the mid-2000s that reduced internet bandwidth costs.

The Event

Rapid, near-universal adoption of mobile telephony across much of Africa within roughly two decades, followed by widespread adoption of mobile money services beginning in Kenya and spreading to dozens of other African countries during the 2010s.

Effects

Dramatically expanded access to basic communication and financial services for hundreds of millions of previously unconnected and unbanked Africans; growth of technology hubs, startups, and international venture investment; new government revenue debates over mobile transaction taxation; persistent gaps in smartphone and broadband access limiting the depth of digital inclusion.

Historical complexity: The mobile phone and mobile money revolution cannot be explained by any single cause. It resulted from a specific convergence of policy liberalization, falling global hardware costs, targeted local innovation, and new international infrastructure investment — a combination that historians and economists continue to study for lessons applicable to other regions and sectors.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Informal Enterprise from the Colonial Period to the Digital Age

What Changed

  • Small traders and entrepreneurs gained access to mobile money accounts for receiving payment and managing savings
  • Social media and messaging apps allowed informal businesses to reach customers far beyond their immediate neighborhood
  • Mobile phone-based credit scoring allowed some informal traders to access small loans previously unavailable to them
  • Digital delivery and ride-hailing platforms created new categories of informal, platform-based work

What Stayed the Same

  • Much economic activity remained unregistered and outside formal taxation, as it had been throughout the colonial and postcolonial periods
  • Workers in the informal economy continued to lack access to formal labor protections, benefits, and job security
  • Capital remained scarce and expensive for the smallest-scale entrepreneurs, despite new mobile lending products
  • Informal enterprise remained the primary source of livelihood for large shares of the urban and rural workforce across much of the continent

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of informality alongside genuine technological change illustrates that new tools can improve the efficiency and reach of existing economic patterns without necessarily transforming the underlying structural conditions — regulation, capital access, and labor protection — that shape those patterns.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Day with a Mobile Phone in Contemporary Urban Africa

Consider how a single mobile phone might structure an ordinary day for a small trader in a large African city during the 2010s or 2020s. In the morning, she checks a mobile money balance to confirm payments received from customers the previous evening, then uses a portion of those funds to pay a supplier via mobile transfer rather than carrying cash across the city. During the day, she uses a messaging app to coordinate with regular customers, sending photographs of new stock and confirming delivery times with a motorcycle courier booked through a ride-hailing or delivery app. In the evening, she uses her phone to check remittances sent by a sibling working in another country, funds that help cover a portion of household expenses and school fees for her children. She may also use the same device to follow news and social media discussion of a current political controversy, and to make a small savings deposit into a mobile-based savings product intended to build toward a larger purchase.

This single device, often a relatively basic smartphone rather than the most advanced model, thus mediates commerce, family communication across distance, civic information, and personal financial planning — functions that a generation earlier would have required a bank, a post office, a newspaper, a landline telephone, and considerable time and travel to accomplish separately, if they could be accomplished at all. At the same time, her access to these functions depends on continued affordability of data and airtime, a reasonably reliable source of electricity to charge the device, and a functioning agent network for cash-in and cash-out services — dependencies that remain more secure in her urban setting than they would be for a similarly situated user in a remote rural area.

Chapter Review and Innovation Checkpoint

This chapter has examined Africa's technological and scientific transformation from the 1990s to the present: the spread of mobile telephony and mobile money, the growth of digital economies and technology hubs, the state of scientific research and higher education, innovation in agriculture and energy, and the broader social effects of new technology on family life, civic participation, and youth enterprise. Before moving to the textbook's concluding chapter on Africa's future, students should be able to explain both the achievements and the limitations of this transformation, and to evaluate critically concepts such as "leapfrogging" that are often used to describe it.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined the technological and scientific transformation of Africa from the 1990s to the present, focusing on telecommunications, mobile money and fintech, digital economies and startups, scientific research and higher education, and innovation in agriculture and energy. It has argued that this transformation should be understood neither as a simple story of catching up to technologies developed elsewhere, nor as a uniform, continent-wide phenomenon, but as an uneven process shaped by specific historical conditions, policy choices, and geographic and economic inequalities.

The chapter began by tracing the roots of Africa's telecommunications gap to colonial and early postcolonial infrastructure patterns, in which fixed-line telephone networks reached only a small fraction of the population, concentrated in capital cities and serving colonial administrators and elites. The liberalization of telecommunications markets during the 1990s and 2000s, combined with falling global handset costs, allowed private mobile network operators to build networks that reached far beyond what fixed-line infrastructure ever had, producing one of the most rapid technology adoption processes in modern economic history.

Building on this mobile telephone infrastructure, Kenya's M-Pesa system, launched in 2007, created a globally influential model for mobile money, allowing users to send, receive, and store money using only a basic mobile phone, without requiring a traditional bank account. This innovation, and the many similar mobile money systems that followed it across the continent during the 2010s, brought basic financial services to hundreds of millions of previously unbanked Africans, with documented effects on household financial resilience and, in some studies, modest poverty reduction.

The chapter then examined the growth of broader digital economies and technology hubs in cities such as Nairobi, Lagos, Cairo, Cape Town, and Kigali, which produced companies addressing local needs in e-commerce, logistics, agriculture, health, and education, and which attracted growing — though geographically concentrated — international venture capital investment. It considered the state of scientific research and higher education across the continent, noting significant growth in university enrollment alongside persistent funding and capacity constraints, and examined efforts to build pan-African research capacity alongside ongoing challenges related to the emigration of highly trained scientists and engineers.

Innovation in agriculture and energy was examined as a further example of technology adapted to specifically African conditions: mobile-based agricultural information and credit services, and off-grid solar and mini-grid energy systems serving populations bypassed by centralized national electricity grids. The chapter then turned to the broader social effects of mobile and digital technology, including transformed family communication across distances of migration, the use of social media for civic and political organizing, and uneven effects on gender relations and opportunity.

The chapter examined the central role of Africa's large youth population in both consuming and producing new technology, including through informal, gig-economy, and creative-industry enterprise that has often used digital tools to become more efficient and better connected to wider markets without necessarily becoming formalized in the eyes of governments and tax authorities.

Finally, the chapter critically examined the concept of "leapfrogging" — the idea that Africa has skipped earlier, more infrastructure-intensive stages of development on the way to advanced mobile and digital technology adoption. It argued that this concept captures a genuine and important pattern in telecommunications and financial services, but that it should not be overextended to suggest that mobile technology alone can substitute for continued investment in more fundamental forms of infrastructure, including reliable electricity, transportation, formal banking, and industrial capacity.

Throughout, the chapter emphasized that Africa's technology transformation, like the historical developments examined in earlier chapters, has been the product of African agency and innovation operating within specific, often constraining structural conditions — colonial and postcolonial infrastructure legacies, global capital flows, and continuing economic inequality between and within African countries. Understanding this balance between achievement and constraint is essential to interpreting Africa's technological present and anticipating its future, the subject of the textbook's concluding chapter.

Students should leave this chapter with a clear sense that technology in Africa is neither a simple success story nor a simple story of dependency and exclusion, but a complex, ongoing historical process whose ultimate consequences — for economic development, political freedom, gender equality, and Africa's place in the global economy — remain to be determined by choices that governments, entrepreneurs, investors, and ordinary Africans continue to make.

The chapter's evidence base — telecommunications statistics, economic research studies, venture capital reports, government policy documents, and the everyday experiences of users and entrepreneurs — illustrates the kinds of sources historians of the very recent past must use, since the period examined in this chapter is still unfolding and its long-term historical significance is still being determined.

This chapter connects backward to earlier chapters on colonial economies, postcolonial development, urbanization, and globalization, and forward to the textbook's final chapter, which considers how the patterns examined here — demographic growth, technological change, economic opportunity, and governance — may shape Africa's future in the decades ahead.

Key Terms

Leapfrogging
The concept that a society can skip an earlier stage of technological development, moving directly to a more advanced stage, as when many Africans gained mobile telephone service without ever having fixed-line service.
Mobile money
Financial services, such as sending, receiving, and storing money, provided through a mobile phone account rather than a traditional bank account.
Fintech
Financial technology; companies and products that use digital tools to provide banking, payment, lending, or insurance services.
Digital economy
Economic activity conducted through digital platforms and tools, including e-commerce, digital payments, and online services.
Startup ecosystem
The network of entrepreneurs, investors, incubators, and support institutions that enables new technology companies to form and grow in a particular location.
Venture capital
Investment capital provided to early-stage companies, typically in exchange for an ownership stake, in anticipation of significant future growth.
Technology hub
A city or district that concentrates technology companies, investors, and skilled workers, such as Nairobi or Lagos.
Incubator
An organization that supports early-stage startups with resources such as workspace, mentorship, and connections to investors.
Digital divide
The gap between populations with reliable access to digital technology and the internet and those without such access.
Broadband
High-speed internet connectivity, as opposed to slower, more limited connections.
Undersea cable
A fiber-optic cable laid along the ocean floor connecting internet infrastructure between continents or coastal regions.
Off-grid energy
Electricity generation and use that is not connected to a centralized national power grid, such as household solar systems.
Mini-grid
A small, localized electricity generation and distribution system serving a limited area not connected to the national grid.
Informal economy
Economic activity that occurs outside formal government registration, taxation, and regulation.
Digital literacy
The skills and knowledge needed to use digital devices and services effectively.
E-government
The use of digital technology by government agencies to provide services and information to citizens.
Financial inclusion
The extension of accessible and affordable financial services to populations previously excluded from formal banking.
Unicorn (startup)
A privately held startup company valued at over one billion dollars.
Telecommunications liberalization
The process of ending state telecommunications monopolies and opening markets to private and competitive providers.
Research capacity
The institutional, financial, and human resources available to conduct scientific and scholarly research.
Brain drain
The emigration of highly trained professionals from their home country to other countries offering better opportunities.
Brain circulation
The movement of skilled professionals between countries in ways that create ongoing exchange and return, rather than permanent, one-directional emigration.
Diaspora investment
Capital invested in a home country by members of its emigrant diaspora community.
Gig economy
An economy characterized by short-term, freelance, or platform-based work rather than traditional long-term employment.
Data localization
A policy requiring that data generated within a country be stored on servers physically located in that country.
Digital identity
A system of identification and verification conducted through digital means, increasingly used for accessing government and financial services.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it demonstrates that African history did not end with independence, decolonization, or the challenges of the late twentieth century discussed in earlier chapters. It is an ongoing, unfinished story in which Africans continue to adapt available tools — from mobile phones to solar panels to social media — to solve specifically local problems, often in ways that attract global attention and, in the case of mobile money, global imitation.

The technology transformation examined here shaped later African history by expanding financial inclusion, creating new categories of entrepreneurship and employment, and connecting African civic and cultural life more directly to global conversations, while also revealing and in some ways deepening existing inequalities between countries, regions, classes, and genders. Understanding both the achievement and the unevenness of this transformation prepares students to think critically about claims — whether celebratory or dismissive — made about Africa's technological future.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is one that runs throughout this textbook: African societies have consistently shown the capacity for practical innovation and adaptation in response to the specific conditions they face, from ancient ironworking to medieval manuscript culture to twenty-first-century mobile money, even as those innovations have always occurred within larger structures of global economic power and inequality that Africans did not fully control. This tension between agency and constraint is central to understanding not only Africa's technological present, but its history as a whole.

As the textbook turns to its concluding chapter on Africa's future, students should carry forward the central questions this chapter has raised: who benefits from technological change, who is left behind, what continuities persist alongside rapid transformation, and what choices — by governments, entrepreneurs, investors, and ordinary Africans — will shape whether the promise of Africa's digital transformation is more broadly and equitably realized in the decades ahead.

Part X — Contemporary Africa
65

Africa and the World

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"For most of the twentieth century, Africa's international relationships were described in terms of what the continent received — aid, loans, missionaries, advisers. In the twenty-first century, a growing share of the conversation concerns what Africa offers — markets, minerals, votes at the United Nations, labor, land, and a demographic future unlike that of any other continent on Earth."

In the first week of September 2018, delegations from more than fifty African heads of state traveled to Beijing for the seventh Forum on China-Africa Cooperation, known by its acronym FOCAC. Motorcades of African leaders moved through streets lined with the flags of their nations alongside the red flag of the People's Republic of China. Chinese state media described the gathering as evidence of an unbreakable friendship between China and Africa stretching back to the era of anticolonial solidarity. African leaders, for their part, used the occasion to announce infrastructure agreements, trade deals, and financing packages worth tens of billions of dollars. A little more than a decade earlier, few outside specialist policy circles would have predicted that an Asian power with a rapidly growing economy would become one of the most consequential external actors in African economic life, rivaling or in some respects surpassing the influence of Europe and the United States, the two regions that had dominated Africa's external relations for more than a century through conquest, colonization, and the Cold War.

This chapter studies Africa's place within a changing global system during the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, tracing how the continent's international relationships diversified beyond the former colonial powers of Western Europe and the Cold War superpower rivalry examined in earlier chapters. Students will learn about Africa's relationship with China, whose trade, investment, and infrastructure financing expanded dramatically after the 1990s; with Europe, whose historical ties to Africa remain deep but increasingly contested; with the United States, whose engagement has shifted across security, trade, and health priorities; with India and the Gulf states, whose economic and diasporic connections to Africa have grown substantially; and with the broader project of South-South cooperation, in which African states have sought common cause with other developing regions of the world.

The chapter also examines two dimensions of Africa's global connections that operate below the level of state-to-state diplomacy: the ongoing movement of African people across borders and oceans as migrants, students, workers, and refugees, and the persistent and growing influence of the African diaspora on politics, culture, and economics both within Africa and in the countries where diaspora communities have settled. It considers how African governments and institutions, including the African Union discussed in an earlier chapter, have sought to project African interests collectively rather than negotiate with powerful external partners one country at a time, and it examines Africa's expanding role in cultural, sporting, and diplomatic life on the world stage.

It matters because how Africa is perceived and how Africa engages with the rest of the world shapes economic development, political sovereignty, and the daily lives of hundreds of millions of people. For much of the colonial and early postcolonial period, Africa's international relationships were shaped overwhelmingly by the interests and priorities of European colonial powers and, during the Cold War, by the strategic competition between the United States and the Soviet Union, discussed in the chapter on Africa and the Cold War. Since the end of the Cold War, and especially since the early 2000s, African states have increasingly engaged a wider and more varied set of international partners, giving many African governments greater room to negotiate on their own terms, while also raising new questions about debt, sovereignty, labor conditions, environmental impact, and the terms on which African resources and markets are integrated into the global economy.

Much has changed in this period. China moved from a marginal trading partner to Africa's largest single trading partner and a major source of infrastructure financing. India, though a colonial-era trading partner through networks tied to the Indian Ocean world discussed in earlier chapters, expanded its economic and diplomatic engagement substantially in the twenty-first century. Gulf states such as the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar became significant sources of investment, particularly in ports, agriculture, and finance, and Turkey expanded diplomatic and commercial ties across the continent. The United States shifted its African engagement across administrations, at times emphasizing counterterrorism and security partnerships, at times prioritizing trade and investment initiatives, and at times focusing heavily on public health, particularly through programs addressing HIV/AIDS.

Much has also continued. Europe remains Africa's largest source of foreign direct investment in aggregate and its most significant partner in development assistance, migration policy, and historical and cultural ties, even as European influence has become one voice among several rather than the dominant external presence it once was. The structural position of many African economies as exporters of raw materials and importers of manufactured goods and services — a pattern with deep roots in the colonial economic structures discussed in earlier chapters — has persisted even as the identity of Africa's trading partners has diversified. Debates over whether new international partners represent a genuine alternative to older patterns of external dependency or simply a different set of external actors pursuing similar extractive interests continue to shape both scholarly and political discussion of Africa's global position.

The evidence historians and social scientists use to understand this period includes trade and investment statistics from international financial institutions, bilateral agreements and loan contracts, diplomatic communiqués and summit declarations, migration and remittance data, opinion surveys conducted across African countries, journalism and scholarship examining specific infrastructure projects and their local effects, and the accounts of African policymakers, business leaders, workers, and communities directly affected by international investment and trade. This evidence reveals both the expanding room for maneuver that African governments have gained from a more diverse set of international partners and the persistent structural challenges — debt burdens, unequal trade terms, and uneven benefits — that continue to shape Africa's position in the global economy.

This chapter connects directly to earlier chapters on colonial economies, the Cold War, economic development and globalization, and the African diaspora, while also looking forward to the textbook's concluding chapter on Africa's future, in which questions about international partnership, debt, trade, and diplomacy will remain central to understanding the choices facing African governments and societies in the decades ahead.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain how Africa's international relationships diversified beyond former colonial powers after the end of the Cold War.
  2. Trace the growth of China's trade, investment, and infrastructure financing relationship with African states since the 1990s.
  3. Describe the changing character of Africa's relationship with Europe, including trade, aid, migration policy, and historical memory.
  4. Analyze the shifting priorities of United States engagement with Africa across security, trade, and public health.
  5. Identify the growing economic and diasporic ties linking Africa to India and the Gulf states.
  6. Evaluate the aims and limitations of South-South cooperation as a framework for African international relations.
  7. Assess the significance of African migration, remittances, and diaspora communities for African economies and societies.
  8. Compare different perspectives on whether new international partnerships represent a genuine alternative to older patterns of dependency.
  9. Interpret debates over debt, sovereignty, and the terms of international trade and investment agreements involving African states.
  10. Connect this chapter to earlier discussions of colonial economies, the Cold War, and globalization.
  11. Contextualize Africa's expanding diplomatic, cultural, and sporting presence on the world stage.
  12. Distinguish between state-to-state diplomacy and the everyday, individual-level connections of migration, remittances, and cultural exchange that link Africa to the world.

Historical Context

To understand Africa's contemporary global relationships, students should recall the long history of external engagement with the continent discussed throughout this textbook: the trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean trade networks that connected West and East Africa to the Mediterranean, Arabia, and Asia centuries before European colonization; the Atlantic slave trade and the colonial economic systems that oriented African production toward European markets and reduced most African economies to exporters of raw materials; and the Cold War period, examined in an earlier chapter, during which African governments frequently found themselves courted, pressured, or destabilized by the competing strategic interests of the United States and the Soviet Union.

The end of the Cold War around 1991 removed the bipolar framework that had structured much of Africa's international relations for more than four decades. Superpower competition for African allegiance diminished as the Soviet Union collapsed, and many African governments that had relied on Cold War-era patronage from one superpower or the other faced a sudden reduction in external support, contributing to the political and economic crises of the 1990s discussed in earlier chapters on democracy, governance, and economic development.

At the same time, the 1990s and 2000s saw the rise of new economic powers whose growing wealth and global ambitions created new possibilities for African international engagement. China's extraordinary economic growth from the 1980s onward, driven by market reforms and export-oriented industrialization, created enormous demand for raw materials — oil, minerals, timber, and agricultural commodities — that African economies could supply, alongside a large pool of Chinese capital, construction expertise, and manufactured goods that African governments and consumers sought. India's economic liberalization beginning in 1991 similarly expanded its global economic reach, including toward Africa, building on older historical ties through the Indian Ocean trading world and through Indian diaspora communities established in East and Southern Africa during the colonial period, discussed in earlier chapters.

Gulf states, enriched by decades of oil wealth, increasingly sought to diversify their own economies and food supplies by investing in African agriculture, ports, and infrastructure, while also pursuing religious, diplomatic, and strategic interests connected to the Horn of Africa and the Red Sea, a region discussed in earlier chapters on Axum and the Indian Ocean world. Turkey, Brazil, and other so-called "emerging" or "middle" powers likewise expanded diplomatic missions, trade offices, and cultural programs across the continent during the 2000s and 2010s, seeking new markets, diplomatic allies, and, in some cases, religious and cultural connections.

Meanwhile, Europe's relationship with Africa evolved from the direct colonial administration examined in earlier chapters, through the patron-client relationships of the early postcolonial period, toward a more institutionalized framework of trade agreements, development assistance, and migration policy coordinated partly through the European Union. This relationship remained the single largest source of European Union-administered aid to any world region and a major destination for African migration, students, and trade, even as its relative share of Africa's total international engagement diminished alongside the rise of other partners.

Throughout this period, African governments and regional institutions, including the Organization of African Unity and its successor the African Union discussed in an earlier chapter, sought — with mixed success — to negotiate collectively with powerful external partners rather than allowing bilateral relationships between individual African states and much larger foreign powers to determine the terms of the continent's international economic integration. This context of diversifying partnerships, evolving multilateral institutions, and enduring structural challenges frames the specific relationships and phenomena examined in the sections that follow.

Section 65.1

Africa in the Global System

Africa's place in the global system has never been static. Earlier chapters traced how African societies participated in the trans-Saharan gold and salt trade, the Indian Ocean commercial world linking the Swahili Coast to Arabia and Asia, and the Atlantic economy that, however violently and unequally, connected West and Central Africa to the Americas and Europe. The colonial period reorganized these connections around the needs of European empires, and the Cold War superimposed a new layer of strategic competition on top of colonial and early postcolonial economic patterns. This chapter examines the period from roughly the end of the Cold War to the present, during which Africa's international relationships diversified more rapidly and more broadly than at any point since the era of European colonization began.

Several forces drove this diversification. The end of Cold War bipolarity removed the framework that had structured much of Africa's Cold War-era diplomacy, discussed in an earlier chapter, in which African governments often aligned with the United States or the Soviet Union in exchange for military and economic support. The rise of new economic powers — most significantly China, but also India, Brazil, Turkey, and the Gulf states — created a much wider field of potential international partners for African governments seeking trade, investment, and diplomatic support. Meanwhile, African governments and institutions, including the African Union, increasingly sought to assert African agency in shaping the terms of international engagement, rather than serving primarily as the object of external powers' strategic competition.

Scholars sometimes describe this period as one of growing "multipolarity" in Africa's international relations: rather than choosing between a dominant colonial power or one of two Cold War superpowers, African governments by the twenty-first century could often choose among several major international partners, weighing the terms, conditions, and strategic implications of each relationship. This has given many African governments greater negotiating leverage than they possessed during the colonial or Cold War periods, though scholars disagree — as this chapter's Historical Debate feature explores — about how much this expanded set of choices has actually changed the underlying structural position of African economies within the global economy.

It is also important for students to recognize that "Africa's" relationship with the world is not a single, uniform story. Fifty-four recognized African states, along with numerous subnational regions and cities, engage international partners in widely varying ways depending on their natural resource endowments, geographic location, political systems, and historical ties. Oil-exporting states such as Nigeria and Angola have different international relationships than landlocked states with limited natural resource wealth. States along the Red Sea and Horn of Africa, of strategic importance for global shipping and security, have different relationships with external powers than states in the interior of the continent. This chapter examines general patterns while emphasizing that specific African states and regions experience these patterns quite differently.

Section 65.2

Relations with China

No single external relationship has transformed Africa's international economic position more dramatically since the 1990s than its relationship with China. China's trade with Africa grew from a modest few billion dollars annually in the 1990s to hundreds of billions of dollars by the 2010s, making China Africa's largest single trading partner for the continent as a whole, though the specific balance of trade and investment varies considerably from country to country.

This relationship has taken several distinct forms. Chinese state-owned and private companies have invested heavily in African extractive industries, particularly oil in countries such as Angola and Sudan, and minerals including copper, cobalt, and various other metals critical to global manufacturing and, increasingly, to the batteries and electronics required for renewable energy and digital technology. Chinese firms have financed and constructed major infrastructure projects — railways, roads, ports, airports, dams, and telecommunications networks — often through concessional or resource-backed loans in which African governments repay Chinese lenders using revenue from natural resource exports or through more conventional sovereign borrowing.

The institutional framework for this relationship has been formalized through the Forum on China-Africa Cooperation, established in 2000 and held roughly every three years, alternating between Beijing and African host cities. FOCAC summits typically produce joint declarations and specific financing pledges covering infrastructure, agriculture, health, education, and security cooperation. China's 2013 Belt and Road Initiative, a global infrastructure and investment program, extended this framework further, with numerous African states signing memoranda of understanding to participate in Belt and Road-financed projects.

Chinese engagement in Africa has also included substantial migration of Chinese workers, managers, and small business owners to the continent, alongside growing numbers of African students studying in Chinese universities and African professionals training in Chinese-funded programs. Chinese state media and cultural institutions, including Confucius Institutes established at African universities, have sought to build cultural and linguistic ties alongside economic relationships, part of a broader effort — examined further in the Historical Debate feature below — to build what scholars call "soft power," or influence achieved through cultural and diplomatic appeal rather than military or coercive means.

This relationship has generated significant debate, both in Africa and internationally, about its costs and benefits. Supporters point to genuine infrastructure achievements — railways, ports, and power plants that Western donors and lenders had often been unwilling or unable to finance — delivered relatively quickly and often without the political conditions regarding governance or human rights that Western development assistance frequently attached. Critics point to concerns about debt sustainability, the quality and labor practices of some Chinese-financed projects, environmental impact, and the risk that African governments could become excessively dependent on a single external creditor, sometimes described using the contested term "debt-trap diplomacy," examined critically later in this chapter.

Section 65.3

Relations with Europe

Europe's relationship with Africa carries the deepest and most complicated historical layering of any external relationship examined in this chapter, shaped by centuries of trade contact, the Atlantic slave trade, colonial conquest and administration, and the decolonization struggles discussed in earlier chapters. In the postcolonial period, Europe — first through individual former colonial powers and increasingly through the institutions of the European Economic Community and later the European Union — remained Africa's largest source of development assistance, foreign investment, and, for many African countries, its largest trading partner well into the twenty-first century.

The European Union developed a series of formal frameworks governing its economic relationship with African, Caribbean, and Pacific states, beginning with agreements in the 1970s that provided preferential trade access and development aid to former colonies, and evolving over subsequent decades into more comprehensive economic partnership agreements intended to gradually open African markets to European goods in exchange for continued European market access for African exports. These agreements generated significant debate within Africa, with critics arguing that opening African markets to European competition too rapidly could undermine local industries still developing the capacity to compete internationally, an argument connecting to broader debates about development strategy discussed in the chapter on economic development and globalization.

Migration has become an increasingly central and often contentious dimension of the Europe-Africa relationship, particularly following the sharp increase in irregular migration across the Mediterranean Sea during the 2010s, when large numbers of migrants and asylum seekers from across Africa and the Middle East sought to reach Europe, often via dangerous sea crossings organized by smuggling networks. European governments and the European Union responded with a combination of increased humanitarian and development assistance directed at African countries of origin and transit, stricter border enforcement, and agreements with North African governments intended to reduce departures, policies that drew significant criticism from human rights organizations concerned about conditions faced by migrants intercepted or returned under these arrangements.

At the same time, Europe's relative economic weight in Africa has diminished as other partners, particularly China, have expanded their engagement, prompting European governments and the European Union to develop new initiatives — including infrastructure investment programs intended in part to offer an alternative to Chinese-financed projects — aimed at maintaining European relevance and influence on the continent. European governments have also faced growing pressure, particularly from younger African publics and activists, to address historical grievances connected to colonialism, including debates over the restitution of African cultural artifacts held in European museums, apologies for colonial-era violence, and the broader legacy of colonial economic extraction discussed throughout this textbook.

Section 65.4

Relations with the United States

United States engagement with Africa since the end of the Cold War has shifted across several distinct priorities, reflecting changing strategic interests and the priorities of successive presidential administrations. During the 1990s, U.S. Africa policy often emphasized democratization and conflict resolution, reflecting the wave of political liberalization and, in some countries, devastating civil conflict discussed in earlier chapters on democracy, governance, and conflict and peace in contemporary Africa.

Trade and investment became a more prominent theme with the passage of the African Growth and Opportunity Act in 2000, which granted eligible sub-Saharan African countries preferential access to U.S. markets for a range of exports, particularly textiles and apparel, agricultural products, and certain manufactured goods, provided that beneficiary countries met conditions related to market-based economic policies, labor rights, and governance. AGOA has been renewed multiple times and remains a significant, if contested, framework shaping U.S.-Africa trade relations, with debates continuing over its actual economic benefits for African economies relative to its conditions and limitations.

Public health became a defining feature of U.S. engagement with Africa following the launch of the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief, known as PEPFAR, in 2003, which became one of the largest bilateral global health initiatives in history, providing HIV/AIDS treatment, prevention, and support services across dozens of African countries and saving millions of lives, a development connecting directly to the public health themes discussed in the chapter on health, education, and social change. Subsequent U.S. health initiatives extended to malaria prevention, maternal and child health, and, during the COVID-19 pandemic, vaccine distribution, though the scale and consistency of U.S. health assistance has varied across different administrations and budget priorities.

Security cooperation has grown as a significant, and at times controversial, dimension of U.S.-Africa relations, particularly following the rise of extremist and militant groups in the Sahel, Somalia, and elsewhere on the continent, discussed in the chapter on conflict, peace, and reconciliation. The United States established the U.S. Africa Command (AFRICOM) in 2007 to coordinate American military engagement on the continent, including training programs, counterterrorism operations, and, in some cases, direct military action, generating debate about the appropriate balance between security assistance and the risk of entangling the United States in African conflicts or being perceived as prioritizing security over development and governance. U.S. engagement has also included periodic trade and investment summits and initiatives aimed at mobilizing American private investment in African infrastructure and business, though American investment levels in Africa have generally remained smaller than European or, in later years, Chinese investment.

Section 65.5

Relations with India and the Gulf States

India's relationship with Africa draws on historical connections stretching back through the Indian Ocean trading world discussed in earlier chapters, as well as the substantial Indian diaspora communities established in East and Southern Africa during the colonial period, many of whose members became prominent in trade, commerce, and, in some cases, political life, as discussed in earlier chapters on colonial society. Following India's economic liberalization beginning in 1991, Indian trade and investment in Africa expanded considerably, with Indian companies active in sectors including telecommunications, pharmaceuticals, information technology, and energy.

India has positioned itself diplomatically as a partner emphasizing shared historical experience as formerly colonized nations and mutual economic benefit rather than the scale of infrastructure financing associated with China, hosting periodic India-Africa Forum Summits to coordinate trade, investment, and development cooperation. Indian pharmaceutical companies, in particular, have played a significant role in supplying affordable generic medications to African health systems, connecting to the public health themes discussed elsewhere in this textbook, while Indian information technology and telecommunications firms have participated in Africa's digital economy growth discussed in the chapter on technology and innovation.

Gulf Cooperation Council states — including the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar — expanded their economic engagement with Africa substantially during the 2000s and 2010s, driven by a combination of strategic and economic motivations. Gulf states have invested heavily in African ports, particularly along the Red Sea and Horn of Africa coasts, reflecting both commercial interests in African trade and strategic interests in securing shipping routes and regional influence in a part of Africa with deep historical ties to the Arabian Peninsula, discussed in earlier chapters on Axum, Islam in Africa, and the Indian Ocean world. Gulf sovereign wealth funds have also invested in African agriculture, in some cases acquiring or leasing large areas of farmland to grow food crops for export back to food-import-dependent Gulf economies, a practice that has drawn criticism from land rights advocates concerned about the effects of such acquisitions on local farming communities.

Religious and cultural ties have also shaped Gulf-Africa relations, particularly in the historically Muslim regions of North Africa, the Sahel, and the Horn of Africa discussed in the chapter on Islam in Africa, with Gulf-funded religious institutions, educational programs, and charitable organizations playing significant roles in some African Muslim communities. Turkey has pursued a similarly expansive diplomatic and commercial strategy, opening numerous new embassies across the continent during the 2010s and expanding trade, construction contracts, and, in some cases, military cooperation, particularly in Somalia and Libya, reflecting Turkey's own ambitions to expand its global diplomatic and economic reach beyond its traditional focus on Europe and the Middle East.

Section 65.6

South-South Cooperation

Alongside its bilateral relationships with major powers, Africa has participated in broader efforts to build cooperation among developing countries of the Global South, a concept with roots stretching back to the Non-Aligned Movement and Pan-African diplomacy of the independence era discussed in earlier chapters, when African, Asian, and Latin American leaders sought to assert an independent path between the Cold War superpowers and to advocate collectively for a more equitable international economic order.

In the contemporary period, South-South cooperation has taken institutional form through groupings such as BRICS — originally Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa, later expanded to include additional members — which has sought to coordinate the economic and diplomatic interests of major developing and emerging economies, including through the establishment of the New Development Bank as an alternative source of development financing to traditional Western-dominated institutions such as the World Bank and International Monetary Fund. South Africa's membership in BRICS has given it a distinctive diplomatic platform, though scholars debate how much South Africa's participation has translated into broader benefits for the African continent as a whole, as opposed to primarily advancing South Africa's own national interests.

African states have also pursued South-South cooperation through trade and investment relationships with Brazil, whose historical and cultural ties to Africa — particularly to Portuguese-speaking African countries such as Angola and Mozambique, and more broadly through the African diaspora heritage of Brazilian society discussed in the chapter on the African diaspora — have supported growing agricultural, energy, and infrastructure cooperation. Regional South-South initiatives, including cooperation among African, Asian, and Latin American agricultural research institutions, have sought to share technology and expertise particularly relevant to tropical and developing-country contexts, distinct from technology developed primarily for wealthy, temperate-climate economies.

Scholars and policymakers continue to debate how much South-South cooperation genuinely differs from Africa's relationships with wealthier Northern powers, given that emerging powers such as China, India, and Brazil pursue their own national economic and strategic interests in Africa much as European and American actors do. Supporters of the South-South framework argue that shared historical experiences of colonialism and underdevelopment, along with an explicit rhetorical commitment to non-interference and mutual respect for sovereignty, distinguish these relationships in important ways from older North-South patterns. Critics argue that the underlying economic dynamics — the exchange of African raw materials for manufactured goods, technology, and capital — often resemble earlier colonial and postcolonial trade patterns regardless of whether the external partner is located in the Global North or the Global South, a debate connecting directly to the Historical Debate feature examined later in this chapter.

Section 65.7

Migration and the Diaspora

Beyond formal diplomatic and trade relationships between governments, Africa's connection to the rest of the world operates powerfully at the level of individual and family migration. Africans have migrated internationally for education, work, family reunification, and, in significant numbers, to escape conflict, persecution, and economic hardship, discussed in earlier chapters on conflict and peace and on urban Africa. Major destinations for African migrants have included Europe, particularly former colonial powers such as France, the United Kingdom, Portugal, and Belgium; the Gulf states, where large numbers of African workers, particularly from East Africa and the Horn, have taken up labor migration positions in construction, domestic work, and services; North America; and other African countries, since the majority of African international migration actually occurs within the continent itself rather than to destinations overseas.

Remittances — money sent by migrants back to family members in their countries of origin — have become an economically significant flow for many African countries, in some cases exceeding the value of foreign direct investment or development assistance received by the same country. These remittances support household consumption, education, healthcare, and small business investment, connecting individual migration decisions directly to the economic wellbeing of families and communities that remain in Africa, a pattern that echoes and extends the internal migration and remittance patterns discussed in the chapter on urban Africa.

The African diaspora — communities of African descent living outside the continent, including both those connected to the historical Atlantic and Indian Ocean slave trades discussed in earlier chapters and more recent voluntary migrants and their descendants — has increasingly been recognized by African governments and the African Union as a significant economic and diplomatic resource. The African Union has designated the diaspora as a "sixth region" of the continent, alongside its five geographic regions, reflecting efforts to formally engage diaspora communities in investment, skills transfer, and diplomatic advocacy on Africa's behalf in the countries where diaspora communities reside.

Diaspora communities have also played significant roles in shaping international perceptions of Africa, in advocating for African interests in the political systems of countries such as the United States, the United Kingdom, and France, and in maintaining cultural, religious, and family ties that sustain connections between Africa and its diaspora across generations. At the same time, "brain drain" — the emigration of highly trained African professionals, discussed in the chapter on technology and innovation — remains a significant concern for many African countries, even as growing numbers of diaspora professionals have returned to Africa or established investment and business ties connecting their countries of origin to international networks, a pattern sometimes described as "brain circulation" rather than simple, one-directional loss of talent.

Section 65.8

Trade and Diplomacy

African states have sought, with varying degrees of success, to strengthen their collective bargaining position in international trade and diplomacy rather than negotiating individually with much larger and more powerful external partners. The African Continental Free Trade Area, signed in 2018 and implemented gradually in subsequent years, represents the most significant recent effort in this direction, aiming to reduce tariffs and other barriers to trade among African states themselves, in the hope of building larger, more integrated African markets that could support industrialization and reduce the continent's historical dependence on exporting raw materials to, and importing manufactured goods from, external partners, a pattern with deep roots in the colonial economic structures discussed in earlier chapters.

African states have also sought to coordinate diplomatic positions on major international issues through the African Union and through African representation at global institutions such as the United Nations, the World Trade Organization, and international climate negotiations, where African governments have advocated collectively for positions including climate finance and adaptation support, reform of international debt and financial institutions, and greater African representation in global governance bodies such as the United Nations Security Council, which African states have long argued underrepresents the continent relative to its population and its stake in international peace and security issues.

International debt has become an increasingly urgent issue in African diplomacy during the 2010s and 2020s, as a number of African countries accumulated significant sovereign debt — owed to a diverse mix of traditional multilateral lenders such as the World Bank and International Monetary Fund, bilateral government lenders including China, and private international bond markets — creating debt service burdens that in some countries consumed a very large share of government revenue, limiting resources available for education, health, and infrastructure investment. Debates over debt relief, debt restructuring, and the transparency of loan terms, particularly loans from China and other newer bilateral lenders, have become a significant focus of African diplomatic engagement with international financial institutions and creditor governments.

Diplomacy has also extended into cultural and sporting arenas, with African nations increasingly hosting and participating in major international events, and African athletes, musicians, filmmakers, and writers gaining substantial global audiences and influence, a theme explored further in the chapter on culture, literature, and the arts. African governments have in some cases used cultural diplomacy and sporting achievement deliberately to build international profile and soft power, complementing more traditional forms of state-to-state economic and security diplomacy examined throughout this chapter.

Section 65.9

Africa's Global Influence

While much analysis of Africa's international relations understandably focuses on the influence that outside powers exert on the continent, students should also consider the ways in which Africa itself increasingly shapes global affairs. Africa's population, projected to continue growing substantially through the middle of the twenty-first century, discussed further in the textbook's concluding chapter, gives the continent growing weight in international demographic, economic, and diplomatic calculations. African votes at the United Nations General Assembly and other multilateral bodies represent a significant bloc that other powers actively court in pursuit of diplomatic support on a wide range of international issues.

African cultural production — including Nigerian and other national film industries, African popular music genres that have achieved substantial global commercial success and influence on international popular culture, African literature that has won major international literary prizes, and African fashion, visual art, and design — has increasingly shaped global culture rather than simply receiving cultural influence from wealthier regions, a pattern examined in greater depth in the chapter on culture, literature, and the arts. African athletes have achieved extraordinary success in numerous international sporting competitions, and African sporting institutions and leagues have grown in scale and international visibility.

Africa's mineral resources, particularly those critical to renewable energy technology and batteries such as cobalt and various rare earth elements, have positioned several African countries as significant players in the global transition toward renewable energy and away from fossil fuels, giving these countries potential new forms of economic leverage even as questions remain about whether this leverage will translate into broader economic development or simply reproduce older patterns of raw material extraction discussed throughout this chapter and the broader textbook.

At the same time, Africa's growing global significance has not eliminated the structural constraints that have long shaped the continent's international position: the concentration of manufacturing and high-value economic activity outside the continent, continued dependence on external capital and technology for many forms of economic development, and vulnerability to global economic shocks, as demonstrated dramatically by the COVID-19 pandemic's disruption of international supply chains and its revelation of stark global inequalities in vaccine access, discussed further in the chapter on health, education, and social change. Understanding Africa's global influence requires holding both dimensions in view simultaneously: genuine and growing significance in global demographic, cultural, resource, and diplomatic terms, alongside persistent structural constraints rooted in the long history of colonial and postcolonial economic relationships examined throughout this textbook.

Historical Vocabulary
Multipolarity

Multipolarity describes an international system in which power and influence are distributed among several major states or blocs rather than concentrated in one dominant power (unipolarity) or divided between two competing powers (bipolarity, as during the Cold War). In the African context, scholars use the term to describe how African governments, since the 1990s, have increasingly been able to choose among multiple major international partners — including China, the United States, the European Union, India, and Gulf states — rather than being confined to relationships with a single former colonial power or one of two Cold War superpowers.

Students should use this concept carefully. Multipolarity describes an expanded field of choices available to African governments, but it does not automatically mean that African states have escaped structural economic dependence on external capital, technology, and markets, a distinction examined further in this chapter's Historical Debate feature.

Historical Vocabulary
"Debt-Trap Diplomacy" (a Contested Term)

"Debt-trap diplomacy" is a term used, primarily by Western commentators and policymakers, to describe a claimed strategy in which a lending country — most often China in recent discussion — deliberately extends loans to a borrowing country that it knows the borrower will struggle to repay, with the intention of later seizing strategic assets or extracting political concessions when the borrower defaults. The term is frequently applied to Chinese infrastructure lending in Africa, though its accuracy is heavily disputed.

Students should treat this term critically rather than accepting it uncritically. Detailed empirical studies of specific loan agreements have generally found limited evidence that Chinese lenders have systematically pursued asset seizure as a deliberate strategy, though genuine concerns about debt sustainability, loan transparency, and the terms of specific agreements remain well documented and important. Historians and social scientists urge students to distinguish between the broad, often politically charged claim of "debt-trap diplomacy" and the more specific, evidence-based debates over debt sustainability and loan terms examined in this chapter's Historical Debate feature.

Primary Source Window

Joint Declarations from Forum on China-Africa Cooperation Summits

Creator: Chinese government and African Union/African state representatives · Date: Various summits, 2000–present · Type: Diplomatic joint declarations and official communiqués

Context: Since 2000, China and African governments have issued joint declarations at each Forum on China-Africa Cooperation summit, outlining shared principles and specific commitments regarding trade, investment, infrastructure financing, and diplomatic cooperation. These declarations are official diplomatic documents crafted collaboratively by Chinese and African government representatives, intended for public and international audiences.

These declarations have consistently emphasized principles of mutual respect for sovereignty, non-interference in internal affairs, and "win-win cooperation," alongside specific pledges of financing for infrastructure, agriculture, education, and public health projects across participating African countries.

Analysis: These declarations are valuable evidence of the officially stated principles and priorities guiding China-Africa relations, but students should recognize their limits as historical evidence. As diplomatic documents crafted for public consumption, they emphasize cooperation and mutual benefit and are unlikely to reference specific tensions, disagreements, or the less favorable terms of particular loan agreements. Historians must supplement such official declarations with other forms of evidence — investigative journalism, loan contract analysis, and the accounts of workers, communities, and officials directly involved in specific projects — to construct a fuller picture of how China-Africa cooperation actually functions on the ground.

Historical Debate

Is China (and Are Other New Partners) a Genuine Alternative to Older Patterns of Dependency, or a New Version of the Same Structural Relationship?

The question: As China, India, Gulf states, and other partners have expanded their engagement with Africa, scholars and policymakers have debated whether this diversification represents a genuine transformation of Africa's position in the global economy, or whether it largely reproduces the extractive, raw-material-for-manufactured-goods pattern that characterized colonial and early postcolonial trade with Europe.

Interpretation A — Genuine Diversification and Increased African Leverage: Some scholars argue that the expansion of African partnerships beyond former colonial powers has given African governments meaningfully greater negotiating leverage, since they can now play potential partners against one another and choose financing and investment terms more favorable than those historically available from a single dominant colonial power. They point to substantial, genuinely needed infrastructure — railways, ports, power plants — financed and built with new partners' support, often more quickly than earlier Western-financed alternatives, as evidence of real, tangible benefit.
Interpretation B — Continuity of Structural Dependency Under New Partners: Other scholars argue that regardless of which external power is involved, the underlying economic relationship — African states exporting raw materials, minerals, and agricultural commodities in exchange for manufactured goods, technology, capital, and, increasingly, debt — has remained structurally similar to colonial-era patterns. They point to the continued concentration of manufacturing and high-value economic activity outside Africa, the persistence of debt burdens regardless of creditor identity, and specific instances of labor, environmental, or governance concerns connected to foreign-financed projects as evidence that new partners have not fundamentally altered Africa's position in the global economic hierarchy.

Evidence: Interpretation A draws on infrastructure completion statistics, comparative financing terms across different lenders, and African government statements emphasizing new leverage and options. Interpretation B draws on debt sustainability analyses, studies of specific project labor and environmental conditions, and long-term trade composition data showing continued reliance on raw material exports across multiple different trading partners.

Why it matters: How students and policymakers understand this debate shapes recommendations about how African governments should approach international partnerships going forward — whether the priority should be diversifying partners further, negotiating harder on specific loan and investment terms, building African industrial and manufacturing capacity to change the underlying trade pattern, or some combination of these strategies.

Student task: Based on the evidence described above and elsewhere in this chapter, which interpretation do you find more persuasive, or do you think elements of both are correct? What additional evidence would help resolve this debate more conclusively?

Multiple Perspectives

Whose Interests Are Served by Africa's Expanding Global Partnerships?

From the perspective of African government officials negotiating infrastructure financing: Expanded international partnerships offer valuable new options for financing urgently needed roads, railways, and power plants, often on terms and timelines that traditional Western donors, with their governance and human rights conditions, have been unwilling or unable to match, even as officials must carefully evaluate the specific debt and repayment terms attached to each offer.
From the perspective of local communities near foreign-financed infrastructure projects: New roads, ports, and power plants can bring genuine benefits — employment, improved transportation, and access to electricity — but communities have also raised concerns in specific cases about land displacement, environmental damage, labor conditions on construction projects, and the extent to which local workers and businesses benefit compared to foreign contractors and workers.
From the perspective of Western governments and commentators concerned about strategic competition: The expansion of Chinese, Gulf, and other new partners' influence in Africa represents a significant shift in global strategic balance, raising concerns about declining Western influence, the terms and transparency of new lending arrangements, and the long-term implications for African governance, debt sustainability, and alignment with Western strategic interests.
From the perspective of African advocates of continental economic integration: The proliferation of bilateral relationships between individual African states and powerful external partners risks perpetuating a pattern in which African states negotiate from relative weakness one at a time; building stronger continental institutions and the African Continental Free Trade Area is necessary to give African states greater collective bargaining power regardless of which external partners they engage.
Cause and Effect

Africa's Pivot Toward a More Diverse Set of International Partners

Long-Term Causes

Colonial-era economic structures orienting African economies toward raw material export; decades of Cold War dependence on one of two superpowers for aid and security support; long-standing African aspirations, expressed since the independence era, for genuine economic and diplomatic sovereignty rather than dependence on a single external patron.

Short-Term Causes

The 1991 collapse of the Soviet Union and end of Cold War bipolarity; China's rapid economic growth and rising demand for raw materials beginning in the 1990s; India's 1991 economic liberalization; the 2000 founding of FOCAC and the 2000 passage of AGOA; the 2013 launch of China's Belt and Road Initiative.

The Event

Rapid diversification of Africa's trading partners, investors, and diplomatic relationships beyond former colonial powers and Cold War superpowers, with China becoming Africa's largest trading partner and India, Gulf states, Brazil, and Turkey substantially expanding their engagement by the 2010s and 2020s.

Effects

Expanded infrastructure financing and construction across the continent; growing African government negotiating leverage in some contexts; renewed debates over debt sustainability and loan transparency; efforts to build African collective bargaining power through the African Continental Free Trade Area and African Union; continued structural dependence on raw material exports despite partner diversification.

Historical complexity: Africa's pivot toward more diverse international partnerships cannot be explained by any single cause. It resulted from a specific convergence of geopolitical change, the economic rise of new global powers, and deliberate policy choices by African governments and institutions — a combination whose long-term consequences for African economic development remain actively debated and historically unresolved.

Continuity and Change Over Time

From Colonial Trade Patterns to Contemporary Global Partnerships

What Changed

  • African governments gained a much wider range of international trading and financing partners beyond former colonial powers
  • New infrastructure financing arrangements, particularly from China, delivered large-scale construction projects on different terms than traditional Western development assistance
  • African regional institutions, including the African Union and the African Continental Free Trade Area, sought to strengthen collective African bargaining power
  • Diaspora communities and remittance flows became increasingly recognized as significant economic resources connecting Africa to the wider world

What Stayed the Same

  • Most African economies continued to rely heavily on the export of raw materials and agricultural commodities rather than manufactured goods
  • Manufacturing and high-value economic activity remained concentrated primarily outside the African continent
  • African governments continued to depend on foreign capital, technology, and expertise for major infrastructure and industrial projects
  • Debt burdens, regardless of the identity of the lending country or institution, continued to constrain government spending and policy choices in many African states

Why the pattern matters: The persistence of structural economic patterns alongside the genuine diversification of international partners illustrates that changing which countries a state trades and negotiates with does not automatically transform the underlying position of that state's economy within the global economic system. Understanding both the real change and the real continuity is essential to evaluating claims about Africa's contemporary global position.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Continent Connected: Global Ties in Ordinary African Life

Consider how global connections might shape an ordinary week for a family in a mid-sized African city during the 2020s. A father working as a supervisor on a Chinese-financed road construction project earns wages that support his household, even as he and his coworkers debate among themselves the fairness of pay differences between local and expatriate staff on the project. His sister, working as a domestic worker in the Gulf state of Qatar, sends a portion of her monthly wages home via a mobile money transfer service, funds that help cover school fees for her nieces and nephews. A cousin who emigrated to France a decade earlier calls weekly using a video messaging application, discussing both family news and, increasingly, opportunities for the cousin to invest in a small family business back home. A daughter studying at a local university uses course materials partly funded by a European Union education partnership program, while also following the international social media accounts of Nigerian musicians and Kenyan long-distance runners whose global fame has become a source of national pride.

This single household, in other words, is connected simultaneously to Chinese infrastructure financing, Gulf labor migration, European family migration and educational partnership, and a globally recognized African popular culture — illustrating how the state-to-state diplomatic and economic relationships examined in this chapter are experienced concretely, and often simultaneously, in the daily lives of ordinary African families, well before or beyond any formal diplomatic summit or trade agreement.

Chapter Review and Global Relations Checkpoint

This chapter has examined Africa's diversifying relationships with China, Europe, the United States, India, the Gulf states, and the broader Global South since the end of the Cold War, alongside the significance of migration, remittances, and the African diaspora, and Africa's own growing cultural, demographic, and diplomatic influence on world affairs. Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined Africa's evolving place in the global system from the end of the Cold War to the present, tracing how the continent's international relationships diversified substantially beyond the former colonial powers of Western Europe and the Cold War superpower framework examined in earlier chapters. It has argued that this diversification reflects both genuine new opportunities for African governments and persistent structural continuities rooted in the long history of colonial and postcolonial economic relationships.

The chapter began by situating this diversification within the end of Cold War bipolarity and the rise of new global economic powers, particularly China, whose rapid economic growth from the 1990s onward created enormous demand for African raw materials alongside a growing capacity to finance and construct major infrastructure projects across the continent. It examined the institutional framework of the Forum on China-Africa Cooperation and China's Belt and Road Initiative, alongside ongoing debates over the benefits and risks of Chinese-financed development, including debates over debt sustainability and the contested "debt-trap diplomacy" narrative.

The chapter then traced the evolution of Europe's relationship with Africa from direct colonial administration through the trade and aid frameworks of the postcolonial period to the increasingly contested politics of migration and historical memory shaping the contemporary Europe-Africa relationship. It examined shifting United States priorities across democratization, trade through AGOA, public health through PEPFAR, and security cooperation through AFRICOM, and considered the growing economic and diasporic ties linking Africa to India and the Gulf states, including Indian pharmaceutical and technology investment and Gulf investment in African ports and agriculture.

South-South cooperation was examined as both an institutional framework, through groupings such as BRICS, and a contested concept, with scholars divided over whether cooperation among developing countries meaningfully differs from older North-South economic patterns. The chapter then turned to the significance of African migration, remittances, and the African diaspora, recognized by the African Union as a "sixth region" of the continent, as connections operating below the level of formal state diplomacy but with substantial economic and cultural significance for African households and communities.

The chapter examined African efforts to build collective economic and diplomatic strength through institutions such as the African Continental Free Trade Area and the African Union's coordinated positions in international forums, alongside the growing urgency of debates over international debt sustainability affecting numerous African states during the 2010s and 2020s. It considered Africa's growing global cultural, demographic, and resource-based influence, from globally successful African cultural production to the continent's significance for renewable energy mineral supply chains, while emphasizing that this growing influence has not eliminated longstanding structural constraints.

Throughout, the chapter emphasized that understanding Africa's contemporary global position requires holding two things in view simultaneously: the genuine and historically significant diversification of the continent's international partnerships since the end of the Cold War, giving many African governments greater negotiating leverage and access to new forms of investment and cooperation, and the persistent structural continuities — reliance on raw material exports, dependence on external capital and technology, and vulnerability to global economic shocks and debt burdens — that connect Africa's contemporary global relationships to the longer history of colonial and postcolonial economic structures examined throughout this textbook.

Students should leave this chapter with a nuanced understanding that Africa's relationship with the world is neither a simple story of continued dependency and exploitation under new external actors, nor a simple story of triumphant diversification and newfound leverage, but a complex, ongoing historical process whose ultimate consequences for African economic development, political sovereignty, and global influence remain to be determined by choices that African governments, regional institutions, international partners, and ordinary African citizens continue to make.

The chapter's evidence base — trade and investment statistics, diplomatic declarations, loan agreements, migration and remittance data, and the accounts of officials, workers, and communities directly affected by international partnerships — illustrates the kinds of sources historians and social scientists must use to understand a still-unfolding period of African and global history.

This chapter connects backward to earlier chapters on colonial economies, the Cold War, economic development and globalization, and the African diaspora, and it connects forward to the chapter on culture, literature, and the arts and the textbook's concluding chapter on Africa's future, in which questions about international partnership, debt, migration, and global influence will remain central to understanding the choices and possibilities facing African governments and societies in the decades ahead.

Key Terms

Multipolarity
An international system in which power and influence are distributed among several major states or blocs rather than concentrated in one or two dominant powers.
South-South cooperation
Economic, diplomatic, and technical cooperation among developing countries of the Global South, intended as an alternative or complement to relationships with wealthier Northern powers.
Debt-trap diplomacy
A contested term describing a claimed strategy in which a lending country deliberately extends loans a borrower cannot repay in order to later extract strategic assets or political concessions.
Infrastructure financing
Loans, grants, or investment provided to fund the construction of roads, railways, ports, power plants, and telecommunications networks.
Foreign direct investment
Investment made by a company or individual in one country into business interests located in another country, typically involving significant ownership or control.
Remittances
Money sent by migrants working abroad back to family members or communities in their country of origin.
Diaspora
A population living outside its ancestral homeland, maintaining cultural, economic, or political ties to that homeland.
Bilateral agreement
A formal agreement between two countries, as distinct from a multilateral agreement involving several parties.
Multilateral institution
An international organization involving multiple member states, such as the United Nations, World Bank, or African Union.
Preferential trade access
Reduced tariffs or special market access granted to particular countries' exports, often under a specific trade agreement.
Resource-backed loan
A loan in which repayment is tied to or secured by revenue from a natural resource, such as oil or minerals.
Soft power
The ability to influence other countries through cultural appeal, diplomacy, and example rather than military or economic coercion.
Strategic partnership
A formalized cooperative relationship between countries emphasizing shared long-term political, economic, or security interests.
Non-alignment
A foreign policy stance, prominent during the Cold War, of not formally allying with either major superpower bloc.
Concessional loan
A loan offered with more favorable terms, such as lower interest rates or longer repayment periods, than standard market lending.
Extractive industry
Economic activity focused on removing raw materials, such as minerals, oil, or timber, from the earth for sale, often with limited local processing or value addition.
Debt sustainability
The capacity of a government to meet its debt obligations without requiring exceptional financing or default, an important measure of fiscal health.
Development assistance
Financial or technical aid provided by governments or institutions to support economic and social development in another country.
Migration corridor
A recognized route or pattern of migration between specific countries or regions.
Continental free trade
Trade conducted with reduced tariffs and barriers among countries within the same continent, as pursued by the African Continental Free Trade Area.
Vaccine diplomacy
The use of vaccine donations or sales as a tool of international diplomacy and influence.
Security partnership
Cooperation between countries focused on military training, counterterrorism, or defense capability.
Sovereign debt
Debt owed by a national government, as opposed to debt owed by private companies or individuals.
Global value chain
The full range of activities, often spanning multiple countries, involved in producing a good or service from raw material to final sale.
Emerging power
A country experiencing rapid economic growth and increasing international influence, such as China, India, or Brazil in recent decades.

  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), describe the partnership, analyze its historical significance, and reflect on what it reveals about Africa's contemporary global position.

Library resources to use: International relations and development economics journals, World Bank and IMF debt sustainability reports, diplomatic archives and joint declarations, investigative journalism archives, and the MGU History Library's reference materials on globalization and contemporary Africa.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter matters because it demonstrates that African history, and African agency, did not end with independence or with the Cold War discussed in earlier chapters. Africa's relationship with the world remains an active, unfolding, and often contested process in which African governments, institutions, and ordinary citizens continue to negotiate the terms of the continent's engagement with a rapidly changing global system.

The diversification of Africa's international partnerships examined here shaped later African history by expanding the range of financing, investment, and diplomatic options available to African governments, while also generating new debates over debt, sovereignty, and the terms on which African resources, labor, and markets are integrated into the global economy. Understanding both the genuine new opportunities and the persistent structural continuities in these relationships prepares students to think critically about claims — whether celebratory or dismissive — made about Africa's international position.

The larger theme this chapter reveals is one that runs throughout this textbook: African societies and governments have consistently sought to navigate relationships with more powerful outside actors — from ancient trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean trade partners, to European colonizers, to Cold War superpowers, to the diverse array of twenty-first-century global partners examined here — always seeking greater advantage and autonomy within structures of global power that Africans did not single-handedly create and do not fully control. This tension between agency and constraint, evident across the whole sweep of African history covered in this textbook, remains central to understanding Africa's present global position.

As the textbook turns to its discussion of culture, literature, and the arts, and finally to its concluding chapter on Africa's future, students should carry forward the central questions this chapter has raised: which partnerships best serve African development and sovereignty, how African states and institutions can build greater collective bargaining strength, what role migration, remittances, and the diaspora will continue to play in connecting Africa to the world, and how the choices made by African governments, international partners, and ordinary Africans will shape the continent's place in an increasingly multipolar and interconnected global system in the decades ahead.

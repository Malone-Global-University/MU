Part IV — West Africa and the Great Sahelian Empires
19

West Africa Before Empire

Africa: A History of a Continent

Chapter Opening

"Long before Ghana, Mali, and Songhai commanded gold, salt, and the loyalty of merchants from across the Sahara, West Africa was already a region of farmers, fishers, smiths, traders, and ritual specialists who had spent centuries building the social and economic foundations on which those later empires would rise."

Most accounts of West African history begin with empire: with Ghana's gold, with Sundiata's rise, with Mansa Musa's famous pilgrimage across the Sahara to Mecca. These are extraordinary stories, and later chapters in this textbook will tell them in full. But beginning the story there creates a serious distortion. It suggests that West African history starts with the appearance of large states, as though nothing of historical importance happened before kings, courts, and trans-Saharan caravans entered the written record. That suggestion is false, and it obscures one of the most important truths about how complex societies emerge anywhere in the world: empires do not appear out of nothing. They are built on dense layers of earlier human achievement — agricultural knowledge accumulated over centuries, networks of exchange linking villages and regions, systems of kinship and authority that organized labor and resolved disputes, religious and ritual traditions that gave communities shared meaning, and modes of settlement that allowed populations to grow and specialize.

This chapter examines that foundational period of West African history — the centuries before the rise of Ghana, roughly from the end of the last major desiccation of the Sahara through the first millennium CE, during which West African peoples developed the environmental knowledge, agricultural systems, social institutions, and exchange networks that later sustained some of the most celebrated states in African history. It asks what life was like for the farmers, herders, fishers, and artisans whose labor and ingenuity created the wealth that imperial courts would later tax, and whose local institutions of governance and kinship would shape how later empires actually functioned at the village level, far from the courts of kings.

The region under study here is vast and ecologically varied. West Africa stretches from the Atlantic coast eastward across the Sahel to the Lake Chad basin, and from the southern fringe of the Sahara southward through savanna grasslands to dense tropical forest along the Gulf of Guinea. No single environment, economy, or society can represent this whole region. A fishing community on the Niger's inland delta lived a fundamentally different life from a herding family on the Saharan fringe or a yam-farming household in the forest zone near the coast. Understanding West Africa before empire requires understanding this diversity, not flattening it into a single undifferentiated "early Africa."

What changed during this period was substantial. Populations grew. Agricultural systems intensified and diversified, adapting to dramatically different rainfall and soil conditions across the region. Iron-working spread and transformed tool-making, warfare, and farming. Trade routes lengthened and thickened, connecting communities hundreds of kilometers apart even before camel caravans crossed the Sahara in large numbers. Settlements grew from small villages into substantial towns with specialized occupational quarters — a transformation visible in the archaeological record at sites like Jenné-jeno on the Niger's inland delta, occupied from roughly 250 BCE. Political authority became more concentrated in some areas, as lineage heads, ritual specialists, and successful traders accumulated followers, wealth, and influence that could be passed to descendants or built into more formal institutions.

What continued, just as significantly, were patterns that would persist for centuries even after the rise of great empires: the centrality of kinship in organizing labor, marriage, and inheritance; the importance of land and water rights negotiated at the local level; the spiritual authority of ancestors, earth shrines, and ritual specialists in legitimating political power; and the fundamental reliance of West African economies on farming, herding, fishing, and craft production carried out largely by households and lineage groups rather than by centralized state bureaucracies.

The evidence historians use to reconstruct this period is different from the evidence available for later, better-documented eras. There are no royal chronicles, no Arabic geographers' detailed accounts, no extensive court records for this earlier period. Instead, historians rely heavily on archaeology — excavated settlement sites, pottery sequences, iron-smelting furnaces, burial goods, and the analysis of plant and animal remains that reveal what people ate and farmed. They rely on linguistic evidence, since the historical relationships between language families can reveal patterns of migration, contact, and cultural change that occurred long before anyone wrote anything down. They rely on oral traditions recorded much later, which preserve genealogies, migration accounts, and foundational narratives that, while not literal transcripts of distant events, often encode real historical information about lineage, settlement, and political change. And they rely, increasingly, on environmental and climate science, since shifts in rainfall and the gradual desiccation of the Sahara fundamentally shaped where and how people could live.

This chapter matters because it corrects a common misunderstanding: that African history before recorded empire was static, simple, or historically uneventful. In fact, the centuries covered in this chapter were a period of dynamic transformation — agricultural innovation, demographic growth, social differentiation, and the construction of trade and political networks that made the rise of Ghana, and later Mali and Songhai, not a sudden miracle but the natural culmination of long-term West African historical development. Understanding this chapter is essential to understanding everything that follows in Part IV of this textbook.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Describe the major environmental zones of West Africa and explain how each shaped patterns of settlement and subsistence.
  2. Explain how and why early West African societies adopted and adapted agriculture and pastoralism in different regions.
  3. Trace the development of settlement patterns from small farming villages to larger towns with occupational specialization.
  4. Analyze the forms of local political authority that existed before the rise of large states, including lineage-based and "big man" systems of leadership.
  5. Identify the regional and long-distance exchange networks that connected West African communities before extensive trans-Saharan trade.
  6. Describe the role of kinship, lineage, and age-grade systems in organizing labor, marriage, and social obligation.
  7. Explain the religious and ritual systems that gave early West African communities shared meaning and legitimated authority.
  8. Evaluate how the developments of this period created the social, economic, and political foundations for later Sahelian empires.
  9. Compare the historical experiences of different regions and peoples within West Africa rather than treating the region as historically uniform.
  10. Distinguish between the types of evidence historians use to reconstruct pre-literate African history and explain the strengths and limits of each.
  11. Assess competing historical interpretations about why and how early state-like authority emerged in parts of West Africa.
  12. Connect the developments of this period to the themes of later chapters on Ghana, Mali, and Songhai.

Historical Context

To understand West Africa before empire, students must first set aside the assumption that complex society requires writing, monumental architecture, or centralized kingship. By many of the measures historians use to assess social complexity — settlement size, occupational specialization, long-distance exchange, ritual elaboration, and the existence of recognized political authority above the level of the individual household — West African societies had already achieved a great deal by the early centuries of the first millennium CE, well before any of the famous Sahelian empires existed.

The deeper background to this period lies in the broader history of food production across Africa, discussed in Chapter 5. By roughly 1000 BCE, agriculture and pastoralism had become established across much of West Africa, following different timelines and taking different forms in different ecological zones. In the drier Sahelian zone bordering the Sahara, pastoralism and the cultivation of drought-resistant grains such as pearl millet and sorghum dominated. In the wetter savanna and forest zones further south, root crops such as yams, along with African rice in some river floodplain areas, became central to subsistence. These differences were not incidental. They produced fundamentally different social organizations, settlement patterns, and historical trajectories that persisted for centuries.

A second crucial background factor is the long-term desiccation of the Sahara. Several thousand years earlier, the Sahara had been a far wetter environment, supporting lakes, rivers, and pastoral populations whose rock art still survives in places like the Tassili n'Ajjer plateau. As the region progressively dried out over the course of the third and second millennia BCE, populations that had once lived across the Sahara were pushed toward its margins — toward the Nile Valley in the east, toward the Sahel and the great river systems of West Africa in the south. This long migration and concentration of population along the Niger, Senegal, and other river systems is part of the deep prehistory that set the stage for the societies this chapter examines.

A third background factor is the spread of ironworking. Iron technology appears to have developed independently or through diffusion in several parts of West Africa by the middle of the first millennium BCE, with important early evidence from sites in the Nok culture area of central Nigeria. Iron tools transformed agriculture by making land clearance and cultivation more efficient, and iron weapons transformed warfare and the balance of power between communities. The spread of ironworking is closely tied to many of the social and political changes discussed in this chapter, since the ability to produce iron tools and weapons became, in many communities, a source of both economic value and ritual prestige — blacksmiths in many West African societies occupied a distinctive, often semi-sacred social position precisely because of the transformative power associated with their craft.

Finally, students should understand that this period was not isolated from the wider world. Trans-Saharan contact, while less intensive than it would become from the eighth century CE onward with the spread of Islam and the wider use of camels, was not entirely absent even earlier. Some trade goods, ideas, and possibly small population movements crossed the desert in earlier centuries, and West Africa's river systems — especially the Niger — provided internal corridors of exchange that linked distant regions long before any caravan from North Africa arrived. The societies examined in this chapter, in other words, were neither isolated nor static. They were dynamic, interconnected, and continuously changing in response to environmental conditions, technological innovation, and social and economic opportunity.

Section 19.1

The West African Environment

No understanding of West African history is possible without first understanding West African geography. The region forms a series of roughly parallel ecological bands running east to west, each shaped primarily by distance from the equator and the resulting patterns of rainfall. In the far north, the Sahara itself forms an immense barrier of sand, gravel, and rock, sparsely inhabited except around oases and along ancient drainage routes. South of the Sahara lies the Sahel, a transitional zone of low and highly variable rainfall, dominated historically by drought-resistant grains and by pastoral economies centered on cattle, sheep, and goats. South of the Sahel lies the savanna, a band of grassland interspersed with trees, receiving enough rainfall to support more intensive grain agriculture and larger, denser populations. Still further south lies the forest zone along the Gulf of Guinea coast, where heavy and reliable rainfall supports tropical forest vegetation and a fundamentally different agricultural system based on root crops such as yams, along with tree crops such as the oil palm.

Cutting across these latitudinal bands are the great river systems of West Africa, above all the Niger, which flows in a vast arc from its source in the highlands near the Guinea coast, north and east into the Sahel before bending south again toward the Gulf of Guinea near present-day Nigeria. The Niger's inland delta — a vast network of seasonally flooded channels, lakes, and floodplains in the area of present-day Mali — created one of the most agriculturally rich and densely populated regions anywhere in pre-modern West Africa. Other major rivers, including the Senegal, the Volta, and numerous smaller systems, performed similar functions on a smaller scale, supporting floodplain agriculture, fishing economies, and corridors of transport and exchange.

This environmental diversity had profound historical consequences that this chapter will trace throughout. Communities in the Sahel needed strategies for managing highly variable rainfall and for combining herding with grain cultivation; their political and social institutions often needed to accommodate seasonal mobility. Communities in the Niger inland delta could rely on the river's annual flood to fertilize fields and could specialize heavily in fishing, rice cultivation, or cattle herding depending on their location relative to the floodplain — and this very degree of specialization encouraged trade between groups with different economic profiles living close to one another. Communities in the forest zone faced different challenges: clearing dense vegetation for farming, managing higher disease burdens associated with humid climates, and developing crops and techniques suited to heavy rainfall.

Historians studying this environmental diversity must resist two opposite errors. The first is treating environment as destiny — assuming that geography alone determined social outcomes without leaving room for human choice, innovation, and historical contingency. The second is ignoring environment altogether and treating West African history as though it unfolded on a blank, undifferentiated stage. The most accurate approach recognizes that environment created possibilities and constraints within which human communities made their own history through technology, social organization, and political choice.

Section 19.2

Early Societies and Settlement

By the early centuries of the first millennium CE, West African settlement patterns had moved well beyond small, dispersed farming hamlets. Archaeological excavation at Jenné-jeno, on the Niger's inland delta in present-day Mali, has revealed one of the clearest pictures available anywhere in sub-Saharan Africa of how early settlement grew in scale and complexity over time. Founded around 250 BCE as a relatively modest settlement, Jenné-jeno grew over succeeding centuries into a substantial town covering dozens of hectares, with evidence of distinct occupational quarters for different craft activities, extensive iron production, fishing and rice cultivation adapted to the floodplain environment, and goods imported from considerable distances — including copper, which does not occur naturally anywhere near the site and must have arrived through long-distance exchange networks.

The significance of Jenné-jeno for historians lies precisely in its timing and character. It demonstrates that urban-scale settlement, occupational specialization, and long-distance trade existed in West Africa centuries before the rise of Ghana as a recognized political power, and apparently without the kind of centralized monumental kingship associated with later Sahelian states. Some archaeologists have described early West African urbanism of this kind as "clustered" or "heterarchical" — meaning that settlement complexes were often made up of multiple specialized communities living near one another, exchanging goods and services, without necessarily being unified under a single ruling authority in the way later empires would be. This model challenges assumptions, common in older historical writing, that urbanization and political centralization must develop together. In West Africa, it now appears that substantial towns could exist well before — and somewhat independently of — the kind of centralized state institutions historians traditionally associate with "civilization."

Settlement growth of this kind did not happen everywhere at the same pace or in the same form. In the drier Sahelian zone, settlements tended to remain smaller and more dispersed, reflecting the constraints of a more marginal agricultural environment and, in many areas, a greater reliance on mobile or semi-mobile pastoralism. In the forest zone, settlement patterns were shaped by the demands of clearing dense vegetation and by different crop cycles associated with yam cultivation, which in some areas supported smaller, more dispersed communities and in others — particularly where soils were especially fertile — supported denser populations organized into village clusters.

What unified these diverse settlement patterns was a broadly similar trajectory: population growth driven by increasingly productive and reliable agriculture, growing social differentiation as some households or lineages accumulated more land, livestock, or trade goods than others, and the gradual emergence of settlement hierarchies in which some towns functioned as central places for surrounding villages, coordinating exchange, providing specialized craft goods, and in some cases becoming seats of emerging political authority.

Section 19.3

Farming and Herding

The economic foundation of every West African society in this period was some combination of farming, herding, and fishing, calibrated to local environmental conditions. In the Sahel, pearl millet and sorghum — both highly drought-resistant grains capable of surviving the region's unpredictable and often meager rainfall — formed the backbone of agriculture, frequently combined with cattle, sheep, and goat herding. Many Sahelian communities practiced what scholars call agro-pastoralism, in which the same households or closely related lineages combined grain cultivation with livestock herding, sometimes moving herds seasonally to take advantage of pasture made available by the rains while maintaining permanent or semi-permanent farming settlements.

In the savanna zone, more reliable rainfall supported denser cultivation of millet, sorghum, and, in suitable areas, African rice — a crop independently domesticated in West Africa, distinct from Asian rice, that became especially important in floodplain regions like the Niger inland delta. Savanna communities generally supported larger and more settled populations than the Sahel, and the greater agricultural surplus available in good years helped underwrite the kind of craft specialization, trade, and social differentiation visible at sites like Jenné-jeno.

In the forest zone, yams formed the central staple crop across much of the region, supplemented by other root crops and by the oil palm, which provided both food and oil used for cooking and other purposes. Forest agriculture required different techniques than savanna or Sahelian grain farming — most importantly, clearing dense vegetation, often through controlled burning, and managing soil fertility in environments where heavy rainfall could rapidly leach nutrients from cleared land. Forest communities also relied more heavily on hunting and gathering supplementary foods than their savanna and Sahelian counterparts, since forest environments offered a wider range of wild plant and animal resources even after the spread of agriculture.

Fishing communities along the Niger, Senegal, and other major rivers, as well as along the Atlantic coast, developed their own distinctive economic specializations, often trading fish, dried or smoked for preservation, to inland farming communities in exchange for grain or other goods. The degree of economic specialization visible by the early first millennium CE — communities focusing heavily on farming, herding, or fishing depending on their location, and then trading with neighboring communities to obtain what they did not produce themselves — is one of the clearest pieces of evidence that West African economies of this period were neither simple nor self-sufficient at the household level. They were regionally integrated systems built on exchange.

The introduction and spread of iron tools during this period, discussed in the Historical Context section above, intensified agricultural productivity across all these zones by making land clearance, soil turning, and harvesting more efficient than was possible with stone or wooden tools alone. The combination of iron technology and increasingly sophisticated agricultural knowledge — knowledge of crop rotation, soil management, and the particular demands of different local environments — allowed West African populations to grow significantly over the centuries covered in this chapter, providing the demographic foundation for the more complex societies that would follow.

Section 19.4

Local Political Systems

Before the rise of large states like Ghana, political authority in West Africa was overwhelmingly organized at the level of the lineage, the village, or, at most, a small cluster of villages. Historians and anthropologists describe many of these systems as segmentary societies — communities organized into nested kinship groups, from the household up through the extended family, the lineage, and sometimes a wider clan, in which authority rested with senior members of each group rather than with a single centralized ruler standing above the whole community.

Within this segmentary framework, several overlapping sources of authority typically coexisted. Lineage heads — usually senior men, though in some matrilineal societies senior women held comparable authority within their own kin networks — controlled the allocation of farmland among lineage members, mediated disputes, and represented the lineage in dealings with other kin groups. Earth priests or other ritual specialists, often distinct from lineage heads, held spiritual authority over land and agricultural fertility, performing rites believed necessary to maintain the community's relationship with ancestral spirits and local earth deities. In many communities, the first-settler lineage in a given territory held a special claim to this ritual authority over the land, regardless of which lineage subsequently became the largest or most prosperous.

Alongside these inherited forms of authority, many West African communities of this period also recognized what scholars sometimes call "big man" leadership — influence and authority earned through individual achievement rather than birthright alone. A man (or, in some societies, a woman) who demonstrated unusual success in farming, trade, warfare, oratory, or the accumulation of followers and dependents through marriage alliances, generous hospitality, or successful leadership in defense against raiders could accumulate informal authority that sometimes hardened, over a lifetime or across generations, into more formal and inheritable positions of chieftaincy.

It is important for students to understand that these systems of local authority were neither weak nor primitive simply because they lacked the scale of later empires. Segmentary and big-man systems were often highly effective at organizing labor for farming and construction, resolving disputes over land and marriage, mobilizing communities for defense, and managing relationships with neighboring groups through alliance, marriage, or controlled conflict. They were, in a meaningful sense, sophisticated political technologies suited to societies organized primarily around kinship and agriculture rather than around large territorial states.

At the same time, historians recognize that certain conditions could push these local systems toward greater centralization over time. Where trade routes concentrated unusual wealth in particular locations — as appears to have happened at Jenné-jeno and at other emerging towns along the Niger — successful lineage heads or big men controlling access to that trade could accumulate enough wealth and followers to extend their authority over neighboring lineages and villages, laying the groundwork for the kind of more formal, territorially extensive political authority that would eventually characterize the kingdom of Ghana.

Section 19.5

Trade and Regional Exchange

Long before camel caravans carried gold north and salt south across the Sahara in large volumes, West African communities had already constructed dense regional networks of exchange. These networks operated at multiple scales simultaneously. At the most local level, neighboring villages with different economic specializations — a fishing community, a farming community, a herding community, all located within a day's walk or canoe journey of one another — traded food surpluses, allowing each community to diversify its diet and obtain goods it could not easily produce itself. At a somewhat larger regional scale, exchange connected communities across dozens or even hundreds of kilometers, often following river corridors like the Niger, which provided efficient transport for bulk goods by canoe.

The archaeological evidence from Jenné-jeno is particularly revealing on this point. Excavations there have recovered copper — a metal that does not occur naturally anywhere near the site — along with other goods that must have arrived through exchange networks extending well beyond the immediate region, quite possibly reaching toward copper sources in the Sahara or even further afield. This evidence demonstrates that West African communities were already integrated into long-distance exchange networks centuries before the intensification of trans-Saharan trade typically associated with the rise of Ghana from the eighth century CE onward.

What were people exchanging, and why did it matter? Iron tools and weapons, produced by specialist blacksmiths, were valuable goods that moved between communities with and without local iron ore deposits or smelting expertise. Salt, essential for human and animal nutrition and especially scarce in regions far from the coast or from inland salt deposits, was another crucial trade good even before the famous gold-salt trade of later centuries reached its full scale. Foodstuffs, particularly dried fish and surplus grain, moved between specialized fishing, farming, and herding communities. Craft goods — pottery, woven cloth, leatherwork, and ornamental items — moved along these same networks, often carrying social and ritual significance beyond their practical use.

The historical significance of this regional exchange system is twofold. First, it demonstrates that West African economies were never simply collections of self-sufficient subsistence farmers; they were interconnected systems in which specialization and exchange created mutual economic dependence between communities. Second, and just as importantly, this regional exchange system provided the essential economic and logistical foundation on which the famous trans-Saharan gold and salt trade would later be built. The merchants, trade routes, and commercial expertise that sustained Ghana's wealth did not appear from nowhere when North African and trans-Saharan traders began seeking West African gold in greater volume; they grew out of centuries of accumulated West African experience in organizing regional trade.

Section 19.6

Kinship and Social Organization

Kinship was the single most important organizing principle of early West African social life, shaping labor, marriage, land rights, inheritance, and social obligation in ways that affected nearly every aspect of daily existence. Most West African societies of this period organized themselves around lineages — groups of people who traced descent from a common ancestor, whether through the male line (patrilineal descent), the female line (matrilineal descent), or, in some societies, through both lines depending on the context (a system sometimes called double or bilateral descent).

These lineage systems determined far more than abstract identity. They governed who had rights to farm particular plots of land, since land in most West African societies of this period was held collectively by lineages rather than owned individually in the manner familiar from later European legal traditions. They governed marriage practices, including the widespread custom of bridewealth — the transfer of livestock, goods, or labor from a groom's family to a bride's family, which compensated the bride's lineage for the loss of her labor and reproductive capacity and created lasting alliances between the two kin groups. They governed inheritance of property, livestock, and, in many societies, political or ritual office.

Alongside lineage, many West African societies organized social life through age-grade systems — groups of individuals, usually of the same sex, initiated into adulthood together and bound throughout their lives by ties of mutual obligation and shared experience. Age grades often performed important collective labor functions, such as clearing new farmland, building or repairing communal structures, and providing organized military defense. They also served important social functions, creating bonds of solidarity that cut across individual lineages and helping to integrate the broader community.

Gender shaped social organization in ways that varied significantly across the region. In many West African societies, women held substantial economic importance through agricultural labor, craft production (particularly pottery and textile work), and market trading, even in societies where formal political authority rested primarily with men. In some societies, particularly those with matrilineal descent systems, women's lineage status carried direct political significance, since inheritance of land, office, or other forms of authority passed through the female line. Historians caution against assuming that West African gender systems of this period mirrored later European patterns of strict female subordination; the evidence instead suggests considerable variation, with women's authority and economic role differing significantly from one society to another and changing over time.

It is essential for students to understand that these kinship structures were not simply private or domestic arrangements separate from "real" politics and economics. They were the fundamental institutions through which West African societies of this period organized labor, allocated resources, resolved conflict, and constructed political authority. The lineage heads and big-man leaders discussed in Section 19.4 derived much of their authority directly from their position within these kinship systems.

Section 19.7

Culture and Religion

Religious belief and ritual practice permeated nearly every dimension of life in early West African societies, providing shared meaning, regulating relationships between people and the land, and legitimating political authority. While specific beliefs and practices varied enormously across the region's many distinct peoples and languages, certain broad patterns recur widely enough to merit discussion as general features of the period, while still respecting the genuine diversity that existed beneath these broad patterns.

Most West African religious traditions of this period recognized a supreme creator deity, generally understood as remote from everyday human affairs, alongside a much larger array of spiritual forces more directly engaged with daily life: ancestral spirits, earth deities and nature spirits associated with particular places, and spiritual forces associated with fertility, healing, and protection. Ancestors held particular importance. The deceased members of a lineage were widely believed to remain spiritually present and capable of influencing the fortunes of their living descendants, which gave funerary rites, ancestor veneration, and the maintenance of proper relationships between the living and the dead enormous social and religious significance.

Earth shrines, mentioned already in connection with political authority, exemplify the close connection between religion and the practical organization of society in this period. The belief that particular lineages — typically those understood to be the original settlers of a given territory — held special spiritual authority over the land's fertility, mediated through ritual relationships with earth deities, provided religious legitimacy for systems of land allocation and political authority that might otherwise have rested on raw power alone.

Ritual specialists, including priests associated with particular shrines or deities, diviners who interpreted signs to answer questions about the causes of misfortune or the proper course of action, and specialists in healing who combined practical herbal knowledge with ritual practice, formed an important category of social authority distinct from, though often overlapping with, lineage and political leadership. Blacksmiths, discussed earlier in connection with iron technology, frequently held a related kind of ritually significant status; the transformative power involved in turning raw ore into useful tools and weapons through fire was widely understood across West Africa as carrying spiritual as well as practical significance, and blacksmiths in many societies occupied distinctive — sometimes honored, sometimes feared or socially set apart — positions as a result.

Material and artistic culture from this period, though more difficult to reconstruct than later, better-documented eras, provides important evidence of the cultural sophistication of early West African societies. The terracotta sculptures associated with the Nok culture of central Nigeria, dating from as early as the ninth century BCE, demonstrate remarkable artistic skill and suggest the existence of ritual or commemorative traditions whose full meaning remains only partly understood. Pottery styles recovered from sites like Jenné-jeno reveal both practical technology and aesthetic tradition, and changes in pottery style over time provide archaeologists with one of their most important tools for tracing cultural change, population movement, and contact between communities.

Section 19.8

Foundations for Later Empires

This chapter's central historical argument can now be stated directly: the developments traced in the preceding sections — agricultural intensification, settlement growth and occupational specialization, the emergence of lineage- and big-man-based political authority, the construction of regional and long-distance exchange networks, the elaboration of kinship systems capable of organizing large cooperative labor forces, and the development of religious traditions capable of legitimating political authority — together constituted the essential foundation on which the kingdom of Ghana, examined in the next chapter, would be built.

It is tempting, when studying West African history, to treat the rise of Ghana as a kind of historical beginning — the moment when West Africa "enters history" through contact with North African geographers and the trans-Saharan gold trade. This chapter has argued that such a view fundamentally misunderstands the historical process. Ghana did not create West African agriculture, regional trade, settled towns, or systems of political authority. It inherited and built upon centuries of prior West African development, channeling pre-existing economic and political structures into a larger and more centralized framework capable of taxing trade, commanding military force across a wider territory, and engaging diplomatically and commercially with distant trading partners across the Sahara.

Specific continuities deserve emphasis. The lineage-based and big-man systems of local political authority discussed in Section 19.4 did not disappear when larger states emerged; they persisted as the basic units of local governance within and beneath the larger imperial structure, a pattern that would remain characteristic of West African states for centuries, including Ghana, Mali, and Songhai. The regional exchange networks discussed in Section 19.5 did not need to be invented when trans-Saharan trade intensified; they already existed and could be extended and connected to new trans-Saharan routes and partners. The religious traditions discussed in Section 19.7, including the spiritual authority of earth shrines and ancestral lineages, continued to operate alongside — and sometimes in tension with — the new religious and political authority that Sahelian rulers would later construct, including, eventually, the adoption of Islam by ruling elites in Ghana and successor states.

Understanding this continuity is essential for avoiding a common distortion in popular understanding of African history: the assumption that African states and civilizations appeared suddenly, fully formed, often attributed implicitly or explicitly to outside contact or influence. The evidence reviewed in this chapter demonstrates the opposite: West African complexity and historical achievement developed through internal processes operating over many centuries, on the foundation of West African agricultural knowledge, social institutions, and economic networks, well before intensified contact with North Africa and the wider Islamic world added new dimensions to an already dynamic and sophisticated regional history.

Section 19.9

Diversity in West African History

Having traced common patterns across this chapter, it is essential to close by reemphasizing diversity, since the greatest distortion any single chapter risks introducing is the impression that "West Africa before empire" was a single, uniform society. It was not. The region encompassed dozens of distinct language groups, many mutually unintelligible, organized into societies with substantially different economies, kinship systems, religious traditions, and political structures.

Sahelian pastoral and agro-pastoral communities, savanna grain-farming communities, Niger floodplain fishing and rice-farming communities, and forest-zone yam-farming communities each developed historical trajectories shaped by their particular environments and economic systems, even as they remained connected to one another through the regional exchange networks discussed earlier in this chapter. Mande-speaking peoples of the western Sahel and savanna developed differently from peoples speaking languages in entirely different families further east and south, even when they shared broadly similar agricultural technologies or kinship structures.

This diversity matters for more than descriptive accuracy. It explains why the kingdom of Ghana, when it eventually rose to prominence, did not simply impose a single uniform system across all of West Africa; it had to incorporate, accommodate, or coexist with the diverse local societies, economies, and political traditions that already existed across the region. The same will be true of Mali, Songhai, and the forest kingdoms examined in later chapters of Part IV: each of these later states operated within a West Africa that remained, throughout its history, a region of remarkable internal diversity rather than a single undifferentiated whole.

Recognizing this diversity also helps students avoid treating West African history as a simple linear story of progress from "simple" to "complex" societies, with empire as the inevitable destination. Many West African societies of this period — including pastoral communities in the Sahel and forest communities in the south — never developed large centralized states at all, by choice or by the constraints of their environment, while remaining historically dynamic, economically sophisticated, and culturally rich on their own terms. The rise of Ghana and later Sahelian empires represents one important historical pathway among several that existed across the diverse societies of pre-imperial West Africa, not the single measure of historical achievement against which all other West African societies should be judged.

Historical Vocabulary
Segmentary Society

A segmentary society is a form of social and political organization built from nested kinship groups — households, extended families, lineages, and sometimes wider clans — in which authority rests with senior members of each group rather than with a single centralized ruler. Segmentary societies can mobilize labor, resolve disputes, and coordinate defense effectively without the kind of centralized bureaucratic state structure historians associate with later empires.

Many pre-imperial West African societies functioned as segmentary societies, and elements of segmentary organization persisted at the local level even after larger states like Ghana, Mali, and Songhai emerged. Understanding this term helps students avoid the mistaken assumption that "real" political organization requires centralized kingship.

Historical Vocabulary
Desiccation

Desiccation refers to a long-term process of drying, in this context the gradual reduction in rainfall and increase in aridity that transformed the Sahara from a comparatively wet, lake-dotted region into the desert known today, a process that unfolded over several thousand years beginning roughly in the fourth millennium BCE.

Desiccation is an essential background concept for West African history because it explains why population concentrated along the Sahel and the major river systems of West Africa — areas that retained enough water and vegetation to support farming and herding — rather than remaining dispersed across what had once been a far more habitable Saharan landscape.

Historical Vocabulary
Big Man Polity

A big man polity is a political arrangement in which authority and influence are accumulated by an individual through personal achievement — success in farming, trade, warfare, oratory, or the gathering of followers and dependents — rather than through inherited office alone. Big-man authority is typically less stable and less easily transmitted across generations than formal chieftaincy or kingship, though over time successful big men in some West African societies established lineages of leadership that became more formally inheritable.

This concept helps historians explain how some early West African communities developed concentrated political authority gradually, through individual achievement and accumulated prestige, rather than through sudden conquest or external imposition.

Primary Source Window

Al-Idrisi, Description of West African Trading Towns (12th century)

Creator: Muhammad al-Idrisi, North African geographer · Date: mid-12th century CE · Type: Geographic compendium drawing on earlier travelers' and merchants' reports

Context: Al-Idrisi compiled one of the most influential medieval Arabic geographic works, drawing on earlier accounts, including reports from North African and Saharan merchants who had traveled into West Africa. Although al-Idrisi wrote centuries after the period covered in this chapter, his account preserves descriptions of West African towns and trading networks that historians believe reflect long-standing regional patterns of settlement and exchange rather than entirely new developments.

Al-Idrisi described West African towns as places where merchants gathered to exchange salt, metal goods, and agricultural products, noting the organized markets and the presence of distinct quarters where particular goods were traded or particular crafts practiced.

Analysis: Because al-Idrisi wrote well after the period examined in this chapter, historians must use his account cautiously, as indirect evidence rather than a direct description of earlier centuries. However, when read alongside the archaeological evidence from sites like Jenné-jeno — which shows occupational quarters and long-distance exchange goods centuries before al-Idrisi wrote — his account helps confirm that the patterns of organized regional and long-distance trade he described had very deep roots in West African history, rather than emerging suddenly alongside Islamic trans-Saharan trade.

Historical Debate

Why Did More Centralized Political Authority Emerge in Some Parts of Early West Africa but Not Others?

The question: By the centuries just before Ghana's rise, some West African regions show evidence of growing political centralization — concentrated wealth, emerging settlement hierarchies, and more formalized leadership — while other regions retained predominantly segmentary, lineage-based systems for much longer. Historians disagree about what best explains this difference.

Interpretation A — Trade-Driven Centralization: Some historians argue that access to long-distance trade routes and valuable trade goods was the primary driver of political centralization. Communities positioned along key trade corridors, such as the Niger inland delta, could accumulate unusual wealth that ambitious leaders used to attract followers, fund military force, and extend authority over neighboring groups, eventually producing the kind of concentrated political power associated with early Ghana.
Interpretation B — Agricultural Surplus and Demographic Pressure: Other historians emphasize agricultural productivity and population growth as the more fundamental cause. In this view, regions with unusually productive agricultural environments, such as the Niger floodplain, supported denser populations and greater social differentiation regardless of trade access, and trade wealth, where it existed, was as much a consequence of prior agricultural surplus and population concentration as an independent cause of political change.

Evidence: Archaeological evidence from Jenné-jeno shows both unusually productive floodplain agriculture and early evidence of long-distance exchange, making it difficult to fully separate the two factors at this particular site. Comparative evidence from regions with strong agricultural potential but weaker trade connections, and from regions with strong trade connections but more limited agricultural potential, can help historians evaluate which factor better predicts the emergence of more centralized authority.

Why it matters: This debate shapes how historians understand the deeper causes of state formation throughout African history, including the rise of Ghana itself, and more broadly informs comparative debates about the origins of early states in many regions of the world.

Student task: Based on the evidence presented in this chapter, which interpretation do you find more persuasive for the case of Jenné-jeno and the Niger inland delta? What additional evidence would help historians resolve this debate more confidently?

Multiple Perspectives

Experiencing Growing Settlement and Trade in the Niger Inland Delta

From the perspective of an established farming lineage: Growing trade and settlement could bring welcome opportunities to exchange agricultural surplus for valuable goods such as iron tools, salt, or imported copper ornaments, strengthening the lineage's wealth and prestige relative to neighboring kin groups.
From the perspective of a specialist craft community, such as ironworkers: Increasing demand for iron tools and weapons could bring growing prosperity and social importance, but also required careful management of the ritual status often associated with smithing, which set blacksmith communities apart from farming lineages in ways that could bring both respect and social distance.
From the perspective of a newly arrived migrant family seeking land: Established lineages with prior claims to land and ritual authority over local earth shrines could make it difficult for newcomers to gain full access to farmland or political standing, often requiring new arrivals to negotiate subordinate or client relationships with established lineages in exchange for land use rights.
From the perspective of an ambitious "big man" leader: The growth of trade and settlement offered new opportunities to accumulate followers, wealth, and influence beyond what inherited lineage status alone could provide, but also created competition with other ambitious individuals and established lineage heads over access to land, trade routes, and political authority.
Cause and Effect

The Rise of Jenné-jeno as an Early Urban Center

Long-Term Causes

The exceptional agricultural productivity of the Niger inland delta floodplain; the spread of ironworking technology improving agricultural efficiency; centuries of population growth across the wider Sahel and savanna region following the desiccation of the Sahara.

Short-Term Causes

The specific decision of early settlers around 250 BCE to establish a community at this particular location on the floodplain; the early development of fishing, rice cultivation, and cattle herding adapted to the immediate local environment; the establishment of exchange relationships with communities possessing copper and other goods not locally available.

The Event/Transformation

Gradual but substantial growth of the settlement over several centuries into a town with distinct occupational quarters, extensive iron production, and evidence of long-distance trade goods.

Effects

The establishment of a major regional economic center; the accumulation of wealth and trade connections that, in the broader Niger basin region, helped lay the foundation for the rise of Ghana and successor Sahelian empires; a lasting demonstration that urban-scale settlement could develop without centralized state authority.

Historical complexity: The growth of Jenné-jeno cannot be reduced to a single cause. It resulted from the interaction of favorable environment, technological innovation, demographic growth, and the gradual construction of exchange relationships extending well beyond the immediate region — a combination of factors typical of early urbanization processes studied by historians and archaeologists around the world.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Village Life from the Pre-Imperial Period Through the Rise of Ghana

What Changed

  • Some settlements grew dramatically in size and economic specialization, as at Jenné-jeno
  • Trade networks lengthened and intensified, eventually connecting West Africa to trans-Saharan and North African markets
  • Political authority in some regions concentrated into more formal and territorially extensive chieftaincies and, eventually, the kingdom of Ghana
  • Religious life in some communities began incorporating new influences, including, in later centuries, Islam among ruling elites

What Stayed the Same

  • Lineage-based kinship systems continued to organize land allocation, marriage, and local dispute resolution at the village level
  • Farming, herding, and fishing remained the foundation of most household economies
  • Earth shrines and ancestral ritual continued to provide religious meaning and to legitimate local authority, even as new political structures emerged above the village level
  • Blacksmiths and other craft specialists retained distinctive social and sometimes ritual status

Why the pattern matters: Recognizing this combination of continuity and change helps students understand later Sahelian empires not as a complete break from earlier West African society, but as a new political layer constructed on top of enduring local institutions that continued to shape everyday life for most West Africans throughout the imperial period.

Everyday Life and Social History

A Farming Household on the Niger Floodplain

For most people living in the centuries examined in this chapter, daily life centered on the agricultural calendar and the household compound rather than on distant political developments. A typical farming household on the Niger floodplain would organize its year around the river's seasonal flood cycle: preparing and planting fields as the floodwaters receded, tending crops such as rice, sorghum, or millet through the growing season, and harvesting before the next flood arrived. Fishing, often carried out by specific household members or by specialized fishing communities living nearby, supplemented the diet and provided goods for trade with farming and herding neighbors.

Within the household, labor was organized by age, gender, and kinship status. Senior men and women typically directed the labor of younger relatives and, in wealthier households, of dependents or clients attached to the lineage through marriage alliances or other social obligations. Children began contributing labor — minding livestock, helping with planting and harvest, assisting in craft production — from a young age, and the gradual assumption of greater responsibility marked the passage toward adult status, often formalized through age-grade initiation in societies that practiced this institution.

Housing typically consisted of round or rectangular structures built from local materials — mud brick, wattle and daub, or similar techniques adapted to local climate and available resources — organized into compounds that housed extended families. Pottery, produced often by women using techniques passed down within particular lineages or communities, served both practical storage and cooking functions and, in many communities, carried social and aesthetic significance reflected in distinctive local styles that archaeologists use to trace cultural patterns and exchange relationships.

Religious practice was woven into daily and seasonal life rather than separated into a distinct sphere: rituals marking planting and harvest, rites of passage marking birth, initiation, marriage, and death, and ongoing relationships with ancestral spirits and earth deities shaped how households understood their place in the world and their relationship to their land and community. For most people in this period, this combination of agricultural labor, kinship obligation, and religious practice — far more than any distant political development — constituted the substance of daily life.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has examined West Africa in the centuries before the rise of Ghana and the other great Sahelian empires, arguing that this earlier period deserves serious historical attention in its own right rather than being treated merely as an undifferentiated prelude to imperial history. West Africa in this period was a region of striking environmental diversity, stretching from the Sahara's southern fringe through the Sahel and savanna to the dense forests of the Guinea coast, and this environmental diversity shaped equally diverse patterns of subsistence, settlement, and social organization.

Agriculture and pastoralism, adapted to local conditions, supported steadily growing populations across the region. The spread of ironworking, evidenced as early as the Nok culture of central Nigeria, transformed farming, craft production, and warfare. Settlement patterns evolved from dispersed villages toward larger, more complex towns, exemplified by the remarkable archaeological record at Jenné-jeno on the Niger's inland delta, which demonstrates occupational specialization and long-distance trade connections centuries before any recognized Sahelian empire existed.

Political authority in this period rested primarily on kinship and personal achievement rather than centralized state institutions. Segmentary, lineage-based systems organized most communities, with authority distributed among lineage heads, earth priests, and other ritual specialists. In some places, particularly where trade or agricultural surplus concentrated unusual wealth, ambitious "big man" leaders accumulated followers and influence that, over time, could harden into more formal and inheritable chieftaincy.

Regional and long-distance exchange networks connected communities across considerable distances well before intensified trans-Saharan trade developed from the eighth century CE onward. These networks moved iron, salt, food surpluses, and craft goods, creating economic interdependence between specialized farming, herding, and fishing communities and providing the commercial infrastructure on which later trans-Saharan gold and salt trade would be built.

Kinship organized nearly every dimension of social life, determining land rights, marriage practices, inheritance, and social obligation through lineage systems that varied between patrilineal, matrilineal, and bilateral forms across different societies. Age-grade systems, where present, organized collective labor and military defense and created bonds of solidarity beyond individual lineages. Gender roles varied considerably across the region, with women holding significant economic and, in some societies, political importance.

Religious and ritual life, centered on ancestor veneration, earth shrines, and a range of ritual specialists including diviners, healers, and ritually significant blacksmiths, provided shared meaning and legitimated political and social authority throughout this period. Material culture, including the remarkable terracotta sculpture of the Nok culture and the pottery traditions recovered from sites like Jenné-jeno, provides important evidence of the artistic and technical sophistication of early West African societies.

This chapter has emphasized that the developments traced here directly prepared the ground for the rise of Ghana and later Sahelian empires. The agricultural surplus, settlement growth, political authority structures, trade networks, kinship institutions, and religious traditions developed in this period did not disappear when larger states emerged; they persisted as the essential local foundation on which imperial authority was constructed and through which most West Africans continued to experience daily life even after the rise of celebrated kingdoms and empires.

Finally, this chapter has insisted on the genuine diversity of pre-imperial West Africa, cautioning against treating the region as a single undifferentiated society. Sahelian pastoralists, savanna grain farmers, floodplain fishing and rice-farming communities, and forest-zone yam farmers each followed distinct historical trajectories shaped by their particular environments, even while remaining connected through regional exchange. This diversity persisted throughout the imperial period that followed and remains a defining feature of West African history to the present day.

Understanding this chapter equips students to approach the chapters that follow — on Ghana, Mali, Timbuktu, Songhai, and the forest kingdoms — with a clearer sense of what these celebrated states inherited from earlier West African history, and what continued to operate beneath and alongside imperial authority throughout the centuries these later chapters examine.

  • Prompt 5 (Causation): Choose either the growth of regional trade networks or the emergence of more centralized political authority in parts of pre-imperial West Africa, and write an essay explaining its causes. Distinguish between long-term underlying conditions and more immediate triggers, and explain why a single-cause explanation would be insufficient.

MGU Library Research Project

Reconstructing a Pre-Imperial West African Community

This project asks you to use multiple types of historical evidence to reconstruct a picture of daily life and social organization in a pre-imperial West African community, similar to the methods archaeologists and historians use to study Jenné-jeno and similar sites.

  1. Choose a region and environment. Select one West African ecological zone discussed in this chapter — Sahel, savanna, Niger floodplain, or forest — and research its specific environmental conditions, including rainfall, soils, and available resources.
  2. Research subsistence strategies. Using library and database resources, investigate what crops, livestock, or fishing practices were characteristic of communities in this zone during the centuries examined in this chapter.
  3. Research social and political organization. Investigate what historians and anthropologists know about kinship systems, lineage organization, and political authority among peoples associated with this region in the pre-imperial or early imperial period.
  4. Evaluate the evidence used. Identify what kinds of evidence — archaeological, linguistic, oral traditional, or later written sources — historians rely on for this particular region and discuss the strengths and limitations of that evidence.
  5. Write your findings. In a 3–5 page essay (or equivalent multimedia presentation), describe the environment, economy, and social organization of your chosen community, citing the evidence you used and reflecting on what remains uncertain or contested among historians.

Library resources to use: Archaeological journals and databases, African history reference collections, the MGU History Library's materials on West African archaeology and oral tradition, and scholarly works on early West African urbanism and kinship systems.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter has asked students to rethink a common assumption embedded in many popular accounts of African history: that the story of West Africa begins meaningfully with Ghana, Mali, and the gold-laden caravans that crossed the Sahara. That assumption, however understandable, obscures centuries of West African historical achievement that made those later empires possible in the first place. The farmers who developed sophisticated agricultural systems suited to wildly different environments, the blacksmiths who transformed tool-making and warfare, the traders who built regional exchange networks across hundreds of kilometers, and the lineage heads and big-man leaders who constructed workable systems of authority without centralized states — all of these people and processes deserve to be understood as historically significant in their own right, not merely as an unnamed backdrop to imperial history.

This chapter also matters because of what it reveals about how historians study societies that left no written records of their own. The combination of archaeological excavation, linguistic analysis, environmental science, and careful, critical use of later written sources demonstrates that the absence of indigenous writing does not mean the absence of historical knowledge. Students who understand how historians reconstruct this period are better equipped to understand similar methodological challenges throughout African history and throughout world history more broadly, wherever societies are studied primarily through evidence other than their own contemporary written records.

The themes introduced here — environmental diversity, agricultural adaptation, kinship-based political authority, regional exchange, and the gradual, locally rooted construction of more centralized power — will recur throughout Part IV of this textbook. When students read about Ghana's gold trade, Mali's imperial administration under Mansa Musa, or Songhai's military and commercial power, they should recognize these later achievements as built upon, rather than separate from, the foundations examined in this chapter.

Finally, this chapter's insistence on regional diversity carries a lesson that extends well beyond West Africa. Africa as a whole, examined across the full scope of this textbook, is not one place with one history; it is a continent of extraordinary regional, environmental, linguistic, and cultural diversity, in which broadly similar processes — agricultural adaptation, social organization, political development, exchange, and religious life — took distinctly different forms in different places and times. Understanding West Africa before empire prepares students to carry that same attentiveness to diversity and complexity into every subsequent chapter of Africa's long and varied history.

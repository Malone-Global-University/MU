African American Urban History

African American urban history examines the making of Black city life in the United States, from emancipation and Reconstruction to the Great Migration, neighborhood organization, political struggle, deindustrialization, mass incarceration, and contemporary urban change.

Overview

This textbook traces the historical development of African American urban communities and the institutions, cultures, and conflicts that shaped them. Students study migration, segregation, street organizations, civil rights politics, economic transformation, crime, punishment, gentrification, and community memory in order to understand the long struggle for survival, autonomy, and belonging in Black urban America.

Learning Outcomes

  • Explain the historical roots of African American urban life after emancipation.
  • Analyze the impact of migration, segregation, and redlining on Black neighborhoods.
  • Describe the role of youth clubs, social clubs, and street organizations in urban identity.
  • Understand the relationship between civil rights politics and urban community power.
  • Evaluate the effects of deindustrialization, the drug economy, and the war on drugs.
  • Assess how mass incarceration and redevelopment reshaped Black urban communities.
  • Interpret the cultural memory and historical legacy of African American city life.

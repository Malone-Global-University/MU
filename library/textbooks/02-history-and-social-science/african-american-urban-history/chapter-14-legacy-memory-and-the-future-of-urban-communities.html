Part V — Fragmentation, Redevelopment, and the Contemporary City

Legacy, Memory, and the Future of Urban Communities

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"The historical record does not end in a verdict. It opens into a question — not what happened, but what we intend to do with what we now know." — Paraphrase of a common theme in urban policy scholarship, late twentieth century

Every major city in the United States carries within its geography the accumulated weight of decisions made over more than a century. The shape of a neighborhood — where its streets run, what its housing stock looks like, how its schools are funded, which businesses have come and gone, which populations stayed and which were pushed out — reflects not only the choices of the people who live there now but the choices of planners, politicians, bankers, real estate agents, judges, police chiefs, and federal officials who may have been dead for decades. The neighborhoods that this textbook has examined — the South Side of Chicago, South Central Los Angeles, North Philadelphia, Southeast Washington, D.C., East Oakland, the near West Side of Detroit — are legible as historical documents if one knows how to read them. Their poverty, their vacant lots, their under-resourced schools, and their elevated rates of violence are not natural conditions. They are the products of a history that this textbook has traced from the Great Migration through redlining, deindustrialization, the crack epidemic, the War on Drugs, mass incarceration, and gentrification's uneven redevelopment.

This final chapter asks a different kind of question than those that preceded it. The earlier chapters were primarily retrospective — they asked how we got here, tracing the structural forces and individual decisions that shaped Black urban communities over more than a century. This chapter is both retrospective and prospective. It asks: How should we interpret the history we have traced? What does it mean to remember a history of violence, exclusion, and institutional failure without either romanticizing the past or surrendering to despair about the present? What have communities themselves built in response to that history, and what can those efforts teach policymakers, scholars, and citizens about what kinds of interventions actually work? And what does the long view of urban history suggest about the conditions under which American cities might become more just, more livable, and more equitable for the people who have been excluded from their prosperity for so long?

The question of historical interpretation is not merely academic. How a society interprets its past shapes what it is willing to do in the present. If street organizations are interpreted primarily as criminal enterprises that emerged from cultural pathology, then the appropriate policy response is suppression — more policing, longer sentences, aggressive prosecution. If they are interpreted primarily as adaptive responses to structural exclusion — communities of young men building forms of belonging, protection, and identity in conditions of concentrated poverty, racial segregation, and institutional abandonment — then the appropriate response is different: address the structural conditions that made those organizations meaningful in the first place. These two frameworks have competed in American policy debates since at least the 1960s, and which one prevails at any given moment has enormous consequences for the people who live in the neighborhoods where street organizations operate.

The question of community resilience is equally important and equally contested. Throughout this textbook, one of the central themes has been the tension between structural forces and human agency — between the enormous power of policies, markets, and institutional decisions to shape the conditions of urban life, and the equally real capacity of individuals and communities to build meaning, solidarity, and collective life within those conditions. Black urban communities have never been simply the passive victims of structural forces, however powerful those forces have been. They have organized churches, mutual aid societies, civic organizations, labor unions, political movements, cultural institutions, and community development organizations that have sustained collective life under extraordinarily adverse conditions and that have, in many cases, produced models of community building and problem-solving that deserve more recognition than they typically receive. The history of community resilience — of what people have built and how they have survived — is as much a part of the historical record as the history of suppression and decline.

Violence prevention programs represent one important dimension of the community response to the conditions this textbook has described. Beginning in the 1990s and accelerating in the 2000s and 2010s, a range of organizations developed approaches to reducing street violence that drew on community knowledge, employed individuals with direct experience of street life, and were grounded in an understanding of violence as a public health problem rather than simply a criminal justice problem. Programs like Cure Violence, Advance Peace, and dozens of city-specific initiatives demonstrated measurable reductions in violence in the neighborhoods where they operated, and they did so through methods — credible messengers, street outreach, conflict mediation, hospital-based intervention — that law enforcement alone had consistently failed to produce. These programs deserve serious examination both as historical phenomena and as sources of policy lessons.

The urban policy lessons of this history are both sobering and, in a certain sense, clarifying. The interventions that have been most consistently harmful to Black urban communities — urban renewal displacement, concentrated public housing, mandatory minimum sentencing, mass incarceration, aggressive gang suppression, and zero-tolerance policing — have each, at the time of their adoption, been presented as solutions to the problems of urban poverty and crime. Each has instead deepened those problems, for reasons that an understanding of the history behind them could have predicted. The interventions that have been most consistently beneficial — stable affordable housing, quality schools with adequate resources, community-based violence intervention, investment in workforce development and local economic opportunity, and the empowerment of community organizations with genuine resources and autonomy — have been chronically underfunded, politically contested, and treated as marginal rather than central to urban policy. The gap between what evidence suggests works and what policy has actually provided is one of the most important findings of the urban history this textbook has traced.

The future of American cities — and specifically the future of the Black urban communities whose history this textbook has traced — remains genuinely open. The concentrated poverty, racial segregation, and institutional disinvestment that this history has described are not immutable features of the urban landscape. They were produced by specific policies and decisions, and they can be transformed by different policies and decisions. Cities that have made sustained investments in affordable housing, quality schools, workforce development, community health, and violence prevention have demonstrated that conditions can improve significantly. The question is not whether transformation is possible but whether it is politically prioritized — whether the communities that have borne the costs of a century of exclusionary policy will finally be treated as priorities rather than problems.

This chapter concludes the textbook by bringing together the threads of a history that began with emancipation and the Reconstruction-era community building described in Chapter 1. The distance from there to here — from the mutual aid societies of the post-emancipation South to the violence prevention programs of the twenty-first-century city, from the first Black migrations to northern industrial cities to the gentrification-driven displacements of the contemporary urban landscape — is enormous. But certain themes run continuously through that entire history: the insistence of Black communities on building collective life under conditions designed to prevent it; the systematic suppression of those community-building efforts whenever they became effective enough to challenge existing power arrangements; the recurring failure of policy to address the structural conditions that produced urban poverty and street organization; and the enduring possibility, demonstrated again and again, that investment, equity, and genuine political will can change the conditions of urban life in ways that make a real difference to the people who live there. That is the lesson of the history this textbook has traced — not a lesson of despair, but a lesson of what becomes possible when a society is willing to reckon honestly with its own past.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Evaluate competing historical interpretations of Black street organizations, including criminological, sociological, structural, and community-centered frameworks, and assess what evidence supports each.
  2. Trace the major forms of community resilience that Black urban communities have demonstrated across the period covered by this textbook, identifying continuities in organizational strategy across different historical contexts.
  3. Describe the development and major models of community-based violence prevention programs, explaining their theoretical foundations, operational methods, and evidence of effectiveness.
  4. Analyze the urban policy lessons of the history traced in this textbook, identifying patterns in which policies have worsened conditions and which have improved them, and explaining why harmful policies have often been politically preferred over effective ones.
  5. Compare the experiences of different groups — longtime residents, newcomers, community organizations, elected officials, researchers, and street organization members — in interpreting the legacy of the history this textbook has described.
  6. Contextualize contemporary urban challenges — housing insecurity, neighborhood disinvestment, concentrated poverty, street violence, and institutional distrust — within the long historical processes that produced them.
  7. Assess the relationship between historical memory and contemporary policy, explaining how different ways of remembering the past translate into different policy agendas for the future.
  8. Identify the major community development organizations, violence prevention programs, policy reforms, and social movements that have sought to address the conditions described in this textbook, and evaluate their achievements and limits.
  9. Explain what the history of Black urban communities suggests about the conditions under which urban inequality can be reduced, drawing on specific historical evidence from across the textbook.
  10. Distinguish between individual and structural explanations for urban poverty and street violence, and articulate why this distinction matters for both historical analysis and policy design.
  11. Connect the history traced in this textbook to contemporary debates about policing, housing, education, economic inequality, and racial justice in American cities.
  12. Synthesize the major themes of the textbook — migration, segregation, youth culture, drug markets, incarceration, and fragmentation — into a coherent interpretation of the trajectory of Black urban life in twentieth and twenty-first-century America.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. How should historians and students interpret the history of Black street organizations — as criminal enterprises, as social formations, as political responses to exclusion, or as some combination of these? What are the stakes of each interpretive framework?
  2. What forms of community resilience have Black urban communities demonstrated across the period covered by this textbook, and how do those forms connect to the longer history of Black community building going back to Reconstruction?
  3. What do community-based violence prevention programs reveal about the nature of street violence that criminal justice frameworks tend to miss? What models have proven most effective, and why?
  4. What are the most important policy lessons of the history traced in this textbook? Which interventions have consistently worsened conditions, which have consistently improved them, and what explains the political persistence of harmful policies despite their record of failure?
  5. How does the history of Black urban communities challenge or complicate dominant narratives about American urban development, including narratives of progress, decline, and revitalization?
  6. What does the history of community fragmentation described in Chapter 13 suggest about the relationship between neighborhood stability, institutional presence, and violence? What conditions produce stability, and what conditions produce fragmentation?
  7. How should communities, scholars, and policymakers approach the question of historical memory when the history being remembered includes significant violence, criminality, and institutional failure alongside resilience, creativity, and community building?
  8. What would a genuinely just urban policy look like, based on the evidence of what has and has not worked in the history this textbook has traced? What political conditions would be required to implement it?
  9. What does the future of American cities look like from the vantage point of the communities this textbook has examined? What are the most pressing challenges, and what resources — historical, organizational, political, and moral — exist for addressing them?

Historical Context

To understand the questions this chapter addresses, it is necessary to situate them within the full arc of the history this textbook has traced. That history began with emancipation and Reconstruction — the extraordinary achievement of legal freedom followed by its systematic destruction through political violence, the Black Codes, and the construction of Jim Crow. It traced the Great Migration northward, as millions of Black Southerners fled racial terror and agricultural poverty for the industrial cities of the North and West, only to find themselves confined by redlining, restrictive covenants, and discriminatory housing policy to overcrowded, underfunded neighborhoods that the white middle class was simultaneously abandoning. It examined the development of Black youth culture in those neighborhoods — the corner groups, athletic clubs, car clubs, and social organizations that were the precursors of street organizations — and the ways in which territorial identity, neighborhood belonging, and collective self-protection became important dimensions of urban Black youth life in a context of concentrated poverty and racial exclusion.

The middle sections of this textbook examined the political and economic crises that transformed the urban landscape in the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s. The civil rights movement and the Black Power movement produced significant political achievements and significant governmental repression, most infamously through the FBI's COINTELPRO program, which systematically destroyed Black political organizations through infiltration, disinformation, and the murder of key leaders. Deindustrialization eliminated the manufacturing jobs that had provided a path to stable working-class life for the first and second generations of Great Migration families, leaving the third and fourth generations in neighborhoods stripped of economic opportunity. The crack cocaine epidemic of the 1980s transformed the economics of street life, turning what had been local informal economies into more systematically organized drug markets and dramatically escalating the violence associated with territorial control. And the War on Drugs, with its mandatory minimum sentences, gang enhancements, and aggressive policing strategies, produced the mass incarceration of Black urban men at rates that destabilized families, disrupted neighborhood social organization, and, paradoxically, often deepened rather than reduced violence by removing the stabilizing presence of older, more experienced men who had historically served as checks on younger members' most extreme behavior.

The most recent chapters of the textbook examined the contemporary urban landscape — the fragmentation of large street organizations into smaller, less structured crews and cliques; the role of social media in both escalating conflict and creating new forms of reputation-based violence; the displacement of longtime Black residents from gentrifying neighborhoods; and the persistent gap between public policy discourse and community experience in cities that have been simultaneously celebrated for their revitalization and hollowed out by displacement and economic exclusion. This is the historical context within which questions of legacy, memory, and future direction must be understood — not as abstract policy problems but as the living consequences of specific historical choices that can be, and in some cases are being, made differently.

Several intellectual traditions have shaped the scholarly and policy conversation about Black urban communities that this chapter engages. The sociological tradition, running from the Chicago School of urban sociology through the work of William Julius Wilson, Douglas Massey, and Robert Sampson, has emphasized the structural dimensions of urban inequality — the ways in which residential segregation, concentrated poverty, and institutional disinvestment create the conditions for street violence, street organization, and civic disengagement. The criminological tradition, drawing on deterrence theory, social disorganization theory, and more recently, public health frameworks for understanding violence, has examined what kinds of interventions actually reduce violence and under what conditions. The historical tradition, including the work of scholars like Nicholas Lemann, Thomas Sugrue, Arnold Hirsch, and numerous others, has contextualized contemporary urban conditions within the long history of policy choices and structural forces that produced them. And the community-based tradition — the tradition of practitioners, organizers, formerly incarcerated people, violence interrupters, and neighborhood advocates who have built programs and organizations within the communities this textbook has studied — has contributed knowledge about what actually works that no purely academic framework could generate.

The question of historical memory is particularly important for understanding why the history of Black street organizations has been so contested and so consequential for policy. Popular and media representations of street organizations have been overwhelmingly shaped by frameworks that emphasize criminality, pathology, and threat — frameworks that support punitive policy responses and that have historically been used to justify levels of policing and incarceration in Black communities that would be unacceptable in white communities. Scholarly and community-based frameworks have increasingly challenged these representations, arguing that street organizations are best understood as products of specific historical conditions that can be changed, and that punitive responses to violence have consistently failed to address those underlying conditions while producing enormous collateral harm. The contest between these frameworks is not merely academic. It is a contest about what kinds of communities American cities will contain in the decades ahead, and who will have the power to shape them.

The concept of community resilience — the capacity of communities to maintain collective function, social bonds, and institutional life under conditions of severe stress — has become increasingly important in both scholarly and policy conversations about Black urban communities. For too long, the dominant discourse about these communities focused almost exclusively on deficits: what was absent, what had failed, what needed to be fixed from outside. A more accurate and more useful framework recognizes that the same communities that have been subjected to redlining, deindustrialization, mass incarceration, and gentrification have also maintained churches, schools, community organizations, cultural institutions, family networks, and informal support systems of remarkable durability and creativity. Understanding those resilience resources — what they are, how they work, and what conditions support or undermine them — is essential for any honest assessment of the future of urban communities.

The policy landscape of the early twenty-first century has been shaped by a growing recognition of the failures of the punitive approach to urban violence and poverty that dominated policy from the 1980s through the early 2000s. The movement for criminal justice reform, the growing evidence base for community-based violence intervention, the recognition that housing affordability and neighborhood stability are fundamental to community health, and the broader reckoning with racial inequality that intensified in the wake of multiple high-profile instances of police violence against Black people have created a political environment in which different approaches to urban policy have become more visible and more politically feasible than they were even a decade ago. Whether this political moment translates into sustained structural change, or whether it proves to be another cycle of attention followed by retrenchment, is one of the genuinely open questions with which this chapter concludes.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

William Julius Wilson (1935–present)
Sociologist at Harvard University whose 1987 book The Truly Disadvantaged argued that concentrated urban poverty resulted primarily from economic restructuring and the departure of the Black middle class from inner cities following the end of formal segregation, rather than from cultural pathology as argued by some earlier commentators. Wilson's structural analysis was enormously influential in reshaping scholarly and policy discourse about Black urban communities, and his subsequent work — including When Work Disappears (1996) — continued to emphasize the role of deindustrialization and labor market exclusion in producing the conditions of concentrated poverty.
Gary Slutkin (1948–present)
Epidemiologist and founder of Cure Violence (originally called CeaseFire), a Chicago-based organization that applies a public health model to the problem of street violence. Slutkin, drawing on his experience fighting infectious disease in Africa, argued that violence spreads through social networks in ways analogous to disease transmission, and that the most effective intervention is to interrupt transmission using credible community messengers — people with direct experience of street life who can intervene in conflicts before they turn lethal. Cure Violence has been evaluated in multiple cities and has demonstrated significant reductions in shooting incidents in the areas where it operates.
DeVon Boggan (1971–present)
Founder of Advance Peace, a fellowship-based violence prevention model developed first in Richmond, California, that provides intensive support, mentorship, life coaching, and financial stipends to the highest-risk individuals in a community — those most likely to be involved in violent incidents either as perpetrators or victims. The Advance Peace model differs from law enforcement approaches in its frank acknowledgment that the most violent individuals in a community are also the most vulnerable, and that addressing their needs and expanding their options is more likely to reduce violence than punishing them.
Mariame Kaba (1972–present)
Activist, educator, and prison abolitionist who has been one of the most influential voices arguing that community safety is best produced through investment in education, housing, mental health, and economic opportunity rather than through policing and incarceration. Kaba's work, including her widely read essays and her book We Do This 'Til We Free Us (2021), provides an intellectual framework for understanding violence as a social problem requiring social solutions — a framework that connects directly to the structural analysis developed in this textbook.
Alex Kotlowitz (1955–present)
Journalist and author of There Are No Children Here (1991), one of the most widely read accounts of life in Chicago's Henry Horner Homes public housing project in the late 1980s. Kotlowitz's book, which followed two brothers growing up in the project, humanized the experience of urban poverty and public housing for a wide audience and contributed to changing public understanding of what life in concentrated public housing actually meant for the children who grew up there. His subsequent work, including An American Summer (2019), has continued to document urban violence and community life in Chicago.
Michelle Alexander (1967–present)
Legal scholar and author of The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness (2010), which argued that mass incarceration functions as a system of racialized social control that reproduces many of the features of Jim Crow within the formal legal framework of the post-civil rights era. Alexander's book became one of the most widely read works of popular scholarship on race and the criminal justice system in the twenty-first century, reaching audiences well beyond academic circles and contributing significantly to the political momentum of criminal justice reform movements.
Ta-Nehisi Coates (1975–present)
Journalist and author whose 2014 Atlantic essay "The Case for Reparations" brought sustained public attention to the argument that the wealth gap between Black and white Americans is a direct consequence of identifiable historical policies — redlining, housing discrimination, discriminatory wage practices, and the systematic exclusion of Black workers from the wealth-building mechanisms of the postwar economy — for which some form of direct remedy is warranted. Coates's essay, grounded in specific historical research about housing discrimination in Chicago, connected the analytical framework of academic urban history to a broad popular audience.
Cure Violence (founded 1995 as CeaseFire; renamed 2011)
A Chicago-based organization that applies a public health model to street violence, using trained community outreach workers — many of them formerly incarcerated individuals with credibility in street contexts — to interrupt conflicts before they turn lethal, change the behavior and thinking of the highest-risk individuals in a neighborhood, and shift community norms away from violence. Cure Violence has operated in dozens of American cities and in international contexts, and multiple independent evaluations have found it associated with significant reductions in gun violence.
Advance Peace (founded 2016, Richmond, CA)
An organization offering fellowship programs that provide intensive support, mentorship, life coaching, travel, and modest financial stipends to the small number of individuals in each community most likely to be involved in gun violence. The Advance Peace model, developed in Richmond, California, which had one of the highest per-capita homicide rates in California before the program, is associated with dramatic reductions in gun homicides and has expanded to multiple California cities and beyond.
Black Lives Matter (founded 2013)
A political movement founded by Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors, and Opal Tometi in response to the acquittal of George Zimmerman in the killing of Trayvon Martin, and subsequently mobilized around a series of high-profile police killings of Black people. Black Lives Matter is a decentralized movement rather than a single organization, but it has been enormously influential in shifting public discourse about policing, racial inequality, and community safety, contributing to the political conditions in which community-based approaches to violence prevention have gained greater visibility and resources.
Richmond, California
An industrial city in the San Francisco Bay Area whose history parallels in miniature the broad arc of this textbook: a Black community that grew through the Second World War defense industry migration, experienced deindustrialization and concentrated poverty in the postwar decades, suffered high rates of street violence in the crack era, and became in the 2000s and 2010s a nationally significant site for innovative community-based violence prevention. Richmond's dramatic reduction in gun homicides — from a peak of forty-seven in 2007 to eleven in 2014 — is associated with a combination of community violence intervention programs, economic investment, and a shift in police culture toward community partnership.
Community Violence Intervention (CVI) programs
A broad category of community-based, non-law-enforcement programs designed to reduce street violence through outreach, conflict mediation, individual support, and community norm change. CVI programs include hospital-based violence intervention programs, street outreach and interruption programs, group violence intervention programs, and fellowship or opportunity-based programs. Federal investment in CVI programs expanded significantly in the early 2020s, representing a significant shift in federal policy toward community-based approaches to public safety.
Community Development Corporations (CDCs)
Nonprofit organizations, typically neighborhood-based, that pursue economic development, affordable housing, and community revitalization in low-income urban communities. CDCs emerged in the late 1960s and have grown into an important sector of community-controlled economic development, providing affordable housing, small business support, workforce development, and community services in neighborhoods that private markets and government agencies have historically underserved.

Section 14.1

Historical Interpretations: How We Remember Shapes What We Do

The Contest Over Interpretation

How a society interprets its history is never a purely intellectual matter. Interpretations of the past shape the frameworks through which present conditions are understood, and those frameworks shape the policy responses that seem available and appropriate. The history of Black street organizations in American cities has been interpreted in strikingly different ways by different observers — law enforcement agencies, academic researchers, community members, journalists, policymakers, and the members of those organizations themselves — and these different interpretations have carried dramatically different policy implications. Understanding the major interpretive frameworks, the evidence that supports and challenges each, and the political stakes of each is essential preparation for engaging seriously with the question of what to do about urban violence and concentrated poverty.

The oldest and most politically influential interpretive framework is what scholars sometimes call the pathology model — the view that street organizations and urban violence are primarily the products of deficient culture, broken families, and the absence of the values and behaviors that produce success in mainstream American society. This framework has roots in nineteenth-century social commentary about immigrant communities, was applied to Black urban communities in the early twentieth century, and found its most influential modern expression in the early 1960s in Daniel Patrick Moynihan's controversial report on the Black family and in Oscar Lewis's concept of the "culture of poverty." The pathology model has been a persistent presence in American political discourse ever since, surfacing in debates about welfare reform, criminal sentencing, public housing policy, and urban school reform. It consistently locates the causes of urban poverty and violence inside communities rather than in the structural conditions that shape those communities.

The pathology model has been extensively criticized by scholars across multiple disciplines, and the evidence against its most extreme versions is overwhelming. As this textbook has argued throughout, the concentrated poverty, institutional disinvestment, and street violence of American inner cities were not produced by cultural failure. They were produced by redlining, deindustrialization, the construction of concentrated public housing, mass incarceration, and the systematic exclusion of Black Americans from the wealth-building mechanisms that built the white middle class. The behaviors and organizational forms that the pathology model labels as dysfunction — territorial identity, informal economic activity, street organization membership — are, as the earlier chapters of this textbook have argued, adaptive responses to specific structural conditions, not expressions of cultural pathology. When those structural conditions change, behavior changes with them.

The structural interpretation — associated with the sociological tradition running from W.E.B. Du Bois through Robert Park, E. Franklin Frazier, William Julius Wilson, and Douglas Massey — locates the causes of urban poverty and street violence in the structural conditions of residential segregation, labor market exclusion, and institutional disinvestment. Street organizations, in this framework, are understood as adaptive responses to concentrated poverty — as forms of social organization that provide belonging, protection, status, and sometimes economic opportunity to young men in neighborhoods where legitimate paths to these goods are systematically blocked. The structural interpretation supports a very different policy agenda than the pathology model: instead of emphasizing policing, sentencing, and behavioral modification, it emphasizes investment in affordable housing, quality education, workforce development, and the reduction of residential segregation.

A third interpretive framework, which has become increasingly important in both scholarship and community-based practice, might be called the community knowledge model. This framework emphasizes that the most accurate and useful understanding of street organizations and urban violence must be grounded in the knowledge of the communities in which those organizations operate — the knowledge of mothers, elders, faith leaders, youth workers, and the members of street organizations themselves. This knowledge is not simply anecdotal; it includes sophisticated understandings of the social dynamics that produce violent conflict, the relationships and obligations that structure street life, the grievances and aspirations that motivate individual behavior, and the community resources and relationships that can be mobilized to reduce violence. The community knowledge model argues that policy designed without genuine engagement with this knowledge will consistently fail, because it will misread the phenomena it is trying to address.

These three frameworks are not entirely distinct, and the most effective approaches to understanding urban violence typically draw on elements of all three. But they differ in important ways in what they emphasize, what they treat as causal, and what they treat as the appropriate level of intervention. Understanding these differences — and understanding which framework is most consistent with the historical evidence this textbook has presented — is one of the key intellectual tasks of this final chapter.

The Question of Collective Memory

Collective memory — the way a community or a society remembers its past — is never simply a record of what happened. It is a selective, contested, and politically charged process through which communities construct narratives about their origins, their identity, and their values. In the case of Black street organizations, collective memory operates at multiple levels simultaneously: within the organizations themselves, within the neighborhoods where they are based, within the cities that have responded to them, and within the broader American culture that has consumed mediated representations of them through film, music, journalism, and political rhetoric.

Within the street organizations themselves, memory is preserved through oral tradition, through the stories that older members tell younger ones, through the rituals and symbols that mark organizational identity, and increasingly through music, social media, and other digital forms. This internal memory is typically far more complex than external representations suggest. It includes memories of solidarity and mutual support alongside memories of violence, memories of specific grievances that produced specific conflicts alongside memories of reconciliation and peace-making, memories of individual courage alongside memories of individual tragedy. The organizations whose histories this textbook has traced — the Blackstone Rangers and the Black P Stones, the Vice Lords, the Gangster Disciples, the Crips and the Bloods — contain within their collective memories rich and contradictory accounts of who they are and where they came from that do not reduce to the caricatures of media representation.

Within neighborhoods, the memory of street organizations is similarly complex. Long-term residents of neighborhoods where specific organizations have been present for decades hold memories that include both the violence and harm those organizations have caused and the forms of protection, solidarity, and community presence they have provided. This ambivalence is not hypocrisy or moral confusion. It reflects a genuine complexity in the role that street organizations have played in communities that have been chronically abandoned by the institutions that should have provided safety, economic opportunity, and civic infrastructure. The church elder who both grieves the young men lost to street violence and recognizes that the organization they belonged to was the primary institution providing structure and belonging to the young men of her neighborhood is not contradicting herself. She is accurately describing a situation of institutional failure that created conditions in which harmful organizations became, in certain respects, necessary ones.

The politics of memory around street organizations also operates at the level of public policy and popular culture. The dominant representation of street organizations in American popular culture — through films like Boyz n the Hood, Menace II Society, and The Wire, through news coverage focused almost exclusively on violent incidents, and through political rhetoric that has consistently framed street organizations as existential threats to social order — has shaped public understanding in ways that support punitive policy responses and that make it difficult to have honest conversations about the structural conditions that produced those organizations. Documentary filmmakers, scholars, community journalists, and formerly incarcerated writers and artists have produced counter-narratives that offer more complex and more historically grounded accounts, but these counter-narratives have reached smaller audiences and have had less political impact than the dominant representations.

Historical Vocabulary
Collective Memory

Collective memory refers to the shared ways in which a group — a community, a city, a nation, an organization — remembers and interprets its past. Unlike individual memory, collective memory is a social phenomenon, shaped by the stories, rituals, monuments, media representations, and institutional practices through which a group constructs and transmits its sense of its own history.

Collective memory is always selective and always contested. It is shaped by power: the groups and perspectives with the most institutional and cultural authority tend to have the greatest influence over which events are remembered, how they are remembered, and what they are understood to mean. The history of Black street organizations illustrates this dynamic clearly: the dominant collective memory of those organizations, as represented in mainstream media and political discourse, emphasizes violence and criminality while minimizing the structural conditions that produced them and the social functions they served in communities of concentrated poverty and institutional abandonment.

Collective memory matters for policy because the frameworks through which a community or a society remembers its past shape the frameworks through which it understands its present. Communities that have experienced the same history — the same redlining, the same deindustrialization, the same drug epidemic and mass incarceration — often remember that history very differently depending on their position relative to the power dynamics that shaped it. The challenge of honest historical memory is to create space for the full complexity of what happened, including the structural forces, the political choices, the community responses, the individual experiences of both harm and resilience, and the lessons that honest engagement with all of these can provide for the future.

In the context of this textbook, collective memory connects directly to the question of what kind of urban future is possible. Cities that have honestly reckoned with the history of redlining, urban renewal displacement, and mass incarceration — that have documented and acknowledged the specific harms done to specific communities — have generally been better positioned to design effective remedies than cities that have maintained comfortable fictions of benign neglect or market-determined outcomes. Historical honesty is not only a moral virtue. It is a practical prerequisite for effective policy.

The Scholarly Record and Its Development

The scholarly study of Black street organizations has undergone significant transformation over the past several decades, moving from earlier frameworks that emphasized cultural dysfunction toward increasingly sophisticated structural and ethnographic analyses. Key works in this transformation include Sudhir Alladi Venkatesh's ethnographic research in the Robert Taylor Homes, published in Gang Leader for a Day (2008) and in academic articles that documented the social organization of drug markets in public housing in extraordinarily granular detail; Philippe Bourgois's In Search of Respect: Selling Crack in El Barrio (1995), which examined the drug economy in East Harlem through the lens of structural inequality and what Bourgois called the "culture of terror" produced by poverty and racial exclusion; Alice Goffman's On the Run: Fugitive Life in an American City (2014), which examined how aggressive policing and mass incarceration reshape daily life in Black urban neighborhoods; and the oral history collections and community-based scholarship that have brought the voices of community members, formerly incarcerated people, and street organization members directly into the scholarly record.

The work of urban historians — Thomas Sugrue's The Origins of the Urban Crisis (1996) on Detroit's deindustrialization, Arnold Hirsch's Making the Second Ghetto (1983) on Chicago's public housing policy, Robert Self's American Babylon (2003) on Oakland's postwar development, and dozens of other city-specific studies — has provided the structural and historical context within which sociological and ethnographic research can be properly understood. These works collectively demonstrate that the conditions of concentrated Black urban poverty were not naturally occurring but were produced by specific identifiable policies, and that understanding those policies is essential for designing effective remedies.

The most honest and most useful scholarly interpretations of Black street organizations are those that hold multiple frameworks in productive tension — that take seriously both the structural conditions that produced street organizations and the agency of the individuals and communities within them; that document both the harms that organizations have caused and the functions they have served; that are honest about the complexity of individual motivations and community dynamics without reducing street life to either pure victimhood or pure predation; and that connect the specific history of particular organizations in particular places to the broader structural history that this textbook has traced. These interpretations do not produce simple policy prescriptions. But they provide the honest analytical foundation from which effective policy can be built.

Section 14.2

Community Resilience: What Black Urban Communities Have Built

The Meaning of Resilience

The concept of community resilience has become an important analytical tool in urban studies, public health, and community development practice over the past several decades. In its broadest sense, resilience refers to the capacity of a system — a community, an institution, an ecosystem — to maintain its essential functions and recover from disruption. In the context of Black urban communities, resilience refers to the capacity of those communities to maintain collective life, sustain institutions, build social bonds, and resist or recover from the multiple forms of disruption and harm that this textbook has described: deindustrialization, mass incarceration, drug market violence, urban renewal displacement, and gentrification-driven displacement.

It is important to be clear about what resilience does and does not mean in this context. Community resilience is not a euphemism for the ability to endure harm without complaint. The communities this textbook has studied have not been resilient because they were naturally hardy or because their culture prepared them for suffering. They have been resilient because they have possessed — and have built, maintained, and in many cases rebuilt after disruption — the social, institutional, and organizational resources that enable collective action, mutual support, and community life. Those resources include churches, civic organizations, community development corporations, mutual aid networks, cultural institutions, neighborhood associations, violence prevention organizations, and the dense webs of informal relationship and trust that scholars call social capital and collective efficacy. Understanding what those resources are and what conditions support or undermine them is one of the most important analytical tasks in urban history and policy.

This textbook has traced a long tradition of Black community resilience beginning with the Reconstruction era. The mutual aid societies, fraternal organizations, churches, schools, and newspapers that freedpeople built in the post-emancipation South represented an extraordinary act of collective institution-building under conditions of extreme adversity and violence. The organizations they built — particularly the Black church and the mutual aid society — provided the organizational template that Black communities reproduced, in adapted forms, in every subsequent context: in the Bronzevilles and Black Bottoms of northern industrial cities during the Great Migration, in the civil rights organizations of the 1950s and 1960s, in the community development corporations and neighborhood associations of the post-urban-renewal era, and in the violence prevention organizations of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.

This organizational continuity is one of the most important and least recognized aspects of African American community history. The specific institutional forms change — from mutual aid society to church auxiliary to neighborhood association to community development corporation — but the underlying organizational logic remains remarkably consistent: voluntary, community-controlled, collectively self-governed, oriented toward providing collective support and protection to members in a context where formal institutions are unavailable, hostile, or inadequate. The violence prevention programs examined in Section 14.3 are, in this sense, the latest iteration of an organizational tradition that goes back more than a century and a half.

Institutions That Have Sustained Communities

The Black church has remained the most durable and institutionally significant community institution in African American urban life throughout the period this textbook has covered. Churches provided the organizational infrastructure for the civil rights movement, as Chapter 7 described, but their role in sustaining community life has extended far beyond explicitly political activity. Black churches in urban communities have operated schools, credit unions, food pantries, job training programs, housing development organizations, counseling services, and literally hundreds of other social service functions that the state has either failed to provide or provided in ways that were inaccessible, inadequate, or demeaning to Black community members. The persistence of Black institutional church life through deindustrialization, mass incarceration, and gentrification is a testament both to the organizational capacity of those institutions and to the depth of the need they serve.

Community development corporations represent one of the most significant organizational innovations of the post-urban-renewal era. CDCs emerged from the community action programs of the War on Poverty and from the community control movements of the late 1960s, and they have grown into a significant sector of community-controlled economic development. At their best, CDCs provide affordable housing to residents who would otherwise be displaced by rising market rents, support local small businesses, develop workforce skills, and provide the community with a stake in the economic life of their neighborhood rather than being simply a labor pool for outside interests. The CDC sector has significant limitations — chronic underfunding, dependence on government contracts that can be withdrawn, the tension between development goals and community organizing goals — but it represents a genuine attempt to give communities institutional tools for shaping their own economic futures.

Mutual aid networks, which Chapter 1 identified as one of the foundational organizational forms of African American community life, have experienced significant renewal in the twenty-first century. The COVID-19 pandemic in particular demonstrated the continuing relevance of community mutual aid as a mechanism for collective survival in conditions of state failure: in cities across the country, Black communities organized mutual aid networks to provide food, medicine, and essential services to vulnerable community members when government programs were overwhelmed or inaccessible. These twenty-first-century mutual aid networks drew consciously on the historical tradition of mutual aid in Black communities, and many explicitly cited that tradition as a source of both inspiration and organizational knowledge. The continuity between the mutual aid societies of the Reconstruction South and the mutual aid pods of the COVID-era city is not a metaphor. It is a living organizational inheritance.

Cultural institutions — music, art, literature, film, and the spaces that contain and support them — have been equally important dimensions of Black community resilience. The music traditions that emerged from the specific conditions of Black urban life — blues, jazz, gospel, soul, funk, hip-hop — are not only artistic achievements of the highest order. They are also forms of community memory, collective identity, and emotional processing that have provided Black communities with resources for maintaining dignity, expressing grief and joy, and sustaining the sense of collective life that more formal institutions sometimes fail to provide. The relationship between hip-hop culture, which emerged from the conditions described in this textbook, and the communities of concentrated poverty that produced it is a relationship of both reflection and resilience: hip-hop reflected those communities' experiences back to them in forms they recognized, and in doing so created spaces of solidarity and collective identity that had value independent of the structural conditions that produced them.

Youth organizations and mentorship programs have been a particularly important site of community resilience in the face of the conditions this textbook has described. The organizations that have most effectively supported young people in high-violence, high-poverty neighborhoods — including Boys and Girls Clubs, YMCA branches in Black communities, neighborhood athletic leagues, arts programs, and youth-led organizations of various kinds — have done so by providing something that the street, for all its costs, also provided: a sense of belonging, recognized identity, structured activity, and adult investment in young people's futures. The most effective of these organizations are not simply alternatives to street life. They are community institutions that take seriously the question of what young people need and provide it — adults who show up consistently, spaces that are genuinely safe, activities that are genuinely engaging, and pathways to futures that are genuinely imaginable.

Historical Vocabulary
Structural Racism

Structural racism refers to the cumulative and compounding effects of an array of factors that systematically privilege white people and disadvantage people of color, including history, culture, institutional policies and practices, and societal norms. Unlike individual racism — the prejudiced attitudes and discriminatory actions of specific individuals — structural racism operates through the normal functioning of institutions and systems without requiring the conscious racist intent of any individual actor.

In the context of this textbook, structural racism is most clearly visible in the housing policies described in Chapter 3: the Federal Housing Administration's redlining practices explicitly rated neighborhoods' creditworthiness based on racial composition, systematically denying home loans and investment to Black neighborhoods while subsidizing white homeownership in the suburbs. These policies were not personal prejudice, though personal prejudice was certainly present. They were institutional policies that produced racially differentiated outcomes through the normal operation of the housing finance system.

The concept of structural racism is important for historical analysis because it helps explain how racial inequality can be reproduced and deepened without requiring anyone to be personally racist. When mortgage lending practices, school funding formulas, zoning laws, criminal sentencing guidelines, and hiring practices each individually produce outcomes that are racially neutral in form but racially unequal in effect, the cumulative result is a system that reproduces racial inequality through institutional mechanisms rather than through individual prejudice. Addressing structural racism requires changing institutional policies and practices, not merely changing individual attitudes.

The policy implications of the structural racism framework are significant. If urban poverty and street violence are produced by structural conditions — by identifiable policies that can be changed — then the appropriate response is to change those conditions: to end discriminatory lending practices, to equalize school funding, to invest in affordable housing in all neighborhoods, to reform criminal sentencing, to address the employment discrimination that continues to produce racially unequal labor market outcomes. These are harder and more expensive interventions than policing and incarceration. But the historical evidence this textbook has presented suggests they are the interventions that actually address the conditions that produce the problems they are aimed at.

Social Capital, Collective Efficacy, and What Makes Neighborhoods Work

Sociologists have developed several concepts that help explain why some high-poverty neighborhoods maintain relatively low levels of violence while others with similar economic profiles experience high levels of violence. The most important of these concepts are social capital and collective efficacy. Social capital refers to the networks of relationships, norms of reciprocity, and trust that enable people to work together to achieve common goals. Collective efficacy refers specifically to the shared belief among community members that they can work together to maintain social order and achieve desired outcomes — including the expectation that neighbors will intervene to prevent violence or other harm rather than look away.

Research by Robert Sampson and his colleagues, particularly the Project on Human Development in Chicago Neighborhoods, found that collective efficacy was one of the strongest predictors of neighborhood violence rates, even after controlling for poverty, racial composition, and residential stability. Neighborhoods with high collective efficacy — where residents trusted one another, where neighbors knew and looked out for each other's children, where people believed collective action could make a difference — had significantly lower violence rates than demographically similar neighborhoods with low collective efficacy. This finding has important implications for understanding both the causes of urban violence and the design of effective interventions.

Critically, the research also found that collective efficacy is not simply a cultural characteristic of particular populations. It is shaped by structural conditions. Residential stability — the ability of people to remain in their neighborhood over time — is one of the strongest predictors of collective efficacy, because trust and social bonds take time to build and are disrupted by frequent movement. Policies that destabilize neighborhoods — urban renewal demolition, public housing displacement, foreclosure crises, and gentrification — destroy social capital and collective efficacy even when they produce physical improvements in the built environment. This finding is one of the most important lessons of urban sociology for housing and redevelopment policy: the social fabric of a neighborhood is as important as its physical fabric, and interventions that improve the physical environment while destroying the social fabric may do more harm than good.

The history of Black urban communities traced in this textbook can be read through the lens of collective efficacy as a history of the systematic destruction and persistent reconstruction of community social capital. Urban renewal displaced hundreds of thousands of families from established neighborhoods with high social capital into new environments where social bonds had to be rebuilt from scratch — and in the case of high-rise public housing projects, often did not rebuild because the design of those projects was specifically hostile to the informal outdoor interaction through which neighborhood social bonds are typically formed. Mass incarceration removed large numbers of men — many of whom, despite their criminal convictions, were important nodes in neighborhood social networks — from their communities, destabilizing those networks and reducing the collective capacity for informal social control. Gentrification displaces longtime residents who have invested in community relationships and institutions and replaces them with newcomers who often have little connection to the social fabric of the neighborhood. Each of these disruptions makes the next one harder to recover from, as the cumulative depletion of social capital makes communities less able to resist further disruptions.

Section 14.3

Violence Prevention Programs: Models, Evidence, and Lessons

The Limits of Punishment-Only Approaches

The punitive approach to urban violence — more police, longer sentences, more aggressive prosecution — dominated American policy from the early 1980s through the 2010s, and its record is one of the clearest examples of what this chapter earlier called the gap between what evidence suggests works and what policy has actually provided. The United States incarcerated more of its population than any other country in the world, and the communities most affected by that incarceration were Black urban neighborhoods. Yet despite this extraordinary investment in punishment, urban violence remained devastatingly high in many cities for decades. The crack epidemic of the 1980s and early 1990s produced murder rates in cities like New York, Washington, D.C., Los Angeles, and Chicago that remain among the highest recorded in the modern era. The subsequent decline in violence in the 1990s — a genuine and significant development — was almost certainly driven primarily by factors other than incarceration, including the decline of the crack epidemic itself, demographic change, economic growth, and specific policing tactics like hot-spot policing that focused resources on the highest-crime locations.

The limitations of pure punishment as a violence reduction strategy are not difficult to understand from the perspective of the structural analysis this textbook has developed. If violence is concentrated in specific communities because of the structural conditions of concentrated poverty, residential segregation, institutional disinvestment, and the absence of legitimate economic opportunity, then removing individuals from those communities through incarceration does not change those underlying conditions. It may reduce violence in the short term by removing specific actors, but it leaves in place all of the structural conditions that produced those actors and that will produce their replacements. And as Chapter 11 described, mass incarceration carries enormous collateral costs — family disruption, the stigma of a criminal record that blocks access to housing and employment, the removal of network nodes that maintained informal social order — that can actually increase community vulnerability to violence in the medium and long term.

The emerging consensus in violence prevention research is that effective strategies must address both the immediate dynamics of violent conflict and the underlying conditions that produce it. Law enforcement has a legitimate role in this work — particularly in identifying the highest-risk individuals, communicating consequences, and responding to violence when it occurs. But law enforcement alone, without the community trust, social investment, and structural change that make alternative futures genuinely imaginable for the young men most likely to be involved in violent incidents, cannot produce the sustained reductions in violence that communities need and deserve.

The Public Health Model and Cure Violence

The public health model of violence, developed most systematically by Gary Slutkin and applied through Cure Violence (originally CeaseFire), represents a fundamental reconceptualization of what violence is and what kind of intervention can most effectively reduce it. The public health model treats violence not primarily as a crime problem requiring punishment but as a communicable condition spreading through social networks in ways that are at least partly analogous to the spread of infectious disease. Just as diseases spread through contact — through the mechanisms of transmission that connect individuals in a population — violence spreads through social networks as grievances, norms, and behaviors transmit from person to person and group to group. And just as disease transmission can be interrupted by targeting the specific mechanisms through which it spreads, violence transmission can be interrupted by targeting the specific social dynamics through which it spreads.

The Cure Violence model deploys two types of community workers to interrupt violence transmission: outreach workers and violence interrupters. Outreach workers build long-term relationships with the highest-risk individuals in a neighborhood — typically young men between fourteen and twenty-five who are actively involved in the street conflicts that produce violent incidents — providing mentoring, support, and connections to services. Violence interrupters, who are typically individuals with direct personal experience of street life and credibility in the specific communities where they work, respond in real time to conflicts that are escalating toward violence, intervening to cool tensions, mediate disputes, and prevent retaliatory cycles from spiraling out of control. The interrupter's credibility is essential to their effectiveness: they can engage with people who would refuse contact with police or other official actors, and they can communicate in ways that are recognized as authentic rather than as outside imposition.

Multiple independent evaluations of Cure Violence programs have found significant reductions in shootings and killings in the neighborhoods where the programs operate. A 2009 evaluation of the Chicago program by the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health found reductions in shootings of between 16 and 34 percent in program sites compared to comparison areas. Similar evaluations in New York City, Baltimore, Philadelphia, Kansas City, and other cities have found broadly comparable results. These results are not uniformly positive — program effectiveness varies significantly by implementation quality, funding stability, and community context — but the overall evidence base is strong enough to support significant expansion of the model.

The theoretical foundations of the Cure Violence model also illuminate some of the most important dynamics of street violence that purely criminal justice frameworks tend to miss. The observation that most street violence is reciprocal and retaliatory — that killings produce retaliatory killings that produce further retaliation in cycles that can continue for years — is not new, but the public health framework makes it actionable in ways that criminal justice frameworks do not. If the primary mechanism driving violence is the obligation of retaliation — the norm that a slight, an injury, or a killing demands a violent response — then the primary point of intervention is the moment when that obligation is being debated and negotiated, not the moment when the police arrive after a shooting. Cure Violence programs operate at precisely that earlier moment, in real time, in the social relationships where the decision to retaliate is being made.

Advance Peace and Fellowship-Based Models

The Advance Peace model, developed in Richmond, California, by DeVon Boggan, extends the community-based approach to violence prevention in a direction that is even more explicitly focused on addressing the needs and expanding the options of the highest-risk individuals in a community. The model is based on the observation that gun violence is highly concentrated — a very small number of individuals in any given community are responsible for a disproportionate share of violent incidents, either as shooters or as targets of retaliation — and that addressing violence effectively requires engaging that small number of highest-risk individuals directly and intensively.

The Advance Peace fellowship provides participants with a combination of one-on-one mentorship from a trained life coach, case management services, connections to education and employment, travel experiences that expose participants to possibilities beyond their immediate neighborhood, and modest financial stipends that provide some economic security during the fellowship period. The program's premise — that the young men most deeply involved in street violence are also the young men with the most severe deficits of support, opportunity, and reason for hope — challenges the punitive framework's tendency to treat those most involved in violence as the people the community needs to be protected from, rather than as community members whose needs are simply more extreme than others'.

Richmond's results have been dramatic. The city's homicide rate fell from forty-seven killings in 2007 to eleven in 2014, a reduction of more than seventy-five percent, during the period when the program was active and funded. This reduction cannot be attributed entirely to Advance Peace — other factors, including community policing changes and economic investment, also played roles — but the timing and the concentration of reductions in the specific networks where the fellowship operated suggest that the program was a significant contributing factor. Richmond's experience has made it one of the most studied and most replicated models in the violence prevention field, with adaptations operating in Sacramento, Stockton, Fresno, and other California cities, and interest in the model extending nationally.

Group Violence Intervention and Focused Deterrence

A third important model in the violence prevention landscape is group violence intervention (GVI), also known as focused deterrence, associated primarily with the work of criminologist David Kennedy. The GVI model, which originated as Operation Ceasefire in Boston in the 1990s and has been replicated in dozens of cities, combines direct communication with the highest-risk groups in a city — typically through organized call-ins attended by gang members, community leaders, law enforcement, and service providers — with a clear and credible message: violence will be met with the full force of coordinated law enforcement action against the entire group, but every individual who wants to leave violence behind will receive direct support in doing so.

The focused deterrence model differs from both traditional law enforcement and from community-based intervention in important ways. It acknowledges explicitly that most young men involved in street violence are not primarily motivated by a desire for violence — they are motivated by the same desires for safety, respect, and economic opportunity that motivate everyone — and that the violence they participate in is largely the product of the specific dynamics of group conflict, retaliation, and honor that characterize street life. The model attempts to change those dynamics by changing the cost-benefit calculus of group violence: making it clear that violence will bring consequences not just to individual shooters but to their entire network, while simultaneously making clear that help is available for those who want a different path.

Boston's initial implementation of Operation Ceasefire is one of the most extensively studied violence reduction interventions in the literature. Youth homicides in Boston fell by approximately sixty-three percent in the two years after the intervention was launched, a reduction far greater than any comparison city experienced in the same period. Subsequent implementations in other cities have produced more mixed results, reflecting the sensitivity of the model to implementation quality, community trust, and local context. But the model's theoretical coherence — its explicit engagement with the group dynamics that drive street violence — has made it influential in both research and practice communities.

Historical Vocabulary
Community Investment

Community investment refers to the directed flow of resources — financial capital, institutional support, public services, physical infrastructure, and social support — into neighborhoods and communities that have experienced long-term disinvestment. Community investment is the opposite of the disinvestment that this textbook has traced through the history of redlining, white flight, urban renewal, and the concentrated poverty that resulted from those policies.

Community investment takes many forms. Physical investment includes affordable housing construction and rehabilitation, school facility improvement, park and recreational space development, commercial corridor revitalization, and infrastructure repair. Economic investment includes small business lending, workforce development, job training, and the attraction of employers who hire locally. Social investment includes violence prevention programs, mental health and substance use treatment, after-school programs, mentorship, and the community organizations that coordinate these services. Institutional investment includes improving the quality of schools, health clinics, libraries, and other public services in disinvested neighborhoods.

The concept of community investment carries an important historical dimension. For the communities this textbook has studied, disinvestment was not a neutral process of market failure. It was actively produced by government policy — by the redlining that denied mortgages, by the urban renewal that destroyed neighborhoods, by the highway construction that bisected Black communities, by the public housing that concentrated poverty, and by the mass incarceration that removed men from their families and communities. Genuine community investment must therefore be understood not merely as benevolent charity but as a form of historical repair — an acknowledgment that specific harms were done by specific policies, and that those harms create an obligation to specific remedies.

The politics of community investment are complex and contested. Disinvested neighborhoods are typically represented by political actors with limited power, are targeted by developers and investors with different interests than existing residents, and are subject to the dynamics of gentrification that can produce physical improvements while displacing the communities that investments were ostensibly intended to benefit. Genuine community investment requires mechanisms for community control over the direction and benefits of investment — not merely investment in communities, but investment by and for communities.

Hospital-Based Violence Intervention Programs

Hospital-based violence intervention programs (HVIPs) represent another important model in the community violence intervention landscape. These programs are premised on the observation that the emergency room is a critical intervention point in the cycle of street violence: the survivors of violent incidents — gunshot wound patients, stabbing victims — are at extremely high risk of future involvement in violence, either as retaliating parties or as future targets of retaliation. The period of hospitalization, when the reality of violence is most visceral and the individual is most open to reflection on their situation, represents a window of potential intervention that traditional approaches have largely ignored.

Hospital-based programs assign trained intervention specialists — often themselves survivors of violence, with credibility in street contexts — to make contact with gunshot wound patients in the emergency room and during their hospitalization, building relationships, connecting patients to services, and providing support during the critical period when the decision about retaliation is being made. These programs have been implemented in hospitals across the country, including in Oakland (Caught in the Crossfire), Philadelphia (Sisters Against Senseless Killing), and Baltimore (the Violence Intervention Program), and evaluations have found significant reductions in re-injury and re-arrest among program participants compared to non-participants.

The hospital-based model also connects violence prevention to the broader public health infrastructure in ways that have important policy implications. When hospital systems take responsibility for violence intervention as a health issue rather than a criminal justice issue — when they hire intervention specialists, train emergency department staff in trauma-informed approaches, and connect patients to community resources — they are implicitly acknowledging the public health framing that Cure Violence and other programs have advocated: that violence, like other public health conditions, is most effectively addressed through prevention and intervention rather than through punishment after the fact.

The Public Health Approach to Violence: Theoretical Foundations and Program Logic

The public health model of violence begins with an observation that may seem obvious but has important policy implications: most of the people who kill or are killed in American cities know each other. Street violence in urban communities is not primarily random predation by strangers. It is concentrated within specific social networks, driven by specific conflicts that follow recognizable escalation patterns, and shaped by specific norms — particularly the norm of retaliation — that give violence its cyclical, compounding character.

The core insight of the public health approach is that violence is not simply the sum of individual violent choices. It spreads through social networks. When one person in a network is shot, the pressure on others in that network to respond violently is enormous — driven by grief, fear, and the norms of honor and loyalty that structure street life. Interrupting that cycle requires intervening in the social dynamics of the network, not just apprehending individual offenders after violence has occurred. — Paraphrase of Cure Violence program documentation

These three components address different parts of the violence cycle and are most effective when implemented together.

The credible messenger — someone who has lived the life of the people they are trying to reach, who speaks their language, who understands their situation from the inside rather than as an outside observer — can access relationships and conversations that no police officer, social worker, or community organizer without that specific experience can access. This insight has fundamental implications for how violence prevention programs should be staffed and led.

Section 14.4

Urban Policy Lessons: What the History Tells Us

Patterns of Policy Failure

One of the most sobering lessons of the urban history this textbook has traced is the consistency with which well-intentioned policies have produced harmful outcomes in Black urban communities. This pattern is not coincidental. It reflects a recurring failure to engage honestly with the structural conditions of those communities, to take seriously the knowledge and interests of the people who live in them, and to evaluate policies honestly in terms of their actual effects rather than their intended effects. Understanding why policies have failed is at least as important as understanding what has worked, because the pattern of failure reveals the structural biases and political dynamics that must be addressed for more effective policy to be possible.

Urban renewal — the federally funded program that demolished "blighted" neighborhoods in American cities from the 1950s through the 1970s — is perhaps the clearest example of a policy whose effects on Black communities were almost entirely negative despite its stated humanitarian goals. As Chapter 12 described, urban renewal destroyed hundreds of thousands of units of low-cost housing, displaced hundreds of thousands of Black families from established neighborhoods, destroyed the social capital of communities that had sustained themselves through generations of adversity, and replaced mixed-use neighborhoods with high-rise public housing projects and institutional facilities that served primarily white-dominated universities, hospitals, and civic institutions. The clearance of Black neighborhoods for what journalist James Baldwin memorably called "Negro removal" was not a policy mistake. It was a consistent and predictable outcome of a policy designed without meaningful input from affected communities and implemented by agencies whose interests and relationships were primarily with real estate developers and institutional expansion.

The construction of large-scale high-rise public housing, as Chapter 3 described, represented another policy that concentrated poverty, destroyed social capital, and created conditions actively hostile to community life. The design of high-rise towers — long anonymous corridors, minimal outdoor common space, elevators that functioned as sites of crime rather than community interaction, density that exceeded what existing community institutions could serve — was almost perfectly calibrated to undermine the informal social control mechanisms that sustain safe neighborhoods. When concentrated poverty, architectural hostility to community life, chronic underfunding of maintenance and services, and the deliberate placement of the poorest and most disadvantaged families in the same buildings were combined, the result was predictably the concentrated pathology that was then used to justify further stigmatization of the residents.

The War on Drugs, as Chapters 10 and 11 described, represents the third major policy failure in the history this textbook has traced. The mandatory minimum sentencing laws, the crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity, the gang enhancements, the zero-tolerance policing strategies, and the dramatic expansion of the prison system — all presented as necessary responses to the crack epidemic and the violence associated with it — produced mass incarceration that has had devastating and lasting effects on Black urban communities without producing the sustained reductions in drug use or drug market violence that were their stated justification. The War on Drugs is perhaps the most dramatic example of a policy whose primary effect on the communities it claimed to protect was devastating harm.

Understanding why these harmful policies were adopted and sustained despite evidence of their failure requires attending to the political dynamics that shape urban policy. Each of these policies benefited powerful interests — real estate developers and urban institutions (urban renewal), construction contractors and housing agencies (public housing), law enforcement agencies and prison systems (the War on Drugs) — while harming communities that were politically marginal. The communities most affected by these policies — Black urban communities, largely poor, largely excluded from the political processes that determined their fate — had limited capacity to block policies they opposed or to demand accountability for the harms those policies caused. The political economy of urban policy has consistently privileged the interests of the politically connected over those of the politically marginal, and the communities this textbook has studied have been paradigmatically politically marginal for most of the period under examination.

What Has Actually Worked

The evidence for what has actually improved conditions in Black urban communities points consistently toward a cluster of interventions that the dominant policy framework has chronically underfunded and politically undervalued. Understanding what these interventions are and why they work — and why they have been politically marginalized despite their effectiveness — is one of the most important lessons this textbook can offer.

Stable, affordable housing is perhaps the most fundamental prerequisite for community health. Research across multiple disciplines has established that housing instability — the condition of being frequently threatened with or actually experiencing eviction or displacement — is strongly correlated with poor health outcomes, poor educational outcomes, and involvement in street violence. Children who move frequently do worse in school, develop fewer lasting social bonds, and are more vulnerable to recruitment into street organizations. Adults who face housing insecurity have less capacity to invest in neighborhood relationships, community organizations, or long-term planning of any kind. The consistent finding that housing stability is a necessary foundation for other positive community outcomes implies that housing policy — affordable housing production, eviction protection, anti-displacement policy — must be treated as a public safety issue, not merely a housing issue.

Quality public education — adequately resourced schools with experienced teachers, manageable class sizes, comprehensive curricula, and genuine connections to the communities they serve — is consistently identified in both research and community testimony as the most important institutional resource for young people's future prospects. The systematic underfunding of schools in Black urban communities, which this textbook has traced through the history of property-tax-based school finance, is not merely an educational failure. It is a public safety failure, because the young men most likely to be involved in street violence are overwhelmingly young men whose schools failed to engage and retain them. The relationship between school failure — both the school's failure to serve students and the student's failure to remain enrolled — and subsequent involvement in street life is one of the most consistent findings in the research literature on urban violence. Effective education policy is violence prevention policy.

Economic opportunity — not simply job training programs but genuine pathways to living-wage employment in stable industries — is the third pillar of effective community investment. The deindustrialization story traced in Chapter 8 removed the economic foundation of Black working-class life in most northern and western cities, leaving behind labor markets that offered primarily low-wage service jobs without the stability, benefits, or career ladders that manufacturing employment had provided. The young men who became most deeply involved in drug markets in the 1980s and 1990s were, in many cases, the grandchildren of Great Migration workers who had achieved genuine working-class stability through factory employment — stability that had been eliminated not by their choices but by the decisions of corporate managers, trade policy makers, and urban planners who had no obligation to the communities their decisions destroyed. Rebuilding economic opportunity in disinvested communities requires not merely job training but genuine investment — in infrastructure, in small business development, in living-wage employment creation, and in the removal of the barriers to employment that criminal records create.

Community-based violence intervention, as Section 14.3 described, has a strong and growing evidence base for effectiveness and deserves to be funded and expanded as a core component of public safety strategy rather than as a marginal program competing for resources with law enforcement. The federal Community Violence Intervention initiative, launched in the early 2020s with unprecedented levels of federal investment in non-law-enforcement violence reduction, represented a significant shift in the policy landscape — an acknowledgment that the communities most affected by violence have knowledge and capacities that deserve institutional support, not just surveillance and suppression. Whether this investment is sustained and expanded, or whether it represents another cycle of attention followed by retrenchment, will be one of the most important tests of the political will to take the lessons of urban history seriously.

The Question of Repair: Reparations and Structural Remedies

The history this textbook has traced raises, inescapably, the question of repair — of whether the specific, identifiable harms done by specific, identifiable policies to specific, identifiable communities create an obligation to specific remedies. This is the question that the reparations debate addresses, though that debate is often conducted at a level of abstraction that obscures the most important issues. The question is not primarily the abstract philosophical question of whether descendants of enslaved people are owed compensation for slavery — though that question is also legitimate. It is the more concrete historical and policy question of whether the specific harms done by redlining, urban renewal, discriminatory lending, exclusion from GI Bill benefits, mass incarceration, and other identifiable policies to identifiable communities in identifiable cities create an obligation for identifiable remedies.

When historians have documented, as they have with great specificity, that the Federal Housing Administration's redlining policies denied mortgage credit to Black neighborhoods while subsidizing white homeownership in the suburbs, and that this policy produced a wealth gap between Black and white Americans that persists to the present day, they are providing the factual foundation for a very specific policy question: what remedies are appropriate for this documented, policy-produced harm? When urban planners document that the construction of highways through Black neighborhoods in the 1950s and 1960s destroyed specific communities, displaced specific populations, and reduced the property values and economic prospects of specific neighborhoods for generations, they are documenting a harm that can in principle be remedied — not necessarily by rebuilding what was destroyed, but by providing the resources and opportunities to affected communities that the original policies denied them.

Several American cities have engaged seriously with the reparations question in recent years, conducting studies, establishing commissions, and in some cases implementing specific remedies. Evanston, Illinois, established a local reparations program in 2021 providing housing assistance to Black residents who had been harmed by the city's historical redlining practices. Chicago's 2015 reparations ordinance for victims of police torture under commander Jon Burge provided financial compensation and other services to specific documented victims. These local efforts are limited in scope, but they represent an acknowledgment that historical harm creates contemporary obligation — an acknowledgment that is itself a significant departure from the denial and deflection that have historically characterized official responses to demands for racial repair.

Cause and Effect

The Sources and Limits of Community Resilience in Black Urban Communities

Structural Sources of Resilience

Long-established community institutions — churches, mutual aid networks, fraternal organizations, civic associations — provide organizational infrastructure, social capital, and models of collective action that persist through periods of disruption. Residential stability, where it has been maintained, preserves the social bonds and neighborhood knowledge that enable collective efficacy. Cultural traditions — music, art, food, religious practice — provide shared identity and emotional resources. Dense family and kinship networks provide material support and social belonging.

Structural Threats to Resilience

Residential displacement — through urban renewal, eviction, gentrification, and foreclosure — destroys the social capital accumulated over decades of neighborhood life. Mass incarceration removes community members from their social networks and returns them with the added burdens of criminal records that block access to housing and employment. Chronic underfunding of community institutions — schools, health clinics, parks, libraries — depletes the organizational infrastructure on which resilience depends. Drug market violence creates a climate of fear and distrust that undermines the informal social control and community norms that sustain collective life.

What Resilience Has Produced

Despite devastating structural conditions, Black urban communities have maintained functioning institutions, produced cultural achievements of world historical significance, sustained family and community bonds across generations of disruption, organized the most significant civil rights movements in American history, developed innovative violence prevention models that have been adopted nationally and internationally, and produced political leaders, scholars, artists, and activists who have transformed American public life. These are not small achievements. They are the record of communities that have maintained their humanity and their capacity for collective action under conditions designed to crush both.

What Resilience Cannot Do Alone

Community resilience cannot substitute for structural change. The most resilient communities cannot compensate indefinitely for the absence of adequately funded schools, living-wage employment, stable affordable housing, and equitable public services. The history this textbook has traced is a history not of communities that failed because they lacked resilience but of communities whose resilience was consistently overwhelmed by the magnitude of the structural forces arrayed against them. The lesson is not that communities need to be more resilient. It is that the structural conditions that exhaust resilience need to be changed.

Policy implication: Programs that aim to strengthen community resilience without simultaneously addressing the structural conditions that deplete it — policies that celebrate community strengths while cutting the resources communities depend on — represent a form of policy mystification: acknowledging community capacity while refusing to address community need. Genuine support for resilience requires both celebrating community strengths and addressing the structural conditions that make those strengths necessary.

Section 14.5

The Future of American Cities: What Urban History Suggests

The City as Historical Project

American cities are not natural objects. They are historical projects — the accumulated results of millions of individual and institutional decisions made over more than a century, embedded in physical infrastructure, legal frameworks, and social relationships that persist long after the specific decisions that created them have been forgotten. The racial geography of American cities — the concentrated poverty of specific neighborhoods, the spatial segregation of Black and white populations, the pattern of disinvestment that marks the neighborhoods this textbook has studied — is not the product of natural market forces or cultural sorting. It is the product of specific, identifiable policies that have been documented in detail by historians, sociologists, and investigative journalists: the redlining maps of the Home Owners' Loan Corporation, the discriminatory loan practices of the Federal Housing Administration, the highway construction programs that bulldozed Black neighborhoods, the siting decisions that concentrated public housing in specific areas, the mass incarceration policies that removed hundreds of thousands of Black men from their communities. These policies produced the contemporary urban landscape, and understanding them is a prerequisite for imagining and building a different one.

The future of American cities will be shaped, as the past has been, by choices — about where to invest and where to disinvest, about whose displacement is acceptable and whose housing security must be protected, about how public safety is defined and how it is pursued, about what kinds of communities are considered worth building and sustaining. The history this textbook has traced provides a basis for evaluating those choices — for distinguishing between policies that have consistently produced harm and policies that have consistently produced benefit, for identifying the communities whose interests have been consistently subordinated to others', and for understanding what kinds of structural changes would be necessary to produce a genuinely more equitable urban future.

Several dimensions of the contemporary urban landscape deserve particular attention as sites where the future is being shaped. The housing affordability crisis, which has intensified dramatically in the 2010s and 2020s, represents one of the most significant threats to the long-term viability of Black urban communities. The same neighborhoods that were redlined and disinvested for decades are now, in many cities, among the most rapidly appreciating real estate markets in the country — a transformation that generates wealth for property owners and developers while displacing the longtime residents whose presence, paradoxically, helped create the cultural and social conditions that make the neighborhoods desirable. Anti-displacement policy — including strong tenant protection laws, community land trusts, inclusionary zoning that requires affordable units in new developments, and investment in community-controlled affordable housing — is essential to preventing the completion of the historical irony in which communities that bore the costs of disinvestment are expelled from their neighborhoods before they can benefit from reinvestment.

The debate over policing and public safety, which intensified dramatically in the wake of multiple high-profile police killings of Black people and the political mobilization of the Black Lives Matter movement, is another dimension of the contemporary urban landscape where fundamental choices are being made. The argument between those who see more police and more aggressive policing as the key to urban public safety and those who see community investment, violence intervention, and reform of the criminal justice system as the necessary alternative is not new — it has been the central debate in urban policy for at least sixty years. What is new is the scale of the political mobilization demanding a different approach, the quality of the evidence base for community-based alternatives, and the level of public understanding of the historical record of harm produced by punitive approaches. Whether this combination is sufficient to produce lasting policy change, or whether it represents another cycle of mobilization followed by retrenchment, is one of the genuinely open questions of the present moment.

What Young People in Disinvested Communities Need

The most direct way to ask the question of urban futures is to ask what the young people growing up in the communities this textbook has studied actually need to have genuinely good lives and to contribute to genuinely good communities. The answer is not mysterious, and it is not culturally specific. Young people growing up in disinvested urban communities need what all young people need: schools that genuinely educate them and prepare them for futures they can imagine as genuinely possible; neighborhoods that are safe enough to allow the ordinary social development that children require; adults — family members, teachers, mentors, community leaders — who invest in them consistently and who communicate, through their investment, that those young people matter; economic conditions that make the work of building a legitimate life feel achievable rather than futile; and communities that value and celebrate their full humanity.

The tragedy that this textbook has traced is that all of these things — adequate schools, safe neighborhoods, consistent adult investment, economic opportunity, human dignity — have been systematically denied to the communities under examination, not through cultural failure or individual failing but through specific political and policy choices. The street organizations that emerged in those communities were not the cause of that denial. They were a response to it — imperfect, often harmful, internally contradictory, but reflecting a genuine and legitimate human need for belonging, protection, status, and identity that the surrounding society refused to provide through legitimate channels.

Understanding this history does not require romanticizing street organizations or minimizing the harms they have caused. The violence of drug markets and territorial conflict has killed thousands of young people who deserved to live, and the grief of that violence is real and devastating. But understanding the history does require honest engagement with the conditions that made those organizations meaningful to the young people who joined them — conditions of concentrated poverty, institutional abandonment, and structural exclusion that cannot be understood without the kind of historical analysis this textbook has provided. And it requires commitment to the structural changes that would make those organizations unnecessary: genuine investment in schools and housing and economic opportunity, genuine reform of the criminal justice system, genuine anti-displacement protection for communities facing gentrification, and genuine political will to treat the communities that have borne the costs of a century of exclusionary policy as constituencies deserving of genuine investment and genuine power.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Black Urban Life: Continuities and Changes from Reconstruction to the Present

What Has Changed

  • Formal legal segregation has been abolished, and Black Americans have full formal citizenship rights including the right to vote, hold office, and access public accommodations on equal terms
  • A substantial Black professional and middle class has developed, with significant representation in medicine, law, education, corporate management, government, and many other fields
  • Black cultural production — music, literature, film, art, food — is recognized globally as among the most significant and influential in the world
  • The geography of Black America has diversified, with significant Black suburban and exurban populations, a reverse migration of Black Americans to the South, and Black communities in cities and regions far beyond the original Great Migration destinations
  • The political representation of Black Americans, while still far from proportional, has expanded dramatically from the Reconstruction-era high-water mark and its post-Reconstruction suppression
  • Violence prevention programs, reentry support organizations, and community development institutions have developed sophisticated knowledge and practice bases that offer genuine alternatives to purely punitive approaches

What Has Stayed the Same

  • The racial wealth gap — the difference in median wealth between white and Black American households — has not narrowed significantly and in some periods has widened, reflecting the compounding effects of a century of unequal access to wealth-building institutions
  • Residential segregation in American cities remains extremely high by international standards, reflecting the continuing effects of historical redlining and the persistence of discriminatory housing practices in more subtle forms
  • Black Americans continue to be incarcerated at rates dramatically higher than white Americans, reflecting the continuing racial disparities in criminal justice that mass incarceration produced and deepened
  • The pattern of building community institutions as alternatives to excluding formal institutions, which runs from the Reconstruction-era church and mutual aid society through every subsequent era of Black community building, continues in the violence prevention programs, community development corporations, and mutual aid networks of the present
  • The fundamental political dynamic — the subordination of Black community interests to those of more powerful constituencies in policy processes that formally include but rarely genuinely center those communities — persists across the history this textbook has traced

Why the pattern matters: The combination of genuine formal progress and persistent structural inequality that characterizes the trajectory of Black American life from Reconstruction to the present reflects the specific character of American racial hierarchy: a system that has proven capable of accommodating formal rights and individual success while reproducing systemic inequality through institutional mechanisms that formal rights alone cannot address. Understanding this pattern is essential for understanding why the work of achieving genuine racial equity in American cities remains unfinished and urgently necessary.

The Political Conditions for Change

The history this textbook has traced suggests that structural change in urban conditions requires more than good policy ideas or compelling evidence. It requires political conditions in which the communities most directly affected by urban inequality have sufficient power to shape the policies that govern their lives. The communities this textbook has studied have been politically marginal throughout most of the history under examination — their poverty limiting their political participation, their racial composition making them targets of punitive rather than supportive policy, their geographic concentration giving them limited influence over the broader coalitions that determine urban and federal policy. The moments of genuine improvement in those communities' conditions — the War on Poverty's community action programs, the community development movement of the late 1960s, the federal investment in community violence intervention in the early 2020s — have consistently occurred during periods of unusually high political mobilization by the communities themselves or their allies.

This suggests that the future of Black urban communities depends in significant measure on the political organization and mobilization of those communities — on the continued development of community power sufficient to demand genuine investment, genuine protection from displacement, genuine reform of the criminal justice system, and genuine accountability from the institutions that shape community conditions. The history of Black political organizing in American cities — from the Reconstruction-era Republican mobilizations described in Chapter 1 through the civil rights movement described in Chapter 7, through the neighborhood organizations, community development corporations, and political mobilizations of the post-civil-rights era — is a history of communities building and exercising political power against significant opposition, and it is a history that offers both inspiration and practical lessons for the present.

The work of building genuinely just cities is not a project with a clear endpoint. The history this textbook has traced reveals urban America as a continuously contested space in which different communities, interests, and visions of the future compete for the power to shape conditions and outcomes. The communities that have been most consistently excluded from that competition — the Black urban communities whose history this textbook has traced — have demonstrated remarkable persistence and creativity in asserting their interests and building their capacity for collective action. That persistence and creativity, combined with an honest historical analysis of the structural conditions that have produced urban inequality and the policy choices that can change them, is the foundation on which a more just urban future can be built. The history does not guarantee that future. But it provides the knowledge, and the reason, to work toward it.

Historical Debate

How Should Street Organizations Be Understood? Competing Interpretive Frameworks

The question: Black street organizations have been studied, debated, and represented in American public life for more than half a century. But the frameworks through which they are understood — and the policy responses those frameworks support — differ dramatically depending on whether one starts from a criminal justice framework, a structural sociology framework, a community knowledge framework, or a historical framework. Understanding what is at stake in this interpretive debate is essential for engaging seriously with urban policy.

Interpretation A — The Criminal Enterprise Framework: The criminal enterprise framework, which has dominated law enforcement and much popular discourse, treats street organizations primarily as criminal enterprises engaged in the production and distribution of illegal goods and the use of violence to protect market territory. In this framework, street organization members are rational actors who have chosen criminal careers, and the appropriate policy response is suppression: law enforcement action to disrupt organizations, prosecute members, and incapacitate leaders. This framework has been the basis for most major law enforcement initiatives targeting street organizations from the RICO prosecutions of the 1980s through gang injunctions, gang databases, and the use of conspiracy statutes to prosecute organizational membership. Its policy prescriptions are politically popular because they are punitive and because they locate the problem — and the responsibility — inside the communities experiencing violence rather than in the structural conditions that shape those communities.
Interpretation B — The Structural Sociology Framework: The structural sociology framework, developed through the work of scholars including William Julius Wilson, Robert Sampson, Elijah Anderson, and many others, locates the causes of street organization formation and street violence in the structural conditions of concentrated poverty, residential segregation, labor market exclusion, and institutional disinvestment. In this framework, street organizations are adaptive responses to conditions in which legitimate paths to the goods that all young people seek — belonging, safety, status, economic security — are systematically blocked. The same young men who join street organizations in a neighborhood of concentrated poverty would, in a neighborhood of stable employment, quality schools, and functioning institutions, be more likely to channel their social needs through legitimate organizations. This framework supports policy prescriptions focused on structural change: affordable housing, quality schools, workforce development, and the investment that makes legitimate futures genuinely available.
Interpretation C — The Community Knowledge and Harm Reduction Framework: A third framework, increasingly influential in both practice and scholarship, emphasizes that the most accurate and useful understanding of street organizations must be grounded in the direct knowledge of the communities in which they operate. This framework, developed through community-based research, oral history, and the practice wisdom of violence intervention workers, emphasizes the social complexity of street life — the mixture of genuine loyalty and genuine exploitation, genuine protection and genuine danger, genuine solidarity and genuine harm — that neither the criminal enterprise framework nor the structural framework fully captures. Policy grounded in this framework emphasizes pragmatic harm reduction: not primarily trying to eliminate street organizations through suppression or eliminate their structural conditions through social investment, but engaging with the organizations and their members where they are, working to reduce the harm they cause and the harm done to them, and expanding individual options without requiring idealized behavioral transformation as a precondition for support.
Interpretation D — The Historical Framework (This Textbook's Approach): The historical framework, which this textbook has developed, situates street organizations within the long history of Black community building and state suppression — understanding them as the latest iteration of a recurring pattern in which communities excluded from legitimate institutional support build alternative forms of collective organization, and in which those alternative organizations are then suppressed by the state when they become effective enough to challenge existing power arrangements. This framework does not minimize the harms that street organizations cause, but it insists that those harms cannot be understood apart from the structural history of exclusion, disinvestment, and institutional failure that produced the conditions in which street organizations became meaningful and necessary. It supports policy prescriptions that combine structural investment, genuine community power, and pragmatic harm reduction, and it insists that honest historical analysis is a prerequisite for effective policy design.

What is at stake: The interpretive framework one uses to understand street organizations determines what solutions seem available and appropriate. If organizations are primarily criminal enterprises, then suppression is the main tool. If they are primarily structural responses, then structural investment is the main tool. If they are primarily complex social formations requiring harm reduction, then engagement and pragmatic support are the main tools. And if they are historically rooted formations requiring both structural change and historical reckoning, then the full complexity of the historical analysis must inform every dimension of the policy response. The stakes of this interpretive debate are not academic. They are measured in human lives — the lives of young people who will be imprisoned or supported, displaced or housed, abandoned or invested in, depending on which framework prevails in the political decisions that shape their communities.

Student task: Using evidence from across this textbook, evaluate which of these four interpretive frameworks is best supported by the historical evidence. For each framework, identify at least two pieces of evidence from the textbook that support it and at least one limitation of the framework that the evidence reveals. Then assess which framework, or which combination of frameworks, you find most persuasive, and explain what policy approaches follow from your assessment.

Multiple Perspectives

The Legacy of Street Organizations: Multiple Perspectives

From the perspective of longtime community residents and elders: The legacy of street organizations in communities that have lived with them for decades is deeply ambivalent. Elders who have watched neighborhood children grow into street organization membership understand — from the inside — both the social logic that makes those organizations attractive to young men without other options and the devastating costs that the violence associated with them imposes on communities that have already paid enormous costs in poverty, displacement, and institutional abandonment. The grief of parents who have lost children to street violence is real and profound, and it does not diminish because the structural conditions that produced that violence are also real and profound. Community elders hold both truths simultaneously in a way that neither the criminal enterprise framework nor the structural framework alone fully accommodates.
From the perspective of current and former street organization members: People who have been members of street organizations tend to describe their experience in ways that emphasize belonging, loyalty, and mutual protection as much as violence and criminality. The decision to join a street organization was typically experienced not as a rational choice to pursue criminal profit but as a social and emotional response to specific conditions — the need for protection in a dangerous environment, the desire for belonging in a context of institutional abandonment, the appeal of a social world that offered recognition and status that legitimate institutions denied. Many former members describe their time in street organizations with a mixture of nostalgia for the solidarity they experienced and grief for the harm they both caused and suffered. Their voices are essential to any honest understanding of what street organizations are and what conditions produce them.
From the perspective of law enforcement and criminal justice institutions: Law enforcement agencies have understood street organizations primarily through the lens of crime and order — as organized criminal enterprises whose violence threatens public safety and whose activities must be suppressed through prosecution, incarceration, and disruption. This perspective has produced enormous resources for anti-gang enforcement and has been politically dominant in urban policy for decades. It is not without validity: the violence associated with street organizations has killed many people and caused immense suffering in the communities where it occurs. But this perspective has also produced the mass incarceration described in Chapters 10 and 11, with its enormous collateral costs, and has consistently failed to address the structural conditions that produce street organizations in the first place. Law enforcement perspectives on street organizations, while capturing one real dimension of the phenomenon, are systematically incomplete in ways that limit their usefulness as a guide to effective policy.
From the perspective of violence intervention workers: People who work professionally in violence prevention — outreach workers, violence interrupters, hospital-based intervention specialists, fellowship coaches — develop a distinctive perspective on street organizations that is grounded in direct, sustained engagement with the people most deeply involved in street life. This perspective tends to emphasize the fundamental humanity and social complexity of street organization members — their genuine desire for safety and belonging, their capacity for change when presented with genuine alternatives, and the social pressures and obligations that make exit from street life extremely difficult even for those who deeply want to leave. Violence intervention workers are skeptical of both purely punitive approaches, which they see as counterproductive based on direct experience, and purely structural approaches, which they see as necessary but insufficient for addressing the immediate dynamics of violence. Their perspective is grounded in practice knowledge that no purely academic or policy framework can fully capture.
From the perspective of urban scholars and historians: Academic researchers who study street organizations, urban poverty, and community violence bring analytical tools — longitudinal data, comparative analysis, historical contextualization, systematic evidence review — that are essential for moving beyond anecdote and ideology in the policy debate. The scholarly consensus that has emerged from several decades of rigorous research is broadly consistent with the structural framework this textbook has developed: that street organizations are products of identifiable structural conditions that can be changed, that punitive approaches have consistently failed to address those conditions, and that investment in housing, education, economic opportunity, and community-based violence intervention is more consistent with the evidence of what works. Scholars bring important knowledge to the policy debate, but they also bring the limitations of their own position — typically observing communities from the outside, however sympathetically, in ways that can miss dimensions of lived experience that only direct community knowledge can provide.
Constitutional Connection

Constitutional Rights, Community Safety, and the Future of Urban Policy

The history traced in this textbook raises profound questions about the relationship between constitutional rights and the conditions of life in American cities. The Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection of the laws — the constitutional foundation of civil rights law — has been interpreted by courts as prohibiting explicit racial discrimination by government actors, but it has not been interpreted as requiring the structural remedies that would produce genuinely equal conditions of life for Americans of different races. This gap between formal legal equality and substantive equality of conditions is one of the central constitutional tensions in American life, and it is nowhere more visible than in the concentrated poverty and institutional disinvestment of the Black urban communities this textbook has studied.

Several constitutional issues are directly relevant to the future of urban communities. First, the Fourth Amendment's prohibition of unreasonable searches and seizures has been interpreted in ways that give law enforcement significant latitude to conduct stops, searches, and surveillance in high-crime neighborhoods — latitude that has been exercised primarily in Black and Latino communities and that has contributed to the erosion of the trust between communities and police that effective public safety requires. The Supreme Court's 1968 Terry v. Ohio decision authorizing stops based on "reasonable suspicion" rather than probable cause created the constitutional framework for the stop-and-frisk practices that were deployed most aggressively in New York City and that generated both significant community opposition and growing evidence of limited effectiveness.

Second, the Eighth Amendment's prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment has been interpreted to permit sentences of extraordinary length — life sentences for nonviolent drug offenses under three-strikes laws, life-without-parole sentences for crimes committed by juveniles — that are inconsistent with the evolving standards of decency recognized in other constitutional democracies and increasingly challenged by advocates of criminal justice reform. The Supreme Court's Eighth Amendment jurisprudence has moved in recent years toward greater scrutiny of the harshest sentences, particularly for juvenile offenders, but the broader challenge of reducing the mass incarceration described in this textbook requires legislative action rather than constitutional litigation.

Third, the equal protection clause has been invoked in challenges to racially discriminatory policing practices, discriminatory enforcement of drug laws, and the racial inequities in school funding that have left Black urban schools chronically underfunded relative to white suburban schools. These challenges have had mixed results in the courts, reflecting the difficulty of proving discriminatory intent as opposed to discriminatory effect under current equal protection doctrine. Advocates for structural remedies have increasingly argued for shifting from an intent-based framework, which requires proving that specific actors acted with racially discriminatory purpose, to an effects-based framework, which would allow constitutional challenges to policies that produce racially disparate outcomes regardless of intent. This shift, if adopted, would significantly expand the constitutional basis for challenging the structural policies this textbook has described.

The most far-reaching constitutional questions raised by this history involve the relationship between democratic citizenship and the conditions of life. A genuine interpretation of equal citizenship — the principle that all Americans have equal standing as members of the political community — would seem to require that all Americans have access to the basic conditions of a decent life: safe housing, quality education, economic opportunity, and communities that sustain rather than endanger health and human development. The constitutional tradition has not yet embraced this interpretation of equal citizenship, but the history of Black urban communities makes its absence acutely visible and the case for it compelling. The future of urban policy will be shaped in part by whether the constitutional framework expands to require the structural conditions of genuine citizenship, or whether it remains limited to prohibiting explicit formal discrimination while tolerating the structural inequality that produces it.

Everyday Life and Social History

Living in the Contemporary City: Everyday Life in Communities This Textbook Has Studied

For the people who live in the neighborhoods this textbook has examined — the South Side of Chicago, South Central Los Angeles, North Philadelphia, Southeast Washington, D.C., East Oakland — daily life at the beginning of the twenty-first century combines the continuing effects of the structural conditions this textbook has traced with the genuine achievements of community resilience and the new possibilities created by violence prevention programs, community development initiatives, and the broader political reckoning with racial inequality that intensified in the 2010s and 2020s.

For families in these neighborhoods, the texture of daily life is shaped by persistent material constraints that the history of disinvestment has produced. Housing is expensive relative to income, and the threat of displacement — through rent increases, gentrification, or eviction — is a constant source of anxiety. Schools, despite decades of reform efforts, remain underfunded relative to suburban counterparts, and the quality of instruction varies dramatically within school districts in ways that persistently disadvantage the children of the poorest families. Employment options are limited, and the employment discrimination that people with criminal records face — which affects a very large share of the adult male population in high-incarceration communities — compounds the already significant barriers to stable employment for people without college credentials.

The presence of street violence shapes daily life in ways that are difficult to convey to those who have not experienced them. The decisions made by families in high-violence neighborhoods — which route to take to school, which parks are safe to use, whether to allow children to play outside, how to respond when a family member is threatened — reflect a constant background calculation of risk that is both exhausting and normalizing. Children who grow up navigating these calculations develop coping strategies, perceptual acuity, and social knowledge that are genuine adaptations to a genuine environment — but they also absorb a relationship to danger and institutional authority that shapes their development in ways that are costly over the long term.

Alongside these continuing burdens, the same neighborhoods contain extraordinary cultural richness, social warmth, and community vitality. The churches that have sustained Black community life for generations remain centers of social life, cultural expression, and collective action. Community organizations — violence prevention programs, after-school programs, community development corporations, mutual aid networks — are genuinely present in ways they were not a generation ago, providing resources and connections that make real differences in individual lives. The cultural production emerging from these communities — the music, the visual art, the literature, the food cultures — continues to be some of the most vital and innovative in American life. The complexity of contemporary urban community life resists reduction to either pure suffering or pure resilience: it is both, simultaneously, in the way that all human communities contain both hardship and vitality, both what has been destroyed and what has been built.

The most hopeful dimension of life in these communities is the presence of young people who are navigating the structural conditions this textbook has described with intelligence, creativity, and genuine aspiration — young people who know their history, who understand the structural forces that have shaped their circumstances, and who are building lives and organizations that insist on a different future. The young community organizers, violence interrupters, artists, educators, and entrepreneurs working in the communities this textbook has studied are the latest iteration of the long tradition of Black community building that began, in this textbook, with the freedpeople of the Reconstruction South. Their work is not separate from the history this textbook has traced. It is the continuation of it.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has brought together the threads of a history traced across thirteen preceding chapters to address the final questions of this textbook: How should we interpret what we have learned? What has Black urban community building produced in the face of the structural conditions this history has described? What has the evidence shown about which policy interventions work and which have failed? And what does the long view of urban history suggest about the conditions under which a more just urban future might be built?

The chapter began with the question of historical interpretation, examining the competing frameworks — the pathology model, the structural sociology framework, the community knowledge model, and the historical framework — that have shaped scholarly and policy debates about Black street organizations and urban violence. The historical evidence this textbook has presented is most consistent with the structural and historical frameworks: street organizations are products of identifiable structural conditions, not cultural pathology, and those conditions can be changed by different policies and investments. Understanding this does not require minimizing the real harms that street organizations have caused, but it does require rejecting interpretive frameworks that locate the causes of urban poverty and violence inside communities rather than in the structural conditions that have shaped those communities.

The chapter then examined community resilience — the extraordinary organizational and cultural life that Black urban communities have built and maintained in the face of the structural conditions this textbook has described. From the mutual aid societies and fraternal organizations of the Reconstruction South through the churches, civic organizations, and community development institutions of the twentieth century to the violence prevention programs, community land trusts, and mutual aid networks of the twenty-first, Black communities have consistently built institutional responses to the conditions of structural exclusion that have shaped their lives. These responses are not substitutes for structural change — no amount of community resilience can fully compensate for the absence of adequate schools, affordable housing, and economic opportunity — but they represent genuine achievements of organizational creativity and collective determination that deserve recognition and support.

The examination of violence prevention programs revealed a growing evidence base for community-based, non-law-enforcement approaches to urban violence. The Cure Violence model, the Advance Peace fellowship, group violence intervention, and hospital-based violence intervention programs have each demonstrated measurable reductions in violence in the communities where they operate — reductions that purely punitive approaches have consistently failed to achieve. These programs are grounded in the same community knowledge and structural analysis that this textbook has developed, and their effectiveness is consistent with the broader theoretical argument that violence is best addressed by addressing the conditions that produce it rather than by punishing the individuals who respond to those conditions with violence.

The urban policy lessons section examined the patterns of policy failure and policy effectiveness that the history of this textbook reveals. The policies that have consistently worsened conditions in Black urban communities — urban renewal displacement, concentrated high-rise public housing, mandatory minimum sentencing, mass incarceration, zero-tolerance policing — have each been adopted despite abundant evidence of their harmful effects, reflecting the political dynamics that consistently privilege more powerful interests over those of the communities most directly affected. The policies that have consistently improved conditions — stable affordable housing, quality schools, economic opportunity, and community-based violence intervention — have been chronically underfunded and politically marginalized, again reflecting those political dynamics. The lesson is not that good policy is impossible but that it requires the political conditions that make it possible — specifically, the political power of affected communities to demand investment and accountability rather than suppression and neglect.

The section on the future of American cities argued that the concentrated poverty, residential segregation, and institutional disinvestment that this textbook has traced are not immutable features of the urban landscape. They were produced by specific policies and can be changed by different policies. The housing affordability crisis, the debate over policing and public safety, the reparations discussion, and the expanding community violence intervention field all represent sites where the future is being actively contested. The future that emerges will depend on the political choices made in the present — choices about who has power, whose interests are treated as priorities, and whether the communities that have borne the costs of a century of exclusionary policy will finally be treated as genuine priorities deserving of genuine investment.

This final chapter also addressed the question of collective memory — how communities, scholars, policymakers, and the broader public remember the history this textbook has traced, and why that memory matters for the future. Collective memory is never neutral: it is shaped by power, and the dominant collective memory of Black street organizations has been shaped by frameworks — crime news, Hollywood film, political rhetoric — that serve the interests of the punitive policy regime this textbook has critiqued. Counter-memories — grounded in community knowledge, historical analysis, and the voices of the people most directly affected — are essential resources for the political work of building a different future. This textbook is intended as a contribution to that counter-memory: an attempt to tell a more complete and more honest story about what has happened in American cities and why, as a foundation for honest engagement with what needs to happen next.

The history that this textbook has traced does not end in despair. It ends in the recognition that the conditions of Black urban life — the concentrated poverty, the street violence, the mass incarceration, the displacement — are not natural or inevitable. They are historical products of specific, identifiable policies, and they can be changed. The communities that have survived and built and organized and created through more than a century of structural exclusion possess knowledge, organizational capacity, cultural resources, and political determination that represent genuine assets for the work of building more just cities. What is required, beyond those community resources, is the political will to invest genuinely, to reform honestly, and to take seriously the historical record that this textbook has tried to make accessible to a new generation of students. That is the work to which this history calls those who have engaged with it.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Advance Peace
A fellowship-based violence prevention model developed in Richmond, California, that provides intensive support, mentorship, life coaching, and stipends to the highest-risk individuals in a community — those most likely to be involved in violent incidents. Associated with dramatic reductions in gun homicides in communities where it operates.
Collective efficacy
Sociologist Robert Sampson's term for the shared expectation among neighborhood residents that they can work together to maintain social order and achieve desired outcomes — including intervening to prevent violence. One of the strongest predictors of neighborhood violence rates, and one of the most important outcomes disrupted by policies that destabilize residential communities.
Collective memory
The shared ways in which a group — a community, city, or nation — remembers and interprets its past. Collective memory is always selective and contested, shaped by power dynamics that determine which events are remembered and how. The dominant collective memory of Black street organizations has been shaped by frameworks that emphasize criminality while minimizing structural context.
Community development corporation (CDC)
A nonprofit organization, typically neighborhood-based, that pursues economic development, affordable housing construction and rehabilitation, and community revitalization in low-income urban communities. CDCs emerged from the War on Poverty community action programs and have grown into a significant sector of community-controlled economic development.
Community investment
The directed flow of resources — financial capital, institutional support, public services, physical infrastructure, and social support — into neighborhoods and communities that have experienced long-term disinvestment. Distinguished from charity by its acknowledgment that historical harm creates contemporary obligation and by its emphasis on community control over the direction and benefits of investment.
Community land trust
A nonprofit organization that acquires and holds land on behalf of a community, developing and maintaining affordable housing and other community facilities on that land. Community land trusts remove land from the speculative market, preserving long-term affordability and providing a mechanism for community control over the benefits of neighborhood investment.
Community resilience
The capacity of a community to maintain collective life, sustain institutions, build social bonds, and resist or recover from disruption. In the context of Black urban communities, resilience refers to the organizational and cultural resources through which communities have sustained collective life under conditions of structural exclusion and institutional abandonment.
Community violence intervention (CVI)
A broad category of community-based, non-law-enforcement programs designed to reduce street violence through outreach, conflict mediation, individual support, and community norm change. CVI programs include hospital-based violence intervention, street outreach and interruption, group violence intervention, and fellowship-based programs. Federal investment in CVI programs expanded significantly in the early 2020s.
Credible messenger
An individual whose direct personal experience of the conditions being addressed — particularly direct experience of street life and incarceration — provides authentic authority in reaching and influencing people who would not respond to those without that experience. Credible messengers are the foundation of effective community violence intervention programs, particularly in the Cure Violence model.
Criminal enterprise framework
An interpretive framework that understands street organizations primarily as organized criminal enterprises engaged in illegal markets and violence for profit. The dominant framework in law enforcement and much popular discourse; supports punitive policy responses emphasizing prosecution, incarceration, and organizational disruption.
Cure Violence
A Chicago-based violence prevention organization (originally called CeaseFire) founded by epidemiologist Gary Slutkin that applies a public health model to street violence, using trained community outreach workers and violence interrupters to interrupt violent conflicts before they turn lethal and to change community norms around violence. Multiple independent evaluations have found significant reductions in shootings in program areas.
Focused deterrence
A violence reduction strategy, also called group violence intervention, that combines direct communication with the highest-risk groups in a community — typically through organized call-ins — with a clear message that violence will trigger coordinated law enforcement action against the entire group, while simultaneously offering direct support to individuals who choose to stop participating in violence. Associated with significant violence reductions in Boston, Oakland, and other cities where it has been well implemented.
Harm reduction
A public health approach to problems like substance use and violence that emphasizes pragmatic reduction of immediate harms over idealized behavioral transformation as a precondition for support. Applied to violence prevention, harm reduction means engaging with people in the conditions of their actual lives, working to reduce the harms they cause and suffer, and expanding their options without requiring them to first achieve idealized behavioral change.
Historical framework
An interpretive approach that situates street organizations within the long history of Black community building and state suppression, understanding them as products of specific structural conditions produced by identifiable historical policies. The interpretive framework developed in this textbook, which supports policy responses combining structural investment, community power, and historical reckoning.
Hospital-based violence intervention program (HVIP)
A program that assigns trained intervention specialists to make contact with victims of violence in the emergency room and during hospitalization, building relationships and connecting patients to resources during the critical period when the decision about retaliation is being made. Evaluations have found significant reductions in re-injury and re-arrest among participants.
Neighborhood effects
The independent effect of neighborhood conditions — beyond individual and family characteristics — on outcomes like educational achievement, employment, health, and violence. Research consistently finds that concentrated poverty has negative effects on residents' outcomes even controlling for individual poverty, justifying policies that reduce residential segregation and concentrated poverty rather than simply addressing individual needs.
Pathology model
An interpretive framework that attributes urban poverty and street violence primarily to deficient culture, broken families, and behavioral dysfunction within affected communities. The pathology model has been politically influential but is inconsistent with the evidence, which shows that the structural conditions of residential segregation, labor market exclusion, and institutional disinvestment are the primary determinants of concentrated poverty and street violence.
Public health model of violence
An approach that treats violence as a communicable condition spreading through social networks in ways analogous to infectious disease, and that identifies prevention and interruption of transmission — rather than punishment after the fact — as the primary intervention strategy. The theoretical foundation of the Cure Violence program and much of the community violence intervention field.
Reparations
Monetary compensation or other remedies provided to individuals or communities for specific, identifiable historical harms. In the context of American racial history, reparations discussions typically address the wealth gap produced by slavery, redlining, exclusion from GI Bill benefits, and other documented, policy-produced harms to Black Americans. A few American cities and institutions have implemented limited local reparations programs.
Reentry
The process by which formerly incarcerated individuals transition back to community life after release from prison or jail. Reentry is typically characterized by significant barriers — to housing, employment, public benefits, and civic participation — created by criminal records and the stigma of incarceration. The reentry movement advocates for removing or reducing these barriers as a matter of both justice and public safety.
Restorative justice
An approach to addressing harm that emphasizes repairing relationships between offenders, victims, and community rather than punishing offenders through incarceration. Restorative justice practices include victim-offender mediation, community conferencing, and circles that bring together affected parties to address the harm caused and determine how to repair it. Applied in schools, communities, and criminal courts as an alternative or complement to traditional punitive approaches.
Returning citizen
A person who has been released from prison or jail and is returning to community life. The term is preferred by many formerly incarcerated people and their advocates over terms like "ex-offender" or "ex-convict" because it emphasizes the person's ongoing membership in the community and their citizenship rights rather than their criminal record.
Social capital
Sociologist Robert Putnam's term for the networks of relationships, norms of reciprocity, and trust that enable people to work together for shared goals. Social capital is a key resource for community resilience and collective efficacy, and it is depleted by the residential displacement, mass incarceration, and institutional disinvestment that this textbook has traced.
Structural explanation
An interpretive framework that locates the causes of social phenomena — poverty, violence, school failure, street organization membership — in the structural conditions of society rather than in individual or cultural characteristics. The structural framework supported by the evidence of this textbook: the structural conditions of residential segregation, labor market exclusion, and institutional disinvestment are the primary determinants of the phenomena this textbook has examined.
Structural racism
The cumulative and compounding effects of policies, institutional practices, cultural norms, and historical decisions that systematically disadvantage people of color. Unlike individual racism, structural racism operates through the normal functioning of institutions without requiring the conscious racist intent of any individual actor. The redlining, urban renewal, and mass incarceration policies this textbook has traced are all examples of structural racism in operation.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

William Julius Wilson (1935–present)
Harvard sociologist whose structural analysis of urban poverty, particularly in The Truly Disadvantaged (1987), challenged cultural explanations and established the scholarly foundation for understanding concentrated urban poverty as a product of economic restructuring and residential segregation rather than cultural failure. His work influenced a generation of urban researchers and provided an intellectual framework for structural approaches to urban policy.
Gary Slutkin (1948–present)
Epidemiologist and founder of Cure Violence, who applied a public health infectious disease framework to the problem of street violence, developing the violence interruption model that has been adopted in dozens of American cities and internationally. Slutkin's work represents a paradigm shift in how urban violence is conceptualized and addressed.
DeVon Boggan (1971–present)
Founder of Advance Peace, the fellowship-based violence prevention model developed in Richmond, California, that provides intensive support and expanded opportunities to the highest-risk individuals in a community. Richmond's dramatic homicide reduction during the period of Advance Peace's operation has made Boggan one of the most influential practitioners in the community violence intervention field.
Michelle Alexander (1967–present)
Legal scholar and author of The New Jim Crow (2010), which argued that mass incarceration functions as a system of racialized social control analogous to Jim Crow and contributed enormously to public understanding of and political mobilization around criminal justice reform. Alexander's work brought the scholarly analysis of mass incarceration to a broad popular audience.
Ta-Nehisi Coates (1975–present)
Journalist and author whose "The Case for Reparations" essay in The Atlantic (2014) provided detailed historical documentation of the specific policies — particularly redlining in Chicago — that produced the racial wealth gap and made the argument for structural remedies accessible to a broad audience. His subsequent work continued to connect historical analysis to contemporary policy and political questions.
Mariame Kaba (1972–present)
Activist, educator, and prison abolitionist whose work provides an intellectual framework for understanding community safety as the product of social investment rather than punishment, and whose grassroots organizing has contributed significantly to the political movement for criminal justice reform and community investment.
Cure Violence (Chicago, founded 1995)
The organization that developed and disseminated the public health model of violence interruption, deploying trained community outreach workers and violence interrupters to interrupt violent conflicts in real time and to change community norms around violence. Multiple independent evaluations confirm the model's effectiveness; it has been adopted in dozens of American cities and in international contexts.
Advance Peace (Richmond, CA, founded 2016)
The organization that developed the fellowship-based model of intensive support for the highest-risk individuals in a community, associated with dramatic reductions in gun homicides in Richmond and subsequently in other California cities. The Advance Peace model is an influential example of the movement toward support-based rather than punishment-based approaches to urban violence.
Black Lives Matter (founded 2013)
A decentralized political movement founded in response to police killings of Black people that has been enormously influential in shifting public discourse about policing, racial inequality, and community safety. Black Lives Matter has contributed to the political conditions in which community-based approaches to public safety have gained greater visibility and resources.
Richmond, California
An industrial city in the San Francisco Bay Area that became in the 2000s and 2010s a nationally significant site for innovative community-based violence prevention, demonstrating through a combination of community violence intervention, economic investment, and police reform that dramatic reductions in urban violence are achievable even in communities with long histories of concentrated poverty and high murder rates.
Community Violence Intervention (CVI) programs
The broad field of community-based, non-law-enforcement programs for reducing street violence that has grown significantly since the 1990s and received unprecedented federal investment in the early 2020s. CVI programs represent a genuine policy shift toward community-centered approaches to public safety grounded in the structural analysis this textbook has developed.
Evanston, Illinois
The city that in 2021 became the first American municipality to implement a reparations program, providing housing assistance to Black residents who had been harmed by the city's historical redlining practices. Evanston's program is limited in scope but represents a significant precedent for the acknowledgment that historical harm creates contemporary obligation.

Final Discussion: What Can Urban History Teach Us About the Future?

This textbook began with a question embedded in its title: From Migration to Fragmentation. The journey from the Great Migration — millions of Black Americans leaving the Jim Crow South for the industrial cities of the North and West, carrying with them the organizational traditions, cultural knowledge, and determination built through generations of survival under conditions designed to crush them — to the fragmentation of the contemporary urban landscape, where the large institutions and territorial organizations of the mid-twentieth century have given way to smaller, more atomized crews operating in a world of social media reputation management, gentrification-driven displacement, and the continuing aftermath of mass incarceration, is a journey of more than a century. That journey has produced enormous suffering and enormous creativity, devastating loss and remarkable survival. It does not have a clear moral, but it has many important lessons.

Chapter Reflection

This chapter — and this textbook — ends where it began, with the insistence that the history of Black street organizations in American cities cannot be understood apart from the longer history of Black urban life, and that Black urban life cannot be understood apart from the long history of structural exclusion, policy failure, and community building that this textbook has traced from the Reconstruction South to the contemporary city. The journey from emancipation to the present has been a journey through more than a century of extraordinary creativity, devastating loss, remarkable resilience, and persistent structural inequality — a journey whose lessons are urgently relevant to the policy debates of the present moment.

The most important lesson may be the simplest: the conditions of Black urban life — the concentrated poverty, the street violence, the mass incarceration, the displacement — are not natural. They are historical. They were produced by specific, identifiable policies and decisions, most of them made by people who are now dead, whose effects persist in the present through the mechanisms of inherited wealth, institutional structure, physical infrastructure, and accumulated disadvantage. Because those conditions are historical rather than natural, they are subject to historical change — they can be different if different choices are made. The communities that have lived with those conditions know this better than anyone, having built and rebuilt their collective life through more than a century of disruption and suppression and found, again and again, ways to sustain dignity, solidarity, and hope in conditions designed to extinguish all three.

The violence prevention programs, community development institutions, and political movements examined in this chapter represent the latest iteration of that long tradition of community building — the latest attempt to address the conditions of structural exclusion through the combination of community knowledge, organizational capacity, and political will that has been the characteristic response of Black urban communities to adversity throughout the history this textbook has traced. Whether those efforts will be sufficient to produce lasting structural change depends not only on their quality and persistence but on the political conditions that determine what resources and support they receive and what institutional obstacles they face. Creating those political conditions — building the power necessary to demand genuine investment, genuine reform, and genuine accountability — is the work to which the history this textbook has traced calls those who have engaged with it.

Urban history, at its best, does not simply record what happened. It makes visible the human choices that have shaped the world we inhabit and that continue to shape the world we are building. The concentrated poverty of American inner cities, the violence of drug markets and territorial conflict, the mass incarceration of Black and brown men, the displacement of longtime residents by gentrification — none of these are natural disasters. They are the cumulative results of human choices, made over generations, by people with power over people without it. Understanding those choices — who made them, why, and with what effects — is the prerequisite for making different choices in the future. That is the reason urban history must be studied. That is the reason this textbook was written. And that is the reason its readers, armed with the analytical tools of historical inquiry and the evidence of what has and has not worked, have everything they need to be part of building something better.

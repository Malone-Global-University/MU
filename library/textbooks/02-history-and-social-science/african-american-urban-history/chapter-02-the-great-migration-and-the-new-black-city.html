The Great Migration and the New Black City

From Migration to Fragmentation: The Urban History of Black Street Organizations

Chapter Opening

"I am in the darkness of the South and I am trying my best to get out of it." — Letter to the Chicago Defender, 1917, from an unnamed Black Southerner seeking information about migration north

Sometime in the early spring of 1916, a man named Robert Hicks — a sharecropper from Hattiesburg, Mississippi — read a copy of the Chicago Defender that had been passed to him by a neighbor who had received it from a cousin already living in Chicago. The Defender was the most widely circulated Black newspaper in the country, its pages full of news about wages, opportunity, and the relative freedom of Black life in the urban North. It ran advertisements for industrial jobs. It published letters from readers who had successfully made the journey and described their lives in the city in terms that seemed almost impossible to believe from the vantage point of a Mississippi sharecropper: regular wages, schools for children, freedom from the daily humiliations of Jim Crow, and, above all, escape from the ever-present threat of racial violence that governed every dimension of life in the Deep South. Robert Hicks folded the newspaper, spoke with his wife, and began making plans. Within a year, he and his family were living in a rooming house on the South Side of Chicago, he was employed at a meatpacking plant on the city's industrial corridor, and his oldest son was enrolled in a Chicago public school for the first time in his life.

Robert Hicks was not a historical figure recorded in official documents or celebrated in formal histories. His story is composite and representative rather than literally documented — drawn from the patterns of tens of thousands of actual letters, oral histories, and migration records that historians have painstakingly recovered from archives across the country. But his experience captures something essential about the Great Migration: that it was not a mass movement organized from above, not a government program or a coordinated campaign, but rather an enormous accumulation of individual and family decisions made by people who had analyzed their own circumstances clearly and determined that the risks of moving were less than the risks of staying. The Great Migration was, in this sense, one of the largest exercises of collective agency in American history — a decision made by hundreds of thousands of separate individuals, each drawing on their own reading of their situation, who together transformed the demographic geography of the United States.

Between approximately 1910 and 1970, more than six million Black Americans left the South and moved to cities in the North, Midwest, and West. This movement — called the Great Migration by historians, though it was in fact two distinct waves separated by the Depression era — was one of the most consequential demographic transformations in American history. It created the large Black urban communities that would become the seedbeds of twentieth-century African American culture, politics, and community life. It fueled the music that became the soundtrack of modern America — jazz, blues, gospel, rhythm and blues, soul, and eventually hip-hop. It produced the political constituencies that would reshape Democratic Party politics and eventually elect Black mayors, representatives, senators, and a president. And it created the concentrated, segregated, underserved urban neighborhoods whose history — of extraordinary cultural vitality coexisting with persistent poverty, structural exclusion, and eventually devastating deindustrialization and the crack cocaine crisis — is the central subject of this textbook.

Understanding the Great Migration requires understanding what Black Southerners were moving away from as clearly as what they were moving toward. The push factors — the conditions in the South that made leaving an attractive risk — were the full apparatus of Jim Crow: the violence, the disenfranchisement, the economic exploitation, the daily humiliations, the complete absence of legal protection, and the ever-present threat of lynching that hung over Black communities throughout the South. These were not simply abstract political conditions. They were the daily texture of life, experienced in the body, in the household, in the fields, and in every interaction with white authority. The man who left Mississippi in 1917 was not making a vague gesture toward a better life. He was making a precise calculation that the specific conditions of his specific situation — the debt that never cleared, the landlord who cheated at the settle, the sheriff who would not protect him, the Klan rider who came at night — were conditions that might kill him or his children if he stayed.

The pull factors were equally concrete. Northern cities were expanding rapidly in the first decades of the twentieth century, driven by industrial production that was accelerating toward the enormous demand that the First World War would create. The steel mills of Pittsburgh and Gary, the meatpacking plants of Chicago, the automobile factories of Detroit, the shipyards of Baltimore and Philadelphia — all of them were hungry for labor, and as European immigration was first disrupted by the war and then sharply curtailed by the Immigration Acts of 1921 and 1924, their managers began, for the first time and with varying degrees of enthusiasm, to look southward for Black workers to fill positions that had previously been reserved for white immigrant labor. The wage differential between the South and the North was dramatic: a Black agricultural worker in Mississippi might earn fifty cents a day, while a Black industrial worker in Chicago might earn two to three dollars per day for the same hours. The material incentive for migration was, in the bluntest economic terms, enormous.

But the Great Migration was not reducible to economics. It was also a flight from terror and a search for dignity. The letters that Black migrants wrote to the Chicago Defender and to the Defender's sister publications in other cities, the letters they sent to friends and relatives who had already migrated, and the oral histories collected from aging migrants decades later all testify to the fact that the search for human dignity — the desire to live as full citizens rather than as subjects — was as powerful a motivating force as any wage calculation. The man who could vote in Chicago, whose children could attend a school with real books and trained teachers, who could walk down a sidewalk without stepping aside for a white man, who could address a white man by his last name rather than by "sir," who could not be lynched for looking at a white woman — that man had gained something that no wage differential can fully capture. He had gained, in some real and meaningful sense, a portion of the human recognition that slavery and Jim Crow had denied.

What migrants found in northern cities was better than what they had left — and far worse than what they had hoped for. The North was not free of racism. It was racist in different ways: less overtly violent, less legally codified, but powerfully operative through housing markets, labor market discrimination, school segregation, and police practices that bore their own particular relationship to Black bodies and Black communities. The neighborhoods to which Black migrants moved were confined by a combination of real estate covenants, discriminatory lending, and organized white hostility that was remarkably effective at containing Black settlement within specific and overcrowded geographic areas. The wages, while higher than the South, were typically lower than those earned by white workers in comparable positions, and Black workers were routinely excluded from the better-paying skilled trades and from union membership in the unions that controlled access to industrial work. The schools that Black children attended were, in most northern cities, underfunded and overcrowded compared to schools in white neighborhoods, despite the absence of legally mandated segregation. The promise of the North was real, but it was also partial and contested in ways that would generate grievances, rebellions, and the eventual conditions of the urban crisis.

This chapter traces the history of the Great Migration from its origins in the conditions of the Jim Crow South through the development of the major Black urban communities that the migration produced. It follows Black migrants onto the trains and buses that carried them northward, into the boarding houses and apartment buildings where they first settled, into the factories and stockyards where they worked, and into the churches, clubs, newspapers, and neighborhood associations through which they rebuilt community life in a new context. It traces the formation of iconic Black urban communities — Bronzeville on Chicago's South Side, Harlem in New York, Paradise Valley in Detroit, and Central Avenue in Los Angeles — as places where extraordinary creativity and culture coexisted with systematic exclusion and structural deprivation. And it follows the first generation of northern-born Black Americans into the schools, streets, and neighborhoods that their parents had made — into a world that was neither the South their parents had fled nor the free North that the Chicago Defender had promised, but something new, something that would have to be understood and navigated on its own terms.

The connection between the Great Migration and the later history of Black street organizations is not incidental but foundational. The neighborhoods that the migration created — confined, overcrowded, institutionally underserved but culturally rich — were the specific geographic and social environments in which the youth organizations that eventually became street organizations first emerged. The experience of being young, Black, male, and urban in mid-twentieth-century America — navigating the streets of neighborhoods that were simultaneously claimed by multiple generations, multiple institutions, and multiple forms of authority — was shaped at every turn by the history the Great Migration had produced. Understanding that history is an essential precondition for understanding what came after.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Identify the major push factors that drove Black Southerners to leave the South during the Great Migration, including racial violence, economic exploitation, agricultural crisis, and political disenfranchisement.
  2. Explain the major pull factors that drew Black migrants to northern and western cities, including industrial employment opportunities, higher wages, relative political freedom, and the presence of established Black communities.
  3. Trace the major geographic patterns of the Great Migration, identifying principal routes of movement, states of origin, and primary destination cities.
  4. Describe the role of migration networks — family chains, churches, and Black newspapers — in organizing and sustaining the movement of people from the South to northern cities.
  5. Analyze the conditions that Black migrants encountered in northern cities, including labor market discrimination, housing segregation through restrictive covenants and redlining, and police practices.
  6. Compare the experiences of Black migrants in at least two major destination cities — Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, New York, or Los Angeles — noting both common patterns and significant differences.
  7. Describe the major institutions — churches, newspapers, civic organizations, fraternal orders, and mutual aid societies — that Black migrants built or adapted in northern and western cities.
  8. Evaluate the relationship between the Great Migration and the development of African American cultural forms, including the Chicago blues, bebop jazz, gospel music, and the Harlem Renaissance.
  9. Explain the causes and consequences of major episodes of racial violence in northern cities during the migration era, including the East St. Louis Massacre (1917), Chicago Race Riot (1919), and Tulsa Race Massacre (1921).
  10. Assess the significance of the Great Migration for the development of African American political power in northern cities, including the growth of Black electoral participation and the emergence of Black political leadership.
  11. Connect the geographic patterns of Black settlement in northern cities — concentrated in specific neighborhoods by discriminatory housing practices — to the later development of Black youth culture, street organizations, and urban poverty.
  12. Interpret primary sources from the migration era, including migration letters, newspaper accounts, oral histories, and demographic data, as historical evidence with specific perspectives and limits.

Guiding Historical Questions

Questions for Historical Inquiry

  1. What were the most important factors driving Black Southerners to leave the South during the Great Migration? Were economic calculations or the desire for freedom and dignity more powerful motivating forces?
  2. How did migration networks — families, churches, newspapers — shape who migrated, where they went, and what they found when they arrived? In what ways did these networks both enable and constrain individual choices?
  3. What did Black migrants find when they arrived in northern and western cities? In what ways was the North better than the South, and in what ways did racial discrimination persist in different forms?
  4. How did Black migrants build community institutions in new cities? What did they bring with them from the South, and what new institutions did the northern urban environment require?
  5. How did the geographic confinement of Black settlement in northern cities — produced by restrictive covenants, discriminatory lending, and organized white hostility — shape the character of Black urban communities?
  6. What is the relationship between the Great Migration and the flourishing of African American cultural production — music, literature, visual art — in the first half of the twentieth century?
  7. How did northern white communities respond to Black migration, and why did racial violence erupt in so many northern cities during the migration era?
  8. In what ways did the children of migrants experience northern cities differently from their parents? What did growing up in a Black urban neighborhood in the 1930s, 1940s, and 1950s look like?
  9. What is the relationship between the Great Migration and the later emergence of Black street organizations in Chicago, Los Angeles, and other major cities?

Historical Context

The Great Migration did not begin suddenly or with a single catalyzing event. It was the culmination of decades of accumulated pressure — economic, political, social, and psychological — that had been building in Black communities throughout the South since the end of Reconstruction. The long arc from emancipation through Jim Crow to mass migration is a story of systematic constriction: of possibilities opened by emancipation and then closed by the apparatus of racial terror and legal subordination that constituted the Jim Crow order, until the constriction became intolerable and the possibility of moving northward presented itself as the most viable option available.

To understand the urgency that drove so many people to leave, students must keep in mind the full weight of what daily life under Jim Crow entailed. This was not merely a set of inconvenient legal restrictions. It was an entire social order organized around the subordination of Black people to white authority, enforced by the constant threat of catastrophic violence. Lynching — the extrajudicial murder of Black people by white mobs, typically with the complicity or direct participation of law enforcement — was not a rare event. Between 1877 and 1950, more than 4,000 documented lynchings of Black people took place in the American South. Most occurred in the 1890s and 1900s, but they continued through the first decades of the twentieth century with sufficient frequency to maintain a culture of terror. A Black man in Mississippi or Alabama or Georgia in 1910 or 1915 knew men who had been lynched, knew families from which men had been taken. The knowledge was not abstract. It lived in the body, in the hypervigilance that characterized Black social behavior in white presence, in the careful calibration of expression and demeanor that was required for survival. The decision to migrate was, among other things, a decision to escape this particular form of existential exposure.

The economic context of the migration is equally important and somewhat more complex than a simple wage-gap story. By the 1910s, the agricultural economy of the South was under severe stress. The boll weevil, a cotton-destroying insect that spread from Mexico across the cotton-growing South between 1915 and 1922, devastated cotton crops in county after county, destroying the already precarious economic basis of sharecropper households. Floods, particularly along the Mississippi River, added to agricultural disruption. And the global cotton market was increasingly competitive, as Egyptian and Indian cotton producers undercut American prices. Sharecroppers who had already been struggling under a debt system designed to keep them permanently behind now faced a complete collapse of the crop income on which their arrangements depended. For many, the combination of the boll weevil and the flood of 1915 was the final push that made migration not a distant aspiration but an immediate practical necessity.

At the same moment that these push factors were intensifying, the pull of the industrial North was growing more powerful than it had ever been. The outbreak of the First World War in Europe in 1914 had dramatic effects on the American labor market. European immigration, which had been providing the northern industrial economy with approximately one million new workers per year, dropped precipitously as transatlantic travel became dangerous and then, for German and Austro-Hungarian nationals, impossible. At the same time, the war created an enormous demand for industrial production — for steel, for munitions, for ships, for vehicles, for cloth — that taxed the capacity of northern factories to the limit. Labor shortages became acute in industries that had never previously needed to look south for workers. For the first time on a significant scale, northern employers began recruiting Black workers from the South, sending labor agents to southern cities, placing advertisements in Black newspapers, and offering train tickets to men who would sign on to work in northern factories.

The Chicago Defender, founded in 1905 by Robert Sengstacke Abbott, played a role in the Great Migration that has no precise parallel in any other migration in American history. The Defender was not merely a newspaper. It was, for hundreds of thousands of Black Southerners, a lifeline to information, hope, and possibility. Abbott, the son of freed slaves and a graduate of Hampton Institute, built the Defender into the most widely circulated Black newspaper in the country by the 1910s, with a circulation that reached deep into the South through the network of Pullman porters — the Black men who staffed the sleeping cars on America's railroads — who carried the paper into towns and cities throughout the region. The Defender documented anti-Black violence with unflinching directness, published advertisements for northern industrial employment, ran stories about the better conditions available to Black workers in Chicago, and explicitly encouraged migration. Its famous "Great Northern Drive" campaign of 1917 asked Black Southerners to join a coordinated migration to Chicago on a specific date, as a collective assertion of Black mobility and northern opportunity. The campaign was partly promotional and organizational reality was messier than the Defender's rhetoric suggested, but it illustrated both the paper's centrality to the migration and the way in which the migration was understood as a political as well as an economic act.

The specific geography of the migration was shaped by the railroad networks of the South and the existing migration chains that connected specific southern towns to specific northern destinations. Chicago received the majority of its Black migrants from Mississippi, Arkansas, and Alabama, following the Illinois Central Railroad's main line northward. Detroit drew heavily from Georgia and the eastern Carolinas along routes served by the Southern Railway and its connections. Philadelphia and New York drew from Virginia, the Carolinas, and Georgia along the Atlantic Coast. Los Angeles drew from Texas and Louisiana. These geographic patterns were not accidental. They reflected both the routes that labor agents traveled and advertised, and the family and community networks that channeled subsequent migrants along established paths. Once a significant number of people from a particular Mississippi county had settled in a particular Chicago neighborhood, the network was established: new arrivals had contacts who could help them find housing and work, and the social world of the northern neighborhood began to take on some of the character of the specific southern community from which it had grown.

The demographic scale of the migration was extraordinary. In 1900, approximately ninety percent of Black Americans lived in the South, and the vast majority of those were in rural areas. By 1970, roughly half of all Black Americans lived in the North and West, and by that time the majority of Black Americans — North and South — lived in urban areas. This was one of the most rapid and complete demographic transformations of any population in American history. It transformed not only the geography of Black America but the character of American urban life, the dynamics of American politics, and the content of American culture in ways that have continued to reverberate for a century.

The First Great Migration, roughly from 1910 to 1930, brought approximately 1.6 million Black Southerners to northern cities. The Second Great Migration, from approximately 1940 to 1970, was even larger, bringing an additional four million or more. The two waves were distinct in character as well as scale. The first wave was composed primarily of young adults making their way in conditions of relative uncertainty, establishing the initial settlements and institutional frameworks that later arrivals would build upon. The second wave was larger, moved more rapidly, and entered a northern urban landscape that was already well established in terms of Black institutional life but that was simultaneously beginning the dramatic processes of deindustrialization and suburbanization that would transform the economic foundations of urban Black communities in the postwar decades. The second wave arrived, in other words, just as the industrial economy that had made the first wave's settlement viable was beginning to contract — a timing that would have profound consequences for the later history of Black urban poverty, street organizations, and urban crisis.

The context of northern urban life that migrants entered was shaped above all by the housing market, which is to say by the geography of racial exclusion. Northern cities were not legally segregated in the way that southern cities were. There were no Jim Crow laws requiring Black people to sit in separate sections of buses or to use separate water fountains. But they were racially segregated in their residential patterns to a degree that approached and in some cases exceeded the formal segregation of the South, through a set of mechanisms — restrictive covenants, redlining, blockbusting, and organized white violence against Black families who attempted to move into white neighborhoods — that will be examined in detail in the next chapter. The result was that Black migrants were channeled into specific, confined neighborhoods: the South Side of Chicago, Harlem in New York, Paradise Valley in Detroit, North Philadelphia, and Central Avenue in Los Angeles. These neighborhoods became, over time, among the densest and most culturally productive in American urban history — and also the poorest, most underserved, and most subject to the structural disinvestment that would eventually fuel the crises examined in later chapters of this textbook.

Major People, Groups, and Places

Reference — People, Groups & Places

Robert Sengstacke Abbott (1868–1940)
Founder and editor of the Chicago Defender, one of the most influential African American journalists in American history. Abbott, born to formerly enslaved parents in Savannah, Georgia, built the Defender into the most widely circulated Black newspaper in America, with a readership that extended throughout the South via the Pullman porter network. His paper's explicit advocacy for Black migration northward made it a primary engine of the Great Migration. Abbott's own biography embodied the migration narrative: a child of the South who built his life and fortune in Chicago.
Ida B. Wells-Barnett (1862–1931)
Journalist, anti-lynching activist, and co-founder of the NAACP, Wells-Barnett was one of the most important figures in documenting the violence that drove the Great Migration and in building the organizational infrastructure of Black Chicago. Her investigative journalism on lynching in the 1890s helped lay the groundwork for the migration by documenting for northern audiences what Black Southerners already knew: that the South was a place of systematic, organized racial terror. After moving to Chicago, she was a central figure in that city's Black civic life.
Marcus Garvey (1887–1940)
Jamaican-born Black nationalist leader and founder of the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), Garvey arrived in the United States in 1916 and built the largest Black mass organization in American history, with chapters in dozens of cities and an estimated membership of two to four million people during the early 1920s. Garvey's message of Black pride, economic self-sufficiency, and pan-African solidarity resonated powerfully with recent migrants who found that the North had not delivered on its promises of full equality. His legacy — both his organizational achievement and his eventual federal prosecution and deportation — shaped Black political thought for decades.
James Weldon Johnson (1871–1938)
Author, poet, diplomat, and NAACP executive director, Johnson was one of the central figures of the Harlem Renaissance and one of the most important Black intellectuals of the early twentieth century. His 1925 anthology The Book of American Negro Spirituals and his contributions to Black literary life helped define the cultural achievements of the migration era. His 1930 work Black Manhattan remains one of the most important historical accounts of Black New York in the migration period.
Langston Hughes (1902–1967)
The most celebrated poet of the Harlem Renaissance, Hughes gave literary voice to the experience of Black urban life in the North with an unsparing clarity and a formal innovation — drawing on blues, jazz, and the vernacular speech of working-class Black communities — that was new in American literature. His poems, stories, and essays captured the contradictions of the Great Migration promise: the vitality of Black urban culture, the persistence of discrimination, and the enduring search for a freedom that northern cities never fully delivered.
Pullman Porters
The Black men employed by the Pullman Company to staff the sleeping cars of American passenger trains. The Pullman porters were one of the most important occupational groups in African American life during the migration era, providing stable employment, relatively good wages, and national mobility to tens of thousands of Black men. Their role in distributing the Chicago Defender and other Black newspapers throughout the South was crucial to the spread of information about migration opportunities. The Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, founded by A. Philip Randolph in 1925 and recognized as a legitimate union by the Pullman Company in 1937 after a twelve-year struggle, was one of the most important victories in the history of African American labor organizing.
A. Philip Randolph (1889–1979)
Labor organizer and civil rights leader, founder of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters and one of the most important Black political figures of the mid-twentieth century. Randolph's successful organization of the Pullman porters demonstrated that Black workers could win union recognition even against major corporate opposition, and his 1941 threatened March on Washington — which he called off when President Roosevelt issued Executive Order 8802 prohibiting racial discrimination in defense industries — established the political tactic of mass mobilization as a lever for federal civil rights action. Randolph's career bridged the labor movement and the civil rights movement in ways that were foundational to both.
The NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People)
Founded in 1909 by a coalition of Black and white activists including W.E.B. Du Bois, Ida B. Wells, and Oswald Garrison Villard, the NAACP was the primary civil rights organization in America during the migration era. Its legal arm challenged racial discrimination in the courts, its publications documented anti-Black violence, and its local chapters provided organizational infrastructure for Black civic life in cities across the country. The NAACP's growth closely tracked the Great Migration: as Black populations grew in northern cities, NAACP membership and chapter strength grew with them.
The National Urban League (founded 1910)
An organization established specifically to assist Black migrants in making the transition to northern urban life, the National Urban League provided job placement, housing assistance, social services, and training in urban workplace norms to newly arrived migrants. Its "opportunity" campaigns and its relationships with northern industrial employers made it one of the primary institutional bridges between the rural South and the urban North during the Great Migration. Though sometimes criticized for its accommodationist approach — it worked with white employers and philanthropists to integrate Black workers gradually — the League provided crucial practical assistance to tens of thousands of migrants.
Bronzeville, Chicago
The informal name for the Black neighborhood on Chicago's South Side that became one of the most densely populated and culturally productive Black communities in America during the Great Migration. Concentrated along State Street and its surrounding blocks from roughly 26th Street to 63rd Street, Bronzeville — also called "the Black Metropolis" by sociologists St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton — was home to hundreds of thousands of Black Chicagoans by the 1940s, and it was the birthplace or incubator of an extraordinary range of African American cultural, political, and intellectual achievement.
Harlem, New York City
The neighborhood in upper Manhattan that became the symbolic capital of Black America during the Great Migration and the center of the Harlem Renaissance. Originally a middle-class white neighborhood, Harlem transitioned to a predominantly Black neighborhood as white residents fled and Black migrants from the South, the Caribbean, and other parts of the country moved in between approximately 1905 and 1930. By the late 1920s, Harlem was the center of Black literary, artistic, and intellectual life in America and a symbol, internationally, of the aspirations and achievements of the "New Negro."
Paradise Valley and Black Bottom, Detroit
The adjacent neighborhoods on Detroit's east side that constituted the heart of Black Detroit during the migration era. Paradise Valley was the commercial and entertainment heart of Black Detroit, home to jazz clubs, restaurants, and Black-owned businesses. Black Bottom was the residential neighborhood from which it grew, densely populated with migrants from the Deep South who had come to work in the automobile factories. Both neighborhoods were destroyed by urban renewal in the 1950s and 1960s — a displacement that would have devastating consequences for Black community life in Detroit.
Central Avenue, Los Angeles
The main commercial and cultural corridor of Black Los Angeles during the migration era, stretching south from downtown through the neighborhoods of Jefferson Park, Adams, and Leimert Park. Central Avenue was the home of a distinctive jazz scene, Black-owned businesses, and the community life of the African American community in a city that was rapidly growing during the Second Great Migration. The Central Avenue corridor was defined by different pressures than the older, more enclosed Black neighborhoods of Chicago and New York, reflecting the specific geography and racial politics of the West Coast.
The Illinois Central Railroad
The primary transportation artery of the First Great Migration to Chicago, the Illinois Central ran north from New Orleans through Mississippi, Tennessee, and Kentucky to Chicago, following the same route that cotton and agricultural goods traveled from the Mississippi Delta to northern markets. Black migrants from Mississippi, Louisiana, and Tennessee rode the IC north, and Chicago's Black South Side was settled in large part by people who had traveled that route. The Illinois Central's route shaped the specific character of Black Chicago in ways that were visible in the neighborhood's dialects, foodways, music, and community networks for decades.
The Defender, the Pittsburgh Courier, and the Black Press
The network of Black newspapers — most importantly the Chicago Defender and the Pittsburgh Courier, but including dozens of city-specific publications — that provided Black Americans with news, information, and perspectives that the white press ignored or distorted. During the migration era, the Black press was not merely journalism but a form of civic infrastructure, organizing migration, documenting violence, celebrating achievement, and connecting dispersed communities into something resembling a national Black public sphere.

Section 2.1

Leaving the South: Push Factors and the Decision to Migrate

Violence, Terror, and the Limits of Survival

When historians speak of the "push factors" driving the Great Migration, they are using a somewhat clinical term for what was, at the human level, a combination of terror, humiliation, exhaustion, and calculation so intense that it drove millions of people to leave the only places they had ever known, leave behind family members, graves, churches, and communities, and cast themselves into an uncertain journey toward cities they had never seen and where they knew no one. The clinical language of push and pull factors is analytically useful, but students should not let it obscure the human reality of what was being pushed.

Racial violence was perhaps the most viscerally motivating push factor. The practice of lynching reached its statistical peak in the 1890s but remained a regular feature of southern life well into the twentieth century. Between 1900 and 1930, approximately 1,100 lynchings of Black people were documented in the South — an average of nearly forty per year, with clusters in the Deep South states of Mississippi, Georgia, Alabama, and Louisiana. These documented cases certainly undercounted the actual number of killings, since many were never reported and others were classified as "justified" homicides or recorded as suicides by the same law enforcement officers who had participated in them. Lynching was not only a murder but a spectacle. Many lynchings were public events, announced in advance, attended by crowds of hundreds or thousands, and photographed for postcards that were sold as souvenirs. The spectacle was the point: it was designed not only to kill the specific individual victim but to terrorize the entire Black community into a posture of submission before white authority.

Alongside the dramatic violence of lynching, the daily texture of life under Jim Crow included a constant series of smaller degradations and dangers that cumulatively produced an existential condition of vulnerability and subordination. A Black man in Georgia in 1915 navigated every public interaction with white people through a careful choreography of deference: removing his hat in the presence of white women, stepping off the sidewalk to let white men pass, using the back door of any establishment, accepting the address of "boy" from men younger than himself, and never looking a white man directly in the eyes with any suggestion of challenge. Failure to observe these rituals of racial deference could result in anything from a verbal rebuke to a beating to arrest to death. The white southern social order relied on the constant performance of Black subordination, and it enforced that performance with the casual brutality that comes from absolute impunity.

The economic dimension of the push was both structural and precipitous. The structural element — the sharecropping and debt peonage system that had kept Black agricultural workers in chronic economic dependency since the end of Reconstruction — had been building pressure for decades. A typical sharecropper family in Mississippi in 1914 owed their landlord for seeds, fertilizer, tools, and food consumed during the growing season; the accounting at harvest time, controlled by the landlord with no independent oversight, almost invariably showed that the value of the crop fell short of the debt, meaning that the family would enter the next year deeper in debt than the last. This was not accidental. It was the intended design of the system. Landlords had every incentive to structure accounting in ways that preserved the labor of Black families on their land, and they had the support of local law enforcement — including vagrancy laws that criminalized leaving a labor contract — to prevent those families from seeking better arrangements elsewhere.

The precipitating crisis came with the boll weevil. The boll weevil was an insect that had been spreading eastward from Mexico since the 1890s, destroying cotton by infesting the bolls before they could be harvested. By 1915, the weevil had reached most of the major cotton-growing areas of the Deep South, and its effect on cotton yields was catastrophic. In county after county across Mississippi, Alabama, and Georgia, cotton harvests in 1915 and 1916 fell by fifty to ninety percent from their previous years' levels. For a sharecropper whose entire economic life depended on the cotton crop, this was not merely a bad year. It was a complete collapse of the economic basis of existence. The timing coincided with the northern industrial recruitment campaigns that were beginning to offer real wages for industrial work, and for many the coincidence was decisive. When the choice was between a devastated cotton crop, deepening debt, and the prospect of being imprisoned for vagrancy if you left, versus the possibility of two dollars a day in a Chicago meatpacking plant, the calculation was not difficult.

Agricultural Decline and Economic Displacement

Beyond the immediate crisis of the boll weevil, the structural transformation of the southern agricultural economy in the early twentieth century was eroding the economic position of Black rural workers across a wide range of circumstances. The expansion of mechanized agriculture — mechanical cotton pickers, tractors replacing mules, new cultivation techniques — was beginning to displace the hand-labor that had been the economic basis of the plantation system. This displacement was not yet as severe as it would become after World War II, when the widespread adoption of the mechanical cotton picker essentially eliminated the need for the Black sharecropping labor force in the Mississippi Delta. But its beginnings were visible, and Black agricultural workers who were paying attention could see that the future of manual agricultural labor in the South was not bright.

The consolidation of landholding also squeezed small Black farmers and sharecroppers. The small number of Black landowners who had managed to acquire property during or after Reconstruction — a significant achievement against enormous odds — faced persistent pressure from white neighbors and local government officials who used tax assessments, legal manipulation, and outright violence to dispossess them. The story of Black landownership in the South between 1900 and 1940 is largely a story of loss: total Black-owned farmland peaked around 1910 and then declined steadily for decades as the mechanisms of dispossession — some legal, some extralegal — worked to return Black-owned land to white hands. Families who lost their land typically had no option but to enter the sharecropping system, increasing the pool of dependent agricultural labor even as the economic basis of that labor was being undermined.

The political dimension of the push was inseparable from the economic. Black political life in the South had been effectively destroyed by the disenfranchisement campaigns of the 1890s and 1900s, and without the vote, Black communities had no mechanism for addressing any of the economic, legal, or safety concerns that affected them. The sheriff who ignored reports of violence against Black people, the school board that allocated Black schools a fraction of the per-pupil funding of white schools, the county agent who directed agricultural assistance to white farmers and not to Black ones, the judge who reliably found Black defendants guilty and white defendants innocent — none of these officials faced any accountability to the Black community because the Black community could not vote. Political exclusion was not simply a denial of a formal right. It was a condition that made all other conditions worse, because it removed the possibility of any political remedy for any grievance.

The Emotional Geography of Departure

The decision to migrate was not made only through rational economic calculation, and it was rarely made without profound emotional cost. The letters that Black Southerners wrote to the Chicago Defender and to family members who had preceded them north are documents of both determination and grief — determination to escape conditions that had become intolerable, grief at the prospect of leaving parents, siblings, churches, and communities that had been the substance of life. Many migrants left without knowing whether they would ever return or ever see aging parents again. In an era before widespread telephone access and when travel back south was expensive and, for many, dangerous — a Black man who had left the South under circumstances that angered his former landlord could not safely return — departure was often understood as permanent. The migration letters collected by historians and archivists are, among other things, records of farewells.

The churches played a complex role in the departure. Historically, Black churches had been anchors of community life — the institutions most closely associated with the bonds of kinship, memory, and mutual obligation that constituted the social fabric of rural Black communities. For many migrants, leaving meant leaving the church where they had been baptized, married, and where they expected to be buried. Ministers were often ambivalent about the migration: some actively encouraged it, some actively discouraged it out of concern for their congregations' survival, and some simply understood that their members were going to leave regardless and tried to prepare them and to maintain connections with those who had already gone. The Black church both facilitated the migration — as an information network, as a source of contacts in destination cities, and as the institutional basis for the new congregations that migrants established in northern cities — and was disrupted by it, as communities lost their young adults and their most economically active members to the northern journey.

Historical Vocabulary
The Great Migration

The Great Migration refers to the movement of approximately six million Black Americans from the rural South to cities in the North, Midwest, and West between approximately 1910 and 1970. Historians typically identify two distinct waves: the First Great Migration (c. 1910–1940), which brought approximately 1.6 million people to northern industrial cities primarily to fill wartime labor shortages; and the Second Great Migration (c. 1940–1970), which brought four million or more to a wider range of destinations including West Coast cities and was driven by both continuing racial violence and the massive labor demands of World War II industrial production.

The term itself is significant. It frames the movement as a "great" historical event — comparable in scale and consequence to other great migrations in world history — and it emphasizes the collective character of what was simultaneously an enormous accumulation of individual decisions. The word "migration" rather than "emigration" or "exodus" (though both of those terms were also used contemporaneously) suggests a movement within a single national space, a claim to belonging that the migrants were asserting: they were not leaving America, they were moving within it.

The Great Migration transformed American cities, American culture, American politics, and the character of African American life in ways that were so comprehensive and so lasting that virtually every dimension of contemporary Black American experience has roots in this movement. The Black urban communities of the North and West — with their distinctive music, politics, literature, religious life, and social organization — are the primary legacy of the Great Migration. So, in a different way, is the concentrated urban poverty, structural exclusion, and neighborhood disinvestment that became more visible as the industrial economy that sustained the first migrants' lives began to contract in the decades after World War II.

Section 2.2

Industrial Opportunity: The Pull of Northern Cities

The Industrial Economy and the Labor Demand

The northern cities that Black migrants moved toward in the 1910s and 1920s were in the midst of one of the most extraordinary periods of industrial expansion in American history. The automobile industry, centered in Detroit but extending to cities across the Midwest and Northeast, was growing with a speed that seemed to many observers almost miraculous. Henry Ford's introduction of the moving assembly line at his Highland Park plant in 1913 had transformed not only automobile manufacturing but industrial production more broadly, establishing the principle of mass production through standardized, sequential labor that would come to define twentieth-century industry. The demand for labor in Ford's plants, and in the dozens of supplier industries that grew up around them, was enormous and growing.

Chicago's meatpacking industry — the stockyards at the south end of the city, which gave the city its nickname "Hog Butcher for the World" in Carl Sandburg's 1914 poem — employed tens of thousands of workers in conditions that were brutal by any standard. Upton Sinclair's 1906 novel The Jungle, though it focused primarily on the unsanitary food production conditions in the packinghouses, had also documented the exploitation of immigrant workers who performed the slaughter and processing work. When European immigration declined during and after World War I, the packinghouses found in Black Southern migrants an available labor supply, and they actively recruited through labor agents sent to Mississippi and Alabama. The wages were not high by any absolute measure, but they were dramatically higher than what agricultural work in the South paid, and the work, however grueling, was not supervised by a white landlord with a whip and the backing of the county sheriff.

Pittsburgh's steel mills, Gary, Indiana's integrated steel complexes, the shipyards of Baltimore and Philadelphia, the tobacco factories of Winston-Salem and Richmond (which employed Black workers but at the lowest-skill levels), the foundries of Cleveland and Cincinnati — all of these industries were hungry for labor during the wartime and postwar boom years, and all of them, to varying degrees, became employers of Black migrants. The degree to which Black workers were welcomed or tolerated in northern industries varied significantly: some employers actively recruited and relatively fairly treated Black workers, while others used them primarily as strikebreakers, knowing that the desperation of men who had no other options could be used to undermine white union workers' bargaining power. This use of Black workers as strikebreakers was one of the most powerful sources of anti-Black sentiment among white northern workers, and it contributed directly to the racial violence that characterized the labor conflicts of the 1917–1921 period.

Wages, Work, and the Limits of Industrial Employment

The wage difference between the South and the North was real and significant, but it was not the whole story of Black workers' economic experience in northern industry. Black workers in northern factories typically earned more than they had in the South, but they earned substantially less than white workers performing comparable work, and they were systematically confined to the heaviest, dirtiest, most dangerous, and least-skilled positions in most industries. In the steel mills, Black workers were concentrated in the blast furnace department — the most dangerous and physically brutal work in the mill — and were excluded from the rolling mills, the skilled machinist positions, and the supervisory roles that paid significantly more. In the meatpacking industry, Black workers handled the slaughter floor work and the heavy cutting, while white workers controlled the skilled butchering and packing work that qualified for higher pay grades.

Union membership was another critical dimension of wage and working-condition inequality. The major industrial unions of the era — the AFL (American Federation of Labor) craft unions and many of the industrial unions — either formally excluded Black members, maintained separate and subordinate Black locals, or operated in ways that effectively prevented Black workers from competing for skilled positions. The United Mine Workers was one of the few unions that attempted, with mixed success, to organize Black and white workers equally, and the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters demonstrated that all-Black unions could successfully fight for recognition. But for most Black industrial workers in the migration era, union membership was not available, which meant they had no formal institutional leverage over wages, hours, or working conditions. The absence of union representation was one of the most important structural disadvantages of Black workers in northern industry, and it contributed to the persistence of the wage gap between Black and white workers even as both groups' absolute wages rose during periods of economic expansion.

The First World War created a particular dynamic in the northern labor market that illustrates both the opportunity and the vulnerability of Black workers' position. As wartime production demands expanded and the labor supply was simultaneously contracted by the draft, Black workers found opportunities in industries and positions that would not otherwise have been open to them. Many performed well, proving to skeptical employers that Black workers could handle industrial work. But when the war ended and white veterans returned to the labor market, many of these gains were reversed. Black workers who had moved into positions vacated by white workers during the war were displaced when those workers returned. The pattern of Black workers being used as a labor reserve — brought in when white workers were unavailable and pushed out when they returned — was a recurring feature of the industrial economy throughout the migration era and beyond.

The Relative Freedom of Northern Urban Life

The economic dimension of the northern pull, important as it was, cannot be separated from the social and political dimensions. Black migrants did not move to northern cities only for higher wages. They moved for the complex of experiences that they meant when they used the word "freedom" — a word that appeared constantly in the migration letters, oral histories, and oral testimonies of the migration era. What they meant by freedom was not identical from person to person, but it included a set of recurring themes: the ability to walk down a public street without performing racial deference, the ability to vote and to have that vote count, the ability to participate in public life as a citizen rather than as a subordinate, the possibility of addressing conflicts through the legal system rather than being subject to a legal system that was openly an instrument of racial domination, and the possibility that one's children might receive a genuine education and have access to economic opportunities that were systematically foreclosed in the South.

The comparative freedom of northern life was real, and migrants experienced it as real. A Black man who had spent his life in Mississippi walking into a Chicago polling place and casting a ballot — something that was not merely symbolically important but literally dangerous in many parts of the South — was having an experience that mattered. A Black woman who could ride a Chicago streetcar without being forced to the back, who could try on clothes in a department store without being told to leave, who could address white people with the same formal courtesy that she received from them — these were not trivial experiences. They were affirmations of a human dignity that had been systematically denied.

But the relative freedom of the North had real limits that migrants also discovered quickly. Northern racism was different from southern racism in form, but it was not absent. Northern white people who welcomed Black labor in their factories objected strenuously to having Black neighbors. The mechanisms of residential segregation — restrictive covenants, discriminatory real estate practices, organized neighborhood violence against Black families who attempted to move into white areas — were, if anything, more systematic and more effective in many northern cities than the legally mandated segregation of the South. The irony of the Great Migration, which became more apparent with each passing decade, was that it moved Black Americans from a form of racial oppression that was legally explicit but spatially dispersed across the rural South, into a form of racial oppression that was legally implicit but spatially concentrated in dense urban enclaves — and that this spatial concentration would prove to be one of the most powerful mechanisms of racial inequality in modern American history.

Racial Violence in the North: The Red Summer of 1919

The white North's hostility to Black migration found its most explosive expression in the wave of racial violence that historians call the "Red Summer" of 1919. Between April and November of 1919, racial violence erupted in at least twenty-six American cities, including Chicago, Washington, D.C., Omaha, Knoxville, and dozens of smaller cities and towns. In most cases, white mobs attacked Black neighborhoods, killed Black residents, and destroyed Black property, while local law enforcement either stood by or participated. The violence was not spontaneous. It grew from specific anxieties: the return of Black veterans from World War I, who were asserting their dignity and their rights more forcefully than the white South or North was prepared to accept; the labor market competition between Black migrants and white workers in northern industries; and a broader white reaction to the demographic and social changes that the migration was producing.

The Chicago Race Riot of July 1919 was the most destructive of the Red Summer episodes. It began on July 27 when a Black teenager named Eugene Williams drowned in Lake Michigan after crossing an invisible racial boundary on the lakefront beach and being stoned by white swimmers. The police refused to arrest the white men responsible. Fighting between Black and white youths that afternoon escalated over the following days into a full-scale riot in which white mobs invaded Black neighborhoods, Black residents defended themselves, and the violence continued for thirteen days. When it ended, 38 people were dead — 23 Black and 15 white — more than 500 were injured, and approximately 1,000 Black families had lost their homes to fire. The Chicago Commission on Race Relations, established by the governor in the aftermath of the riot, produced a landmark 1922 study, The Negro in Chicago, that documented the structural conditions — housing discrimination, labor market exclusion, police bias, and neighborhood conflict — that had produced the violence and that continued to characterize Black life in the city.

The Chicago riot and the Red Summer more broadly had complex effects on Black migrants and the communities they had built. On one hand, they were profoundly disillusioning — confirmation that the North was not the promised land of freedom and opportunity that the Chicago Defender had implied. On the other hand, the defense of Black neighborhoods against white mobs — the fact that Black residents fought back, that some white attackers were killed, that the violence did not produce a rout of the Black community but rather a contested struggle — contributed to a new assertiveness in Black urban communities, a refusal to accept white violence with the passive submission that Jim Crow required in the South. The "New Negro" self-assertion that characterized Black intellectual and cultural life in the 1920s had roots in the experience of the wartime migration and the Red Summer as well as in the cultural achievements of the Harlem Renaissance.

The Tulsa Race Massacre of May 31 to June 1, 1921, was the most catastrophic episode of anti-Black racial violence in the North American continent in the twentieth century. The Greenwood District of Tulsa, Oklahoma — known as "Black Wall Street" because of its concentration of Black-owned businesses, professional offices, hotels, and cultural institutions — was the most prosperous Black commercial district in the country. In the massacre, white mobs, some deputized by local authorities and some supported by airplanes from which shooting or bombing may have occurred, burned thirty-five blocks of Greenwood to the ground, killed an estimated 100 to 300 Black residents, and left 10,000 Black Tulsans homeless. The massacre was systematically covered up for decades by local authorities, with records destroyed and survivors intimidated into silence, and it was not officially acknowledged and investigated until a 2001 Oklahoma state commission report.

Section 2.3

Migration Networks: How the Movement Was Organized

Family Migration Chains

The Great Migration was not, in most of its dimensions, an organized movement. There was no central coordinating body, no formal migration organization, no government agency directing people where to go or providing support for their journey. It was, as historians have emphasized, a movement organized primarily through personal networks — through the dense webs of kinship, friendship, church membership, and community connection that linked people in specific southern communities to people who had already made the journey to specific northern destinations. Understanding these networks is essential to understanding both the logistics and the experience of the migration.

The chain migration pattern was fundamental. In a typical chain migration, one person — often a young man, but sometimes a woman or a married couple — made the initial journey, found work and housing in a northern city, and then sent word back to family and community members in the South about conditions in the new place. This information — in letters, in occasional return visits, in messages carried by the Pullman porters who traveled between North and South — created a feedback loop that shaped subsequent migration decisions. If the reports were good — work available, wages decent, conditions better than in the South — additional family members or neighbors were likely to follow. If the reports were discouraging — work hard to find, housing expensive, racism persistent — others might delay or redirect to a different destination.

The practical importance of these chains for newly arriving migrants cannot be overstated. Moving to a city where you knew no one, where you had no housing arranged, where you did not know which neighborhoods were safe or how to navigate the employment process, was a frightening prospect. The chain migration pattern meant that most migrants arrived not into a void but into a receiving network: a cousin who would let you sleep on the floor while you found your footing, a former neighbor who knew which employers were hiring and how to apply, a church member who could tell you which blocks to avoid and which landlords were reliable. This network function was one of the most important contributions that earlier migrants made to the success of the migration as a whole — not just by going first, but by remaining connected to the communities they had left and making that connection useful to those who followed.

The Role of Black Newspapers

The Chicago Defender played a role in the Great Migration that is difficult to overstate. Robert Abbott's paper reached deep into the South through the Pullman porter network, providing Black Southerners with information about conditions in the North that was unavailable from any other source. The Defender reported northern wages, listed employers who were hiring, described the legal protections available to Black residents in northern states, celebrated the achievements of Black migrants who had made good in Chicago, and — with a directness that the white southern press would never have permitted — documented anti-Black violence throughout the South. Reading the Defender, a Black Mississippian could compare the conditions of his life in granular detail with conditions available elsewhere, in a way that had not been possible for earlier generations.

The paper was not merely informational. It was explicitly ideological: Abbott believed that migration was both individually beneficial and politically transformative, a mass assertion of Black freedom that would shift the balance of power between Black and white America. His editorials encouraged migration in terms that were sometimes grandiose but consistently passionate, framing the movement northward not merely as an economic decision but as a declaration of independence from a racial order that had been imposed by force and could be escaped by movement. The Defender's famous campaign of 1917, calling on Black Southerners to join a "Great Northern Drive" on May 15th, was partly performative — it overstated the organized character of the migration — but it reflected a genuine political understanding of what the migration meant.

The Pittsburgh Courier, founded in 1910, performed similar functions for Black migrants moving to the East and Midwest. The Courier was, by the 1930s and 1940s, the most widely circulated Black newspaper in the country, surpassing even the Defender, and it combined vigorous journalism about racial injustice with extensive coverage of Black community life, business, entertainment, and sports. Alongside the Defender and the Courier, a network of city-specific Black newspapers — the Amsterdam News in New York, the Baltimore Afro-American, the Los Angeles Sentinel, the Cleveland Gazette — provided local information for migrants in specific cities and maintained connections to the national Black community through wire-sharing agreements and syndicated columnists.

Churches as Migration Networks

The Black church played a dual role in the migration that was somewhat paradoxical: it was both a reason to stay and a mechanism for leaving. Churches were the central community institutions of rural Black life in the South, and leaving one's church meant leaving the most important social network of one's life. Many ministers actively discouraged migration, worried about the moral dangers of city life and the disruption of their congregations. Yet at the same time, the Baptist and Methodist denominational networks that linked churches across the country also provided pathways through which migrants could find a church community in a new city, and the church was the first institution that most migrants sought out upon arriving in the North.

The storefront church was a characteristic institution of the early northern Black migrant community. As established Black churches in northern cities found themselves overwhelmed with new arrivals who had different worship styles, different musical traditions, and different cultural references than the existing congregation, migrants frequently established their own congregations in rented storefronts, basements, or converted shops. These storefront churches were often affiliated with southern denominations rather than established northern ones — the Baptist associations, the Church of God in Christ, the Holiness churches — and they preserved worship styles that were more demonstrative, more musical, and more emotionally expressive than the more restrained worship of established northern Black churches. The storefront church was more than just a place of worship; it was a community center, a social service agency, a political meeting space, and, for many migrants, the primary institution through which they navigated the transition from rural southern to urban northern life.

Historical Vocabulary
Chain Migration

Chain migration refers to a pattern of population movement in which later migrants follow routes and settle in destinations that were established by earlier migrants from the same community, guided by the information and assistance that earlier migrants provide. In chain migration, the social networks of family and community connection act as the primary organizational infrastructure of the movement, replacing formal institutional coordination with informal personal bonds.

Chain migration is particularly important for understanding the Great Migration because it explains why Black migrants from specific communities in the South tended to cluster in specific neighborhoods in the North. Migrants from Clarksdale, Mississippi, often settled in a particular section of Chicago's South Side, not because of any formal plan but because the first Clarksdale migrants had settled there, told their Clarksdale relatives and neighbors about housing and employment in that area, and created a receiving network that directed subsequent Clarksdale migrants to the same neighborhood. Over time, this concentration meant that specific northern neighborhoods carried the social character of specific southern communities — their churches, their food traditions, their musical styles, their family networks — creating in the urban North a distinctive set of communities that were culturally continuous with their southern origins while simultaneously adapting to a radically new environment.

Chain migration also had important implications for the politics and economics of northern Black communities. The clustering of migrants from specific origins created powerful social solidarity within neighborhoods but also created potential for conflict between groups from different regional origins. The tensions between migrants from different states or regions, and between recent migrants and longer-established Black residents, were features of virtually every major Black urban community during the migration era, and they shaped the political and organizational life of those communities in complex ways.

Labor Agents and Institutional Recruitment

Beyond the organic networks of family and community, the Great Migration was also shaped by more formal institutional actors, most importantly the labor agents sent by northern employers into the South to recruit Black workers. Labor recruitment was not a new practice in 1915 — northern employers had long used agents to recruit European immigrants — but the systematic recruitment of Black workers from the South was a significant departure from previous practice, driven by the wartime labor shortage. Labor agents traveled to southern cities, placed advertisements in local Black newspapers (and sometimes in the white press), visited churches and barbershops, and offered train tickets and advance wages to men who would agree to work for their employers.

The southern response to these labor agents was revealing. White southern employers and municipal governments recognized immediately that the loss of Black labor was an economic and social threat, and they responded with a combination of legal harassment, physical intimidation, and legislative action. Several southern states and cities passed laws requiring labor agents to obtain local licenses — licenses that were routinely denied to anyone recruiting Black workers for northern employment. Agents who were discovered operating without a license were arrested, fined, or run out of town. In some cases, Black workers who had already been recruited and were waiting for the northbound train were arrested on vagrancy or contract-violation charges to prevent their departure. The systematic legal obstruction of Black migration by southern authorities was a remarkable demonstration of the degree to which the Jim Crow system depended on the captive labor of Black workers who had no other options — and the degree to which it was threatened by the existence of those options.

The National Urban League, founded in New York in 1910, played a complementary role to the labor agents from the opposite direction: instead of recruiting workers from the South, it helped workers who had already arrived to find employment, housing, and social services in the North. The League's relationships with northern employers were carefully cultivated and sometimes controversial — critics argued that it functioned too much as a supplier of cheap labor rather than as an advocate for Black workers' rights and wages — but its practical assistance to newly arrived migrants was significant. The League maintained offices in most major cities and served as a first-stop resource for migrants who had arrived without established contacts, providing referrals to housing, employment, health services, and community institutions.

Section 2.4

New Urban Communities: Building Life in the City

Chicago and Bronzeville: The Black Metropolis

No city was more central to the First Great Migration than Chicago, and no neighborhood was more central to the history of Black Chicago than Bronzeville. The South Side neighborhood that became Bronzeville was already home to a small but established Black community before the migration began — several thousand Black Chicagoans who had arrived in earlier decades, had established churches, businesses, and civic organizations, and had built a modest middle class of professionals, business owners, and skilled workers. The Great Migration transformed this small community into one of the largest and most culturally dynamic Black urban neighborhoods in the world.

Between 1910 and 1930, the Black population of Chicago grew from approximately 44,000 to more than 230,000, almost entirely through migration from the South. By 1940, the Chicago Black community numbered more than 275,000, representing nearly eight percent of the city's total population. These new residents were concentrated in a narrow strip of the South Side — the "Black Belt" — that was prevented from expanding into adjacent white neighborhoods by the restrictive covenant system, which will be examined in detail in the next chapter. The density of the Black Belt was extraordinary: block after block of apartment buildings, rooming houses, and converted single-family homes packed with families, some of them sleeping in shifts. The physical conditions were often crowded, poorly maintained, and expensive — Black renters paid premium rates for the privilege of being confined to a single neighborhood — but the cultural and community life that developed within those conditions was extraordinarily rich.

Bronzeville was sometimes called the "Black Metropolis," a term that sociologists St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton used as the title of their landmark 1945 study of Black Chicago. The term was apt in ways that went beyond mere celebration. Bronzeville was, in certain respects, a self-contained city within a city, with its own commercial districts, entertainment venues, newspapers, churches, hospitals, political organizations, professional associations, and cultural institutions. The concentration of Black life that the restrictive covenant system imposed had an unintended consequence: it created a captive market for Black businesses and a dense community that was capable of supporting Black institutions at a scale that would not have been possible if Black residents had been dispersed throughout the city. The Defender was published there. Jazz and blues clubs lined State Street and Cottage Grove. The Regal Theater and the Grand Theater hosted nationally prominent Black entertainers. The Wabash Avenue YMCA was a gathering place for Black civic life. Policy (numbers) games run by Black operators provided informal credit and economic circulation in a community excluded from formal banking. And the Democratic Party political organization of the South Side, increasingly dominated by Black aldermen and committeemen, gave Bronzeville a degree of formal political power that no Black community in the South could have approached.

The tension between Bronzeville's cultural vitality and the structural conditions of its existence was constant and productive in some ways, ultimately destructive in others. The forced concentration of Black life created community solidarity and institutional density, but it also created overcrowding, exploitative landlordism, inadequate public services, and the physical deterioration of the housing stock. The apartments that migrants moved into in the 1910s and 1920s had often been built as single-family homes and then subdivided by landlords who understood that Black renters would pay high prices for housing they could not obtain elsewhere. The physical quality of housing in Bronzeville was systematically worse than in comparable white neighborhoods, even when the rents were comparable or higher. This was not a natural outcome of poverty; it was a direct consequence of the housing market structure that confined Black residents to a specific area and allowed landlords to extract premium rents for substandard conditions.

Detroit: The Motor City and Black Labor

Detroit's Black community grew even more dramatically than Chicago's during the Great Migration. The automobile industry's insatiable appetite for labor drove a recruitment effort that brought Black workers from Georgia, Alabama, and the Carolinas into the Motor City in enormous numbers. Detroit's Black population grew from approximately 5,700 in 1910 to more than 120,000 by 1930, and it would continue growing through the Second Great Migration to reach nearly 300,000 by 1950. Henry Ford, who had complex and contradictory racial attitudes, employed Black workers at his River Rouge complex in larger numbers and in slightly less discriminatory ways than most of his competitors — partly out of a genuine paternalism, partly because he saw Black workers as an alternative to the union organizing that his white workers were pursuing. By the early 1940s, Ford employed roughly 10,000 Black workers at River Rouge, a larger concentration of Black industrial workers than existed in any other single plant in the country.

Black Detroit was concentrated in the area known as Paradise Valley and Black Bottom — adjacent neighborhoods on the east side of the city that were defined by the same combination of restrictive covenants, racial real estate practices, and organized white resistance to Black residential expansion that characterized Black neighborhoods in Chicago. The neighborhoods were densely settled, culturally rich, and, like Bronzeville, a center of extraordinary musical life: Detroit's jazz scene, which gave birth to or shaped artists including Earl Hines, McKinney's Cotton Pickers, and dozens of others, was centered in the clubs and theaters of Paradise Valley. The neighborhoods were also the home of Black political organization that, by the 1940s, was producing Black representatives in the Michigan legislature and influential figures in the Detroit city council.

Detroit's labor history during the migration era was particularly complex because of the intersection of race and union organizing. The United Auto Workers, formed in 1935, was one of the most important unions in American labor history, and its struggle to organize the automobile industry against the fierce resistance of Ford, General Motors, and Chrysler was one of the defining labor conflicts of the 1930s. Black workers were caught in a painful position: many had been recruited by Ford specifically as a potential counterweight to unionization, and Ford's relatively better treatment of Black workers made many of them loyal to him in ways that white union organizers struggled to overcome. But the UAW's eventual success, and its general commitment to racial equality (despite the prejudices of many individual members), created the possibility of a broad interracial working-class coalition that would shape Detroit's politics for decades. The relationship between the Black community and organized labor in Detroit was one of the most consequential and contested features of that city's political life.

New York and the Harlem Renaissance

Harlem's transformation from a middle-class white neighborhood to the symbolic capital of Black America was one of the most consequential urban transitions of the twentieth century. The area of upper Manhattan north of Central Park had been developed in the 1890s and early 1900s as a residential neighborhood for middle-class white New Yorkers, with apartment buildings and brownstones targeted at the expanding white professional class. When the real estate market overexpanded and vacancies accumulated, landlords desperate for tenants began renting to Black residents, initially through the efforts of Black real estate broker Philip Payton and the Afro-American Realty Company. White residents fled, Black residents moved in, and by the 1920s Harlem was the most densely populated and culturally productive Black neighborhood in America — the destination for Black migrants from the South, from the Caribbean, from the rest of the Northeast, and from Black communities throughout the country who wanted to be at the center of Black cultural and intellectual life.

The Harlem Renaissance — the extraordinary flowering of Black literary, artistic, and musical production that is associated with Harlem in the 1920s — was the most visible cultural expression of the Great Migration's creative energy. Writers including Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen, Claude McKay, Nella Larsen, and dozens of others produced a body of literature that was new in American letters: urban, Black, formally innovative, and deeply engaged with the contradictions of Black life in a country that proclaimed freedom while practicing racial oppression. Visual artists including Aaron Douglas created a new Black visual aesthetic. Philosophers and intellectuals including Alain Locke and W.E.B. Du Bois developed the theoretical frameworks for understanding what Locke called the "New Negro" — a Black American who insisted on full humanity and equal citizenship without apology or deferral. Musicians developed jazz and blues into art forms of international significance. And all of these achievements were interconnected: the writers heard the music, the artists responded to the literature, the intellectuals theorized what the musicians were doing, and the whole complex of cultural production was embedded in the specific social world of Harlem — its clubs, churches, barbershops, social clubs, and the extraordinary human density of a great Black community at the height of its creative power.

The Harlem Renaissance had complex relationships to the Great Migration and to the conditions of the Black working class. The literary and intellectual production of the Renaissance was largely the work of an educated Black middle class and professional class — people who had education, connections to white cultural patrons, and access to publishing networks that were unavailable to most Black migrants. Langston Hughes was unusual in his consistent attention to working-class Black life; many Harlem Renaissance writers were more interested in asserting Black sophistication and high cultural achievement than in representing the experience of the migrants who were packing Harlem's overcrowded tenements. The working-class Harlem that most migrants inhabited — the Harlem of domestic workers, factory workers, street vendors, and numbers runners — was both the material basis of the Renaissance and a world that the Renaissance's most celebrated figures sometimes treated with ambivalence or patronizing sympathy rather than genuine representation.

Philadelphia: Older Communities and New Arrivals

Philadelphia had a distinctive character among Great Migration destinations because of the city's long history of Black community life. Philadelphia's Black community was the largest free Black community in antebellum America, and its institutions — churches, schools, fraternal orders, and mutual aid societies — were among the oldest and most developed in the country. The Great Migration brought an enormous number of new Black residents to Philadelphia, but it brought them into a city with an existing Black institutional infrastructure and an existing Black elite that had established relationships and expectations about community standards, social mobility, and the proper relationship between Black Philadelphians and the white power structure.

The tension between the older, established Black community and the newer Southern migrants was a feature of Black Philadelphia during the migration era, as it was of Black communities in most destination cities. The established Black middle class and professional community — the descendants of antebellum free Black Philadelphians, the AME Church leadership, the graduates of Lincoln and Cheyney universities — sometimes viewed the Southern migrants with a mixture of sympathy and social distance, worried that the migrants' Southern folkways, perceived lack of urban sophistication, and association with working-class culture would undermine the respectable image of the Black community that middle-class Black leaders had carefully constructed. This class tension within Black communities was a recurring feature of the migration era, visible in debates about church worship styles, neighborhood social norms, and the proper strategies for advancing Black rights.

Los Angeles: Western Expansion and the Second Migration

Los Angeles received a relatively small Black population during the First Great Migration but became one of the major destinations of the Second Great Migration, particularly during and after World War II. The specific character of Black Los Angeles — sprawling, dispersed across a wide geographic area, organized around the automobile rather than walking and transit, without the dense neighborhood life of Chicago or New York — was shaped by the city's geography, its relative newness, and the timing of the Black migration that shaped it. Central Avenue, stretching south from downtown, was the heart of Black Los Angeles in the migration era, with jazz clubs, restaurants, and Black-owned businesses serving a community that was establishing itself in a city that was simultaneously constructing its own identity.

The defense industries of Southern California during World War II — the aircraft factories, the shipyards at Terminal Island and San Pedro, the munitions plants — were the primary pull factors drawing Black migrants to Los Angeles in the Second Great Migration. Executive Order 8802, issued by President Roosevelt in 1941 in response to A. Philip Randolph's threatened March on Washington, prohibited racial discrimination in defense industries, creating an opening for Black workers in the California defense boom that drew people from Texas, Louisiana, and other southwestern states in large numbers. Los Angeles's Black population grew from approximately 75,000 in 1940 to more than 170,000 by 1950 — a 126 percent increase in a single decade. This rapid growth strained housing and community institutions, as the existing Black community was overwhelmed by new arrivals, and it concentrated Black settlement in the neighborhoods of Central Avenue, Watts, and Compton that would become, decades later, the geographic context for some of the most dramatic events in the history of Black street organizations.

Historical Vocabulary
Race Riot

The term race riot has been applied in American history to a wide range of violent episodes involving racial conflict, but historians have increasingly recognized that the term obscures more than it reveals by suggesting a symmetrical conflict between racial groups. Many of the events described as "race riots" in early twentieth-century America were in fact one-sided attacks by white mobs on Black communities, in which Black residents defended themselves when able but were the primary victims and their property the primary target of destruction.

The Chicago Race Riot of 1919, the Tulsa Race Massacre of 1921, the Rosewood Massacre of 1923, and dozens of similar events throughout the migration era were, in practice, organized white violence against Black communities that had become too successful, too assertive, or too visible for the comfort of the surrounding white community. The term "massacre" has increasingly replaced "riot" in historical usage for events like Tulsa, where the one-sided character of the violence is clearest.

The term "riot" is also applied to events in the 1960s and later, such as the Watts Uprising of 1965 and the Detroit Uprising of 1967, in which Black urban residents rose in rebellion against police violence, economic deprivation, and racial inequality. These events were very different in character from the white mob attacks of the migration era — they were primarily Black-initiated acts of rebellion against oppressive conditions rather than white attacks on Black communities — and the conflation of the two types of events under the single term "race riot" has historically served to obscure these differences.

Students should be alert to the ideological implications of the terminology applied to racial violence and should evaluate specific uses of terms like "riot," "massacre," "uprising," and "rebellion" critically, asking what the choice of terminology implies about who the protagonists and who the victims are, and whose violence is framed as illegitimate.

Everyday Life and Social History

Life in Bronzeville: An Ordinary Day in Black Chicago, 1925

To understand what the Great Migration produced at the human level, it helps to enter the texture of ordinary daily life in a migration-era Black urban neighborhood. Consider a Tuesday morning in Bronzeville, the heart of Black Chicago, in 1925. The day begins before dawn in an apartment on South State Street — a four-room flat that houses a family of five and two boarders, a common arrangement in a neighborhood where housing was scarce and rents were high. The father, James, has worked the night shift at the stockyards on the South Side; he comes home smelling of blood and cattle as his wife, Ella, is cooking breakfast for the children before school. The boarders, two young men who arrived from Mississippi three months ago and are still finding their footing, leave early for the labor exchange where day workers gather in hopes of pickup work on the docks or in one of the warehouses.

The children — twelve-year-old Robert, ten-year-old Mabel, and seven-year-old Thomas — walk to the Wendell Phillips school, a Chicago public school that, unlike the explicitly segregated schools of the South, operates under formal race-neutral policies, though in practice it serves an almost entirely Black student body because of the residential concentration of the Black Belt. The school is overcrowded — classrooms that were built for thirty students hold forty or more — but the teachers include several Black women who are graduates of Howard University and Fisk and who take the education of their students seriously as a matter of civic and community obligation. Robert's teacher, Miss Johnson, has assigned the class a reading from the Chicago Defender, which she uses regularly as a text for literacy and civic education.

On State Street, the commercial heart of Bronzeville, the day's business is already underway. Jesse Binga's bank — the Binga State Bank, the largest Black-owned bank in the North — is open for deposits and loans, serving a community that has been systematically excluded from white-owned banks. A block away, a tailor shop owned by a man from Louisiana, a barber shop operated by a Memphis family, a grocery store run by a couple from Georgia, and a beauty parlor owned by a woman who trained in Madame C.J. Walker's products all conduct their business with the Black community that constitutes their entire market. The visible presence of Black-owned businesses along State Street represents both the institutional achievement of the community and the economic consequence of its confinement: Black Chicagoans spend their money in Black Bronzeville because they cannot always spend it elsewhere without encountering hostility or exclusion.

In the evening, the neighborhood's other life begins. The jazz clubs and blues joints along State Street and on 35th, where the music continues until early morning, draw customers from throughout the neighborhood and, on weekends, from the white North Side as well. Louis Armstrong, who arrived in Chicago from New Orleans in 1922, is playing at the Sunset Cafe on 35th Street — his Hot Five recordings are already making him famous, and his nightly performances are drawing crowds that understand they are witnessing something extraordinary. The music that migrants have brought from New Orleans, Memphis, and the Mississippi Delta is being transformed in Chicago's clubs into something new: louder, more urbane, more commercially sophisticated, but still rooted in the same emotional core of blues feeling that came from the cotton fields and the churches of the South. The Black Metropolis is producing culture that will outlast the conditions of its production by a century.

After the children are in bed, James and Ella sit in the kitchen and talk quietly about the letter they received this week from James's mother in Mississippi. She is not well. The cost of bringing her to Chicago is more than they can manage right now, and the idea of sending James back to Mississippi — back to the county where he left under circumstances that the landlord remembers clearly — is frightening. This is the reality that the migration letters describe only obliquely: the knowledge that the freedom of the North was purchased at the price of separation from family, and that the people left behind were still living under conditions that the migrants had escaped. The freedom of Bronzeville was real and significant. It was also partial, costly, and surrounded by a city that made clear, in a hundred ways, that it wanted the labor of Black workers more than it wanted the presence of Black people.

Map Study

The Geography of the Great Migration: Routes, Origins, and Destinations

To understand the Great Migration, students should be able to identify and analyze the following geographic features and patterns on maps of the United States:

  • The Black Belt: The arc of counties in Alabama, Mississippi, Georgia, and South Carolina with large Black populations, named for the dark, fertile soil on which cotton was grown. This region was the source of the majority of First Great Migration migrants to Chicago, Detroit, and other midwestern cities.
  • Major railroad routes: The Illinois Central (Mississippi/Louisiana to Chicago), the Southern Railway (Georgia/Carolinas to Washington and Northeast), the Louisville and Nashville (Alabama/Tennessee to Chicago and Detroit), and the Atlantic Coast Line (Florida/Carolinas to the Northeast). These were the primary transportation arteries of the migration, and their routes shaped where people went.
  • Primary destination cities: Chicago, Detroit, New York/Harlem, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Washington D.C., Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Cincinnati — the cities of the industrial North and border states that received the bulk of the First Great Migration. Also, Los Angeles, Oakland, Seattle, and other Western cities that became major destinations of the Second Great Migration.
  • The "Black Belt" neighborhoods: Within destination cities, the specific neighborhoods where Black migrants settled — Bronzeville on Chicago's South Side, Harlem in upper Manhattan, Paradise Valley in Detroit's east side, North Philadelphia — defined by the restrictive covenants and racial real estate practices that confined Black settlement to specific areas.
  • Industrial corridors: The locations of the major industrial facilities — Chicago stockyards, Detroit automobile plants, Pittsburgh steel mills, Gary steel complexes — relative to Black residential neighborhoods. The spatial relationship between workplace and residence was shaped both by industrial geography and by housing discrimination.
  • Sites of racial violence: The locations of the East St. Louis Massacre (1917), Chicago Race Riot (1919), Tulsa Race Massacre (1921), Rosewood Massacre (1923), and other major episodes of anti-Black violence during the migration era illuminate the geography of white resistance to Black migration.

Primary Source Window

Migration Letters: Black Southerners Write to the Chicago Defender, 1916–1917

Creator: Anonymous Black Southerners  ·  Date: 1916–1917  ·  Archive: Emmett J. Scott Collection; published in the Journal of Negro History, 1919  ·  Type: Personal letters; primary sources

Context: Beginning in 1916, the Chicago Defender began receiving a flood of letters from Black Southerners seeking information about migration to the North. Emmett J. Scott, secretary of Tuskegee Institute, collected hundreds of these letters in 1916–1917 and published a selection in the Journal of Negro History in 1919, providing an invaluable primary source record of the voices, concerns, and motivations of ordinary Black people in the early stages of the Great Migration. The letters are anonymized but identified by state of origin.

Note on reading: These letters were written by people who did not have access to formal education in many cases, and their spelling, grammar, and punctuation reflect that. Historians present these letters as they were written, without corrections, as a matter of respect for the historical record and because the specific language of the letters — including the ways their writers expressed themselves — is itself historically significant evidence.

From a man in Hattiesburg, Mississippi, 1917: "Dear Sir: I saw your ad in the Defender about the labor situation in Chicago...I am a young man 21 years old...I wish to know if I can get a job their. I know something about the packing houses their and feel I could get in...Please let me know as soon as possible about it. I am in the darkness of the South and I am trying my best to get out."
From a man in Anniston, Alabama, 1917: "I want to come north to Chicago and I want to ask you some questions about it...I am getting $1.50 a day and its very hard to get this. I have a wife and 2 children who I am trying to get out of this land of suffering. Do you know anyone in Chicago who wants a man to work or do you know what kind of work I can get...P.S. Please dont publish my name in the paper."
From a woman in New Orleans, Louisiana, 1917: "I am a widow woman with six children ages from five years to seventeen I am hard working but wages are so low down here and provisions are so high that I cannot possibly send my children to school as they should go...I have been trying so hard to save enough to take us north but it seems impossible to save while living here. I am a good cook, laundress, and housekeeper and I know I can get work in Chicago."

Significance and analysis: These letters are among the most direct and revealing historical documents of the Great Migration, because they come from the people who made the migration rather than from journalists, officials, or social workers observing them. They reveal the specific economic calculations that migrants were making (the comparison of wages, the calculation of costs), the family structures and responsibilities that shaped migration decisions, the fear of disclosure (the postscript asking not to publish the name illustrates the real dangers of being known to want to leave), and the blend of economic calculation and emotional aspiration ("the darkness of the South") that characterized the decision to migrate.

Limits of the source: These letters represent people who were sufficiently literate to write a letter and sufficiently informed to know the Defender existed — a self-selected population that may not be representative of all migrants. They do not represent migrants who could not write, migrants who received information through other channels, or migrants who moved without ever contacting the Defender. The publication of a selection by Emmett Scott also means we are reading letters that Scott considered representative or significant, which may have introduced its own selection bias.

Questions for students:

  1. What specific push factors are expressed in these letters? What specific pull factors? How do these align with the broad categories of push and pull factors discussed in the chapter?
  2. What do these letters reveal about the family circumstances of potential migrants? How did family responsibilities both motivate and complicate the decision to migrate?
  3. One writer asks the Defender not to publish his name in the paper. What does this request tell us about the conditions under which these letters were written? What was the writer afraid of?
  4. The phrase "the darkness of the South" in the first letter is an extraordinarily evocative expression of what the writer was experiencing. What dimensions of Jim Crow life do you think it encompassed? What was this person describing when he wrote those words?
  5. These letters were written to a newspaper. How might the knowledge that one is writing to a publication affect what a person chooses to say and how they choose to say it? What might these writers have expressed differently in a private letter to a family member?
Historical Debate

What Drove the Great Migration? Interpreting the Push-Pull Dynamic

The question: Historians have long debated the relative weight of "push" factors — the conditions in the South that drove Black Southerners out — versus "pull" factors — the conditions in the North that attracted them. This may seem like a simple question, but it has significant implications for how we understand the migration: was it primarily a flight from oppression, or an exercise of economic agency in search of opportunity? The answer matters not only for historical understanding but for how we assess the role of structural conditions versus individual choice in producing major population movements.

Interpretation A — The "Flight from Terror" View: The dominant early interpretation of the Great Migration, emphasized by contemporary observers including W.E.B. Du Bois and by early historians of the movement, framed the migration primarily as a flight from racial terror, economic exploitation, and political disenfranchisement. In this view, the migration was driven above all by the unbearable conditions of southern Black life — the violence, the debt peonage, the disenfranchisement, the daily humiliations of Jim Crow — that made staying untenable. The North's specific attractions were secondary; what primarily mattered was the necessity of leaving. This interpretation emphasizes the coercive character of the push factors and foregrounds the agency of Black Southerners in refusing to accept the conditions imposed on them.
Interpretation B — The "Economic Agency" View: A competing interpretation, developed particularly by economists and social historians who have analyzed migration data quantitatively, emphasizes the economic rationality of migration decisions and the importance of pull factors — specifically, the wage differential between the South and the North — in driving the timing and scale of the migration. In this view, Black Southerners migrated when and where they did not primarily because conditions in the South became suddenly worse (though the boll weevil crisis was significant), but because conditions in the North became suddenly better — specifically, because World War I labor shortages opened industrial employment in northern cities that had previously been closed to Black workers. This interpretation emphasizes migrant agency and rational economic calculation.
Interpretation C — Integrated Push-Pull Analysis: Most contemporary historians take an integrated view that refuses to treat push and pull factors as separate, sequential causes. In this view, the Great Migration cannot be understood through either push or pull alone because both were necessary conditions: without the economic opportunity in the North, the push of southern oppression would not have produced migration (as it had not for the fifty years between the end of slavery and the First World War); without the ongoing and intensifying oppression of the South, the pull of northern wages would not have been sufficient to induce the disruption and risk of migration. What changed around 1915 was not that conditions in the South became suddenly and uniquely worse, but that for the first time the combination of southern push and northern pull created conditions in which migration was both necessary and possible for hundreds of thousands of people simultaneously. This view also emphasizes the role of information networks — the Black press, migration chains, labor agents — in making both the need and the possibility visible to people who might not otherwise have known about either.

Why it matters: The interpretation of migration causation is not merely an academic question. If the Great Migration was primarily a flight from terror, then the structural conditions of racial oppression bear primary responsibility for its consequences — including the conditions of northern Black neighborhoods that were shaped by the mass movement of people into confined spaces. If it was primarily an exercise of economic agency, then the emphasis falls on the choices and capacities of migrants themselves, which has different implications for policy and for the assessment of responsibility. The integrated view suggests that both structural conditions and individual agency were necessary parts of the explanation — a position that refuses the false choice between a story of victimhood and a story of pure self-determination.

Student task: Using the evidence presented in this chapter, evaluate which of these interpretations is best supported by the historical record. What kinds of evidence would most clearly distinguish between the push and pull interpretations? And what does the evidence of the migration letters suggest about how migrants themselves understood their own decisions?

Multiple Perspectives

Arriving in the City: Multiple Perspectives on the Great Migration

From the perspective of new migrants from the rural South: Arriving in Chicago, Detroit, or New York for the first time was an overwhelming experience — the noise, the scale, the density, the speed of urban industrial life were qualitatively unlike anything in the rural South. The first impressions were often a mixture of awe and disorientation. The freedom was real: you could walk down a street without performing racial deference, your children could go to a real school, you could vote. But the city was also cold in ways that were not only meteorological: northern white people were not warm to Black newcomers, housing was expensive and hard to find, and the social norms of the city — its pace, its anonymity, its emphasis on formal credentials and industrial time-discipline — were unfamiliar. Many migrants found that the hardest part of the transition was not the work but the loss of community: the absence of the kinship networks, the church fellowship, and the neighborhood familiarity that had been the fabric of life in the South.
From the perspective of established northern Black communities: The older, established Black communities of northern cities — the descendants of antebellum free Black populations or of earlier migration waves — had complex reactions to the new arrivals. There was genuine solidarity and a sense of obligation to help people who were fleeing conditions that all Black Americans understood. But there was also anxiety: established middle-class Black communities worried that the association of the Black community with the visible poverty, unfamiliar cultural styles, and working-class habits of the new arrivals would undermine the careful respectability that they had built up over decades of negotiating with white urban society. The tensions between "Old Settlers" and new migrants were a feature of virtually every major northern Black community during the migration era, and they shaped debates about social norms, church practices, political strategy, and neighborhood governance.
From the perspective of white northern workers and neighbors: Many white northern workers and residents experienced Black migration with hostility that they expressed in a range of forms, from avoidance and social discrimination to organized violence. White workers in industries where Black migrants were employed — particularly in meatpacking and steel — often viewed Black workers as a threat to their wages and to the union organizing that they were attempting, because employers used Black workers as strikebreakers and potential replacements for white labor. White residents in neighborhoods adjacent to expanding Black communities organized neighborhood associations, used restrictive covenants, and in many cases resorted to physical violence — bombing homes, organizing mob attacks on Black families who moved in — to prevent the racial integration of their neighborhoods. This organized white resistance to Black migration shaped the geography of Black settlement in northern cities more powerfully than any other single force.
From the perspective of northern industrial employers: Northern employers who recruited and employed Black migrants had economic motivations that were not primarily humanitarian. Black workers filled a labor shortage, they accepted wages that white workers were beginning to resist, and they could be used — as many employers openly calculated — as a check against union organizing by white workers. The relationship between Black workers and northern employers was thus fundamentally exploitative even when it represented a genuine improvement over conditions in the South. Some employers, like Henry Ford, maintained relatively consistent employment of Black workers even during economic downturns when they might have been first to be laid off. Others used Black workers primarily as a reserve labor supply, hiring them aggressively during expansion and dismissing them first during contraction.
From the perspective of Black women migrants: Black women's experience of the Great Migration was shaped by the specific economic position of women in the northern urban economy as well as by the broader conditions of race. The primary employment available to Black women in northern cities was domestic service — cooking, cleaning, childcare, and laundry work in white households — which was in many respects a continuation of the domestic labor that Black women had performed under slavery and Jim Crow, now for wages rather than under coercion. Domestic service was poorly paid, offered no legal protections, and involved the same racial servility — entering through back doors, being addressed by first name while addressing employers formally — that characterized so much of southern Black life. For many Black women migrants, the freedom of the North was experienced most acutely not in the workplace but in public life: the ability to ride a streetcar without moving to the back, the absence of the explicit racial codes of southern public space, and the possibility, in the anonymity of the city, of a social life that was not conducted under the constant surveillance of white employers and neighbors.
Cause and Effect

The Great Migration: Structural Causes and Long-Term Consequences

Long-Term Causes

Decades of accumulating pressure under Jim Crow: racial terror, economic exploitation through sharecropping and debt peonage, political disenfranchisement, educational exclusion, and the systematic denial of the economic resources and legal protections that would have been necessary for genuine Black freedom in the South. These conditions were building from the end of Reconstruction onward, creating a migration potential that required only the right combination of triggering factors to become actual movement.

Short-Term and Proximate Causes

The boll weevil crisis (1915–1922) that devastated cotton agriculture; World War I, which simultaneously curtailed European immigration and created massive industrial labor demand in the North; the active recruitment campaigns of northern employers and labor agents; the information network created by the Chicago Defender and other Black newspapers; and the specific geographic opportunities created by the railroad networks connecting the South to northern industrial cities.

The Development

Approximately 1.6 million Black Southerners moved to northern and western cities between 1910 and 1940, followed by approximately four million more between 1940 and 1970. The migration created large, concentrated Black urban communities in Chicago, Detroit, New York, Philadelphia, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Los Angeles, and dozens of other cities, transforming the demographic and cultural geography of America.

Long-Term Consequences

The creation of Black urban communities that became the centers of twentieth-century African American cultural, political, and intellectual life; the development of the Black political constituency that reshaped northern Democratic politics and eventually fueled the civil rights movement; the establishment of concentrated, segregated Black neighborhoods that combined extraordinary cultural productivity with structural economic vulnerability; and the creation of the geographic and social context in which Black street organizations would eventually emerge.

Historical complexity: The Great Migration's consequences were never simple or unidirectional. The same concentration that produced Bronzeville's extraordinary cultural life also created the conditions of overcrowding, landlord exploitation, and physical deterioration that made Black urban neighborhoods particularly vulnerable to the deindustrialization and disinvestment of the postwar decades. Understanding this dual character — the creative achievement and the structural vulnerability — is essential to understanding both what the Great Migration produced and what it set in motion.

Continuity and Change Over Time

Black Community Life: From the Rural South to the Urban North

What Changed

  • The geographic and physical context of Black life shifted from rural, dispersed agricultural communities to dense urban neighborhoods, with all that this implied for the character of social interaction, community organization, and daily experience
  • The economic basis of Black life shifted from agricultural labor, controlled by white landowners and structured around the agricultural calendar, to industrial wage labor, controlled by factory owners and structured around the clock, the shift, and the industrial production schedule
  • Black Americans gained formal access to the ballot in northern states, participating in electoral politics in ways that were literally impossible in the Jim Crow South — a development with significant long-term consequences for the growth of Black political power
  • Black cultural life flourished in new forms that were specifically urban: jazz clubs, blues venues, literary salons, newspapers, department stores, and the dense anonymous social world of the city produced cultural innovations that would not have been possible in the social context of the rural South
  • The experience of childhood changed dramatically: northern cities offered Black children schools that were formally integrated and more generously resourced than southern segregated schools, and the urban environment offered a set of experiences — the diversity of the city, the anonymity, the mobility — that shaped the next generation's expectations and self-understanding in ways that differed from the South

What Stayed the Same

  • The fundamental fact of racial discrimination persisted in the North, expressed through different mechanisms — housing market exclusion, industrial wage discrimination, police bias — but producing racial inequality in resources, opportunity, and safety that paralleled in important ways the inequality of the South
  • The Black church remained the central institution of community life, as migrants established new congregations in northern cities and sought out the church community as the primary institutional anchor of the transition
  • The mutual aid tradition and the practice of community-based collective support continued and adapted to urban contexts, through fraternal orders, burial societies, benefit associations, and the informal practices of neighborhood solidarity
  • The pattern of Black community building — the construction of autonomous institutions as responses to exclusion from white-controlled institutions — continued in the North, producing Black-owned banks, insurance companies, newspapers, entertainment venues, professional associations, and civic organizations
  • The fundamental experience of navigating a white-controlled society while maintaining Black dignity, community, and cultural identity continued to shape Black life in the North as it had in the South, even as the specific forms of that navigation changed

Why the pattern matters: The combination of real change and persistent structural inequality that characterized the transition from the rural South to the urban North established the fundamental conditions of northern Black life for the decades that followed. The freedom was real. The inequality was also real. And the concentration of Black communities in specific neighborhoods — produced by housing discrimination — created the geographic conditions within which the youth organizations, street crews, and eventually street organizations that are central to this textbook's story would emerge in the generations after the migration.

Constitutional Connection

Housing Discrimination and Constitutional Rights: From Restrictive Covenants to Shelley v. Kraemer

The geographic confinement of Black migrants in northern cities was produced primarily through the private law mechanism of restrictive covenants — contractual provisions in property deeds that prohibited the sale or rental of property to people of specific racial or ethnic groups. These covenants were private contracts between property owners, not state laws, and for decades the courts held that they were constitutionally permissible because they involved private choices rather than state action. This was the constitutional doctrine of the "state action" requirement: the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause prohibits states from denying equal protection, but it does not (under the dominant interpretation through the mid-twentieth century) prohibit private actors from making discriminatory decisions.

The NAACP's legal challenge to restrictive covenants, led by Thurgood Marshall and other lawyers in the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, argued that when courts enforce private restrictive covenants — when a court issues an injunction preventing a Black family from moving into a house they had purchased — the courts are engaging in state action and thereby violating the Equal Protection Clause. The Supreme Court accepted this argument in Shelley v. Kraemer (1948), holding 6-0 that while private parties could enter into restrictive covenants, courts could not enforce them without violating the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection.

The Shelley decision was a constitutional milestone, but its practical impact was more limited than its legal significance suggested. Judicial enforcement was only one of several mechanisms through which residential segregation was maintained. Real estate industry discrimination — the refusal of white real estate agents to show properties in white neighborhoods to Black buyers, the refusal of mortgage companies to lend to Black buyers in white areas, the steering of Black buyers only to properties in already-Black or transitional neighborhoods — continued after Shelley and produced much of the same segregation without requiring judicial enforcement. Federal housing policy, through the redlining practices of the Home Owners' Loan Corporation and the Federal Housing Administration (which will be examined in detail in Chapter 3), actively reinforced residential segregation through the explicit racial classification of neighborhoods for lending purposes. And organized white community violence — bombings, arson, and mob attacks on Black families who attempted to move into white neighborhoods — continued in northern cities throughout the 1940s, 1950s, and 1960s, providing the threat of violence as a substitute for the legal enforcement that Shelley had prohibited.

The constitutional history of housing discrimination illustrates a recurring pattern in American civil rights law: the formal prohibition of specific legal mechanisms of discrimination did not produce the end of the discrimination itself, because the underlying racial hierarchy that discrimination served was sustained by other mechanisms that were not simultaneously prohibited. Understanding this pattern — the persistence of discrimination through changed legal forms — is essential to understanding both the history of Black urban communities and the limitations of legal reform as a mechanism for addressing structural racial inequality.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has traced the history of the Great Migration, one of the most consequential demographic transformations in American history, from its origins in the conditions of the Jim Crow South through the development of the major Black urban communities that the migration created. It has examined the push factors — racial violence, economic exploitation, agricultural crisis, and political disenfranchisement — that drove Black Southerners to leave the only homes they had ever known, and the pull factors — industrial wages, relative political freedom, and the promise of escape from the daily humiliations of Jim Crow — that drew them toward northern and western cities. It has traced the networks — family chains, Black newspapers, and churches — through which the migration was organized and sustained, and it has examined the conditions that migrants encountered in their destination cities.

The world that Black migrants entered in northern cities was better than the world they had left, in ways that were real and significant: higher wages, formal political rights, relative safety from the racial terror that had been a constant of southern Black life, and the possibility of a social world that was not organized around the explicit performance of racial subordination. These were not trivial improvements. For people who had lived under Jim Crow, they represented genuine liberation — the liberation of being able to vote, to walk freely, to address people without ritualized deference, to have one's children attend a school with real books and trained teachers. The freedom of the North was incomplete, compromised, and would prove more fragile than the initial experience of it suggested. But it was real.

And yet the North failed to deliver on its full promise. The housing market in northern cities was organized to confine Black residents to specific, overcrowded neighborhoods through restrictive covenants, discriminatory lending, and organized white violence against Black families who attempted to move into white areas. The labor market in northern industries excluded Black workers from skilled positions, union membership, and the better-paying occupations, and used them as a reserve labor supply that could be hired in expansion and discarded in contraction. The police, the courts, and the public schools served Black residents less well than they served white residents, in ways that reflected the same racial hierarchy that nominally the North had rejected. The result was a set of Black urban communities that were simultaneously centers of extraordinary cultural vitality and laboratories of structural inequality — communities that produced jazz, blues, literature, and political movements of world-historical significance while being systematically denied the economic and institutional resources that would have been necessary to sustain their vitality against the pressures that would come.

The major Black urban communities that the migration created — Bronzeville in Chicago, Harlem in New York, Paradise Valley in Detroit, Central Avenue in Los Angeles — were genuine achievements. They were places where Black American culture and community reached a kind of fullness and institutional completeness that had not been possible before the migration created the critical mass necessary to sustain them. The churches, newspapers, banks, insurance companies, entertainment venues, civic organizations, and professional associations of these communities represented the realization, in an urban industrial context, of the same fundamental impulse to build autonomous community institutions that the freedpeople of the Reconstruction era had expressed in a very different context. The Black Metropolis was, in this sense, the culmination of more than half a century of institution-building under conditions of racial exclusion.

The Harlem Renaissance and the parallel cultural flowerings in Bronzeville and other Black urban communities were the most visible expressions of what the Great Migration made possible. The extraordinary concentration of talented, creative, ambitious Black Americans in the specific social world of the northern Black urban neighborhood — the barbershops where ideas were exchanged, the church services where oral tradition was sustained and transformed, the nightclubs where musicians competed and collaborated, the literary societies where writers read each other's work — produced a cultural explosion that shaped American art, music, and literature for the rest of the twentieth century and beyond. The music alone — jazz, blues, gospel, rhythm and blues — has been one of the most consequential contributions to world culture in modern history, and it grew directly out of the specific conditions of the Great Migration.

The racial violence of the migration era — the East St. Louis Massacre of 1917, the Red Summer riots of 1919, the Tulsa Race Massacre of 1921, and dozens of smaller episodes of organized anti-Black violence — revealed the depth of white resistance to Black migration and the degree to which the freedom of the North was conditional and contested. These episodes of violence were not random. They were organized responses to specific perceived threats: Black economic competition, Black residential expansion, Black assertiveness in claiming rights and resources. They demonstrated that the migration had not moved Black Americans out of a context of racial threat but into a different context of racial threat, one that was less legally explicit but no less real.

The Second Great Migration, which brought an additional four million Black Southerners to northern and western cities between 1940 and 1970, occurred in a context that was both more promising and more treacherous than the first wave. The formal legal conditions for Black civil rights were gradually improving — the wartime Executive Order prohibiting employment discrimination, the postwar NAACP legal victories, and eventually the legislative achievements of the 1960s civil rights movement — while at the same time the economic conditions of northern industrial cities were beginning the long deterioration that would culminate in the deindustrialization crisis of the 1970s and 1980s. The second wave of migrants arrived just as the industrial economy that had sustained the first wave's settlement was beginning to contract, and they moved into neighborhoods that were already showing the signs of disinvestment and physical deterioration that would accelerate dramatically in the following decades.

The geographic pattern of Black settlement in northern cities — concentrated in specific neighborhoods by the mechanisms of housing discrimination examined more fully in the next chapter — was the single most important structural feature of the world that the Great Migration created. It was this concentration that made possible the extraordinary cultural and community achievements of the Black Metropolis. And it was this same concentration that created the conditions of vulnerability — to disinvestment, to poverty concentration, to the collapse of the industrial employment base — that would produce the urban crises of the late twentieth century. The neighborhood was the unit of the Great Migration's achievement, and the neighborhood would be the unit of its unraveling.

For the history that this textbook ultimately traces — the history of Black street organizations — the Great Migration's most important legacy is the creation of the urban neighborhoods in which those organizations emerged. The young people who formed the first Chicago street organizations in the 1950s and 1960s were the children and grandchildren of migrants, growing up in the neighborhoods that the migration had created and that housing discrimination had confined. The territorial identities, the neighborhood loyalties, the organizational forms, and the social needs that would shape the emergence of street organizations were all products of the specific urban geography that the Great Migration and its aftermath had produced. Understanding the migration is understanding the ground from which everything that followed grew.

Finally, this chapter has illustrated a theme that will recur throughout this textbook: that the conditions of Black urban life were not natural or inevitable but were produced by specific political choices — choices about housing policy, labor market regulation, urban investment, and the enforcement of civil rights — made by specific people and institutions at specific historical moments. The crowded, poorly maintained apartments of Bronzeville, the restricted employment opportunities of Black Detroit, the racial violence of the Red Summer — none of these were the natural consequences of Black poverty or cultural failure. They were the products of a set of political and economic arrangements designed by white interests to extract the labor of Black workers while excluding them from the full benefits of urban citizenship. Recognizing this is not a political position but a historical one: it is what the evidence shows.

Review Materials

Key Terms

Boll weevil
An insect that spread across the American South between 1915 and 1922, devastating cotton crops and destroying the economic basis of the sharecropping system. The boll weevil crisis was one of the most important short-term push factors driving the First Great Migration.
Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters
The labor union founded by A. Philip Randolph in 1925 to organize the Pullman porters, the Black men who staffed American passenger rail cars. After a twelve-year struggle, the union was recognized by the Pullman Company in 1937, making it one of the most significant victories in African American labor history.
Chain migration
A pattern of migration in which later migrants follow routes and settle in destinations established by earlier migrants from the same community, guided by the information and assistance that earlier migrants provide through personal networks of family and community connection.
Chicago Defender
The most widely circulated Black newspaper in America during the migration era, founded in 1905 by Robert Abbott. The Defender's advocacy for Black migration northward and its distribution through the Pullman porter network made it a primary informational engine of the Great Migration.
First Great Migration
The movement of approximately 1.6 million Black Americans from the rural South to northern industrial cities between approximately 1910 and 1930, driven by the combined pull of wartime labor demand and push of racial violence and agricultural crisis.
Freedmen's Bureau
See Chapter 1. During the migration era, the legacy of the Freedmen's Bureau's failure to secure land redistribution remained relevant as the continuing economic vulnerability of Black Southern agricultural workers was a central push factor of the migration.
Ghettoization
The process by which a specific population is confined to a specific geographic area through discriminatory practices, creating a concentrated and segregated community. The ghettoization of Black urban communities in the North through restrictive covenants, redlining, and organized white resistance was the primary mechanism through which the Great Migration created Black urban neighborhoods.
Great Migration
The movement of approximately six million Black Americans from the rural South to cities in the North, Midwest, and West between approximately 1910 and 1970. Historians identify two waves: the First Great Migration (c. 1910–1940) and the Second Great Migration (c. 1940–1970).
Harlem Renaissance
The extraordinary flowering of Black literary, artistic, musical, and intellectual production centered in Harlem, New York, during the 1920s. The Harlem Renaissance produced major writers including Langston Hughes and Zora Neale Hurston, visual artists including Aaron Douglas, and intellectual frameworks including Alain Locke's concept of the "New Negro."
Labor agent
A representative sent by northern industrial employers into the South to recruit Black workers, offering wages, train tickets, and information about employment in northern factories. Labor agents were crucial in the initial organization of the Great Migration and were systematically harassed by southern authorities who recognized the threat to their captive labor supply.
National Urban League
Organization founded in 1910 to assist Black migrants in the transition to northern urban life, providing job placement, housing assistance, social services, and training. The League's relationships with northern employers and social service agencies made it a primary institutional bridge between the rural South and the urban North during the migration era.
New Negro
A term used by Black intellectuals of the 1920s, particularly Alain Locke, to describe a new Black self-assertion and cultural confidence associated with the urban North and the Harlem Renaissance. The "New Negro" rejected the accommodationism of the Booker T. Washington era and insisted on full equality, cultural pride, and unapologetic Black self-expression.
Pull factors
The conditions in a destination that attract migrants, as opposed to the push factors that drive them from their place of origin. In the Great Migration, pull factors included northern industrial wages, relative political freedom, the presence of established Black communities and institutions, and escape from the racial terror of the Jim Crow South.
Pullman porter
A Black man employed by the Pullman Company to staff the sleeping cars of American passenger trains. Pullman porters were one of the largest Black occupational groups of the early twentieth century and played a crucial role in the Great Migration by distributing Black newspapers throughout the South and serving as carriers of information between Black communities in different cities.
Push factors
The conditions in a place of origin that drive people to leave, as opposed to pull factors that attract them to a destination. In the Great Migration, push factors included racial violence and lynching, economic exploitation through sharecropping and debt peonage, political disenfranchisement, agricultural crisis, and the daily humiliations of Jim Crow.
Race riot
A term applied to episodes of racial violence in American history. Historians have increasingly recognized that many events described as "race riots" — including the East St. Louis Massacre (1917), Chicago Race Riot (1919), and Tulsa Race Massacre (1921) — were one-sided attacks by white mobs on Black communities rather than symmetric conflicts, and the term "massacre" is often more accurate for the most destructive episodes.
Red Summer (1919)
The period between April and November 1919 during which racial violence erupted in at least twenty-six American cities. The Red Summer represented the peak of organized white resistance to Black migration and Black urban assertiveness, and it demonstrated the limits of northern "freedom" for Black Americans while also contributing to the emergence of the "New Negro" assertiveness that characterized Black political culture of the 1920s.
Restrictive covenant
A contractual provision in a property deed prohibiting the sale or rental of property to people of specific racial or ethnic groups. Restrictive covenants were the primary legal mechanism through which Black settlement in northern cities was confined to specific neighborhoods during the migration era, and they remained judicially enforceable until the Supreme Court's 1948 decision in Shelley v. Kraemer.
Second Great Migration
The movement of approximately four million Black Americans from the South to northern and western cities between approximately 1940 and 1970, driven primarily by World War II industrial labor demand, continuing racial violence in the South, and the growing availability of information about conditions in western cities. The Second Migration greatly expanded Black urban populations and established major new communities on the West Coast.
Shelley v. Kraemer (1948)
A Supreme Court decision holding that judicial enforcement of racially restrictive covenants in property deeds violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The decision prohibited courts from enforcing restrictive covenants but did not address the other mechanisms — real estate industry discrimination, federal lending policy, and organized white violence — that continued to maintain residential segregation.
Storefront church
A small, usually newly established religious congregation meeting in a rented storefront, basement, or converted commercial space, typically associated with the Black migrant communities of northern cities. Storefront churches were often affiliated with southern Black denominations and preserved the worship styles, musical traditions, and community functions that migrants brought from the South, serving as primary community institutions for recent arrivals.
Tulsa Race Massacre (1921)
The destruction of Tulsa's Greenwood District — "Black Wall Street," the most prosperous Black commercial district in America — by white mobs, some deputized by local authorities, on May 31 to June 1, 1921. The massacre killed an estimated 100 to 300 Black residents, destroyed 35 blocks of buildings, and left 10,000 Black Tulsans homeless. It was systematically covered up for decades by local authorities and not officially investigated until 2001.
Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA)
The mass Black nationalist organization founded by Marcus Garvey, which reached an estimated membership of two to four million people in the early 1920s. The UNIA's message of Black pride, economic self-sufficiency, and pan-African solidarity resonated powerfully with northern Black migrants who had found that the North had not delivered on its promises of full equality.

Major People and Places

Identification & Significance

Robert Sengstacke Abbott (1868–1940)
Founder of the Chicago Defender, the most widely circulated Black newspaper in America during the migration era. Abbott's paper played a central role in informing, organizing, and encouraging Black migration to Chicago, making him one of the most consequential figures in the history of the Great Migration even though he was a journalist rather than a labor organizer, civil rights leader, or government official.
Marcus Garvey (1887–1940)
Founder of the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), the largest Black mass organization in American history. Garvey's message of Black pride, pan-Africanism, and economic nationalism attracted millions of followers among Black urban migrants who had found that the North did not deliver on its promises of equality. His federal prosecution for mail fraud and eventual deportation demonstrated the capacity of the federal government to suppress Black political organizing that threatened white economic and political interests.
Langston Hughes (1902–1967)
The most celebrated poet of the Harlem Renaissance, Hughes gave literary voice to working-class Black urban life with unsparing clarity and formal innovation drawing on blues and jazz. His work challenged the bourgeois aspirations of some Harlem Renaissance writers and insisted on the cultural authenticity and value of ordinary Black urban experience.
A. Philip Randolph (1889–1979)
Founder of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters and civil rights leader whose 1941 threatened March on Washington forced President Roosevelt to issue Executive Order 8802 prohibiting racial discrimination in defense industries. Randolph bridged the labor movement and the civil rights movement and pioneered the use of mass mobilization as a political lever for federal civil rights action.
Zora Neale Hurston (1891–1960)
Novelist, folklorist, and anthropologist whose work, particularly the 1937 novel Their Eyes Were Watching God, drew on the Black Southern oral tradition to create literary art that was simultaneously ethnographically precise and aesthetically innovative. Hurston's work represents the continuity between the southern roots of the migration and the northern cultural flowering it produced.
St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton
Sociologists whose 1945 study Black Metropolis: A Study of Negro Life in a Northern City was the most comprehensive scholarly analysis of a Black urban community produced during the migration era. Their documentation of Bronzeville's institutional life, social stratification, economic conditions, and cultural practices remains an essential historical source for understanding what the Great Migration created.
Bronzeville, Chicago
The Black neighborhood on Chicago's South Side that became the center of Black metropolitan life during the Great Migration, known as the "Black Metropolis" for its concentration of Black institutions, businesses, cultural venues, and community organizations. Bronzeville was both the achievement of the Great Migration and the location of many of its contradictions: cultural vitality coexisting with overcrowding, exploitative landlordism, and economic vulnerability.
Harlem, New York City
The symbolic capital of Black America during the migration era, center of the Harlem Renaissance, and the destination for Black migrants from the South, the Caribbean, and the rest of the Northeast seeking the center of Black cultural and intellectual life. Harlem's extraordinary cultural flowering in the 1920s made it the most celebrated Black community in the world, even as its material conditions — overcrowded housing, exploitative rents, limited employment — remained a testimony to the limits of northern freedom.
Greenwood District, Tulsa ("Black Wall Street")
The most prosperous Black commercial district in America before the 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre, which burned 35 blocks to the ground and killed up to 300 Black residents. The destruction of Greenwood illustrated the degree to which Black economic success was not safe from racial violence and the systematic ways in which Black wealth accumulation was subject to organized destruction.
Illinois Central Railroad
The primary transportation route of the First Great Migration to Chicago, running from New Orleans through Mississippi, Tennessee, and Kentucky. The Illinois Central's route shaped the specific character of Black Chicago by determining which southern communities most easily connected to which northern destinations, producing the chain migration patterns that gave Bronzeville much of its Mississippi Delta character.
The Chicago Defender
The most important institutional actor in the Great Migration other than the migrants themselves, providing information about northern opportunities, documenting southern violence, and explicitly advocating for Black migration through its editorials and its famous "Great Northern Drive" campaign of 1917.
The National Urban League
The primary social service organization assisting Black migrants in the transition to northern urban life, providing job placement, housing assistance, and training. The League's relationships with northern employers and philanthropists made it a crucial but sometimes controversial institutional bridge between the rural South and the urban North.

Chapter Reflection

The Great Migration was the most consequential demographic event in African American history, and its consequences for American urban life have not yet fully worked themselves out. Everything that this textbook goes on to examine — the segregated neighborhoods, the youth organizations, the crack cocaine crisis, the war on drugs, the mass incarceration of Black men, the gentrification of Black neighborhoods — is rooted in the specific geographic and social world that the Great Migration created. The young people who formed the first Chicago street organizations in the 1950s were the children of migrants. The neighborhoods in which those organizations emerged were the neighborhoods that the migration had built and that housing discrimination had defined. The economic conditions that made those neighborhoods vulnerable to the crises of the 1970s and 1980s were the conditions produced by the intersection of migration-era housing confinement and postwar deindustrialization. To understand the story this textbook ultimately tells, one must first understand the story this chapter has told.

But the Great Migration was not only, or even primarily, a story that ends in tragedy. It was first of all a story of human determination — of millions of people who analyzed their circumstances with clear eyes, concluded that the risks of moving were less than the risks of staying, and cast themselves into an uncertain journey toward a better life. The fact that the better life they found was compromised, contested, and ultimately threatened by forces they did not control does not diminish the achievement of having found it. The cultural productions of the Harlem Renaissance and Bronzeville's jazz age, the political achievements of the northern Black voting bloc, the institutional life of the Black church and the Black press and the Black business community in migration-era cities — these were genuine achievements of genuine people acting with genuine agency in conditions that were designed to limit them. Recognizing structural inequality does not require denying human accomplishment. Both were real.

The migration also reveals, with particular clarity, a pattern that runs through all of American history: the pattern of Black Americans exercising agency to escape or improve their conditions, followed by white responses designed to contain or reverse those improvements. The push of Jim Crow was met by the pull of northern opportunity, and the migrations that resulted were met by restrictive covenants, race riots, and organized exclusion. When Black workers entered northern factories, they encountered union exclusion and strategic use as strikebreakers. When Black neighborhoods developed cultural and economic vitality, they were targets of organized white violence. When Black families attempted to move into better housing, they were bombed and threatened back. And when the formal legal mechanisms of exclusion were eventually struck down — as restrictive covenants were in 1948, as formal employment discrimination was in 1964 — other mechanisms (redlining, discriminatory lending, real estate steering, police enforcement of neighborhood boundaries) were ready to perform the same functions through different means. This pattern — of Black advancement followed by white resistance, of formal legal change followed by informal structural persistence — is not a conspiracy theory. It is what the historical evidence shows.

The chapter has also illustrated the tension between community and confinement that will characterize much of what follows in this textbook. The geographic concentration of Black life in specific neighborhoods — produced by the mechanisms of housing discrimination — simultaneously created the conditions for extraordinary community solidarity, institutional density, and cultural production, and the conditions for structural vulnerability, poverty concentration, and the physical deterioration that would accelerate with deindustrialization. The neighborhood was both the achievement of the Great Migration and its potential undoing. How that tension played out — through the decades of segregation, deindustrialization, drug crisis, and incarceration that later chapters examine — is the central story of Black urban America in the second half of the twentieth century. Understanding it clearly requires the historical foundation this chapter has provided.

As students move forward through this textbook, they should carry with them the image of the migrants themselves: the young men and women who rode the Illinois Central northward with everything they owned in a single bag, the families who crowded into rooming houses on South Side Chicago while the father figured out the meatpacking plant's shift system and the mother found domestic work in Lincoln Park, the children who sat in overcrowded classrooms in Bronzeville and learned to navigate a city that was simultaneously more free and more hostile than the world their parents had left. These were the people whose children and grandchildren would grow up in the neighborhoods that the Great Migration had created, who would form the youth clubs and street organizations that later chapters examine, who would be arrested and incarcerated in the millions during the war on drugs, who would be displaced by gentrification as their neighborhoods were eventually reclaimed by capital. The connection between those children and the migrants who made them possible is the thread that runs through everything that follows.
